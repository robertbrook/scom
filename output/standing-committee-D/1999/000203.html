Transnational Information and Consultation of Employees Regulations 1999

9.55 am

Mrs. Angela Browning
(Tiverton and Honiton)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Transnational Information and Consultation of Employees Regulations 1999 (S.I. 1999, No 3323).

The debate is indicative of new Labour—it is unnecessary and old-fashioned, and it shows no working knowledge of the way in which businesses have progressed since the 1970s. The measure has its genesis in the social chapter, from which the Conservative party negotiated an opt-out in the interest of all British businesses. That occurred when my right hon. Friend the Member for Huntingdon (Mr. Major) negotiated and signed the Maastricht treaty.

Under the Conservatives' stewardship in the previous Parliament, businesses were protected from outmoded, socialist and very European measures, and from Governments who think that they know better than businesses what should happen in a business. I worked for a British manufacturer in the 1970s. I ran my own business for 10 years during the 1980s and spent time with thousands of companies in this country and elsewhere, especially with manufacturers, which had traditionally often had a unionised work force.

The Committee will recall the culture in British manufacturers and in other companies during the 1970s. Our record included one of the highest rates of industrial strife measured in days lost. That was because of the way in which the unions and management failed to work together in the workplace.

That was the 1970s. The measure is old-fashioned because things have moved on for many reasons. In the 1980s, the Conservative party's Acts of Parliament reformed trade unions and gave control of them to the membership by taking it away from those who ran them and held power in them. Communication in the workplace between the management and the work force changed.

Companies are in a new millennium. There is a unionised work force in my constituency. When I go as a Member of Parliament to talk to a company, I expect the shop stewards to be sitting at the table with management. They have not needed an Act of Parliament or an interfering, busybody Government to tell them that that is the way to run their company. The approach has changed over the past 20 years.

4 We have not seen such an environment in all European countries. We recognise that France, for example, has been slow to put in place such trade union reforms as those from which we have benefited for 15 years. We still see there the power of the trade unions and the way in which strike action is still often a primary measure rather than a last resort.

That culture does not apply here, but the Government have decided not to build on what has been a sea change, not only in workplace trade union relations but, I hope, in the management of British businesses since the early 1980s. The combination of those and other factors has meant that there is a difference in the way in which work force and management communicate and combine when there is union recognition in a workplace.

Labour Members commonly throw across the Chamber the accusation that Conservative Members are anti-union. I shall put my credentials clearly on the table. I am not anti-union; I am "anti" the implication that provisions should be mandatory in the workplace. Union recognition should be voluntary. As a Member of Parliament, I frequently suggest that constituents with a difficult dispute in the workplace take advice from their union.

That is the difference. We have moved on, not because of Labour Members, who would still have us in the 1970s taking confrontational stances when issues are to be discussed, but because of the Conservative party, which liberated paid-up trade union members. [ Interruption.] Labour Members may laugh, but they know that that is true.

We should consider some of the other factors that have made a difference to the way in which management and unions communicate in the workplace. There are two matters to consider regarding the works councils. The first is recognition in the workplace, and the way in which the measure introduces the role of the unions. The second is that commonsense businesses and managers would do, are doing and have done for many years such things for themselves. That is why the measure is so old-fashioned and so 1970s. I am surprised that Government Members—

Mr. Lindsay Hoyle
(Chorley)
: Some of us do not remember the 1970s.

Mrs. Browning
: I am sure that younger hon. Members will wish to learn from us older MPs who have years of experience of working in British businesses such as manufacturers. I am happy to share my experiences with the hon. Member for Chorley (Mr. Hoyle); I hope that he will sit up and pay proper attention.

I want to give an example. In the 1980s, I fought the seat held by the hon. Member for Crewe and Nantwich (Mrs. Dunwoody), for whom I have great respect. There were then two main employers in Crewe—Rolls-Royce plc's motor car division, which was doing well, and British Rail Engineering Ltd, which was publicly owned. I used to go around canvassing. I am interested in what people do at work, and so would ask on the doorstep how people were getting on.

JPEG 5 At Rolls-Royce, even trainees would say, for instance, that there had been an order from the United States during the previous week. No matter what job a member of the big work force did, they would know such things. They worked for a company with such a line of communication. Management believed it essential that news was communicated, that people were motivated by it, that people understood the purpose of their job in the plant and that they contributed to the overall success of the company. When people from BREL were asked, they would shrug their shoulders and say that they did not know.

The contrast between the approaches of two different companies in the same town was stark. The difference was not only to do with unions and the work force, but to do with management styles. The contrast was between a privately owned company and one still in the public sector. The privatisation of so many big companies in the 1970s and 1980s by the Conservative party changed their workplace culture.

Many other factors were introduced. When I used to be in business on my own, back in the real world, I would talk to American or Japanese businesses. Both would claim credit for introducing just-in-time systems into the workplace. But in the early 1980s, just-in-time systems represented a change in culture for purchasers and suppliers. The very nature of the changes, especially in the manufacturing sector, meant that management had to communicate more with their work force. Quality-related issues became important. There were one or two unsuccessful experiments, but the general principles of improving quality and performance related to the output of goods and involved every person at every level in the workplace, both on the shop floor and in management.

***The Government want to impose works councils on a work force simply because they exist in a company's European operation That reflects the European Union's employment policies, under the aegis of the social chapter. The Government think that works councils are right for the United Kingdom, but they are not. Their attitude displays a lack of understanding of the way in which British companies have developed. The Government have not acknowledged how management and work forces work together. They do communicate. Representations are officially placed on the table when there is a matter for negotiation or discussion.

One of the Government's first acts was to sign up to the social chapter as part of the Amsterdam treaty. We are not discussing a new measure. It has already been imposed by Europe. The Government signed up not just for legislation that is yet to come—I am sure that there are more horrors in store—but for retrospective legislation. The size of the fines is outrageous. The Central Arbitration Committee can set up employment appeals and civil fines of up to £75,000 can be imposed. Since the Government came to power, we have known that although they talk the language of business, they do not understand how to run one. I hope that for once the Minister will give an accurate figure of the on-costs to business of imposing the regulations on their affairs. Up to now, British businesses with overseas interests have not needed 6 Ministers to tell them how to communicate. Will there be a mini-Alastair Campbell session for businesses so that the Government can teach them how to communicate better?

According to the Department of Trade and Industry, the recurring cost to business will be £14.5 million. If the Government were consultants setting up a system in a business, they would jolly well have to justify the benefits to charge a fee like that. I would have been glad to receive that fee to encourage businesses to improve their communications—an area in which I specialised. I was never underemployed and was reasonably well remunerated. But the Government are imposing more costs. They have created another regulation task force in the Cabinet Office to remove the very regulations that they are imposing day after day, and which add to overall business costs. That money has to come off the bottom line.

More importantly, the Government have the arrogance to tell business that when it comes to communication, they know best. They think that they know how to create the structures in a workplace that allow management and work force to work together for mutual benefit. But that is another sop to keep their European partners happy. The Government owe them a few favours because they are dragging their feet on joining the euro and abandoning the pound. Somewhere along the line, they have to throw them the odd titbit to show that they are good Europeans and that they are on board. I understand the motivation, but the regulations are bad for British business and old-fashioned; they have a place in the 1970s, but not in the new millennium. When I speak to representatives of businesses—this applies more to big businesses than to small businesses—I get one message, which is that they want the Government off their backs. This measure does nothing to encourage businesses to believe that the Government are serious about business and understand it or, for that matter, that they should be taken seriously when they talk about lifting burdens, deregulation and the rest. It is all a load of tosh.

The Minister for Competitiveness (Mr. Alan Johnson)
: Much as I would love to join in a philosophical debate about the change in employment relations since the 1970s, the changes in the trade union movement and changes in the whole gamut of the workplace, I would much rather discuss the European works council directive, which is completely different. I understand that I will have another chance to respond to some of the points that have been made, but I think that it is obligatory for me to place on the record something about what we have been debating this morning. It is not the end of civilisation as we know it. In fact the hon. Member for Tiverton and Honiton and the rest of the Conservative party are alone of all the centre right mainstream political parties in Europe because no politician from any sister party would have made such a speech. It would be absolutely incomprehensible to them, as it is to the Confederation of British Industry, the Engineering Employers Federation and the European JPEG 7 study group, which consists of 50 employers' representatives who worked closely with us on the regulation.

Mrs. Browning
: I have a letter from the European study group, dated December 1999, which says,

"These regulations are expected to propose the introduction of automatic legally binding collective agreement. This concept was abandoned when tried in the 1970s and is not welcomed by employers."

Surely the hon. Gentleman does not support that.

Mr. Johnson
: I am sorry, but I did not catch the name of the organisation.

Mrs. Browning
: The European study group. I think that was the name of the organisation that the Minister just mentioned.

Mr. Johnson
: The European study group has worked closely with us.

Improving information and consultation is a primary objective of collective arrangements and is especially important in large multinational organisations where management decisions may be taken far away from the employees affected. That is why the Government welcome the extension to the United Kingdom of the European works council directive, which lays down sensible minimum standards for informing and consulting employees at European level.

The regulations that we are discussing today fulfil not only a manifesto commitment—I remind the Opposition that we actually fought the previous election on the basis that we would sign the social chapter, which is probably one of the reasons for our overwhelming victory, but the Government are also implementing directive 97/74, which extends the European works council directive to the UK.

The European works council directive sets out requirements for informing and consulting employees at the European level in undertakings, which may include partnerships or other forms of organisation, as well as companies or groups with at least 1,000 employees throughout the member states and at least 150 employees in each of two or more of those member states. It therefore applies only to large multinational undertakings and organisations such as Glaxo Wellcome, British Telecom, Unilever and NatWest, which have already reached agreements voluntarily with no problems whatsoever.

Mrs. Browning
: Voluntarily.

Mr. Alan Johnson
: Well, I shall come to that. We are discussing voluntary arrangements. The regulations set out procedures for negotiating a European works council directive between management and employees. This process is not imposed on anyone; it is triggered either as a result of a management initiative or after a written request from at least 100 employees or their representatives in two or more member states. There is no obligation to negotiate if no request is received. It is mainly for the parties concerned to decide what details are contained in the European works council agreements. 8 Negotiations occur between management and employees, with the latter represented by a special negotiating body consisting of representatives of employees from all the European Economic Area member states. The area in which the undertakings operate goes wider than the European Union and includes Norway, Liechtenstein and Iceland, which voluntarily entered into the measures. In general, a ballot of the entire UK work force will select the United Kingdom's members of a special negotiating body, unless a consultative committee exists. A consultative committee whose members were elected by a ballot of all UK employees and which performs an information and consultation function on their behalf may appoint from its number the UK representatives on the special negotiating body.

Special provisions apply if management refuse to negotiate within six months of receiving a request for a European works council or if the parties fail to conclude a negotiated agreement within three years. In such cases, a European works council must be set up in accordance with the statutory model set out in the schedule to the regulations. The fallback position therefore applies only if a company and work force cannot voluntarily agree their own arrangements. The schedule sets out requirements concerning the size and establishment of a European works council and lists topics on which it has the right to be informed and consulted.

The regulations apply on a UK wide and will be enforced through the central arbitration committee and the employment appeals tribunal in Great Britain and the industrial court in Northern Ireland. The central arbitration committee and the employment appeals tribunal may refer a case to the Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service if conciliation is considered possible. The employment appeals tribunal may impose a financial penalty if management is found to have acted in breach of the regulations without reasonable excuse. The hon. Member for Tiverton and Honiton quoted a figure of £75,000, but I doubt whether that would mean much to Glaxo Wellcome, British Telecom or Unilever.

Mrs. Browning
: To whom does the hon. Gentleman think that corporate fines are passed on?

Mr. Johnson
: If I had been speaking about a fine of £750,000, the hon. Lady might have had a point, but I was speaking about one of £75,000. The fine is meant to be an impediment to organisations that refuse to comply with proper practice, having received a petition from their employees to set up a European works council. It should also be an impediment to companies whose employees are not receiving information and are not being consulted on issues that affect their working lives. The measure is very reasonable in such circumstances. Some Labour Members think that they do not go far enough—

Mr. Richard Page
(South-West Hertfordshire)
: Will non-compliance with such penalties lead to a prison sentence?

Mr. Johnson
: Only under contempt of court, I presume. I am speaking about civil fines, which do not operate under criminal justice procedure.

JPEG 9 The regulations address the issue of confidentiality and provide that management may withhold information or require a European works council to hold it in confidence where,

"according to objective criteria, the disclosure of the information or document would seriously harm the functioning of, or would be prejudicial to, the undertaking".

The regulations contain specific measures to ensure that the information offered by an employer, which will sometimes be sensitive, is kept confidential. If employees believe that management are withholding information or imposing confidentiality beyond what is permitted by the regulations, the special negotiating body or European works council members can appeal to the central arbitration committee, which would rule case by case. The employees and members of a special negotiating body and the European works council are given protections when asserting their rights or performing duties under the regulations. Employment tribunals and industrial tribunals in Northern Ireland will hear claims that relate to victimisation or unfair dismissal.

An important feature of the regulations is that they do not apply to undertakings that have concluded voluntary agreements where these provide for transnational information and consultation of the employees, and cover the entire work force in the European Economic Area. Such agreements must have been made by the deadline laid down in the relevant directive. There is no attempt to interfere with voluntary agreements already reached.

Transitional provisions apply to undertakings, here and abroad, that have started negotiations under the law of another member state, before the entry into force of the UK regulations. Where a UK-based undertaking has previously concluded a European works council agreement in another member state, it may be made subject to the UK regulations if both parties consent. Otherwise, it remains under the jurisdiction of the member state under whose legislation it was concluded.

The European works council directive provides a flexible structure under which multinational enterprises can formulate agreements to inform and consult their employees at a European level. In implementing the directive through these regulations, we are conferring on UK employees the same rights to information on their business that their colleagues in other EU states enjoy.

By opposing the measure, the hon. Member for Tiverton and Honiton suggests that only British workers of all the work forces in a multinational company covering the whole of the European Economic Area can be left out of the loop and managers can disregard their interests and fail to inform or to consult them. We are extending the same rights that their colleagues in other EU states have enjoyed for the past three years.

Many UK companies are already covered by the directive as a result of their extensive operations throughout Europe, the vast majority of which already include their UK work force in their arrangements, but until now they have not been obliged to do so. 10 Previously, they could exclude what was often their largest single European work force from receiving information that directly affected them. The regulations end that anomaly.

The Government are mindful of the voluntary tradition in UK employment relations and the directive acknowledges the benefit of that approach by exempting organisations that already have European-level information and consultation procedures. The original directive allowed companies two years to set up such agreements, and when we signed up to the directive we negotiated a similar two-year period for newly covered companies to establish their own arrangements. Therefore, we announced in 1998 that companies that had voluntary agreements in place by 15 December 1999 would not be covered by the obligations in the regulations. In that way, companies were aware of the opportunity to develop their own arrangements and had time to do so. That is an important provision, and of the vast majority of existing EWC European works council agreements about three-quarters are voluntary agreements.

In developing the regulation, we have listened to both sides of industry. Before implementation, we carried out a full three-month consultation on the detailed policy and regulations. We also held a seminar explaining the consultation proposals and allowing interested parties to provide less formal feedback. As a result of the responses, we made a number of changes before laying the final regulations. The changes have improved the final package, which follows the principle of avoiding unnecessary "goldplating" of the directive and does not disturb the existing arrangements of companies that have EWCs or voluntary agreements. As a result of the thorough consultation that we have undertaken, implementation has not proved to be controversial with either the CBI or TUC or with anyone other than the hon. Member for Tiverton and Honiton and there has been little comment in the media on the regulations.

My Department commissioned an independent report into the costs associated with European works councils based on a survey of companies with real life experience of operating agreements. The results of that study were incorporated into the regulatory impact assessment, a draft of which was included in the consultation. While the directive has been fully implemented, the regulatory impact of our policy has been minimised.

Let us be clear who is covered by the regulations. They do not affect companies that already have agreements. They do not affect the small local business man running his operation with 60 employees. They do not affect the large-scale enterprise with thousands of employees, if it has no operations in the European Economic Area. The regulations cover only multinationals that have at least 1,000 employees in total and at least 150 employees in each of at least two different EEA countries.

Mrs. Browning
: Can the Minister confirm just how many companies are affected? As he will know, his Government quoted 110 countries, but that figure has been challenged by many people, not least the law firm JPEG 11 Ashurst Morris Crisp, which says that it is an underestimate of the number of companies.

Mr. Johnson
: I saw that article in the Financial Times. It is the only article published anywhere and it gave no supporting information. The best analysis is the TUC's analysis of the companies affected, which we have used throughout and which has not been challenged from any quarter. We say that the measure will affect 111 companies in this country.

It should be emphasised that the regulations are mainly concerned with procedures rather than the content, which is left to the parties. In the first instant the regulations give precedence to voluntary agreements negotiated prior to their introduction. Where a voluntary agreement does not exist the regulations provide for the establishment of negotiating procedures so that management and employees can come to their own agreement. It is only where negotiations fail that the fall-back provisions contained in the schedule will apply.

The regulations will be enforced by the parties themselves, by the CAC or the EAT. The Government judged it unnecessary for an entirely new body to be created to enforce the regulations, so new powers are to be granted to those existing bodies. Both are well acquainted with employment relations matters. The EAT will be able to impose a civil financial penalty of up to £75,000 on an employer where appropriate, but a penal approach is not the aim of the regulations. The emphasis throughout is on negotiation. Both the CAC and the EAT will look first to see if conciliation is possible before hearing a case.

We want management and employees to come to an agreement that they can both support and abide by. The three years negotiating period is a generous one. There should not be a need for the statutory European works council to be foisted upon any organisation.

Where agreements are in place, business is often concerned about confidentiality. There is a balance to be struck. If meaningful consultation is to take place, important and sometimes sensitive details of the companies' plans and financial position may be discussed. It is important therefore that business is reassured that confidential information will be treated properly. The regulations impose a duty on those involved in the consultation process not to disclose any information provided to them by management on a confidential basis. A breach of that duty could lead to a civil action against the person who leaked the information. But at the same time there is an onus on management not to impose confidentiality where it is unnecessary or to withhold information, the release of which would not cause the organisation serious harm or be prejudicial to it. The employee side may appeal to the CAC if they believe that management are in breach of this obligation and the CAC will judge the matter on a case-by-case basis.

These regulations are an important element in the Government's promotion of partnership and fairness at work alongside competitiveness and adaptability. As far as multinational companies are concerned, 12 decisions taken in one country may affect employees in another.

These regulations give UK employees in large multinationals minimum rights to information and consultation at European level that are sensible for them and sensible for business. I therefore ask this Committee to defeat the motion and approve the regulations.
