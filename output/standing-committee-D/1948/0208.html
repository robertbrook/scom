AGRICULTURAL MARKETING BILL

OFFICIAL REPORT

Resolved:

"That during the proceedings on the Agricultural Marketing Bill the Committee do meet on Thursday and thereafter on Tuesdays and Thursdays at Half-past Ten o'Clock."—[ Mr. T. Williams.]

The Chairman
: Before I call any Amendments, I wish to remind the Committee that the Financial Resolution of 25th January in connection with the Bill is on the Table. I desire to call attention to the fact that adequate notice of Amendments should be given, as moving them without notice causes inconvenience, and I do not intend to call manuscript Amendments.

Clause 1.
—(COMPOSITION OF MARKETING BOARDS.)

10.31 a.m.

Major Sir Thomas Dugdale
: I beg to move, in page 1, line 12, to leave out lines 12 and 13.

The purpose of the Amendment is to remove the upper limit in the composition of the marketing boards. I would refer the Committee to the existing law on the subject, which is governed by Section 14 of the Agricultural Marketing Act, 1933, which appears to be entirely satisfactory. As the Bill before us is drafted, there is no real upper limit, since the Minister may at will increase the number above the 24 stated in the Bill. Hon. Members on this side of the Committee have no objection to the Minister having these powers, but we fail to see why he should be able to increase the number above 24, whereas, in preparing a scheme, a board has not the same privilege.

An example of where it may be necessary to have a number in excess of 24 would be in a very comprehensive scheme, covering a number of different commodities and covering England, Wales and Scotland, and, possibly, Northern Ireland. No useful purpose 4 would seem to be served by including the words which I am proposing to delete while the existing law, under Section 14 of the 1933 Act, is entirely satisfactory. In any event, when any board is preparing its scheme it is necessary to have the size of the board specified in the scheme. Since any scheme must have the approval of Parliament, there appears to be no object in placing an upper limit on the number of members, which is binding on producers, but not on the Minister. I rather expect the Minister's argument to be that, to have a workable body, it is a good thing to have an upper limit in order that it shall be compact. I would answer that by saying that if he is taking powers to increase over 24 the boards should have-equal powers. I think that the Bill would be improved if we omitted these words.

The Minister of Agriculture (Mr. Thomas Williams)
: The hon. and gallant Member for Richmond (Sir T. Dugdale) has put my case, perhaps, better than I could do. The two lines to which he refers act merely as guidance to marketing boards when deciding on the membership of a board. It would be possible for a board to have so many members that it would be unwieldy and could not discharge its business with dispatch. The Lucas Committee recommended that there should be an upper limit in order to obviate that possibility. They suggested a maximum of 16 members. However, on further examination, and in the light of experience over several years, we thought that to adopt the practice of the joint stock companies was perhaps wise, and that we should fix the upper limit at 24.

As the hon. and gallant Member suggests, a board may be dealing with a wide range of products and may need slightly more than 24 members. That would apply particularly if the board covered the whole of the U.K. where representation of regional bodies had to be provided. I hope that the decision of the Government will be approved. It is merely guidance to a marketing board, rather than the laying down of an impracticable proposition. All that the Government were called upon to do was to devise this guidance for a board which was formulating a scheme. I hope that the words providing for a minimum of eight and a maximum of 24, except in JPEG 5 very special circumstances in which the Minister may feel that a number in excess of 24 is necessary, will be retained, and that the hon. and gallant Member will not press the Amendment.

Mr. Turton
: I hope that the Minister will reconsider this matter. This is no party question but, as he said, a question of giving guidance to an agricultural marketing board. The size of the board must have relation to the commodity produced and if we were seeking to formulate a scheme dealing with tomatoes and cucumbers we should not need a very large board. In fact 24 would be an unwieldy number for such a board, but if we were dealing with livestock products a very large board, fully representative of all the branches of the industry, would be required.

Under the Socialist Act of 1931 and under our Act of 1933 that was recognised, and it was felt that Parliament should make the decision when a scheme was put forward. If a board put up a top-heavy superstructure Parliament, acting with the advice of the Minister, would say that the number was too large and must be cut down. I suggest that between now and the Report stage the Minister might look at the question again to see if the guidance contained in the Clause is really necessary. It seems to be putting in a lot of unnecessary words, for the Minister allows himself to agree to a larger figure.

The Lucas Committee were not very wrong when they said that the average number on the board should be 16. If it is permitted to be more than 24, too many boards will think that Parliament considers 24 to be the average and will appoint 24 members and so make the Act unworkable. I am not asking that there should be a snap Division on this, but that the Minister should reconsider the question in view of what has been said by my hon. and gallant Friend the Member for Richmond (Sir T. Dugdale).

Mr. York
: I had hoped that the Minister would address himself to the argument of why it is better for the Minister to come to the decision on the number of members on the board than for the board to come to that decision. When one is considering a large commercial venture like a marketing board 6 it would seem that the board, run on commercial lines, would be far more capable of judging the number of directors—for that is what it amounts to—than the Minister. In refusing to accept the Amendment, I hoped that he would give some reason why he or his Department is more capable of making the decision than is the board. I hope that he will take the advice of my hon. Friend the Member for Thirsk and Malton (Mr. Turton).

Mr. T. Williams
: The hon. Member for Thirsk and Malton (Mr. Turton) knows that I am always willing to consider any point raised in Committee on any Bill for which I am responsible. I do not think it right, proper, or wise for Parliament to interfere, or even for the Minister to interfere, so long as the board do what seems to be the right thing. It may interest hon. Members, particularly the hon. Member for Thirsk and Malton, to know that the tomato and cucumber scheme which has been submitted suggests a board of 30, which he thinks is far too many.

Colonel Gomme-Duncan
: I beg to move in page 1, line 12, after "Minister," to insert:

"or in Scotland the Secretary of State or in Northern Ireland the Home Secretary."

It might be for the convenience of the Committee also to consider at the same time the next Amendment: In page 1, line 17, after "Minister," to insert:

"or in Scotland the Secretary of State."

It is not clear that the reference to the Minister is in fact a reference, in the case of Scotland, to the Secretary of State for Scotland, although it is possible, under Clause 21, where it says that the Act JPEG 7 shall be construed as one with the principal Act, that that definition may cover the point. Even if that is so, we think it should be perfectly clear in the early stages of the Bill, and I hope the Minister will give a categorical assurance that the Secretary of State for Scotland is meant in the case of Scotland so that we might clarify the situation at the beginning of the Bill.

The Joint Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Thomas Fraser)
: The hon. and gallant gentleman has said that there is a later Clause which says that the Bill must be read in conjunction with the principal Act. That is the answer to the query which he has raised. In his Amendment he proposes to add after "Minister":

"or in Scotland the Secretary of State or in Northern Ireland the Home Secretary."

Nowhere in the Bill does it say that the Minister shall, in England, be the Minister of Agriculture and Fisheries. It merely says "the Minister" and the Minister is defined in Section 18 of the Act of 1931. The definition reads:

"in relation to a scheme applicable both in England and Scotland, … the Minister of Agriculture and Fisheries and the Secretary of State for Scotland acting in conjunction,"

and

"in relation to a scheme applicable only in England, … the Minister of Agriculture and Fisheries;"

and

"in relation to a scheme applicable only in Scotland, … the Secretary of State for Scotland."

There is the definition of the Minister and this Bill proceeds to refer to "the Minister" and not to the Minister of Agriculture and Fisheries. I think it is fully understood that it includes the Home Secretary in Northern Ireland and that as regards Scotland, it means the Secretary of State for Scotland. I hope that the hon. and gallant Member will accept that explanation as being adequate and will agree that in the circumstances there is no necessity to accept the Amendment.

Colonel Gomme-Duncan
: In view of the Joint Under-Secretary's assurance and the smile of the Minister—and I trust there will be no hanky-panky—I beg to ask leave to withdraw the Amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Mr. Hurd
: I beg to move, in page 1, line 18, to leave out from "appointment," to the end of line 20.

This Amendment is to delete the reference to particular people and particular categories from whom the Minister has to select his members of the marketing boards. We feel that the Minister should be given an entirely free hand, and that the Clause should simply require him to select people who in his opinion are qualified for appointment. Our view is that if any particular categories are specified in the Bill the Minister will be handicapped in getting the best team.

In selecting a team of this kind the Minister will do well to find people with the same background. I have in mind that some of the nationalised corporations and boards are suffering today because the directors of these bodies have not got the same background but are a hotchpotch collection of people who have been selected because of their eminence in certain spheres of public administration and trade unions. But they have not got a common background which enables them to see the problems before the board in the same light. It may be desirable, for example, to have a housewife on one of the marketing boards. I would suggest to the Minister that if he decides that that is right, and that there is a suitable woman, he would do well to select an experienced member of the Women's Institute movement because she would have the same background as members on the board who have been elected by the producers.

We want the Boards to work together as a team—not only the producers' elected representatives but those whom the Minister has nominated. We do not want wrangles to go on within the board, and there is a real risk of that if the Minister is bound by the Bill to consider particularly these categories of

"commerce, finance, administration, public affairs or the organisation of workers."

He may choose his best people outside those categories and as far as the public interest is concerned the Bill reinforces what the original Act laid down—that there should be public committees of investigation. If the board does go too far in one direction and is perhaps too biased on the producers' side the Minister can take prompt powers to JPEG 9 check them immediately. We are anxious that the Minister should get a real working team and we believe that that will best be done by having people with a common background rather than by picking them here and there to satisfy particular interests which have nothing to do with the problems that the board is tackling.

Mr. Gerald Williams
: In political experience it has always been found dangerous to specify certain classes or certain types of people for fear that some may be left out. The list must be completely exhaustive or the matter must be left to the discretion of the Minister. The Minister must spend time hearing everyone's suggestions as to who they think should be included, so as to be satisfied that the list is exhaustive, or, else, as in our opinion, the list had better be completely omitted. There may be people who are not included in the definition proposed in the Bill. I suggest that a man with important experience of agriculture may well not be included in the definition of having capacity in "commerce, finance, administration, public affairs," etc. That might even be the case in regard to someone who has had great experience on marketing boards in the past. There might be a desire to include a housewife, as has been suggested. Unless everyone is covered by the definition it would be better to leave the Minister free, because he is responsible to Parliament for his actions. In this case we had better leave the choice to him.

The Joint Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture (Mr. George Brown)
: I am surprised that this Amendment has been pressed so hard. To judge by the remarks of the hon. Member for Tonbridge (Mr. G. Williams) we must chose between spending a lot of time on this matter or accepting the Amendment. There is nothing very different in this provision in the Bill from the provision in the 1933 Act which has worked extremely well, and which, as the hon. Member for Thirsk and Malton (Mr. Turton) said, was one of the Acts for which his side of the House could claim responsibility and credit. In that Act, where there were fewer people to be co-opted to the boards, it was laid down that the persons co-opted should be persons who 10 were specially qualified for membership of the boards by reason of their commercial or financial ability. All the classes who are omitted by our definition, as the hon. Member for Tonbridge pointed out, are left out by that former definition.

The question of whether someone well qualified in agriculture is included in the definition is beside the point because the purpose of this provision is to put some members who, in a general way, will be the representatives of or the watch-dogs for the public interest, on the board; those who are well qualified in agriculture are in any case on the Board. Those who have knowledge of marketing schemes are on the board. There might be other people who know about agriculture but they would quite easily be embraced by this definition. The definition is so wide. Those chosen as members must have

"capacity in commerce, finance, administration, public affairs or the organisation of workers."

To take the case of an experienced member of the Women's Institute, if she is not engaged in public affairs in her rural area I do not know who is. We are making heavy weather of something that will not get us into difficulty and which even in a narrower field has not got us into difficulty up to now.

The point about Members having the same background could be a most retrograde argument to develop in this Twentieth Century. For example, the mixing up here of Members whose backgrounds are not the same does not mean that we cannot co-operate as a team and bring our different kinds of experience to bear, to the great improvement of the final product. It seems to the Minister that the real point about this definition is that we are to put on these boards a small number of people with special experience to safeguard the public interest. It should be the kind of experience that will make them useful either because they are engaged in fields related to those on which the Board is making an impact, or have shown capacity in those fields. We feel that, having regard to the work to be done by these boards, very broad categories should be laid down, and I hope that the Amendment will be withdrawn.

Mr. T. Williams

Subsection (5) applies where the Minister, having made an order under Subsection (2), wishes for one reason or another to replace it wholly or partly by another order under Subsection (2). Subsection (5) gives the necessary power to revoke or vary the first order by the second one. This second order, because it is an order made under the powers in Subsection (2), has to go through all the procedure of Subsections (3) and (4) before the Minister can make it. I hope that is transparently clear.

Subsection (6) applies where the Minister wishes merely to withdraw the whole or part of a direction given by order under Subsection (2). If he proposes to withdraw the whole he may revoke the order. If he proposes to withdraw only part he may vary the order so far as is necessary in order to withdraw that part. There is no need for such an order to go through the procedure of Subsections (3) and (4).
