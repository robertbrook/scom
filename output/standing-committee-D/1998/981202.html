Draft Double Taxation Relief (Taxes on Income) (Ireland) Order 1998

4.30 pm

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Dawn Primarolo)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Double Taxation Relief (Taxes on Income) (Ireland) Order 1998.

Good afternoon, Mr. Jones. I am pleased to introduce the order, which deals with a protocol that updates an existing treaty with Ireland.

Before I describe the treaty provisions, I hope that you will excuse me for congratulating the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton (Mr. Gibb) on his promotion to the Front Bench. This is the first Committee on double taxation in which he has led for the Opposition. I look forward to seeing him when we consider other double taxation treaties and when we debate the Finance Bill in the House. He has been assiduous in financial matters, and I am sure that he will continue to account admirably for himself, although you will excuse me for saying that I hope he does not do too admirably when he faces me.

Like our predecessors, the Government are committed to maintaining and, when circumstances permit, extending the United Kingdom network of double taxation agreements. Such agreements have enjoyed cross-party support for some time. The previous Government presided over the 100th treaty. It is not politically controversial to say that our double taxation treaties have been and will be very successful, and I hope that they continue to enjoy cross-party support.

The business community, too, welcomes the agreements because they reduce tax-related barriers to international trade and investment and provide certainty of treatment in several ways. First, they reduce withholding tax on investment flows; secondly, they allocate taxing rights and thus prevent double taxation; and, thirdly, they provide a mechanism for tax authorities to resolve difficult cases.

The protocol with Ireland will, if approved, amend the double taxation convention between our two countries, which was signed in 1976 and which has been amended by two previous protocols. The convention generally follows the model text that was established by the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development, which was in use when the convention was signed.

The protocols make several changes to deal with the practical problems of operating some of the convention's provisions and to take account of changes in the two countries' tax systems. They cover five main areas.

4 First, the present protocol provides for a building site or a construction or installation project to be regarded as a permanent establishment only if it exists for more than six months. Previously, the convention contained no such provision and a building site or construction project could constitute a permanent establishment from the outset. Thus, a UK-resident firm working on a construction project in Ireland could be liable to Irish tax on its profits even if the project lasted less than six months. That has caused UK firms problems in practice. The new rule will benefit UK contractors who seek to do business in Ireland at a time when the building industry there is booming. It will also give them greater certainty about their tax position in Ireland.

The second protocol removes the payment of tax credits on dividends that go from one country to residents of another country. Ireland is abolishing tax credits on dividends next April, so UK residents would in any event receive no Irish tax credits on Irish dividends from next April. Dividends from one country to charities and pension funds that are resident in another will be exempt from tax on dividends in the source country.

The third protocol introduces tighter anti-avoidance provisions, aimed at countering arrangements intended to take advantage of the convention. Such an arrangement could consist of routing interest from the UK through Ireland to a third country to take advantage of the relief from UK tax under the convention.

Fourthly, the Government have used the order to bring capital gains rules closer to those in the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development model text, and to permit the tax charge on gains made by people who left the UK for temporary residence abroad.

Fifthly, the protocol also brings rules for Government service remuneration and pensions closer to the OECD model. It deals specifically with a problem relating to the remuneration of teachers' pensions. Difficulties have occurred because of diverging decisions made by UK special commissioners and by the Irish High Court in appeal cases. Those decisions have widened the definition of Government service in the UK while narrowing the same definition in Ireland. Without an alteration to the convention, some teachers' remunerations would be exempt from tax in both the UK and Ireland, while former teachers' pensions would be taxed without relief in both countries.

The protocol has been negotiated at a time of increasingly close co-operation between the UK and Ireland, which is an important trading partner as well as a close neighbour. The protocol will help to ensure that the double taxation convention continues to encourage trade and investment between the two countries.

I commend the order to the Committee, and I shall be happy to answer questions.

JPEG 5 Double taxation treaties have genuine cross-party support. Often, such support spells impending doom, but the case for Britain's entry into bilateral treaties with other countries around the world to prevent different tax jurisdictions from taxing the same profit or income twice is unassailable. The prevention of double taxation encourages cross-border trade, and Britain has always been an international trading nation.

The economic theory of comparative advantage has been an economic reality for Britain that has generated real wealth. That is why Britain has the largest network of double taxation treaties in the world, as the Financial Secretary said. We have treaties with more than 100 countries. As the hon. Lady pointed out, that figure was reached under the previous Government.

Ireland is Britain's fifth largest trading partner. It is therefore vital that the double taxation treaty between the UK and Ireland is kept up to date. There is no question of the Opposition wanting to break cross-party consensus on the matter. We have serious concerns, however, about the Government's handling of treaty negotiations and about their nurturing of Britain's very valuable network of double taxation treaties and conventions.

Last July, the Government introduced legislation to end repayment of the tax credit on dividends to non-UK taxpayers and to pension funds. They also halved the tax credit by reducing it from 20 per cent, to 10 per cent. That change has profound consequences for double taxation treaties, as many of them contain provisions that enable overseas investors to reclaim the tax credit from the Inland Revenue when they receive dividends from UK companies. The double taxation treaty between the UK and Ireland enables residents of the Republic of Ireland to reclaim the credit on their dividends from UK companies, less a 15 per cent, withholding tax. When the credit was 20 per cent, it enabled a repayment—albeit a small one—to be made. But when the credit falls to 10 per cent, next year, it will be wiped out by the 15 per cent, withholding tax.

The Government did not simply abolish the tax credit in last July's Budget because doing so might have required a huge number of our double taxation treaties to be renegotiated, so reducing the figure to 10 per cent, was, they thought, a clever wheeze to save money in repayment without having to renegotiate all the treaties. However, that annoyed some of our treaty partners. Last July, we expressed concerns that halving the credit in such a way might threaten our treaty with the United States. The Paymaster General assured us that his Department had had discussions with the United States Government and that no retaliatory measures would be taken. On 1 October this year, the Treasury announced that the United Kingdom-United States double tax treaty is to be renegotiated.

We now have before us an amendment to the United Kingdom-Republic of Ireland treaty that has been necessitated by two principal reasons: the change to the dividend tax credit, and the change to the permanent establishment article because of recent tax cases in the United Kingdom and Ireland over teachers' pensions.

I understand that unless the treaty passes all the legislative procedures during this calendar year, it will not be in force by 1 April next year. If it is not in force by 6 then, a huge loophole will be created when the new lower tax credit comes in next year. Irish pension funds and charities will be able to continue to reclaim tax credits without any withholding, leading to the development of a host of Irish-based investment products designed to exploit the loophole.

The loophole arises because in the former treaty, charities and pension funds could reclaim the tax credit without any withholding. So reducing the credit to 10 per cent, would not render that repayment redundant, as it would in all other circumstances in which a 15 per cent, withholding tax exists. The loophole was created by the Government in their tax credit reforms; no such loophole existed before July 1997. We have another example of how Labour's first Budget has impacted on our double tax treaty network, requiring renegotiation.

Will the Financial Secretary answer these questions? First, will she confirm that unless the treaty is in place by the end of the year, a tax loophole will exist next year caused by the halving of the tax credit? Secondly, given that treaty renegotiations are, by necessity, carried out only once a generation per country, why has the treaty not adopted more extensively the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development model, which is what usually happens when treaties are renegotiated? Why, for example, is there no independent personal services article? Is it because of the rush—the need to get the treaty on to the books by the end of the year?

I ask my third question more out of curiosity than in a spirit of barbed criticism. Why is this protocol between the United Kingdom and the Government of Ireland, whereas the original treaty is between the United Kingdom and the Republic of Ireland? Why is there a difference in wording? Has Ireland ceased to be a republic in the mean time, pending its inclusion in the Commonwealth?

Fourthly, can the Minister explain why, in article 1 of the protocol, the new article 5(5) differs from the previous article 5(5) in the original treaty? Why will a building, construction or installation project that lasts for more than six months be a permanent establishment, when the period usually provided for in double tax treaties is 12 months?

Fifthly, in article 2 of the protocol, can the Minister explain why paragraph 5 of article 11 has been removed? Sixthly, I refer to article III of the protocol, which is the old version of article 12—the interest article. The former article 12(5) of the treaty said that the treaty would not apply if the debt was created "mainly for the purposes" of using the treaty as a standard anti-avoidance provision. How does the new wording of article 12(5) change its meaning and why has it been changed?

Seventhly, will the hon. Lady define the term "gains" in article 4 of the protocol, which relates to capital gains tax? How will it be distinguished from the phrase "capital gains", which is used in the old treaty? If there is no difference between the two terms, why has the change been made? Where is the word "gains" defined in UK tax legislation?

10 The hon. Gentleman asked why the treaty mentions "Ireland", not "Republic of Ireland", as defined in the convention. I refer him to the Inland Revenue press release of 9 November and remind him that the practice of referring to "Ireland" is in line with the Belfast agreement. The term "Ireland" is used in the protocol, and has the same meaning as "Republic of Ireland" in the convention. The treaty thus reflects changing circumstances.

The hon. Gentleman asked several questions about articles and the way in which they related to Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development provisions. I shall go through the articles in numerical order, which was not quite the order in which the hon. Gentleman made his points, but is easier for me.

Article I deals with permanent establishment. Under OECD provisions, a building site, construction or installation project constitutes a permanent establishment only if it lasts longer than 12 months. As the hon. Gentleman said, and as I pointed out in my opening remarks, the protocol provides for a building site, construction or installation project to be a permanent establishment if it lasts longer than six months.

Currently, there is no time limit on arrangements between Ireland and the United Kingdom. Both countries agreed that six months was a reasonable starting point. From a United Kingdom point of view, that is a considerable improvement.

The hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon asked about gains and losses. He knows that we cannot put a figure on how much we gain or lose—I hope that we do not lose—in the negotiations. We are trying to ensure that we facilitate trade, and we are obviously in a strong negotiating position—the hon. Gentleman referred to the fact that Ireland is our fifth largest trading partner. It is important that treaties facilitate that position; this treaty does, and is acceptable and agreeable from our point of view. The hon. Gentleman will forgive me for saying that I cannot answer such questions—that is not because I am being difficult, that is because of the nature of the negotiations, and is always the case with double taxation treaties.

The hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton then asked about article II and dividends. A direct investor is defined by the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development as a company that holds 25 per cent, of another company's capital; the protocol defines the proportion as 10 per cent. Our view is that it is more realistic to make a distinction between direct and portfolio investment by reference to voting powers than by reference to share ownership, because that more accurately reflects investments. That is why there is a slight divergence, which the hon. Gentleman pointed out. The OECD model gives us an acceptable pattern, but there are variations on it. Of course, it is right that we justify such variations.

The hon. Gentleman than asked about article III and interest. There are no equivalent provisions to those of the OECD. The article is an anti-abuse measure designed to prevent artificial arrangements which exist mainly to obtain the benefits of the convention. He then asked about article V and Government services. He asked why article 18(3) refers only to educational institutions. The reason is JPEG 11 that it is important to deal with the specific circumstances of teachers. I agree with the hon. Gentleman that other groups are affected by the problem, but it is much less widespread among those other groups, and the negotiations concluded on that basis.

Dawn Primarolo
: If the hon. Gentleman will forgive me, I shall give way to him before I finish, but not now. If I respond to the substantive points first, that will make it much easier to deal with what are complicated issues.

The hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton then asked what he said would be his final question, although he went on to ask two more. He asked why income from estates of deceased persons has been excluded from the taxation of income not expressly mentioned. Both the United Kingdom and Ireland have reserved rights in respect of the article with the OECD to tax income paid to our respective residents from the estates of deceased persons in the course of administration. The reason for that is that payments would lose the original identity of income on entering the estate and in effect become a new source of income. Without the special wording, the sole taxing rights would rest with our treaty partners, which would lead to double taxation.

The hon. Gentleman then asked about the capital gains article. The text is not in the OECD model, but the OECD commentary recognises that countries may want to include such a provision. The UK seeks to include that text in double taxation conventions because it ensures, for example, that taxing rights remain in the country where immovable property is situated, even if the gain relates to shares in companies whose assets consist mainly of land. Without that reference, it would be easy to avoid the charge of UK capital gains tax.
