The Minister of State, Department of Social Security (Mr. Stephen Timms): Today we come to the part of the Bill that deals with pension sharing on divorce. Clause 15 gives effect to schedule 3, which amends part II of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973. The clause gives the courts in England and Wales the power to make pension-sharing orders on proceedings for divorce or nullity of marriage. Pension sharing by agreement is dealt with by a separate route under clause 20.

We have already referred to the extensive dicussions on this clause and on the clauses that follow. In June 1998, the Government published a draft Bill, on which the Social Security Select Committee took substantial evidence—it published its report in October. The hon. Member for Gainsborough (Mr. Leigh) and my hon. Friend the Member for Gravesham (Mr. Pond)—and for some of the time the hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs (Mr. Flight)—were involved in the work of the Social Security Select Committee on this matter.

By common consent, the Select Committee did an excellent job. The Government responded to its report in January, the Bill was published in February and the Select Committee published a further report last week, commenting on the changes that have been introduced into the Bill. The report said:

"Pre-legislative scrutiny by departmental select committees is only one of the strands of modernising the House of Commons being piloted during this Parliament. The Government has benefited from prior exposure of its proposals and has responded to technical points raised by interested parties. Much has been done to improve the transparency of the process by which interested parties are consulted in the formulation of Government legislation."

Our task has been made much easier by the work of the Select Committee, to which I pay tribute.

The provisions in schedule 3 will allow pension rights to be treated like other assets when a couple divorce or if a marriage is annulled. They will allow a proportion or all of the value of the pension to be Col. 300 transferred from one spouse to the other as part of the overall financial settlement.

Pension sharing will increase the choice available to divorcing couples and give greater flexibility to the courts when they are deciding the fairest division of material assets. It will promote certainty and clarity for people who are going through divorce and make it easier for them to achieve financial independence through a clean break.

Pension sharing will not be compulsory. Couples can still earmark and offset pensions against other assets when those options are more appropriate. Pension sharing is not intended to be of particular benefit to either women or men. In practice, however, it is likely that it will help more women than men because more men will have pensions to be shared.

We do not underestimate the complexity of pension sharing. The detailed provisions in the Bill will be supported by clear rules and regulations to ensure that the process works successfully.

There are five main points to make about the schedule. First, it places pension sharing in England and Wales within the framework of the Family Law Act 1996. Couples will be able to apply to pension share as soon as the court receives the statement of marital breakdown. The court may make an order before the divorce, but the pension-sharing process will take effect only after the divorce.

Secondly, there will be a stay period, during which no action should be taken on the order. Either party can then appeal the order. The pension-sharing order will be stayed until the end of the appropriate period for giving notice of appeal. Pension-sharing orders will not, therefore, become effective until the divorce order has been made and the stay period has expired.

Thirdly, although it will be possible to appeal against pension-sharing orders up to and beyond the point of implementation, the court will not be able to set aside or vary an order if that would adversely affect the pension scheme. The court will be able to make further orders as it thinks fit to put the parties in the position that it considers appropriate.

Fourthly, the couple must meet the pension scheme's administrative charges that arise from pension sharing. If the couple are ordered to share, the court can apportion the administrative expenses that arise from sharing between the couple in a way that is appropriate. If the court chooses not to exercise that power, the member spouse will be expected to pay the charges. It is important that the charges do not fall on anyone else, such as other members of the scheme, the Government or the taxpayer.

Finally, the Bill restricts the combination of earmarking and pension-sharing orders and the making of multiple pension-sharing orders in relation to any given pension. That is important for several reasons. We want to encourage clean-break agreements. We want to be fair to both parties—former spouses should Image Col. 301 not have several bites of the cherry and the scheme member's remaining pension should be safe.

We also need to protect the position of previous spouses, as illustrated by the case of a man and his second wife who are obtaining a divorce. When the man divorced his first wife, she may have been granted an earmarking order. If the second wife were then granted a pension share, the husband's pension fund would be reduced, which in turn would reduce the first wife's retirement income. We need to protect against that.

The provisions in the schedule are designed to ensure that pension sharing is processed efficiently through the courts, can be delivered effectively by the pension schemes and meets the requirements of the couple who are divorcing or whose marriage has been annulled.
