4.32 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Northern Ireland (Mr. John McFall)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Water (Northern Ireland) Order 1999.

The order deals with two main areas: water resources and cleanliness, and water recreation and navigation. It will replace the Water Act (Northern Ireland) 1972 and will also amend the river bye-law making powers in the Laganside Development (Northern Ireland) Order 1989.

Part II of the order deals with the powers of the Department of the Environment for Northern Ireland to promote the conservation of water resources and the cleanliness of waterways and underground strata. Those powers have been available since 1972, and are now being revised to take account of changes in Government policies, increased public environmental awareness, scientific advances and EC directive requirements.

4 The order will introduce a number of new provisions: first, the power to charge fees for discharge consents and abstraction licences; secondly, an improved system for processing applications for discharge consents including powers to hold public inquiries; and thirdly, the strengthening of pollution prevention measures.

The powers of the Department of the Environment to combat water pollution will be extended by article 12 to enable enforcement notices to be served. These can specify the remedial action to be taken by a person holding a discharge consent to ensure compliance with consent conditions. At present, a discharger can be prosecuted in the courts for failure to meet consent conditions but the Department has no power to require particular improvements to be carried out. Article 14 extends the Department's powers to make regulations to prevent water pollution by, for example, agriculture or industry.

In view of Northern Ireland's ample natural supplies of water, it has not been considered necessary to use the powers in the 1972 Act to make water abstraction regulations. The need for such regulations is currently under review. Further consultations would be carried out on any proposals for abstraction or impounding regulations. The opportunity has been taken to revise the existing powers, and article 20 provides for the control of impounding works as well as water abstraction. It also provides for local inquiries and appeals and for regulations to contain provisions for the protection of existing rights or interests.

Article 17 contains new powers for the Department to serve notices for anti-pollution works and operations. Such a notice would be served on a person who is responsible for a water pollution incident and would require works to be carried out to remove the polluting matter or to mitigate the effects of the pollution. An anti-pollution works notice could also be served to require measures to prevent polluting matter entering a waterway or underground strata. Article 19 provides for a person served with a works notice to have access to land in order to carry out the anti-pollution measures.

The Department of the Environment maintains a public register of discharge consents, but article 30 requires the information on the register to be extended to include details of the classification and the quality of waterways, applications for discharge consents and the associated decisions, the results of the analyses of effluent samples, the steps taken in consequence, enforcement notices and works notices.

The order recognises an increasing demand for water-based tourism from local residents and foreign visitors attracted by the cruising, angling and recreational possibilities on Northern Ireland's waterways. Under the Water Act 1972 the Department of Agriculture's powers to promote the recreational use of waterways are limited to minor works, but under articles 41 to 45 the Department will be able to carry out a wider range of projects to improve and restore waterways for recreational and navigational use. Article 48 provides powers to acquire land by JPEG 5 agreement or vesting. Such works would be subject, to the availability of finances. That will be a big issue in view of the major capital costs of canal regeneration.

Article 46 enables the Department to enter into arrangements with other bodies or persons who may be able to draw on private finance or grant from sources such as the national lottery or European Community programmes. To meet EC obligations in the case of an inland waterway restoration project, there will be a requirement to undertake an environmental assessment of the effects of the restoration.

Under article 47, the Department will be able to cooperate with authorities in the Republic of Ireland on a waterway scheme. The powers are in line with those available under the drainage order in relation to Lough Erne, allowing the successful joint restoration of the Shannon-Erne waterway in 1994.

With the expansion of the demand for water-based recreation, there is a need for better regulation to provide for the safety of users and to reconcile potentially conflicting activities. Article 49 extends the Department's powers to regulate the recreational and navigational use of waterways. To meet the cost of providing an improve service on waterways, article 50 will enable charges to be levied for the use of facilities and the licensing of craft.

The order will contribute to the Government's sustainable development policies by strengthening powers to combat water pollution and conserve water resources. It will promote the recreational use of canals and other waterways. The order will facilitate the transfer of certain functions to the new Northern Ireland Department of Culture, Arts and Leisure and the cross-border inland waterways implementation body.

I commend the order to the Committee.

The order does, however, beef up the enforcement section, which I will cover later, and part III gives new powers to the Department of Agriculture—at least I think that they are new powers; schedule 8 does not appear to repeal legislation in relation to part III. Does the order create new legislation relating to waterways, canals and their use, especially for recreational purposes, or transfer a power or responsibility from another Department of State in Northern Ireland to the Department of Agiculture?

Turning to specific matters, article 4 of part I refers to the functions of the Department of the Environment. That also covers the duties of the Environment and Heritage Service which, as a non-departmental public body, is part of the Department's responsibility. The first duty set out in article 4 is to promote conservation and cleanliness of water resources. It says:

"The Department shall … promote the conservation of water resources of Northern Ireland"

and

"promote the cleanliness of water in waterways and underground strata."

Paragraph (2) states that the Department will

"have regard to … the protection of public health".

That brings us to the difficult problem of the relationship between the Department of the Environment as the overarching body and the two non-departmental public bodies, the Environment and Heritage Service and the Water Service.

It is the responsibility of the Water Service to provide drinking water of a quality that matches the standard set in European legislation and to discharge effluence from its sewage treatment plants, which also have to meet stringent European standards. It is also responsible for water quality in the sea off the shores of Northern Ireland and, in particular, off the main bathing beaches. The Water Service is, therefore, responsible for public health through the quality of the water that it provides, but its responsibilities overlap with those of another part of the Department. The Minister should clarify the demarcation between the duties of the Environment and Heritage Service and those of the Water Service. What legislation covers the quality of drinking water in pipes and taps? The order JPEG 7 does not seem to cover that, but the Department of the Environment in Northern Ireland is responsible for the cleanliness of water and its public health aspects.

Mr. Fabricant
: When a friend of mine, Peter Lacey, became head of the health service for the state of Queensland, Australia and there was some minor poisoning of the water due to local flooding in northwest Queensland, he decided to determine what regulations covered water quality. He discovered that they went back to the 19th century and simply required the water supply to be potable. Can the Minister say what regulations cover Northern Ireland and confirm that there are no strange anomalies whereby Northern Ireland, unlike the remainder of the United Kingdom, has more clearly defined water regulations, just as Queensland's water regulations were non-existent compared with those of New South Wales?

Mr. Moss
: I am not tempted to go to Queensland, but the point follows on from what I was saying. Can the Minister clearly demarcate between the responsibilities of the Water Service and those of the Environment and Heritage Service in relation to quality, not just of drinking water through the taps, but concerning the public health liability?

"The Department may, in relation to any description of waters … by regulations prescribe a system of classifying the quality of those waters".

That is fine. We all hope and expect that the Department has stringent objectives for water quality, but the order does not state whether those requirements and regulations will have some commonality with those pertaining to the remainder of the United Kingdom. It would be unacceptable if Northern Ireland had different standards from those pertaining to water quality in Scotland, England and Wales. The order does not state that those standards should comply also with European Union regulations.

Article 7 deals with the pollution of water courses. That will be policed by the Environment and Heritage Service. Some of the pollution is from effluent discharge from the sewerage plants operated by the Water Service. We have already established that that comes under another section of the Department of the Environment. I know from experience that there were examples—not numerous but more than I cared to have to own up to when I was a Minister—of water services being the main culprit in polluting water courses, which another section within the same Department was supposed to be policing. How can a Department act as poacher and gamekeeper on water quality?

That was alluded to in the paper on water and sewerage services in Northern Ireland, which was published by Lord Dubs, the Under-Secretary of State with responsiblity for the environment in Northern Ireland last November. He referred in his opening comments to the problem of the Department having to face both ways. One section of the Department is responsible for sewerage effluent, which sometimes causes severe pollution and large fish kills. Let us not 8 beat about the bush, there have been some serious incidents. Yet the same Department, wearing a different hat, is supposed not only to police but to enforce water quality. I presume that water services are still under the remit and control of the Department of the Environment and that, if devolution comes about and the Department is reorganised, the Water Service will remain within its remit. Perhaps the Minister will confirm or deny that. Has one side of the Department ever taken the other side to court, or does Crown immunity apply in those circumstances? If there is Crown immunity, how long does the Minister believe that it should remain, given the seriousness of the incidents?

Article 11 deals with the power to make a scheme of charges, particularly in relation to discharge consents. Article 11(1) says:

"the Department may require the payment to it of such charges as may be specified".

Therefore, the Department is introducing charges for discharge consents. I presume that schemes for payment already exist. If not, I hope that the Minister will clarify that. Again, is there parity with the rest of Great Britain in regard to the payment of charges for discharge consent?

Article 12 deals with enforcement notices, to which the Minister referred in more detail in his opening remarks. It has been a bone of contention over the years that, even when perpetrators of pollution were identified, the Government did not seem to have the teeth either to enforce convictions or to extract a reasonable penalty. I think that the Minister said that the Government perceived that to be the case and that article 12 is not only new, but provides much more powerful, beefed-up enforcement than existed in the past. I believe that to be the case, but I should like the Minister to confirm it. Certainly under article 12(3)(a) a conviction on indictment could be

"imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 years or to a fine or to both".

Perhaps, for the first time, there are serious enforcement provisions in the legislation.

Article 16, which deals with anti-pollution works and operations, gives the Department power to carry out any works that it deems necessary in relation to problems associated with pollution—disposing of matter polluting water courses and the rest of it. Article 16(1) says:

"the Department shall be entitled to carry out investigations for the purpose of establishing the source of the matter and the identity of the person who has caused or permitted, whether knowingly or otherwise, the matter to be present in relevant waters".

Who will conduct those investigations? Only when I read the consultation paper did I realise that in many cases the Water Service is doing the monitoring on behalf of the Environment and Heritage Service. I am not saying that the waters are muddy, but the responsibilities of one part of the service certainly overlap with those of another.

Will the Minister confirm that monitoring and, often, investigation of pollution is carried out by the Water Service? At what stage, if at all, is an JPEG 9 independent body called in? In the past, concerns have been publicly expressed that matters arising from certain investigations were not examined in sufficient detail. The words, "cover up" were used by some to indicate that they were less than happy with the outcome of certain reports. I do not believe that either branch of the Department has ever shortchanged society in Northern Ireland by terminating or improperly conducting investigations. However, it is not surprising that the public sometimes question the veracity of the findings, given that two branches of the same Department are involved.

Article 17 deals with

"Notices requiring persons to carry out anti-pollution works and operations",

and will enable the Department to serve a works notice on a person or persons who cause pollution. Is the provision new, or have the works notices to which paragraph (2) refers been issued in the past? If so, how frequently have they been issued?

Article 20, which deals with the abstraction and impounding of water, again impinges on the responsibility of the Water Service, which provides drinking water and constructs and operates dams. The matter is covered in article 20(1)(b), which refers to "impounding works". Impounding works for which the Water Service is responsible will therefore be regulated by the Environment and Heritage service.

Article 20(2) states:

"Regulations under paragraph (1) shall not apply to a person who … holds a licence under Article 10(1)(a) of the Electricity (Northern Ireland) Order 1992".

Will the order, or some other legislation, require the Water Service to hold a licence before it can abstract water from rivers or, for example, from Lough Neagh, where a huge abstraction serves many consumers in Northern Ireland? I suspect that, as a Crown body, the Water Service will not need such a licence. Will the Minister confirm that?

Part III, which is entitled

"Powers of the Department of Agriculture in Relation to Waterways",

deals with what I believe are new powers granted to the Department of Agriculture. Article 2(2) states that a waterway

"includes any river, stream, watercourse, inland water … or tidal waters and any channel or passage of whatever kind … through which water flows".

A number of exceptions are then given. I thought that that paragraph gave the Department of Agriculture powers over canals and man-made waterways. However, the definition also includes rivers, streams and water courses. What happens when a conflict of interest arises? Article 41 gives the Department the power to

"execute and maintain such works, including the dredging and maintenance of channels and the provision and maintenance of aids to navigation".

I can understand why that power has been given for waterways such as canals, which have recreational uses involving boats, but how does it apply to rivers that are subject to conflicts of interest between water services that extract from the rivers, local farmers who have abstraction rights for irrigation purposes—given 10 Northern Ireland's rainfall, I cannot believe that there are many—and the rights of riparian users such as fishermen?

The order is not specific about arbitration in relation to conflicts or interest. Problems might arise where a Department has powers to dredge and clear channels for boats—which is to be applauded in certain situations—and other users have completely different interests. Will the Minister clarify what arbitration would occur in that situation?
