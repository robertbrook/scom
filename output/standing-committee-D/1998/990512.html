Education (Education Standards Etc. Grants) (England) Regulations 1999

4.30 pm

Mrs. Eleanor Laing
(Epping Forest)
: I beg to move,

"That the Committee has considered the Education (Education Standards Etc. Grants) (England) Regulations 1999 (S.I. 1999, No. 606).

 I begin by sending Committee members the apologies of my hon. Friend the Member for Maidenhead (Mrs. May), who normally leads for the Opposition in the many Committees that consider education matters. She is unavoidably engaged elsewhere this afternoon, and is very sorry not to be here.

I gather that there is a general consensus among Committee members that brevity is of the essence this afternoon, [HON. MEMBERS: "Hear, hear."] I am glad that I have the agreement of Labour Members. My speech will not take long because I have only a few, albeit important, points to make, and I am sure that the Under-Secretary will answer my questions equally briefly.

In relation to the regulations, Opposition Members are concerned about the fact that a considerable sum is held by the Government in the Department for Education and Employment and distributed to local education authorities and schools. The Government use their own priorities rather than those of LEAs and schools. They may use that system intentionally—they are entitled to take a view and to carry out their policies accordingly. However, that system is inconsistent with the constant calls that we hear for devolution and for giving power to the people or institutions at, as it were, the grass roots rather than the centre. If decentralisation is the aim, why are the Government continuing, in these regulations and in others, to place power and finance at the centre?

When local management of schools was first introduced some years ago, financial autonomy was devolved to head teachers and away from LEAs. I well remember that the considerable sums that were saved could be spent directly on education at the chalk face rather than on administration. All those who were involved, especially those at the local level, regarded that policy as being correct.

Schedule 8(1) give carte blance to the Secretary of State to "determine further conditions" in relation to future payments. Given that the regulations already contain many conditions, why was that power felt to be necessary? The Secretary of State can use that power if he wishes. Will an appropriate amendment be 4 considered in the House or in a Standing Committee? Will the power involve democratic input in any way, or will decisions be rubber-stamped by the Department?

The regulations list 68 categories of expenditure—a large number—which will be covered by standards fund bids and which will be decided by the Government, who will be able to establish their own priorities. Is there any mechanism by which the Government can listen to local authorities or head teachers when deciding what further priorities there should be?

A further concern is the bidding process and the amount of money that is being distributed from the centre through that process. The standards fund money must all be bid for by local educaiton authorities, if my reading of clause 6(1) is correct. I am sure that the Minister will tell me if that is not so. Such a bidding process is time consuming and expensive. It is in the best interests of the children to whom we are trying to give the best possible education to spend so much time, effort and money on the bidding process, rather than giving autonomy of expenditure to the school itself?

I know, from speaking to head teachers in my constituency, how much they are able to achieve when given the ability to respond to changing circumstances and to deal with crises and problems that arise in their own school and their own area. When they have the financial ability to deal with those problems, they can do it quickly and well. However, if they have to go through the whole bidding process, it will take financial resources away from the chalk face, and also take up a great deal of time. Sometimes, problems arise that have to be dealt with quickly—particularly in special needs cases. It makes sense to give autonomy to the head teacher to deal with such problems.

A written answer in Hansard on 22 April 1999 at column 682 makes it clear that of a total 8,714 bids—not just standards fund bids, but bids generally—received by the Department for Education and Employment since November 1998, only 2,998 have been successful. I am not complaining about the ratio of bids to success—that is probably fair enough. However, I question the amount of time, money and other resources that went into those unsuccessful bids, of which there were more than 5,000. Those schools or LEAs thought that they had a reasonable case for bidding for the extra resources that they needed. Not only were they unsuccessful in obtaining them, but they wasted time, money and effort in the process.

I have two other matters to address concerning some anomalies in the regulations. It appears that under schedule 1(2)(a), if compared with schedule 1(2)(b), there will be a 100 per cent. grant for training someone to become a head teacher, but only a 50 per cent. grant for training a head teacher in post. I am sure that there is a reason why the regulations have been drafted in this way—we should like to know what it is. It seems rather strange. There is also an anomaly in schedule 1(3)(a), when compared with schedule 1(3)(b), in that there is once again a 100 per cent. grant for an advanced skills teacher in a specialist school, whereas an AST in a non-specialist school attracts only a JPEG 5 50 per cent. grant. It has recently been reported that schools in the north-east are finding it difficult to afford ASTs at all. If ASTs are a key plank in the Government's policy, why are there not 100 per cent. grants for them throughout?

I promised to be brief. I have stated our general objection to the regulations, which is that they represent more money down the drain into bureaucracy, instead of going directly to where it helps teachers and their pupils. It is a pity that the Government have decided to create yet more piles of paper. I worry about the number of regulations that pass over the desks of head teachers and other teachers, who should be spending their time getting on with teaching, instead of dealing with more and more bureaucracy.

Mrs. Laing
: I want to make it clear that all Conservative Members support good education standards. We always aimed for such standards while in government, and we continue to do so. We are trying to help the Government to do the best for every child in this country.

Mr. Clarke
: I am delighted that the hon. Lady rose to the fly that I cast on the surface. The Conservative party's verbal commitment to education standards is clear, but the issue is supporting additional resources to make that commitment real. That is what the regulations before the Committee are all about.

The hon. Member for Epping Forest began by asking whether money was being distributed according to Government priorities. We plead guilty to that. We have a rang of priorities, which are set out in schedule 1. The hon. Member for Tiverton and Honiton (Mrs. Browning) raised some of them. They include the numeracy strategy, the literacy strategy, early excellence centres, advanced skills teachers, family literacy, family numeracy, special education needs, the national grid for learning, ethnic minority JPEG 11 achievement and music services. We unashamedly believe that we need to put resources and money into those areas, and that the purpose is to raise education standards. I do not apologise one iota for that. We were elected on the slogan of "education, education, education", and it is right that we are living up to it.

The spending is additional to the regular spending that comes through the standard spending assessment settlement and the regular settlement. It will enable schools and local authorities to devote extra resources to the tasks set out in schedule 1. Some might argue that resources should not be so devoted, that all the money should go through the local education authority system and that nothing should be added to the standards fund. I disagree with that. I could point to the precedent of the previous Government, who also used direct funding, but we need to leave such petty points aside. The Government have priorities and means of achieving them, which are set out openly, directly, fully and clearly to enable proper debate and discussion.

I shall go through the points made by the hon. Member for Epping Forest one by one. Regulation 8(1) states:

"The Secretary of State may from time to time determine further conditions on the fulfilment of which the making of any payment under these Regulations shall be dependent."

 First, that condition was included in the previous recommendations under the previous Government, but leaving that aside, I want to give a couple of examples that might help the hon. Lady. Yesterday, in Committee, we were talking about the issue raised by the hon. Member for Harrogate and Knaresborough of informing school governors and head teachers about their standards fund allocation. No doubt, I shall read tomorrow that it was a Liberal Democrat game. However, we are actively considering ways of ensuring that the full standards fund allocation for each school is available to the governors and head of the school before the financial year starts, so that they are able to plan their budgets. I accept the point that many people have made about the need for schools to have the information early enough to be able to plan their budgets.

The hon. Member for Epping Forest asked what conditions we might impose. Let me give her an example. We might want to impose a condition on a local education authority to make information and money available to schools so that the position was clear. The power under which we would impose such a condition is set out in regulation 8(1). To give a further example, we have allocated substantial resources to the national grid for learning and we are actively considering several specific conditions to ensure that there is an open-access system throughout the country, so that cartels are not created in certain areas. Those are the sorts of matters that will arise as the process continues. The power in regulation 8(1)—which is similar to the powers in previous legislation—is important in order to ensure that money is well spent. It has not nefarious purpose.

Some might say that it is somehow wrong for the Government to require an LEA that is making a 50 per cent. contribution to a particular area to inform schools in its authority, by a particular date, how much it has spent, and that it should be a matter for the LEA. We must agree to differ on that. I believe that it is reasonable for the Government to lay down certain conditions. If the hon. Member for Harrogate and Knaresborough were to argue that it would be good practice, good government and good administration to resolve those matters as early as possible so that people knew the situation, I would agree with him. That it is a correct and valid point. However, it is always the way that we learn from experience and move forward. Regulation 8(1) gives us the power to continue to develop the process.

In reponse to the further point made by the hon. Member for Epping Forest, we widely consult head teachers and LEAs on various aspects of the standards fund. Many areas are the subject of a substantial consultation process, in which a considerable number of representations are made, and which we genuinely take into account.

I shall now turn to regulation 6(1) and the alleged bidding process, to which the hon. Member for Epping Forest and others referred. The regulation does not set out a bidding process, it sets out an application process—the process for making a claim. For exactly the reasons set out by the hon. Member for Harrogate and Knaresborough, in the case of many of the funds, the LEA must make a contribution—sometimes 50 per cent. and sometimes a different proportion—and the LEA must indicate that it wishes to take advantage of the allocation made to it. When it considers its budget, it will decide whether it is in a position to do that. Some LEAs decide not to apply for the money—not many of them, because they mostly think that it is a good thing—but some, for reasons of ideology, or because they feel that they do not have the resources, decide not to apply. Regulation 6(1) states that the money will not be paid unless the LEA asks for it. That is not an onerous requirement. Again, I make the partisan point that previous legislation under previous Governments established that requirement. It is good practice and we should welcome it.

JPEG 13 Issues have been raised concerning a bidding process, which is not covered in regulation 6(1). It is true that, in some of the lines in the regulation, local education authorities bid for a sum of money, but the process is quite rare. Substantial amounts of the standards fund are not bid in competitions but allocated automatically.

I shall give examples of cases that involved a bidding process. I am familiar with the national grid for learning and with local education authority submissions for information and computer technology. To begin with, the process was extremely patchy. Some LEAs were outstanding and deserved support because they had put together worthwhile propositions, but others were a long way off the pace. Had we allocated money to them, they would not have spent it properly and resources would have been wasted. Therefore, we began a bidding process, which has improved quality over successive years. Those authorities now submit coherent and innovative plans.

Other lines are not subject to a bidding process and there is room for party political or internal argument about the desirability of bidding at each line. The bidding to which the hon. Member for Epping Forest referred in a parliamentary answer on 22 April, quoted in column 682 of Hansard, concerned capital funds. I have responsibility for the new deal for schools, for which there were about 5,000 applications—I am speaking from memory—of which about 1,000 were granted. Eighteen years of Conservative Government underinvestment has created a massive hole, which we are trying to plug by allocating money. A large proportion of the numbers referred to in the parliamentary answer were bidding for the capital process.

Mr. Clarke
: No, I am not saying that. Regulation 6(1), to which she referred, is concerned with the formal application process. It is not a question of competitive bidding, but of whether an LEA decides to undertake the application process. However, in a significant number of the lines set out in schedule 1—I have not calculated the figure—there is an automatic allocation formula with no competitive bidding.
