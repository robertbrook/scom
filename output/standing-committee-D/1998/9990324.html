Draft Social Security (Incapacity, Earnings and Work Trials) Pilot Schemes Regulations 1999

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. Hugh Bayley)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Social Security (Incapacity, Earnings and Work Trials) Pilot Schemes Regulations 1999.

I share with the Committee my pleasure, Mr. Wells, at being under your chairmanship. As I entered the Room, I heard you saying under your breath, "I hope that he is not going to make any mistakes." I can put my hand on my heart and say that I have never been to a Committee considering a statutory instrument and made a mistake. That is perfectly true, because never before have I been a member of a Committee considering a statutory instrument. With your guidance, Mr. Wells, I hope that my so far perfect record will be maintained.

The regulations form part of the Government's policy of helping disabled people move into or to try out work. They are the first regulations to be made using the new piloting power provided under the Social Security Act 1998. That power allows beneficial changes to be made on a pilot basis, to see which are the most effective in helping people with disabilities or long-term illnesses who wish to move towards the world of work. I shall describe how the regulations will help them.

The regulations provide for two pilot schemes—the incapacity earnings provision, and work trials. The pilot schemes will operate for a year from 11 April 1999. The schemes will operate in 15 Benefit Agency districts that cover areas in England, Scotland and Wales. They will thus be available to more than 300,000 people who live in the locality of those pilot offices and who receive benefit on the ground of incapacity. They are part of a package of measures, which will also include testing the introduction of a jobfinder grant of £200 and a jobmatch payment of £50 a week payable for six months. The regulations will modify the Social Security (Incapacity for Work) (General) Regulations 1995 for those who can benefit from the pilot schemes.

The first scheme, for work trials, will allow people who have been incapable of work for more than 196 days to be treated as being incapable of work while undertaking work trials, a current Employment Service programme for jobseekers. The programme will allow employers to offer vacancies to clients on an unpaid basis for up to 15 working days. That will provide an opportunity for clients to try out a job, to see if it suits them, and it allows employers to evaluate the suitability of clients. During the 4 trial, the clients will remain on benefit, and the employers and clients take part without obligation. Work trails are used for full-time vacancies of at least 16 hours a week that are expected to last for at least six months.

The second pilot scheme is for incapacity earnings. The provision will allow people living in a pilot area to do a small amount of any work, subject to a limit of £15 a week without losing benefit on the ground of incapacity. That can provide a valuable stepping stone for people wanting to move into work.

The regulations also provide for a smooth end to the pilot schemes if it is decided not to run further pilots or if the schemes are introduced nationally. On the expiry of the regulations, a person will be able to benefit from the pilot measures for a further six months. That will give people a reasonable time to adjust to the ending of the pilot schemes.

The two schemes are designed to give people claiming incapacity benefit the opportunity to try out work without losing their benefit, and we hope that they will give some of those with disabilities a real opportunity to take the first steps in moving from benefit into work.

To inform future decision-making, we shall be undertaking a comprehensive monitoring and evaluation of the pilot schemes. That will allow us to understand the impact of the regulations, and to decide whether it is appropriate to implement the changes nationally.

In the past, the welfare system marginalised people with disabilities by writing them off to a life of dependence. We have a duty to recognise the skills, abilities and ambitions of disabled people. We are therefore reforming the welfare state to make it inclusive and to empower disabled people to achieve their ambitions.

We are aware that many people suffer a severity of disability or a degree of ill health that means that they cannot work. We are committed to giving them financial support, so that they can live dignified and secure lives. However, 2 million disabled people already work, and surveys suggest that more than 1 million others want to work. We want to help them.

Our approach is based around four strands. First, we are removing obstacles to working from the benefit system. We have introduced a one-year linking rule for incapacity benefits and have removed restrictions on voluntary work. Before the change, the higher rate of incapacity benefit linking rule lasted for only eight weeks; we have extended it to 52 weeks.

Secondly, we are implementing measures to ensure that work pays, such as the introduction of the national minimum wage and the new disabled person's tax credit.

Thirdly, we are promoting radical changes in the workplace to ensure equality of opportunity. We are building on the provisions of the Disability Discrimination Act 1995, introducing the disability rights task force and working to set up a Disability Rights Commission.

Fourthly, we are providing active help and encouragement to people with disabilities to move into work through our new deal for disabled people.

All that is no less than a systematic attack on the barriers to work that are faced by disabled people. Many disabled people want to work, and we are firmly JPEG 5 committed to helping them do so by focusing on their capacities rather than their incapacities. Given that aim, and given that the regulations have been supported by disabled people and the organisations that represent them, I trust that the Committee will have no hesitation in welcoming such beneficial and worth while changes. I commend the regulations to the Committee.

Mr. Bayley
: A range of important points have been raised by hon. Members, and I shall try to respond to each of them in turn.

The hon. Member for Brentwood and Ongar started off by referring to the contributory principle. I appreciate that we should not debate that subject at any length in this Committee, because it is not the matter before us, but changes were made to the benefits system before the general election. For example, we are extending the contributory principle by introducing widowers' benefits, which is a new contributory benefit. In other sectors, there is a move in the opposite direction, but it is certainly not true that the Government are abandoning the national insurance principle.

The pilot areas were chosen to provide a good range of locations in both rural and urban areas, a good geographical spread and a range of demographic differences, including areas with high unemployment and areas with low unemployment. We shall learn lessons that will be applicable throughout the country.

The number of disabled people claiming incapacity benefits within the pilot areas is around 300,000. We have no idea how much take-up there will be as a result of the 10 pilot schemes. The point of piloting them is precisely to find out which of these measures will have an effect in providing the opportunities—which everyone in the Committee seems to support, and which is central to the Government's policy—to enable disabled people who have, effectively, been debarred from employment to get into employment.

"We know that there is a rather high success rate with these trials and that people are kept on."—[ Official Report, House of Lords, 23 March 1999; Vol. 598, c. 1281.]

Mr. Bayley
: The measures that we are taking have been used for some time, as broadly comparable measures have been available to people who are unemployed and claiming jobseeker's allowance. They have been successful in enabling people to get back into the labour market, but they are not available to people on incapacity benefit. The point of the pilot is to make such measures available to people who are on incapacity benefit, without putting them in the invidious position of having to come off that benefit and start claiming jobseeker's allowance, which would reduce their standard of living.

Therefore, we know that the measures work generally for people who are unemployed and seeking work. We want to include people who are on incapacity benefit, but we do not know how the measures will work. Some will work better than others, but we shall find out from the pilot. I was asked for how long each of the measures will be available. The regulations will establish a pilot for a 12-month period, with a six-month run-on at the end of that period if we decide not to roll out the measures more widely, or to extend them. The £15 earning measure will be available throughout that period. The three-week work trials, as its name suggests, will be available for three weeks. The six-month jobmatch payment will be available for six months, and the £200 payment for those entering the labour market off incapacity benefit is a one-off grant that will be available at that point.

Through other new deal pilot innovative schemes, we found that people simply wanted to get a taste of work to the tune of one hour a week. We are working with two commercial companies—two restaurant chains—which are providing opportunities for people to re-entre the labour market on that basis. The measures that I have described, such as the job match payments, are intended to take people to that next steps. If someone increased their hours beyond the £15 limit, they would—if they met the conditions, as in most circumstances they would—become eligible for the job match payment that would give them a £50-a-week top-up. That top-up is paid to the individual, not the employer so that if he or she moves in stages from three or four hours a week to 16 hours a week a part-time job match payment will be given. As the hours increase, the individual will have access to other benefits from different parts of the benefit system. Neither I nor any member of the Government has ever said that we expect, or even hope, to get one million people off incapacity benefits and into work. We have said that the survey evidence shows that there are more than one million disabled people—having looked it up before the debate, I can give the precise figure of 1.4 million—on incapacity benefits who say that they would like to work. We are not rolling out a national programme with the expectation that a certain number of people will get into work. We know that the barriers to work for disabled people are prodigious, but we also know that many people facing long-term sickness on benefits are enthusiastic to work. We want to build opportunities that will enable such aspirations to be met and that is what the pilot schemes are designed to achieve. We are working in uncharted areas with clients who often have prodigious obstacles to overcome. We want to help them meet their goals.

My hon. Friend the Member for Ellesmere Port and Neston and the hon. Member for Winchester asked whether a three-week work trial was long enough. For many jobs it will be. The three-week period is flexible. It will constitute 15 working days, which would normally be consecutive, but could constitute, for example, 30 half days, again consecutive, over a six-week period.

The scheme differs from that offering the £15 wage opportunity. People who participate in a work trial will not be paid. We intend involvement in the scheme to lead to paid employment.
