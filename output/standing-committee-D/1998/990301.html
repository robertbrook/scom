Draft Scottish Parliament (Elections etc.) Order 1999

4.30 pm

The Minister for Home Affairs and Devolution, Scottish Office (Mr. Henry McLeish)
: I beg to move, That the Committee has considered the draft Scottish Parliament (Elections etc.) Order 1999.

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the draft Scottish Parliament (Disqualification) Order 1999 and the draft Scottish Parliament (First Ordinary General Election and First Meeting) Order 1999.

Mr. McLeish
: I am pleased to present the three orders to the Committee. The orders mark a significant stage in the preparation for elections to and the first meeting of the Scottish Parliament. The order relating to the general election and first meeting is made under section 2(1) of the Scotland Act 1998; it sets the date for the first election to the Scottish Parliament as 6 May this year. It also lays down that the first meeting of the Parliament will take place on 12 May at 9.30 am in the General Assembly Hall of the Church of Scotland on The Mound in Edinburgh.

Dr. Norman A. Godman
(Greenock and Inverclyde)
: I am sorry to intervene so early. Constituents have suggested that election day should be a public holiday. What is the Government's view?

Mr. McLeish
: That subject goes slightly wider than the scope of the orders. Suffice it to say that there will be sufficient celebration in Scotland at the opening: a public holiday might create further difficulty. I hope that celebrations will ensue, but I do not think that there is a need for a public holiday.

Although the order to which I just referred sets the day of the first election, the Scottish Parliament (Elections etc.) Order 1999 makes detailed provisions for the conduct of elections to the Parliament. It also makes provision for the special arrangements that will be necessary when the elections to the Parliament are combined with local government elections, as will happen on 6 May this year. Section 12(1) of the 1998 Act, whose provisions for elections were the subject of our lengthy debates during its passage through the House provides for the order.

Before talking a little more about the elections order, I should like to record our thanks to the representatives of the political parties and electoral administrators whom we have consulted during the past few months. I am aware 4 that, because of such a broad spectrum of opinion, it has not always been possible to please everyone with every aspect of the arrangements, but I am confident that what we have will provide a sound basis for the arrangements for future elections.

The order reflects to a great extent existing United Kingdom electoral law under representation of the people legislation. We have not tried to reinvent the wheel.

(West Aberdeenshire and Kincardine)

Mr. McLeish

I was saying that much of the content of the order reflects UK electoral law. Electoral law is sometimes criticised for being arcane, and hon. Members might be aware that a wide-ranging review of electoral practices is currently being undertaken by a working group under the chairmanship of my hon. Friend the Minister responsible for deregulation, drugs and elections—an interesting combination of portfolios. In time, no doubt the recommendations from the review will apply to elections to the Scottish Parliament as they do to the rest of the United Kingdom. But that is not our business today.

Modifications to existing practices have been necessary to take account of the additional member electoral system.

The order takes account of the Registration of Political Parties Act 1998, which allows the use of official party emblems on ballot papers, and controls the names that parties may use at elections so as to reduce confusion. The order also takes account of the recommendations made by the committee on Standards in Public Life chaired by Lord Neill. Those recommendations concern expenses that may be incurred by political parties during an election campaign, and I shall speak about those in more detail later.

I should like briefly to explain the provision that the order makes for detailed election arrangements and the changes that have been made to current practices. Earlier, I mentioned that we have taken into account the Neill committee's proposals on expenses to the extent allowed by powers under the Scotland Act 1998. The order imposes expense limits and penalties for constituency candidates on the same basis as applies to Westminster JPEG 5 elections. The order imposes limits on expenses for individuals who are standing as independent candidates in the regional ballots. Those limits will be structured in a similar way to those imposed on constituency candidates.

The limits for an individual candidate are the aggregate of the constituency limits in the region. The order sets a limit of £1.5 million on party expenditure in an election. That limit is intended to cover all a party's campaign expenditure, which is not normally incurred in relation to a particular constituency candidate.

Mrs. Maria Fyfe
(Glasgow, Maryhill)
: Having read the draft order, I should be grateful for clarification on the following matter. When someone is standing for a constituency and a regional seat, can the relevant expenditure be claimed for both candidacies?

Mr. McLeish
: The answer is no. Each constituency candidate will have an election agent, and a process will be undergone similar to that for candidates standing in Westminster elections. The £1.5 million limit set by the parties applies to the system that lists candidates' names. An entry must be given, not for each candidate, but by a person appointed to deal with the list system. So the two are quite distinct and separate.

We welcome the voluntary agreement reached by the main political parties in Scotland on the national limit on party expenditure and on some other matters covered by Lord Neill's committee, for which the order does not make provision. However, there have been some objections to the absence of limits at a regional level.

Lord Neill did not propose to make any attempt to sub-divide party expenditure into national and regional elements. However, the form of return proposed for expenses will show separately the expenses incurred by each regional election agent. Transparency is as effective a safeguard as we need against parties targeting expenditure on a particular region.

There should be no problem about election expenses limits, as they will be dealt with by election agents. However, if—as the hon. Member for West Dorset (Mr. Letwin) suggests—a candidate is on the regional list system, the constituency expenditure is separate for accounting purposes. Anything else that is spent, is spent by the national party up to a level of £1.5 million. That covers—

Mr. McLeish
: Part of the remit of the committee in Scotland headed by Professor King is to look at the details of the new system and to get procedures adopted on which all the parties agree. In that sense, the committee is breaking new ground.

To clarify, for all regional list candidates all expenses are submitted as part of national accounts, including expenses set against each of the regional lists. These will go down as national accounts. Anyone who stands on his own as an individual candidate at regional level has an expenses limit that is equal to the sum of the limits for the constituencies in that region. If he is also a constituency candidate he will have a constituency limit applied to his canvassing and campaigns in the constituency. The two limits will apply to that individual. It is a complex subject but I think that that answers the questions.

We welcome the voluntary agreement reached by the main political parties in Scotland on the national limit on party expenditure and on some other matters covered by JPEG 7 Lord Neill's committee. Lord Neill did not propose any attempt to sub-divide party expenditure into national and regional elements, which we have already discussed. The form of return proposed for these expenses will, however, show separately the expenses incurred by each regional election agent. Transparency is as effective a safeguard as we need against parties targetting expenditure.

Hon. Members will note that for the elections to the Scottish Parliament we have not applied section 93 of the Representation of the People Act 1983. That provision restricts the way in which broadcasters may cover elections, in particular by making the agreement of all candidates necessary to certain types of report. In this we are in line with the approach proposed in Wales, and indeed which was taken at the elections for the Northern Ireland Assembly. The Home Office is consulting the political parties about the possibility of disapplying section 93 at future elections.

The rules, which set out all the practical arrangements for the election, are contained in schedule 2 of the order. These rules cover the key electoral process from the publication of the notice of election, through the submission of nominations, polling procedures, the count, the announcement of result and the disposal of documents. The rules also cover the ballot papers. Rule 28 specifies that the colour of ballot papers must be different. For the elections on 6 May we have guided returning officers that the ballot paper for the constituency candidates will be purple and the regional ballot paper will be peach. The ballot paper for local government elections will be white, as is normal.

The rules make provision for a poster to appear within each polling station, which will provide additional guidance for electors on the procedure for voting. The rules set the text and we are working on a design which will be recommended to returning officers. Honourable Members will also know that we have started a detailed publicity campaign, which has been agreed by political parties from all sides, that will inform voters what to expect when they enter polling stations and what the ballot papers will look like.

The voting procedure at the elections will be very similar to that at present. On each ballot paper the elector will be asked to place an X against their preferred choice. The ballot paper will then be placed in die respective ballot box. There will be three ballot boxes at each station—one for me constituency election, one for the regional Member papers and one for the local government elections. Returning officers have received guidance that the ballot boxes should be colour coded to help avoid any confusion.

The timing of the count of the Scottish Parliament elections will begin as soon as is practicable after the polls have shut at 10 pm. That will, we have every reason to hope, mean that an overnight count will enable the results to be known during the course of the night. The count for the local government elections will be delayed until the following day and the legislation provides that the counting should not start before 10 am the following day. That will give discretion to the local returning officer to set die exact time in light of local circumstances.

8 Honourable Members will appreciate that, given the size of the order, a great deal of further detail could be mentioned on which I would be happy to comment further if asked.

The arrangements that we have put in place are sensible and will work. Elections are always very complex matters. This order, however, should prove to be a sound basis for all the elections to come.

As part of this debate about elections, we are also considering an order under section 15 of me Scotland Act 1988, which disqualifies a number of office-holders from membership of the Scottish Parliament.

The Government intend that me Scottish Parliament should represent the widest possible range of interests in Scotland, and it might be helpful to set this order in context if I briefly explained who will be eligible to be a Member of the Scottish Parliament.

In essence, those who are eligible to be Members of this House will also be able to be MSPs. In addition, ordained members or ministers of any religious denomination, Commonwealth and Irish Republic citizens as well as EU citizens resident in the UK will be able to stand. Peers will also be able to be MSPs.

However much it would be inappropriate for certain office-holders to be Members of this House, so would it be inappropriate for certain office-holders to be Members of me Scottish Parliament. Section 15 of the Scotland Act 1998 makes provision about who should be disqualified from membership of the Parliament. Such people include civil servants, members of the armed forces, members of police forces and judges. That covers about 1 million people.

Section 15 also gives Her Majesty power, by Order in Council, to specify office-holders who should be disqualified from being Members of the Parliament. One of the orders that we are debating today deals with mat.

Mr. Ernie Ross

Mr. McLeish
: Let me take hon. Members through the process. The political agents who are involved will have knowledge of the system. On entering the poling station, advice will be available, and at larger polling stations, extra people will be employed by the returning officers to ensure that help is afforded to older people and those with special difficulties, At the point where the ballot is cast, the ballot boxes will be colour coded in peach, purple and white—people may put their ballot papers in the wrong box. After the conclusion of voting at 10 o'clock in each of the areas where counts are taking place, boxes for the Scottish parliament will be opened, and a check will be made on the colours in each box. If a ballot has been cast in the wrong box, it will be transferred to the right box. The boxes will then be counted properly at the place of the count.

That process will be repeated for the local government boxes, so that if Scottish Parliament papers are in a box for the local government elections, they will be transferred. All the right colour papers will thereby end up in the right colour boxes. That will overcome the problem, which we have foreseen, that people may, for a variety of reasons, put ballot papers in the wrong box. If someone makes a mistake, it will be rectified before proper verification at the count. That can only be helpful to all concerned.

Mr. McLeish

The disqualification of certain office-holders has a precedent in the House of Commons Disqualification Act 1975, which consolidated a previous Act. In considering which office-holders should be disqualified from the Scottish Parliament, we paid particular heed to that well 10 established and well understood precedent, which embodies principles that have been well known for 40 years. We concluded that it would not be appropriate to depart radically from that approach at this stage. The orders makes provisions for the first elections, which will continue until such time as the Scottish Parliament makes its own provisions. I am sure that hon. Members will agree that it is right for the Scottish Parliament to be able to decide the scope of the disqualification provisions for its membership.

In deciding who should be disqualified, we applied the criteria used to determine whether an office-holder should be disqualified from the House of Commons. Our aim has been to maintain the Scottish Parliaments's independence and safeguard MSPs from undue influence by the Executive through the exercise of patronage. The disqualification order would disqualify office-holders who would be disqualified from becoming a Member of Parliament, if we thought that it was appropriate to the circumstances of the Scottish parliament—if office-holders were operating in devolved matters or in areas where Scottish Minister had an interest, for example. Such office-holders will be accountable to the Parliament, so it would clearly be wrong if they could also be an MSP. Officer-holders who fall into that category would include members of Scottish water authorities or members of Scottish Enterprise.

Office-holders holding a disqualifying office would be required to resign that office before giving their formal consent to nomination. This provision is contained in the conduct of elections order. The consent to nomination must be given by the last day for the delivery of nomination papers, and may be submitted up to one month before that. For the Scottish Parliament elections, consent to nomination must be submitted by 13 April. That would mean that people holding disqualifying offices would be required to resign by that date at the latest.

We think that the list of disqualified office-holders strikes the right balance between ensuring that the Parliament is open to a wide range of talented people, but is properly independent and able to hold the Executive to account.
