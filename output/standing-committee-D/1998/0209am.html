They do not know whether the taper is 70 per cent., 55 per cent., or anything about it. They know what they are getting now, and perhaps if they get a better job they will see—six months later when the necessary adjustments have been made—that they did not receive much net benefit. The evidence that tapers influence people's behaviour is negligible. Can the Financial Secretary cite academic studies showing that by changing tapers people change their labour market decisions—or even that they understand what a taper is? I would be surprised to hear about them.

Imposing an arbitrary cut-off point, which would provide help if the income was a penny below the line, but none if it was a penny above, is not the way to solve the problem of higher rate taxpayers. Such a system would create discontinuity, which is undesirable. A better way would be to maintain the taper at 70 per cent.—this is a slighly radical proposal—and spend the extra money on higher credits. Therefore, the credit would extend a little further in terms of distribution, but not into the income ranges that we have discussed.
