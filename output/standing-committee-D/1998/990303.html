Local Government Finance (England) Special Grant Report (No. 41)

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Ms Glenda Jackson)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Local Government Finance (England) Special Grant Report (No. 41).

I welcome the debate on the Special Grant Report (No. 41), which is about our proposals to pay grant to certain schemes put forward by particular local authorities under our rural bus challenge competition. The Committee will know that the Chancellor announced in last year's Budget an additional £50 million a year for rural public transport in the United Kingdom. The grants that we shall be discussing today are part of that £50 million.

This new money is one of the positive steps that the Government are taking to assist rural communities, improve mobility, provide more travel choice for people who live in the countryside and reduce rural isolation, a particular form of social exclusion. Car ownership is higher in rural areas than in towns, but access to jobs, shops, schools, entertainment and hospitals is more restricted for people without a car. Public transport, particularly the rural bus with its flexibility and adaptability, can help to remove those restrictions.

The £50 million a year announced in the last Budget showed the Government's commitment to rural public transport, particularly in the rural bus. It is helping rural communities to flourish, providing alternatives to the private car and protecting the environment. That £50 million is an annual total for the whole of the United Kingdom. It is divided among the four nations by the well-established Barnett formula. The English share is £41.7 million, £32.5 million of which has already been distributed to local authorities via the new rural bus subsidy grant that is set out for the current financial year in the Special Grant Report (No. 36) that was approved by the House last July. Local authorities have used that money for new services that provide new travel opportunities.

In addition, £4.2 million has been made available for community-based initiatives in rural areas and £5 million was set aside for a rural bus challenge competition, the results of which are set out in the Special Grant Report (No. 41).

In July, we announced our plans to hold a challenge competition. Local authorities were invited to put forward innovative schemes and we made it clear that bids could be for capital as well as for current account expenditure. We also stressed several important 4 factors to be taken into account in developing proposals, such as the need to consider public transport networks, improve links between remote rural areas and existing public transport services, consider the needs of women and people with disabilities and take account of interaction with community transport providers as well as bus operators.

Bids were invited from county and unitary councils, and passenger transport authorities in August last year. By the closing date in October, we had received a total of 214 bids from 54 authorities. The total value of the bids was more than £41.5 million. I was particularly impressed that so many local authorities worked so quickly and effectively to produce so many worthwhile bids.

The timing of the Budget announcement last year, plus the need to develop criteria and consult on proposals for the new rural bus subsidy grant, meant that in the first year authorities have not been able to spend the full £32.5 million that is available to them. We have added a further £6.5 million to the initial £5 million, enabling us to fund even more worthy schemes.

Even with that increase, total bids far exceeded the £11.4 million that was available. There were many disappointed authorities, but there will be at least two more challenge competitions for bids to be considered. In reaching decisions on this year's challenge, we examined the schemes against the published criteria with the greatest care. We aimed at a balanced range of schemes—large and small, simple and technically more advanced—from all over the country. Time does not permit me to list all the schemes supported, but I expect that the Committee will want me to mention at least some of the successful bids.

Durham will receive £592,000 for a scheme that aims to improve public transport access for coalfield communities, where the need for good links with other centres can be acute. Partnerships will be developed with bus operators to improve information and waiting facilities, and to extend the Access bus to serve former mining villages. The scheme will also involve the establishment of a hire-care or taxi-bus service to improve access to areas with employment opportunities.

Norfolk is to receive £444,500 for its Norfolk coast strategy, which aims to provide new services and new shelters, and provide for the installation of public information terminals at principal tourist information centres.

In Wiltshire, we are supporting a £463,000 scheme for the evocatively named Wiltshire Wigglybus—three new bus services providing a flexibility routed service for rural communities in Pewsey Vale between Devizes and Burbage.

Somerset will receive £130,000 for the Mendips flexible bus project, which will provide a dedicated vehicle and driver for peak-time services linking villages in the Mendips to the commercial network for journeys to Bath, Bristol and Frome, and flexibly routed off-peak services, including evening services, to Midsommer Norton.

JPEG 5 In Nottinghamshire, £1.15 million will go to the Worksop area for a real-time information and mobility management system based on satellite tracking, and for a smartcard ticketing system.

In Peterborough, we are giving £74,000 for a rural taxi-bus service that will provide links between the main railway station and the surrounding area.

For Gloucestershire, £25,000 will be given for a scheme to provide a rural transport brokerage, which will make full use of ambulances and other council vehicles to provide public transport services when not otherwise in use.

I shall outline the other contents of the report. Annex A lists the amounts payable to each of the 33 successful authorities for the 46 schemes approved. The total cost of those schemes is £11,418,694—more than double the £5 million initially allocated to the challenge competition. The report seeks approval for the total cost of the schemes. Some of the schemes listed will last one year, others will last longer. The funding figures shown are the total scheme costs and not necessarily the annual costs. It seems only right to place before the Committee the full costs for which the Government seek approval.

Annex B details the main features of the report, including an explanation of the objectives of the competition and the criteria against which the bids were assessed, including the extent to which the bids took account of the key factors highlighted in the bidding invitation.

The conditions and arrangements for payment are set out in annex C. Any costs to be incurred this year will be paid immediately. Costs for future years that exceed £25,000 will be paid in instalments upon receipt of a statement from the authority of what it spent the previous year and what is projected for future years, as a simple matter of financial propriety. Similarly, at the end of each scheme, the authorities must provide an audit certificate showing that the funds have been used in accordance with the purpose for which they were paid, and a statement setting out the extent to which the aims and benefits of the schemes have been achieved.

I hope that I have explained the key features of the report. It demonstrates the importance that the Government attach to rural communities and the transport opportunities available to them. It shows that we are determined, as part of an integrated transport policy, to make sure that people have transport options other than the private car. It shows also that the Government are thinking of those who cannot afford a private car but who still have transport needs and who deserve to be brought into the wider community of work and leisure.

The special grants that are the subject of the report represent a variety of bright, innovative ideas by local authorities to improve rural bus transport. I am sure that they will prove their worth in years to come and that they will encourage others to adopt similar ideas. I commend the report to the Committee.
