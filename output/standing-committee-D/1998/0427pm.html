title HABITUAL RESIDENCE TEST

'.—In section 137(2)(a) of the Social Security Contributions and Benefits Act 1992, at the end, insert—

Mr. David Rendel (Newbury): I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

I am delighted to introduce the clause. Before discussing it, I should say that my colleagues and I have discussed whether the word, "whether" should replace the word, "where" near the end of the clause. The two words mean much the same thing in this context. The Government may want to accept the clause, but may prefer "whether" to "where". If so, I should be delighted to accept an amendment to that effect on Report, provided that the clause is incorporated into the Bill today, as I hope that it will be.

Following the Swaddling judgment, the Government said that they would produce new proposals on the habitual residence test which, as it was then conducted, was deemed contrary to European law. I am happy to say that such new proposals are formulated in the clause and I hope that the Government will choose to adopt them. Since the Swaddling judgment, the Government have introduced new guidelines for adjudication officers on how the tests should be put into practice. I shall bring those to the Committee's attention to argue why they are Col. 1034 not sufficient. The new guidelines say that a claimant of income-based jobseeker's allowance or income support of any nationality who,

"1. was previously habitually resident in the UK and


2. moved and lived in another country and


3. returns to resume their previous residence


is habitually resident immediately on arrival in the UK."

That helps to overcome some of the problems with the habitual residence test, but certainly not all of them. The guidelines add:

"In deciding the habitual residence question the AO should take account of the length and continuity of the previous residence in the UKand whether the claimant has sufficient links with that previous residence to be regarded as picking up the pieces of their old life."

It is still relevant to include the new clause, which goes beyond the current guidelines because some groups of people who clearly should receive benefit are still likely to be refused benefit under the test. For example, those people who might be likely to fail the habitual residence test immediately on arrival, even after the change in the guidelines, would include someone who left the country as a young child and did not have the chance to establish habitual residence, perhaps because he or she was taken abroad by parents who then died, moved on or retired back to this country when the child had grown up.

If such a person returned to this country as an adult, believing that he or she was a citizen of this country, and was totally reliant on the state immediately after returning here, it would seem reasonable, logical and fair that he or she should be supported. One might think that the fact that such people were not working in this country when they were young should not be especially relevant to the case because, according to natural justice, they should not be excluded. However, it seems that, under the new guidelines, such a person would be excluded.

That is one example of a case where one might wish to go beyond the current guidelines, but there are similar cases—for example, where people have grown up in this country and had a period of habitual residence here when they were young, but have moved away and sold their houses. Their friends and family move away and they may have nowhere in particular to return to, but having been abroad for some time, and perhaps having met with some misfortune and been made redundant in another part of the world, they come back to this country an find that because they cannot be said to be picking up the pieces of their old lives, they fail the habitual residence test, even under the new guidelines.

So there are groups of people whom we would like to include among those who have the rights to benefits, but we cannot do so under the current guidelines—even the new guidelines produced since the Swaddling judgment.

Some people might ask what is wrong with the habitual residence test and why we need to change it to include more people. There are clear reasons for that, which are widely supported in all parts of the House, as I hope to demonstrate. The habitual residence test was first introduced on 1 August 1994 to overcome the problems of what was known as benefit tourism, whereby people of other nationalities might come to Britain hoping to take advantage of our welfare system. Clearly, we do not want people to take advantage of our system who have no right Image Col. 1035 to benefit from it. At the same time, the test seems to have extended beyond that group and to have excluded from benefits some people whom we would regard as vulnerable, who should have the right to claim benefits in this country and who should not have been excluded as a result of the test.

Interestingly, the Labour party opposed the change when it was introduced. In fact, when it was introduced in 1994, early-day motion 311 tabled by the Under-Secretary, no less, called for the abolition of the test. The early-day motion stated:

"That this House is deeply concerned about the effects of the habitual residence test on both British citizens and those with right of abode returning to the United Kingdom; notes that the test is being used to prevent vulnerable people from claiming benefits, for example, a young mother and her 10-month-old baby now residing in York who had recently returned from Cyprus to live in the United Kingdom; and calls upon the Government to abolish this test immediately."

That early-day motion was supported by Liberal Democrat Members and Labour Members, and possibly by some Conservatives too. The Minister of State was among its supporters. Therefore, we have good support for the abolition of the habitual residence test.
