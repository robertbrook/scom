legislation Education (Infant Class Sizes) (Grant) Regulations 1999

10.30 am

Mrs. Theresa May (Maidenhead): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Education (Infant Class Sizes) (Grant) Regulations 1999.

I welcome you, Mr. Stevenson, to the Chair. This is the first time that I have had the pleasure of serving under your chairmanship.

The regulations arise from sections of the School Standards and Framework Act 1998 on the requirement placed by the Government on local authorities to ensure that a limit is placed on infant class sizes in maintained schools, whether they are infant schools or primary schools.

Like other Committee members, I spent many long hours in Standing Committee debating in depth the School Standards and Framework Bill and, indeed, the clauses to which the regulations refer. Unfortunately, we were unable to see the Government's proposed draft regulations on those issues. We would have preferred to have been able to consider them as a whole, even if they were only draft outlines.

The regulations, S.I. 1999, No. 14, relate to section 3 of the Act. They provide for the Secretary of State to regulate the terms on which grants may be paid to local education authorities to reduce infant class sizes. Money will be made available for the employment of extra staff and for capital projects such as the building of new classrooms. Such projects might include the provision of additional space for, say, an extra classroom to reduce numbers in an existing infant class. Traditionally, a school might have 32 or 33 pupils in its infant class and would therefore require extra space to reduce that number to 30. A school might also incorporate classes from other schools. Some local education authorities are looking at merging schools to achieve the infant class size reduction that is required under the Act.

Much has been made of the fact that, to back up their pledge to ensure that local education authorities are able to reduce infant class sizes, the Government have made grants available to authorities. However, the regulations raise a number of questions about the Government's intentions in relation to those grants and the way in which they will be used by local education authorities.

The circumstances in which grants will be made available to local authorities, given the fluctuating nature of pupil numbers in schools, was debated particularly strenuously in Standing Committee. In any one or two years, an infant class might contain Col. 4 more than 30 pupils. As I understand it, under the plan for reducing infant class sizes, local education authorities will have to apply for money in advance. An authority might anticipate that circumstance and apply for a grant to provide, say, extra capital for the building of a classroom to accommodate extra pupils. However, pupil numbers might subsequently prove such that the classroom was not in fact needed to meet the infant class size reduction proposals.

The regulations attach conditions to payment of the grant. First, the grant can be paid only if the local authority's plan to reduce class sizes has been approved by the Secretary of State. Secondly, the Secretary of State can determine what amount of the grant applied for shall be provided to a local education authority, and whether it should be 100 per cent. or less than 100 per cent. of the approved expenditure to which it relates.

I wish to concentrate on part III of the regulations, under the heading "Supplemental", especially regulation 9, which provides that the Secretary of State may attach certain requirements to payment of the grant to the local education authority. Under those requirements the Secretary of State may require the local authority to repay the grant and may require the payment of interest on sums due. I do not recall the issue of repayment of grant being raised at all during discussions on the School Standards and Framework Act 1998.

Mr. Don Foster (Bath): That is right.

Mrs. May: The hon. Gentleman confirms my recollection of what happened in the Standing Committee.

It is, at best, unfortunate that the Government failed to raise the issue when we considered the 1998 Act because it is fundamental to the way in which the Government propose to fund their infant class size reduction pledge, and to the expectations of local education authorities in planning their budgets and making plans to reduce infant class sizes. The suggestion that, having been made a grant to ensure the reduction of infant class sizes, the Secretary of State may somehow require that the grant or some portion of the grant be repaid to the Government, is an issue that has not been raised before. If that is the Government's intention, it should be made absolutely clear.

The Government made an announcement last week, with much fanfare and blowing of trumpets, about money being made available—it was a sum that they had already announced twice before, but we are getting used to that—but they failed to give the detail. Another lesson that we are learning from this Government is that the devil is always in the detail. The headlines may sound fine—motherhood and apple pie—but when one considers the detail, one discovers the dangers that are lurking and the practical problems that will arise.

Therefore, I ask the Minister to make absolutely clear what proposals on the repayment of grants accompany the regulations. A local education authority, which has submitted its infant class size Image Col. 5 reduction plan as required under the Act, had it approved by the Secretary of State and received a Government grant under part II of the regulations, be it 100 per cent. or less of its approved expenditure, will spend that money on whatever in its plan has been approved, on the understanding that that is what was required to achieve its infant class size reduction plan and what the Government agreed it could do.

If the Government are now saying that, at some stage in future, they might ask the local education authority to repay some or all of the grant, that is an entirely different picture, and it is necessary for local education authorities to know exactly where they stand. Many have already made proposals and many have received notification of grant on the understanding that it was given to them so that they could build classrooms or employ teachers in order to achieve reductions in infant class sizes with no expectation that, somewhere down the road, they will have to adjust their budgets to meet the repayment requirements.

Mr. Don Foster: Has the hon. Lady read regulation 9(3)(d), which refers to the ability of the Secretary of State—admittedly after consultation—to "vary" the requirement? Could the Secretary of State suddenly require the repayment of a grant that had already been allocated, or of interest on that grant?

Mrs May: I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman for raising that pertinent point. He is absolutely right—a local authority might apply for a grant and receive it on the understanding that it may have to repay it, and subsequently have to repay it. I am particularly concerned about grants that have already been allocated to local education authorities. Have the Government told LEAs that have had their grant applications approved that some of the grant may have to be repaid? Have they told those LEAs that the relevant conditions may vary? Although the Government may have said that a grant does not need to be repaid, they may be able subsequently to require an LEA to repay it.

I have discussed the repayment of grant, but we should also consider the payment of interest on sums that are due to the Secretary of State. I have discussed regulation 9(2)(b) and regulation 9(2)(d), but we also need to consider regulation 9(2)(c), which refers to

"the payment to the Secretary of State of other sums related to the value of assets acquired, provided or improved with the aid of grant".

What does that rather sinister sub-paragraph mean? The Secretary of State may say to an LEA, "Fine. You want to build classrooms in X, Y and Z primary schools in order to meet the infant class sizes pledge. By doing so, you will improve the value of a school's assets. We will also require you to pay us some sums of money." That was never hinted at during the passage of the School Standards and Framework Act. With reference to regulation 9(2)(c), what "other sums" of money do the Government have in mind?

Regulation 9(2)(d) refers to

"the payment of interest on sums due to the Secretary of State".

Col. 6 Are the sums those that are contained in regulation 9(2)(c), or do they involve interest that would be due if a local authority were subsequently required to repay a Government grant? Could a local authority be required to repay interest, perhaps over a specified period?

In a nutshell, the problem with the regulations is that they give LEAs no confidence in Government grants. We are all acquainted with the difficulties that LEAs and local authorities suffered as a result of the bad settlement that many of them received from the Government this year. Local authorities are having to increase other budgets to passport increases in education spending to schools.

Social services departments are suffering because of the way in which the Government chose to provide funding. Shire counties and shire unitaries have been hit especially hard by their settlement. LEAs are already in the difficult position of having to search for extra funds. Some London LEAs, for example, find it difficult to fund a teachers' pay award, which was not fully funded by Government. They are already finding it difficult to deliver the education budgets that they believe are right for their schools. Those budgets may be raided by the Government to repay grants that were made available in the expectation that the money needed to be paid only because it was meeting a Government pledge. Many local education authorities would question the Government's intentions and examine closely their glossy headlines about grants.

The regulations introduce uncertainty for local authorities, schools and council tax payers because of the bills that they may have to pay should the Government requirement repayment of sums or payment of extra sums to the Secretary of State under the regulations. That was not mentioned when the School Standards and Framework Bill was being considered and it is incumbent on the Government to make absolutely clear exactly what burdens they will impose on local education authorities through the regulations.

If a school uses money that it is given to reduce class sizes and for new build to build classrooms for key stage 2, in order to free up facilities for key stage 1, does that give the Government the right to say, "You have not met the letter of the law. Therefore, we can ask the LEA to reclaim the money"? Schools need to be as flexible as possible in interpreting the regulations and meeting the class size pledge. Will the Minister clarify whether the Government are specifically saying, "Unless the build is for key stage 1, you are not entitled to the money and we have the right to claim it back"?

Members will accept that—as is always the case—the regulations have been drafted to cover every eventuality. We know that that is sometimes needed. The letter of the law is what matters in the end. I should be livid if I discovered that a local authority had built not the extra classrooms but a town hall and that I or my successors could not do a thing about it.
