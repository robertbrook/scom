legislation Rent Acts (Maximum Fair Rent) Order 1999

10.30 am

Mr. Nigel Waterson (Eastbourne): I beg to move, That the Committee has considered the Rent Acts (Maximum Fair Rent) Order 1999 (S.I. 1999, No. 6).

I am delighted to serve under your fair but firm chairmanship, Mrs. Roe.

The measure is ill thought out and designed to solve the wrong problem. It sends the wrong message to those who might be interested in investing in our private rented sector. We should consider the survey that the property research unit of the department of land economy at Cambridge University carried out for the British Property Federation. It found a discernible change in investors' attitudes towards investment in the private rented sector. It claims that while only 30 per cent, of the institutions covered by the survey were currently involved, through equity investment or lending, in the private rented sector, twice that number are considering investing and believe that they could have started or increased their involvement in the next two years.

The survey also concludes that Housing Ministers' proposals to regulate rent increases in the regulated fair rent sector could be portrayed as a response to specific problems. Even so, more than half the institutions viewed Ministers' proposals negatively and 40 per cent. believed that regulation would be extended to the assured and assured shorthold sector. The survey claims that institutions' almost unanimous response to the prospect of extending regulations to those sectors was that they would not invest in the private rented sector.

The survey concludes that the long-term importance of the findings depends on the funding that is actually needed. An estimated £3 billion net new investment per annum is required for the private rented sector to grow effectively. The survey's final comment is:

"viability depends on the market sector remaining free of controls. If regulation is extended, institutional interest will disappear very quickly."

That is a crucial point.

With the possible exception of the left on the Government Benches, we all support a viable and successful private rented sector in this country. The term "social inclusion" will remain a slogan unless people have decent homes. The private rented sector has a pivotal role in providing many of those homes.

Many landlords agree that old Labour's fair rent formula was intrinsically fair. It provided that a fair rent constituted a market rent that would prevail for the relevant property in circumstances in which demand did not substantially exceed supply. Col. 4 Therefore, it was a genuine market rent—as someone famously said, "You can't buck the market"—but it factored out the scarcity element in a specific area. The tenant was protected against the scarcity premium and the landlord received a fair return on the inherent value of his investment.

The British Property Federation and other landlords' organisations are genuinely apprehensive that the order will have an impact on capital values, cash flow, and therefore, the ability to fund repairs and improvements. It cannot be sensible to widen the gap between market and regulated rents. That differential has narrowed recently. The British Property Federation estimates that, using the capping formula in the order and assuming a constant retail price index of 2.5 per cent, per annum, it will take approximately 24 years for market and regulated rents to converge. Moreover, if market rents show any sign of moving forward, the gap will start to widen again.

Approximately 5 million fair rent determinations have been made since a Labour Government introduced the formula in 1965. It is estimated that regulated tenants have benefited at the expense of landlords to the tune of £100 billion. Indeed, quite a number of registered fair rents still have a long way to go to achieve the levels envisaged under the previous formula.

The Minister may seek to rely on some recent court decisions in order to justify this unhappy measure. A reference document produced on 17th June 1997 and updated on 11th January 1999 by the social policy section of the Library states:

"In the case of Spath Holme Ltd v Greater Manchester and Lancashire Rent Assessment Committee (1995) the landlord successfully argued that, as the company was offering similar properties in the same block at much higher assured tenancy rents, those of regulated fair rent tenants should be allowed to rise to levels higher than allowed by the RAC. The Court of Appeal held that the tenancies were similar and that scarcity of rented accommodation was not a factor in this case."

At the time, my hon. Friend the Member for Hertsmere (Mr. Clappison), who was the Minister responsible, made it clear that the then Conservative Government rejected calls to intervene to stop changes in the light of the Spath Holme case.

That case was followed in October 1997 by another Court of Appeal decision in Curtis v. London Rent Assessment Committee. There are various issues in that case that do not concern us today, but the essential point—as set out in the reference document to which I referred earlier—is that the Court of Appeal

"reaffirmed the principle established in the Spath Holme judgment that the starting point for a determination of a fair rent under section 70 of the 1977 Rent Act could only be the market rent, adjusted for scarcity."

Those decisions do no more than confirm the market rent approach, and that is also endorsed by the Institute of Rent Officers and Rental Valuers. What is more, rents declined sharply in the 1970s and 1980s—so much so that some fair rents do not even cover the cost of maintenance. What has happened since has been an inevitable market correction. The National Image Col. 5 Trust pointed out in its response to the consultation paper that a large catching-up operation had been taking place.

There is evidence from the BPF and elsewhere that because fair rents are reviewed on a two-year cycle, the majority of rents have already been reviewed, or are in the process of being reviewed, in the light of those judgments. Does the Minister agree with the view expressed by the Institute of Rent Officers and Rental Valuers in their response to the consultation document, that to cap increases now appears to be shutting the stable door after the horse has bolted?

The Government should not be allowed to portray the issue as one between wicked rapacious landlords and poor downtrodden tenants. Apart from the substantial and responsible companies that are members of the BPF, there are also small landlords who can ill afford the Labour Government's brand of social engineering. I am indebted to my hon. Friend the Member for Beckenham (Mrs. Lait) for giving me an example, in a letter from one of her constituents, who writes:

"Dear Mrs. Lait, I am writing on behalf of my 83 year old mother who purchased and then rented out a flat 35 years ago to a tenant who then became a 'sitting regulated tenant'."

She goes on:

"Because the rent my mother receives does not even cover the cost of repairs and maintenance to the flat she has tried to sell it, but because of the tenant she would have to sell the flat for half of its market value which doesn't seem very 'fair'."

She then goes on to say that she and her husband have an elderly parent, who is

"paying extra to live in sheltered accommodation, paying for carers, home helps etc."

while at the same time subsidising the tenant. She concludes:

"To impose these further restrictions on this rent and what is rightfully hers is surely immoral and wrong."

There are also major constitutional issues that I would like the Minister to deal with seriously in her reply. First, the measure is, in effect, retrospective legislation. It is a fundamental principle of natural justice in this country that that should be avoided wherever possible.

Secondly, the order came into force on 1 February. Its effect, in simple terms, is to insert a new paragraph into schedule 11 of the Rent Act 1977. I question the constitutional propriety of using secondary powers to amend primary legislation in that way. Indeed, the Minister for London and Construction, has been positively brazen on the subject. Speaking in the House on 26 January 1998, he said:

"A more attractive option, which may be possible through secondary legislation, is the application of a maximum limit to the size of the rent increase".

He made it clear that that aim would require primary legislation. He said:

"in view of the many other pressures on parliamentary time, any opportunity for that would be likely to lie some way ahead."—[Official Report, 26 January 1998; Vol. 305, c. 125.]

So the Government are following their usual fashion of going round the other way. On what precedent is the Minister relying for that constitutional outrage?

Col. 6 Does the Minister accept that a consequence of the measure may be a reversal of improvements in recent years in the private rented sector? Why did the Government ignore the views of the supply side of the industry in the consultation process and disregard sensible proposals for less damaging options?

Is not the Government's real motive for introducing the measure to save on housing benefit payments? Does the Minister accept that the cap might have perverse effects, such as protecting wealthy tenants with, perhaps, two homes and large incomes, as well as tenants receiving housing benefit, whose full rent is paid by the state? Does the Minister have any proposals to compensate landlords who are unfairly treated by the order? Does an application for a new registration made on 1 February escape the cap? The formula includes the figures of 7.5 per cent, and 5 per cent. Why are those fixed amounts, no matter how long the interval between registrations?

Under the primary legislation, regulated tenants can enter into rental agreements with landlords to pay more realistic rents. Will such agreements be overridden by the cap in the order?

Does the minister accept that, under the order, the retail prices index increase will be two months short? That is because the RPI figure published in any month gives the RPI two months earlier. So the published RPI figure for the months of the new registration will be used, as opposed to the actual RPI figure for the month of the previous registration.

Why is Scotland, apparently, not to have the benefit of a similar measure? Finally, will the Minister give an absolute commitment that the Government will not seek to extend such limits—or ones similar to them—to assured and assured shorthold tenancies that currently allow landlords to charge open market rents?

I hope that the Minister will respond to my questions. I would be content, on one or two of them, if she would write to me after the Committee has finished sitting. Conservative Members have important concerns about the order, which has not been thought through. Its ultimate effect may be to reduce the supply of decent rented accommodation. I will not press for a Division, but that is subject to the Minister's comments.

10.42 am

The Minister for Local Government and Housing (Ms Hilary Armstrong): The hon. Gentleman raised a number of issues, and voiced concerns that the British Property Federation has also expressed.

I hope to reassure Committee members of the Government's commitment to a healthy private rented sector. However, we also wish to achieve the right balance for those who are in regulated tenancies. I therefore welcome the opportunity to explain to the Committee the steps that we have taken to limit rent increases for fair rent tentants.

The Government are totally committed to providing a private rented sector that gives real choice to people seeking housing. There is a clear demand for privately rented accommodation, and estimates for household growth indicate that that demand will continue. We Image Col. 7 are, therefore, committed to a policy framework for the sector which encourages new investment by responsible landlords. However that does not mean that we can ignore the present problems in the sector, particularly where those affect the most vulnerable tenants. Not all vulnerable tenants are on housing benefit. That is one reason why the higher rent increases have produced so much concern among regulated tenants.

Some commentators, as well as the hon. Gentleman, have expressed fears that controls on fair rent increases will be seen as a prelude to wider rent controls, and that that will discourage new investment in the private rented market. Let me lay those fears to rest. The order addresses a transitional problem in a small and declining part of the rental market sector. The number of tenancies affected by the new limits on fair increases is small—some 120,000 private tenancies and some 235,000 secure tenancies of registered social landlords.

The limits will not apply to lettings made after 15 January 1989; that is the deregulated sector which now accounts for 90 per cent, of all private lettings. They will not apply because landlords and tenants were aware when the Housing Act 1988 was introduced that market rents would be charged for new lettings and that rent increases would follow market trends.

We have given repeated assurances that we have no plans to change the legal framework for deregulated private tenancies and I am happy to repeat those assurances today. I am pleased to say that there are signs that the message is getting across. Research published by the British Property Federation last year, the survey to which the hon. Gentleman referred, suggests that those institutions looking to invest in the sector were much less concerned about our proposals than those not looking to invest. In other words, those who were in touch with the Government's intentions and listening to what we were saying recognised that this was not an extension to all tenancies.

Sir Michael Spicer (West Worcestershire): Can the Minister say how many private sector properties are available to let?

Ms Armstrong: I do not have the figures with me this morning. I am happy to let the hon. Gentleman have them. This order would not affect any of them because if they were to be let, subsequent to the order, they would be let under the Housing Act 1988 and, therefore, would be part of the deregulated sector.

It may be helpful if I explain why fair rent tenants have been facing exceptionally high rent increases in recent years, why we felt it appropriate to intervene to moderate increases in those particular and limited circumstances and how the new limit will operate. When the Housing Act 1988 introduced market rents for all new lettings, the right of existing regulated tenants to a fair rent was not changed. Those tenants and their landlords may still apply to have a fair rent registered for the tenancy every two years by an independent rent officer. They can appeal against the rent officer's decision to a rent assessment committee.

Col. 8 Indeed, that answers one of the points that the hon. Member for Eastbourne (Mr. Waterson) raised. Where rent levels are agreed privately and not registered they will not come within the terms of the order. When setting fair rents, rent officers and committees are required by the Rent Act 1977 to take into account the age, character, locality and state of repair of the property, but to disregard any premium resulting from a scarcity of similar accommodation in the locality. The principle was first established long before that—in 1965.

In the past, rent officers tended to decide rents by referral to fair rents previously determined for other comparable properties in a locality. Before 1989, when no evidence on market rents was available, that was the only practical approach. More recently, a number of landmark court cases have held that rent officers and committees should give more weight to market rents for comparable properties now that there is a growing volume of open-market evidence.

The starting point should generally be the comparable market rent, which is then adjusted to take account of scarcity. Changing to this method caused fair rents to rise steeply in recent years. Fair rent increases have been well above increases in the retail prices index over the same period. Many tenants have faced rent increases on reregistration that were at least 10 percentage points highter than the increase in the retail prices index in the same period. In Merseyside, the average increase on reregistration was recently about 45 per cent.; in London, some tenants faced rent increases of 60 per cent, or more in two years. For example, a tenant in Hammersmith and Fulham had a rent increase of 65 per cent, in 1998, which took the rent to £7,750 per annum from £4,700 per annum in 1996. We all agree that it is the amount of increase that has proved a problem for some tenants. I have had representations about this from colleagues on both sides of the House.

The previous Government introduced market rents for new lettings in 1989, but left intact the fair rent provision for existing tenants. We do not believe that at that time anyone could reasonably have expected rent increases of this magnitude under a fair rents system. Indeed, the right hon. Member for Louth and Horncastle (Sir P. Tapsell), who wrote to me last April, said:

"This seems to be a situation in which the courts have in effect overridden the declared aim of Parliament in passing the fair rent legislation and have changed the policies of the previous Government as well as the present one … it cannot make sense for rent restriction to be abandoned to this extent."

It is true that many landlords did not acquire their tenanted properties as speculative investments, or indeed, in some cases, acquire them for investment reasons at all. They may have inherited a property, built it, acquired it to house employees, or purchased it as part of a much wider property portfolio. However, that does not alter the fact that acquisition will have taken place against the background of the fair rent controls as they were understood and expected to work at the time. The idea that growth in rental income might massively exceed inflation will not have occurred to these landlords when they acquired Image Col. 9 their properties. Therefore, given the problems being reported to us, we felt that there was a strong case for Government intervention.

The order does not change the way in which fair rents are determined under the Rent Act 1977. It simply limits exceptionally high fair rent increases under the Act. Linking rent increases to the change in the retail prices index means that they will follow the change of market rents. In addition, in recognition of fair rent increases being artificially low in the early 1990s, we have allowed a further uplift of 7.5 per cent above RPI for the first application made after the order comes into force and 5 per cent, for subsequent applications. Ultimately—except in the most extreme cases—this will allow fair rents to reach the limit that they would have reached had we not introduced a limit, but at a slower rate.

There are two exemptions from the limit. It will not apply if there is no existing fair rent registration for the property. We also felt that there should be an incentive for landlords to carry out major works to the property. The order therefore exempts landlords from the limit where a substantial rent increase—which we have defined as a rent increase of at least 15 per cent.—is due because of an improvement in the property's condition resulting from repairs or improvements by the landlord.

Mr. Waterson: Does the Minister accept that an effect of the order may be to widen the gap again between regulated and market rents? If so, does she consider that a good thing?

Ms Armstrong: I have made it clear that the order will slow down movement towards direct comparability between the two. However, any Government must consider the interests of the entire group affected by an order. We cannot consider only the landlord's interest. We must consider what the law assumed, and what people assumed that the law meant, when the new regulations came into play, and the effect that changes in the market have had on those assumptions.

We do not accept that the measures override the principle of a fair rent. Most landlords and tenants could never have antcipated increases of the magnitude that have occurred in some parts of the country under fair rent legislation. It has been suggested that the order introduces a new element of affordability into the fair rent system and requires the rent officer to take account for the first time of a tenant's personal circumstances. It does no such thing. The provisions apply uniformly to all tenants, irrespective of their personal circumstances. In many cases, the fair rent will continue to be determined exactly as before under the original Rent Act principles. It is only in cases where the increase in fair rent would otherwise have outstripped inflation by a generous margin that the order will slow the increase to a level that is manageable for the tenants and consistent with what would until very recently have been the high point of the landlord's reasonable expectations.

Col. 10 The hon. Member for Eastbourne asked how the Government justified the measure as retrospective legislation. It is not retrospective. It applies only to applications for rent registration made to a rent officer on or after 1 February, the date when the order came into effect. He also asked why the order did not apply to Scotland. It relates to limits to fair rents in England and Wales. The fair rent provisions in Scotland are broadly similar, but fair rent increases are generally significantly lower there. There are therefore no plans to introduce a limit in Scotland, but my colleagues will keep the relevant matters under review and act if necessary.

The hon. Gentleman also expressed constitutional outrage—which I thought was a bit over the top—because the measure was being passed through secondary legislation.

Mr. Waterson: I have got over it now.

Ms Armstrong: Good. The order indeed modifies primary legislation. It modifies schedule 11 to the Rent Act 1977 in respect of those applications for registration of a new rent to which the order applies. Section 31 of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, under which the order is made, provides the Secretary of State with the power to include within the order

"provisions excluding, adapting or modifying any provision made by or under any enactment (whenever passed) relating to rent."

That provision in the 1985 Act was subject to parliamentary scrutiny during its passage through both Houses, though I accept that neither the hon. Gentleman nor I were Members of Parliament then.

I think that the hon. Member for Eastbourne referred to the European convention on human rights—

Mr. Waterson: No.

Ms Armstrong: In that case I shall not mention it. I do not want to detain the Committee longer than I need to.

In summary, the order is designed simply to ensure that the protection that has long been afforded to regulated tenants works as it was intended and expected to do. The important point is that it is a limited measure to address a small and transitional problem. It is not, I stress again, the prelude to wider rent control. The vast majority of tenancies are, and will continue to be, unaffected by the order. I do not expect Conservative Members to go as far as the hon. Member for Louth and Horncastle who in a letter to me in January, declared the measures "eminently fair and reasonable". However, I hope that they will agree, on the basis of what I have said, to let the order stand.

Ms Armstrong: I reject what the hon. Member for West Worcestershire (Sir M. Spicer) said. The order is small and specific; it is designed to solve a particular problem. I repeat what I said earlier.
