Mr. Howard Flight
(Arundel and South Downs)
: I was talking about whether people with stakeholder pensions will be obliged to buy annuities on retirement. I particularly wanted to point out that the Revenue argument against abolishing the requirement to buy annuities is bogus. Its argument is that pension fund contributions enjoy the benefit of being tax free and that anything other than an annuity would damage the Revenue.

If one considers what already happens for those who operate draw downs for people up to the age of 75, it will be seen that the position for the Revenue is broadly tax neutral. There may be pluses and minuses but, to the extent that funds are drawn down, they are taxed as income. In the event that the surviving spouse of an individual dies, leaving funds in a pension scheme, those funds are then taxed at death at, I believe, the rate of 35 per cent. They could just as easily be added to an individual's estate or taxed at a standard probate rate.

The argument that moving to drawdown arrangements would result in a loss of revenue is false. In the present climate in particular, drawdown is likely to benefit pensioners and stakeholder pensioners. It permits them to enjoy the improved capital returns from equity investment, which are likely over time to continue to be significantly better than fixed-interest investment, which underlies the standard annuity. More particularly, the option of when an annuity may be purchased protects a whole generation of 146 stakeholder pensioners from potentially being forced into buying annuities at unfavourable times and subsequently experiencing significant inflation.

Equity-linked with-profits annuity schemes are at present unattractive to stakeholder pensioners because of the rule that not more than 3 per cent. above the laid-down amount can be distributed. In the event that a return of more than that amount is achieved, it can be deferred to years when equity returns are less favourable, but any accumulated surplus on death reverts to the insurance fund and not to the estate, spouse or heir of the survivor. That discourages many people from using equity-linked with-profits annuities.

My main point is that the Government are clearly set on the money purchase route for stakeholder pensions. I do not see why an option in the scheme could not be flexible enough to allow final salary-linked pensions, especially if companies were willing to offer that option. They would give pensioners much more security and they would be better able to know what their pension might be. If that is not to be permitted, it is essential that it is not a requirement that an annuity is bought at retirement.

In addition, the general requirement that applies to certain personal pension schemes that an annuity has to be purchased at the age of 75 should be reconsidered in the light of current pensioners' wishes and what stakeholder pensioners will want.
