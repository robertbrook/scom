Mental Health Act 1983 Code of Practice

10.30 am

Mr. Philip Hammond
(Runnymede and Weybridge)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Mental Health Act 1983 Code of Practice.

It is a pleasure, Mr. Cook, to serve in a Committee under your Chairmanship. I hope that our proceedings this morning will be enlightening and interesting.

The code of practice does not have statutory force, but it has a powerful influence on the practice followed in our mental health institutions. Because the code can be cited in legal actions—for example in cases relating to claimed negligence—it is very important. The Opposition understand the difficulties of achieving a balance between the interests of patients, the public and the professionals involved in treating patients. We are not attempting to take a sledgehammer to the code of practice, which in many respects is widely welcomed by all those concerned with mental health.

However, the code has provoked some concerns. The Opposition's duty is to subject such measures to proper scrutiny. Our intention today is not necessarily hostile to the Government or their proposals but stems from the belief that, where the rights of vulnerable people are affected, it is right and proper that Parliament should scrutinise the regulations and that it would be failing in its duty if it did not probe the intentions behind the code as well as its practical implications and potential weaknesses.

We are especially concerned about the consultation process. The code of practice has been out to consultation for more than two years. One might think that all interested parties would therefore have had adequate opportunity to present their views, but in two important areas—the provisions for random searching of patients and visits by children to patients in psychiatric hospitals—the code of practice incorporates wording inserted late in the proceedings, and certainly after representations had been made by a number of interested and qualified bodies. That betrays an arrogance in the Government's approach to this matter that we have detected elsewhere.

I understand that, until now, the code of practice with its regular revisions has always been accepted as a consensus document, arrived at after widespread consultation. It is therefore unfortunate that at least two of the major institutions representing patients' interests are now on the warpath over the insertion of provisions after the consultation procedure. I shall deal with that more fully later.

4 There have been many stylistic changes in the code and its organisation. Chapter 1 now helpfully summarises the guiding principles underlying the code. Perhaps less helpfully for a Government who insist that there is no rationing in the national health service, the first page of chapter 1 also contains the most explicit statement on rationing in the NHS that I have seen. It states that patients assessed for admission should

"have their needs taken fully into account, though it is recognised that, within available resources, it may not always be practicable to meet them in full".

That may seem an uncontroversial, rather commonsense statement, but it sits uncomfortably with that made in the House before Christmas by the Minister for Public Health, the right hon. Member for Dulwich and West Norwood (Ms Jowell), who told my right hon. Friend the Member for Maidstone and The Weald (Miss Widdecombe) that there was no rationing in the national health service.

The question of rationing interfaces with that of compulsory treatment orders, to which I shall return. In a regime where the most appropriate drugs are rationed—for example, the use of atypical anti-psychotic drugs for the treatment of schizophrenia—it is important that all available best treatments are offered to patients, regardless of cost, before comtemplating the constraining of their freedom through measures such as compulsory treatment orders.

An explicit debate is needed on rationing, not just in mental health but throughout the NHS. Nevertheless, practising mental health clinicians, with whom I spoke in preparation for this morning's debate, expressed the deep-rooted feeling—of which I am sure the Minister is aware—that mental health is a Cinderella service, notwithstanding the Government's reassuring words. A director of one mental health trust said that we should contrast the provision of expensive treatment for HIV with less expensive but heavily rationed treatments for schizophrenia. Such contrasts inevitably lead those working in mental health to feel that, in some respects, they are bearing the brunt of rationing.

We are also concerned by the absence of proposals for a new mental health Act. Before the general election, it was widely understood across the mental health community that a Labour Government would be committed to introducing a new mental health Act during this Parliament. It now appears that one will not be introduced, although I understand that consultations are taking place. This code and the new guidance that it offers will therefore be regarded, to some extent, as a short-term substitute for a new mental health Act. Clinicians and practitioners will look to the code for guidance on recent areas of concern, such as the Bournewood judgment, to which I shall refer in a moment.

Before dealing with the substantive issues raised by the code, I wish to make two minor, technical points about its drafting. In future, when producing such documents—this one is 110 pages long—will the Minister ensure that the Department produces a JPEG 5 highlighted version, with any changes printed in italics? That is common practice in, for example, the legal profession, where draft contracts are passed back and forth between parties. Highlighting makes it easier for someone who is not familiar with the detail of the documents to locate important changes.

I should like to raise another minor point. Can the Minister clarify what paragraph 20.9 is intended to say? A typographical error in the first line of that paragraph makes it impossible to understand its precise meaning.

In addressing the substance of the code, I shall attempt to adopt a chronological approach, although not all the issues that I wish to raise fall neatly into the code's existing chapter structure. The first matter relates to compliant incapacitated patients. Those are not voluntary patients; they have no mental capacity to consent to treatment, but are deemed from their behaviour not have to dissented from treatment.

The issue was highlighted by the Bournewood judgment. The Minister will know—at least I hope that he knows—that I have a personal interest in the Bournewood judgment because the Bournewood community and mental health trust provides services in my constituency. Patient "L", on whose behalf the legal action was taken, is my constituent. The judgment relates to the scope of section 131 of the Mental Health Act 1983. When the judgment was received, the Minister's predecessor, the hon. Member for Brent, South (Mr. Boateng), expressed concern that it could result in a large number of patients who are currently admitted informally having to be subject to formal admission processes.

To put that into context, at any one time about 13,000 patients in the system are formally detained. If the initial judgment in the Bournewood case, which stated that those patients would have to be formally detained, was allowed to stand, it was estimated that figure would rise to approximately 35,000—a startling increase. It would have added 48,000 admissions a year to the number that had to be processed. It is clear that that would have created a substantial increase in the work load of people working in the mental health sector.

The case went to the House of Lords, which, much to the relief, I suspect, of Ministers, determined that informal admission procedures could continue and that it would not be necessary formally to admit all those patients. However, the concerns that were raised in the case of patient "L" have not gone away. The gist of what Lord Justice Steyn said at the time of the judgment, which he considered carefully, was that those patients who would continue to be admitted informally would continue not to benefit from any of the protections provided by the Mental Health Act 1983. In particular, they would continue not to have the right to apply for, or to be automatically referred to, mental health review tribunals in accordance with the provisions of part V of that Act.

Lord Steyn said:

"Given that such patients are diagnostically indistinguishable from compulsory patients, there is no reason to withhold the specific and effective protections of the Act of 1983 from a large class of vulnerable mentally incapacitated individuals. Their 6 moral right to be treated with dignity requires nothing less. The only comfort is that counsel for the Secretary of State has assured the House that reform of the law is under active consideration."

It will be some years before a revised mental health Act is introduced, and we had hoped that the Government might have sought to deal in the code of practice with the matters that were raised in the Bournewood judgment. That would ensure that patients who are informally detained benefit—as far as is practicable—from the rights that formally detained patients have under the Mental Health Act 1983.

Chapter 10 of the code of practice relates to the powers of the police to remove people to a place of safety. I have no difficulties in theory with that chapter, but I want to raise some practical difficulties, which underlie a problem that runs throughout the code, namely, that it relates to an ideal world. The code raises matters without referring to the real constraints that practitioners face on the ground. Reality is different from the situation in the code.

Paragraph 10.5 suggests that hospitals should be preferred over police stations as a place of safety. No one would disagree that that is an ideal situation—patients should be taken to hospitals rather than police stations as a place of safety. However, practitioners with whom I have spoken are concerned about the resource implications of delivering straight to a hospital patients who have hitherto been kept in a police station as a place of safety. They are also worried about the associated diversion from treatments of patients who are already in that hospital. Will further resources be made available to hospitals to cope with that additional work load?

That leads me on to paragraph 10.3, which refers to the responsibility of police officers to "remain in attendance", where appropriate, and where the protection of others so requires,

"when the patient is taken to a place of safety other than a police station".

Once again, that clearly has substantial resource implications. When a patient is taken to a police station, police officers are, by definition, available if needed. If the patient is taken to a hospital, good practice suggests, and the code recommends, that where it is appropriate—for reasons relating to the protection of the safety of any persons—police officers should remain in attendance. We all know the truth—the police are over-stretched and under-resourced. I am told by clinicians that, in practice, police officers normally remain at the hospital for only a short time—and one can fully appreciate their reasons for doing so. Has the Home Secretary had discussions about making available additional resources that will help the police to discharge the duties placed on them by paragraph 10.3? If the code gives guidance that is theoretical and cannot be implemented in practice, that could leave a damaging lacuna. If the guidance in the code cannot be implemented, clinicians will have to fall back on their own ingenuity, which is potentially dangerous.

Chapter 14 of the previous code stated:

"all patients should be given, throughout their stay in hospital, as much information as possible about their care and treatment".

JPEG 7 That seems rather unexceptional—it represents good practice, and respects the dignity of patients and their right to be informed. Those words were removed from chapter 14 of the present code, and I should appreciate the Minister's explanation.

Paragraphs 2, 9 and 10 of chapter 16 deal with treatment and the requirement for consent or a second opinion, in the context of electro-convulsive therapy. Various groups expected the code of practice to incorporate, by reference, the Royal College of Psychiatrists' guidelines on the administration of electro-convulsive therapy. Its 1997 report found that there was poor practice in two thirds of ECT clinics, including badly trained staff and the use of obsolete equipment.

There are serious concerns about electro-convulsive therapy. It is a contraversial treatment and there is not universal agreement on its effectiveness. Policy varies widely across health authorities, and it would appear that it is not always used as a genuine last-resort treatment. At present, for a patient to receive ECT, either the patient must consent or there must be a second clinical opinion. That is to say, the treatment is administered under section 58 of the Mental Health Act 1983. Will the Minister comment on suggestions that it should be moved from section 58 to section 57 of the Act, which provides that both consent and a second opinion are required before ECT can be given? Will he explain what the involvement of the National Institute for Clinical Excellence will be establishing uniformity in mental health treatment generally and ECT in particular?

Chapter 19 deals with the management of difficult patients. May I cite a small example of a creeping use of politically correct and, in some cases, wholly meaningless language in this document? I refer Committee members to paragraph 19.12, which refers to restraint of patients. An otherwise sensible paragraph concludes by saying that any restraint used should be

"sensitive to gender and race issues."

I understand how physical restraint of a patient might need to be sensitive to gender issues, but I have racked my brains to think how physical restraint could be sensitive to race issues or what race issues it could raise. Is there any substance to that, or is it simply a nod in the direction of political correctness?

On a more substantive point, chapter 19 contains no reference to the use of CS spray. The Minister will be aware that CS spray is increasingly used by the police, either when conveying difficult mentally ill patients to hospital or when they are called into psychiatric hospitals to deal with management problems. Serious questions remain over the interaction of CS gas with psychoactive medication. I am not necessarily critical of the use of CS gas; I understand that it is an important tool in the armoury of the police. I know, from talking to police officers, how difficult and frightening it can be to have to restrain seriously mentally ill patients, but given that this issue raises 8 such widespread public concern, I am astonished that the code of practice contains no reference to the use of CS gas.

I also want to address the question of mixed wards and sexual harassment. The Minister will know that my hon. Friend the Member for New Forest, East (Dr. Lewis) has run something of a campaign on the issue of mixed wards in psychiatric hospitals. I was surprised when I looked at the code to find only one passing reference to the segregation of patients by gender, and certainly nothing that could be described as prescriptive guidance to that effect. That contrasts sharply with the Secretary of State's apparently strong line on this issue in responding to my hon. Friend the Member for New Forest, East when he has raised these matters in the House. Can the Minister explain why the code contains no clear guidance to trusts on phasing out mixed accommodation as soon as possible?

Some hospitals have written policies on dealing with sexual harassment complaints and when to involve the police in such complaints, but, rightly or wrongly, there is a perception in the mental health community that not all such allegations from patients in psychiatric hospitals are treated with appropriate seriousness. Will the Minister consider whether all hospitals should be required to have a written policy on dealing with sexual harassment complaints and whether that should be made explicit in chapter 19?

That brings me to the two most controversial and important chapters of the code, chapters 25 and 26. Those chapters have caused most concern to the bodies interested in these matters, not least because that is where the consultation process has broken down most significantly.

Chapter 25 deals with the powers to search patients. The code of practice permits all hospitals admitting patients under the Mental Health Act to search patients at random. This guidance, which appears to have resulted from a case relating to Broadmoor hospital, is outlined in paragraphs 2 and 3. What the Opposition and many outside object to is an apparent generalisation to all psychiatric hospitals of what is considered reasonable and appropriate in special hospitals. It can be argued that generalised search policies upset the therapeutic balance in a hospital, and the code does not appear to rule out the searching of patients admitted voluntarily. Will the Minister clarify that point?

The code of practice acknowledges that there is a conflict between the maintenance of a therapeutic environment and the maintenance of security in the hospital, but it also acknowledges that random searches are required only where patients have violent or criminal propensities. It therefore appears to recognise that the measure is suited only to special hospitals and it calls into question why the code appears to extend that to all psychiatric hospitals.

The wording in chapter 25.3,

"The policy may extend to routine and random searching without cause",

JPEG 9 is offensive to many people. Although it is understood that random searching may be required in some cases, to say that it should be "without cause" seems to invite entirely arbitrary searching, which can never be helpful.

Chapter 26, which is probably the most controversial aspect of the code of practice, deals with visits by children to patients in psychiatric hospitals. The code of practice states:

"A visit by a child should only take place following a decision that such a visit would be in the child's best interests."

That mirrors the terminology of the Children Act 1989. We fully accept that the interests of the child must be paramount where there is any concern about the child's safety or security. However, the code of practice shifts the burden of proof. It makes the automatic assumption that all psychiatric patients are dangerous to children. It starts from the premise that no visits should be allowed unless a positive decision has been taken that such a visit would be in the child's best interests.

When this chapter went out for consultation, it did not include all the following—critical—words:

"A visit by a child should only take place following a decision that such a visit would be in the child's best interests. Decisions to allow such visits should be regularly reviewed."

Something that is so obviously controversial should not have been inserted into the code of practice at the last minute after lengthy and widespread consultation. Clearly, the wording follows the Secretary of State's guidance on high security special hospitals, which was issued in the light of the Ashworth case. We all accept the need for careful provisions to ensure that the terrible events that took place at Ashworth are not repeated, but that does not justify treating all patients in psychiatric hospitals in the same manner as patients who are in special hospitals because they are a danger to others.

Only about 15 per cent. of formal admissions to all psychiatric hospitals under the Mental Health Act are criminal sections, and they are usually admitted to special hospitals. It is highly inappropriate that the chapter should suggest that the remaining 85 per cent. of patients pose a risk to children. Indeed, the Prison Service rules for children's visits clearly state that account must be taken of the prisoner's background and offence when deciding whether a child's visit is appropriate. The code of practice does not give the same scope for considering the particular circumstances of the patient in question, such as the nature of his illness and his history. The code of practice discriminates unreasonably against mental health patients and puts them in a worse position than people who have been convicted of criminal offences and who are in prison subject to Prison Service rules.

It is also worth noting that this chapter gives no guidance on who should decide to permit the visit of a child or how the decision should be made. Giving such a large amount of discretion to individual hospitals and social workers is likely to lead to regional disparities and unfairness.

10 Should not the code of practice distinguish between special hospitals and other psychiatric units? Should not it be assumed in the case of the latter that there is no reason to start from the premise that children would be in danger by visiting a patient unless the patient's history—diagnosis and other surrounding circumstances—indicates that? Should not there be procedures for reviewing a decision not to allow visits to take place? The chapter presumes that visits will be allowed only in exceptional circumstances. Will the Minister deal with the concerns that the code's provisions may contravene article 8 of the European convention on human rights, which is mentioned in chapter 1 of the code? Article 8 states that everyone has the right to respect for his private and family life, although that can be restricted on grounds of public safety or the prevention of crime. Many commentators doubt whether a blanket ban is consistent with article 8.

The Minister will be aware that the public and the press have expressed concerns recently about patients who suffer from personality disorders, and questions have been raised in the House about the admissibility of personality-disordered patients for treatment to psychiatric hospitals. How will the code deal with that topical and serious matter? What guidance does it offer to clinicians to help them to deal with the admissibility of personality-disordered patients? While the Minister is at it, will he also acknowledge the need for an open debate on that matter before we proceed with other measures, including the possibility of introducing renewable detention orders?

I am sorry to have taken up so much of the Committee's time, Mr. Cook, but I hope that the Minister will deal with all the matters that I raised. I hope that he will deal not simply with the high-profile issues of searching and child visits, which have attracted much publicity, but with the less dramatic but equally important matters that I identified. It is our duty, on behalf of the vulnerable people whose lives will be affected by the code, to bring all those matters into the open and to air them in the Committee.

Dr. Evan Harris
(Oxford, West and Abingdon)
: I must say how pleased I am to serve on this Committee under your chairmanship, Mr. Cook. I share some of the concerns raised by the hon. Member for Runnymede and Weybridge (Mr. Hammond), but I hope to be more positive than he was about some of the changes that have been made in the code of practice.

I welcome many of the changes that have been made in the code, including, for example, greater clarity in the role and functions of hospital managers in the discharge of patients. Concerns that were raised in the Glen Grant case and in the Huzzey case have been taken on board. Perhaps in contrast to the hon. Member for Runnymede and Weybridge, I also welcome explicit recognition of the fact that gender and race issues have to be considered in relation to the management of patients. In view of recent cases, it should not be hard for Committee members to appreciate why racial sensitivities must be borne in JPEG 11 mind in questions of restraint. It is appropriate to mention that matter explicitly in the guidelines, because they lead to professional and legal decisions about the appropriateness of care.

I also welcome the root-and-branch review of the Mental Health Act 1983 which the Government are undertaking. I share little of the hon. Gentleman's confusion about the Government's legislative intentions. I imagine that their review is based on a presumption that some changes in the legislation are needed, and I appreciate the fact that the Government have a crowded parliamentary agenda. However, in a meeting about 12 months ago I was disappointed when the Minister's predecessor said that he ruled out absolutely making any changes to the 1983 Act during this Parliament. I hope that the Government will keep an open mind on that matter, that the review will be full and that it will recognise the relevant matters that need to be discussed.

I share the hon. Gentleman's concerns about late additions. I, too, noticed the remarkable statement in chapter 1 that people to whom the Act applies should

"have their needs taken fully into account, though it is recognised that, within available resources, it may not always be practicable to meet them in full".

That is a welcome admission of a reality, but the Government do not necessarily support it. Indeed, they have repeatedly said that it is a reality that they inherited. The shortage of beds for mentally ill patients may not be as obvious to the media as the shortage of intensive care beds. Critically ill patients do not bear the stigma associated with mental illness. Unlike the mentally ill, they are rarely transferred to hospitals that are miles away from their families, although some cases have recently come to light.

The guidelines refer to the duties incumbent on other people, but the Government have a duty to ensure that mental health gets the recognition that it deserves and is no longer treated as a Cinderella specialty. They must also recognise the importance of bed numbers in local areas, so that mentally ill patients have the support that they need and families can visit them.

The Government have stated their ambition to put more resources into mental health and to establish frameworks that will raise the quality of care throughout the country. The matter is now urgent. Further resources must be provided for mental health so that the rationing to which chapter 1 refers is reduced as much as possible. Similar funding must also be provided for social services. Funding should not be taken from one area to provide for another, as has happened in my constituency. That would be a false economy.

Where rationing occurs, it should be based on the ability to benefit from the treatment and related to the severity of the condition. Does the Minister agree that the decision to treat should not be based on the cause of the signs and symptoms at presentation? Other drugs—certainly, one other drug to which recent attention has been paid—have been rationed 12 according to cause rather than to the severity of the condition or the ability to benefit from the treatment. That is wrong and unethical practice. Where two patients with illnesses that have arisen from different causes might benefit equally from a particular treatment, I, as a doctor, would not be willing to decide who deserved treatment on the basis of the relative political correctness of the causes. The Minister must take this opportunity to make it clear that such things will not happen.

I agree with the comments of the hon. Member for Runnymede and Weybridge on electro-convulsive therapy, and I shall not therefore detain the Committee by adding to them. I also agree with his comments about the use of CS gas. He made some important points in relation to the Bournewood case. Clearly, the House of Lords' ruling that overturned the Court of Appeal hearing relieved the Government of a large resourcing problem. I shall not comment on the merits of that ruling, but it highlighted whether patients who are mentally incapable of giving consent have the same level of protection as those who are formally detained under the Mental Health Act.

As far as I am aware, prisons constitute a different case. The physicians who were looking after a constituent of mine who was starving himself in prison found it extremely difficult to get him transferred to a hospital, despite the fact that he had become physically ill, simply because a psychiatrist deemed him not to be mentally ill at that point. The division of medical services between prisons and hospitals is a continuing problem and there is a considerable amount of mental illness in prisons. If prison medical services are brought under the NHS, the problem can be solved and consideration of the rights of the mentally ill in prisons can be brought up to scratch. Many hon. Members will be aware of cases in their constituencies that have raised concerns about the consideration awarded to mentally ill prisoners.

I also associate myself with what the hon. Member for Runnymede and Weybridge said about personal searches. Although I shall not detain the Committee by adding to that, I should like to comment on one of his major concerns—the issue of child visits. The hon. Gentleman and I received some of the same briefings, and I am especially grateful for the information provided by MIND; Schizophrenia—A National Emergency, known as SANE; the National Schizophrenia Fellowship; and the Mental After Care Association, which were all worried about children's visits. Those organisations do not always concur; they often have different perspectives.

We are concerned not only about civil liberties and the rights and consideration of the interests of people detained, but about the stigma that continues to be attached to mentally ill patients. The media are obsessed with the more extreme cases of people who may be a danger to the general public, but it must be stressed time and again—Ministers do not do this enough in public—that that applies to only a small minority of mentally ill patients. According to the latest available research, the risk to the public has reduced despite the problems caused by the JPEG 13 underfunding of community care. It is sad not only that the Secretary of State makes statements that cannot be supported by the evidence, but that such statements give rise to the stigma that community care has failed and that the public are at a greater risk. The inference in the code that all hospitals and mentally ill patients pose a threat to visiting children does nothing to detract from the impression given by comments in the media and by Ministers that the mentally ill pose a general threat to the community.

Mental illness is widespread. The number of people who will encounter mental illness is shocking. If the proportion of people who will suffer from mental illness is applied to those who work in the House, we must accept that people around us will, have or are suffering from mental illness. It is vital—not only because of the numbers but because of the principle—that those people are protected as much as possible from the stigma and fear that surrounds mental health.

The wide terms of the chapter give rise to concern. It is entitled:

"Visiting patients detained in hospital or registered mental nursing homes".

That takes the definition beyond special hospitals. However, paragraph 26.3 refers only to hospitals having written policies on the arrangements. The distinction is unclear because the title also refers to "registered mental nursing homes".

Organisations that work with the mentally ill have expressed concerns. The Mental After Care Association believes that

"the blanket wording of the Code implies that all detained patients (including those not in a Special Hospital) are a risk to children."

Although it agrees that

"all hospitals should have written guidelines on visits by children",

it believes that

"there should be no automatic presumption of risk across all patients."

The Minister will know from a letter written by the Mental After Care Association on 6 January that'

"patients' ability to maintain good family connections while in hospital is often a key element of their welfare and their responsiveness to treatment".

My limited experience of clinical practice in this area has shown me that that contact is vital. There is a concern that the need to have a presumption of no visits will lead to delays in a stretched health service and that those delays may occur at a critical time in a patient's care. The presumption of no visits before guidelines are followed and a decision is made will therefore not benefit patient care.

Other concerns are listed by SANE, which often does not take the same view as other organisations. In the guidance that I and other members of the Committee have received its adviser says that SANE does not believe that it would be appropriate to have that presumption of peril.

14 The impression given to the media by Ministers' language is as important, if not more important, as the wording. It is therefore regrettable that the final paragraph of the Minister's answer on 19 January 1999 stated:

"the Code is drafted in wide terms to ensure that all visits by children are considered in this way …. However, where hospitals or units care for mentally disordered offenders or those patients with more severe forms of mental illness, policies on visiting will need to be more detailed and rigorously enforced."—[ Official Report, 19 January 1999; Vol. 323, c.427.]

The sweeping generalisations and the presumption of peril mitigate against the need to ensure that the considerations of people with mental illness are dealt with equally. I urge the Minister to make an early statement in an attempt to destigmatise the mentally ill, but, broadly, I welcome many of the Government's policies and many of the changes in this guidance.

Mr. John Gunnell
(Morley and Rothwell)
: I, too, welcome the Government's work, but I agree with two points made by the hon. Members on both sides of the Committee. It might be helpful if I briefly recount the background to the code, its purpose and how we arrived at today's draft code.

Section 118 of the Act states that the Secretary of State shall prepare, and from time to time revise, a code of practice for the guidance of all health and social services professionals and managers in relation to the use of the Mental Health Act. Section 118 also requires that relevant bodies be consulted on proposed revisions to the code before it is issued, and that the Secretary of State shall lay the code before Parliament for 40 days.

In accordance with section 118, this version of the code has been subject to extensive consultation. We received more than 130 responses from health and local authorities, NHS trusts, community health councils, voluntary organisations, the legal profession, the Royal College of Psychiatrists and the Royal College of Nursing. Those responses were considered by officials in my Department and by the Mental Health Act Commission, who have a responsibility to monitor the effective operation of the Mental Health Act. Subsequent amendments to the text of the code were made in the light of those responses, including the changes made to child visits, before it was laid before the House in December. Both sides of the Committee would probably agree that a new version of the code is long overdue. It was last revised in 1993.

The purpose of the current revision is to update guidance in the code to reflect relevant developments in case law—in particular the Bournewood judgment and the judicial review of the search policy at Broadmoor; to reflect further amendments to the Mental Health Act and other relevant legislation; and to reflect changes in terminology and developments in practice over the past five years.

The Act does not impose a legal duty to comply with the code, but failure to follow it can and has been referred to in legal proceedings. Guidance in the code is a powerful lever in ensuring high quality and consistent practice in mental health service provision. We have spent more than two years revising the code to ensure that it is workable and that it accurately reflects the legal responsibilities outlined in the Mental Health Act, which have recently been clarified in substantial case law.

20 In addition to changes in specific guidance, the code has been substantially reworded and reordered for clarity's sake. The general principles that were previously repeated in several places throughout the code have been drawn together in chapter 1. That provides the background against which the rest of the guidance in the code can be read.

I shall not read out all of the six general principles contained in the draft code, but I should point out that they lay a solid foundation for the principal recommendations contained in the code itself. The six principles are extremely important and should be considered in the context of all patients who receive treatment for mental disorder. Much of the guidance in the code is therefore relevant to the treatment and care of both formal and informal patients.

At the beginning of the new code, we have also included a section setting out the key elements of the care programme approach that should underpin the care of all patients with mental disorders, whether they are detained in hospital or receiving after-care in the community.

The essential elements of the care programme approach include a thorough assessment of health and social care needs, the provision of a key worker to coordinate care, a robust care plan and a regular review of the plan. Those principles are widely supported, are congruent with sound health and social care practice, and are reiterated in the Government's strategy document entitled, "Modernising mental health services".

The Government are consulting key interest groups about harmonising and streamlining the care programme approach and care management processes, in order to retain good practice and to develop and revise where necessary.

We have also taken care in chapter 1 to reinforce the importance of ensuring effective communication between staff and patients. Patients with mental disorders are particularly vulnerable: they may lack insight into their condition or be unable to make decisions for themselves. We have stressed the need for staff to have access to specialist help—including, where necessary, interpreters—so that they can communicate effectively with patients.

Unless it is in the public interest for information to be shared, all clinicians have a clear duty of confidence towards their patients. In the spirit of the victim's charter, the code includes new guidance on the sharing of information with the victims of mentally disordered offenders.
