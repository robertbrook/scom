legislation Draft European Parliamentary Elections Regulations 1999

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. George Howarth): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft European Parliamentary Elections Regulations 1999.

It is a pleasure to be meeting under your wise chairmanship, Mr. Atkinson. Despite the occasionally difficult history of some of these matters, I am sure that you will keep us all in order.

The Committee may recall that the European Parliamentary Elections Act 1999 received Royal Assent on 14 January. My eyes mist over at the thought of it. The Act provides that future elections to the European Parliament in Britain will be conducted using a regional list voting system. The Act, as I am sure we all recall, had a somewhat stormy passage through the other place. The Committee may be relieved to know that I have no intention of going over the same arguments about the voting system. I know that my hon. Friend the Member for Chorley (Mr. Hoyle) will be relieved to hear that.

The draft regulations govern the way in which the election will be conducted. The key text in British electoral law is the Representation of the People Act 1983, which is a consolidation of several earlier pieces of legislation. That Act has a couple of hundred sections and several schedules, including one containing what are known as the election rules. The regulations and rules governing all other elections, such as local elections and European parliamentary elections, are based on adapting and applying the 1983 Act to suit the circumstances of the elections in question. Therefore, although the European Parliamentary Elections Act 1999 sets out the broad framework for future European parliamentary elections, the secondary legislation that we are debating this afternoon provides the subsidiary detail, applying and amending the 1983 Act as the new system requires.

We have already consulted political parties, electoral administrators and interested bodies on the draft regulations. I am grateful to all those who took the time to comment, as that process has helped us to refine the draft and enabled us to lay the regulations before the Committee.

I cannot in the time available take the Committee through every instance where the existing rules have been applied, disapplied or amended to take account of the new system. Instead, I shall highlight the main features and, in particular, explain the reasoning behind the changes that were commented on during the consultation process.

Col. 4 In each region registered political parties will be able to put forward lists containing as many names as there are seats to be filled in that region, or fewer names if they wish. Independent candidates will be able to stand. Nominations will, not surprisingly, have to be accompanied by a signature from each candidate indicating their consent to the nomination. No signatures will be required from the electorate. Given the size of the regional electorates, we have decided to do away with the subscriber requirement, which did not demonstrate support among the electorate but placed an unwelcome burden on electoral administrators, who had to check that signatures were genuine. It would also have been onerous on political parties and any independent candidates.

A nomination will also have to be accompanied by a deposit of £5,000, both for party lists, however many names they contain, and for independent candidates. The closing date for nominations is 13 May.

Under the expenditure regime that we are proposing, an individual candidate or a party contesting a single region the maximum that can be spent on the campaign is £45,000 multiplied by the number of seats in the region. For example, in the north-east, which has four seats, the maximum is £180,000. I should point out that the regional limit is not affected by how many candidates a registered party chooses to place on its list. For a party contesting two or more regions, the maximum that it can spend will be the sum of the relevant regional limits. This means that a party contesting every region will be subject to a limit of £3.78 million, which is the total of all 11 regional limits.

A party's expenditure limit is designed to cover all its election expenditure. The old distinction between constituency expenditure, which was subject to a limit, and national campaigning, which was unlimited, will no longer apply. As a result of the report of the Committee on Standards in Public Life, all expenditure has to be accounted for and be subject to a limit. The national limit of £3.78 million is in line with the Neill Committee's recommendations, which I hope that the Committee will agree are appropriate.

However, parties contesting more than one region are free to spend their money where they choose. They do not have to spend particular amounts within particular regions for two reasons. First, it is not appropriate for the Government to tell parties how they should run their campaigns. Some parties may want to campaign regionally while others may have a national strategy and surely it should be up to them to decide. Secondly, in a list based election where the emphasis is on encouraging the electorate to vote for parties rather than particular individuals, it would be very hard to distinguish between regional and national expenditure. I should add that the Neill Committee has also welcomed the regulations governing party expenditure.

The regulations provide for the freepost arrangements to remain pretty much as they are now, except that in the case of parties it will be one entitlement to the freepost for the list as a whole rather than for each candidate on the list. Again, I hope that the Committee will agree that that is a sensible arrangement.

We have decided not to apply section 93 of the Representation of the People Act for these elections. The committee will recall that that is the section that prevents Image Col. 5 broadcasters from covering a particular electoral area unless they have the consent of all the candidates contesting that area. Clearly, when regions can have over 50 candidates, that would simply not be practicable. However, I hope that the Committee will be reassured to know that the broadcasters will still be bound by their general and statutory duty to provide fair and impartial coverage. I should also add that the question of party election broadcasts is one for the broadcasters rather than for the Government. Polling day will be 10 June and the polls will be open from 7 am to 10 pm.

As in previous elections, counting cannot begin until the polls close in the last EU member state on the evening of Sunday 13 June. Counting will be conducted at Westminster parliamentary constituency level, although the regulations allow an acting returning officer who is responsible for more than one Westminster constituency to combine them for counting purposes. It has been suggested that returning officers should be required to produce individual results for each Westminster constituency. The Government's aim is for these—and indeed any—elections to be conducted in the most efficient and cost-effective way. We believe that the decision whether to combine constituencies should be for the local returning officers to make, since they are the people best placed to know what would be most efficient in the particular circumstances of their areas. We understand, however, that in most places, votes will be counted on an individual constituency basis.

All the parties and individual independent candidates will have the right to be represented at all the local counts. They will also have the right to ask for a recount if they are unhappy with the way in which the count has been conducted. Once the local count has been completed to everyone's satisfaction the returning officer will transmit the result to the regional returning officer who will in turn give him or her clearance to declare it locally. When all the local results have been passed to the regional returning officer, he or she will perform the seat allocation calculation using the by now famous, and in some quarter infamous, d'Hondt divisor. I intend to keep my promise to my hon. Friend the Member for Birmingham, Edgbaston (Ms Stuart) and shall not enter into a discussion about Mr. d'Hondt, who is long dead, other than to say that the Bill that prompted the regulations is peppered with responses to d'Hondt and his system and the various ways of calculating the results.

The political parties and independent candidates will be allowed to be present at the regional centre where the seat allocation calculation is to be performed and will be able to ask the regional returning officer to repeat the calculation if they suspect an arithmetical error. However, they will not be able to ask for a full regional recount. There are two good reasons for that. First, the regional result is nothing more than an amalgamation of local results. As I said, parties and individual candidates will be entitled to be present at all the local counts and to ask for recounts at that stage. If they signify that they are happy with the local results, there should be no need for a full regional recount when those local results are added together.

Mr. Howarth: I shall come to that point before I complete my remarks. As the hon. Member for West Dorset (Mr. Letwin) has rightly said, similar recount arrangements for the Scottish Parliament and the Welsh Assembly have been put in place.

An example of the ballot paper can be found on page 44 of the draft regulations. The design is based on the results of extensive research undertaken by the university of London constitution unit and Social and Community Planning Research. The design has been well received and attracted little comment during the consultation process.

One or two people have since suggested that the arrow that accompanies the instruction to the voter to place his or her cross in one of the boxes, which are set out in a horizontal line, could be taken to indicate that the voter Image Col. 7 must place his or her cross in the box nearest the arrow. We do not believe that that confusion will arise. One of the conclusions in the research was that using

"various kinds of arrows to direct attention to die boxes …received a positive response."

The research recommended that with a landscape format instructions should be located on the left-hand side of the page.

Finally, I should like to say something about how vacancies will be filled. If the Member of the European Parliament whose death or resignation has caused a vacancy was originally elected on a party list, the vacancy will normally be filled by the next available person on that party's list. However, that person must produce a certificate from the party nominating officer to show that the party is still content to be represented by that person. That will guard against the possibility that a candidate on a list may have changed party between the original election and the time when the vacancy occurs.

If the MEP whose death or resignation has caused the vacancy was originally elected as an independent, or if a party list has been exhausted, a by-election will be held. Those arrangements are identical to those that the House has approved for Scottish and Welsh elections. I hope that I have addressed some of the concerns.

Mr. Letwin:I would not pursue this point if I did not know that the Minister is trying to give an honest account of such matters. I draw his attention to Pepper v. Hart, which is important. Mr. Howarth: When the result of the local count has been accepted by the returning officer, there is no scope to order a recount at local level. Nor is there scope for the Home Office to provide the guidance requested by the hon. Member for West Dorset, because it would be contrary to the regulations. We have difficulty therefore in agreeing to the hon. Gentleman's request. I accept that that should be so, because in this country we have no experience of the voting system. The procedures are new and; in my view, they are correct, although some people will disagree. They are appropriate for the bodies to which people will be elected.

I hope that I have dealt with the anxieties of some hon. Members, if only by my final assurance. The regulations will enable the next and future elections to the European Parliament to be conducted using the proportional regional list system that this Parliament has already approved in the shape of the European Parliamentary Elections Act 1999. I commend them to the Committee.

Mr. James Clappison (Hertsmere): I, too, welcome you to the Chair this afternoon, Mr. Atkinson. In some respects, I join the Minister in the spirit of his remarks about the nature of the new system, which is a much more complicated system than the current one.

I do not want to take issue with the Minister about every point of detail that he has outlined this afternoon. He has outlined a great deal of subsidiary detail amending the Representation of the People Act 1983. He knows that we think that the new system is inferior to the current one, that it will disappoint electors and that the more they see of it, the more they will be disappointed. We understand that they will not hear a great deal about it in advance of the election. Nevertheless, we accept that the system is being implemented by the present Government.

The Minister owes the Committee more of an explanation than he has given about recounts. I have listened carefully to the answers that the Minister has given my hon. Friends, who have, perfectly properly, questioned him. I accept that he has done his level best to explain the regulations to the Committee and to answer hon. Members' questions. However, he has not answered hon. Members' questions about recounts, and the Government will have to reconsider those points. Is the Minister saying that no provision will be made for a recount at regional level if all the officers are satisfied at local level? If so, that is a recipe for serious trouble in future.

I heard what the Minister said about the possibility of a review on recounts after the first elections, and I understand that Ministers say such things when they are in a bit of a hole. Given the vast size of the constituencies, perhaps a close result in a European election will be a rare occurrence. It may be rarer than in parliamentary elections, which involve sufficiently large electorates. However, a close result in a European election is very conceivable—perhaps not in the next election, or even the election after that, but perhaps in the future. Serious problems will arise from the Government's attitude towards recounts. At each local level, there may be a big difference in the votes, but when they are added together Col. 10 at regional level—without having to apply the atrocious seat allocation formulae—the result may be very close. Provision must be made for a recount in such circumstances.

I cannot understand what is so different about European elections compared to our parliamentary elections. In European elections, boxes are opened in many different local places, and results are counted in a regional centre. In the elections with which we are familiar as Members of this Parliament, boxes from many different electoral areas are opened and the votes counted. People have better knowledge of some electoral areas than others. The results from different boxes in different electoral areas and parishes may be very different, but the final result could be very close. In that case, in the parliamentary constituency as a whole with 60,000 or 70,000 electors, there must be a recount.

It is not good enough for the Government to leave matters as they are at European level. There may have to be provision for a recount. When the votes in all the local areas have been added up, there may be differences between them, but in the summation, the result could be close. The Minister owes us more of an explanation about the details, if he wants matters to be satisfactory.

I do not propose to go through everything that the Minister discussed, save one important subject, which he mentioned last and that is by-elections. The Government are in the habit of leaving that subject until last when European elections are discussed. It is tucked away in part IV of the regulations, which make provision for filling seats when vacancies arise, as they inevitably do in any parliamentary system.

When the European Parliamentary Elections Act 1999 was debated in Committee—that seems a long time ago, given the various stages that it went through here and in the other place—we said that by-elections were an honourable part of our political tradition and that there should be by-elections when vacancies arose in the European Parliament. The Government were determined that when a vacancy occurred, it should be filled by the next person on the list. We were told that, among other things, a by-election would upset the carefully proportional result that had been reached in the original European election. In his reply to the debate, the Minister said:

"A by-election for a single seat in a region would be won by the party with the greatest electoral strength there, irrespective of whether it had held the vacant seat."—[Official Report, 5 March 1998; Vol. 307, c. 1247.]

That was the Government's attitude then—some of us thought that that was the idea of parliamentary democracy. We live and learn things from the Government every day.

To be fair to the Government, that much is in the Act. It is tucked away in a schedule, headed, "Minor and Consequential Amendments." I suppose that for this Government, elections would be a minor and consequential matter. Schedule 2 regulations provide for a by-election to be held in specified circumstances and require

"a seat last filled from a party's list of candidates to be filled, in specified circumstances, from such a list (without a by-election)."

Image Col. 11 That was the position in March 1998 when we debated the issue in Committee. We had been led to believe that the regulations would eventually make provision for a by-election to be held in certain circumstances. In other circumstances, a seat last filled from the party's list of candidates would be filled, in specified circumstances, from such a list. We understood the broad position to be that, in certain circumstances, perhaps when there had been an independent candidate or the list had been exhausted, there would be a by-election. In other circumstances when there were still people on the list, the vacancy would be filled by the next person on that list.

That is the broad position that we were led to expect from everything that the Government said during the passage of the 1999 Act, and nothing was said to the contrary during our Committee proceedings in March 1998.

On 8 June, in a written parliamentary answer, the Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department said:

"Our intention is that where a vacancy arises from the death or resignation of an MEP who was a candidate on a party list, the vacancy will be filled by the next eligible and willing candidate on that list, provided that the party concerned is still content to be represented by that person."—[Official Report, 8 June 1998; Vol. 313, c. 436.]

Now that we are in opposition, we are getting used to reading parliamentary written answers several times to search for the clue that will tell us what die answer is really about. If we read this one carefully, we shall see that the clue lies in the word "eligible".

On first reading, we would probably think that that answer confirmed what we were told when the Bill was being considered by the Standing Committee—that the vacancy would be filled by the next person on the list. However, in a careful and astute reading of the answer attention would have been drawn to the word "eligible", which lays the foundations for the regulations.

If a vacancy has arisen, paragraph 17(2) of part IV of the regulations states:

"The returning officer shall take such steps as appear to him to be reasonable to contact the first choice"—

first choice means the next person on the list—

"to ask whether he will—

  1. state in writing that he is willing and able to be returned as an MEP, and
  2. deliver a certificate signed by or on behalf of the nominating officer of the registered party which submitted the relevant list stating that he may be returned as that party's MEP."

If I am not mistaken, that paragraph takes matters a stage further than we were led to believe when we were considering the Bill in Committee. It confirms what is pointed to in the Minister's written answer—when a vacancy arises because of the death or resignation of a Member of the European Parliament, or for some other reason, it will be filled by the next person on the list but—this is the crucial development—only if that person has the approval of that party's authorities.

It is apparently not enough that the person who is next on the list should still be a member of the same party as he was when the European election took place, when his Col. 12 name first appeared on the list. He must also be approved, or certified, by the party's authorities—the nominating officer—when the vacancy arises.

That is a new development and a refinement of a system that is already replete with opportunities for centalised party control and the manipulation of candidates. It denies the electorate the choice of their individual MEP, but presents them instead with a list. We all know about that, because the House has debated it at some length, but this is a new development: not only must the vacancy be filled by the next person on the list, but that person must be approved by the party's authorities.

The Government must say a little more about that development, rather than mentioning it only at the end of speeches or tucking it away in paragraph 17 of pat IV of the regulations or a schedule entitled "Minor and Consequential Amendments". They must own up, and tell us what it is about. Why have they chosen that course? Will the Minsiter explain why it is not sufficient for the vacancy to be filled by the next person on the list?

I abhor the system, but I take it at face value: why is it not sufficient that that person is simply a member of the same party as he was at the time of the European election? The candidates' names will all appear on the list and on the ballot papers, and the electorate will be able to see who they are and where they are on the list. Why is it not sufficient that that person should still be a member of the same party? Why must he be approved by the party's authorities? Will the Minsiter confirm what would happen under the present system? My region—the eastern region—has a list of eight candidates for the European Parliament. For the sake of argument, let us say that the Labour party has three MEPs elected—that may be pessimistic or optimistic—and that number three for whatever reason, leaves the European Parliament. Number four is still a member of the Labour party at the time that the vacancy arises but would have to be certified by the party authorities. If they are not prepared to certify him, the vacancy goes to number five and if he is not satisfactory, to number six. Why should number four not take his place? Why does the order that appears before the electorate on the ballot paper have to be overridden by the fears of the central party authorities?

The Minister owes the Committee more explanation as to why that should be. Many people will think that it is simply a way of imposing the maximum amount of centralised party control on the system. Why is it not left so that the place goes to the next person the list who is a member of the party? Why does he have to have the approval of the central party authorities? The Minsiter will have to answer that question as well as others about recounts asked quite properly by my hon. Friends.

We think that it would be much better to hold a by-election and let the electorate decide, regardless of how many of them choose to vote—we know that there are variations for European elections. A by-election is a good thing because it allows the electorate to have a fresh say over who they want to represent them. Above all else, it puts the matter in the hands of the electorate rather than in the hands of the party machine. As things stand, it appears that the maximum amount of power will be given to the party machine, without two hoots being given about the electorate.
