Welfare Reform and Pensions Bill

10.30 am

The Chairman

He and I have worked closely together in the past and I look forward to our doing so again on this Committee. He will guide the Committee through the part of the Bill dealing with our central reforms on the single work-focused gateway to the benefit system, employment zones and other measures relating to job seekers' allowance.

Other hon. Members bring to the Committee a wide variety of experience. The hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs (Mr. Flight) and my hon. Friends the Members for Doncaster, North (Mr. Hughes), for Colne Valley (Kali Mountford) and for Gravesham (Mr. Pond) clearly enjoyed their experience on the previous Session's Social Security Bill and have come back for more. I am also pleased to see that the hon. Members for Beckenham (Mrs. Lait), for Canterbury (Mr. Brazier) and for Gainsborough (Mr. Leigh) and my hon. Friend the Member for Cumbernauld and Kilsyth (Mrs. McKenna) 4 are on the Committee. I enjoyed their contributions on second Reading, although there was not much that I could agree with in what Opposition members said, except where the hon. members for Canterbury, for Gainsborough and for Grantham and Stamford revealed that they had not agreed with large chunks of the previous Government's programme. Perhaps we shall return to that matter during our deliberations. Other members of the Committee bring experience from the Social Security Select Committee, previous social security and education bills and elsewhere.

It may be helpful if I briefly outline the measures in the Bill so that the Committee can judge whether the sittings motion allows for their appropriate scrutiny.

The Bill introduces radical modernisation of the benefits and pensions system. The world has changed dramatically since Beveridge designed our welfare system 50 years ago. Then, women worked mainly at home and Beveridge assumed that the would stay there. Today, two out of three women of working age work outside the home. Fifty years ago, the average life expectancy for men in retirement was about a year. Today, things have changed enormously for the better. Disabled people's expectations have changed dramatically: they are not willing to be consigned to the margins. Beveridge simply assumed that disabled people would not work. Today, disabled people rightly want to play a full part in our society and to fulfil their ambitions alongside everyone else.

The Bill has four key areas: a package of welfare-to-work measures, modernisation of disability benefits, modernisation of bereavement benefits and reform of the pension system.

The first area that we shall consider is pension reform. The Bill will establish the statutory basis for stakeholder pension schemes. By providing the framework for our new low-cost pension schemes, the Bill will give up to 5 million people who are without a second funded pension the chance to save for their retirement with the benefits of one.

The second major aspect of pension reform is delivery of our commitment to enable pension sharing. Pension rights will be treated like other assets, providing greater flexibility for those going through divorce and for the courts. There has been extensive consultation on those measures. The draft legislation was scrutinised in detail by the Social Security Select Committee, which produced a valuable report. The Select Committee included some hon. Members who are members of this Standing Committee—the hon. Members for Arundel and South Downs and for Gainsborough and my hon. Friend the Member for Gravesham. The Bill also makes improvements to the regulatory regime for occupational and personal pensions.

The second key area is reform of bereavement benefits. The Bill will extend the bereavement benefit to men for the first time. The lump sum payable to provide more help with immediate costs, such as funeral expenses, will be doubled and, again for the first time, will be payable to widowers as well as widows. Bereavement benefit will be time limited where there are no children involved, but the JPEG 5 Bill provides for transitional non-means-tested help for widows and widowers without children for the six months following bereavement.

The third key area is a package of welfare-to-work measures. The Bill will introduce the new single gateway for people of working age. That will ensure that, for the first time, people of working age can have the help and support that they need to get into or return to work, bringing the Employment Service, the Benefits Agency and local authorities together in a way that suits the individual rather than the bureaucracy. That will be a fundamental change for the better in the way in which our welfare system works and it will have far-reaching implications.

The second welfare-to-work measure in the Bill is the introduction of a requirement for joint claims to jobseeker's allowance from young couples without children. That will ensure that both members of a couple have access to the support and advice that they need from the Employment Service and, if they have been unemployed for more than six months, access to the new deal for young people.

The Bill also provides for the new programme of employment zones to focus resources on areas that suffer from persistently high unemployment, building on the prototype zones which are already operating. The new personal job account will help unemployed people aged 25 or over to improve their skills, obtain extra help in finding work and even obtain help to set up in business for themselves.

The Bill will reform and modernise support for disabled people and includes provisions to introduce a personal capability assessment. The reforms will end the assumption that people on incapacity benefit cannot be helped, in the right circumstances, to work.

The Bill will restore incapacity benefit to its original intention of helping people who lose their income when illness or disability forces them to give up work. In future, it will be paid to those who have worked and made the right level of contributions in one of the previous two tax years. In addition, further provisions in the Bill introduce a fairer partnership between the state and employers by changing the way in which occupational or personal pensions are treated.

The Bill contains other changes in disability and sickness benefits, which were welcomed by hon. Members on both sides of the House on Second Reading. It will extend the higher rate mobility payment in disability living allowance to severely disabled children aged three and four. In future, people who are disabled in youth will be entitled to incapacity benefit without having to meet the usual contribution conditions. My right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Social Security announced on Second Reading that the age limit for entry for that benefit will be increased to 25 for disabled young people in higher education. We propose to introduce an amendment in Committee to achieve that.

The Bill is wide-ranging and important. It begins the vital task of transforming and modernising our welfare system. We shall no longer abandon people merely to receiving benefits, when their position could be transformed by active help and advice. We have a major 6 task ahead of us as we scrutinise the Bill clause by clause and I am confident that we shall be able to carry out our task in a good-natured and friendly Committee.

Proper parliamentary consideration should be given to its provisions on stakeholder pensions and pension splitting on divorce and to its welfare measures, although the Bill should be called the welfare destruction Bill rather than the Welfare Reform and Pensions Bill. The public and Parliament have been made a fool of. Such behaviour would seriously undermine the cause of good, honest government and the respect with which British people regard institutions such as the legislature and executive. It would also result in legislation that is even more unworkable and contradictory than that which the Government have hitherto produced.

That is a serious matter. The Government are using their majority to spew out legislation that has not been properly considered. Courts will be clogged up with unnecessary litigation and people will feel disappointment and injustice. I turn to aspects of the Bill relating to the time allowed by the sittings motion for debate, the order in which matters will be considered, and the appropriateness to the Bill's substance of the logic set out in the motion. The Bill's first few clauses deal with the stakeholder issue. Easy access for everyone to pension provision is, at first sight, naturally attractive to all reasonably minded men and women, and to Opposition Members. The desirability of more savings and long-term savings, and of people having better provision for their old age and retirement, should be the substance of political consensus. We would be only too delighted to facilitate such developments.

I do not know whether the Government mean their rhetoric—I do not like to accuse people of being two-faced or using double-speak. If contradictions exist between the rhetoric and the deeds of the Government, I prefer to think that the lesser of two evils is the explanation and that it is the result of a lack of homework, consideration, proper planning, and—I repeat—a lack of consultation. The Bill contains evident contradictions—rather than mere weaknesses—not least in relation to stakeholder pensions. First, it is, sadly, far from true that everyone would be advantaged by greater pension provision and greater savings. Not only is it a lie, but it would be irresponsible and cruel to promote the idea that everyone would necessarily gain by saving more for their retirement. I explained on the Floor of the House why that is the case. At current annuity rates, it is necessary to accumulate at least £80,000—or possibly £100,000—to generate an income in retirement that will correspond to means-tested benefits that would be forgone such as income support, housing benefit and council tax relief. Consequently, those who saved more—£150,000 to £200,000—would gain some positive return, but it would be small. Setting aside such an amount might not be worth the sacrifice of current consumption or the investment risk.

Saving such an amount from a modest salary requires an inordinate—I use the term advisedly—sacrifice. Someone on average earnings of £18,000 a year would have to save £1,000 a year throughout 40 years of working life—he or she might save more in some years and less in others, but would have to maintain the level of saving. An accumulation of £40,000, which at a 3 per cent. real 10 investment return would yield £75,000 to £80,000, would put the saver at the break-even point, which makes it pointless to save.

For example, it would have been used where an employer has to wind down an occupational pension scheme or a group personal pension scheme and replace it with a so-called stakeholder pension, or where—unless the amendment to which I prematurely referred were to be adopted—a personal pension is replaced with something less desirable than a stakeholder pension. The Government have not thought through the issue, and the lack of consultation has made the problem inevitable. Even if my hon. We would want to hear what the National Association of Pension Funds, and groups such as trade unions and friendly societies had to say on the matter. We would want to hear from those sections of the industry that can exploit a trust-based stakeholder pension as the Government narrowly conceive it, from the unit trust and investment trust industries, or from the new OEICs—open-ended investment companies. Some people are more excited about the prospects associated with the Treasury's rival LISA scheme. The question is whether to adopt a restrictive or a permissive approach. If one adopts a permissive approach, one should agree to the amendment. We have also tabled other amendments that will leave out many of the clause's subsequent provisions.

We have no objection to the idea of OPRA maintaining a register, or to it—or another appropriate regulator, such as the Financial Services Authority—overseeing the whole system and carefully investigating allegations of abuse. If our amendments were agreed to, those requirements will be minimal but essential, and, importantly, registration will not be a requirement. If they are not agreed to, only those schemes that have been explicitly accepted by OPRA will be accepted as stakeholder pensions on day one of the Bill's enactment. An employer will have a legal obligation to provide a stakeholder pension but, because one or more of his employees has a personal pension that has not been registered by OPRA, for example, he will not be able to satisfy his legal obligation by pointing to an employee's existing personal pension. What will happen? The employer might say to the employee, "I am sorry, I shall be fined by OPRA unless you give up your personal pension tomorrow and take out a stakeholder pension." The amendment has two purposes: first, it would allow personal pensions to qualify; and, secondly, in combination with other amendments, which we shall discuss later, it would ensure that occupational pension schemes, final salary schemes and LISAs also qualify by meeting the clause's minimal requirements. Registration should not be an essential part of that mechanism.

I hope that the amendment's purpose is clear and that the Government will accept it. They should do so, unless their real agenda is to have a restrictive version of the personal pension scheme, which will exclude vehicles that currently give good value, and to restrict rather than enhance the choice available to people who seek to provide for their retirement.
