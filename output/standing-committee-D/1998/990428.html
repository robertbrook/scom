Draft European Parliamentary Elections (Northern Ireland) (Amendment) Regulations 1999

4.30 pm

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. PauI Murphy)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft European Parliamentary Elections (Northern Ireland) (Amendment) Regulations 1999.

The background to the order concerns the European elections legislation that the House considered some time ago. As members of the Committee will know, the method in Great Britain by which European Members of Parliament are elected is now different, in that they will be elected on a regional list system. In Northern Ireland, however, the system that has been used for some time for obvious reasons is the single transferable vote under which Northern Ireland is treated as a single constituency. The House has already decided that the STV system will remain in Northern Ireland and that three Members will be elected under that system.

I shall now explain several technical, detailed amendments that were made when the changes were made in Great Britain and which now apply to Northern Ireland. The European Parliamentary Elections Act 1999 changed some of the terminology applicable to all European parliamentary elections in the United Kingdom. Consequently, references to European "parliamentary constituencies" and "representatives" no longer exist. Such descriptions will be amended to "electoral regions" and "Members of the European Parliament". Incidentally, no alteration will be made by the regulations to the existing mechanism for triggering a by-election or associated electoral procedures.

Members of the Committee will know that that is not the case in Great Britain. It now has a different system for by-elections, because they relate to substitutes in a regional list system. That will not apply to Northern Ireland; if a by-election is created there, the present system will continue and an ordinary by-election will be held.

Another change is that the regulations made under the Registration of Political Parties Act 1998 will now apply to Northern Ireland. As for section 93 of the Representation of the People Act 1983 and the legislation for the Northern Ireland Assembly elections, which prohibit broadcast media coverage of a constituency campaign issue without the consent of 4 all the candidates within that constituency, such consent is not required to cover issues that are considered to be national.

As I am sure the Committee will recognise, that distinction is often somewhat artificial, particularly given that, for the election, the whole of Northern Ireland is one constituency. It is impractical to seek the permission of all the candidates who may chose to stand in the electoral region—in this case, Northern Ireland. For the provision not to apply will make practical sense and will, for the elections, bring Northern Ireland into line with the rest of the United Kingdom.

We are also amending section 75 of the Representation of the People Act 1983. The Act limits the amount of expenditure not approved by an election agent, but which can be incurred by a third party during an election—in other words, when a body other than the candidate or his or her party incurs expenditure. The 1983 Act limited that amount to £5. As the provision applied to parliamentary elections, it was found to be in breach of article 10 of the European convention on human rights and it was the judgment of the European Court of Human Rights in the Bowman case that the low limit on such spending placed an unjustified restriction on freedom of expression. We have therefore increased the sum for Northern Ireland from £5 to £5,000.

There is a reason for that increase. The Neill committee suggested a limit of £500 for each parliamentary constituency. Northern Ireland has 18 parliamentary constituencies, but Wales has 14 and some regions in England, Wales and Scotland have as few as 10. However, as the sum of £5,000 will be applied for the purposes of that election in all the regions of Great Britain, it makes sense that the same sum should apply in Northern Ireland.

Mr. William Ross
(East Londonderry)
: Will the £5,000 be equitably spread over the varying populations of the regions in Great Britain, or will there be a wide variation, depending on electoral numbers?

Mr. Murphy
: There is a wide variation in England, Scotland and Wales. As I said, many of the electoral regions in England are differently composed to the electoral regions in Wales. There are 14 in Wales and 10 in the north-west of England; Northern Ireland has 18, so there is no exact equivalence.

Mr. Dale Campbell-Savours
(Workington)
: If that is correct, does it mean that the figure per head of population will be less in some regions than in others?

Mr. Murphy
: Yes, but we are not referring to ordinary electoral expenses. We are referring to expenses that can be incurred by third parties. For example, the Society for the Protection of Unborn Children can spend that money supporting or not supporting a candidate in a particular constituency where it could previously spend only £5, even though the agent for the candidate would not have been involved in such spending or policy making.

JPEG 5 The European court has said that the £5 limit was an infringement of human rights, and it has consequently been changed. The figure in Britain has been determined at £5,000. It makes sense to have a similar determination for Northern Ireland. In the Northern Ireland context, for example, organisations that are not political parties could support either pro or anti-agreement candidates in the election to the European Parliament. They would therefore be bound by the new rule and not the rule that would allow them to spend only £5—a rule that today seems rather strange.

The basic figure of £500 for each Parliamentary constituency was recommended by the Neill committee, and I imagine that the £5,000 figure arose because many electoral regions in England have 10 members.

Mr. Campbell-Savours
: I am sorry to press my hon. Friend on the matter, but is there not a danger that a group of candidates could aggregate or pool each candidate's £5,000 to create an artificial position, and thus a separate campaign?

Mr. Murphy
: I do not know how it will run. This is the first time that the recommendation has been followed. We shall have to see what happens. For instance, I know that were my hon. Friend the Member for Ilford, South (Mr. Gapes) allowed to speak, he could give an example of a trade union that wanted to back one of my party's candidates about 20 years ago. For various policy reasons, the Labour candidate did not want the backing of that trade council. However, because the agent decided that the party did not want such backing, the issue went to court. It is that sort of thing that the regulations seek to avoid.

Mr. Ross
: I am sure that the Minister must by now have had the opportunity to read the yesterday's proceedings in the other place, where my noble Friend, Lord Molyneaux raised that very question. Is the Minister yet able to tell the Committee that any number of individuals could put up £5,000? It is not a question of 10 candidates being able to pool 10 times £5,000, but of individuals, constituents and members of the public not involved in the election—one, five, 10, or even 50 of them—each being able to put up £5,000. Does not that matter deserve the most serious consideration? If I can catch your eye later, Mr. Winterton, I hope to expand on it.

Mr. Murphy
: I am sure that when the hon. Gentleman has expanded on that, towards the end of the debate, I shall be able to answer some of those questions in detail. As a member of the Ulster Unionist party, does he agree in general that we do not want a great difference between the regulations for Belfast and those for Cardiff, Edinburgh and London? I accept the general point, and shall return to it. The change is not of our volition but is a consequence of a ruling by the European Court of Human Rights.

Member for East Londonderry (Mr. Ross) will remember, involved the SPUC. In April 1992, a Mrs. Bowman distributed 1.5 million SPUC leaflets in constituencies throughout the United Kingdom. The case specifically related to the 25,000 leaflets distributed in Halifax. The judgment was upheld on a technicality.

It is interesting to read the Neill report in that context. The Minister alluded to the fact that the Neill report recommended a limit of £500. Significantly, however, paragraph 10·63 of the section in the Neill report that refers to the Bowman case states:

"It is relevant to note that section 75"—

of the Representation of the People Act 1983—

"is confined to activities which are intended to promote or prejudice the electoral prospects of 'particular candidates in a particular constituency'. Leaflets designed merely to bring factual information to the attention of voters or to assist a national campaign without referring to particular candidates fall outside the section."

I did not use the word "sinister" lightly. This seems to afford people rights, individually and collectively, to embark on a negative personal campaign against someone—not only in European elections; presumably, it will also apply to parliamentary elections.

Unless constraints are imposed—which the regulations do not contain and to which the Neill report does not allude—we shall face a serious predicament. The maximum that a parliamentary candidate may spend is about £8,000. At £500 a throw, we need only 16 people in a parliamentary constituency to be spending as much as the parliamentary candidate promoting his party and its policies and manifesto.

That is insidious because if someone purports to distribute leaflets that support a different policy from that of the main parties, he or she cannot have the JPEG 7 money, but if a person sets out to attack someone personally, he or she will receive it. Why have the Government let the proposal through?

Neill suggested £500, but that did not have to be the figure. In paragraph 10·59, which deals with the European ruling, the report states:

"The judges observed that there may be circumstances in which the right of freedom of expression and the right to enjoy a free election may come into conflict and it may be considered necessary, in the period preceding or during an election, to place certain restrictions, of a type which would not usually be acceptable, on freedom of expression, in order to secure the 'free expression of the opinion of the people in the choice of legislature.'"

That seems to give a green light to the Government to say, "Hold on a minute. If we take this to its logical conclusion, we end up with a potentially dangerous situation." Why have the Government not built in a failsafe which would in certain circumstances mitigate against the

"free expression of the people in the choice of the legislature."

Paragraph 10.59 of the report continues:

"In striking the balance between these two rights each Contracting State had a 'margin of appreciation', meaning thereby that each State is (within reasonable limits) entitled to adopt the solution which best suits its circumstances and traditions."

There is the get-out. The Government do not have to introduce the proposal. Each contracting state, each member of the European Court of Human Rights, can decide what is best with regard to how it runs its own elections.

I shall make two points. First, there is a margin in the European legislation, so we do not have to follow everyone else; we have freedom of action, best judged on how we do things. Secondly, the recommendation of £500 for a parliamentary constituency has been aggregated to £5,000 for European constituencies, but the Minister has not told us whether the £5,000 limit applies to European elections in Great Britain—in England, Scotland and Wales.

Mr. Murphy
: Yes, it does.

Mr. Moss
: The Minister confirms that it does. So each individual can spend £5,000 to attack particular candidates. Individuals will have access to substantial amounts of money. I repeat that this is not a pro-Europe or an anti-Europe policy; we are talking about attacking candidates.

The Government should rethink the order. I have not voted against many orders in my time, but today we should lay down a marker that the regulations could be highly dangerous. I suppose that by making an issue of this today, it will perhaps enter the public domain and more people will begin to use the facilities. However, until the Minister adequately explains why enormous sums could be allocated to individuals who wish to destroy people's candidacies for European parliamentary elections, I, for one, in discussion with my Whip, may well vote against the regulations. Why is the figure so high?

4.50 pm

Mr. William Ross
(East Londonderry)
: I hope to return to what was said by the hon. Member for North-East Cambridgeshire (Mr. Moss), who spoke for Her Majesty's Opposition, but there are some other issues in the regulations that we should consider as well.

The statutory instrument is largely technical, as the Minister suggested, but it does perpetuate the gerrymandering introduced in 1979 by the proportional representation system in Northern Ireland. In the Minister's opening remarks, he said that that system was used in Northern Ireland for obvious reasons, but he did not spell out what they were. Therefore, perhaps those on the Committee who once knew and those who have never been told should be reminded.

Under the terms that prevailed at the time in regard to elections to Europe, Northern Ireland was entitled to two seats only in the European assembly. That was deliberately increased to three seats, and proportional representation was introduced to ensure that a representative from the minority nationalist population was elected. It was blatant gerrymandering—let there be no mistake about that. The Social Democratic and Labour party was the chosen party at that time. Now it is being strongly challenged by Sinn Fein for that seat.

The longer I live through politics the more I find that when things are done for the wrong reason—and that change from the United Kingdom law was made for a JPEG 9 wrong reason—eventually they end in tears. I was one of the few, even in my own party, who were prepared to say that we would have been far better with only the two seats. We were howled down not only by Labour, but by every party in the House when we drew attention to the gerrymandering that had taken place. That background has brought us to where we are today with three seats in Northern Ireland.

I would like to move on to some of the technical issues. The description of Northern Ireland as an "Assembly constituency" will be replaced by the term "electoral region" by regulation 4(b), and the representative will become what is described, as has been elsewhere for a long time, as an MEP.

The schedule shows how the new ballot paper will look. It would be interesting to me, and I hope to all of us, to discover whether the Liberal Democrats, who were such a feature of an election or two in Great Britain, will find a means of circumventing the protection that has now been invented for all party names and symbols.

As it happens, I am one of those representing my party—it is all-party representation—on the Home Office working party on electoral law, which, as the Minister will be aware, talked about the matter a great deal. There is no easy answer or way to protect party symbols and names, as we all know. The system that has been introduced for the European election is the best shot that people can take at the moment. I am always certain that ingenious minds will find a way around such legislation to try to undermine parties, so although this is the best shot, it is not the end of it— more will come down the road.

I am not clear whether the symbols will be in glorious technicolour or whether we will have to be satisfied with the grey monochrome of old-time photographs. Emblems such as the red rose, the torch and our own symbol of the Union Jack in the shape of Northern Ireland are all in colour. If they are printed in black and white, they will lose something. So could we be told whether they will be in colour, which would, no doubt, produce difficult printing problems for those who print the ballot papers?

I should like an assurance on one thing in particular; that, when the ballot papers are printed, it will be possible to read the writing under, in or around the symbols, because I have great difficulty reading that writing as it is printed in the order. I hope that the standard of printing for the ballot papers will be somewhat higher.

Will the Minister clarify the position of independents? If they want to run for election under a certain symbol, will they have to turn themselves into representatives of a political party, which is not difficult? Will they have to register themselves as a party or can an individual register a symbol? In addition, if they must do those things, what is the time limit? Folk would like to know.

To turn to the sums expendable by candidates, do the new sums accurately reflect inflation? I hope that they do. The sums allowed in Northern Ireland are considerable. I worked it out on the basis of 1.1 million electors at 6.4p a head. It worked out that candidates 10 could spend £70,400, plus the £14,900 that individuals can spend as they please. That makes a total of £85,000 per candidate, and that is a minimum because there are actually more than 1.1 million electors. I do not think that many candidates in Northern Ireland or the United Kingdom will spend that much, but it is a measure of the sums that candidates can throw away in an election if they want to.

I also wish to draw the Minister's attention to the fact that, in replying to the debate in the other place last night, his noble Friend Lord Dubs said that the system of first past the post would apply to by-elections. That is wrong. That is not how a single transferable vote by-election works. It would only be first past the post if one individual received 50 per cent. plus one of the votes cast. Otherwise, one must go through the procedure of redistributing the votes from the bottom until someone passes the 50 per cent. threshold. I put that on the record in case the noble Lord's speech confuses anyone.

I was also interested in the comments that were made about the broadcasting element. The Committee will be aware of the long-running row between the broadcasting authorities and, in particular, the minor parties, over the right to broadcast throughout the United Kingdom. To put it briefly, because I do not want to weary the Committee with unnecessary detail, the three major parties have no problem getting UK-wide broadcasts, but, as regional parties, the Scottish Nationalists, Plaid Cymru, the Ulster Unionist Party, the Social Democratic and Labour Party, the Democratic Unionist Party and any other party you care to name, will be restricted to its own region. However, the question of the unity of this kingdom extends beyond the borders of Northern Ireland, Wales, England and Scotland, and concerns every citizen. If people want to make a case which is vital to the whole nation, they should be allowed to do so and not be restricted to their own region, even if it has a greater bearing on the region than elsewhere.

The broadcasting element should be kept in mind, not just for the forthcoming election, but for future reference. Election matters are not usually contentious across parties. Electoral law concerns every hon. Member and it is our duty to ensure that it is fair to everyone and that it operates in favour of none. I am sure that every hon. Member accepts that.

My final point concerns the increase in third party election expenses from £5 to £5,000. The hon. Member for North-East Cambridgeshire drew attention to the matter. The increase was introduced because of the activities of Mrs. Bowman, who is not known to any hon. Member. She and her organisation are prolific producers of paperwork on abortion matters. It does not matter whether one agrees with the lady, but she found a loophole in the law and she and her group went through it. They all wound up in court and she got off on a technicality because the summons was issued out of time. She then went to the European Court of Human Rights, which made an interesting judgment. The Government decided to do something about it. The willingness of Governments, time after JPEG 11 time, to do something instead of first thinking through what should be done leads to a lot of grief for the House and the country.

The matter requires a lot more thought than has been given to it so far. It cuts across parties and is fundamental to our democracy. We should not simply pass regulations and go away and forget about them. The Minister said earlier that we cannot ignore the matter and that the regulations must be passed. I understand that the limit applies also to European elections in Great Britain.

Let us consider the position. The European Court of Human Rights allowed a coach and horses to be driven through a sensible restriction that this nation has long placed on individual candidates. The principle was that outsiders were allowed to spend only 50p, which was later raised to £5. That was to protect candidates from prosecution if some fool spent a few pounds on them during an election, but the European judgment has turned that principle on its head and outsiders may now attack a candidate comprehensively.

The point raised by the hon. Member for Workington (Mr. Campbell-Savours) on the different sizes of the electorate is also valid. Mrs. Bowman went to the European Court of Human Rights and others will do the same on the issue of unfair treatment in different electorates.They will win. That may happen not in this election but in a subsequent election, and the sum involved may be £500, which applies to United Kingdom elections; or £5,000, which applies to European elections. Various odds and sods—one, two, three, four, five or 100 of them—may allegedly be prepared to put up £5,000 to attack any Committee member.

I do not hold with the American idea that spending more money on an election makes one certain to win it. Spending more money may help, but not as much as the Americans think. However, the ability to buy time can have a great effect on the outcome of elections. One could buy time oneself, or someone could buy time on one's behalf, and he might attack another candidate rather than help one directly.

Committee members who are interested in this subject should get hold of the Neill report—most of us will have picked it up, but most of us will not have had time to read it—and they should turn to the section that deals with limits on campaign expenditure. They should begin reading it somewhere near paragraph 10·51, and they should read all of the section that is entitled,

"Implications of the Bowman judgment".

I confess that I have not had time to read all of it—I managed to get my hands on the report only today.

Paragraph 10.59 states:

"The judges observed that there may be circumstances in which the right of freedom of expression and the right to enjoy a free election may come into conflict".

Those rights do indeed come into conflict; there is no doubt about that. Paragraph 10.59 continues:

"In striking the balance between these two rights each Contracting State had a 'margin of appreciation' … meaning 12 thereby that each State is (within reasonable limits) entitled to adopt the solution which best suits its circumstances and traditions."

The solution that best suits our circumstances and traditions involves the present arrangements—we support a figure not of £5,000 or of £500 but of, perhaps, £50. If we go beyond the minimum we will open a can of worms, and we may live to regret that.

Paragraph 10.60 states:

"The majority concluded that, because the restriction in section 75 was set as low as £5, in practical terms it operated a total barrier on Mrs. Bowman's publishing information with a view to influencing the voters of Halifax in favour of an anti-abortion candidate."

How does that statement relate to the electoral law on a third party's attempts to influence the electorate during an election campaign? The paragraph continues:

"It was, therefore, out of proportion to the legitimate aim it sought to achieve."

That suggests that the judges thought that there was a proportion, which is, as I have already said, very low.

Paragraph 10.61 states:

"The immediate effect of Bowman is that it will be necessary for the United Kingdom legislature to amend section 75"—

of the Representation of the People Act 1983—

"so as to raise the limit on expenditure above £5."

That is what we want to do.

Paragraph 10.64 states:

"We suggest for consideration by the Government that the amended figure to be inserted in section 75(1)(ii) should be more than £100 and should be of the order of £500."

This is a serious issue.

Paragraph 10.69 states:

"in the dissenting judgment of Judges Loizou, Baka and Jambrek"—

I hope that I have pronounced their names correctly—

"(who held that the spending limit was not disproportionate) it was stated that 'There can be no doubt that limits on election campaign spending maintain equality of arms as between candidates, a most important principle in democratic societies and in the electoral process.'"

The £5,000 sum will create inequality between candidates. The Government should, therefore, seriously reconsider the matter.

Paragraph 10.70 states:

"We do not think that the majority would have disagreed with this sentiment"—

the sentiment that was expressed by the three judges—

"It was the derisory limit of £5 to which they objected."

There is a great deal more, but I shall leave it to hon. Members to go away and consider the matter. I hope that the Government Whip who is in Committee today will take on board my comments. I sincerely believe that we are going down an extremely dangerous path that we should leave as soon as possible. We may not be able seriously to reconsider the matter before the forthcoming election, but we should do so before voters go to the polls in a year's time.
