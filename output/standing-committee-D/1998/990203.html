legislation Draft Apportionment of Money in the National Lottery Distribution Fund Order 1999

4.30 pm

The Secretary of State for Culture, Media and Sport (Mr. Chris Smith): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Apportionment of Money in the National Lottery Distribution Fund Order 1999.

The purpose of the order is to adjust temporarily the shares of national lottery income to good causes so as to provide the New Opportunities Fund with the funding that we promised it to deliver three new initiatives.

It may help Committee members if I describe some of the background. The Government announced their intention in the White Paper that we published in the summer 1997 to establish a New Opportunities Fund, and the National Lottery Act 1998 made that a reality. NOF, as it is generally known, will fund initiatives in health, education and the environment. There is no question of the lottery taking over core funding responsibilities in those areas. That expenditure will be additional to core Government support. I was pleased to be present at NOF's official launch last Friday, when it invited applications for its first initiative—healthy living centres.

Hon. Members will recall that we were able to create NOF without disadvantaging the existing good causes. They had originally been told to plan on the basis that they would get 20 per cent. of the £9 billion, which it was anticipated would be raised over the course of the operator's licence, which will end in September 2001. That amounted to £1.8 billion each. In the event, it became clear that the National Lottery was likely to raise an additional £1 billion, taking income for the good causes to £10 billion, rather than £9 billion. That additional £1 billion made the initial NOF programmes possible.

Mr. James Gray (North Wiltshire): Does the right hon. Gentleman agree with my arithmetic? Twenty per cent. of £9 billion is £1.8 billion, and 16.6 per cent. of £10 billion is £1.66 billion. Mr. Smith: The hon. Gentleman is incorrect. The 16⅔ per cent. cam into operation only from the date of the passage of the National Lottery Act 1998. Until then, 20 per cent. had been coming in, so the overall amount that the good causes anticipate receiving is Col. 4 £1.8 billion. In fact, the proposals that we are considering today will ensure that the sum is £1.85 billion.

An accounting procedure was in place that earmarked funds for the overall national lottery distribution fund for the purposes of NOF. No money was put into any account other than the overall national lottery distribution fund. The money was earmarked prior to the passage of the legislation. If the Bill had not been enacted, no money would have been given to NOF. As was made clear at the time, funds were given to NOF only because the legislation was passed, and they were given at the moment that it was passed. We told Parliament explicitly what was happening and what we were doing. At that stage, some hon. Members attempted to raise objections, but Parliament was perfectly happy with the procedure.

Mr. Smith: Opposition Members are hung up about supposed breaches of the additionality principles, so it might be helpful if I restate—as I have done on many occasions on the Floor of the House—the very simple and basic additionality principle. Lottery money should not be used to replace Exchequer expenditure; Image Col. 9 it should be used to add to what the Exchequer does. That principle was clearly expressed by the former Prime Minister, the right hon. Member for Huntingdon (Mr. Major) in a speech to an English Heritage conference in September 1994, when he said:

"We will make no case-by-case reductions on conventional public spending programmes to take account of awards from the Lottery. The money raised by the Lottery will not replace pubic expenditure".

The former Prime Minister did not follow his own words, and reduced case-by-case spending, especially on the arts. In July 1996, however, he was backed by the then Secretary of State for National Heritage, the right hon. Member for South-West Surrey (Mrs. Bottomley), who said in a reply to the Heritage Select Committee:

"Lottery funds are not intended to substitute for funds which would otherwise have been provided by conventional public expenditure."

"In consequence, when the public expenditure programme is increased or decreased, that, in itself does not prevent, for example, the use of Lottery funds to secure projects or programmes beyond the scope of foreseeable affordability,"

"but similar to those hitherto financed by public expenditure or adding lottery funds to the level of affordable public expenditure in any particular area."—[Official Report, House of Lords, 4 November 1996; Vol. 575, c. 568.]

Exactly the same principle applies to sport, on which the Exchequer rightly spends money. The national lottery allocates additional money, which is also spent on sport. In the same way, the Exchequer spends money on mainstream services in health, education and the environment. The additionality principle is not about replacing but about adding. That is what the order does.

It was originally planned, however, that at the end of September 1999, the percentage shares for the Millennium Commission and NOF would switch so that the Millennium Commission would receive 13⅓ per cent. rather than 20 per cent. as at present, and NOF would receive 20 per cent. rather than 13⅓ per cent. as at present. I have now decided that the Millennium Commission's share of lottery income should remain unchanged at 20 per cent, until it reaches its target of £2.017 billion, when all of its 20 per cent. will go to NOF.

Where does that leave the good causes? Assuming that income to the good causes over the lifetime of the licence is £10.6 billion, arts, sport, heritage and charities will each receive at least £1.85 billion, which is £50 million more than originally anticipated; the Millennium Commission will receive just over £2 billion; the National Endowment for Science, Technology and the Arts has already been given its £200 million endowment; and NOF will receive £1 billion. I stress that those are my best estimates. Accurately predicting future lottery income is almost as difficult as predicting the numbers of the winning balls. That is why our plans have been made on the basis of cautious forecasts. If, as we all hope, more money is raised, all the good causes will share in it.

I am clear that the continued success of the national lottery is very good news for all good causes. It is changing the face of the United Kingdom for the better and bringing benefits, direct and indirect, to all of us.

To conclude, the order is essentially a technical measure that will give effect to the policies that we announced in September 1998. I have gone beyond that announcement, however, by giving the distributing bodies an undertaking that there will be no further change to their percentage shares for the rest of this licence and beyond. That is in addition to the undertaking that has already been given that each good cause is guaranteed 16⅔ per cent. of lottery proceeds after the current licence expires. That guarantee was never given to them by the previous Government.

Image Col. 15 I am sure that the good causes welcome the greater certainty that our proposals will give them, and I commend the order to the Committee.
