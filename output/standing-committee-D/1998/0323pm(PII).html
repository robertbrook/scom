clause Clause 22
title CASH EQUIVALENTS

Mr. Davies: I beg to move amendment No. 172, in page 18, line 11, at end insert—

'(1A) Such regulations shall ensure that all such calculations and verifications are based on the methodology currently accepted by the actuarial profession for the purpose of valuing pension assets and liabilities, and that no such calculation or verification shall include any amounts in respect of any pension benefits other than those which the rules of the relevant pension scheme at the relevant time oblige the trustees or managers of such a scheme to pay out to pensioners or their dependants.'.

Image Col. 453 This is an important issue, although perhaps not the most momentous, that concerns pension splitting and the methodology that will be used. The purpose of the amendment is to avoid all doubt and peradventure—for the greater security of the public and the greater clarity of the law—and any unnecessary costs, litigation and disputes. I have therefore ventured my hand at a definition of the methodology to be used, which is that

"Such regulations shall ensure that all such calculations and verifications are based on the methodology currently accepted by the actuarial profession for the purpose of valuing pension assets and liabilities"—

In other words, it must be the same technology that actuaries are using to advise their clients for any other purpose, be it to ensure that an occupational fund is solvent, or valuing pension assets for transfers of assets from one fund to another. The definition continues:

"and that no such calculation or verification shall include any amounts in respect of any pension benefits other than those which the rules of the relevant pension scheme at the relevant time oblige the trustees or managers of such a scheme to pay out to pensioners or their dependants."

The object of that phrase is to remove any doubt as to whether the cash equivalent transfer value should include an element for discretionary benefits. It is the practice of trustees in some funds to use some of the surplus available to give benefits higher than those that they are contractually obliged to provide under the rules of the scheme. It is always a problem for actuaries valuing a pension scheme, or someone's share in an occupational pension scheme, whether to take account of the fact that, although there is no obligation on the trustees to provide certain benefits in practice, such benefits have been provided or there is some expectation that they should be.

We must have clarity on the matter. It would be very wrong if people were to lose a share of their pension, under pension-sharing or pension-splitting arrangements, as a result of the actuary responsible for the valuation process taking account of benefits that people will not receive because the trustees will not use such discretion. Of course, it is perfectly possible that any surplus in a pension scheme that might provide a basis for discretion today will no longer exist at the relevant time, when the decision is made about discretionary benefits. We believe that it is right to take a conservative view on the matter—in other words, to make a prudent view—and to make sure that it is clear to actuaries what their responsibilities are in the valuation process.

Mr. Timms: As the hon. Gentleman explained, the amendment has two parts. On the first part, I agree that it is important to ensure that, where necessary, a cash equivalent should be calculated using accepted actuarial methods. That is clearly the case for valuing salary-related schemes, but in a pure money-purchase scheme, the value of the pension fund is clear and there is no need for any actuarial assumption to be made.

Col. 454 Where an estimate of the value of the benefits does need to be obtained, as in a salary-related scheme, we will ensure in regulations that cash equivalents are calculated in a manner approved by the scheme's actuary. In turn, the actuary will be subject to the professional guidance issued by the Institute of Actuaries and the Faculty of Actuaries in Scotland, and that guidance will be extended to include pension sharing. I hope that that satisfies the hon. Gentleman on the rules for cash equivalence.

The second part of the amendment is more difficult because it would prevent any benefit that the scheme is not obliged to pay from being included in the calculation of the cash equivalent. The hon. Gentleman advocated that provision on the grounds that it would create confidence and certainty, and everyone would know where they stood. The trouble is that it would do so in a way that would work to the disadvantage of beneficiaries of pension sharing. The intention is that, for ease of administration, the cash equivalent for pension sharing should be the same as that which applies now to early leavers. However, the amendment would create a difference between the two, as well as being unfair to the former spouse.

In relation to normal transfer values, the established custom is for additional benefits to be awarded at the discretion of the trustees or employer, and the cash equivalent must take account of any benefits that will accrue if the custom continues unaltered, unless the trustees have ruled that such benefits should be ignored. The most important of those discretionary benefits tend to be increases over and above the statutory minimum for pensions in payment.

In addition, the trustees cannot make a direction to ignore discretionary benefits unless they have consulted the scheme actuary and obtained a written report. That must include the actuary's opinion on whether there would be any adverse implications for the funding of the scheme if the value of discretionary benefits was included in the cash equivalent calculation. It is right that similar provisions should apply in pension-sharing cases. There is a well-established procedure in relation to early leavers. If the scheme is well funded to meet the discretionary benefits and chooses to include them in cash equivalents for early leavers, it is right that it should also include them in pension-sharing cases. Otherwise, the cash equivalent calculation in a pension-sharing case will understate the effective value of the member's rights, and that would be unfair to the former spouse. That is not what we intend in the clause. I hope that what I have said will persuade the hon. Gentleman to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Davies: It works either way. There is a danger that if the trustees do not continue with a more generous policy on discretionary benefits, the loser in a pension-splitting exercise may be the member of the pension scheme, who may lose a share of something that he will never receive because he will not get the discretionary benefits. Should the unfairness benefit the transferor or the transferee? It is my considered Image Col. 455 view that the bias should be towards the transferor who, after all, earns the pension. We would prefer to err on his or her side.

Our objection to the clause is that it provides for guaranteed minimum pensions and protected rights to be subject to pension sharing. Guaranteed minimum pensions are the portion of a classical, salary-related pension that corresponds to the proportion of the contributions which represent the state earning-related pension scheme rebate.
