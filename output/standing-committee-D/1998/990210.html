Hallmarking (Hallmarking Act Amendment) Regulations 1998

4.30 pm

Mr. Christopher Chope
(Christchurch)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Hallmarking (Hallmarking Act Amendment) Regulations 1998 (S.I., 1998, No. 2978).

 It is a pleasure to move the motion under your chairmanship, Mr. Cunningham.

The Opposition prayed against the regulations so that we could have a proper discussion about them. There is much confusion about the Government's policy on hallmarking. Are the Government introducing the regulations because they have to or because they want to? Michael Winwood, the secretary of the British Hallmarking Council said:

"We have had to accept the situation. We had no choice."

 I hope that we will find out from the Minister how the regulations interact with the European Union's proposed directive on precious metals. Do the Government support the directive, which would force all EU countries to use the same hallmarking system and require all silver and precious goods to have the E symbol on them?

The starting point for our debate is that article 30 of the European Union treaty promotes the free movement of goods. Who would have presumed that that article would, 20 or 30 years later, be interpreted in such a way as to outlaw the ancient and revered practice of hallmarking in this country? Hallmarking is one of the original ways in which we ensured consumer protection. We have had a British lion silver hallmark uninterrupted since 1544—for more than 450 years. Is that simply to be abolished and replaced by symbols that nobody understands and which have no tradition, relevance or meaning for the British consumer? Is this yet another example of the erosion of our sovereignty, which Europe continues to salami-slice away?

The regulations make the situation much more complicated for the ordinary consumer, who is familiar with the concept of sterling silver. It now appears that we will have many varieties of silver. We will not quite know what they are unless we look at the detailed number on them. Many varieties will come from other countries in the European Economic Area. Some will be manufactured to different levels of fineness from the old sterling silver. Will that benefit the consumer or will it lead to consumer concern and confusion?

Consumer protection is at the heart of the issue. It is an offence for any trader to sell or describe an article in gold, silver or platinum unless it is hallmarked. Hallmarking provides the customer with a guarantee of the fineness of 4 the precious metal content. The most recent changes to hallmarking system, which incorporated platinum into it, were introduced by the Labour Government in 1975.

Our concern is that the present system of clarity is being replaced by one of confusion. It will no longer necessarily be possible for consumers to know the date on which a precious item was manufactured. They will find that items that are not manufactured in this country can carry the lion hallmark, but they will know that it is purely a decorative symbol. There is much concern that if the next directive, which is currently being discussed, comes into effect, it will prevent the use of the lion on English silver altogether.

So we would like the Government to answer quite a few questions this afternoon. In particular, we would like them to confirm that they still believe that manufacturers' self-certification, as practised in some continental countries, offers a lower form of guarantee to consumers than does the independent marking of precious metal articles, as practised by the UK assay offices.

The issue is causing people outside much concern. What proposals has the Department to enlighten the British consumer about the contents of the regulations, which have already been passed into law, notwithstanding our prayer?

Mr. Chope

What appears to have happened is that when we signed up to the European Union single market, we had not realised all of its implications. We have had to comply with rulings of the European Court of Justice, especially in the Dutch case that lies behind the regulations, which appear inconsistent with the original basis on which we entered the single market and accepted its system.

4.37 pm

The Minister for Competition and Consumer Affairs (Dr. Kim Howells)
: I join the hon. Member for Christchurch (Mr. Chope) in saying how good it is to serve on a Committee under your chairmanship, Mr. Cunningham. This is the first time that it has happened to me—and, I am sure, to many other members of the Committee.

The hon. Member for Christchurch focused on the most crucial issue: whether we will be able to help consumers to understand what they are purchasing and to make the right decisions. That ought to underpin any consumer strategy of any Government.

JPEG 5 If I may, I shall give an account of what we have done through the Hallmarking (Hallmarking Act Amendment) Regulations 1998, and what we have not done, to remove any confusion that may have arisen in the minds of members of the Committee.

The regulations, which amend the Hallmarking Act 1973, were made for three reasons. First and foremost they were made to align UK law with European Union rules on the free circulation of products in the single market. We have ensured that UK legislation reflects the existing position in Community law. That is therefore a matter of clarity.

Secondly, the regulations are intended to ensure that United Kingdom manufacturers are not disadvantaged by the new freedom to trade in articles of precious metal in Europe on the basis of national hallmarks—indeed, to ensure that they can take full advantage of it. That is important: it is a question not just of the validity of imported precious metal but of our ability to export.

Thirdly, given the need to amend the Hallmarking Act 1973 in line with European rules, we have also taken the opportunity to update the Act to take account of long-standing concerns of the British Hallmarking Council and of a part of the trade.

I should stress at the outset that there is no question of reducing the high level of protection that hallmarking and the Hallmarking Act 1973 afford to consumers in the United Kingdom. The hon. Member for Christchurch was right in that respect—we must be very careful. Some of the amendments made by the regulations will, however, increase consumer choice and make hallmarks more readily understandable to consumers. I hope that both those factors will strengthen the value of hallmarking in the future. Greater freedom of movement of hallmarked articles of precious metal will be of benefit to UK exporters, given that UK hallmarks will be accepted throughout the EEA.

I shall briefly list the changes that the regulations have made to the Act. The definition of "approved hallmarks" in section 2 of the Act has been amended to allow for the recognition of specific hallmarks from other EEA states. that follows the 1994 Houtwipper judgment at the European Court of Justice and earlier case law, which made clear that our hallmarking legislation, like that of other hallmarking states, was in breach of article 30 of the EC treaty. We will, however, recognise only hallmarks that have been struck under the law of another member state by an independent body, and which provide information equivalent to that provided by our own hallmarks and which is intelligible to consumers in the United Kingdom.

Self marking done by firms themselves will not be allowed. The matter is not to be confused with the regulations' remit, although it is important.

Currently, an EC directive is being considered that would do away with the need for hallmarking. I understand that it is the subject of a stalemate, which is not surprising. Under the directive, we would be confronted with imported goods that might or might not have hallmarks, but it would not matter, because they would have to be sold, accepted and so on. That is a real danger, but it dos not concern the regulations.

Mr. Owen Paterson
(North Shropshire)
: How does the Minister square his remarks about the validity of foreign assaying with comments from a major jewellery business in my constituency? I have a letter which states:

"We have imported jewellery from European countries from time to time, and the metals on occasions are below the correct carat. Sometimes we receive 9 carat when the goods are said to be 18 carat, and occasionally the carat level is narrowly missed."

Dr. Howells
: Obviously I do not know about that particular case. Britain has trading standards officers who regularly test for and find fraudulent goods on sale. Such goods are as likely to be manufactured in this country as in Italy, Germany or anywhere else. There is a danger of fraud, and if we do not get hallmarking under control and ensure mutual recognition of the standards being introduced, we will be in big trouble. That is why the Government have brought the regulations before the House—to ensure a degree of clarity that does not currently exist.

I shall explain about the new measurements of fineness of imported material and material that is made in this country. Part I of schedule 2 has been amended to abolish the distinct hallmarks which were, until the end of last year, struck on imported articles. The same marks can now be struck both on imports and on UK-manufactured articles. In other words, goods that are imported into this country can be hallmarked by British hallmarkers. Reputable firms—on the continent, at least—are increasingly seeking information about how that is done, so that they can enjoy the same advantages of recognition in this country as they do on the continent. The distinction between imports and UK articles that are hallmarked at UK assay offices was also considered to be in breach of article 30 of the treaty, because it discriminated against articles of precious metal that were manufactured elsewhere in the EEA.

JPEG 7 The same part of the schedule has also been amended to increase the range of finenesses to which UK assay offices can hallmark—the point that was made by the hon. Member for North Shropshire (Mr. Paterson). That is intended to ensure that UK manufacturers can produce articles of precious metal to the same principal standards of fineness as their competitors in Europe. It limits the likelihood of standards of fineness appearing on the UK market with which UK manufacturers would be unable to compete. We have not, however, extended the range of finenesses to include all those which exist in Europe, but the principal standards only. That is because it is generally agreed that it is not in the interests of consumers to encourage—as the hon. Member for Rochford and Southend, East (Sir T. Taylor) put it—a proliferation of standards that would make it difficult to compare value for money and could lead to confusion.

The new standards of fineness which have been added are those that appear in the agreed international standard, together with additional "pure" standards which have been agreed by all member states in the context of negotiations on a proposed directive on the marking of precious metals. The schedule has been further amended to require that all UK hallmarks should include a millesimal indication of the standard of fineness. I hope that all members of the Committee have before them a copy of the explanatory leaflet. We are going to ensure that those leaflets are placed in retailers' shops so that people can see for themselves exactly what the emblems and figures mean.

Given the increased range of finenesses which will appear on the market, it is more important than ever that consumers should be able to understand the information about fineness that is contained in hallmarks. However, the existing traditional fineness symbols—such as the lion for sterling silver and the crown for gold—will continue to appear, alongside the millesimal mark, as voluntary marks. In practice, the assay offices are continuing to strike traditional marks unless a manufacturer specifically requires them not to do so. Information from the London assay office has indicated that the vast majority of its clients continue to want traditional pictorial marks.

Section 4 of the 1973 Act has been amended to remove the requirement that hallmarks should include a letter indicating the year in which the article was hallmarked. To be candid with the Committee, that is the part of the legislation about which I feel most uneasy. I have been told that it is a device that is mainly used by experts—we see it on the "Antiques Roadshow". It is another consequence of European case law, which prevents member states from requiring an article of precious metal to be re-hallmarked merely because it does not bear an indication of the date of hallmarking. Because the requirement cannot be made of imported articles that were hallmarked elsewhere in the EEA, it would be unfair to make such a requirement with regard to articles that are hallmarked here—although, in practice, most UK manufacturers are likely to continue to ask for the date letter to be struck on a voluntary basis, as is allowed by the amended Act. The leaflet indicates the letter for this year, which is, I think, Z.

8 The process of formulating the regulations has been long and difficult. It required widespread consultation and liaison with the European Commission, which is never easy; it also meant a good deal of liaison and consultation with British retailers and manufacturers. The result is a careful balance between the requirements of European law and the needs of consumers and the industry. I think that we have got the balance right by maintaining a high level of consumer protection and ensuring that manufacturers and consumers in the United Kingdom can benefit from the new freedom of movement of articles of precious metal, while at the same time meeting our European obligations.

The basic principles of hallmarking have not changed. Recognised hallmarks, whether or not they have been struck in the United Kingdom, will continue to consist of an indication of the registered sponsor—that is the manufacturer or importer of the article—the assay office where the article has been tested and hallmarked, and the fineness of the precious metal.

Perhaps it would be useful if I end my speech at this point and listen to what Committee members have to say. I shall try to answer any specific questions later.
