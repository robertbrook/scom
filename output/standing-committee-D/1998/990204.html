The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Paul Murphy)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Departments (Northern Ireland) Order 1999.

4 On 18 December 1998, the First Minister designate for Northern Ireland, the right hon. Member for Upper Bann (Mr. Trimble), and the Deputy First Minister designate, the hon. Member for Newry and Armagh (Mr. Mallon), issued a statement setting out their proposals for the structures of Northern Ireland Departments, north-south implementation bodies and areas of co-operation. The order takes account of the part of the statement that concerns the Departments. It provides the legislative base for an Office of the First Minister and Deputy First Minister and a total of 10 other Departments in accordance with those proposals. They are the direct result of implementing the Good Friday agreement and of detailed negotiations between the First Minister and Deputy First Minister in Belfast in the run-up to Christmas.

The Member for South Down (Mr. McGrady) was incorrectly described in the list of Members as the Member for South Devon. Those are equally pleasant constituences, but I know which one the hon. Gentleman want to represent. He was instrumental during an evening of intense negotiations, which went on until 4 o'clock in the morning, between and on behalf of the new Administration designate in Northern Ireland and the Government of the Republic of Ireland, on the north south implementation bodies and, of course, the Departments, which are the sole responsibility of the First Minister and Deputy First Minister. They enshrined the agreement in legislation, and negotiated the agreement after Good Friday. Therefore, as such, the proposals are not Government proposals. The order is simply our method of framing in law what was agreed.

The Committee will recall that the Good Friday agreement—for which people voted—stated that there should be up to 10 Departments in the new Northern Ireland Administration. After many weeks of deliberation, the conclusion was reached that there should indeed be 10 Departments. At the moment, there are six Departments: the Department of Education, the Department of the Environment, the Department of Finance and Personnel, for which I am responsible, the Department of Agriculture, the Department of Economic Development and the Department of Health and Social Services.

The proposed departmental structure will retain three existing Departments: the Departments of Education, of the Environment and of Finance and Personnel. Three other existing Departments will be renamed. The Department of Agriculture will become the Department of Agriculture and Rural Development; the Department of Health and Social Services will become the Department of Health, Social Services and Public Safety; and the Department of Economic Development will become the Department of Enterprise, Trade and Investment. Five new Departments will be created: the Department of Culture, Arts and Leisure; the Department of Higher and Further Education, Training and Employment; the Department for Regional Development; and the Department for Social Development. In addition, the Office of the First Minister and Deputy First Minister will be created.

JPEG 5 The order does not specify the functions of those Departments. A further order on the transfer of functions, which is to be enacted through subordinate legislation, is currently being prepared. That order will redistribute the functions of the current six Departments.

Mr. William Ross
(East Londonderry)
: When can we expect that order?

Mr. Murphy
: It will simply provide for the creation and renaming of the Northern Ireland Departments; it will not establish their functions. The order will be enacted as soon as possible. If the hon. Gentleman wants me to provide details of the Department's functions, I am perfectly happy to do so.

The negotiations that took place on 18 December, and which led to the creation of the Departments, examined their functions in considerable detail. We do not have that information at the moment, because the Stormont negotiations on the distribution of functions concluded only in the last few days.

Rev. Martin Smyth
(Belfast, South)
: Is the Minister satisfied that those negotiations have dealt with all the functions of the Northern Ireland Government? I understand that, during the preliminary discussions, some 15 areas of responsibility were overlooked. Must we wait for those areas to be defined at a later date?

Mr. Murphy
: The hon. Gentleman is right. There are areas—not necessarily controversial areas—that were for various reasons omitted from the preliminary negotiations. I am given to understand that the discussions have now finished, and that all the Northern Ireland Government's functions have been dealt with.

In addition to providing a new departmental structure, the order will consolidate the Ministries of Northern Ireland Act (Northern Ireland) 1921, which deals with the administration of public services.

There are four key elements in the consolidated provisions. First, the order will re-enact the requirement that each Department is subject to the direction and control of its Minister. It will also maintain the authority of a departmental Minister to distribute his or her Department's functions among its officers.

Secondly, the order will transfer powers currently enshrined in the Ministries Act (Northern Ireland) Act 1944, which enable the assignment and transfer of functions between Departments. Those powers will be conferred on the First Minister and Deputy First Minister acting jointly.

Thirdly, it will enable junior Ministers to exercise functions to the extent authorised by determination of the First Minister and Deputy First Minister, which was enacted in section 19 of the Northern Ireland Act 1998.

Fourthly, other existing provisions that would be re-enacted by the order will include giving Departments the status of corporate bodies, provision for the use of official seals by Departments, and for documents to be accepted as evidence of acts of Departments. Normal procedures for an Order in Council include a public 6 consultation period, but this draft order was not published. However, the changes to departmental structures contained in it are very different from normal in that, after 18 December, the world knew precisely what the departments were, and there was a tremendous amount of negotiation in Stormont and discussion in the public arena after 18 December. Furthermore, the order was circulated to, and considered by, the First Minister, Deputy First Minister and the Northern Ireland Assembly party leaders. In addition, the proposed changes to the organisation and structure of Northern Ireland Departments was discussed and agreed by the Northern Ireland shadow Assembly on 18 January 1999. A final report and determination by the First Minister and Deputy First Minister is to be brought to the Assembly on 15 February 1999.

I conclude by summarising the key provisions of the order. It establishes an office of the First Minister and Deputy First Minister. It provides for 10 other Northern Ireland Departments. It requires departmental functions to be subject to the direction and control of the departmental Minister, and it enables functions to be assigned to, or transferred between, Departments by order made by the First Minister and the Deputy First Minister acting jointly.

The order is much more important because, in effect, it will put into operation, when devolution takes place—as we hope it will, relatively soon—an entirely new Administration in Northern Ireland based on agreement between the parties about precisely what type of Administration they require. That is why I hope that the Committee will endorse the new Departments for Northern Ireland, bearing in mind that they were approved by the Assembly in January this year and, I hope, will be finally determined later in February.

4.55 pm

Mr. Eddie McGrady
(South Down)
: I welcome the order as an important step in the progress towards implementation of the Good Friday agreement. It is the distillation of many months of intense negotiations, in various formats, among the representatives of the parties in the Northern Ireland Assembly—there were 10 parties at the last count, but there may be 11 since I left, as there is a tendency to change in certain quarters.

I am surprised to hear hon. Members say that there is a lack of information about which executive function comes under the headings of particular departmental designations. At least two detailed reports have been published. The first was the agreement itself, which was published on 18 December and received widespread publicity. The second was an updated report, which was published and debated in the Assembly on 18 January. Those who are interested in Northern Ireland have had ample opportunity to find out the minutiae of the disciplines, which are described under the headings. The fifteen points that were not included, although they were supported, were not significant in terms of the totality of the executive functions allocated under each of the headings.

I am somewhat disappointed at the tendency to think that because there are 10 Departments there are jobs for the boys—I heard that phrase this afternoon, apart from it being a sexist remark, it is an insulting 10 one. The reason why I find it insulting is that people of all parties and all persuasions sat day in and day out for eight months to try to work out the best way to deliver the services that the people of Northern Ireland need. Hon. Members should remember that we have a die-cast departmental designation that has not really changed since 1973; some argue that it has not really changed since the 1920s and 1930s.

All the parties, irrespective of political philosophy and aspirations, were determined to make use of what has been described as a blank page. We did not want to be circumscribed by the past or dictated to by powers developed by the civil service. We wanted—and were determined to have—an entirely new dynamic in the service of Northern Ireland.

As for the allegation of jobs for the boys or girls, seven parties of very disparate viewpoints—from extreme unionism to extreme republicanism—tabled their interpretations, based on their own examination of the best way to deliver the appropriate services to the people whom they represent. The other three parties did absolutely nothing. They did not issue one piece of paper on departmental designations. The Ulster Democratic Unionist party, which has been highly critical of the whole process, has issued not one jot of paper. It is the same with the United Kingdom Unionist party and the minor party that has since been formed, which I think is called the Independent Unionist party, with its three members. They have issued not one scrap of paper, yet they have been major critics.

All but two of the parties that submitted documentation designated 10 Departments. For the record, I have to correct what I said earlier. The DUP did make a submission. It submitted a paper showing eight Departments, plus one at the centre. We must take reality as it is. The nature of the disciplines under each of the headings and how they are balanced between the Departments is clearly illustrated in the two reports of 18 December and 18 January that I mentioned.

On 18 January, the Assembly discussed the report for a few days and noted it with approval. It did not "determine" the report—as we say in Northern Ireland—but noted it with approval, with 75 votes for and 24 votes against. That is a 75 per cent. majority for the basis of the order that we are discussing today. It was a cross-party and cross-community endorsement from one extreme to the other. That is significant, and it should be noted and acted upon.

There has been criticism of the whole concept of the agreement, but not one constructive alternative has been proposed. That is a negative attitude to take while the rest of us are trying to engage the entire community and its representatives in a new governance for Northern Ireland. I find the process extremely exciting and rewarding. There are political difficulties involved, which I am not in any way dismissing, but I hope that by 15 February the proposal will receive the same endorsement as it did on 18 January, with a 75 per cent. majority. I think that 51 per cent. is required on a cross-community basis—it cannot be a simple majority.

JPEG 11 I endorse the order that is before the Committee, and, in due course, we shall discuss the more detailed aspects of its consequences. It is the culmination of many years work by participants who differ on their political outlook and future aspirations. We hope that the new governance will be effective, efficient and germane to the problems that we have in Northern Ireland.

Mr. Lembit Öpik
(Montgomeryshire)
: We welcome the order, which is part of the long-overdue process of devolution brought into the realms of possibility by the Good Friday agreement. Other aspects of the agreement are relevant to the establishment of these bodies, such as progress on decommissioning, but they are not directly relevant to the debate today or to the order, so we should not dwell on them.

In line with the spirit of devolution, the exact nature of the structures and the new Northern Ireland Executive must be a matter for those involved in the Northern Ireland Assembly and local people. I was, therefore, encouraged to hear the Minister say that extensive consultations have taken place, and that the order has tried to enshrine the opinions of those actively involved in Northern Ireland.

We welcome the fact that the Department of Agriculture is being extended, and will become the new Department of Agriculture and Rural Development. As anyone who has been following the fortunes of the countryside knows, rural life is about much more than just agriculture. A farmer suggested to me that the Department should have been called the Department of Agriculture, Rural Development and Flooding, but I shall not put that forward as a serious proposal.

I welcome the separation of the Department of Education and the Department of Further Education and Employment, because there are benefits in separating two qualitatively different aspects of education. However, as has been said, there are concerns about funding. What assurance can the Minister give that one part or other of the education system will not be starved through competition between the two Departments?

I should be grateful for the Minister's response on several points. First, I am concerned about the lack of a Department for equality. That is no doubt a consequence of consultation in Northern Ireland, but many people—especially within the nationalist and republican communities—are concerned that the fine words on equality may not be put into practice. What assurance is there that there will be a junior Minister responsible for equality issues, perhaps working out of the First and deputy First Minister's office?

Secondly, the order does not make it clear how a tourism strategy is to develop—perhaps I have missed the reference. Tourism seems to come under the remit of the Department of Enterprise, Trade and Investment, while visitor amenities are dealt with by the Department of Culture, Arts and Leisure. That surely shows a lack of logic, because if tourism is to 12 enjoy an increasing role in the Northern Ireland economy, it deserves to be handled holisticalfy. There is no north-south implementation body for tourism, which is an area in which north-south co-operation might be especially valuable. What does the Minister regard as the right way to ensure a holistic and strategic approach to the important area of tourism?

Thirdly, the order provides for junior Ministers to exercise functions, to the extent authorised by a determination under the Northern Ireland Act 1998, by the First Minister and Deputy First Minister. That gives Ministers quite a lot of responsibility. I am not saying that that is a negative proposal; it is a natural consequence of empowering an Assembly that Ministers will be able to authorise functions without always having to check with somebody else. However, that fact makes it even more important that we do all we can to ensure that the Assembly does not develop a profoundly confrontational etiquette that prevents Ministers from co-operating with each other— otherwise, we will encourage endless criticism of ministerial decisions within parties or on the Floor of the Assembly.

My fourth point relates to article 8, which provides for functions to be assigned or transferred between Departments by joint order of the First Minister and the Deputy First Minister. That gives those two officers an awful lot of power, although I am sure that we feel confident that the present incumbents of the posts operate sensibly and co-operatively. It is important that both individuals exercise those powers in a way that creates a responsible precedent. It would be damaging if personalities got in the way of the political process in Northern Ireland. We must be aware of the importance of the early days, months and years of the Assembly so that acceptable procedures and behaviours, which will make it easier for the Assembly to function in the long term, can be set down.

One concern that I do not have is whether there will be enough for politicians in Northern Ireland to do. I have never known politicians to be short of work, and even less to be unable to justify their existence. Furthermore, given the size of the responsibility—the administration of an entire nation in the United Kingdom—10 Departments is a reasonable number. It is not excessive. I am relaxed that those individuals have enough to do.

The order is helpful because it continues a logical process, in which genuine decision-making responsibility is being assigned to Northern Ireland in a well-thought-out way, which is the essence of devolution. We must plan for success, and the order is a logical and sensible approach in the context of that process and outlook.
