Value Added Tax (Finance) Order 1999

4.30 pm

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mrs. Barbara Roche)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Value Added Tax (Finance) Order 1999 (S.I. 1999, No. 594).

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the Value Added Tax (Buildings and Land) Order 1999 (S.I. 1999, No. 593).

Mrs. Roche
: I believe that this is the first time that I have had the pleasure of being in a Committee under your chairmanship, Mr. Olner, and I look forward to the experience tremendously. I am sure that I speak for the whole Committee.

The Government are committed to fairness in taxation, the orders demonstrate that commitment. Order No. 593 is an anti-avoidance measure. Order No. 594 confirms that certain backroom services supplied to businesses in the finance sector should properly attract VAT.

The buildings and land order is designed to stop VAT avoidance. It amends schedule 10 to the Value Added Tax Act 1994 to strengthen an existing anti-avoidance measure introduced in March 1997 that restricts the scope of the option to tax land and buildings. Section 51(2) of that Act allows for schedule 10 to be amended by a Treasury order. The order stops a device that is used to get around the original measure, and it will protect £30 million of revenue each year.

It may help if I explain the background to the orders. Businesses supplying services that are exempt from VAT, such as banks, insurance companies and universities, do not add VAT to their income. In turn, however, they cannot claim back all the VAT that they incur on their own capital and overhead costs, which includes the construction of the buildings that they occupy. Some businesses have tried to get the best of both worlds by not adding VAT to their income but by entering into avoidance schemes to spread the VAT cost of their buildings over many years.

The method of doing that was complicated. Instead of occupying the buildings immediately, some of the businesses that I have referred to leased the property to associated companies and then took a lease back again before taking up occupation. By opting to tax the rents they were able to recover all the VAT incurred on the building, whereas without that device, very little of the tax would have been recoverable. Although they paid VAT on the rents charged by the associated company, that was spread over a considerable number of years.

4 The measure introduced in March 1997 was intended to counter those lease and leaseback schemes by preventing exempt businesses from opting to tax the leasing of buildings that they actually intended to occupy. In that way, businesses could not inflate their recovery of VAT on the cost of the buildings. That measure, however, was confined to buildings falling within the capital goods scheme—essentially those buildings that cost £250,000 or more to purchase, construct or refurbish.

Some people, still seeking to avoid tax, have found a way around that by artificially bringing forward the date of the lease to a time before the building actually existed—that is, before construction has started. At that stage, the building is not a capital item within the capital goods scheme, so the 1997 anti-avoidance measure does not apply.

These newer schemes have mainly been used by universities and colleges of further education, although they were capable of much wider use, and there was a real risk of others jumping on the bandwagon. Many of those using the schemes were already receiving funding from the Government, which is calculated on a gross cost basis. Tax avoidance undermines the basis of managing public expenditure, with the result that the shortfall in revenue could lead to other worthy causes being deprived of their fair share of state funding.

The order will stop that VAT avoidance scheme. It will bring into the scope of the 1997 measure not only a building that is regarded as a capital item under the capital goods scheme, but the lease of land on which such a building will be constructed. To prevent the parties from merely transferring the development after the lease is granted but before building works have started, the measure will apply either when the building is a capital item for the person granting the lease or when it is a capital item for a person to whom the site is subsequently transferred.

It has been a mischievous scheme, which has been marketed and taken up even though the Government's views on avoidance have been made clear and although Customs and Excise has explained time and again that such schemes are likely to be closed. I propose today to do just that. Not only will the order stop any future use of such schemes, but it will bring existing schemes to a timely halt. That will be done by treating the original lease as though it had been made on 10 March 1999, the date that the order came into force. Consequently, any rents received after 10 march 1999 in respect of an existing scheme will become exempt from VAT. The practical effect of the measure is that the VAT originally recovered will become subject to annual adjustments over 10 years under the capital goods scheme in order to reflect the true use of the building.

The order merely tightens an existing anti-avoidance measure. People who tried to avoid the measure knew full well its purpose and knew about our commitment to stop tax avoidance. They should not be surprised that the schemes have been closed down.

In addition, as part of our commitment to fairness in taxation, we believe that each group or business sector should pay its fair share of tax. Recent decisions taken by the courts have meant that the share of VAT paid by the JPEG 5 finance sector has been reduced. The amendments made by order No. 594 are designed to redress the balance and restore the position to what was intended before those cases and to what is required under European law. I stress that they do not introduce new taxation. They do not affect the treatment of the services that financial institutions offer consumers or businesses. The amendments affect some services bought in by financial institutions, which were always intended to be taxable and are taxable in other member states of the European Community. The changes therefore restore a level playing field.

Most services provided by financial institutions are exempt from VAT, but the finance sector makes its contribution by paying VAT on the goods and services that it buys in, which are generally taxable. The VAT on them is not recoverable by the institution if it is used in making exempt supplies. Recent court decisions have extended exemption to cover may such services.

In the finance sector, European law requires a clear distinction to be made between intermediary services, which are exempt, and financial management services, which are taxable. That distinction has been blurred by the courts. While those cases were developing, Customs and Excise undertook extensive consultation in Europe on the issues, which established that the way in which the exemption was being interpreted in the United Kingdom courts was out of step with the position elsewhere.

For those hon. Members who follow such matters, there has been some comment suggesting that the changes introduced by the order are contrary to European law—in particular, that credit card management services are exempt under the sixth EC VAT directive, and that the European Court of Justice has ruled to that effect. That is quite wrong. The changes have as a primary aim the proper implementation of the relevant EC directive, in accordance with the UK's treaty obligations, and reflect the consultation exercise, the relevant rulings of the European Court of Justice, and —very important—our legal advice.

It may help the Committee if I give a few examples of the kind of services at issue. They include services provided by a motoring organisation to a bank in connection with devising and marketing an affinity credit card; clerical, administrative and management services in connection with a hire purchase operation; and, in a case currently before the courts, computer processing of credit card transactions and general management of the card operation. In none of those cases does the subcontractor provide the exempt credit. Contracting out such administrative services is no different from contracting out book-keeping, secretarial or legal services. They should all be taxable.

The changes were introduced without notice by Treasury order laid on Budget day, 9 March, and coming into effect on 10 March. There is a history of the finance sector forestalling tax changes by artificial prepayment schemes, and the timing was designed to limit the opportunity for that. We received clear legal advice that it was impossible under existing EC law to provide anti-forestalling provisions in the circumstances.

6 The changes restore the proper scope of exemption for services bought in by financial institutions. They do not introduce new taxation, nor do they constitute any widening of the scope of VAT. They are necessary to ensure that the finance sector continues to pay its fair share of VAT.

Mr. Oliver Heald
(North-East Hertfordshire)
: How much extra revenue will result?

Mrs. Roche
: As we have shown in the Red Book, the changes wil protect revenue. It is estimated that the Value Added Tax (Finance) Order 1999 will restore about £100 million of revenue currently being lost as a result of court judgments. It is not about raising extra revenue, as the hon. Gentleman suggests, but about protecting the revenue base.

The order is necessary to ensure that the finance sector continues to pay its fair share of VAT, and to ensure continued compliance with European law. It does not introduce a tax on supplies made to consumers or businesses, other than the financial service providers themselves, and will ensure that the finance sector shoulders its fair share of the tax burden.

I look forward to a constructive debate and hope that at its conclusion, both orders will meet with the approval of the Committee.

Mr. John Whittingdale
(Maldon and East Chelmsford)
: I start, Mr. Olner, by joining the Minister in welcoming you to the Chair. I think that it is the first time that I have had the pleasure of serving under your chairmanship. I have no doubt that you will be firm but fair.

I am also grateful to the Minister for her introductory remarks explaining both of the orders. We are debating two separate orders; both are somewhat technical and difficult, but they are extremely different. The first is narrowly targeted and intended to close a loophole that the previous Government first moved to attack. The second is a substantial extension of the VAT base, in direct breach of the Chancellor's assurance. Its effect is likely to be to push up the cost to banking customers, and it is highly probable that it also contravenes European law.

Let me deal first with the Value Added Tax (Buildings and Land) Order 1999. Prior to 1997, a financial institution, such as a bank, which was VAT-exempt, could get around that by exploiting the option-to-tax rules. If a bank, say, decided to construct a new building, it could set up a subsidiary company to which it would lease the land and then construct a building on it. If the bank opted to pay VAT on the rents that it charged to the company, all VAT on construction costs would become recoverable. The subsidiary could then sub-lease the property back to the bank, at which stage VAT is payable on the rent and is not recoverable. The benefit to the bank is mainly in terms of cash flow because the VAT that is recovered on construction costs is offset by the VAT on rental payments. There is a potential substantial cash flow saving.

JPEG 7 In 1997, as the Minister explained, the previous Government tried to prevent such schemes by disapplying the option-to-tax rules for expenditure on a capital item that cost in excess of £250,000, where the intention was that it would be used by a partially exempt business.

Mr. John Burnett
(Torridge and West Devon)
: Does the hon. Gentleman agree that those expenses would not be entirely recoverable against subsequent rents? In so far as they are capital expenses, they are not recoverable and would, therefore, be a net loss to the exempt organisation, which would usually be the bank.

Mr. Whittingdale
: I am trying to summarise the matter in a reasonably succinct way, and I agree that I may have glossed over some complications. In some circumstances, what the hon. Gentleman says may be correct. I did not want to take up too much of the Committee's time with the detail of what went on in some cases. The general principle, however, which I hope that the hon. gentleman accepts, is that the scheme was intended to get around the VAT payment and to benefit cash flow.

The previous Government moved to close the scheme. However, they disapplied the option-to-tax rules only where the grant of the land was made within the period that applied for the capital goods scheme. That allowed the anti-avoidance rules to be easily circumvented by ensuring that the land was leased through a subsidiary before construction began.

The buildings and land order closes that loophole by disapplying the option-to-tax rules for the entire period after the expiry of the capital goods scheme. I assume that that was the original intention in 1997, so the Opposition do not oppose the order. However, we have some concerns.

As the Minister explained, the order comes into effect for all grants signed after 10 March 1999. However, it also deems that those signed previously will apply from that date. That means that, because of the operation of the capital goods scheme, VAT on construction costs will be clawed back by Customs and Excise over the next eight years from schemes from which it has already been recovered. In that respect, the order is retrospective. It will upset arrangements that a business entered into in all good faith in 1997.

Some might say that it is not unreasonable for a financial institution that exploited the loophole in the way that I described to be prevented from getting away with it. The new rules apply, however, not just to inter-group schemes, but to third-party arrangements. A company that has calculated rental yields on the basis of capital costs may find that it has to pay back VAT as well over the next seven or eight years. That will completely upset the economics of the original transaction. In such cases, third-party arrangements will need time for renegotiation. What estimate has been made of the number of such schemes that will be affected?

The measure is not confined to the financial services sector. Potentially, all those in the exempt sector may be affected, including private hospitals, universities—which 8 the Minister mentioned—sports clubs and charities. Has the Minister made any estimate of the potential cost of the measure to those institutions? Why should they have to bear that cost when they were merely taking advantage of the rules as they stood at the time?

Mrs. Angela Browning
(Tiverton and Honiton)
: Does my hon. Friend agree that it would have been extremely helpful if a compliance cost assessment had been made available to the Committee in advance of this debate?

Mr. Whittingdale
: I entirely agree with my hon. Friend. The Minister produced a figure in her speech, but there has been no real breakdown of the sectors in which the recovery of VAT will take place. I hope that the Minister will give us a little more detail about the costs and who will have to bear them.

I turn to the Value Added Tax (Finance) Order. It is not a narrow measure, nor does it close a loophole. It represents a major extension of the VAT base. That may come as something of a surprise to Opposition Members, because they will recall the Chancellor saying in his Budget speech:

"On VAT, today I propose there will be no increase in rates and no extension of VAT." —[ Official Report, 9 March 1999; Vol. 327, c. 186.]

If I remember correctly, Labour Members all waved their Order Papers at that point.

We are used to the Chancellor failing to mention tax rises in his Budget speech—they become apparent only when one examines the Red Book. This is actually worse. The Chancellor said one thing in his speech, but the truth is exactly the opposite. This measure is a major extension of VAT which, according to the Red Book and according to the Minister a few moments ago, will bring in an extra £100 million a year. The Government said—and the Minister repeated it this afternoon—that the change merely restores the position prior to recent legal challenges, but that is not true either.

Under paragraph 13(b)(d) of the sixth directive, financial services institutions are generally exempt from charging VAT. That is translated into UK law by schedule 9 of the Value Added Tax Act 1994, under which the exempt categories are spelled out in group 5. The fifth of those deals with the making of arrangements for any transaction covered by the first four categories, which generally cover dealings in money, securities or credit. In fact, many financial institutions have chosen to outsource certain services. In particular, those offering credit card facilities frequently use a third party.

From the time of the introduction of VAT in 1973 until 1995, credit card management companies were exempt from VAT. That was certainly the case for the original Joint Credit Card Company, which used to administer Access from Southend. When that company, which had by then become Signet, was acquired by FDR, it was on the understanding from Customs and Excise that it was exempt from VAT. In 1995, however, Customs and Excise arbitrarily changed its mind and declared that credit card management was no longer exempt. Since then there have been several legal challenges. Three of them—the Civil Service Motoring Association, Lloyds TSB and Continuum—all resulted in defeats for Customs and JPEG 9 Excise. The hearing of the fourth case, FDR, was not concluded until January this year, but a judgment is expected imminently.

There can be no doubt that the order is intended to pre-empt that judgment. It has been made with no consultation with the industry and under the cloak of Budget secrecy, for which there is no justification whatever. The Minister attempted to justify it by saying that there was a danger of forestalling, but it is extremely difficult to see how such a measure could be forestalled.

That is in marked contrast with the position under the previous Government, when a similar measure affecting insurance was introduced. On that occasion, a consultation document was issued which led to the publication of draft clauses. That allowed the industry to make representations and the order was improved as a result. The failure to consult in this instance flies in the face of the code of practice of Customs and Excise, which says that the Government intend to consult on tax policy matters wherever it is reasonable to do so. It sets out a number of benefits that come from consultation, but it also gives cases where consultation might not be appropriate; none of those apply in this case.

I have mentioned the three legal cases that Customs and Excise lost. Even more significant, however, is the judgment in the Sparekassernes Datacenter case. That was a ruling by the European Court of Justice that the services provided by Sparekassernes Datacenter fell within the exempt categories as laid down in the sixth directive. That is highly relevant, because the services provided by SDC were essentially payment transfer services. SDC acted as an outsourced data centre—essentially, a back office for a large number of small Danish savings banks. It dealt with automatic teller machine transactions and with Dankort card transactions—Dankort is the Danish credit card. The Danish Government attempted to claim that the services provided by SDC were not VAT exempt. The European Court of Justice ruled, however, that specific payment functions had been delegated to SDC and that it should therefore be exempt under the sixth directive.

I understand that Customs and Excise and the Minister have attempted to argue that the SDC judgment has only limited application. To date, however, that has certainly not been the view of the tribunals, and there is good reason to believe that it will not be the view of the tribunal in the FDR case. We therefore face the real prospect that in perhaps just a few days the order will be found to be illegal under European law.

It is hardly surprising that the industry was astonished and aghast when it learned of the order. It had been widely understood that the FDR case would be the test decision, and its outcome would be watched throughout the industry. Suddenly, however, the Government are attempting to pre-empt that decision. My question to the Minister is, why? What does she propose to do if the tribunal rules in FDR's favour under European law, as many observers believe that it will? Will she introduce a new order to reverse the decision? I can tell her one thing that will happen: the Government will immediately find themselves faced with an application for judicial review.

The order is not confined to the provision and management of credit card services. It may cover call centres, sponsors of affinity cards, loan administrations, 10 fund administrations, dealing systems and many other corporate finance services. It is very unclear, however, which of those will be covered and which will not. It is hardly surprising that the British Bankers Association has said that the measure is flawed, that it will create uncertainty and that it will lead to increased litigation.

All that we do know is that Customs and Excise expects the measure to bring in £100 million. Will the Minister provide a breakdown of that figure, so that we have a better idea of who will be expected to charge VAT? One thing is certain: someone will have to pay, and the most likely person is the banking customer. VAT will increase the cost of credit card management and all other services affected by the 17.5 per cent. charge. Clearly, the chances are that those extra costs will be passed on and that the customer will end up paying.

The alternative is that banks and other financial institutions will take such services back in-house, so that they do not have to pay VAT. If that happens, operators such as FDR will be likely to move overseas to somewhere where VAT is not charged. The Minister spoke about creating a level playing field, but the playing field is not level at all. A number of other countries in the European Union, such as Luxembourg, do not levy VAT on the services in question. It would be not in the least surprising if some British operators decided to set up in such a country. It is worth noting that FDR alone employs about 2,500 people based in Basildon, which is next door to my constituency. Those jobs and many others are being put at risk.

The order is dishonest, because it directly contradicts Budget assurances; it is flawed, in that it is ambiguous and vague about which services it covers; it is damaging, as it raises the cost of particular financial services and threatens jobs; and there is a very strong likelihood that it is also illegal.

It is not too late. I appeal even now to the Minister to withdraw the order and to think again. That is also the view of the British Bankers Association, which has asked for the order to be withdrawn to allow time for the industry to sit down with Customs and Excise and redraft it. At the very least, we should wait for the tribunal judgment to see whether it is in accordance with European law.

If the Minister refuses to listen, the Opposition will have no alternative but press the orders to a vote.

Mr. Burnett
: I am delighted to join the Minister and the hon. Member for Maldon and East Chelmsford (Mr. Whittingdale) in welcoming you, Mr. Olner, to the Chair.

With regard to the Value Added Tax (Buildings and Land) Order, I should state the liberal Democrats' support for anti-avoidance legislation. Such legislation should, however, be fair, and it should not catch innocent transactions. It should also be accurate and effective.

Hon. Members who debated last year's Finance Bill remember the Government's trumpetings about the effectiveness of their anti-avoidance provisions. We saw the immense errors made in the stamp duty reserve tax anti-avoidance provisions, which as a result of JPEG 11 incompetence, have cost the Exchequer more than £1,000 million. That resulted, I believe, from just two deals—the BP-Amoco deal and the Vodaphone deal. If we are to have anti-avoidance provisions, will Ministers please ensure that they are effective and fair?

Freehold land and leasehold property of more than 21 years' duration are invariably presumed exempt from VAT, so VAT is not payable on the supply of a property—on the transfer of a property or on rent. That is not always a benefit, because a landowner or lessor cannot recover VAT on costs. Costs or fees for architects, planners and lawyers will be even higher when brown-field land rather than green-field land is developed. A land owner can elect to waive the exemption to VAT and bring property into charge for value added taxation. The land owner therefore has to charge VAT on subsequent sale proceeds or rents and can recover VAT on his inputs.

That is not too bad if the purchaser or lessee is registered for VAT because the VAT can be recovered on the price of freehold land or the VAT on the rent. It would not suit a bank or insurance company, which are exempt, because, omitting a complication of partial exemption, any VATable supply to a bank or insurance company is not recoverable by the exempt organisation. There is an in-built cost on the transaction of about 17.5 per cent.—the rate of VAT.

Order No. 593 is overly complex and its rules impact on entirely innocent transactions. A great onus of inquiry is being put on vendors and lessors.

I shall return to that, but perhaps I may refer the Minister to coincidental funding. If a bank funds the construction of a new shopping centre and decides to take one of its units as a branch, will that transaction be caught by the order? Will the option to tax not apply to the letting to the bank and, thus, the rents payable to the exempt company?

My next point is on funding. If a developer borrows to undertake development and grants a lease to, say, an insurance company—an exempt organisation—and uses the premium on the lease to repay the debt, wholly or in part, will the option to tax be disapplied so that the developer cannot recover VAT on its development costs?

I am sure that the Minister and other members of the Committee are aware of my next point, which, again, relates to funding. If a developer undertakes fitting-out works for the tenant, which the tenant pays for separately, is that in fact funding, and will the option to tax be disapplied?

Fourthly, the option is disapplied if the intended use is not wholly or mainly for eligible—that is, taxable—purposes. Wholly or mainly is usually arbitrarily interpreted as an 80 per cent. test. As a matter of fact, I am sure that the Minister will agree that as far as "mainly" is concerned, anything over 50 per cent. complies. This definition adds the word "wholly." I should like the Minister to tell the Committee whether the 80 per cent. rule is intended to apply, or a slightly lower percentage. Will the Minister also elaborate upon new paragraph (2) (3AAA)

12 and the meaning of the word "transferred"? Does that cover the grant of a lease or does it cover only an outright transfer of a freehold?

Vendors and landlords will have to have significant information about their purchasers and tenants. If they were in the capital goods scheme, and potentially caught by the disapplication, the 1997 rules require vendors and lessors to know the intended use—for example, by reference to the unlegislated 80 per cent. test to which I referred. The new rules mean that even if they are not in the capital goods scheme, if the purchaser or tenant undertakes work—maybe just fitting-out work—which will put the building into the capital goods scheme, will that be caught? The test is not about the vendor or lessor's intentions, but about the intention of the person responsible for financing. I look forward to the Minister's comments on those points.

I turn to the Value Added Tax (Finance) order No. 594. It may help the Committee if I go back to basics on value added tax, which is supposed to be fairly simple. The best rate, strangely enough, is to be zero-rated on one's supplies. One does not have to charge VAT, but one can reclaim it. Businesses that are standard-rated—that is, most manufacturing businesses and many service industries, but not banks and insurance companies, to which I shall refer later—charge VAT, currently at 17.5 per cent ., on what they supply or make, which is their outputs. However, they can recover the VAT on what they purchase. For example, a spanner factory pays VAT on the steel that it buys, and can recover it, and when it sells its spanners it charges VAT on what it sells, and accounts for the difference to Customs and Excise.

There is a third main category for VAT, which is the exempt category into which fall banks and insurance companies, among others. It sounds very good being exempt, but I am afraid that it is not as good as it sounds. One cannot charge VAT to one's customers and when one pays for one's supplies one cannot recover it. I shall not confuse the issue too much by talking about partial exemption, but that means, roughly speaking, where a supplier makes standard-rated and exempt supplies and in those circumstances there can be some recovery of input tax.

There is much to criticise in order No. 594 as it impacts on the management, processing and clearing, including settlement, of payment card transactions and others.

I wonder whether the Minister has any idea of the VATable turnover of this industry now. I understand that it is something like £500 million a year. It excludes Barclaycard, but I shall come back to Barclaycard in a while. That company is a vertically integrated business, which means that it falls within Barclays Bank plc and its services are VAT exempt notwithstanding the order.

I should add for the Minister's benefit that the companies that make these currently exempt transactions that will fall foul of the order, such as FDR, have very little partial exemption recovery and, if the statutory instrument becomes law, they will have very little benefit indeed from the recovery of input tax. So, effectively, it is a one-way ticket that hikes up the cost by 17.5 per cent.

We are not dealing here with tax avoidance, these companies have been exempt for VAT purposes since the inception of value added tax in 1972. The problem now JPEG 13 is that the Government are seeking to bring them into the VAT net, which will add 17.5 per cent. to their costs. The companies are currently exempt from VAT and deal exclusively with banks, which are also exempt from VAT and cannot recover VAT on supplies made to them. It is rather like the Government saying to members of the Committee, "You have a very valuable researcher to whom you pay £20,000 a year. From tomorrow, that goes up to £23,500 a year—an increase of £3,500 a year."

However, in the long run the Government will not be any better off, as the hon. Member for Maldon and East Chelmsford said. These activities will be brought in-house, like Barclaycard, and there will be redundancies and even less competition. I shall outline a number of aspects, but I hope that members of the Committee will bear it in mind that the order is likely to bring about significant redundancies and less competition.

There are other grounds for criticising the Government on account of the order.
