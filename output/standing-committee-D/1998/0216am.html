Social Security Contributions (Transfer of Functions, etc.) Bill [Lords]

The Minister of State, Department of Social Security (Mr. Stephen Timms): I beg to move.

That, during proceedings on the Social Security Contributions (Transfer of Functions, etc.) Bill [Lords], the Committee do meet on Tuesdays at half-past Ten o'clock and at half-past Four o'clock and on Thursdays at Nine o'clock and at half-past Four o'clock, except for Thursday 18th February, when the Committee will meet at half-past Ten o'clock and at half-past Four o'clock.

First, I welcome all hon Members to the Committee. In particular, may I welcome you, Mr. McWilliam, to the Chair? In the past, you and I have discussed a variety of matters, although not, as far as I can recall, social security. I am delighted that you are in charge because you will guide our deliberations with a firm and fair hand, as ever.

I also welcome the hon. Members for Grantham and Stamford (Mr. Davies) and for Newbury (Mr. Rendel), who will speak on behalf of the Opposition parties. We will shortly be joined by the Economic Secretary to the Treasury, my hon. Friend the Member for Leicester, West (Ms Hewitt), who will share the burden of ministerial contributions to the Committee. At the moment, she is speaking at a conference in the City.

I am pleased to say that the hon. Member for Newbury supproted the Bill on Second Reading, although the hon. Member for Grantham and Stamford did not. It will be interesting to discover Conservative Members' position on the matter this morning, given the confusion that appears to surround it. It became clear on Second Reading that the shadow Chancellor, in the excellent Deregulation Task Force report, strongly supported the Bill's thrust. I commend that splendid document, into which we may well dip during our deliberations, to those Conservative Members who have yet to read it.

Primarily, the Bill will combine the collection of all national insurance contributions with that of income tax. At the moment, 94 per cent. of national insurance contributions are collected by the Inland Revenue, along with PAYE or schedule D tax. It is therefore sensible to combine the organisations as well.

Secondly, the Bill will enable the newly integrated organisation to improve customer service to employers, employees, self-employed contributors and others. For the first time, customers will be able to sort out all tax and national insurance affairs through a single organisation.

Thirdly, the Bill will bring together the decision-making and appeals functions for tax and national insurance contributions within a single organisation That is a sensible provision, given that many Col. 4 issues, such as employment status, are common to both functions. New appeals on contributions matters, which the Government introduced in the Social Security Act 1998, will therefore be heard by the tax appeal commissioners.

I was intrigued to notice that the hon. Member for Grantham and Stamford signed up to a report by the tax law review committee that was published last February, which calls for the merger of the national insurance contributions disputes procedure with the tax appeals system. That report, which I have studied carefully, is another splendid document. As far as I can see, it contains no minority report or dissenting note. Of course, the bill will bring about the very measures that the committee, including the hon. Gentleman, sought. I hope that he will reflect that point in this comments this morning.

Fourthly, in the long term, we will gradually align the rules governing tax and national insurance contributions to simplify the system and reduce employers' costs. We will also streamline enforcement methods and thereby deal with non-compliant employers and contributors in a more efficient and cost-effective manner.

Fifthly, the powers of inspectors to check national insurance records are being transferred with Contributions Agency officers. The powers have been in existence since the national insurance scheme was established in 1948 and have not been a problem for employers in 50 years. The main change is that we are decriminalising the offence of delaying or obstructing an inspector and aligning it with the tax civil penalty system, but we are not extending the powers from national insurance contributions to cover tax.

Sixthly, concerns have been expressed elsewhere that the Bill could result in an ever-increasing exchange of information on the affairs of contributors and taxpayers between my Department and the Inland Revenue. I can reassure the Committee on that. The Bill protects flows of information that are necessary to maintain services, but goes no further on information sharing. The Benefits Agency must have the information on contributions that it needs to pay out benefits out benefits punctually and accurately. That flow is properly protected. The fight against fraud needs to continue. We are committed to that. Measures to allow disclosure of information for that purpose are in place and will continue through explicit statutory gateways.

I am delighted that we have been joined by my hon. Friend the Economic Secretary to the Treasury, who will share the ministerial burden in the Committee. I look forward to informative and illuminating discussions. I hope that all Committee members will feel assured that our deliberations have been for the benefit of those who contribute to and depend on the national insurance system.
