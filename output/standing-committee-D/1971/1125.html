The first point is that the unitary system was designed to cure the division between town and country in the organisation of services. Whatever one's views on the unitary system or the two-tier system or what emerged as something in between in the Redcliffe-Maud proposals, that remains a paramount aim. All of us who have had any concern with local government felt that the gap between town and country, inevitably in the area in which we live, had widened; that this was unreal; that, on the whole, 10 the town dweller wished to go into the country—the motorcar makes this available to him—and the country dweller wished to shop in the town and the two became more and more involved.

The second point is the desire to have one planning authority dealing with the total planning of an area. On Second Reading, I think I said that the basis behind the 1947 Town and Country Planning Act was to create 140 planning authorities. Even this may appear to pure planners—if one can use that adjective with the word "planners"—to be going too far and the figure should be much smaller. At any rate that was the accepted figure in 1947. The basis of this Local Government Bill is to provide something like 420 different planning authorities.

I said to the Secretary of State and to the Minister that, working within the context which they were working—and possibly that is where the misunderstanding arose between the Minister and I, and when he says that I was commending things, I was within the context which was given me and not within the question of the general principle—it may well be that we have reached fairly nearly where we ought to be, but I submit that the context itself is wrong.

If one had had the unitary system—as modified, of course—it would have been possible to cut down the number of planning authorities and they all would have been able to have the same sort of powers in the planning field, and I maintain that that would have been an advantage. We shall seek to show—and perhaps to remedy, if we are fortunate—that there will be considerable conflict as a result of the Bill between one set of planners and another, and this cannot be to the advantage of planning in general in this country. However, this is obviously a matter for a later stage, and I promised to keep to the rules of order. I thought that I ought to mention it at this moment, because it is germane to the whole question of the unitary authority and the present Bill.

The third basis to which I should like to refer is that what we now have under the Bill, and would not have had under the Redcliffe-Maud proposals, is that there are separate services administered—there is a logic for it, I have to admit—in different ways by different authorities. One of the great advantages of the JPEG 11 unitary system was that all the services were administered together, and I think we have lost as a result of this. So that also is something that weighs with us.

I do not say that if one provides education at the district council level it will be inefficient—I do not believe that—but there are tremendous advantages in having a much larger educational authority. I pointed out that it could cope with the many minority interests—the handicapped children, the blind, the deaf, and so on—and that this is a positive advantage in our educational system.

At least if we are never to agree on anything else in this Committee, Sir Stephen, I think that I would carry every hon.
