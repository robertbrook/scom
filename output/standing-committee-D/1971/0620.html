The main argument here stems from what other countries have done. Col. 142 It has not happened since 1958 in the Common Market countries. There has been no change in their social provisions. They already had better provision than we have, and they have if anything increased the difference—but as a result purely of domestic policies and not of harmonisation.

In France, if one retires at 60, the pension is 20 per cent. of average earnings for the last 10 years. If one retires at 65, it is 40 per cent., and at the age of 70 it is 60 per cent. These are large increments for the pensioner who stays at work—much larger than for those who stay at work in this country. It is a minimum of £2.50, which is exceedingly low, and a maximum of £10. But the majority will do better in France than in this country.

In Germany, the situation is substantially superior to ours. The pension is 60 per cent. of insurable earnings over 40 years of insurance and is reduced for every year for which there is no insurance. But in most cases, of course, there is insurance. That level approximates to the level aimed at by the National Superannuation Bill, which was lost in 1970, and is well above any apparent intention of the British Government at this time.

In Italy, the pension is 1.85 per cent. of average earnings for the highest three years of the last five years multiplied by the number of years of contribution up to 40. That is a complicated formula, but it works out in practice at a minimum pension of £4 and a maximum of 74 per cent. of average earnings three years before retirement. There is an automatic revaluation of pensions each year for every 2 per cent. change in the cost of living index. That is a substantially better protection for the pensioner population in Italy than we have.

In Belgium, the situation is similar to that in Germany—60 per cent. of average gross earnings for which there has been insurance provided over a period of 45 years—that is, the whole of working life. In the Netherlands, the pension is nearly £10 less one-fiftieth for every year short of insurance below 50. That is a similar kind of formula although with a rather higher average level of pension at nearly £10. In Luxembourg, the basic pension is £4 plus 1.6 per cent. of total insured earnings, giving a minimum of £6.50 a week and a maximum of five-sixths of Image Col. 143 insured earnings—very much higher than we provide.
