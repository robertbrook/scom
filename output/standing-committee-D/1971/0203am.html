The Bill as it stands will maintain the City of London with its antiquated and undemocratic government and also maintain the rather unfair influence which I shall show it can play over the government of other parts of London. The City's undemocratic form of government is being preserved.

It might be worth refreshing the memory of the Committee with some details of the rather undemocratic structure of government in the City, because it does not fit in with the pattern and the structure of local government which we have been discussing for the rest of the country in the Bill.

The prime instrument of government in the City is the body known as the Court of Common Council, referred to time and again in the Bill and references to which are deleted in the consequential Amendments. On frequent occasions in the Bill the Court of Common Council is equated with the council of one of the London boroughs. The Bill provides in various places for things that can be done and certain powers exercised either by a London Borough Council or by the Common Council of the City of London.

This Court of Common Council is a body not of the relatively modest size which Ministers have suggested is appropriate for the size of county councils or even district councils with populations up JPEG 1221 to 200,000. It is still a body consisting of 159 members. I understand that there are mumblings and grumblings in the City and suggestions that it should be reduced to 130 members, but it is considerably larger than any of the councils which we are proposing to set up under this Bill in the recommendations from the Home Office referred to earlier by my hon. Friends. The Minister and the Redcliffe-Maud Report were reluctant to see councils much larger than 60, 80 or 100 members. Here we have a population of 7,000 in the control of a council of 159 members.

The other anomaly is that while there are only 7,000 electors for parliamentary purposes in the City of London, when it comes to local government elections or elections for the Court of Common Council, there is a considerably inflated electorate consisting largely of businessmen, many of them fairly well-off, some very wealthy, who swamp the total residential population of the City. What is more serious is that in the situation where there are a large number of influential people and a small number of people who live in the area, instead of having a secret ballot where people can make their vote without it being seen, the procedure for elections for the Court of Common Council still follows the traditions of the ward mote, where initial election is by show of hands.

My right hon. Friend the Member for Fulham (Mr. Michael Stewart) has pointed out the difficutlies that he found on one occasion when he attended a ward mote. On arrival, distinguished people told him, "Quick, put up your hand". Thinking that this was an instruction for something he had to do, he found himself voting for a councillor without knowing the name of the candidate. This system of ward motes is surely outdated in this day and age.

Even more extraordinary is the system by which one becomes a candidate for the Court of Common Council. As I understand it, even under the amendments in the City of London (Various Powers) Act, 1967, one can only become a candidate for the Court of Common Council if one is a freeman, either by inheritance or by purchase. It costs Â£3.15 to become a freeman, or Â£2.10 for members of livery companies, because in those cases it is assumed that they have paid enough to their livery company already.

1222 It seems monstrous to tell people that in order to exercise their democratic rights to stand as candidates for the council of a local government in this country, they must pay Â£3.15 before they can stand for election. In fact, however, the position is worse than this. Not only must one pay Â£3.15, but one's application to buy a free-manship in the City of London has to be approved by the Court of Common Council itself. The Court of Common Council is not alone. There is a two-tier, or perhaps a two-sphere, system of government in the City as eslewhere. There is also the court of aldermen, which includes the Lord Mayor and consists of 26 aldermen. There are 26 wards in the City and one would imagine that each ward would have one alderman. In fact, that is not the case. The wards of Cripplegate Within and Cripple-gate Without are combined, for the purpose of electing aldermen, into one ward. but to make up the 26 there is also an alderman who sits for the ward of Bridge Without. The ward of Bridge Without is so much "without" the City that it is not even in the City of London, but is in the London Borough of Southwark. Therefore, a special procedure has to be devised whereby an alderman from outside the City can be elected for this notional ward in order to make up the court of aldermen.

I mentioned a little earlier the Lord Mayor, and here again we might assume that, as provided in this Bill, the Lord Mayor would be elected by the members of the Court of Common Council, perhaps together with the aldermen. But no; in the City there is yet another body, the Court of Common Hall, which is not elected at all, and which in turn elects the Lord Mayor. This council consists of the Liverymen of the City Companies.
