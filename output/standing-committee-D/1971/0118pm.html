Mr. Roger Moate: When we arose this morning I was explaining that there was total opposition to the Thameside proposal from the area that I represent.

When the Maud proposals and, subsequently, the Labour Government proposals, were published there was great opposition to the idea of Kent's being divided into two unitary authorities, because it had made sense as an administrative and geographical whole. The proposals meant that large numbers of freestanding boroughs and towns would be submerged in large unitary authorities of about half a million people. Consequently, Col. 646 when the Conservative Government's proposals were published they were widely welcomed, because they enabled the Kent Council to remain as one unit, using the flexibility of the second tier to enable many small authorities to combine to create a sensibly-sized unit.

That process was proceeding satisfactorily in my area, in which local authorities combined to set up steering committees to enable them to work towards a sensible reform of local government. Then, out of the blue, came a proposal from across the Thames estuary that Kent should be carved into two; that our towns should be merged into a unit with the Medway towns and that we should be linked with an area about 16 miles away, across the Thames Estuary—I doubt whether it is visible on a clear day—an area with which we have no community interest.

I am sure that hon. Members will understand why the idea was received with some scepticism. I have no wish to be unduly critical of Southend—with you in the Chair, Sir Stephen, that would be undiplomatic—but it was not merely the nature of the proposals that excited opposition; it was the way in which they were put forward.

Our local authorities heard of the proposal in the first place through a Press release issued by Southend. The authorities were not consulted, which indicates that these were eleventh-hour proposals, produced out of a desperate desire to create a new entity to give more power to Southend and the Medway towns: Although I sympathise with that desire I suggest that the proposition is impractical.

I refer to the letter that indicates the way in which the proposals were received in our area. It was a reply by the clerk of one of the councils to the Town Clerk of Southend. Hon. Members will be aware of the normal diplomatic and careful language used by town clerks and will recognise its use in the letter, which says:

"Dear Mr. Laws.


Your Council's methods appear to be as deplorable as your scheme is crackbrained and we shall oppose it by every means at our disposal."

That was an official missive from one council to another. Although the others were not couched in exactly the same language the message was essentially similar.
