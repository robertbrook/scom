NATIONAL INSURANCE BILL

Clause 5
FINALITY OF DECISIONS

Question proposed—( 4th July)—That the Clause stand part of the Bill.

10.30 a.m.

Question again proposed.

The Chairman
: I remind the Committee that we are to consider at the same time the following new Clauses:

New Clause 5— "Finding of fact to be conclusive"—

"The provisions contained in section 75(1) of the National Insurance Act 1965, and the corresponding provision in section 50(1) of the National Insurance (Industrial Injuries) Act 1965, that the decision of a claim or question as there mentioned is to be final shall make any finding of fact or other determination embodied in or necessary to a decision, or on which it is based, conclusive for the purpose of any further decision".

New Clause 6—" Decision on origin of injury or disability "—

"A decision (given under section 48(2) of the National Insurance (Industrial Injuries) Act 1965 or otherwise) that an accident was an industrial accident is to be taken as determining the origin of any injury or disability suffered by the claimant".

Mrs. Barbara Castle
: Clause 5 is an alien intruder in a Bill designed to better the lot of the casualties in our society, to make better provision for the old, sick, unemployed, widows, disabled and industrially injured. While the rest of the Bill seeks to improve the eligibility rules of some of these categories and the payments to all of them, sitting like a cruel cuckoo in the nest is Clause 5, which is designed to weaken the rights and lessen the opportunities of justice for injured workers.

On Second Reading, the Secretary of State made a fairly short and, to the ordinary person, obscure reference to the purposes of Clause 5. At that time, having read the Bill hurriedly and listening to the Secretary of State in slight bewilderment, I was unaware of its significance. I believe 358 that I am correct in saying that no great attention was directed to this subject during the Second Reading debate.

If hon. Members will turn to column 720 of HANSARD of 18th May, where the Secretary of State deals with Clause 5, they will be little wiser at the end of the reading those four or five paragraphs than they are now. It was not until trade unions and their legal advisers approached trade union Members and other Opposition Members that the Clause was studied in depth and that what we were asked to do in passing Clause 5 became clear to us.

I am certain that it is not clear to hon. Members on the Government benches and that shortly they will be loyally behind the Secretary of State when the Clause is put to the Committee. Therefore, I wish to spend a little time spelling out just what we are being asked to do, because this is one of the most important Clauses of the Bill.

Clause 5 has one sole purpose and effect. It is to overrule decisions in the House of Lords, such as in the case of Mr. Dowling, Mr. Hudson and Mr. Jones, three injured workmen who, having been awarded injury benefit, had to fight the hon. Gentleman's Department all along the line to get disablement benefit. Although we shall oppose the Clause tooth and nail, we believe that that will not be enough and that is clear, following these recent legal cases, that we must remove any dubiety from the law.

The fact that so much litigation has had to take place to establish what the law was intended to mean proves that the law should be clarified, which is why we have suggested new Clauses 5 and 6. If we are successful in removing Clause 5, I understand that it will be in order to vote on our two new Clauses. But if the Committee overrules our opposition to Clause 5, we shall not have the opportunity to vote on the new Clauses under the rules of order.

I should like to put that on record, because new Clauses 5 and 6 are an integral part of our case, which we put seriously and with sincerity. I should not like it to be assumed from the fact that there is no Division on the new Clauses that we had lost interest in our proposals, or ceased to think that they were vital. The present law must be clarified one way or the other.

Mrs. Castle

The hon. Gentleman's Department, having been defeated in its interpretation by the House of Lords, now says, "That will never do. These injured workmen have got away with their claim. We'll put a stop to that in future." By Clause 5, the Government seek to clarify the law contrary to the long-established rights of injured workmen. The Opposition, by new Clauses 5 and 6, wish decisively to clarify the law in their favour, so that tortuous legal battles will not take place in future.

When I look at the enormous expense to which trade unions have been put in taking such cases through the whole panoply of the law, I believe that the case for new Clauses 5 and 6 is unanswerable. In instancing some of the legal battles that have raged over entitlement to help injured workers, I shall cover a number of years, going back to the period of the Labour Government. Some of these battles were fought when we were in office; some more recently. In some of these cases the DHSS, or its predecessor Ministries, opposed the workmen's claims and the unions' arguments.

As a Labour Government introduced the far-reaching, radically reforming Industrial Injuries Act, 1946, establishing such basic principles as the finality of decision, now set at risk by Clause 5, it is obvious that what has happened has been that subsequent Labour Ministers were persuaded by their Departments to appeal against the principle of the finality of decisions. These are examples where bureaucracy can take control. I know, because I was a Minister, how dependent a Minister is upon Departmental advice in such expert matters. I believe that Ministers like my right hon. Friend, Miss Herbison at that time, who by name was involved in one of these legal disputes, would have been aghast if they had realised to what they were lending themselves in wishing to fight an injured workman's claim. It is imperative to apply the law positively so that no civil servant and no Departmental legal adviser can conduct the sort of campaign that has been conducted in these cases and other cases that I shall put before the Committee this morning.

Two major issues are involved in Clause 5 and new Clauses 5 and 6. The first is the need to establish that an industrial 360 accident has taken place. This is crucial to all other claims for benefit. The 1965 Industrial Injuries Act is a purely consolidating Act, merely consolidating the principles of the 1946 Act and later insurance Acts, and so when talking of the 1965 Act, I am talking of the intentions all along the line from the initiation of the legislation on industrial injuries.

Section 48 of the National Insurance (Industrial Injuries) Act, 1965, entitles a worker to obtain a declaration that the accident he has suffered is an industrial accident. He is entitled to obtain such a declaration irrespective of whether any industrial injury benefit may be payable at that time.

The declaration is of great importance to the worker, because, even if he is not claiming benefit at that time, his condition may deteriorate later, when the records have been lost. All of us have had examples of constituents saying, "I have developed such and such a condition", and "I remember falling a year ago and I believe that this condition springs from an accident I had at work". We know how difficult it is to go back to establish these claims. Therefore, since a man's condition may deteriorate in later years when the records have been lost, the fact that he has a declaration establishing that he has had an industrial accident is vital to him.

There is a second situation, in a sense more tragic, where such a declaration is imperative. The injured man may die and his widow may be seeking an industrial death benefit on the ground that his injury arose through the fault of his employer and that he died from the industrial injury, or from the effects of that injury. The intentions of Parliament in 1946, as repeated in 1965, are clear. Section 48(2) of the 1965 Act reads:

"… any person suffering personal injury by accident shall be entitled, if he claims the accident was an industrial accident, to have the question determined, and a declaration made and recorded accordingly,".

Hon. Members opposite are a jury this morning. I hope that they will try to understand the seriousness of the case I am trying to make. Clause 5 is so detailed that I doubt whether they will have appreciated its importance. Surely the intention of stating

"… any person suffering personal injury by accident shall be entitled, if he claims the accident was an industrial accident, to have that question determined, and a declaration made and recorded…" JPEG 361 was overwhelmingly clear. Yet those words are the subject of dispute, and an interpretation is to be placed upon them in Clause 5 which invalidates the intentions of Parliament since the introduction of industrial injury legislation.

Section 48(4) of the Industrial Injuries Act further says that any such declaration

"shall be conclusive for the purposes of any claim for benefit in respect of that accident,…"

Again, I should have thought that that was self-evident. How has it worked in practice? As I said earlier, a Departmental spokesman can sometimes impose an administrative bias that works in an entirely different way.

I turn now to the first of the recent cases—that of Mr. Dowling—which have inspired the introduction of Clause 5. I assume that the hon. Gentleman does not deny—it was clear from the Secretary of State's remarks on Second Reading—that it was the House of Lords judgment in the case of Mr. Dowling, as well as other cases to which I intend to refer, which led to the formulation of the Clause.

Mr. Dowling met an accident at work in August, 1961. He was lifting a heavy flagstone when he felt a pain in his chest. He was certified unfiit to work, and was found to be suffering from a small hiatus hernia. He claimed injury benefit, but his claim was disallowed. He appealed to a local tribunal, but his appeal was dismissed. His union, which fought the case from the outset, appealed to the commissioners, and the case was heard in London before Mr. Nelson, the union being represented by counsel. A consultant chest physician was brought from Blackpool to give evidence. As will be realised, the cost to the union of fighting such a case was considerable.

The Ministry, as it then was, went to enormous lengths to oppose Mr. bowling's claim. It mobilised doctors, solicitors and counsel. If Mr. Dowling had not been supported by the union, he would have been in a bad way. The commissioner, Mr. Nelson, allowed the appeal, and gave Mr. Dowling a declaration that his accident was an industrial accident—in other words, that the hernia had been caused by the lifting—and awarded him injury benefit.

Section 50(1) of the 1965 Act—the provisions of which are again the subject of Clause 5—states explicitly:

"… any decision of a claim or question in accordance with this Part of this Act shall be final."

362 Thus, one of the important issues upon which hon. Members are being asked to vote this morning is whether Section 50 of the 1965 Act, which incorporates one of the central doctrines of the 1946 Act on the finality of decisions, should now be overruled. That would be the effect of our passing Clause 5. That has been the subject of all the argument over the years.

The doctrine of finality of decisions should have settled the case. The man had gone to the commissioner, who had ruled in his favour that he had had an industrial accident, because he ruled that the hernia had been caused by the lifting. Surely it is in accordance with Section 50 of the 1965 Act that that decision should be final: but not a bit of it. This is the kind of situation that I had not appreciated, and I am sure that many other Members had not.

The second aspect is the finality of decisions. Having been given a declaration that he had had an industrial accident and having been awarded injury benefit, in due course Mr. Dowling went on to claim disablement benefit. He was examined by a medical board, which decided that the hernia was not caused by the lifting and therefore Mr. Dowling had not had an accident. Finality of decision? It would seem to the ordinary person that either the commissioner ought never to have given the injury benefit or that the disablement benefit should have followed automatically, the condition still being there.

That was only the beginning of the legal battle that the man then had to undertake. With the help of his union, he appealed to a medical tribunal, which confirmed the medical board's finding. He then appealed to the commissioner—not Mr. Nelson this time, but a Mr. Neligan—and the union pointed out that if Mr. Nelson had rejected Mr. Dowling's claim for injury benefit, his claim for disablement benefit would have fallen automatically. He would have been told that as he was not entitled to injury benefit, clearly he could not be entitled to disablement benefit. He would not have had the declaration and the acceptance that the condition was caused by the accident. Why should the vast Department of State be allowed a second bite at the cherry? Why should Mr. Dowling or any other injured workman be put at peril a second time on the same question of fact?

I ask the Committee to consider the balance of power involved here. On the one hand, there is an injured workman.

JPEG 363 He may or may not have a powerful union behind him as, thank heaven, Mr. Dowling had. Against him there is the panoply of a Department of State that is using public money to employ medical advisers, legal counsel and all the rest to have a second go at the injured workman who has already won his claim.

At that point, the Ministry argued against what I call the doctrine of origin that is enunciated in the new Clauses. It would seem to any fair-minded layman that if a workman has been given a declaration that he has had an industrial accident, that should be conclusive: but not a bit of it. The Ministry's expert representatives argued that the medical appeal tribunal had been right in ruling that Mr. Dowling had not had an accident at all. They said that all that the law meant was that although an accident is conclusive and binding on a medical appeal tribunal, it did not mean that the claimant had had an accident, but merely that if he did have an accident, it would have been an industrial accident.

These are the arguments available to hon. Members in the legal documents. Is not this typical of the tortuous inhumanity of the bureaucratic mind? Did Parliament really mean when it passed the Acts of 1946 and 1965, that if a man claimed that he had had an industrial accident and had a right to a declaration to that effect, the declaration did not establish that he had had an accident, but that, if he had had an accident, it would have been an industrial accident?

That is what is at issue this morning. This is the new doctrine which Parliament is asked to embody in Clause 5. If Members vote for it, they will be voting for that kind of rigmarole. They will be voting to put workmen endlessly at risk of arguments with the Ministry.

What was even more alarming was that the second Commissioner, Mr. Neligan, dismissed Mr. Dowling's appeal. I ask the Committee to take this on board. When a workman has to appeal, re-appeal and counter-appeal and go to different tribunals, medical or legal tribunals, or statutory authorities, he gets conflicting verdicts according to the statutory authority or medical authority to which he goes. The hon. Gentleman may try to quote that in aid of his defence. He may say, "They can all be wrong, so it is only right for the Ministry to say, 'The previous 364 decision was all wrong, so let us have another.'"

What this means is that no medical tribunal is necessarily sacrosanct and that the Ministry is claiming the right to take a workman through tribunal after tribunal and court after court until it gets the verdict that rules out the workman's claim. I shall proceed to prove it.

As I said, the commissioner, Mr. Neligan, taking a different line from the commissioner, Mr. Nelson, dismissed Mr. Dowling's claim. But the union was determined not to give up. It went to the Court of Appeal, which decided in Mr. Dowling's favour by two judges to one. Mr. Dowling received his disablement benefit and special hardship allowance. But the Ministry did not intend to give up. Someone had got away with an entitlement which officialdom thought might have been over-generous, although the first decision had been in the man's favour. So the Ministry went to the House of Lords.

Again I ask the Committee to realise the disparity of power. What would have happened to Mr. Dowling if he had gone to the House of Lords? The House of Lords dismissed the Ministry's appeal by four to one. In other words, the House of Lords accepted that, when a claimant is granted an accident declaration, it meant that the claimant had had an accident and not merely that, if he had had an accident, it would have been an industrial accident. Indeed, Lord Hodson pointed out that an accident declaration which did not mean that the claimant had had an accident would have been of precious little value to him and would have put him in the position at a later date, when his evidence might no longer be available and the records might have been destroyed, of trying to establish the facts in support of his claim years afterwards. Lord Hodson also pointed out that the injured man might die.

What is the point of repeating in Section 48,

if the Minister can later say that an accident declaration does not mean that the workman had an accident, but merely that if he had an accident, it would have been an industrial accident, although the Ministry says that he never had an accident? The Ministry says, "Sorry, we thought that point was determined and established a long time ago: we have the right to take the man through the courts till the end of time, till we win." Our new Clause 6 is designed to make the point clear in the man's favour and in the sort of terms that Lord Hodson spelled out in the House of Lords. I think that the Committee can appreciate the bitterness of trade unions on this score. They deal with injured members at first hand in hundreds and thousands. They thought that Parliament's intentions were clear. The Labour Government's intentions were clear in 1946, but now there will be legal hairsplitting at enormous cost to establish a decision contrary to Parliament's intentions. The unions say, "You then tell us that we don't respect the law, and yet the Department of Health and Social Security has not respected the law."

The Department has continued to try to twist the law in the direction it wants, using public money to employ every expensive device to do the injured workman out of his entitlement. The Department has never accepted the House of Lords ruling in the Dowling case. The proof of that lies in Clause 5. So furious were the Government 366 with the House of Lords ruling in the Dowling case that they said, "We must change this law. We are not having this nonsense." They never accepted it. They have seized every opportunity arising from slight differences between the Dowling case and other cases to re-argue the whole issue, despite the House of Lords judgment.

They did so in the case of Mr. Jones and Mr. Hudson, two workmen who were awarded injury benefit for myocardial infarction. Once again, the union fought the cases through the commissioner, the High Court and the Court of Appeal, finally taking the Department to the House of Lords, in order to get the medical appeal tribunal's verdict overruled.

Why should it be unsatisfactory? As the evidence of the conflict of findings in these cases to which I have referred reveals, medical boards and tribunals are not infallible. The hon. Mrs. Castle
: If any member of the Committee is shocked by the statement that there is no good reason why the medical judgment of the tribunals of boards should be preferred to the judgment of the statutory authorities, let me JPEG 367 point out that Clause 5 admits exactly that. Clause 5(1) says not merely that the decisions of non-medical authorities should not be binding on medical authorities—there would at least be some consistency if that were the case—but that the decisions of the medical authority will not be binding on the non-medical authority. Why, then, the Secretary of State's horror that medical practitioners and consultants should be expected to accept verdicts contrary to their medical judgment?

The Clause can only mean that the Department wants the right to continue endlessly to contest decisions given in the claimant's favour until they give up in despair, or the Department finds a medical board or tribunal to give it the verdict it seeks, even if it should contradict the finding of an earlier medical board or tribunal. What matters to the Department is the finding rather than by whom it was given.

Here I cite the example of another hiatus hernia case when a medical authority found in the man's favour, which decision was later reversed by a non-medical authority. Did the Minister dispute that decision on the grounds that the medical judgment of the medical tribunal was superior to the judgment of a non-medical tribunal? Not a bit of it, simply because the verdict was the one the Department wanted, namely, against the man. The Ministry did not protest against this case, yet to justify its silence the Secretary of State would have to stand the arguments on their heads.

Let me give details of the case, which is outlined in Decision R(I)11/61 published by the Stationery Office. I suggest that hon. Members obtain copies and study it. In October, 1957, the claimant was lifting a heavy weight with a block and tackle when he felt a stabbing pain in his stomach. However, he continued to work and did not claim injury benefit. A year later he was admitted to hospital and was operated on for hiatus hernia. The insurance officer found that the man had suffered an industrial accident in October, 1957. A medical board found that the hernia was caused by the accident and assessed the resultant disablement at 100 per cent. from the time of the accident. This, one would think, had settled the matter. The Ministry could have appealed against the finding of the medical board, but it did not. Therefore the decision of the medical board was final 368 on any reasonable interpretation of the Act.

A few weeks later the man died, as a result of pulmonary embolism, a complication of the operation to repair the hernia. His wife claimed industrial death benefit, the appeal tribunal finding in her favour. The case was then taken to the commissioner, presumably by the Ministry, although the Ministry had not appealed against the previous medical board's decision and, therefore, had no complaint about the decision.

The commissioner found that the insurance officer's finding—that the claimant's husband had suffered an industrial accident—was valid, but that the decision of the medical board that the incident had caused the hiatus hernia was not. A senior medical officer of the Ministry of Pensions and National Insurance as it then was produced a great deal of evidence on the man's medical history and what his workmates were supposed to have said about his condition.

The man was dead. He could not defend himself against the evidence brought by the senior medical officer of the MPNI. Evidence had not been brought earlier when the appeal should have been made. Yet, although the man was dead, his widow had to fight for the death benefit, She was alone and bewildered and the man who could provide the evidence of his disease was dead. Yet the commissioner accepted the evidence of the senior medical officer in the end.

The commissioner found:

"whereas the operation was necessary for the repair of the hernia and the pulmonary embolism which caused the deceased's death was a complication of that operation, the accident did not cause or aggravate the hernia."

I hope that the Committee will agree that it is nauseous that, the Ministry having failed to appeal against the original medical verdict, it could then re-open the whole medical case after a man's death in order to do his widow out of her death benefit. I hope, too, that in the light of that case the Committee will be able to assess the oddity of the Secretary of State's claim that it would be unsatisfactory for medical tribunals to have their medical judgments overridden by the statutory authorities. But that is just what happened in this case.

This case occurred before the House of Lords ruling in the Dowling case, or in the JPEG 369 cases of Hudson and Jones. If it had come afterwards, the widow would almost certainly have got the death benefit. It is to make sure that people like her do not get their death benefit that Clause 5 has been introduced. The purpose of the Clause is to allow that kind of case to occur again despite the House of Lords ruling.

The simple issue before us is whether, faced with the express provision in the Act that

"any decision of a claim or question … shall be final",

that provision should be amended by Clause 5 to enable the Department to go on endlessly re-opening and re-arguing issues until it obtains the verdict against the claimant that it wants. Of course there must be provision for cases to be reviewed by a medical board. Section 40 of the 1965 Act provides for that in two circumstances: first, a decision may be reviewed where fresh evidence has come to light which seems to indicate non-disclosure of certain crucial information; secondly, an assessment may be reviewed where "unforeseen aggravation" has taken place.

But in the case that I mentioned no such review was called for by the Ministry. There was merely the cavalier reversal of a decision which, if Section 50 means anything, should have been final. Indeed, when an injured workman asks for his assessment to be reviewed on the grounds of "unforeseen aggravation", again the Department pursues him relentlessly until it gets him down.

Let us take the case of another Mr. Jones, Mr. G. T. Jones of Maesteg. He is almost blind, nearly 70 per cent. disabled, and he receives no disablement benefit. I shall not weary the Committee with the details of the case. The crucial point is this. It is conceded that Mr. Jones met with an industrial accident in February, 1953. In 1961, he claimed disablement benefit for uritis and he was assessed at 4 per cent. for life by a medical appeal tribunal—one of those bodies whose medical judgment, we are told, should prevail.

In 1969, he applied for a review of that assessment on the grounds of unforeseen aggravation, and the medical board increased the assessment to 70 per cent. The Department appealed, of course, and the medical appeal tribunal reversed the decision.

The union took up the case. The commissioner twice accepted the union's 370 appeal and twice the case was sent back for re-hearing by medical appeal tribunals. On one occasion, the commissioner sent the case back on the grounds that the medical tribunal had not given adequate reasons for its decision. In March, 1972, the third tribunal again found that there had been no aggravation.

This time, under pressure from the commissioner, it set out its reasons. The tribunal argued that there had been no aggravation, not on the grounds that the man was no worse, but by re-opening the whole of the original issue, namely, whether the eye condition was caused or partly caused by his accident, which had previously been decided by a medical authority.

The tribunal went right back to square one. It drew attention to the report of a consultant, Mr. Pillai. That report had been available before the medical appeal tribunal of 1961 which had found in Mr. Jones's favour. Mr. Pillai had argued in his report that the uritis was not caused by the accident but merely aggravated by it.

The 1961 tribunal had rejected that argument. Yet another medical appeal tribunal, which was supposed to be dealing with aggravation and nothing else, decided to accept Mr. Pillai's argument and to say, in effect, that the uritis had never been caused by the accident and that the worsening of the man's condition was therefore constitutional in origin, and the appeal was rejected. So here we have a situation in which a medical appeal tribunal in 1972 rejected the finding of a medical appeal tribunal in 1961 on the basis of a report which was not fresh evidence and which had been available to the 1961 tribunal.

We argue that under Section 50 of the 1965 Act the findings of the 1961 medical appeal tribunal should have been final. It is intolerable that 11 years later, on the basis of a stale old report, the whole question of the cause of the condition could be re-raised.

Is that just? If the 1961 finding was final, as the Act says it should have been, the 1972 tribunal had no right to resurrect the consultant's old report on the question of the cause of the accident. It should have decided only whether any aggravation had taken place.

It is to make absolutely sure that the judgment of the House of Lords does not prevent this happening over and over again that we have Clause 5 and it enables the JPEG 371 finality of decisions principle to be undermined, whereas New Clause 5 would establish that a decision was indisputable.

If one medical tribunal can reverse the decision of another, what becomes of the sanctity of medical decisions to which the Secretary of State attaches such importance? What happens when medical practitioners and consultants are forced to accept findings contrary to their medical judgment? In this case, the medical authority was overruled by a non-medical authority. It was a question of the Ministry soldiering on through a welter of medical decisions until it found the one that suited it best. Can the Committee be surprised if unions feel bitter and believe that Clause 5 is just a conspiracy to ensure that a workman never gets the final benefit of a decision in his favour, never gets the benefit of any doubt.

I could give hundreds of examples to the Committee, many of them illustrating the point we are trying to make. But before concluding I should like to refer to the case of Mrs. Kingsnorth of Northfleet, Kent. In May, 1963, Mrs. Kingsnorth suffered an industrial accident when she fell and injured her wrist. In September, 1963, a medical board assessed her disablement provisionally at 15 per cent., later reduced to 5 per cent.

In January, 1965, Mrs. Kingsnorth applied for a review on the grounds that she was suffering from a detached retina of the left eye. In June of that year, a medical board accepted the eye condition as being relevant to the accident and made a provisional assessment of 30 per cent. for the two conditions. The Minister was entitled to appeal against that decision, but he did not do so, and therefore, under the Act that decision became final.

In September, 1966 a medical board again examined her and made an assessment of 30 per cent. allowance. This time the Ministry agreed. The Ministry accepted as a fact that, during the accident, Mrs. Kingsnorth fell forward and struck her head. But the Ministry argued that, on balance of probability, the detachment of the retina had nothing to do with it. The provisions in the Clause are concerned primarily with decisions which affect disablement benefit under the Industrial Injuries Act. There is a direct connection in the Clause with the short-term injury benefit and the long-term disablement benefit. Disablement benefit is paid if, as a result of the relevant accident or disease, there is a loss of physical or mental faculty to the extent that the resulting disablement is 1 per cent. or more.

The special medical questions which arise on a disablement claim are determined by medical boards or by medical appeal tribunals. The first question is whether the relevant accident has resulted in a loss of faculty; secondly, to what degree the amount of disablement is to be assesssed; thirdly, what period is to be taken into account in the assessment.

Until 1966, the relevant provisions of the Account were interpreted as meaning that in deciding the question of disablement benefit, the medical adjudicating authorities were not bound in any way by the findings of the lay statutory authorities when they awarded injury benefit in the preceding short-term benefit period. The medical authorities looked at the whole question afresh in order to decide the medical consequences of the accident. That was the position under the 1948 Act, but doubts arose as a result of the medical judgment.

The position changed as a result of the House of Lords judgment in the case of Dowling in 1966. That judgment was interpreted in the Department as meaning that the medical authorities were bound to accept that there had been an accident and some personal injury. It was not considered that it required the medical authorities to be bound by findings as to the nature or extent of the personal injury made by the lay statutory authorities. For instance, if it had apparently been accepted that a hernia was the personal injury caused by an accident, but the medical board found that there was not JPEG 389 in fact a hernia, but only strain of the groin, it would be open to the board to give its decision on the basis that the strain of groin was the relevant injury.

Similarly, if it was felt that where there was more than one personal injury, it was open to the medical authorities to accept only one injury resulting from the accident if they so determined. In the view of the Department that the judgment would be conclusive as to the nature of the injury only in the very exceptional case where the accident and injury were regarded as inseparable, it was decided that the consequences would not be sufficiently serious to warrant any amendment of the Act. I think that it is for that reason that the previous Administration felt that the 1966 judgment did not call for clarification or amendment.

This view of the effect of the Dowling judgment was challenged in the cases of Jones and Hudson, which eventually reached the House of Lords last year. In the course of the proceedings it was contended on behalf of the Secretary of State that the decision in Dowling's case was wrong and should be overruled. That contention was rejected by their Lordships who decided by a majority of four to three that the Dowling decision should not be overruled and by a majority of five to two, that the appeals should be allowed as indistinguishable from Dowling's case. The Dowling judgment had decided, so it was held, that the decision of the statutory authorities was final for all purposes relating to the relevant accident, namely, for disablement as well as for injury benefit purposes. The judgments in all three cases were majority judgments and their lordships foresaw that amending legislation might be necessary.
