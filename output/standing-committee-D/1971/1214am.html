We are as anxious as the Minister to make progress and we do not want unduly to prolong the debate on the Amendment; but I should like to state that there is a great deal of misgiving in many parts of the country, and certainly on these benches, about the sheer practicality, even if the Bill becomes law in anything like its present form, of getting all the administrative changes implemented in our local authorities in the time scale which it sets down.

The Amendment is a modest attempt to give local authorities one year's breathing space to implement the enormous amount of administrative work. Indeed, one might say that they will be suffering from administrative indigestion if town clerks and treasurers attempt to implement, over such a wide range, the problems which the Bill will provide for them.

The first problem is that of staffing. The question of the future of the staff, we all agree is of great importance—the transfer of staff from one authority to another. The appointment of chief officers and other officers is something which many of us, who have sat on local authorities, know takes a very long time. Friend the Member for Widnes (Mr. Oakes) said at the last Sitting, there is a great deal of work done in anticipation of the Bill receiving the Royal Assent. This raises the question whether in some parts the Bill is ultra vires.

There is the question of local government finance. I mentioned staffing only to illustrate the size of the problem. The Government are seeking to set the dates for the future of local government before we have any real knowledge of what the Government intend to do in respect of the future of local government finance, what the financing arrangements will be within the various authorities, how far there will be precepting arrangements, and how far authorities may levy their own rates. Image Col. 379 The disposition of the district under the Bill is important. The area of the district is something of a plum in South Lancashire in that it is generally regarded as a very good development area which is ripe for large development. By existing local government standards, it is a comparatively large district in area. There are, in fact, 12 urban districts only in Lancashire—out of 65—with an area larger than Westhoughton, and six of those 12 areas embrace either large tracts of upland or other land unlikely to be built upon for reasons of nature conservation.

The district unfortunately has been bred on coal, textiles and agriculture. Coal has contributed and textiles are not so significant, but there has been some other industrial developments. In 1950-51 there were proposals to develop the district by building to meet Salford's housing difficulties, but by 1957 Salford changed its mind. Subsequent to 1957 there were proposals for large-scale development to meet the Manchester difficulties, as an alternative to Manchester development in Cheshire, but in 1966 the Manchester scheme was finally abandoned by the then Minister saying that as there was not sufficient prospect of making progress with a town development scheme, he had withdrawn his support from the scheme. This shows that Westhoughton has been looked upon by surrounding large areas such as Manchester and Salford as a suitable place for their large-scale developments. For the purpose of the development plan for the Manchester proposals, it was estimated that 2,700 acres of agricultural land would be used for development, and Westhoughton is still regarded as a major regional growth area of considerable importance.

Following the Minister's abandonment of the Manchester development in 1966, a decision was taken by the county and district councils, acting together, to provide a framework for growth to a population of 40,000 by 1991. For such purposes the Westhoughton district council is now putting down, with the Minister's approval, sewage disposal works at a cost approaching £1 million and has departmental provisional approval for proposals for sewers, the cost of which is estimated to be £2 million, of which about £1 million worth will be commenced in the immediate future, subject to consent.
