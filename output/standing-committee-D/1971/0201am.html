Mr. Silkin
: This series of Amendments deals with the question of aldermen in London. The purpose is to abolish the office of alderman in Greater London after the next election.

I am puzzled, as was the Under-Secretary on a previous Amendment, about the philosophy behind the alderman as a person. Is he a mystical being with tremendous authority and knowledge, who should be exempt from the more menial and mundane exercises in which we, as Members of Parliament, have to indulge? Is he someone who should be above such terrible things as elections? Is he a person whose knowledge is so great that the community cannot do without him?

I am not going back to their earliest history when they were the elders of the 1118 community, but the philosophy behind the office of alderman in recent years has been based on the idea that one wanted somebody who was independent, to come in with his knowledge and wisdom and keep everybody else informed and possibly even in order. This philosophy has become hopelessly out of date. We have seen the emergence of party politics in local government as in central government. We have seen the opportunity given to people democratically to make their choice. Any idea that aldermen were appointed because they were independent has long since gone.

There is an ancient Saxon blood sport known as fiddling the aldermanic vacancies. This blood sport is something about which no one on either side of the Committee can be totally blameless. Both major parties have done it, and the Liberals also did it in their time. This is far removed from the idea of the mystical, independent, non-party spirit, which guided the workings of the council. It has something to do with preserving power; it has nothing whatever to do with democracy.

That is the second reason for moving the Amendment and why it is on all fours with the rejection of the previous Amendment, when the hon. Member for Hove (Mr. Maddan) staged a gallant rearguard action in favour of preserving an emasculated system of aldermen.

The third basis is the general philosophy of what local government is all about. If, as we agree, local government is, or ought to be, local democracy, we have not only the testimony of my hon. Friends who made such very good speeches on the point in the earlier Amendments; we have the testimony of the Under-Secretary of State and of the hon. Lady the Member for Merton and Morden (Miss Fookes), both of whom made the point absolutely clearly. Truthfully, there is no reason why anyone who wishes to serve in local government should not stand for election; it is as simple as that.

That is the general philosophy. Then why do we choose London for a specific series of Amendments rather than putting it together with the previous Amendments? When I say "we" I am perhaps usurping your authority, Sir Stephen, but I know you will forgive me. The answer is that, while London is obviously an area JPEG 1119 of local government as other areas are throughout the country, it is rather a distinctive one.

First, it was in its day the youngest county in the country and it will now emerge as the oldest metropolitan county in the country. One appreciates that, because of the way that London government has grown up and the fact that the last Act dealing with London government was a matter of nine years ago, it will not be totally assimilated to the rest of the country, or certainly not yet, and I am not at the moment arguing the case that it should be. This is something which requires some consideration, but it is not the subject of the Amendments.

What is true, however, is that whether there should be differences in electoral terms, or in powers and so on, between Greater London and the rest of the country, there should certainly be no difference in the expression of democratic government; indeed, on the basis of "all rowed fast but none so fast as stroke", I should have expected London to be rather ahead of the rest of the community. I say this with due deference to my Welsh friends and to everyone else in the Committee. I appreciate that the hon. Lady and I are are in an uncomfortable minority on this.

We are, however, proud of London's system of government and we regard it as somewhat distinctive. For this reason, whatever had been the result of the previous Amendments dealing with the country as a whole, I certainly should have been willing and anxious to move this series of Amendments and I should have hoped to have the hon. Lady's support in that event too.

Summing it up very briefly, I hope that all of us will appreciate that, as the Committee has overwhelmingly decided that it wants a democratic system of local government, that it wants to say goodbye to aldermen in England and Wales, it should not face itself with the absurd anomaly of continuing aldermen in Greater London. This would be totally wrong and the effect in London of abolishing aldermen, while one must not overestimate it, will have some bearing on the percentage of the electorate who will vote in forthcoming London elections.

I have talked about our being proud 1120 of London government. As a Londoner, I can justifiably say that, but we are not terribly proud of the number of people who vote in our local government elections. Sometimes, indeed, it is rather misleading because one tends to draw conclusions from the small number who vote in a London election and assume that that must be true all over the country, and one comes up with some surprises. By and large, however, anything which makes the government of London more democratic must be something which the Committee as a whole would welcome and appreciate. It is in that spirit that I am moving the Amendments.

The Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Mr. Michael Heseltine)
: I appreciate the remarks of the right hon. Member for Deptford (Mr. John Silkin) in that he has drawn the attention of the Committee to what is undoubtedly true—that, on the question of democratic representation, there is a very wide measure of agreement on both sides of the Committee. I do not need to remind the Committee of the Amendment which we felt able to accept from the hon. Member for Widnes (Mr. Oakes), a token, I think, of our virtual unanimity on this matter.

The issue is a simple one as to whether or not on a major issue of this kind we should extend the scope of the Bill to the reorganisation of London. We certainly looked at the matter, as did the previous Government when they were considering the reorganisation of local government. The Committee will be aware that in 1963 a very detailed reconsideration of London's local government was undertaken and a Bill passed by the House which substantially changed the procedures within the London area.

The view of the previous Government—and we believe it was right—was that for a number of reasons it would be wrong now to extend the scope of this Bill to change a part of the London government system. I quote from the last Government's White Paper which dealt with this, Cmnd. 4276 published in February, 1970:

"While some of the Commission's proposals (for example, on matters affecting the election and constitution of local authorities) have general implications, the Government are not proposing to alter the structure of the London authorities or the distribution of functions between them."

JPEG 1121 It is, therefore, an important point to plead at the beginning of my remarks that although one may be willing to accept the principle, as I think we have all indicated, the detail of this subject cannot be divorced from a number of other immediately consequential matters which would, in fact, extend the scope of the Bill to London. Both Governments, having looked at the detail, have decided that that would not be practicable in the circumstances.

It is true that a number of aspects of the Bill extend to the London area, but there is nothing which affects the constitution of the London authorities or the distribution of functions between them. Those are the two areas which both Governments have decided not to incorporate in the reorganisation of local government outside London.

I think that hon. Members opposite ought to be prepared to accept the logic of what they themselves decided—

Mr. Denis Howell
: We did not decide any such thing.

Mr. Heseltine
:—when they were in office. When they looked at the details, their own White Paper clearly indicated that they would not deal with London in the way which they now suggest in the Amendment.

I mentioned the fact that a number of related issues would immediately come up for consideration if this principle were accepted. The size of the councils would automatically be reduced by approximately one-seventh. There are also differences in the electoral arrangements for the councils. For example, they do not have four-yearly elections as we now propose for the rest of the country. Re-warding would be necessary to cope with the adjustments in size.

It seems to us that the whole question of the electoral arrangements which would need to be considered in London should be a matter open to any future Government. I in no way wish to close the door to the principle which we are discussing. We have indicated clearly what are our views in relation to the rest of the country. Some subsequent occasion when the local government structure of London is being considered would be the appropriate time to deal with the electoral arrangements, arrangements for warding, the election of aldermen, if anyone wished to consider 1122 that, and so on. The matter could be considered in a Bill of its own on some subsequent occasion.

It is certainly our view—and I believe that it was the view of the last Government—that we are undertaking a massive reorganisation already; we have gone through a very wide range of consultative processes with regard to the Bill; and at this stage it would be wrong to move away from the principle we have accepted, which is that we shall not tinker with the constitution or the distribution of functions within the London area.
