Mr. Brynmor John: In rising to speak on this Clause, I want to make a protest about the way in which the Government have handled this question of the Local Government Boundary Commission. It is interesting to note that consistently we in the Opposition have urged that the date for the implementation of this legislation is too soon, and will not allow sufficient time for the necessary preparations to be made to set up this body.

We have been told by the Government, equally consistently, that there is no need to concern ourselves at all, that there is plenty of time. However, the Local Government Boundary Commission, in attempting to fulfil the deadline that they themselves have set, have been led into some fairly strange practices.

We are now asked to accede to the proposition that there shall be a Local Government Boundary Commission for England, but this is an absolute and utter farce, because we all know that the Local Government Boundary Commission, admittedly with the tag "designate", has already been set up. We are now being asked to sanction something that was in being as long ago as the first sitting of this Committee.

As far as I can see, until Clause 48 and the seventh Schedule have been sanctioned, there is as yet no authority for the Secretary of State either to set up this Commission, or to expend any money on its task, but nevertheless the fact is Image Col. 1281 that on 25th November, 1971, the Local Government Boundary Commission designate was set up. It has held seven meetings since that date. It has already employed a staff, the annual cost of which will be £15,000 per year. It has rented accommodation at an annual rental of £6,000 per year. It has sanctioned salaries which will commence from 25th November, 1971; the chairman is paid £7,000 per year, the deputy chairman £2,000 per year and the members £1,500 per year, it has already expended money for postage, lighting and has already paid the members of the Commission expenses in travelling to these meetings.

I believe that the setting up of this body by the Government, without any statutory authority whatsoever, without this Clause standing part of the Bill, without this Committee having had any opportunity of discussing it or of commenting upon it, shows a flagrant contempt for this Committee, and a protest ought to be registered this morning.

If you, Sir Stephen, and hon. Members will refer to the Seventh Schedule, you will see that the words there throughout are that

"The Commission shall be a body",


There shall be paid to each member … such salary … and allowances ",

and yet we know that already these things are being done.

Before I am prepared to sanction, I want to know why this has been done. Why has it been felt necessary, in order to keep up with an artificial timetable and an artificial deadline which the Government have set themselves, that this body should be set up and commence work without statutory authority? I hope the Minister will be forthcoming with his answer.

The Minister for Local Government and Development (Mr. Graham Page): Indeed, a commission designate has been set up already as an advisory committee to the Secretary of State. It has been set up as any other advisory body is set up by Ministers when they seek advice from outside their departments. There was no need for any formal move to herald the establishment of the Commission designate, but the Secretary of State took care to ensure that the Commission was not set up until after the House had approved Col. 1282 the general principles of the Bill on Second Reading.

This is a convention of the House, that if it is intended to set up a commission or an advisory body under a Bill, the House shall accept the principles of that first and then the commission shall be merely an advisory body taking no formal steps until it is constituted as a legal body under the Bill when it comes on to the Statute Book.

All the present actions of the Commission are administrative and informal. They are taking written representations from local authorities. If it should be necessary to meet any local authorities in particular cases after the written representations have been received, this will be done in informal conferences and not by statutory public inquiries of any sort.

I should have thought that it was businesslike of the Government to have an advisory committee like this at this stage in the discussions on this Bill in order to keep to a timetable which is certainly not an artificial timetable, as the hon. Gentleman said. It is clearly a timetable which is tight, and the Government have always said that the timetable will be difficult to keep to. Once one has embarked on this kind of reform, surely it is better that one completes it without unnecessary delay, unnecessary disorganisation and anxiety, particularly amongst local authority staff, even more than amongst local authority members.

We are dealing here with a large body of men of the greatest importance to this country. I am anxious that they should not be left in doubt any longer than is necessary. If we waited until this Bill received the Royal Assent before starting any sort of discussions on the working of the legislation, I think the Government would be culpable of neglect of both members and officials in local government.

Mr. John Silkin: I should, with respect to my hon. Friend's point of view, like to deal with another point, namely what will happen after the Boundary Commission is statutorily in existence, when it has received, as is known in the courts, its legitimation per subsequentum matrimonium. I think we would all agree that the Boundary Commission, when it is statutorily in existence, should be independent, that it should be free from interference, but willing always to listen to sensible representations that are made to it.

For that reason I recommend to the Minister of State, Home Office, that the same procedure be followed as is followed in the case of the Parliamentary Boundary Commission. The position here is that the Parliamentary Boundary Commission, from time to time, consults all the major political parties, and when it publishes a fresh set of proposals, it has already met them and it meets them afterwards; it sends them four copies of the provisional proposals several days before publication, and the same applies when it revises or modifies proposals. Finally it sends to the main parties copies of all representations received, details of any local inquiries and a copy of the Deputy Commissioner's report and his recommendations.

This has been a procedure that has been adopted for many years. In the Parliamentary Boundary Commission's Report for 1969, Cmnd. 4084, para 23, page 3, this is what they have to say: It is headed "Discussions with Political Parties".

"In 1942 the Committee on Electoral Col. 1284 Machinery (Cmd. 6408) suggested that, apart from local inquiries held under the statutory arrangements, each Commission should also sit under its Deputy Chairman


'… to hear any representations from the Chief or National Officers of the principal Party organisations with respect to the provisional proposals.'


It was made evident in the House of Commons during the Second Reading debate on the 1958 Redistribution Bill that the three principal parties were anxious to see the suggestion adopted. Indeed the leaders of the parties wrote to the Chairman of the four Boundary Commissions expressing the hope that the Commissions would be prepared to implement it. We adopted the suggestion and a meeting with the chief officers of the parties represented in the House of Commons was arranged shortly after the announcement of the general review."

This is an interesting comment:

"A further 7 meetings were held with the chief officers as the review proceeded. We found these periodic discussions on our progress and procedure most helpful."

Therefore, it would seem that this was the right thing to do with the Parliamentary Boundary Commission. I submit that we ought to carry on much the same procedure for the Local Government Boundary Commission.
