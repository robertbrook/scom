Clause 1
PROVISION FOR JOINT SURVEYS, REPORTS AND STRUCTURE PLANS

Question proposed, That the Clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Arthur Blenkinsop
: We should welcome some comment from the Minister on the Clause. We are in general agreement with its provisions, but we should like to know a little more about the way in which joint surveys will operate.

Although I was a member of the Standing Committee that considered the previous Town and Country Planning Act, I am surprised to find that that Act did not provide these powers. Would the Minister say whether they should also be provided in connection with local plans as well as the wider structure plans? There may be occasions when such powers would be needed—they may already exist, but I doubt it.

4 One or two points of a detailed character have been raised by the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors. The Institution says that the provisions:

"enable voluntary combinations by planning authorities for the purposes of surveys and preparation of structure plans. The Institution considers it essential, however, that such a voluntary combination should be formalised by the setting up of a joint planning committee to keep the structure plan under review and to consider proposals for development of a specially significant character."

Again, we should be interested to hear the Minister's views on that.

The Minister for Local Government and Development (Mr. Graham Page)
: I apologise to the Committee for the length of Clause 1 and for the fact that we are apparently making heavy weather of a short point. However, together, I am sure, with other members of the Committee, I detest legislation by reference. I thought, therefore, that it would be preferable to write out an entirely new Clause rather than to employ the use of cross-references. This has turned out to be a rather long Clause.

The hon. Member for South Shields (Mr. Blenkinsop) and I should reprimand ourselves for not noticing that fact. Although we both served on the Committee stage of the Town and Country Planning Act, 1968, we failed to realise that the wording was such that a structure plan, although it could be prepared by two or more local planning authorities, must be submitted to the Secretary of State for approval in the form of a structure plan by one local planning authority.

Thus, the ridiculous situation arises whereby several local authorities which may join together to create a structure plan—for example, in Hampshire, where Southampton, Portsmouth and Hampshire are joining together in a structure plan for South Hants—must, under present law, take a pair of scissors and chop it up into separate plans for submission.

It goes deeper than that. We do not wish to deter local planning authorities from joining together and deciding that a structure plan should be drawn for the whole of their area. It may well be that, according to the terms of the Local Government Bill, this will not be so necessary in future, having regard to the larger local planning authorities which will come into effect after 1974.

JPEG 5 Nevertheless, we have permitted local planning authorities to initiate their structure plans in a number of areas, and we are arranging a phasing of the submission of structure plans from the local planning authorities to the Secretary of State. Thus, this work is already being undertaken, for it was not possible to wait for local government reorganisation to provide the impetus. Clause 1, therefore, authorises local planning authorities to join together to draw up and eventually submit to the Secretary of State one structure plan covering several areas.

The chartered surveyors suggest that there should be some formal joint planning committee. I should have thought that that was unnecessary, taking into account that considerable powers exist for local planning authorities to set up a joint board, or joint committee, to review and monitor a plan, and to watch developments that may affect it. I do not consider it necessary, therefore, for special provision to be incorporated into the Bill.

I think that it is unnecessary, because of the very term "local plan", dealing with a locality, to provide, as in the case of structure plans, for two local planning authorities to join together. Such provision is necessary in the case of the structure plan as there are a number of places in the country where two or more local planning authorities will wish to join together. However, I do not think that that will be the case with regard to local plans.

Clause 2
PROVISION FOR WITHDRAWAL OF STRUCTURE PLANS, AND AS TO EFFECT OF STEPS TAKEN IN CONNECTION WITH PLANS WITHDRAWN OR NOT SUBMITTED

Question proposed, That the Clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Oakes
: The Opposition have no objection to Clause 2, designed, as it is, to enable the speedy withdrawal of a plan or the submission of another plan. However, I foresee one difficulty under the Clause as it stands.

The local authorities together may make application to the Secretary of State for withdrawal of a plan. That is understandable. The Secretary of State then withdraws it. But the Clause says, "or any of them". What would arise where a joint structure plan was submitted and then the two or more local authorities fall out over it? One authority might say to the Minister, "We want this plan to be withdrawn". Under the Clause, as I understand it, the whole plan must be withdrawn and another plan submitted by the authorities concerned. In other words, one local authority can rock the boat by itself applying for the withdrawal of the plan. It may not arise, because local bodies are sensible, but it could.
