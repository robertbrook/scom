I shall consider a number of problems arising out of my own experiences in the City of Birmingham, and my own constituency of Small Heath. One cannot consult the local people, and the local councillors have to make the best judgment that they can. They do very well in the circumstances, but they never know whether they have got it right. I never cease to be amazed by the problems which ordinary people regard as the most important in their lives.

In the House and in local authorities we concentrate on those things which we think matter, but I find that the abolition of "EAST" in dialled telephone numbers in my constituency has caused far more distress to ordinary people than have some of the great issues of the day. When people want to ring up Mum, or the Church, they can remember "EAST 2345", but they cannot remember "896 2345". That is a matter of importance to ordinary people. If we had community councils they could be consulted on problems directly affecting their neighbourhoods. It may seem odd to open a discussion on neighbourhood councils by talking about telephone exchange numbers, but such questions are relevant to the lives of many people.

In the past three years I have established three residents' associations in my constituency because we cannot have neigh- Image Col. 247 bourhood community councils. When we ask people in the three bodies that I have established in my constituency what they think most important, so that we can get officials and councillors to talk about the problems affecting them, top of the list in each of the three residents' associations has been the road pattern, the transport habits, and the way in which lorry drivers get off overcrowded main roads into back roads, turning what were pleasant suburban neighbourhood roads into what the residents call race tracks—main road diversions.

This has many profound effects. Obviously it disturbs the peace and quiet of the neighbourhood, and there is a grave element of danger to young children. In my constituency, because of the traffic dangers, there has emerged a demand for traffic-restricted roads, and roads in which children can play.

Waiting restrictions are also of great importance. Vehicles and lorries park at all hours of the day and night in front of homes, giving offence, not only because of the parking itself but because, having parked their lorries, long-distance lorry drivers are in the habit of setting about their business at five o'clock in the morning, to the disturbance of everyone living in that neighbourhood.

Some of us in the House, and others in town halls and county halls throughout the country, may not think that these matters are of much importance, but to those who have their sleep disturbed day after day they are probably among the most important matters in their lives. Yet at the moment there is no method whereby people are consulted, or can make representation collectively and authoritatively. In order to get a collective and authoritative representation even greater burdens must be imposed on local councillors and local Members of Parliament.

I have already referred to recreation. This, and the whole field of leisure, is pre-eminently something for a neighbourhood or community council to take under its wing. I am often asked where one can kick a ball about in my constituency. In many of our towns there is nowhere to kick a ball about. I was brought up in the back streets of Birmingham, and I kicked balls about in the street because there was Col. 248 nowhere else to do it. It was important to me to play football in the winter and cricket in the summer, and to chalk three stumps against a wall. However, the tremendous growth in the volume of traffic has made that a dangerous and hazardous business. If we had responded to the changing nature of our society, as the volume of traffic grew the amount of open space for leisure would also have grown. The two things go hand in hand. That has not happened, but there is tremendous demand for it and strong feeling about it.

I had another aspect of leisure brought to my attention by some elderly people in my constituency, who told me that all the shops they used to use have gone, and that the nice new shopping centre is a mile away. The task of carrying the shopping from the new centre has made life much more difficult for them. That, again, is something that should be taken into account. These people were asking for certain leisure areas in which old people could come together and talk, or use them as staging posts between their homes and the shopping centre. That would be a trivial matter to a major local authority, but to the old people in that neighbourhood it is of major importance.

Sport, leisure and recreation are of growing importance. The police and other authorities have many difficulties with young people which could be put down to boredom, a lack of facilities, and a lack of opportunity to enjoy their recreation and leisure. I am not sure that sports facilities would necessarily be used by the residents of a neighbourhood. In Birmingham we have done some interesting research, which shows that the youth of one district go to a neighbouring district to meet their girl friends, do their courting and enjoy their leisure, and the youth of that district go to yet another district. Perhaps the process could be called cross-fertilisation. It is one interesting fact that came out of the survey undertaken by the West Hill Training College for Youth Leaders.
