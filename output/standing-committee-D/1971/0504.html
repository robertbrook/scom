Mr. Sydney Chapman
: I beg to move Amendment No. 16, in page 9, line 16, leave out "or ceases to be in a conservation area".

The Chairman
: With this Amendment we are to take Amendment No. 23, in Clause 8, page 10, line 35, leave out from "building" to second "the" in line 36.

Mr. Chapman
: We all accept that when one makes provision for trying to protect buildings in conservation areas, one naturally excludes listed buildings that are of architectural or historical interest, because the legislation protecting such buildings is far stronger than that proposed for other buildings of interest in conservation areas.

I question, however, whether the direction proposed in the Clause should include provision for excluding what I might term conservation area protected buildings if the conservation area ceases so to be. We all hope that none of the 1,750 areas—which we hope will increase to about 5,000 areas—will cease to be a conservation area. If that should happen, the point of the Amendment is to ensure that those buildings that the local authority has deemed should be protected should remain protected even if no longer in a conservation area.

Without the Amendment the Clause would give a loophole to local authorities. If a local authority, having protected a building, felt that it was in an embarrassing position and that the building should be pulled down, it could accomplish that by taking away the right of that building to be in a conservation area.

That is a classic example of how one could have a conflict between environment and economy, or, in other words, 142 between conservation and commercialism. This modest Amendment seeks to delete the words

"or ceases to be in a conservation area",

so that if any conservation area ceases to be such an area, the buildings will still be protected.

The Minister for Local Government and Development (Mr. Graham Page)
: My hon. Friend the Member for Birmingham, Handsworth (Mr. Sydney Chapman) said that these buildings within a conservation area were protected because the local planning authority had deemed that they should be protected. Surely the authority would have deemed that they should be protected because they formed part of the conservation area. Clause 7 and Schedule 2 are designed to control the demolition of buildings only where this appears to be necessary—

"…in the interests of preserving the character or appearance of the conservation area …"

That is the reason for the protection of these buildings.

If one removes the foundation and basis of that, one removes the conservation area, and one cannot say that one is protecting those buildings because they are preserving the character or appearance of something which is no longer there. Section 277 of the 1971 Act provides that a local planning authority may not only designate an area as a conservation area, but may also cancel any designation previously made. The planning authority has acted, while there has been a conservation area in existence, to protect certain buildings within that area because they help make up the character and appearance of that area. If it is then decided that it is no longer necessary to protect the area by keeping it as a conservation area, the whole reason for preservation of the buildings goes.

Clause 7 provides that where a building included in the direction

"… ceases to be in a conservation area, then the direction shall cease to be in force as respects that building."

I am sure that that is right. The Amendment is mistaken because it would maintain the effect of a direction on a building even after the building ceased to be in a conservation area, which means after the whole purpose of the protection had gone. I must advise the Committee to resist the Amendment.

Mr. Page
: I beg to move, That the Clause be read a Second time.

I have great pleasure in moving the Clause, because it introduces a form of new grant by the Government to assist in the preservation of conservation areas. In another place, the noble lord, Lord Sand-ford, spoke of this matter and the Government felt that there was a gap in the provisions for the protection of conservation areas which could be filled. It was not to be done in quite the same way as the previous Government had considered filling it, but in a way which I hope the Committee will find satisfactory.

The effect of the new Clause is to enable the Secretary of State to make either a grant or loan for expenditure incurred in connection with the preservation or enhancement of the character, or appearance, of any outstanding conservation area. The grant or loan will apply not just to any conservation area, but to one which we are advised will be outstanding. The grant will be for the preservation or enhancement of the character or appearance of that area, and is important for not merely preservation but enhancements.

The Clause has been modelled closely 148 on the provisions of Section 4 of the Historic Buildings and Ancient Monuments Act, 1953, and Section 4 of the Civil Amenities Act, 1967. Those two together enable the Secretary of State, on the advice of the historic buildings councils, to make grants and loans for the purpose of defraying expenditure incurred in the repair or maintenance of buildings of historic or achitectural interest. As the Committee will know, there is an Historic Buildings Council for England, one for Scotland and one for Wales. I said that this provision would apply to conservation areas which we are advised to be of outstanding character, and the advice will come from the Historic Buildings Council, whether it be for England, Scotland or Wales, which has great experience and expertise in this matter.

The idea of a grant of this sort originates from the Preservation Policy Group, set up as long ago as 1966 under the noble lord, Lord Kennet. One of the group's main recommendations—a report was published in 1970—was that there should be a new form of specific grant which would enable the Government to pay local authorities half the loss incurred by them in carrying out conservation schemes. When we studied that, it did not seem to fit the bill and we wanted more information and advice about how to apply a grant of this sort.

The Preservation Policy Group had also recommended pilot projects in the four historic towns, and those were being carried out at the time when the group's recommendations were announced. We said then that we should like to have further evidence of those pilot schemes to see how they worked before we decided exactly what form of grant there should be.

I am particularly anxious that the form of grant should be as flexible as possible, and I thought that there would be some difficulty if we stuck to the formula of half the loss on conservation areas. Also, I wanted to extend it beyond payments merely to local authorities. There are occasions when amenities societies, and even individuals, could be helped to preserve and enhance the character or appearance of an outstanding conservation area.

Subsection (1) has thus been drafted in general terms to allow for as wide a variety of schemes as possible. These JPEG 149 schemes might, for instance, be works for the preservation or enhancement of an area, but I foresee ancillary schemes—perhaps the acquisition of land to open up an area in front of a building so that there is a clear view of the building; or the line of a terrace is improved. Specialist staff might be employed to survey and recommend improvement schemes on properties in accordance with their character and age and so on. We have public relations activities very much in mind and small amounts of money could frequently be spent on that sort of thing.

There is no restriction on the persons or bodies to whom the grant may be made. We shall rely entirely on the Historic Buildings Councils to tell us not only whether it is a case where a grant ought to be made, but to whom it shall be made: whether to the local authority, the owners of the property, an amenity society, or a historic buildings trust.

Subsection (2) enables the Secretary of State to make such conditions as he considers desirable when making a grant or loan. Subsection (3) deals with the conditions of repayment of loans and the interest that may be payable, the terms of which must have Treasury approval as well as being determined by the Secretary of State.

Subsection (4) requires that the grant or loan shall be made only on the advice of the Historic Buildings Councils. As a result of a similar requirement in Section 4 of the Historic Buildings and Ancient Monuments Act, 1953, the councils have had nearly 20 years' experience in advising whether a building is an outstanding building and on the amount of grant or loan which could properly be made to preserve or enhance it. Similarly, the councils will be enabled to advise on what is an outstanding conservation area and which schemes should be accepted.

These councils include in their membership those with wide experience of planning and architecture. Schemes in England will be considered by a committee of the English Historic Buildings Council, a selected committee of people experienced in this type of advice. I imagine that the same procedure will apply in the case of Scotland and Wales, although I hesitate to speak for my right hon. Friends in that connection.

Subsection (5) enables the Secretary of State to pay remuneration and allowances 150 for services rendered by any member of a council in connection with the new functions arising under the Clause. This goes wider than our present intentions, and it is not sought to pay a number of people on the council, or the entire committee. However, the 1953 Act enables an honorarium to be paid to the chairman of a council in addition to his travel expenses, for it is recognised that an adviser will be involved in time and travel when carrying out investigations and advising the Government.

The processing of the conservation schemes will, I think, involve an expert in a considerable amount of work, although not over a long period as his knowledge of the subject would enable him to reach conclusions fairly rapidly, without unduly keeping him from his normal work. It is felt that in those circumstances the chairman of the special committee should be offered an honorarium or fee so that he does not suffer hardship through undertaking this work. The honorarium which we have in mind is £1,500. As I have said, while it is not expected that an expert will be unduly detained from his normal duties, expert advice should be paid for.

I have great pleasure in announcing to the Committee this morning that we have been able to persuade someone of great renown to take on the job—Lord Holford himself. I am delighted to be able to announce that today. I do not want the Committee to think that we are taking Lord Holford away from the great services which he renders to the public in many other ways. I do not think that the job will take up a great amount of his time, but we must be fair and give him an honorarium for undertaking it. I am most grateful to Lord Holford for his acceptance of the task. It gives us confidence in the work envisaged, with the Government contributing £500,000 in the first year, building up to a greater amount in future years.

I therefore ask the Committee to accept not only the new Clause, but what I have said about the administration of the grant and the advice which we shall take in administering it.

    but this sub-paragraph shall not be taken to authorise the preparation of a local plan (joint or other) for the whole or any part of a G.L.C. action area.
  3. In the foregoing sub-paragraph, "adjacent planning authority" means a local planning authority whose area adjoins Greater London.
  4. Different local plans (joint or other) may be prepared for different purposes for the same part of any area.

Duty of planning authorities to prepare local plans for action areas


9.—(1) As soon as practicable after the Secretary of State's final approval of the Greater London development plan—

  1. the Greater London Council shall prepare a local plan for every G.L.C. action area; and
  2. in the case of any other action area—
    1. if it is wholly comprised within a London borough, the council of that borough shall prepare a local plan for the area, and
    2. if not, the council of every London borough in which any part of the action area falls shall prepare a local plan for that part;
but this sub-paragraph shall not be taken to require a council to do again any thing which they have already done.
  1. In the case referred to in sub-paragraph (l)( b)(ii) above, a London borough council may comply with that sub-paragraph by joining with any other borough council concerned, with or without the Greater London Council, in the preparation of a joint local plan.
  2. Where a council are required by this paragraph to prepare a local plan, they shall take steps for the adoption of that plan.

Local plans by direction of Secretary of State


10.—(1) Without prejudice to the foregoing provisions, the Greater London Council or a London borough council shall, if the Secretary of State gives them (either before or after he finally approves the Greater London development plan) a direction in that behalf with respect to any area of Greater London, as soon as practicable prepare for that area a local plan of such a nature as may be specified in the direction, and take steps for the adoption of the plan; but no such directions shall require a council to take any steps to comply therewith until after the Secretary of State's final approval of the Greater London development plan.

  1. Before giving a direction to a council under this paragraph the Secretary of State 190 shall consult the council with respect thereto and, in the case of a direction to be given to a London borough council, he shall also, before giving it, consult the Greater London Council.

General provisions as to local plans


11.—(1) The following provisions of this paragraph shall apply with respect to any local plan prepared under this Schedule by the Greater London Council or a London borough council and also with respect to a joint local plan so prepared by two or more planning authorities; and in those provisions "the council" means the council preparing the local plan or, as the case may be, the authorities who join in the preparation of the joint plan.

  1. The plan shall consist of a map and a written statement and shall—
    1. formulate in such detail as the council think appropriate their proposals for the development and other use of land in the area for which the plan is prepared, or for any description of development and other use of such land (including in either case such measures as the council think fit for the improvement of the physical environment and the management of traffic); and
    2. contain such matters as may be prescribed, or as the Secretary of State may direct.
  2. The plan shall contain, or be accompanied by, such diagrams, illustrations and descriptive material as the council think appropriate for the purpose of explaining or illustrating the proposals in the plan, or as may be prescribed or as may in any particular case be specified in directions given by the Secretary of State; and any such diagrams, illustrations and descriptive material shall be treated as forming part of the plan.
  3. In formulating their proposals in the plan the council shall—
    1. secure that the proposals conform generally to the Greater London development plan as it stands for the time being (whether or not the Secretary of State has finally approved the plan, but taking into account any feature or element of it in the case of which he has indicated his approval), and
    2. have regard to any information and any other considerations which appear to them to be relevant, or which may be prescribed, or which the Secretary of State may in any particular case direct them to take into account.
  4. Before giving a direction to the council under this paragraph the Secretary of State shall consult the council with respect thereto and, in the case of a direction to be given to a London borough council, he shall also, before giving it, consult the Greater London Council.

Publicity for local plan prepared by single council


12.—(1) Where the Greater London Council or a London borough council propose to prepare a local plan, the council shall take such steps as will in their opinion secure—

  1. that adequate publicity is given, in any London borough affected by the plan, to any relevant matter arising out of a survey under section 6 of this Act (including any joint survey) and to the JPEG 191 matters proposed to be included in the plan;
  2. that persons who may be expected to desire an opportunity of making representations to the council with respect to those matters are made aware that they are entitled to an opportunity of doing so; and
  3. that such persons are given an adequate opportunity of making such representations;
and the council shall consider any representations made to them within the prescribed period.
  1. After preparing a local plan, the council shall before adopting it or submitting it for approval under section 14 of this Act (but not before the Secretary of State has finally approved the Greater London development plan) make copies of the plan available for inspection at their office and at such other places as may be prescribed and—
    1. in the case of a plan prepared by the Greater London Council, send a copy of the plan to the council of any Lon don borough affected by the plan.
    2. in the case of a plan prepared by a Lon don borough council, send a copy to the Greater London Council, and
    3. in any case send a copy to the Secretary of State.
  2. Each copy of a plan made available for inspection as required by sub-paragraph (2) above shall be accompanied by a statement of the time within which objections to the local plan may be made to the council who have prepared the plan; and the copy sent to the Secretary of State shall be accompanied by a statement containing such particulars, if any. as may be prescribed—
    1. of the steps which the council preparing the plan have taken to comply with sub-paragraph (1) above, and
    2. of the council's consultations with, and their consideration of the views of, other persons.

Publicity for joint local plans


13.—(1) Where two or more local planning authorities propose to join in the preparation of a joint local plan for a combined area, they shall together take such steps as will in their opinion secure—

  1. that adequate publicity is given in the areas of those authorities to any relevant matter arising out of a survey under section 6 of this Act (including any joint survey) and to the matters proposed to be included in the plan;
  2. that persons who may be expected to desire an opportunity of making representations to any of the authorities are made aware that they are entitled to an opportunity of doing so; and
  3. that such persons are given an adequate opportunity of making such representations;
and the authorities shall consider any representations made to them within the prescribed period:
  • Provided that where one of the authorities is the Greater London Council, that Council shall not be required to take any steps under this sub-paragraph which can in the Council's 192 opinion be taken, and are taken, by the council of any London borough in which any part of the combined area is comprised.
    1. After preparing a joint plan, the planning authorities concerned shall before adopting it or submitting it for approval under section 14 of this Act (but not before the Secretary of State has finally approved the Greater London development plan) make copies of the plan available at the offices of each of the authorities respectively and at such other places as may be prescribed and send copies of the plan to the Secretary of State and (in a case where the Greater London Council is not one of the authorities joining in the preparation of the plan) to that Council.
    2. Each copy of a joint local plan made available for inspection as required by subparagraph (2) above shall be accompanied by a statement of the time within which objections to the plan may be made to the planning authorities concerned; and the copy sent to the Secretary of State shall be accompanied by a statement containing such particulars, if any, as may be prescribed—
      1. of the steps which the planning authorities concerned have respectively taken to comply with sub-paragraph (1) above, and
      2. of those authorities' consultations with, and their consideration of the views of, other persons.

    Power of Secretary of State to suspend adoption


    14.—(1) In relation to a local plan (joint or other) prepared under this Schedule, section 14(1) of this Act shall have effect as if the reference to section 12 were to the following provisions of this paragraph.

    1. If, on considering the statement submitted with, and the matters included in, a local plan so prepared and other information provided by the authority who prepared the plan (or as the case may be, the authorities who joined in its preparation) the Secretary of State is not satisfied that the purposes of paragraph 12(1)( a) to ( c) above or, as the case may be, of paragraph 13(1)( a) to ( c) above have been adequately achieved by the steps taken in that behalf by the authority or authorities, he may within twenty-one days of the receipt of the statement direct that no further steps for the adoption of the plan be taken without such further action as he may specify having been taken in order better to achieve those purposes and his being satisfied that such action has been taken.
    2. A planning authority who are given directions by the Secretary of State under this paragraph shall—
      1. forthwith withdraw copies of the local plan made available for inspection as required by paragraph 12 or 13 above, and
      2. in a case where objections to the plan have been made by any person, notify him that the Secretary of State has given such directions as aforesaid.
