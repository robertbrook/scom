The Under-Secretary of State for Health and Social Security (Mr. Paul Dean)
: This discussion takes place against the sombre background of a level of unemployment far higher than anybody in the Committee would wish to see. Fortunately, the graph is beginning to move substantially in the right direction, and many Government policies are helping to create new jobs and ensure that training and re-training arrangements are expanded.

Against that background, I want first to deal with some of the Figures that the hon. Member for Oldham, West (Mr. Meacher) asked me about when he opened the discussion—figures to which the hon. Member for Rhondda, West (Mr. Alec Jones) has also referred.

In May, 1972—the latest date for which figures are available—approximately 53 per cent. of the registered unemployed were receiving unemployment benefit, or unemployment benefit together with supplementary allowance. About 29 per cent. of the registered unemployed were receiving supplementary benefit alone; 82 per cent. were thus getting benefit of one kind or another.

The interesting feature is that the proportion of unemployed people receiving benefit is fairly constant, and does not fluctuate much with the actual level of unemployment; that is particularly true for those who are unemployed for over 450 12 months. In May, 1972, about 18 per cent. of registered unemployed persons were receiving neither unemployed benefit nor supplementary benefit. I was asked for some information about this.

Normally, those would be people whose resources were such that they were above the supplementary benefit level, because they had resources of savings, in the case of the older men, or because they had occupational pensions. In some cases the claim for unemployment benefit would still be being processed and the persons concerned would not yet have received cover.

The following are the main categories of people not receiving unemployment benefit: those who have exhausted their entitlement to unemployment benefit—the latest figure being about 195,000—those who do not satisfy the contribution conditions because they were formerly self-employed or because they have recently left school or university; married women who had opted out of contributions, and those disqualified because they left their previous jobs voluntarily.

The new Clause seeks to extend the duration of unemployment benefit to 18 months instead of the present 12 months for flat-rate benefit, and to 12 months instead of the present six months for earnings-related supplement. When the earnings-related supplement was introduced in 1966 the previous Administration considered that six months was about right, in that it was long enough to enable people to look for a suitable job without too big a drop in their income, but not so long as to discourage unemployed people from seeking or accepting new jobs promptly. That was the philosophy behind it at that time.

Such a change would have certain practical effects that the Committee should consider. First, the earnings-related supplement is taken fully into account in calculating supplementary benefit. Therefore, those unemployed persons whose financial needs are greatest would not benefit from increased expenditure on extending the duration of earnings-related supplement from six months to 12 months. In other words, such an extension would help those least in need rather than those most in need. The cost of extending the benefit would be about £10 million in the case of the earnings-related supplement.

I should mention briefly one other JPEG 451 practical factor, namely, the interchange-ability with sickness benefit. At present the earnings-related supplement payable during unemployment or sickness is the same, for administrative reasons, and it provides for the linking of claims for either benefit. The new Clause as it stands would disturb that, because it makes no new provision for interchange between the benefits. For instance, it is not clear what would be the position of a man who had been unemployed for nine months and then fell sick. Those are some of the practical difficulties involved in extending the earnings-related supplement.

In 1966 the flat-rate benefit was fixed by the previous Administration at 312 days—that is, a year, excluding Sundays. Before 1966 a good contribution record gave title to up to 312 additional days beyond the standard 180 days. In going for a standard maximum of 312 days the Labour Government in 1966 argued that many people who got the additional days did not want further employment, or had adequate resources. That argument still holds good.

When benefit is exhausted the supplementary benefit arrangements are available. We feel, as did the previous Administration, that the more flexible arrangements that are possible these are the most suitable means of covering those who, for one reason or another, have exhausted their benefit. All who are in benefit—whether through the national insurance arrangements or through the supplementary benefit arrangements—will of course gain from the uprating of the benefits, which comes into operation in October.

I am asking the Committee to consider what, in the present circumstances, is the best way to help those who are unemployed, particularly those who have been unemployed for a fairly long period. I have mentioned what is being done through the national insurance scheme and the supplementary benefit scheme. It is equally important to take into account the redundancy payment arrangements that are in operation, the fact that unemployment is falling, and that through the Industry Bill the Government are about to launch a major new scheme to pump substantial additional resources into the regions that have problems of unemployment and shortage of jobs.

In addition, there is the policy an- 452 nounced in the White Paper, Industrial and Regional Development, under which substantial additional resources and places are being made available for training and retraining. In 1971, it was 20,000. That figure will rise to between 60,000 and 70,000 by 1975. The training centres and allowances will involve additional expenditure of about £100 million in the next four years.

I therefore submit to the Committee that the combination of the cover at present available and about to be uprated in the national insurance scheme, plus the major new advances in the field of regional development and training and retraining arrangements, will provide the most effective way of alleviating the existing problem.
