    990 Of course, there are very many people in Lincolnshire—for example, the Lindsey County Council to whom I shall refer again in a few moments—who mourn the parting and dismemberment of this great and ancient county. Personally and emotionally I share those sentiments. Nevertheless, I accept the logic of the estuarial concept which we have earlier discussed, and I wish the new Humberside every possible success.

    It would be wrong to say that I do not have some misgivings on this question. Nevertheless, I accept the fact that the Humber Bridge will considerably alter the situation, and I hope that the area will become an importan tpart of the United Kingdom—a thriving, thrusting county.

    However, I wish to make abundantly clear that the purpose of the Amendment is not to destroy the concept of Humberside. It is aimed at retaining within Lincolnshire those essentially rural and agricultural parts of North Lincolnshire. After all, Lincolnshire is one of the greatest of farming counties. With my own background, I may perhaps boast that it is the greatest, if the Committee will forgive my local patriotism. All the villages mentioned in the Amendment—I know them very well—are rural villages, and form part of the Lincolnshire rural agricultural scene.

    The amended boundary of Humberside which the Amendment would produce offers a reasonable compromise between those who are excited about the prospect of the new Humberside county and those who wish to retain their Lincolnshire and rural identity. It secures the Humberside concept unimpaired whilst at the same time doing better justice to Lincolnshire.

    The Lincolnshire contribution to the Humberside county will be massive—250 square miles, 275,000 people and nearly JPEG 991 £16 million rateable value. The whole of industrial North Lincolnshire, with ample room for further expansion, will go into Humberside. The rateable value per head of population in Humberside will remain at £42 as proposed in the Bill, and both banks of the Humber will remain united if the Amendment is accepted. But whilst preserving and securing the Humberside concept it will do better justice to Lincolnshire. That is why my hon. Friends were most anxious that this Amendment should be pressed this evening, and that I should urge these things upon my hon. Friends.

    Better justice will be done to Lincolnshire. A much larger part of the continuous area in which the county's traditional primary industry is carried out will remain in Lincolnshire, with Brigg as one of its most important centres. Lindsey, which stands to be rudely shattered wherever the boundary be drawn, will be able to make a more comprehensive contribution to the future Lincolnshire county, where the affinities of Lindsey with its county offices in Lincoln must surely be. Under the amended boundary, five-sixths of the area and just over half the population of Lindsey will go to Lincolnshire.
