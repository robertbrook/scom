I now wish to turn to the Minister's main argument for rejecting the Amendments—the cost. It is always a powerful argument against doing anything. If the will is there, the means can be found. The means that I am about to suggest may not please the Government and their friends; but, before I turn to that, I want to rebut some of the Minister's arguments.

First, he was castigating my hon. Friend the Member for Oldham, West (Mr. Meacher) when he compared the weekly tax concession of £1 all round with the 75p that was to be given to the old people. He said that it was not a fair comparison; it was not comparing like with like. In one sense, he is right: the £1 a week concession was given in the first week in May, but the 75p will be given in the first week in October. He did not mention that difference—a vital difference to the poorer sections of our community.

He said also that part of that £1 would be paid back in increased contributions to pay for the increase in pensions. That is true, but it can be used by the Government to justify, for example, even in the JPEG 227 1971 Budget, giving Lord Stokes, the head of British Leyland, an extra £67 a week tax concession on the grounds that he might be paying a little of that back to give the old-age pensioners 75p or whatever they have had in total since 1971. It must be remembered that, apart from inheritance, capital is largely built up over a period of time, through saving. Capital is also usually vested in one particular person in a family. If one assumed a society in which there was total equality of incomes, and no inheritance whatsoever, in which all capital had to be financed out of saving, and people saved at a constant rate through time, clearly one would not expect an old person to have the same wealth as somebody just starting to work. One could under-estimate the dramatic effect that this has on the mathematical distribution expression of the distribution of wealth.
