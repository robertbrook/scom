: The Committee has had the advantage of hearing the Amendments moved in clear, persuasive and temperate terms. We have also heard 50 an extremely helpful analysis by the right hon. It is common ground that the time is ripe for some move from the forensic procedure of inquiry to an examination in public. The compelling reason is the time factor and the clear danger that structure plans might otherwise become out of date in the context of existing circumstances by the time that they are confirmed. It is an abiding danger in town planning that we have seen over the years—rather a case of what Mr. Harold Macmillan called on one occasion "looking up your trains in last year's Bradshaw." What is wanted is the highest common factor, the economy of time and a full examination of the issues with as much public participation as possible.

The Amendments, as I understand them, are directed to that end. The matter falls under several heads: first, the position of individual objectors under new Section 9 (3)( a) of the 1971 Act, as proposed in the Clause, to which my hon. Friend the Member for Hemel Hempstead (Mr. Allason) referred. Secondly, there is the question of those who will participate, and thirdly the question of the issues which are to be listed under the new subsection (3)( b) in Clause 3 of the Bill.

There is a fourth matter which does not arise under the Amendments but which is vital, and to which I should like to refer during the "Clause stand part" debate if the opportunity arises. That is the actual conduct of the public examination which the Committee should know a little more about before we part with it.

The position of individual objectors under the new Section 9 (3)( a) of the 1971 Act is dealt with in paragraphs 16 to 18 of the consultation document under the heading, "Handling of Objections". In effect, objections under the new proce- JPEG 51 dure will fall into two categories, a kind of categorisation into sheep and goats—those that make the grade as constituting issues for the examination in public, and those that do not. I believe that we ought to ask my hon. Friend how he envisages that the Secretary of State will fulfil his statutory duty of considering the objections that fall into the latter category.

Perhaps the Minister could also tell us how the consideration of objections which will not be dealt with at the examination—and which will presumably take place behind closed doors and without any impact on the outside world—will phase in with the examination in public of the major issues. Not least, how is it proposed that the Government will convince the unseen and unheard objectors that their objections have had the consideration to which they are entitled under the Statute, and under the terms of natural justice? My hon. Friend needs no reminder of the old maxim about justice being manifestly seen to be done.

These questions do not appear to be wholly answered in paragraphs 16 to 18 of the consultation document. In view of their importance, I ask for further elucidation. It would be undesirable if an objector were to feel that he was unseen and unheard at the stage of the structure plan, and that at the local plan stage he was faced with a res judicata which makes his objection at that stage not meaningful.

This matter is important not only in town planning but in the full context of public participation in a democratic society. The Amendments put forward one method—in the case of the Amendment of my hon. Friend the Member for Birmingham, Handsworth (Mr. Chapman) it is perhaps a drastic method of dealing with the difficulties, because of the impact of the time factor—but there is no doubt that both Amendments are animated by an acceptance of the inherent difficulties of the present situation, and they underline the desirability of a flexible approach.

Thus, the question which arises, in relation to the invitation to participants and the listing of issues, is how full participation and a full canvas of all relevant issues may be ensured without creating, or re-creating, that very delay which the new procedure is aimed at eliminating.

The Minister for Local Government and Development (Mr. Graham Page)
: I shall deal with the Amendments one by one, except that I will take Amendments 3 and 4 together, and then go on to pick up observations which have been made by right hon. and hon. Gentlemen during the course of debate.

Amendments Nos. 3 and 4 go to the root of the proposed procedure. Their effect would be to secure to every objector—I stress the word "objector" because it is not the objector with whom we are really concerned in this new procedure—a right to be heard, except where the Secretary of State considers either that an objection is not relevant to a structure plan or that the points at issue in an objection are dealt with in another objection which is to be heard.

On that basis, these Amendments would effectively restore the traditional public inquiry. We should be looking at objections rather than representations. We should be going back to a form of litigation, rather than a form of discussion. As the Government have argued throughout Second Reading and in another place, an inquiry is not the most appropriate method. What we want from this new procedure is the most appropriate method for the Secretary of State to inform himself on the key issues which he needs to consider in public in forming a view on a structure plan. We felt that an inquiry hearing objections was not the best way in which the Secretary of State could so inform himself.

Therefore, the drafting of subsection (3) was quite deliberate. Paragraphs ( a) and ( b) separate the two elements of the 56 Secretary of State's consideration of the structure plan. First, the Secretary of State remains under the statutory obligation to consider all objections to the plan. The consultation document makes clear our intention to provide every possible means of ensuring that objections will be made in writing, and to a degree of detail which precludes the possibility of any doubt existing in the mind of the Secretary of State as to the purpose of the objection. On receiving an objection, the Secretary of State will be bound by statute to consider it, and to discover the real basis behind it.

I should like to refer to the point raised by my right hon. and learned Friend the Member for Hertfordshire, East (Sir D. Walker-Smith). The objections of those who do not make the grade as objectors or who do not make the grade as representatives at the discussions will be considered at the local plan stage. This is really what the Secretary of State has to decide. When he receives an objection he has to decide whether it is an objection on which a discussion could rightly be based on a strategy shown in the structure plan, or whether it is an objection which is more detailed and would be better dealt with on the local plan.

There is an obligation for all objections to be considered on the local plan. That is contained in Section 13 of the 1971 Act. If they are of the type designated in the regulations, a public inquiry should be held about those objections. The objection will be moved one stage further along the procedure if it does not qualify for discussion on the structure plan.

May I also deal with the point raised by my hon. Friend the Member for Hemel Hempstead (Mr. Allason). He gave an example of a by-pass going through an estate because the structure plan chose to put the by-pass to the north instead of to the south of the town. I should have thought that that should be a matter for discussion in the structure plan. A by-pass or a through relief road to a town is one of the major strategies of planning of an area.

Of course, it will be for the Secretary of State to reach a decision on this question. My hon. Friend the Member for Handsworth, during our last sitting, raised that point in very potent terms, when he said:

"…as Clause 3 now stands, it puts the Sec- JPEG 57 retary of State in an invidious position. For example, he might have to decide whether to give the right to one amenity association to appear at a public examination of a structure plan but not to another."—[OFFICIAL REPORT, Standing Committee D; 20th April, 1972, col. 40.]

I readily agree that this is at the basis of our new procedure, and that it is the very heavy responsibility of the Secretary of State to choose the issues and the participants in the structure plan. In so doing, he must ensure that he is not depriving anyone of the right to their objection to the plan being heard and that, if such persons do not qualify for hearing during the proceedings of the public examination of the structure plan, they will be afforded a later opportunity. They should not be deprived of such later opportunity through any fait accompli in the structure plan when it is approved.

So much for the obligation to consider objections. The second of the statutory obligations, imposed by subsection (3)( b), relates to the holding of the examination in public, and of

"…such matters affecting his consideration of the plan as he considers ought to be so examined."

The obligation is placed fairly and squarely under the Secretary of State, and, by law, it will be those matters which he considers ought to be examined. In reaching such a decision he may well be assisted by the chairman of the panel and by the panel itself. When the issue approaches the hearing stage, at which time, as my hon. Friend pointed out at our last sitting, points which previously appeared not to be controversial may suddenly become so, the panel may well prove to be the appropriate source of advice to the Secretary of State. As the consultation document indicates, it might be proper for the Secretary of State at that point—at the approach of or during the course of the hearing—to delegate to the panel his powers of consideration whether or not a matter is relevant.

I think that the panel should be afforded such flexibility when taking such examination in public. According to the provisions of paragraph 28 of the consultation document, if an unforeseen point arises the panel should be able to decide whether it was a point which the Secretary of State would have included as a matter for consideration in the examination.

First, power is taken to approve the Greater London Development Plan in stages, that is to say, to approve any feature or element of the Plan while reserving the decision on other features and elements. That is paragraph 5(2) and consequential changes. There is no ulterior motive in this except that I foresee that when we have the report of the G.L.D.P. inquiry, there may be items in the Plan which then emerges with which we can deal at once. That applies to the rest of the country and not to London.
