Mrs. Castle
: As you rightly suggest, Sir Stephen, Amendment No. 2, which may look a little obscure on its own, is given meaning by Amendment No. 10. Taking the two together, the purpose is shown to be simple.

By Amendment No. 2 we seek to remove the present trinity of rates for the invalidity allowance. It may seem a little odd that we should seek to do this till we turn to Amendment No. 10, where we see that the consequence of Amendment No. 2 would be to substitute, for the age of onset of the trio of allowances, one blanket allowance at the higher rate for all those who are entitled to the invalidity allowance. The purpose is simple. It is to get rid of the difference in financial terms for those qualifying for the invalidity allowance. It is, therefore, designed to put on the same basis all those who have been found, because of chronic sickness or disability, to be entitled to an invalidity allowance in terms of their needs for additional allowance.

The Bill makes a modest increase in the size of the invalidity allowance. Heaven knows, it is a modest one. The hon. Gentleman has on many occasions said that the invalidity allowance scheme is a very modest start, and he will be the first to appreciate that this adjustment in the already modest allowance is more modest still. The position under the Government's proposal is that those under the age of 35 at the time of onset shall get an additional 15p on the higher rate, thus bringing the higher rate to £1.15. Those over 35 and under 45 at the time of onset are to get an additional 10p 6 on the middle rate of 60p, bringing it to the magnificent sum of 70p. Those over 45 and under 60, if men, or 55, if women, are to get 5p on the lower rate of 30p, bringing them to the bonanza sum of 35p. The increase in the higher rate is not exactly lavish—15p does not go very far these days—and having recognised the existence of the need here we can hardly claim that we are discharging our responsibilities over-lavishly by achieving £1.15 on the higher rate.

I make it clear at the onset that even with the increase we think that the higher rate is inadequate. We would like it to have been far more within the sort of range which the Disablement Income Group has been talking about, but, as I have said, we shall not in this Committee be making debating points or unrealistic demands for purely propaganda purposes. So we have put forward these two Amendments as a modest proposition. We are concentrating on principle rather than on rates, attempting to get rid of an invidious distinction in the needs of the chronic sick—a distinction on ground of age of onset.

This point was thoroughly threshed out by my hon. Friends when the National Insurance Bill, 1971, was debated in Committee. I am sure we have reread the proceedings with care and interest because the arguments used then will, no doubt, reappear again today. Indeed, my hon. Friends on that occasion moved a very similar Amendment. They wished then to get rid of this differentiation between those desperately incapacitated groups and to bring them all to the higher rate. There was a certain amount of encouragement at the time in the replies given by the Under-Secretary of State. Indeed, I was heartened to read that he said:

"…I assure the Committee that our minds are not closed on any of these points. This is a new allowance. None of us can be absolutely sure, until we see it working in practice, whether we have got the ages right, whether we have got the steps right."—[OFFICIAL REPORT, Standing Committee G, 18th May, 1971; c. 56.]

Therefore it is an encouraging starting point that the Under-Secretary of State has come today with an open mind, because I assume that he has not closed it in the past 12 months.

It would be very odd indeed if he had done so, because we have had about a year's experience of the working JPEG 7 of the new allowance and I think we can now be sure of two things. In the first place, this discrimination in the allowance on the ground of age has caused a great deal of bitterness. I have had letters from constituents about it, as a number of my hon. Friends have. A chap asked me, "Why am I only to get 60p? Why cannot I have the £1? My difficulties are acute. Why the distinction?" I had to say to him that I could not on any rational basis see why the distinction should be made—not in terms of his need, or in terms of his invalidity or in terms of his financial position.

When eventually the elderly get their turn the figure jumps to £45 million. Once again the Government have managed to find a good way, wrapped up in sociological and human arguments, for starting at the cheapest end of the scale.

In the same way, by refusing to pay the oldest groups at the highest rate, as we suggest in the Amendment, the Government are conveniently managing to save themselves £10 million.
