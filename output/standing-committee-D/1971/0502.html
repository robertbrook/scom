Clause 5
CONTINUANCE IN FORCE OF PROVISIONS RELATING TO CONTROL OF OFFICE DEVELOPMENT

Question proposed, That the Clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. A. E. Cooper
: In asking the Committee not to allow Clause 5 to stand part of the Bill I recognise that I am dealing with something fundamental to the Bill itself. It should be borne in mind that the provisions of this Clause were laid down in the Industrial Development Act, 1965, and at that time they were not regarded as being a permanent part of our legislation. However, we all know that there is nothing more permanent in this life than that which is temporary, and that once something is put on the Statute Book it takes a great deal of effort to get it removed.

When the statement dealing with this subject was published by the Government of the day in 1964 there were obvious reasons why some control of this sort should be placed upon the Statute Book, but I emphasise once more that it was nevertheless regarded only as a temporary measure. For that reason a period of seven years was regarded as adequate.

If it were not for the Bill now before us this provision would expire on 5th JPEG 99 August, 1972, and, since it was put on the Statute Book, it should be borne in mind that on 7th December, 1970, as reported in HANSARD, at col. 45, the Minister increased the O.D.P. limit in Greater London from 3,000 sq. ft. to 10,000 sq. ft. These two things taken together—the temporary nature of the Bill plus the relaxation that has already been taken—imply that there is some doubt in the minds of the Government whether this restriction is any longer necessary.

10.45 p.m.

The Government first introduced a standstill on new offices in London. In doing so they recognised that it would be necessary to examine with the G.L.C. the whole employment situation in London in relation to housing, transport and services, in order to determine the level of office employment acceptable in the long term. Since then the Greater London Development Plan has been placed before the House and the country, and detailed information has been given on the subject. It is felt that sufficient information is now available to the House, and to this Committee in particular, to enable a firm decision to be taken as to the future of office control.

There are certain factors to consider. First, when this plan was introduced there was undoubtedly a proliferation of office accommodation throughout the whole of the Greater London area, much of which was unused and had remained empty for many years. But that is hardly the position today. Offices are being built and absorbed as quickly as they are put up. I know of no case where approval for office accommodation beyond the figure of 10,000 sq. ft. has been refused, which seems to imply that Government Departments, as such, recognise that there is this demand for accommodation and approve it.

At present there is a great deal of frustration because the applicant has three different authorities to pass through before he can obtain final approval. This is clearly time-wasting and costly. The Government, in the Bill, recognise that about £42,000 is spent annually in administering this provision, and that it involves the employment of appropriate staff. London is one of the greatest cities in the world, if not the greatest city. Its population is probably greater than that of most countries, and its financial 100 resources are certainly far greater than the budgets of many other countries. It will always be the trading capital of the world, and particularly of this country. As the Common Market develops, its position and place in the industrial life of this country will become even more important. No matter which political party controls it for the time being the Greater London Council is an authority with great responsibility, and I submit that it is now fully capable of handling the affairs of this great city, especially the question of office accommodation.

The hon. Member very properly said that originally the need for control over office development was proved; he accepted that. He is now saying that there is no longer such a need, for the various reasons that he has given, and he quoted the relaxation made in December, 1970.

The Committee would probably accept that a matter of principle is involved here. The very fact that London and the South-East is such a magnet—the Great Wen, as it has been called—means that the whole question of its resources and supplies comes into consideration. Not only that; above all, the movements of people and the way in which they travel to work need to be considered. These are all vital points in a consideration of the question of how much office development should be allowed in London and the South-East, and what we ought to do about it.

Some of us feared that in the end we might have a great trading office centre in London and the South-East, with people commuting backwards and forwards for many miles. Already, Sir Stephen, apart from the many people who have for some years commuted from your excellent and healthy part of the world—and I have known many of them—people are now commuting from as far as Peterborough, for example. There is an efficient train service, and a motorway, and that can be done.

The idea of an expanding office centre, taking up a disproportionate amount of the bricks, cement and other materials, JPEG 101 together with the labour involved in construction, would not be to the best interests of London, the South-East, and the country as a whole.

As time has gone by it is probably right that the need to control office development has lessened. The hon. Gentleman was right to draw our attention to the relaxation that has been made. But it seems to me that the proper way to do this is in the way that the Minister has suggested, which is to retain the provision for a further five years, while we see how London develops, and so that we can deal with all these problems, at the same time leaving relaxation as a question of administration. An O.D.P. is undoubtedly easier to obtain today than it was four or five years ago. I suppose that that reflects the fact that some relaxation is needed, but there is no reason why this should not continue until eventually, if the House so decides, we can abolish office development control. I do not know whether we shall be able to. Other considerations may arise in five years' time. Certainly the considerations at the moment are important.

Many of the hon. Gentleman's fears can be met if the administration is tackled with some degree of imagination, and with a knowledge, built up over the past seven years, of the problems involved. For that reason, I hope that the Minister will not go back on the provisions of the Clause. I am sure that he will not. It is a valuable Clause. It enables one to judge the situation clearly, almost from day to day, and come to the right conclusion at any moment. For that reason, I regret to say that I could not support the hon. Gentleman.

Mr. Page
: I hope to answer the points made by the hon. Member for Wandsworth, Central (Mr. Thomas Cox) right away. On the question of office blocks remaining empty, I do not think that he JPEG 103 will find that within the controlled area of London and the South-East any recently-built blocks remain empty. As for the inner London suburbs having gigantic office buildings in the centre, with the result that they are deserted outside office working hours, I do not think that we could apply O.D.P. control in considering the life of the suburb in that way. It is undoubtedly a town planning matter which the local planning authority should consider when giving planning permission for the erection of office blocks of this sort. But that kind of consideration, however important, should not enter into the administration of O.D.P.s. It is very important, of course, but it is for the local planning authority to consider.

We are considering whether to delete the Clause. The law at present provides for office development control to lapse on 5th August, 1972, and the Clause has the effect of extending the control by a further five years. There is a five years' limit, with power to reduce that period by order of the Secretary of State. To delete the Clause would bring to an end the operation of the control in August of this year, as provided originally in the Control of Office and Industrial Development Act, 1965, which is now consolidated with the 1971 Act.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Ilford, South (Mr. Cooper) said, that Act was intended as a temporary Measure, and the present limit was imposed in 1965 for that reason. We have altered the application of the control. It now applies only to office development schemes of 10,000 sq. ft. and upwards in the South-East economic planning region.

As I have said in the Second Reading debate, when I gave the reasons why the Government had decided to extend the life of the existing control, the prime intention is to use the control to help in the implementation of the strategic plan for the South-East, the principles of which have been accepted by the Government, 104 and to implement it to ensure that office growth takes place mainly in the growth areas specified in that plan. Therefore, we are not considering London alone—although my hon. Friend the Member for Ilford, South and the hon. Member for Wandsworth, Central (Mr. Thomas Cox) addressed their remarks mainly to London. This is a matter for the whole of the South-East. It is hoped that the aim of concentrating development in growth areas will be achieved eventually through the mechanism of the structure plans and the local plans, about which we spoke on earlier Clauses. This is still some way in the future, as our debates have showed. In the meantime, the more direct control involved in the O.D.P. system will be utilised to inhibit office growth in what the Government consider are unsuitable places, and to channel it into areas which the strategic plan for the South-East indicates are in need of it.

Since the major source of mobile office employment is London, such a policy necessarily means the retention of restrictions in the capital. I go along with my hon. Friend the Member for Ilford, South in all that he has said about the present importance of the capital and its future increasing importance in connection with the Common Market. I assure him that we will administer the O.D.P. controls with close regard to that consideration. The last thing that we want to do, particularly in the City, is to obstruct developments which will have to be carried out as a result of our entry to the Common Market.

At the same time, it would be impossible at this stage to remove control altogether. We can use it in a positive rather than a negative way, particularly in considering whether applications for permits are in accordance with the general trend of Government policy, and whether they are developments for tenants who need to be within the area of London and the South-East.

The Government policy on office location was expressed succinctly in the White Paper on Industrial and Regional Development, published in March, in which the following statement appeared under the heading of "Office Development Permits":

"There is no case for removing this control in view of the planning pressures in the South-East. But in the administration of the control special account will be taken of the importance JPEG 105 of enhancing the prospects of London as an international financial and commercial centre."

I ask my hon. Friend the Member for Ilford, South to appreciate that in the administration of this provision we are agreeing with all that he said about the importance of London. We believe that we can use O.D.P.s positively to meet future demand and channel it into the right areas of London and the South-East, and be necessarily selective in respect of the type of office work for which we give permits.

If the Clause were removed altogether a consequential technical provision, which is included in order to ensure certain unused planning permissions for office development, would go by the board; we would lose the valuable provision made in the Clause that permissions for office development granted before 5th August, 1965, do not lapse in 1974 under the general time limits for implementing planning permission contained in the 1971 Act. That is an advantage which I am sure my hon. Friend would not want to lose by removing the Clause altogether.

I give the assurance that the system will be administered as a transition stage towards eventually moving this control entirely into town and country planning. I have always wanted to do that. It is no secret that I studied the possibility at this stage to see whether we were ready to move it there and dismantle the O.D.P. system. We shall not be ready until the structure plans and local plans for the South-East are far more advanced. When we reach that stage we can consider leaving the matter entirely to local planning authorities and planning law, but until then we must retain our existing control.
