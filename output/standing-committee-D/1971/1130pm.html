Mr. James Lamond

Members opposite, if not to speak now, at least during the debate on local authority boundaries to give closely reasoned and fairly lengthy explanations of why the Government are either to change their minds and accept these representations that we have received, or to reject them.

I was about to give an account of my experience in what I consider to be a unitary authority, as unitary as any authority is likely to be. That authority is in Scotland, but the experience there is not irrelevant. That authority had responsibility for almost all the services provided within its boundaries, with the exception of the fire service and, more recently, water supply, which was placed under the responsibility of the North-Eastern Regional Water Board a year or two ago. Up to that time the authority had supplied water very satisfactorily. For 700 years, this unitary local authority has existed and provided services to as near the satisfaction of the ratepayers as one could 104 wish. Ratepayers, of course, always have a grievance, mainly about the level of rates; but the services provided met their satisfaction.

My experience is that system of the two-tier authorities, has faults. I know the Minister would like to refer to a two-sphere authority. That has been mentioned today. But if there are to be two local authorities within an area, I do not believe that the citizens of the area will regard them as anything other than two-tier. We may call them two-sphere and even suggest that they have equal responsibility and so on. but the people concerned will always regard one as having more authority than the other, one to be blamed by the other when some service is not being satisfactorily provided.

I have noticed that where there are two-tier authorities in Scotland—I am sorry to draw my example from Scotland, but it is relevant—with small district councils, for example, interest in them is not nearly so great as the interest in a bigger local authority, such as Aberdeen. That runs contrary to the experience of my hon. Friend the Member for North-field. A measure of the interest shown by the citizens may be found in the number of candidates presenting themselves for election to local authorities. For example, in the unitary authority of Aberdeen, for 12 seats there were 46 candidates, representing five or six different political parties—we have the S.N.P. there—and independents. That sort of competition for places means that that council is lively and attracts interest and attention.

However, with a district council, the lower tier in a county, it is common for no one to contest a seat for 21 years on end. a state of affairs which is neither healthy nor satisfactory. It means that people are not interested in what is going on. that they realise that at lower tier level it makes little difference who is elected to the district council.

I do not wish that state of affairs to prevail even more widely, as it would under the Bill. Thus. I expect Government supporters to defend their proposals and demonstrate why the careful and lengthy examination made by the Royal Commission has in the end resulted in proposals which, in the Government's opinion, were wrong. What criterion did they use to determine what constitutes a good local authority? I believe that giving a local authority the maximum freedom JPEG 105 to govern its own area is the best criterion.

Much was said in the Government's White Paper about the high ideal of much greater freedom for local authorities. But let us examine the Government's policies, particularly the Bill concerned with council rents, that will tie local authorities hand and foot in fixing council house rents, and even the petty Bill concerning school milk, that refuses to give a local authority power to determine whether it will provide free milk for schoolchildren. If the Government mean what they say about local authorities having more power, they should not deny them that type of power. After all, the authorities are answerable directly, and sometimes more frequently than we, to the people whom they are charging for providing children with free milk? If that is not a sufficiently good check on whether a local authority carries out a certain activity, it is wrong for Parliament to step in and tell them exactly what they must do, down to every last item of expenditure.

Among local authorities there is the feeling that the House of Commons regards them as pawns in a game, of being of not much account. The Bill will do little to dispel that impression. Local authorities feel that whatever they undertake, there is always Big Brother hanging over their heads, changing any proposal which may not suit the Government. I am saying that even a unitary system is a two-tier system, for the Government stands supreme over all local authorities. If we want local authorities to mean something to their citizens, we should be prepared to give them power and trust them to use it.
