NATIONAL INSURANCE BILL

clause Clause 2
title ATTENDANCE ALLOWANCES

Question proposed—(29th June)—That the Clause stand part of the Bill.

10.30 a.m.

Question again proposed.

The Under-Secretary of State for Health and Social Security (Mr. Paul Dean): At our last meeting the right hon. Lady the Member for Blackburn (Mrs. Castle) raised a number of important issues in this "Clause stand part" debate and it may be for the convenience of the Committee if I deal with them now and give hon. Members more information about the way in which we envisage the Clause and the extension of the allowance working in practice.

The right hon. Lady opened her remarks by referring to the Money Resolution. I assure the Committee that the Money Resolution is normal and in accordance with the precedent followed by Governments of both parties. Money Resolutions are always drawn fairly tightly because of the public expenditure considerations involved, but this does not in any way tie the Committee when discussing the conditions for entitlement, or the phasing of the extension of the allowance which appears in Schedule 4.

The Clause extends the scope of the allowance to those requiring attention or supervision day or night, in addition to the existing categories of day and night. The proposal is that the day and night requirement for attention and supervision shall be at the higher rate, uprated by Schedule 1, and that the day or night provision shall be at the lower rate. It is important to remind the Committee that in judging both the existing attendance allowance and the extension of the allowance the main criterion is not the extent of the disability of the person concerned, Col. 316 but the amount of attendance actually required. It is with that in mind that the higher and lower rate have been provided. The right hon. Lady asked me for more details of the proposed arrangements. I interrupted her to explain that the Bill provided for arrangements similar to those currently obtaining for the first stage of the allowance which started in December, 1971. The right hon. Lady quoted subsection (3) but only part of it. If she had read on, she would have seen that the subsection continued:

"but, except in so far as regulations otherwise provide—"

If paragraph (a) is read in conjunction with subsection (2)(b), it provides for the allowance in each of the groups to be paid from the date when that group becomes eligible. In other words, all those people in that group who have applied within the period stipulated will be eligible to receive the allowance from the date laid down. The actual laying down of the dates is provided for in paragraph 3 of Schedule 4, Part II, which deals with commencing orders for the four groups.

The commencing orders have not yet been made, and it was to maintain as much flexibility as possible and to push ahead as fast as possible that the arrangements were made in this way, rather than a rigid series of dates being laid down in the Bill. I shall refer to that in more detail shortly, but that is the background to the provisions of the Clause and the supporting Schedule.

The right hon. Lady claimed that the Government had been forced into the extension by pressure and by the number of hard cases that were omitted from the first phase. That is not so. The Government said at the outset—and there was no party political disagreement about this—that it was desirable to start first with those most severely disabled and to try to get the allowance to them as speedily as possible, a procedure which entailed the drawing of strict criteria for the allowance.

I willingly concede that the criteria which were drawn last year were exactly those which were drawn by the previous Administration in the Bill which fell on the General Election. Admittedly, we made some changes in the allowance, but the criteria remained the same.

Of course, we realised that there would be hard cases. It is a classic phenomenon with allowances of this kind that when Image Col. 317 no allowance is granted, there are no hard cases, and immediately one introduces an allowance, hard cases appear. The Government accepted at the outset that this represented only a start and that the allowance should be extended as speedily as possible. Clearly, some of the heartbreaking cases which have emerged and which have been outside the existing allowance have increased our incentive to push ahead urgently with stage two.

In this connection, I wish to quote some figures relating to allowances that are relevant to understanding the Clause. According to the latest figures available, there have been more than 150,000 claims, compared with the original estimate of 100,000 claims. Awards made totalled 95,000, while awards currently in payment totalled 78,000, the difference between the two figures being largely accounted for by the high death rate among those who receive the allowance.

The figures illustrate the great amount of work done already by the Attendance Allowance Board and its assistants to get the allowance into payment to the large number of eligible, and, equally, claims are still coming in at the rate of more than 1,000 a week. It is important to remind the Committee that we shall be starting stage two within 12 months of stage one beginning, and there is still a substantial flow of claims under stage one.

That will continue after the new arrangements start, because the new arrangements do not in any way disqualify people from claiming for the higher allowance if they feel that they are entitled to it. If they are entitled to it, they will of course be paid the higher rate. We shall therefore be running the continuation of stage one and stage two alongside each other.

"An attendance allowance shall not be payable to a person for any period preceding the date on which he makes a claim."

To the lay mind that does not seem to say anything other than, "You may make a claim for attendance allowance, but until you do make a claim for an allowance, you are not eligible." Gentleman referred me to the operation of the previous attendance allowance arrangements, but there we were not dealing with the type of phasing in with which we are dealing here. Mr. Dean: I know that the legal language of these Bills is difficult to comprehend, but I assure the right hon. Lady that I have looked at it carefully since our meeting. What I have put in layman's language this morning is the legal effect, and it exactly follows the precedent of the previous occasion.

The date when the award becomes eligible will be the date laid down in the commencement orders. We have this six months' period for the taking on of claims in the same way as we had a period before December, 1971. Any person who claims during that period, however long it may take for the process of claim and Image Col. 319 review to take place, will receive payment from the date of the award.

I turn now to the extension of the allowance. I want to give the Committee a little more information and answer one or two of the observations by the right hon. Lady. The extension relates to those who require attention. We estimate that there will probably be 500,000 claims—it is difficult to be accurate and there may be more. We also estimate that there will probably be 250,000 awards. Within the four groups, only rough estimates are possible. We estimate 50,000 awards in the working-age group, 50,000 for children, 40,000 for the 67-74 age group, and 90,000 for those aged 75 and over.

To bring this scheme into operation will be, by any standards, a major job. Stage one—those entitled to allowance by day and by night, is still being continued even before the Attendance Allowance Board has had much time to absorb the lessons learned from this comparatively new allowance. Indeed, one may say that it has had no breather at all.

I am sure that hon. Members will wish to pay tribute to the Attendance Allowance Board, the doctors who work for it and to the lay staff for the magnificent job they have done. I was sorry that the right hon. Lady referred to the lack of humane administration in some instances. By all means let her blame politicians, but I think that the Committee will agree that the Attendance Allowance Board has done a magnificent job of work in bringing this completely new allowance into operation for so many people in such a short time.

The right hon. Lady asked me about the terminal cases. The attendance allowance is not specifically designed for terminal cases. One of the criteria laid down for the original allowance and for this is that the need for attendance must have existed for six months previous to a successful award. There is no doubt that because of the comparatively high death rate amongst those who are in receipt of an award, there will be many cases where this allowance is in operation when the people concerned die. It is the six-month criterion, rather than the actual terminal problem, on which the allowance is based at present.

I was asked how long it would take to bring the extension into operation. I assure the Committee that everybody con- Col. 320 cerned—the Government, the Board, the doctors and lay staff who serve on the Board, local general practitioners and others—will use all their dedication and energy to do this job as quickly as possible. But the job has to be done properly and each case requires careful individual attention. It requires a medical report from the general practitioner in the first instance. It requires a great deal of careful consideration at all stages, including, in the case of someone who has applied, the opportunity for that person to see all the papers and comments and to give his reaction before the case is finally decided.

We believe that it will take two years from the commencement of the new allowance in December. If we can do better, I assure the Committee that we will. It is for that reason that the bringing into operation of the four groups in stage two will be done by commencement orders.

We propose with the first group—the working-age group—that claims will be taken from December, this year, and that payments will begin from 4th June, 1973. That means that people who. claim by 4th June will be paid from that date. We envisage that the last group will become eligible to payment on 2nd December, 1974. I assure the Committee that if we can improve on these dates, we will. We are all anxious that these additions should be brought within the scope of the extended allowance just as soon as it can conceivably be done.

The next point, alluded to by the right hon. Lady, is on the phasing of the extension. It is an agonising choice for anybody to have to make, but we have received the help and advice of the Attendance Allowance Board. I am not sheltering behind the Board. The Government have a responsibility to make up their minds on the matter and they have done so. The Board has advised as to the need for phasing and the order in which the groups should be taken, and we have acted on that advice.

We know from the experience we have of that allowance already that had we opened the floodgates to perhaps half a million claims coming in at one time, we should almost certainly have ended up with those entitled to the allowance having to wait longer, rather than a shorter period, and that is why it has proved necessary to make that agonising Image Col. 321 choice, which has been made on the advice of experts.

Lord Amulree, the Chairman of the Board, is an extremely experienced and gifted doctor. He is the President of the British Geriatric Society. Members of the Board include a consultant in physical medicine, a psychiatrist, a pediatrician, the honorary director of DIG—tribute has already been paid in the Committee to that worthy organisation—a general practitioner, an assistant director of social services, a consultant chest physician and a medical director of a hospice for terminal illness. That wealth of expertise and experience has advised us as to the need for phasing and to the best ways of doing it. We have accepted that advice, and in the circumstances it is the best way to get the allowance into operation in an orderly fashion, without disappointing expectations, and without people having to wait for long periods.

It is equally important that we should have consistency and uniformity of medical standards. The need for the Board to prepare for the new arrangements, to keep track of the existing procedures and claims, and for its members to see a substantial number of them themselves to ensure uniform standards, is most necessary.

The right hon. Lady asked about liaison with the BMA and other organisations. I can assure her that it has been close. Before the first stage of the allowance started, the Board wrote to all general practitioners and sent them the appropriate material. It will be doing so again for each group in the extension of the allowance. Letters are to go out to all general practitioners to acquaint them with the new arrangements and to enlist their co-operation. There is the closest liaison between the Board and the delegated medical practitioners who do a good deal of the work both in the various regions and in the central attendance allowance unit at Norcross, near Blackpool.

I remind the Committee that the powers of review are extensive. Anybody who is dissatisfied with a decision may appeal for a complete review of the case within three months, when the whole process may be gone through again. After three months, it is possible, under slightly differing conditions, for the case to be looked at again. All members of the Col. 322 Committee probably have personal experience of writing to Norcross, to me, or to my right hon. Friend, and we have ensured that the reviews take place. There are more extensive possibilities of a case being looked at again within the review procedure than within the normal national insurance mechanism for appeals against decisions. The review procedure permits substantial flexibility, which is desirable when a new allowance is introduced.

The right hon. Lady referred to the cost of the allowance. The present allowance, including the uprating in the Bill, would cost £25 million in a full year.
