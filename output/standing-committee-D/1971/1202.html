Member for South Shields (Mr. Blenkinsop) at our last Sitting, I was not quite sure, when he finished, which form of provincial government he was advocating. He certainly set out a number of forms early in his speech and toyed with a number of functions for a provincial body. The Amendments, on the face of them, merely substitute provinces for counties, which would mean, I assume, 38 provinces to take over the position of the 38 counties with something of the same powers.

Perhaps I could try to summarise the sort of propositions for provincial government which have been put forward from time to time, and I will start with the interesting one of the hon. Gentleman the Member for Berwick and East Lothian (Mr. Mackintosh), in a book called "The Devolution of Power". In this book he proposed directly-elected councils. This was mentioned in some of the speeches on this Amendment during the previous Sitting of this Committee—the directly- 152 elected councils for nine regions in England with similar assemblies for Scotland and Wales. The hon. Member for Berwick and East Lothian would have given these councils responsibilities much the same as those mentioned by the hon. Member for The Hartlepools (Mr. Leadbitter) for provincial councils: regional planning, highways construction, major housing projects, police and fire services, higher and further education, hospitals and health services, and a number of other general functions

That is one idea, nine directly-elected councils for regions. That, I think, would involve a complete reorganisation of the machinery of government and a reallocation of central and local government functions. The other propositions, including the Redcliffe-Maud one, do not go quite as far as that. They propose provinces as part of a new local government system without completely reorganising the machinery of both central and local government. Redcliffe-Maud, for example, proposed provinces as complementary to the unitary authorities which that Commission recommended. The minority report in the Redcliffe-Maud Report by Mr. Senior proposed provinces as complementary to the two-tier system, so that one had a sort of three-tier system. The Association of Municipal Corporations put forward the proposal for provinces as the upper tier in a two-tier system.

Redcliffe-Maud proposed eight provincial councils for England; they would be indirectly elected with obligations to co-opt additional members. These councils would have the function of the strategic plan, and in exercising that function they would have bound the unitary authorities to follow out that strategic plan of the provincial councils.

Mr. Senior's proposals involved two operational levels, with 35 directly-elected regional councils and something over 148 other councils. The hon. Member for South Shields, I think, advocated at least consideration of Mr. Senior's proposals. Mr. Senior also had a third tier in the five provincial councils which he put on top of the 35 regional councils and the 148 districts.

We come to the Association of Municipal Corporations' proposal for 12 regions for England, having a minority of executive functions as compared with the majority of functions in the most-purpose authorities which the A.M.C. have always advocated.

In short, we accept that there are functions which need to be considered in a regional context. This, was a question which was asked by several hon. Members in speaking earlier to this Amendment.
