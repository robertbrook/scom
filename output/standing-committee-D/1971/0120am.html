Mr. Denis Howell

I have the rare privilege today to speak for the whole of Birmingham, because this is one of the few occasions when the whole of Birmingham politically is absolutely agreed about the disastrous nature of the Government's proposals and is determined in its opposition to them. The city is united in expressing its fears that the proposals which the Committee now has before it will deal a great blow to its future.

Birmingham, like other great cities, has not achieved its status by accident. It 724 achieved it in the past, under the leadership in the city of different political parties, because over the years it has combined the three essential requirements of any great city. The first of these can be stated as the major economic strength to sustain a large population and that, of course, means industry. The second requirement is the ability to house and to succour its people, which means good local government. The third requirement has been the foresight to meet a changing situation from generation to generation and from year to year and an ability to solve its own problems, and that means achieving an understanding between central and local government.

So Birmingham has prospered until now, but it cannot be emphasised too strongly that, if this Government, or any other, undermine the relationships, the city will be put into peril. When that happens, the industrial strength of Birmingham and of the country will certainly be in jeopardy. It is extremely unfortunate that this debate takes place at a moment when Birmingham already has grave fears for its future. The unemployment figures are disastrous. Yet the central Government have been very slow to respond to this situation, to sense that change in the industrial position, to respond to the need to bring in new industry and to expand the existing potential of Birmingham.

On top of this disastrous unemployment situation and the changing economic forces in Birmingham, the ability of the city council to solve its own problems and to govern itself will be removed by the Bill. Birmingham will cease to be an all-purpose authority. It will be left with all the pressures of a large urban population and with no land with which to ease those pressures or to meet that situation.

This Government seem to have a mania for destroying things that are working well. They are taking the great gamble that, in the case of our large cities, and particularly in the case of Birmingham, their proposals will work at least as well as the existing local government structure.

An important criterion is that, unless the Government can show that the new pattern of local government which they are proposing for Birmingham can be guaranteed to work at least as well as the existing situation, they have no business proposing any such change.

Having now spent several months JPEG 725 talking not only to the civic leaders but to the industrial leaders, university people and people of no political persuasion, I can find no-one, whose judgment I respect, who believes that the Government can put their hands on their hearts and say that the proposals for Birmingham will be at least as good for the future government of Birmingham as the existing situation which we are trying to terminate. The opposite is the case. The one authoritative investigation into the government of Birmingham and the future government of Birmingham was the Redclilfe-Maud Committee, who, as we know, plumped for the city region concept which we cannot discuss again today.

When the Government threw overboard the proposals of the Redclilfe-Maud Committee, they struck a serious blow at the viability of the city of Birmingham and not just from the point of view of local government. I cannot emphasise this too strongly, because a good city is a mixture of its industry, its local government, its relation with central government and its dealings with people, and all this is now put at risk.

On top of the changing and deteriorating economic situation in the city, we now have the Government proposals for the reorganisation of the water industry. It is the belief of everybody in Birmingham that this reaffirms all our worst fears of what will happen about the services and the organisation of the local government. The organisation of the City of Birmingham's water supply is a story of excellence, and I think that is a statement which challenges contradiction.
