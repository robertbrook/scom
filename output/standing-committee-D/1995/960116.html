North Wales Fire Services (Combination Scheme) Order 1995

4.30 pm

Mr. George Howarth
(Knowsley, North)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the North Wales Fire Services (Combination Scheme) Order 1995 (S.I., 1995, No. 3218).

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the South Wales Fire Services (Combination Scheme) Order 1995 (S.I., 1995, No. 3230).

Mr. Howarth
: It is incumbent on the Opposition to explain why we have prayed against the order and why this Committee sitting today. In my discussions with colleagues who represent Welsh constituencies, many of whom are here tody, and with those who are concerned about the fire service, both professionally and in local government, it became clear that there was a feeling that the Government's proposals would create the worst of all worlds. There was an impression that the Government had not listened to local authorities or to the public, who have a high opinion of the service that they receive from their local fire brigades. People would be fearful of any change—that is normal—but they are especially fearful about the changes proposed under the two orders.

It was felt that a wider consultation exercise might have resulted in a set of proposals more acceptable to all those who are worried about the future of fire authorities in Wales. Given the fairly drastic local government reorganisation in the Principality, I accept that some changes are necessary. We could not expect everything to remain the same because of the changes in the structure of local government. However, had a more in-depth consultation process been carried out a better result might have been achieved.

A seductive argument for a Greater Gwent fire authority has been put forcibly—and no doubt it will be put forcibly again today by some of my hon. Friends. There are some problems with the order as those who are currently covered by the Mid-Glamorgan authority might feel that the proposal is an encroachment into their area. Equally, they might consider that it would not serve those parts of the Mid-Glamorgan authority area that would be encompassed by the new Greater Gwent authority. I accept that controversy can be fuelled by some of the counter proposals.

What I am about to say is really more a question than a positive suggestion, namely, that to consider making the fire authorities coterminous with police authorities has a certain logic. Both emergency services perform similar functions in terms of strategic 4 support and necessary communications. Indeed, on occasions fire authorities and police authorities work closely together, for example, when servicing industrial emergencies or major road or rail accidents. Such strategic arguments should have been addressed more fully and I should have liked the opportunity to have rehearsed them before we reached this stage.

I accept that problems might arise for north Wales and Dyfed-Powys if we made the police authorities coterminous with the fire authorities. I know from the time that I worked in Wales in the early 1980
s
that considerable areas must be covered. Anyone who has driven regularly in those areas will know about the transport problems when trying to link up the centres. Those problems must be considered properly.

I conclude by asking the Minister whether any commonly used equipment could go to the new authorities. That has a direct bearing on our acceptance of the orders. For example, not all authorities use the same breathing equipment apparatus, so under the new arrangements those fire officers expected to use breathing apparatus from other authorities will need additional training. Not all fire engines are the same, so further training may be required. The same would apply to aerial appliances and much other equipment in common and general usage. Different types of equipment are used by the various authorities, so the new authorities will have to train officers in their use.

I wonder whether an assessment has been made of the cost of retraining and of new maintenance programmes that may be needed for such equipment. We recognise that it is not a straightforward problem. We recognise, too, that something had to change in the fire services because of the changes in local government. However, the Government missed an opportunity to consult more widely and to regulate with greater consent. It is my intention—although I could be persuaded otherwise by a cogent argument—to oppose the orders.

4.37 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Tom Sackville)
: The combining of fire brigades has been widely discussed during the past three years. There has been some controversy, but there has also been consultation and a great deal of discussion between Ministers, professionals and local politicians. As hon. Members know, the order is now in force, but it is incumbent on me to do what I can to reassure those hon. Members who are unhappy with it.

I remind the Committee that the examination of the most suitable brigade structure for Wales started in 1992. Shortly after that, as the hon. Member for Knowsley, North (Mr. Howarth) said, local authority reorganisation was discussed. The 1993 White Paper on the Government's proposals for the future of local government, including fire services, came down strongly in favour of a three-brigade structure. There was a clear perception that it would be a better and more efficient way of providing fire services in Wales. That was based on the clear advice of, among others, JPEG 5 the Chief and Assistant Chief Fire Officers Association and the Institution of Fire Engineers. It is important to stress that that advice remained unchanged throughout that entire period and I know of no resiling from it. Indeed, the advice was confirmed in a speech in Harrogate at "Fire 95" by the president of CACFOA.

Mr. Tim Smith
(Beaconsfield)
: Does my hon. Friend agree that with any reorganisation of fire services the most important criterion for assessing any proposed scheme is efficiency—in other words, whether the people in a particular locality are likely to receive the most efficient service? That means the speed at which they would expect a response to a 999 call. Can he assure me that the professional advice to which he referred—we must all rely on professional advice in circumstances such as this—was that the proposed scheme would be the most efficient scheme for Wales?

Mr. Sackville
: Yes. That is based on the concept that fire services must be provided in a way that does not involve an excessive overhead for each service. The optimum size for a brigade involves consideration of a great many variables, such as geography and local identity. There is a wide variation in the size of brigades across the United Kingdom, from those serving a population of 6·8 million in London to the smallest brigade in the Isle of Wight, which serves 117,000. Most of the old fire brigades in Wales were at the lower end of the scale, serving populations of 200,000 to 500,000. Efficiency can be achieved with a brigade of any size, but it was clearly the judgment of the professionals that larger brigades would be more efficient and involve less overhead, making more resources available for fighting fires.

Mr. Sackville
: Perhaps it was remiss of me not to pay tribute to all the firefighters in Wales and throughout the United Kingdom. We are all lost in admiration for the splendid job done by fire services, both full-time and retained. The hon. Gentleman's constitutents would no doubt agree with that.

Dr. John Marek
(Wrexham)

The Minister has called in aid the chief fire officers' opinion to justify the measure, but was any other opinion sought? He went on to speak about officers, 6 but so far he has quoted only the chief officers. I am open minded on the matter, but I need convincing by more than the opinion of chief fire officers.

Mr. Sackville
: The chiefs, assistant chiefs and the Institution of Fire Engineers were consulted. The matter has also been widely discussed in the Central Fire Brigades Advisory Council for England and Wales, which is the main expert committee that advises the Home Secretary. It is made up of representatives of a series of organisations, including employers organisations and trade unions. I am aware that the proposal has its opponents, but it commands strong and consistent professional support.

Sir Nicholas Scott
(Chelsea)
: Will my hon. Friend the Minister say to what extent the boundaries of the fire brigade provision are coterminous with those of the police, civil defence and other emergency services in Wales? Do overlappings or inefficiencies arise from a lack of co-ordination between the services?

Mr. Sackville
: It has already been said that there are advantages to coterminosity. When there were eight brigades and four police forces in Wales, there was no coterminosity. There will be coterminosity in some parts of the structure, but not in others. The situation has not changed radically.

I have long experience of merging health authorities and ambulance services in England. We always took coterminosity as one factor, but we did not treat it as an absolute, as it cannot always be achieved.

Mr. George Howarth
: May I offer the Minister a word of advice? If he is seeking to persuade Opposition Committee members, he should not pray in aid his experiences with the health service.

Mr. Sackville
: On the contrary, I am proud of the fact that, during my time at the English Department of Health, the number of health authorities was reduced from 192 to just over 100. That was done for reasons similar to those advanced for the present change. There is a limit to how much one wants to spend on overheads, individual training or administrative facilities, as that money always comes from what is available to deliver the service, be it an ambulance, fire-fighting or civil defence service. There is a strong analogy.

Mr. Win Griffiths
(Bridgend)
: The Minister is making an interesting point. Will he tell us what the estimated savings are for the new structures?

Mr. Sackville
: The three new chief fire officers have just got behind their desks and are, for obvious reasons, not yet comitting themselves, but if there are three, rather than eight, administrative structures, there will, over time, be savings. There may also be considerable advantages if, for example, there is a larger training department than there was for a succession of smaller brigades.

No one can commit themselves to a figure now, but it is common sense to say that if there is a suitable figure for each brigade and there are fewer brigades, there are likely to be fewer administrative overheads.

Mr. Win Griffiths
: The Minister's reply is astounding. During reorganisation of local government in Wales, the Welsh Office—and I may have disputed its figures—said that reorganisation would result in specific savings. Is the Minister saying that the current proposal is a leap in the dark and that he has not costed savings? Mr. Sackville
: I am sorry that I cannot come up with any figures that the hon. Member for Bridgend (Mr. Griffiths) could then dispute, but it would be unwise to make any estimates. If three administrative structures and three managements can deliver fire services as well as eight can, there will be savings—that is common sense.

Mr. Nick Ainger
(Pembroke)

That is without any growth in the service and will merely replace the central administration and so on that they were paying. In other words, in the first year it will cost a great deal more to provide the same level of service with three authorities than with the current eight.

Mr. Sackville
: I remind the hon. Gentleman that transitional moneys have been provided. Obviously any claims in the first instance will cost money, but we are aiming for long-term reform and administrative reorganisation. I have no doubt that when decisions are taken by the three new chief fire officers—they cannot be taken instantly—there will be opportunities for savings. That is not the only reason for the change, but it is an important one.

Mr. Sackville

She carefully considered all those ideas, but found no grounds, on which to change the view that the best way to deliver fire services in Wales was to have three fire brigades. In my noble Friend's opinion, nothing emerged during the consultation process to contradict the professional advice.

Mr. Touhig

Mr. Sackville

The question of whether consultation occurred was tested in court. As the Committee will know, there was a judicial review because local authorities believed that that consultation had not been properly undertaken. The High Court decided that consultation had been undertaken. It is not for me or any Member to attempt to contradict Judge May.

Mr. Llew Smith
(Blaenau Gwent)
: The Minister keeps informing us that the Government based their conclusions on professional advice. Does he consider the advice of the Fire Brigades Union to be professional? What response did he receive from that union?

Mr. Sackville
: I am well aware of the attitude of the Fire Brigades Union attitude and its opposition to the proposal. In our opinion, nothing that was said JPEG 9 contradicted the overall view that the optimum arrangement would be to have three fire brigades. As a result, the draft combination schemes were laid before the House on 24 October and, when the 40-day period under the negative resolution procedure expired on 8 December, orders were made on 10 December, which came into force on 11 December. So the shadow authorities responsible for making new arrangements are now working, with the able assistance of Her Majesty's inspectorate of fire services. I have absolute confidence that they will further develop the high level of skill and professionalism that has always been associated with fire services in Wales.

: I want to make one or two points. I emphasise the fact that all eight existing brigades performed well on the Audit Commission's criteria and that six of the eight were above average for the United Kingdom.

Mr. Tim Smith
: It is rather strange that the order mentions the first chief officer by name. Why does it mention the first chief officer and not the second and third? I find that strange, and I hope that my hon. Friend will be able to answer that question.

Mr. George Howarth
: In logic, the hon. Gentleman is perfectly right. One would expect that by reducing the number of fire authorities, one would obtain economies of scale in overheads. Mr. Tim Smith
: We need to distinguish between transitional costs—I accept that any reorganisation incurs extra transitional costs—and the on-going revenue costs, in which common sense suggests that a saving will be made, because there will be a streamlined operation. If that were the only consideration for providing an efficient fire service in Wales, a case could be made for there being only one fire authority but that would not be desirable for the people of Wales.

I do not know a great deal about Wales, but I know sufficient to say that the north of Wales is a long way from the south and that communications between the two are not good. Had coterminosity been the most important criterion, the number of fire authorities would have been coterminous with the new local authorities—that is four in number. I can see the advantages of that. However, the argument in favour of coterminosity is often overstated.

Coterminosity is neat and tidy, but each case should be considered on its merits. I hope that it is not out of order, Mr. Hughes, to mention what happens in Buckinghamshire. It is not suggested that the police force should be reduced or that the fire service should be increased. Both work perfectly well without coterminosity. If, as my hon. Friend said, the smallest authority in England and Wales is on the Isle of Wight, serving some 100,000 people, and the largest authority in Greater London serves more than 6 million people, we could ask the cost per head of providing that service. That would be one way of discovering whether good value for money was provided.

We should consider whether the taxpayer gets good value for money and enjoys the economy, efficiency and effectiveness that might reasonably be expected from a fire service. From what I have heard today, the order is entirely sensible. I hope that it will be supported by the Committee.

Mr. Ainger
: I want to make some points about the Mid and West Wales fire authority, which will be created under the combination order. There will be an impact on more than half the surface of Wales—that is, the area that the authority will cover. I speak as someone who, from 1981 to 1993, was a member of the Dyfed county council public protection committee, which was responsible for overseeing the work of Dyfed fire brigade.

I want to put on record the effect the changes will have on mid and west Wales. My hon. Friend the Member for Clwyd, South-West (Mr. Jones) mentioned the size of the area covered by the North Wales fire authority, but it is not as large as that covered by the Mid and West Wales fire authority.

I spent some time before this Committee sitting looking at maps in the Library. The distance between the far south west—St. Ann's Head—and the far north east—Welshpool is the largest town there—of the area covered by the proposed authority is the same as that between Bristol and Greenwich, Westminster and JPEG 13 Nottingham, or Westminster and Wolverhampton. The hon. Member for Beaconsfield may well say that his police authority takes in the whole of the Thames valley, but I wonder whether he would like to have a fire or police authority that stretched from Bristol to Westminster. Indeed, it would probably pass close to his constituency. I am sure that he would find that unacceptable in terms of accountability. I am sure that it would not be efficient.

As has been said, we have lived through one reorganisation of local government in Wales, we must now live through another. Before the 1974 reorganisation, we were told by Ministers that big was beautiful. We were told that combining local authorities such as Pembrokeshire, Carmarthenshire, Meirionnydd and Monmouthshire, for example, and creating Dyfed, Powys and Gwynedd, would produce efficiencies, but the efficiencies did not come. We had to have new legislation to create local authorities to look after our education, social and fire services.

I do not accept what the Minister says, especially as evidence from the budgets of the three new authorities shows that each will have to spend more than 10 per cent. on new infrastructure. Those costs will not be one-off; they will be on-going. The authorities have had the benefit of using the central administration system of existing county councils, such as their legal services and treasurers, but now they will have to start paying for them or set up their own. That is why there is a significant increase in their running costs.

It is true that there will be an overall reduction in the number of chief fire officers, but the bulk of the administration work of fire brigades is done in the divisions, not at central headquarters. We will find, especially in respect of the three new authorities, that more people will have to be located at headquarters to look after a huge area. That is why the budgets are increasing.

Dyfed fire brigade has an excellent record. It is world renowned for the fires that it has fought on the Milford Haven waterway, as is the West Glamorgan fire brigade in respect of the petrochemical and steel industry complexes in that area. I fear that that excellence might be diluted between two competing authorities, with local pressure being put on the need for more fire brigades in urban areas. Pressure on rural areas will be manifest. Rural fire brigades are already totally dependent on a retained service and it is becoming more difficult to recruit.
