Draft Jobseeker's Allowance (Pilot Scheme) Regulations 1996

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Department for Education and Employment (Mr. Eric Forth)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Jobseeker's Allowance (Pilot Scheme) Regulations 1996.

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the draft Income Support (Pilot Scheme) Regulations 1996.

Mr. Forth
: First, I take the opportunity of wishing happy birthday to the hon. Member for Makerfield (Mr. McCartney), who has been generous enough to share this special day with us. I hope that we shall not spoil it for him; that certainly is not my intention.

Mr. Ian McCartney
(Makerfield)
: It is actually a double celebration, because I became a grandfather as well.

Mr. Forth
: On behalf of the Committee I congratulate the hon. Gentleman on both counts.

The Government are seeking powers to test a new approach to help unemployed people back to work. These regulations, if approved, will enable us to do that through the project work pilots. The regulations are made under powers in section 29 of the Jobseekers Act 1995. That allows pilots of changes to benefit regulations to be set up in limited geographical areas and for a limited, specified period of time. The 1995 Act provides that such regulations must be made

"with a view to ascertaining whether their provisions will, or will be likely to, encourage persons to obtain or remain in work".

That is precisely the aim of project work. The pilots are targeted on people who have been out of work for two years or more. Experience tells us that those people need special help to find work. The pilots aim to build on, and extend, the approaches to assisting long-term unemployed people that we have developed with some success in recent years.

I will introduce the regulations to the Committee first by describing how the pilots will work and how they are designed to meet the particular needs of people out of work for a very long time, and, secondly, by detailing the particular powers that the regulations would introduce.

The pilots will be operated by the employment service in two locations—the Hull and Medway and Maidstone travel-to-work areas. They will apply to all people aged 18 to 50 in those areas who have been claiming unemployment benefits for two years or more. We expect around 8,000 to 9,000 people in total to fall into that group.

When people in the client group attend their first six-monthly restart interview in the pilot, they enter the first 2 part of the pilot, which lasts for 13 weeks. In that period unemployed people are offered intensive and structured help with job search. They will be offered a series of regular advisory interviews to help identify more clearly the barriers to finding a job, and to develop an action plan. The main aim will be to submit people to suitable jobs. But other help will be available for those who need it. They may be referred to job clubs for further help in looking for jobs, or to training for work to improve their skills. A jobfinders grant of between £100 and £400 will also be available to help overcome financial problems in taking work.

We will in addition aim to put unemployed people in direct touch with employers through trial periods during which they can demonstrate their abilities in a job while remaining on benefit. And employers who recruit people from the pilot will be able to claim a workstart subsidy of up to £1,500.

I hope that the Committee will welcome these proposals, and, in particular, that it will wish to note the use of workstart. This approach, which we have tested in separate trials, was advocated by the Select Committee on Employment in its recent report on workfare. It has, of course, received robust support from my Hon. Friend the Member for North Norfolk (Sir R. Howell), who has taken a strong and consistent interest in these issues over a long period. For some people, the intensive voluntary help which I have just described has already begun.

We hope very much that the great deal of help given in the first 13 weeks will enable many people to find work. If, however, after this period, they are still claiming unemployment benefits, they will be allocated a place on a work experience programme, which will last for a further 13 weeks.

The work experience element of project work will be provided under contract, and will involve projects of value to the community. In most cases, people will carry out 18 hours of work experience each week and, in addition, three hours of supervised job search activity. Unemployed people who take part will gain valuable and up-to-date work experience to help demonstrate their abilities to employers. Importantly, they will also continue—with structured help—to look for work. Indeed, providers' contracts are likely to contain incentives to find work for participants. We intend that the full period of work experience should be available, even if there are short interruptions. People may, for example, start work experience but leave for a short spell, perhaps to take a job. If they return to benefit, having failed to stay in employment, they will be given a further place on a work experience project, to complete their full 13 weeks.

We expect that the vast majority of people will welcome the opportunity to take part in work experience. However, if an unemployed person refused to participate without good cause, benefit would be disallowed for up to 14 days in the first instance, and for periods of up to 28 days beyond that.

Over the past few years we have developed a wide range of programmes that have had success in improving the prospects of long-term unemployed people getting back to work. The new approach of project work builds on that experience. The pilots are JPEG 3 designed specifically to tackle the main problems that face people after a long spell of unemployment.

First, they may simply lose touch with the labour market. Their skills may become out of date; their search for work may become unrealistic. The pilots will provide intensive help to put these problems right. Secondly, employers may lack the confidence to take them on without recent work experience. Worktrials, workstart and, of course, the period of work experience help to tackle that problem. Thirdly, unemployed people may merely lose hope, and become demotivated. In those circumstances we have found that a requirement to attend a programme of structured help or advice can be the galvanising factor that leads to fresh hope and purpose.

The requirement to attend work experience in project work also helps to reinfoce a view that has gained considerable ground in recent years—that if people have been out of work for a long time, while the Government clearly have an obligation to provide financial support the taxpayer also has a right to expect that they will take positive steps to improve their own prospects of finding work. I have no doubt that the vast majority of unemployed people wants to work. But, for the few who have no intention of seeking a job, or who are claiming fraudulently, I would expect project work to offer a deterrent.

As the committee will see, the project work process is intensive and contains many different elements of help for unemployed people. The regulations that we are discussing concern one important aspect of the pilots—making people who fail to attend the work experience element subject to benefit sanctions. The regulations would provide that power under the income support legislation, which will apply until October this year, and to the jobseekers allowance once that is implemented.

The income support regulations were sent to the Social Security Advisory Committee in December, immediately following the announcement of the pilots. The SSAC decided not to consult in late February, and the income support and JSA pilot regulations were laid before the House in March. The first referrals to project work, for which the regulations will be needed, will take place in July, and we look to Committee members for support in approving them, so that the project work placements can go ahead.

Both sets of regulations make similar provisions. People who fail to attend the work experience element will be subject to benefit disallowance for up to 14 days on the first occasion, and up to 28 days on subject instances. We have designed the regulations so that they match as far as possible the general sanctions regime that will apply under JSA. However, we have built in a number of important safeguards. First, the requirement to attend must be issued properly. Written notice must be given to the unemployed person, and sanctions cannot apply unless there is a place available on work experience. Secondly, the normal rules for access to hardship payments will apply both under income support and JSA. Thirdly, those who can show good cause for not attending project work wil not be subject to sanction.

4 The provisions list a number of specific examples of good cause. I would draw the Committee's attention in particular to those covering people with caring responsibilities. There is also a general good cause provision to cover circumstances that may provide a genuine reason not to attend, but that we have not been able to foresee in the detailed list. Decisions will of course be taken by independent adjudication officers.

At the suggestion of the SSAC, we included in the income support pilot regulations a general provision on good cause. I understand that the Committee welcomed that and, in the light of the provision and certain other assurances on the position of people doing voluntary work, decided not to put the regulations out for general consultation.

I hope that the Committee will recognise that these regulations come forward to enable a process that has been designed with care. It is based on considerable practical experience of helping long-term unemployed people to find work. The regulations have been considered by the SSAC, and we have taken on board its comments.

We shall, of course, monitor and evaluate the results of project work most carefully. We shall be looking to see how far the approach helps unemployed people to find work when otherwise they would have remained on benefit. We shall consider what new light project work throws on the problems that people out of work for a long time have in getting a job. We shall take that fully into account when deciding further measures to help such people.

It is urgent that we provide extra help for the very long-term unemployed. All the evidence of the past suggests that people unfortunate enough to be out of work for two years or more may be left behind by the recovery unless we provide a special effort to assist them. Further, without help, people may tend to become dependent on state benefits and discouraged about their chances of getting back to work. Our experience shows that a period on work experience can be the key to restoring confidence and independence.

By approving the regulations, the Committee will enable the Government to test out a carefully devised programme of intensive help for people who need it most in the improving labour market. I am sure that the Committee will support that aim, and that it will support the regulations.

I commend the regulations to the Committee.
