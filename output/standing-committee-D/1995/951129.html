legislation Bristol Development Corporation (Area and Constitution) Order 1995

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Sir Paul Beresford): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Bristol Development Corporation (Area and Constitution) Order 1995.

I congratulate you, Mr. Anderson, on what I understand is your first chairmanship. I am sure that it will be exceedingly orderly. The behaviour of Conservative Members will be impeccable and I am sure that that of the hon. Member for Leicester, East (Mr. Vaz) will be as superbly impeccable as ever.

The order is one of four required to effect the wind-up of the Bristol Development Corporation. I shall refer to the other orders in a moment, but this is the only one subject to the affirmative resolution procedure.

The order revokes the Bristol Development Corporation (Area and Constitution) Order 1988, which designated the urban development area and established the corporation. Revocation is in two stages. That part which designated the development area is to be revoked on 1 January 1996 and that part which established the corporation is to be revoked on 1 April 1996.

Under the two-stage process, the development corporation will cease to exercise any operational functions after 31 December 1995. It is therefore appropriate that the urban development area status should cease to exist on 1 January 1996. The corporation will then enter a three-month non-operational period, during which, with minimal staff, it will prepare its final accounts and report and wind up its affairs. It will be finally dissolved as a statutory body on 1 April 1996.

Provisions requiring the corporation to cease to act from 1 January and dissolving it on 1 April will be contained in a dissolution order, which will not be subject to any parliamentary procedure.

The other two wind-up orders were laid before the House on 15 and 16 November and are subject to the negative resolution procedure. One transfers planning functions from the corporation to Bristol city council on 18 December. The other transfers to the Secretary of State for the Environment any property, rights and liabilities that the corporation has not disposed of by 19 December.

Having explained the statutory requirements, I shall now give the reasons for winding up the Bristol Development Corporation. All development corporations were intended from the outset to be limited life agencies. We are able to wind them up when they have largely achieved their aims.

Col. 4 Development corporations were set up to provide a focused approach to the physical and economic regeneration of particularly run-down urban areas. In Bristol, an area to the east of the city centre which had suffered from decline for more than 40 years was designated. That decline was recognised by the city council in its 1988 local plan for east Bristol and in the 1985 report, "Poverty in Bristol". The problems, including dereliction and obsolete infrastructure, were therefore well known, but were considered to be the almost irreversible effects of long-term structural change in manufacturing and demographic patterns.

The achievements of the corporation are considerable. It was established in January 1989, with a remit to revitalise the 360 hectares in its area. In July of that year it published its consultation document, "Vision for Bristol". After extensive consultation, its development strategy was produced in April 1990. That strategic vision has now been secured. By the end of the year, 64 hectares of previously derelict land will have been reclaimed, 116,000 sq m of new floor space will have been developed, almost 700 houses will have been built and 4,600 jobs will have been created. In addition, 6.4 km of road will have been provided and a private sector investment of £235 million will have been levered in. For every £1 of public money invested, the private sector will have invested £3.

The corporation built a major new stretch of road between the M32 and the A4, now known as the Bristol spine road. That important link in the national network now carries 22,000 vehicles a day which previously either used small residential roads as rat runs or went through the congested city centre. The road has reduced delays and pollution and has opened up previously inaccessible land which, in turn, has led to major development opportunities attracting more than £200 million of private investment. All that was achieved in record time within budget and caused minimal disturbance to business premises and none to houses.

Alongside that road is a new and hugely successful £50 million complex at Avonmeads. Within that complex is Bristol's first multiplex 14-screen cinema, a 26-lane bowling alley and several retail outlets and restaurants, some of which are reporting record attendances. But the best judges are the people of Bristol. Independent research conducted in the winter of 1994 showed that 92 per cent. of visitors thought that the Avonmeads development was something of which Bristol could be proud.

In the Avon valley, which has lain derelict for more than 14 years, there is now a thriving urban village. More than 670 houses, many for first-time buyers, have already been built with a further 400 approved and under construction. Despite the difficulties of the market, that development, which is the largest residential development in the city, has proven a huge success. Sir Lawrie Barratt recently visited the site, declared his faith in the area and praised the corporation for its efforts. But the real measure of success is in the sales rates and the attitudes of the new residents. In November 1994, a survey of new residents showed that 97 per cent. were satisfied with the development and that 85 per cent. were willing to recommend the area to their friends.

There are new business parks in St. Philips, St. Annes and at the Unicorn site. All provide employment and Image Col. 5 reverse what had been regarded as an unstoppable drift away from the city centre to out-of-town locations. The corporation has also spent much time and effort trying to secure redevelopment of the important Quay Point site which is next to Temple Meads station. The site is in a prime location and should be a worthy gateway to the city centre, but it has been derelict, or used for car parking, for many years.

The corporation has now prepared the way for future regeneration by assembling the site in its ownership, by undertaking extensive negotiations with prospective developers and local authorities, and by granting planning permission for a comprehensive mixed-use development. Having taken matters through complex and difficult preparation stages, the corporation will shortly be transferring the site to English Partnerships. It will be for it to build on all the work that the corporation has put in to bring the development package, now at an advanced stage of negotiation, to a conclusion.

Ms Jean Corston (Bristol, East): Does that mean that the Bristol Development Corporation has not yet been able to make any proper decisions about the Quay Point site and that that will be handed to English Partnerships? The Minister will know that the developer who agreed previously to develop the site pulled out of the deal and none of us can find out why.

Sir Paul Beresford: I can confirm that the matter has almost concluded. As we are doing today with the conclusion of this urban development corporation, the site will be handed over for the work to be picked up and finalised through English Partnerships.

As I was saying, it will be for English Partnerships to build on all the hard work that the corporation has put in to bring the development package, which is at the final stage of negotiation, to a conclusion. There is now at last an exciting prospect of securing a deal with a developer who will revitalise this important gateway to the city centre and lift confidence for the whole of the city. That would have not been possible without the Bristol Development Corporation.

Those are all major achievements and, more so, when considered against a hostile local political background and the difficult market conditions that prevailed during the early years of the corporation's life. The achievements, although significant, are not the end of the story. The sound foundation for sustainable regeneration that the corporation has created provides major investment and job opportunities for Bristol well into the new millennium. A current forecast by independent consultants KPMG Peat Marwick is that, as a result of the corporation's activities, more than 450,000 sq m of new floor space will eventually be developed; 1,300 houses will be built, many of which will be for first-time buyers; more than 16,500 jobs will be created and £670 million of private sector investment will be attracted. That will give a final ratio of more than £8 of private money for each £1 of public money invested. Most of these potential outputs already have planning permission and should be delivered in the near future. The people of Bristol can be—and, when they see the results, will be—justifiably proud of the corporation's achievements and can thank the corporation's board and staff for the positive change that has been brought to a previously desolate area.

Col. 6 The corporation has set the framework for the rebirth of central Bristol. That done, it is right to wind up the corporation and that is why the order is necessary. I commend it to the Committee.

For example, the questionnaire on the Bristol spine road was sent out to people in the area. the copy that I am holding has no map: it did not tell people where the road would be. The consultation took place in August. If there are times when public consultation should not take place, they are Christmas, new year and August when everyone is on holiday. However, we became used to that.

Labour Members have always opposed the creation of development corporations on the basis that they are quangos not subject to democratic control or local accountability. People can say what they like about local councils, but at least they are subject to democratic control. In Bristol, a cavalier attitude was displayed to all our local authorities from almost the beginning. It was obvious at the outset that the development corporation's proposals conflicted with the Avon structure plan. We were disappointed to discover that there was no attempt to work with local authorities in the way, in which, for example, Sheffield development corporation worked with its local authorities.

The development corporation also said that it would create an expanding employment base, but it is the only development corporation that did no research into the framework for achieving that and the meaning of it. It claimed that it would create 19,000 jobs in phase 1. In Committee in October 1989, the Minister promised 12,000 jobs. Yet on 25 October 1995, The Observer recorded that the Bristol development corporation had created 2,250 new jobs in four years at a cost to the taxpayer of £56,267 for each job, which is the worst urban development corporation performance in the country. We can conclude that it was the Bristol development corporation that gave development corporations a bad name.

According to the Department of the Environment, the national average cost of creating jobs for urban development corporations is £30,736 per job. The Bristol development corporation is £26,000 per job dearer. Even the cost of £30,736 is three times the cost of creating a job through mainstream regional policy programmes.

The National Audit Office report of 25 August 1993 said that jobs in Bristol were claimed by the development corporation by applying density assumption to available floor space instead of quoting the actual number of jobs. In other words, the corporation said, "This square footage will provide 100 jobs. Therefore, we count 100 jobs." Surely it is better to count the actual number of jobs created. The National Audit Office also referred to the fact that development corporation expenditure on housing, social facilities and training and community programmes averaged 1 per cent. It recorded the Bristol figure as less than 0.5 per cent.

The spine road, which is how my constituents still think of the St. Lawrence causeway, is the worst legacy of all. People in the Committee Room from the development corporation will know that I opposed it from the start for a variety of reasons. It was not wanted by local people and I thought that it would conflict with the Avon structure plan which was going to take traffic from the M32 on the Avon link road away from the city centre. The spine road was going to cost £47 million—£100 for every man, woman and child in Bristol. It could not be used by cyclists, pedestrians or buses, and there had been no Col. 8 consultation with public transport operators. There was to be no air-quality monitoring and no lighting. It remains a private road and there is housing for the elderly within 200 m. When I raised that last point, I was told that the elderly residents would be provided with double glazing. They used to open their windows for fresh air, but they now have to keep them closed to avoid traffic fumes.

As I said, there was a conflict with the Avon transport strategy, but the effect on the A4 at Arno's vale is, as predicted, catastrophic. At the compulsory purchase order inquiry in July 1991, someone on behalf of the development corporation said:

"the current volume of traffic on the A4 to the south of Arno's Vale, i.e. 16,000 vehicles between 0700 and 1900 hours, and the increase in that number in the year 2009 if the Spine Road were to be built (34,000 vehicles), as against a figure of 19,000 vehicles if the Road is not built."

On Monday morning, while travelling to a function in the southern part of my constituency, I had to turn round in the road and go by another route, because I could not get through via Arno's vale.

To add insult to injury, the finance director of the Bristol development corporation told The Observer that the corporation was a special case and should not be compared with other urban development corporations, because it had

"absorbed the £47 million cost of a new dual carriageway across the city centre."

That was phrased as if the corporation had done us a favour, yet none of us wanted that road. Indeed, at the compulsory purchase order inquiry, not a single community group or individual spoke in favour of the road. The legacy to Bristol city council, as the successor body, will be capital maintenance costs of £1 million; we all know who will have to pay for that.

My predecessor, Jonathan Sayeed, a Conservative, said in Committee on 17 January 1989 that local people wanted the urban development corporation. He stated:

I do not know whether that was true then—I do not want to impugn my predecessor's motives or disagree with him—but it is not true now, nor has it been during the time I have been a Member of Parliament. Indeed, it was not true during the general election because local people cited his apparently slavish commitment to everything the development corporation did as a reason for not voting for him.

A cosy relationship certainly existed. I have seen correspondence showing that Mr. Sayeed was given draft letters by the chief executive of the development corporation to use when writing to complaining constituents. I want to put it on record that I write my own letters to my constituents. I do not need anyone else to draft them for me.

On the subject of public-private partnerships, Mr. Sayeed said, also in that Committee, that Bristol city council

"did not wish to co-operate with any profit-making venture to regenerate the area. It did not have the wit or the capability to do so."—[Official Report, Second Standing Committee on Statutory Instruments, &c., 17 January 1989, c. 12.]

That seems to be an insulting manner in which to treat one's local authority. Whatever the political issues, surely it is our duty to support or, at least, to try to get on with our local authorities. Mr. Sayeed's statement was Image Col. 9 incorrect, even then. In 1988, the Bristol initiative had set up the president's group of the Bristol chamber of commerce initiative, of which Bristol city council was a part. The record of public-private partnerships in Bristol is second to none—it is a hallmark of Bristol life.

Without wishing to take up too much of the Committee's time, Mr. Anderson, I should like to read out a list of those partnerships. The rubbish that we have heard about Bristol city council not knowing how to proceed with such partnerships was enough to make the chairman of the Conservative party plan a visit to the city centre—subsequently called off—on the assumption that he could cause trouble for the city council.

The list of partnership ventures is as follows: Bristol cultural development partnership; Bristol tourism forum; Bristol 97; Western development partnership; West of England initiative; Bristol energy and environment trust; Broadmead Board Ltd; Bristol housing partnership; Building a better Bristol; Bristol regeneration partnership; Harbourside accord; and Bristol 2000. Nearly every one of those partnerships has either been set up by or joined by Bristol city council, and many have involved the Bristol chamber of commerce initiative, retailers, other local authorities, the training and enterprise council and the Trades Union Congress. That is a fine record of achievement of which any city would be proud and, indeed, Bristol is proud of it.

The most damning indictment of the development corporation is that, in my time as a Member of Parliament for Bristol, I have not met anyone in the professions, business or commerce who has had a good word to say about the development corporation, even though one would have thought that those sections of the city would have found it easiest to co-operate with it.
