4.30pm

Mr. Graham Allen (Nottingham, North): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Merchant Shipping (Distress Signals and Prevention of Collisions) Regulations 1996 (S.I. 1996, No. 75).

I welcome you to the Chair, Mr. Sims.

This may appear to be a very technical measure, but anyone in doubt about its importance should consult this morning's Finanical Times which sadly, but in a timely way, reported the losses of bulk carriers in the past six or so years. In his piece, Charles Batchelor said that 118 bulk carriers have been lost since 1990. That is a staggering figure. In that brief period 587 people died in 34 incidents involving loss of life. Not all those incidents were the result of collisions or of the failure of distress signals, but the Financial Times article states that strandings and collisions accounted for 83 per cent. of the losses in 1995, the last year for which figures are available. That is an appalling catalogue of statistics. No one could have imagined that the figure would be as high as 118 vessels lost. We might have estimated six or seven, including the Derbyshire and other high-profile cases. The percentage of losses due to strandings and collisions is also staggering. Those figures should underline how important are the matters we are considering, however dry and technical they may appear.

The measure is a consolidation regulation; the current international regulations preventing collisions at sea were agreed in 1972. They have subsequently been amended four times and the amendments implemented each time by an order in the House. The orders to be revoked are listed in regulation 1(8) and the measure consolidates all the previous amendments to the international regulations. It also enables future amendments to international regulations agreed by the International Maritime Organisation to be made by a merchant shipping notice rather than by order, a form of deregulation. That is the dry, technical side of the measure.

The sad fact remains that there are high casualties as a result of strandings and collisions in our major sea lanes. I repeat the shocking statistic of 118 bulk carriers alone lost since 1990; and 83 per cent. of those losses last year were due to strandings and collisions.

I have several questions for the Minister; if he cannot answer them now I am sure that he will, as usual, write to us with his reply.

Col. 4 Regulation No. 5 allows the Secretary of State to exempt any ship or description of ships from all or any of the regulation's provisions. Will the Minister explain to the Committee when those wide-ranging extension powers may be used, because they can include

"the number, position, range or arc of visibility of lights or shapes, as well as … the disposition and characteristics of sound-signalling appliances if he"—

the Secretary of State—

"is satisfied that compliance with such provision is either impractical or unreasonable in the case of that ship or description of ships … he may specify … subject to giving reasonable notice"

an exemption from regulation No. 5. Those are wide-ranging powers. No doubt the Minister wishes to put on record the circumstances in which he might use them.

It is essential that ship safety requirements be legislated for and implemented. Unfortunately the Government's deregulatory policy towards shipping has encouraged increased flagging out of British ships, and substandard shipping in United Kingdom waters, which is now causing serious concern. This point was raised by my hon. Friends recently in connection with the Sea Empress. Again I pay tribute to my hon. Friend the Member for Pembroke (Mr. Ainger) for his incredibly expert and conscientious work as Pembroke's Member of Parliament throughout that disaster.

I do not suggest that if the Sea Empress had been British flagged, British officered and staffed by British seafarers the accident would not have happened. What I do say is that there is a likelihood of accidents occurring because of vessels being flagged out, registered elsewhere and officered by people, including seafarers, from other countries. It has been proven statistically that such ships are less safe than British-flagged ships with British crews and officers. There is a longer-term cause for concern about the decline of the British merchant fleet which my hon. Friends will press until we halt, and reverse, the decline. That concern is shared throughout the industry—by the National Union of Marine, Aviation and Shipping Transport Officers, the officers union, by the National Union of Rail, Maritime and Transport Workers, the seafarers union, and by the Chamber of Shipping, which represents shipping employers. The Government should address this matter, not least as a root cause of inadequate compliance with regulations covering distress signals and the prevention of collisions.

Another matter that is pertinent in the context of the Sea Empress accident that turned into a disaster at Milford Haven is that of the resources given to agencies to ensure compliance with the regulations. I have in mind especially the Coastguard Agency and the Marine Safety Agency, whose expertise is being sliced away year by year. The Government may call it efficiency savings or redeployment, but such moves cannot but undermine in the longer term the sea police force of the coastguards and the safety agency which is responsible for safety in our waters and elsewhere. The Minister may wish to underline the fact that it is most Image Col. 5 important that further reductions in the number and expertise, of coastguards especially, be ended, and that coastguards and the MSA be kept up to strength.

Under regulation No. 6 the maximum penalty for non-compliance is £50,000. Of course, in practice, smaller fines are invariably imposed but these are not a sufficient deterrent for unscrupulous operators. Unfortunately, it is often cheaper for such people to bypass the regulations and risk a small fine than to implement them.

The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development has published this year an interesting study entitled "Competitive advantages obtained by some shipowners as a result of non-observance of applicable international rules and standards", which highlights the problem in some detail. I am sure that the Minister is familiar with it, and I ask him to let the Committee know how he intends to overcome the problem of penalties being regarded merely as a cost of business.

The OECD says:

"shipping is still largely a free market which allows considerable scope for shipowners to avoid compliance with internationally agreed rules and regulations as regards safety and the protection of the marine environment".

Does the Minister agree that he should take steps to ensure that the penalties are sufficient to deter people from regarding them merely as a cost of business and to make them take seriously the problems that they create?

It is also a matter of concern that master of ships that contravene the regulations are targeted in regulation 6(1) and can be punished by imprisonment, when it is often the ship operators who let standards fall in order to cut costs. I urge the Minister to keep ship safety standards high by ensuring that ship operators are encouraged not to cut corners but to comply with distress signals and prevention of collision regulations so as to avoid heavy fines.

I have raised four serious points for the Minister's consideration. I return from the rather dry wording of the regulations to say that, as we have seen in the case of the Sea Empress, real people are involved. There is a danger of loss of life and of disasters such as the terrible oil pollution that we have seen scarring the Welsh coastline, perhaps for many months to come, and destroying the marine environment, perhaps for many years to come.

I hope that the Minister will respond positively to the points that I and my hon. Friends will put to him.

The Paliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Transport (Mr. Steve Norris): I apologise in advance to my hon. Friends, who have been extraordinarily patient, for the length of my response to the various matters raised. As you pointed out, Mr. Sims, we are discussing Merchant Shipping (Distress Signals and Prevention of Collisions) Regulations 1996, about which we have not heard a great deal. We have heard much about the Sea Empress, and the hon. Member for Nottingham, North predictably managed to mention the Herald of Free Enterprise and the Estonia. If I may use a maritime metaphor, it is necesary for me to cover the waterfront when responding to debates of this kind, but I shall deal with matters as expeditiously as possible.

First, it is important that collision regulations or COLREGS, as I believe they are known—my hon. Friends will be able to quote that at dinner and cocktail parties—be kept up to date. They were developed in 1863 by the then Board of Trade, working with the French Government. We have to keep pace with changes in technology, such as the use of radar and the new types of craft. As the hon. Member for Nottingham, North said, the last significant revision of COLREGS was in 1972. The COLREGS are the basic rules of the road for mariners. They govern the behaviour of all vessels, regardless of their type and size. They establish rules about which vessels have the right of way, who should take action to avoid a collision, and what lights, sound and distress signals should be used.

The Merchant Shipping (Distress Signals and Prevention of Collisions) Regulations 1996 fulfil our international obligations. They bring into effect in United Kingdom law the latest amendments to the COLREGS agreed by the International Maritime Organisation in November 1993. They allow a pragmatic application of the mast light rules for smaller craft, improve the visibility of all-round lights, recognise the special construction characteristics of the new generation of high-speed craft, and specify special signals for small fishing vessels and trawlers.

As hon. Members know, the Government are keen to consolidate existing legislation wherever possible. I can tell the Hon. Member for Swansea, East that in line with that approach, we have used the 1996 statutory instrument to revoke no fewer than seven sets of earlier regulations dealing with minor COLREGS amendments since 1972. The detailed rules hae been removed from the regulations, and as the hon. Gentleman said, we have used the merchant shipping notice to update them.

It may reassure hon. Members to learn that that approach, which has been used in other merchant shipping legislation for many years, will simplify the arrangements for incorporating future amendments to Col. 12 the COLREGS. The hon. Member for Nottingham North was right to say that the House is clogged up with regulations, and those future amendments which will be brought into effect by merchant shipping notice will reflect exactly decisions reached by the International Maritime Organisation. They will also be discussed fully with the shipping industry and others before their publication.

When we are dealing with matters which are for the British Parliament to determine—that is, those which go beyond the decisions of the IMO—the House clearly must, and will, be involved in the process.

The merchant shipping notice approach has other advantages. It allows for the physical division of detailed technical requirements into areas of specialisation and makes it possible for the industry to direct and manage those technical areas more efficiently and more conveniently. I hope that that is the answer that the hon. Gentleman was seeking.

Mr. Donald Anderson: Of course I accept what the Minister said about simplification, de-clogging and the division between the notices and matters that may come before the House, but there may be matters—I mentioned penalties, for example—about which Members of Parliament feel legitimate concern. Mr. Norris: I can give the hon. Gentleman the assurance that he seeks. The penalties are contained in the Act. The regulations contain the details, with which, as the hon. Gentleman quite rightly said, we should not clog the House. Reference has already been made to the instant expertise that hon. Members seem to acquire on almost every subject under the sun. They might usefully use that expertise in other areas, rather than on such extremely technical matters.

We are determined to deal with the enforcement of the collision regulations. We follow up alleged contraventions and foreign ships are formally reported to their flag states and their owners. For United Kingdom vessels, the Marine Safety Agency takes legal advice about the merits of prosecution and instigates proceedings when there is a good chance of a successful prosecution. In a recent case, for example—the hon. Member for Pembroke (Mr. Ainger) will be interested to know—a master was fined £1,000 for failing to keep a proper look-out.

I want to deal with the question of fine levels. Hon. Members might find it useful to look at the regulations themselves. They will see that in regulation 6(1) offences can be treated either on indictment or on summary conviction. On indictment, for the more serious offence, the punishment can be a prison term "not exceeding two years", as well as a fine. That answers the point raised by the hon. Member for Nottingham, North. On summary conviction there is the £50,000 fine and

"in any other case.… a fine not exceeding the statutory maximum."

Image Col. 13 In almost every matter in which courts are involved, a maximum fine is set by Parliament and the court decides within that limit. I cannot in all honesty tell the hon. Member for Nottingham, North what the average fine is or the number of fines that have been exacted, but I can tell him that £50,000—which the hon. Member for Pendle (Mr. Prentice) referred to as "a cost of business"—represents an extremely severe penalty for the activity that is being contemplated. That would of course be truer at the maximum than at any other figure. The hon. Member for Nottingham, North is right about that, but he knows that such a situation is not in any sense unique to the regulations.

Where the density of traffic is greatest, we take a proactive monitoring role. In 1993, we invested £4 million to update radar surveillance equipment monitoring traffic in the busy Dover strait where, as the hon. Member for Pendle mentioned, the traffic separation scheme is in operation. We recognise the need to speed up the way in which the Coastguard and Marine Safety Agencies deal with reported contraventions of the rules. Both agencies are actively considering how best to take the matter forward in the context of their 1996–97 business plans. We should also remember that CNIS works extremely well. Contraventions are spotted, reports are made and the rule breakers are dealt with.
