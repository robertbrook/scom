Social Security (Adjudication) and Child Support Amendment Regulations 1996

10.30 am

Mr. Keith Bradley
(Manchester, Withington)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Social Security (Adjudication) and Child Support Amendment Regulations 1996 (S.I. 1996, No. 182).

These regulations came into force on 28 February. They severely restrict the circumstances in which social security claimants can appeal to tribunals out of time. They also make changes to the rules for recording and preserving decisions. Those changes are not so controversial, so I shall not delay the Committee on that aspect of the regulations.

We must first consider carefully the previous position. Prior to 28 February this year, tribunals had a general discretion to grant a claimant permission to bring an appeal if he or she had not done so within three months. A claimant had to show that there were special reasons why he or she should be allowed to pursue an appeal. Even so, that was not easy and considerable hurdles had to be overcome. The claimant had to explain why he or she had failed to appeal within the time limit. Moreover, if leave was refused, it was extremely difficult to challenge that refusal through judicial review.

Nevertheless, the system had the advantage that the chair of the tribunal had the flexibility to deal with each case individually on its merits, untrammelled by statutory prescription. These new regulations have removed that flexibility.

What are the new rules that have been introduced by these regulations? It will now be virtually impossible for claimants to obtain leave to appeal to tribunals out of time. Indeed, so high is the hurdle that the Government have erected that it might have been more honest for them to have legislated so that no appeals could be allowed at all. An application for an extension of time can now be granted only if, under the new paragraphs (3A) to (3D) proposed in regulation 2(2)(b),

"it is in the interests of justice".

It is in the interests of justice if

"special reasons exist, which are wholly exceptional"

and those special reasons must

"manifestly constitute a reasonable excuse of compelling weight for the applicant's failure to make an appeal within the time specified."

The chair of the tribunal must ignore the fact that

"the applicant or anyone acting for him or advising him was unaware of or misunderstood"

4 the relevant law, including that relating to appeal time limits. That would presumably include erroneous advice given by the Benefits Agency.

If the applicant sought advice from Benefits Agency staff and was advised that he or she was not entitled to a component of a benefit, not surprisingly that claimant would accept that advice and a result would not immediately bring an appeal against that adverse decision. If, more than three months later, that person discovers that the Benefits Agency was, or might have been, wrong, he would be unable to cite the erroneous advice as a reason for bringing a late appeal against the decision. Will the Minister confirm that my understanding of the regulation is correct and that, in such circumstances, an appeal could not be brought?

Mr. Anthony Steen
(South Hams)
: The hon. Gentleman makes an important point. I have an on-going case in which a constituent was placed in just such circumstances before the regulation came into force. The Benefits Agency managed to avoid its responsibility, as I see it, by claiming that there was not continuous good cause. It argued that the three-week delay could not be justified even though the applicant's father had died during that period. That was before the regulations came into force. I agree with the hon. Gentlamen that the Minister needs to clarify whether the new regulations will make the problem even worse.

Mr. Bradley
: I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman. That is exactly the point. Under the previous rules—before 28 February—there was at least the opportunity for an applicant to make a request to the tribunal. The chair of the tribunal could use his discretion to determine whether there were grounds for a sympathetic consideration, according to the merits of the individual case. For example, in the case mentioned by the hon. Gentleman, the death of an applicant's relative would have been sympathetically considered by the chair of the tribunal. My understanding is that the discretion has been removed under the new arrangements so the death of a relative would be an insufficient ground for delay or even to argue with the tribunal. The case would not even reach that stage because of the constraints imposed by the regulations. The hon. Gentleman's example reinforce my point, for which I am grateful. We shall await the Minister's response.

A second restraint is found in the new paragraph (3D) proposed in regulation 2(2)(b), which states that no account can be taken of a case in which

"a Commissioner or a court has taken a different view of the law from that previously understood and applied."

In other words, the chair of the tribunal has to ignore the fact that an adjudication officer's decision has been shown to be wrong in law. Again, that ground, as I understand it, would not allow for an appeal or give the tribunal the discretion to hear an appeal. Can the Minister confirm that that is the case?

If I am correct in both of my interpretations, Committee members will realise that circumstances would have to be extreme indeed before a claimant had any chance of getting leave to appeal out of time. Serious illness or disability, including mental JPEG 5 disability, might not be sufficient grounds for an out-of-time appeal. Can the Minister confirm that such circumstances no longer apply for late appeals? I refer him to the report by the Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments and the memorandum of the Department of Social Security, which raised questions about the original drafting of regulations or illness. As a result, the regulations have been redrafted but they do not make it clear whether such circumstances of illness can now apply as a ground for a late appeal.

In addition, there is now a stop-gap for the Government of six years. No appeal may be brought more than six years after the decision has been made. I shall return to that issue a little later when I discuss the Government's justification of why the changes are required.

Let us briefly compare the present arrangements with other areas of the law of appeal. The changes appear to place social security claimants who wish to appeal in a far worse position than other litigants. In other jurisdications, the court or tribunal concerned invariably has a general discretion about whether to extend the time allowed. Since February, the social security law has been changed to disallow that discretion.

In the Court of Appeal, for example, the White Book's 1995 Commentary to the rules of the Supreme Court states that it

"is entirely in the discretion of the Court to grant or refuse an extension of time."

Surprisingly, on Radio 4's "Moneybox" programme on 2 March, the Minister said that the new regime simply brought claimants into line with other litigants: indeed, even with the rules, claimants were in a better position than litigants wishing to appeal to the Court of Appeal. That is nonsense. The Court of Appeal has the unfettered discretion to allow late appeals. The regulations deny that opportunity to claimants through social security legislation.

The Government justify the new rules by pointing out that late appeals have been granted against some very old decisions, which causes disproportionate administrative burdens on the social security agencies. That was the line used during the required consultation with the Council on Tribunals, but it is the old trick of using a perceived problem to justify a solution that is more far reaching than necessary.

As I said earlier, the problem of very stale appeals has been dealt with by the introduction of the six-year stop-gap. The late appeals that the Government claim cause so much of the administrative burden and complexity will not be affected, as most appeals—as the Minister knows—apply to recent claims and not to cases that are more than six years old.

On the same "Moneybox" programme, the Minister tried to draw an analogy with claims to industrial tribunals. He said—correctly—that an applicant who misses the three-month time limit for bringing a complaint against unfair dismissal, for example, has an uphill task getting the complaint accepted, but that analogy is very poor because industrial tribunals consider original claims, not requests to consider late appeals.

6 A letter dated 22 September from the Council on Tribunals stated that the council thought that it would be better if the question of discretion were dealt with by the president of the independent tribunal service—in other words, that discretion should not be circumscribed by regulations. That advice appears to have been ignored by the Minister, and I should be grateful for an explanation.

On a slightly more positive note, tribunal chairmen now have to give reasons for allowing or rejecting an application for a late appeal. However, that is likely to be of little practical use to claimants, as it will be virtually impossible to show that a decision to refuse an application is unreasonable in the judicial review sense of "unreasonableness". It will also not be capable of challenge. Although the tribunal chairman has had to make such a decision, one cannot then appeal against it. As the terms on which such an appeal have been severely limited, the measure is a double whammy on the applicant's individual rights.

The Government must be clear about where exactly they are leading us. In a recent speech on the proposed changes to social security administration made to senior managers in the Department of Social Security on 28 February, the Secretary of State for Social Security said:

"Our existing adjudication system is largely hidden from the claimant's view and is so bewilderingly complex that it is hard to see how it can generate confidence. Fundamental to the Change Programme will be a review of the legal basis of decision making and appeals. The outcome of the review must be that claimants get what they are legally entitled to, and that they have the right to challenge a decision."

It is ironic that the regulations seem to fly in the face of that avowed intention to restore confidence in the system and to ensure that claimants have a right to benefit and a right to appeal against decisions.

We need to know whether, as a result of the regulations, the Government have any plans further to limit the right of appeal or even to do away with the appeals system altogether and to move further down the road of a general review of social security decisions—as is now the case with the social fund, for example—rather than having an unfettered right of appeal against decisions through the tribunal service.

Those are serious matters. We must be clear that all our constituents have—and are seen to have—natural justice. They must have the right to appeal against decisions. The Government's measures will severely restrict both that right and the grounds on which people can try to exercise it. I hope that the Minister will be able to allay our fears and that he will answer my specific questions on individuals' rights of appeal.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. Roger Evans)
: I suppose that we should have realised that the kind words of my hon. Friend the Member for South Hams (Mr. Steen) would be followed by a whammy, if not a double whammy.

The Government are committed to improving the performance and service of the independent tribunal service. These regulations are designed to improve that service for the benefit of the public. The point made by the hon. Member for Withington about giving reasons is not controversial, but we believe that it will speed up a decision about an appeal by about two weeks. As benefit cannot be received pending an appeal decision, that is a small but important step towards providing a better and speedier service.

Any system of regulation and appeal to tribunals requires an up-to-date and effective scheme of legislation as to how those rights may be exercised. My hon. Friend the Member for South Hams is therefore mistaken: this is not about deregulation but about improving the service to the public.

Mr. Steen
: All Ministers argue that the orders or regulations that they bring to Committee are not deregulatory but that they are fine tuning or improvements, that they give better value for money or are are good legislative practice. I am like an Agatha Christie, trying to find orders that are deregulatory.

Mr. Evans
: I shall leave my hon. Friend to that hunt, which may be more difficult than he thought. Deregulation is a separate matter: surely it is beyond argument that a proper legislative scheme and framework are necessary for appeals tribunals. Parliament must participate in that decision-making process, of which this order is a part.

The hon. Member for Withington was worried that the arrangements meant that social security appeals cases were treated less favourably than other cases. The Government reject that claim, which must be put in context. A person who is sacked has three months to bring a complaint to an industrial tribunal, and reasons must be given for lateness. A person who is injured—for example, after exposure to asbestosis—must bring a claim to the Court of Appeal within four weeks or show why it should be later. However, the difference between social security appeals cases and claims for dismissal and for personal injury is that a JPEG 11 person making a social security appeal can always make a fresh claim. For dismissal or injury, there is one chance: people who lose in the first instance have one chance, within tight limits, to appeal.

In social security, the issue goes further than that. If new evidence can be produced, it is possible to return to the adjudicating officer and to ask for a review. The argument about late appeals is not about preventing people from getting benefit from now on or about getting benefit backdated from today for 12 months, which can be won on a review. The argument is about whether cases older than 12 months can be reopened. The problem has arisen because several welfare rights organisations have conducted a campaign in which they tried to bring appeals out of time. That is having a disruptive effect on the good administration of the appeals system. The regulations aim to bring justice and order in that respect. When one considers them as a whole, the arrangements are not unreasonable compared to other jurisdictions.

Mr. Evans
: That does not follow at all. If it can be shown that the Benefits Agency has given seriously wrong advice and has made an official error, there is a compensation scheme.

In relation to the regulations, the figures are quite damning: in 1995, 10,000 late applications were made. More than half—such is the scale of the problem—were admitted. The really serious indication that the system was not working was that 3,000 of the applications were apparently granted on the basis that chairmen considered there to be special reasons. However, as far as we can trace from the papers, no reason was given—the applications were simply rubber-stamped through the system.

Difficulties arise with appeals such as, "I appeal against my first award of supplementary benefit or national assistance". Supplementary benefit ceased to exist 10 or more years ago and national assistance ceased in 1966. In 1965, more than 700 applications were more than six years old. The effects of that on the system were very serious. Older appeals clog up the process because it inevitably take tribunals much more than to resolve them as they have to dig through previous history. The Benefits Agency—rightly and reasonably because of the expense involved—does not keep paperwork going back to the 1970s and 1960s. Tribunals have to reconstruct what happened many years ago without the relevant information which results in an unfair process and affects the good order and administration of the system.

My point is simple. As a result of the large number of late applications and the way in which they were being brought, the decision-making process was being slowed down and other members of the public, who were not receiving benefit pending their appeal decision, were being held up. That is why we have decided that it is right and proper to tighten up the process to prevent this ludicrous situation from occurring.

Let me make it clear to the hon. Member for Withington. Under the six-year final rule, if he owed me money or I owed him money, we would have six years in which to bring an action. There must be an ultimate time limit. If people do not bring their appeals in time, they should be subject to some test to avoid every late application merely being rubber-stamped. It is not entirely clear to me why chairmen admitted more than 3,000 late appeals without any apparent reason having been given.

The proper way to deal with that problem is for Parliament to introduce legislation, although I appreciate the first that, as my hon. Friend the Member for South Hams said, this is not the most opportune forum for criticising this measure in detail. We come to Parliament to be challenged on an instrument that tightens up this process. I accept that this Instrument tightens up the process—it is designed to do so and it should do so, but it does so in a way that is broadly fair.
