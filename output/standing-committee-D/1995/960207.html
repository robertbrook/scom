Merchant Shipping (Port State Control) Regulations 1995

4.33 pm

Mr. Graham Allen
(Nottingham, North)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Merchant Shipping (Port State Control) Regulations 1995 (S.I. 1995, No. 3128).

This important statutory instrument is part of a series that is full of fine sentiment that is not backed by adequate Government action. The background to the measure is that 11,000 foreign-registered ships come to our shore and ports each year. About 30 per cent. of those ships are inspected, and 70 per cent. of them are found to have defects. Many then receive exemption certificates and are allowed to sail away unrepaired.

We should congratulate the European Union for pushing this issue and for getting the Government to act by presenting the regulations. Opposition Members welcome implementation of part 1 of the directive on port state control regulations and the additional measures in parts 2 and 3 of the directive to improve inspections, give rights of appeal and provide compensation when ships are detained.

We all agree that improving shipping standards is vital and that safety is a priority. We should also be proud of the United Kingdom's record on port state control, and we should record our thanks to the marine safety inspectors for their excellent record.

It is unfortunate that the Government's current policy of cost cutting, allied to deregulation, puts those important objectives in jeopardy. That policy risks the safety of passengers and the health of our environment. Those principles are also important to the shipping industry, which is right to feel let down by a Government whose policy has led to a staggering decline in the British fleet.

My noble Friend Lord Stanley Clinton-Davis often quotes the fact that, when he left office in 1979, a fleet of 1,278 vessels of more than 500 gross registered tonnes was under the United Kingdom flag. Now, only 270 vessels remain. That is the extent of the decline in the United Kingdom merchant fleet in recent years.

I have stressed before that it is not the number of regulations that the Government enact that makes a difference to shipping standards, but the effective implementation of them. The regulation of ship safety, marine pollution and ship-board living and working conditions depend on high quality and rigorous safety inspections of ships by full-time public servants who are independent of commercial pressures. By imposing cuts on the Department of Tranport agencies 4 that are responsible for maritime safety, the Government are reducing both the ability to clamp down on unsafe or polluting foreign ships coming to or going from the United Kingdom, and the ability to ensure that our ships are up to scratch. I am worried that our previously good record is at risk and I urge the Minister to review shipping policy to ensure that there is no room for declining standards.

In the United Kingdom, port state control is hindered by inadequate resource allocation to the Marine Safety Agency which can currently afford to inspect only about 30 per cent. of ships. Regulation 5(1) of the statutory instrument sets a minimum inspection level of only 25 per cent. That, potentially, could allow the MSA inspection rate to drop even below its current level. I understand that the MSA has assured the National Union of Marine, Aviation and Shipping Transport Officers that the number of port state control inspections will not be reduced by the introduction of the statutory instrument. I hope that the Minister will reaffirm the commitment.

It is not enough merely to say that there will be the same percentage of inspections—they must be as rigorous as current inspections. The Minister may be able to say that he foresees an increase in the number of inspections, because we should never be complacent and should always seek to extend port state control wherever possible.

The poor safety standards of some flag of convenience ships, and foreign-flagged vesels with even worse records, are underlined regularly by the MSA in is detention lists. There are often major deficiencies such as non-operational firefighting equipment and defective life boats. We must ensure that the essential statistics on such deficiencies continue to be published regularly and that sufficient resources are allocated to solve the problems.

However, regulation 16(1) requres less frequent and less comprehensive detention lists than those currently published. I seek a public assurance from the Minister that the United Kingdom will continue good practice and, indeed, that there will be better practice than is required by the bare bones of the statutory instrument. He also has the chance, should he wish to take it, of commenting on the adequacy of the penalties for offences. That matter is a cause of deep concern in the industry.

International conventions are ineffective without independent full-time staff to conduct inspections and enforce regulations. Regulations 14 and 4 require professional expertise and will depend on sufficient numbers of professional staff for their full implementation. In the light of cuts in the MSA, I am concerned about the United Kingdom's ability to meet the requirements of the directive and to maintain and improve the United Kingdom's superior practices.

I have three concerns about the staffing for inspections of marine safety and anti-pollution enforcement in respect of the statutory instrument. First, I am concerned about the numbers in the Marine JPEG 5 Safety Agency and the Coastguard agency, which have been cut. Mr. Robin Bradley, the head of the MSA, supplied an answer to my recent parliamentary question about surveyors in the MSA. He revealed that 22 surveyors left in 1995 and only nine were replaced with new recruits. There is attrition at a time when we should be seeking to hold the numbers or, where resources allow, increase them.

Similarly, the coastguard service, which so often provides safety and environmental back-up on marine matters, is now under review. I understand that 10 to 15 station officers around the country are to go and that 380 auxiliary coastguards are to go, and be replaced by less well-trained and less skilled watch assistants. That is the wrong direction in which to go if we are honest about the commitments in the fine words before us about improving safety, inspection and control of our environment.

My second concern about the statutory instrument relates to levels of training and skills. An alarming example of the lack of training was reported to me recently, concerning Coastguard agency staff who man radio distress monitors. Under an international obligation, they should be trained to operate such distress monitors but, in some cases, they have not received that training. I hope that the Minister will be able to explain that now or at some time in the future.

How are we to uphold the new regulations that we are debating if agency staff are not properly trained? A related and more pervasive example has also been reported to me on several occasions. It is that ship inspectors benefit from seafaring experience. The view of the Chamber of Shipping and the officers' and seafarers' unions is that by running down our fleet, the Government are reducing the number and standard of available inspectors. By the continuation of flagging out and eroding of the fleet, we are bringing about consequences for further down the line. The United Kingdom has always been highly regarded for its seafaring standards, but its record is diminishing because of the need for ship owners to flag out ships, not least because of the lack of support for the industry from the Government.

That creates a continuing loss of skills. I am apprehensive about the fact that staffing cuts, inadequate training and ever-decreasing numbers of engineers and surveyors with necessary seafaring experience will be to the detriment of safety at sea. In the longer term, that will also affect our ability to staff the shipping and insurance business and even the International Maritime Organisation, which relies on Government-trained surveyors to represent the United Kingdom.

I understand that the Government are undertaking a study—perhaps it has been completed—on the levels, and lack of, surveyors. The view of the officers' union NUMAST is that we require 1,200 new trainees each year to feed the maritime industry in all its diversity and, above all, the MSA. I sprung that on the Minister, so if he cannot reply immediately, perhaps he will write and tell me if it is possible to publish the survey urgently.

6 My third and final point is the United Kingdom's ability to meet our treaty obligations in these regulations, which result from the delegation of essential surveys and inspections to the private sector. Some ship surveyors on short contracts with the MSA are employed, before and after, by shipping companies, which could lead to a conflict of interest. The independence of our marine safety inspection services must be maintained, not sacrificed to cost-cutting. Classification societies do a good job, but they can never be as independent as the MSA.

We used parliamentary procedure to object to this order so as to raise vital marine issues. The objectives of high safety standards, reduced pollution at sea, better shipboard conditions and regional co-operation to achieve those aims, suffer from a marked lack of parliamentary time to discuss them. Shipping has become a Cinderella in the transport industry, and I hope that by discussing the matter with the Minister present, we have done something to put that right.

The klondiker Aktinial has been detained since October 1994 and, although the Government promised to produce a White Paper on the safety and health risks associated with such vessels more than a year ago, they have not done so. The lack of parliamentary consideration of such matters means that the Department becomes slacker than it might otherwise be. Will the Minister say when the White Paper will be published?

In addition to finding sufficient time to oversee the effective implementation of regional shipping regulations, we should try to encourage the extension of port state control so that there is a truly global network. That will be one of the incoming Labour Government's priorities, and I urge this Government, in their international talks, to encourage Governments of non-European Union signatories to the Paris memorandum of understanding on port state control to meet the conditions of the European Union directive on port control implemented by the regulations.

We must emphasise the fact that flag state control, as opposed to port state control, is extremely important, too. In discussions with my noble Friend Lord Clinton-Davis, I discovered that in 1978 he proposed an international flag state exchange so that various surveyors from different countries could be trained, and those from less-developed countries could have the benefit of the experience of western European and north American surveyors. That would be the embryo of a global system of marine safety inspectors, so that the flag state could carry out much of the work that is loaded on to the port state. That may bear further examination by the Minister.

Our strategy for shipping for the year 2000 focuses on improving shipping standards in the United Kingdom and, through international collaboration and agreement, improving shipping standards worldwide. Our policy aims to promote maritime safety, to combat marine pollution, to protect the environment, to improve working conditions and to prevent accidents at sea.

The regulations are a valuable step forward, but they must be accompanied by adequate enforcement. JPEG 7 I hope that, because the issue has been raised in Committee, the industry will see that the House takes the issue seriously. I hope that the Minister will be able to respond to our points.

4.49 pm

Mr. James Wallace
(Orkney and Shetland)
: I want to add a few words to what the hon. Member for Nottingham, North said, much of which I agree with. I agree especially with his point about the importance of having sufficient people coming through with experience in the merchant marines to take onshore posts such as surveyors and inspectors. There is growing concern that, before long, there will be a shortage of people with sufficient experience to carry out onshore jobs. I know from past correspondence with the Department that some steps have been taken to address the issue, and if the Minister can update us on the position, that would be welcome.

Lord Donaldson says in chapter 6 of his report:

"In an ideal world Flag States, whose flags are worn by the world's shipping, would lay down, and enforce upon their own shipowners, standards of design, maintenance and operation which would ensure a very high standard of safety at sea."

Lord Donaldson goes on:

"Regrettably it is beyond argument that not all Flag States live up to their responsibilities."

That is why we have before us regulations on port state control, which still has a role to play, even in an ideal world. However, it assumes even greater importance in dealing with ships that are all too frequently below standard.

I welcome the fact that the regulations have a European Union dimension with regard to tackling the problems of shipping safety. The European Union member states have co-operated constructively in addressing these issues. If we are to tackle shipping safety, the more countries that are involved the better. As for ensuring fairness of competition, it would be to the detriment of British ports if we operated a tight regime while the regime across the channel—in Rotterdam, for example—was lax. Therefore, the European approach is very important.

I have two specific points for the Minister. One, raised by the hon. Member for Nottingham, North, concerns the promised consultation document on klondikers. It. Raises the questions of the link to safety and of insurance for shipping in general, not just the klondikers, although they were instrumental in bringing the issue to the fore. It would be interesting to know how much the Government are still laying out for the sums not yet recovered from the incidents in Lerwirk harbour when klondikers ran aground. Some steps have been taken with regard to klondikers, but the important issue of linking insurance and safety standards to licences has yet to be adequately addressed. I would welcome an indication of when the consultation document might be forthcoming.

My second point is technical, but important. Paragraph 9(2)(b) of the regulations states: 8

"A detention notice may:

  1. include a direction that a ship shall remain in a particular place, or shall move to a particular anchorage or berth; and
  2. specify circumstances when the master of the ship may move his ship from a specified place for reasons of safety or prevention of pollution."

I know that it has been flagged up with me that there must be some flexibility. In some areas, the weather can rise up suddenly when action has to be taken. Would a harbour master in Lerwick, for example, have to contact a Marine Safety Agency official or surveyor in Aberdeen, or even further afield, in the small hours to see what could be done about moving a ship which was being detained? Flexibility is important, although the sanction element of detention must be maintained. If the Minister cannot give me an answer today on how, in the interests of safety, flexibility will be observed, perhaps he will write to me.

On the whole, the regulations are welcome. A number of steps have been taken to improve shipping safety, not least since the Braer went aground in my constituency and Lord Donaldson's subsequent report. For example, the monthly announcements of ships that have been detained are welcome. It would be interesting to know whether any pattern has emerged to show that people are waking up to what is happening.

The statutory instrument is another important step forward. But as the hon. Member for Nottingham, North said, making laws is one thing—enforcing them is another. We hope that the enforcement and the resources that it requires will match the step forward taken in putting the regulations before Parliament today.

Mr. Nick Ainger
(Pembroke)
: I, too, shall be brief. I expect that all members of the Committee agree with Lord Donaldson's view of the need to tighten up port state control and with his statement, at paragraph 11.73 of the inquiry report, of the intention

"that our recommendations should force bad operators out of business or force them to improve."

I am worried that bad operators will still be allowed to slip through the net.

Regulation 15 includes a reference to the duties of pilots of vessels entering ports and mooring. The provision states that they shall report any deficiencies that they find in the vessel. What is surprising is that no duty is placed on operators—not of the vessel, but of the berth where the vessel is discharging or loading. A vessel might be at such a berth for many days. It would certainly be there many hours. Yet the order includes no requirement for operators to report deficiencies that they discover while the ship is in use there. That is a clear omission, given that operators of shoreside facilities have a vested interest in ensuring that their operations are as quick and efficient as possible, and in seeing the back of a bad vessel—one with significant deficiencies. I am surprised that the regulations do not deal with that matter.

Another issue comes to mind in relation to the possibility of vessels being allowed to slip through the control net—bearing in mind the problems highlighted by my hon. Friend the Member for Nottingham, North regarding the number of inspectors. There is a need for a system that would show up a vessel as possibly rogue. One of Lord Donaldson's recommendations, made at paragraph 11.49, was that

"if more than one of several ships in the same ownership or management is found to be substandard on inspection in Paris MOU ports, a notice requirement should be imposed on all the ships in the same ownership or management."

That is a sensible, logical recommendation. There is a good chance that a manager of a fleet of vessels who is running one rogue ship is likely to be running many. I am surprised that such issues are not covered by the regulations. An easy way could be provided for MSA inspectors to identify and draw attention to vessels that are likely to cause pollution or other problems.

I expect that most hon. Members would agree with my final observations, which relate to the issues covered by part III of the regulations, entitled:

"Inspection of Familiarity of Crew with Operational Procedures".

That is a vital subject. I do not know whether any hon. Members have read the report on the disaster of the Scandinavia Star, which was a ro-ro ferry operating between Denmark and Sweden. The incident involved horrendous loss of life. Not as many lives were lost as on the Estonia, but the victims were burnt to death, or overcome by fumes. The report showed that one of the main causes of loss of life on that scale was the fact that the crew was multi-lingual and did not know the basic safety procedures. I am concerned that the work load placed on the MSA inspectors will not leave JPEG 11 them time to check on those vital issues. When there is an emergency on a vessel, having to translate in up to seven languages, as was the case on the Scandinavian Star, makes matters worse.

I spent more than 14 years working on vessels which travelled abroad from Milford Haven. It was normal for at least three languages to be spoken on board; in a typical vessel, the captain and chief engineer may have been Norwegian, the first officer may have been Indian and the crew may have been Philippine. There was frequently no common language and issues had to be explained at great length, often using sign language. In an emergency, that can lead to disaster.

My hon. Friend the Member for Nottingham, North has emphasised the need for sufficient staff to carry out the vital inspections—not just the physical inspections of the condition of the vessel, but checks on the ability of the crew to communicate with each other. That is an important issue which the Minister should address.

Mr. Eddie Loyden

12 I welcome the regulations as a step forward. I hope that the Government will Co-operate fully in implementing them. That is also true of ro-ro ferries, whose turnaround time is the speediest that a vessel can manage, but that is achieved by sacrificing safety.

Our maritime industry should work to its maximum efficiency, but that should not be achieved at the expense of the safety and well-being of the men and women who crew ships. That tradition goes back for centuries. The Government should resolve such problems. Other members of the Committee have said that some regulations are meaningless because of a lack of international agreement. Flagging out of ships and registration of vessels has made the control of standards aboard ships very difficult to achieve in this country and throughout the world. When a flag is flying on a vessel, access should be provided for inspection, and vessels should conform to the rules that apply internationally. I hope that the Government will take appropriate action. The maritime group in the House recognised that excellent rules exist internationally, but many nations avoid implementing them to reduce costs.

The regulations are a step forward and should be implemented. The Minister should take on board what was said this afternoon about ensuring the safety and well-being of those who crew ships.
