Draft Ipswich Port Authority Transfer Scheme 1996 Confirmation Order 1996

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Transport (Mr. Steve Norris)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Ipswich Port Authority Transfer Scheme 1996 Confirmation Order 1996.

 May I say what a pleasure it is to refer to you as Sir James? I am sure that your many hon. Friends on the Conservative Benches have been doing so for years—certainly, I have done so, and it is nice to know that I have got it right at last.

The order will enable the Secretary of State to confirm the transfer of the assets, rights, liabilities and duties of the Ipswich Port Authority to the private sector. All property, rights and liabilities of the authority and all functions conferred or imposed on it by any local statutory provision will then be transferred to the successor company set up by the authority.

In furtherance of the general objectives of the Government's policy for ports, the Transport Act 1981 enabled the transfer of the nationalised harbour undertakings of the British Railways Board and the British Transport Docks Board to the private sector in 1981, and of Sealink in 1984. The abolition of the dock labour scheme in 1989 enabled many ports to modernise their working practices and take advantage of up-to-date cargo handling technology. Those actions released competitive forces that had been dormant for years and led to great improvements in productivity. Other ports, including trust ports, had to respond to new competition and become more competitive.

There are more than 100 trust ports in the United Kingdom. Many have their origins in the middle ages, but the legal status of most of them is determined under private Acts of Parliament passed last century. By the end of the 1980s, some trust port authorities had come to see that their position could best— indeed, perhaps only—be developed by moving into the private sector. At the beginning of this decade, ports such as Tees and Hartlepool, and Forth started to pursue the lengthy and expensive private Bill process required for such transfers.

To enable that process to be done less expensively, the Government introduced the Ports Act 1991. The 1991 Act is an enabling measure, providing a simpler and quicker mechanism than the private Bill procedure for trust ports to privatise themselves. The 1991 Act allows the Secretary of State to compel the larger trust ports to privatise.

The procedure for both types of privatisation is similar. A scheme is drawn up for the transfer of a trust port's undertaking to a Companies Act company formed by the vendor port authority. The scheme must be confirmed by 4 order. In the case of compulsory privatisations, parliamentary approval is needed for the transfer order. The subsequent sale of such a company into the private sector, which requires the consent of the Secretary of State, completes the privatisation process.

The powers of compulsion under section 10 of the Ports Act 1991 enable the Secretary of State to consult the board of any trust port with an annual turnover of more than £5.6 million to ascertain its views on privatisation. Last June, my right hon. Friend the Member for Peterborough (Dr. Mawhinney), the then Secretary of State for Transport, consulted the Ipswich Port Authority. The authority was given three months to respond with its views. On 7 November, following the authority's submission, the Secretary of State wrote to the chairman to direct the authority to form a successor company and to prepare a transfer scheme to enable the authority to transfer all its property, rights, liabilities and functions.

The Ipswich Port Authority met the requirement within the Secretary of State's timetable and subsequently published the required notice, inviting representations to the transfer scheme, and the memorandum of association of the successor company. In fact, only one representation was received, which related to matters appropriate for consideration during the working up of the sale documentation. The scheme submitted closely followed the model that the Department of Transport provided, and the few technical amendments now incorporated in the scheme, which appear as a schedule to the order, have been agreed with the Ipswich Port Authority.

The board of the authority originally informed us that privatisation was not in the best interests of the port. However, I understand that, subject to parliamentary approval, the authority will proceed with privatisation expeditiously, as it considers that that will be the best way to remove uncertainty about the future of the port and increase the confidence of potential customers needed to replace ones lost to competitors.

I commend the order to the Committee.
