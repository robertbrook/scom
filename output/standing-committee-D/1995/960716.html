10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Sir Paul Beresford): I beg to move

That the Committee has considered the Pneumoconiosis etc. (Workers' Compensation) (Payment of Claims) Amendment Regulations 1996.

Before we start, Sir James, I take this opportunity to congratulate you on your honour. A few years ago, I discovered for myself that the moment that one is given a title, everybody shifts the nought at least one place to the right—so I advise you to ignore any comments about pay rises and related matters.

My hon. Friend the Member for Teignbridge (Mr. Nicholls) signed the original order relating to this matter on 30 March 1988, so we have an expert on the Committee—if my hon. Friend's memory is up to the task.

The regulations have been made under the Pneumoconiosis etc. (Workers' Compensation) Act 1979, and their purpose is to increase by 2.1 per cent.—the rise in the retail price index—the amounts of compensation paid under the Act to persons who first satisfy all the conditions of entitlement on or after 1 August 1996. People suffering from industrial diseases have the right to sue the employer concerned for damages. However, because certain dust-related diseases take a long time to develop and might not be diagnosed until 20 to 40 years or more after exposure, sufferers and their dependants can experience considerable difficulty in claiming compensation. By the time that the disease is diagnosed, the employer or employers responsible may no longer exist. Parliament enacted the 1979 legislation to provide a measure of compensation to persons who cannot claim through the courts in the normal way. It provides lump-sum payments to sufferers from certain industrial diseases or, where the sufferers have died, to their dependants.

It was never intended that the 1979 Act should provide an alternative to civil action in the courts. Three basic conditions of entitlement must be satisfied before a payment is made. First, that there is no longer relevant employer who can be sued; secondly, that no court action has been brought nor compensation received in respect of the disease; and, thirdly, that industrial injuries disability benefit has been awarded.

Col. 4 My Department does all that it can to administer the compensation scheme in a sympathetic way. Although it is necessary to ensure that the conditions for payment are met, it is recognised that each case is an individual disaster and the Department is as generous as the legislation allows. Since the 1979 Act came into force in 1980, more than 8,700 applicants have made a claim, and approximately 70 per cent. have received payment. The total cost to date has been £43.2 million. Payments made under the Act are additional to any industrial injuries disability benefit awarded.

The Government have given an undertaking to Parliament to review the amounts payable regularly so as to maintain their value. The draft regulations aim at fulfilling that commitment.

I am sure that members of the Committee would agree that the circumstances leading to payments are, to say the least, unfortunate. They reflect the conditions in which some people worked many years ago. All of us recognise that no amount of money will compensate individuals and their families for their loss, but the regulations allow us to give at least some practical and material help. I commend the upgrading of the payment scales to the Committee, and I ask its approval for implementing them.

Once again, the uprating that the Minister announced today is somewhat mean spirited. The sum on offer is related to the current figure for headline inflation, which is 2.1 per cent. Underlying inflation, which excludes mortgage repayments, is 2.8 per cent. Claimants are likely to be of an older age and not paying a mortgage, and today's uprating is unlikely to maintain their already meagre standard of living.

Last year, when inflation was 3.3 per cent., the uprating was 2.2 per cent. The year before that there Image Col. 5 was no uprating at all. Compensation claimants, who have given up their good health for their work, are already finding it hard to make ends meet. Today's announcement will make their life even more difficult.

The hon. Member for Teignbridge introduced the current regulations in 1988. However, the forerunner to them—the Pneumonoconiosis etc. (Workers' Compensation) Act 1979—was introduced by a Labour Government. The Conservative Government have torn away at the Act's proposals, to the extent that there is now no guaranteed annual uprating—and when upratings are awarded, they take account of the headline rate of inflation rather than the underlying rate. The value of the award has been significantly reduced.

The award goes hand in hand with, for example, the reduced earnings allowance. People affected by the regulations are also likely to suffer under amendments to the reduced earnings allowance, which came into effect on 24 March. Under the old rules, people were allowed to stay on REA beyond pension age if they were not working because they were unemployed or sick. Under the new rules, their award is permanently replaced by a retirement allowance. The new rule will apply even if people have not retired, and fully intend to find another job or to carry on working.

As a result of the change, there is no protection for people who currently receive REA, and who are signing on and are looking for work but are not working because of ill health—unless they are under contract of employment. They will immediately suffer a drop in income of up to £29.70. REA is payable at 25 per cent. of the rate and is worth up to £36.60. The retirement allowance is worth only a maximum of £9.90, and can be as little as 50p a week. In another Committee, the Opposition forced a vote on an amendment to the regime but lost by a single vote. That proposed amendment will be reviewed in the context of social security reforms. The draft regulation that we are considering this morning and those that were passed only a few days ago mean that many people will suffer a substantial loss of income. In some cases, they may lose £30 a week.

Despite the penny pinching that I have described, the Government have had to accept in principle a change in the prescription criteria for chronic bronchitis and emphysema for underground coal workers. Earlier this year, my hon. In 1982—much to the Government's credit—those illnesses were added to the prescribed list of diseases for which compensation is available. Since then, many of us have fought to ensure that the qualifying criteria are reviewed. I pay tribute to my hon. "People have asked whether the forced expiratory volume in one second test, or FEV1 test, is a suitable test, whether the test is carried out properly, and whether it is fair to older and shorter miners."

On a separate issue, he added:

"It has been questioned whether the chest radiograph is an appropriate indicator of dust exposure."

In my constituency, of the 584 claims submitted during the first state of the benefit application only 50 applicants were diagnosed as suffering and awarded benefit. It also said that the need for evidence on a chest radiograph of dust retention as an index of exposure should be removed from the prescription.

In a written answer on 21 May this year, the Under-Secretary of State, Department of Social Security said:

Those of my hon. Finally, I turn to the issue of asbestos. In another guise, the Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment has responsibility for health and safety. One of the key issues relating to the benefit is that exposure to asbestos is occurring because of inadequate health and safety regulations, which are not being properly enforced. Many of the people who will receive benefit were infected by asbestos several years ago when little information was available in the public domain, either to employers or employees, about the consequences of inhalation of asbestos. That situation has now been rectified and no employer or employee in Britain has any doubt about the consequences of the failure of health and safety regulations for people who have to work with asbestos.

However, despite the regulations, there still exists in the UK a substantial trade in asbestos products, which has a combined total value of more than £41 million. Given the continuing need for the regulations because of the continued exposure of people to asbestos, leading to the prescribed diseases, does not the Minister think that we now also need, as a precautionary measure, to reconsider the continuation of this trade and to work out what we can do to reduce or eliminate it over a period?

It seems nonsensical that there is common agreement between all parties that the regulations should ensure the payment of benefits to people damaged by asbestos but, at the preventive end, we are still allowing people potentially to be infected by the asbestos trade. There is a need for the House to ensure that we have not more effective means of securing a minimum level of infection for workers in a range of industries, including the construction industry. Therefore, I ask the Minister to come back after the summer recess and provide time in the House for a debate in asbestos and its potentially dangerous effects on a significant number of our citizens.

Last year, the Government published a report, through the Health and Safety Commission, which estimated that deaths from this disease will reach an annual rate of 10,000 over the next decade. That is a huge number of deaths from an industrial-related disease. It is, therefore, incumbent on the House that we debate what we can do on an all-party basis to reduce the use of asbestos in circumstances where it can lead to death and injury and to improve our ability significantly to reduce, through asbestos regulations, the number of people who have to handle asbestos-related products.

Image Col. 9 Finally, I asked a parliamentary question in May this year which revealed that there were almost 2,000 cases of cancer of the larynx according to the 1990 figures—the most recent available. I would have preferred a more updated figure, and perhaps the Minister will provide one at a later stage. I was amazed by the answer, which said that the information on possible exposure to asbestos relating to the diagnosis of cancer of the larynx is not being collected by the Department of Health, the Health and Safety Commission or the Department of Social Security. There is a substantial number of cases of cancer of the larynx, the primary cause of which will probably have been asbestos, but no information has been collated on the extent of the range of cancers and whether it has peaked or is on the increase.

We need to know what to do to reduce the exposure of asbestos. Asbestosis of the lung is an incurable disease. Asbestosis of the larynx is also incurable, but, unlike asbestosis of the lung, we are not collating any evidence about the spread of the disease and how many people are affected by it. Nor has the Department of Health commissioned research into the link between the use of asbestos and cancer of the larynx, despite the fact that the research of the World Health Organisation, the Canadian Government and others shows a clear link between asbestos-related industrial injuries and cancer of the larynx.

There is a sensible approach to that which would be cost effective—it would cost the Government little to provide the up-front resources. We should start the process of ensuring that the Departments of Health and of Social Security, working with the Health and Safety Commission, research and identify the link between cancer of the larynx and asbestosis. They should provide information for use by Ministers on the number of cases of cancer of the larynx which can be related to asbestos exposure. It is nothing more than reasonable that we do that, given that we are doing it already for the disease as it affects other organs, primarily the lungs.

I have tried to be positive and to raise serious questions, some of which I hope the Minister will be able to answer this morning—the others he can put in written form to me and other members of the Committee—so that we can move on today and approve the uprating. But in so doing, we should make further improvements for next year on the working of the regulations so that they are beneficial to the recipients of the benefits. The Minister and I would rather not see those benefits being paid because they are a result of quite severe damage caused to people by exposure to asbestos.

The definition of asbestosis is covered by that which applies to pneumoconiosis: fibrosis of the lung. The two diseases can often be contracted together, however. The regulations do not refer to such a scenario: for example, a fettler who contracts pneumoconiosis from fettling dust might have previously worked with a machine with asbestos brake linings and, consequently, have a combination of both diseases.

The Minister probably knows that the pneumoconiosis compensation scheme that applies to the coal mining industry also covers asbestos, to ensure that both diseases can be taken into consideration. The regulations do not seem to contain a mechanism for considering those diseases together.

I recently dealt with a case that, although it relates to mining, provides an example of someone contracting both diseases. An elderly miner, who worked in the 1950s with a machine with asbestos brake linings, developed asbestos particles in his lungs. That resulted in the development of a carcinoma and part of his lungs had to be removed. He did not have asbestosis because for that to be the case there must be fibrosis of the lungs. The additional presence of coal dust, however, meant that when he was diagnosed by the medical appeals tribunal, he was found to have advanced pneumoconiosis. In that case, we were able to link the two diseases to ensure an appropriate payment under the scheme.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Makerfield said, the tables do not refer to date of development. I refer Image Col. 11 the Minister to part A on page 3 of the regulations and offer as an example the case of a man of 70 who, probably as a fettler, came into contact with asbestos and has a 40 per cent. disability assessment, made up of a combination of asbestosis and pneumoconiosis. Under the regulations he would receive £4,190. The date of development may be 40 years earlier. I referred to a miner who had been in contact with a machine having asbestos linings. He worked with that machine in the 1950s before he went into the forces. He did national service in 1955, so he was exposed to asbestos between 1951 and 1955–40 years ago. The only way to deal with the anomaly in the tables is for the Minister seriously to consider applying to the regulations the same rules that apply in the mining industry—that is, date of development.

A man of 70 with a 40 per cent. disability would receive £4,190 under the regulations, but if the date of development was 40 years earlier the higher sums at the top of the table would apply. The only fair way of dealing with such cases is to ensure that the date of development is determined by date of diagnosis, which will become the relevant age on which the payment will be based. That would eliminate the enormous anomaly to which my hon. Friend the Member for Makerfield referred.

When the two diseases are present, the medical appeal tribunal, which already exists, could decide the quantum issue. If the date of development is the point on which payment is based, the tribunal could decide the date of development and, therefore, the quantum. If the Minister would consider making such a change, the procedures for deciding the date of development and relating that to quantum already exist. My hon. Friend the Member for Makerfield referred to reduced earnings allowance, which has been replaced by retirement allowance for people who have reached the age of 65, but a 12-month restriction on retrospection has also been introduced. The way in which that works could have a great impact on cases that are brought under the regulations. I gave an example of a 70-yearold man. If he retired at 65, it is likely that he was disabled and unable to follow his regular occupation or one of equal standard for 30 years. Under normal circumstances and before the regulations went through the House on 24 March, a man would have been able to claim reduced earnings allowance for the period for which he was disabled and unable to follow his regular occupation. Since 24 March, that cannot be applied. Under the regulations, cases may be proven, payments may be made and it may be established that a man has been disabled for a large part of his working life—but because of the 12-month restriction he will be unable to claim the reduced earnings allowance for the period for which he was unable to follow an occupation of equal standard to that which he followed before contracting the disease, which will result in a substantial loss.

Col. 12 I ask the Minister seriously to consider whether in regulations such as it is necessary for an increase to compensate people who have been disabled and are unable to undertake an occupation of equal standard to that which they had before they contracted the disease. That would only be fair in terms of the compensation payable.

Similarly, on claims made following death—posthumous claims—I ask the Minister to consider introducing a provision for date of development rather than age of death. That would make an enormous impact, because of the anomalies between the sums of money paid. If someone dies after the age of 70, the post-mortem shows him to be full of asbestos, and the date of development goes as far back as 30 or 40 years, it is only fair that the payment should relate to the disease's date of development. As has been said, the apparatus is already in situ to enable that procedure to be carried out. The medical appeal tribunal could give the date of development, which should be the accepted date from which payments under the scheme are made for posthumous claims.

I ask the Minister to take those two major points on board—date of development for a person who is living, and date of development in the event of posthumous claims.
