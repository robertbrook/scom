Draft Education (Northern Ireland) Order 1995

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Michael Ancram)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Education (Northern Ireland) Order 1995.

The order was laid before the House on 21 November and the largest single element of the order consists of the provisions in part II relating to special education, which reflect those already introduced in England and Wales by the Education Act 1993. The provisions are intended to make the education system in Northern Ireland even move responsive than it is now to the wishes of parents of children with special educational needs. They have been the subject of extensive public consultation, both in their own right and as part of the consultation on the draft order, and I believe that they command widespread support. I have also given a commitment that my Department will hold further full consultation in due course on the detail of the main subordinate measures that will be required to give full effect to the legislation.

I shall briefly describe the main features of the draft order relating to special education. Part II of the order consolidates both new and existing special education legislation. Among the existing provisions, I emphasise in particular that the duty remains to integrate statemented pupils with their peers in mainstream schools, as long as to do so is consistent with parental wishes, with the needs of all the children involved and with the efficient use of resources. Under articles 4 and 5, a code of practice for identifying and assessing special educational needs will be drawn up and issued by my Department, again following full consultation. The code will establish workable guidelines for schools, education and library boards and others for the identification of children with special needs and the procedures following from this.

Parents' rights of appeal against decisions by education and library boards will be greatly extended to include six additional circumstances in which parents may appeal: against proposed placements; against the description of children's special educational needs; against decisions by boards not to comply with parental requests for assessments and reassessments; and against decisions by boards in connection with the amendment or discontinuation of statements. The order provides that all such appeals will be to an independent special educational needs tribunal, established under a president, and with a legally qualified chairman sitting with two members 4 appointed from an independent lay panel maintained by the Department. Its decisions will be binding on all parties.

The draft order also sets out a range of provisions aimed at securing improvements to existing practice in several areas. These include provisions to secure greater flexibility at key stage 4; to introduce new arrangements for approving the transfer of pupils to secondary school either a year early or a year late; to streamline procedures for the acquisition of grant maintained status; to enable lay persons to participate in inspections conducted by the Department of Education in Northern Ireland inspectorate; and to provide the trustees of voluntary schools that close with greater latitude in the disposal of their share of the proceeds of the school premises.

The approval and subsequent implementation of the draft order will lead to significant improvements in the education of Northern Ireland children and particularly those with special educational needs. I commend the order to the Committee.

10.34 am

Mr. Tony Worthington
(Clydebank and Milngavie)
: The briefness of the Minister's opening speech belies the significance of the order. Once again, the measures show the people of Northern Ireland the prize to be grasped if we can achieve a full political settlement because we should not deal with such significant measures in this way. If the proposals related to England, Wales or Scotland, they would be contained in a reasonably sized Bill and would be open to full negotiation and discussion.

First, I intend to address the issue of special needs but I hope to address other aspects of the order. My comments on special educational needs fall into two parts. First, why have the Government set up this framework—in particular, its legalistic aspects—to deal with special educational needs? Secondly, what resources are to be devoted to special educational needs?

The Government have followed the English and Welsh pattern by setting up a special educational needs tribunal. Surprisingly, the Minister has forgotten his Scottish background and has not sought an alternative way of dealing with the matter. Sadly, he has remembered his legal background as a barrister and has set up a framework that rewards barristers. In Scotland, this aspect of special educational needs has not been put into a legal framework.

Bringing the law into any issue has consequences. The procedure becomes lengthy and drawn out because the law never seems to deal with anything on time. A parallel tribunal, the special educational needs tribunal for England and Wales, which was set up in September last year, has had 700 appeals but has heard only 143 cases. Already a substantial backlog seems to have built up. A child only gets one chance at an education, and we should not be setting up a framework to deal with special educational needs that leads to long delays before appeals are heard and decided.

JPEG 5 Is it necessary to set up a framework modelled on the court system so that a barrister has to be president or chairman or the tribunal? Is legal language the best medium? On page 14 of the order, article 15 states that a board,

"shall serve a notice on the child's parent".

That is the language of legalism and criminality. Is that really the best way to deal with special educational needs? The framework is potentially intimidating.

A legal framework also means that a panoply of case law will build up. Provision of legal advice will be requested so that parents do not feel intimated by the array of experts called in by the education and library boards. There is already a legal logjam in England and Wales, so we should be seeking an informal and speedier way of resolving disputes. Why has the Minister chosen to take the legalistic road?

It is no use setting up a framework unless it is properly resourced, which brings me to the second part of my speech. The framework will represent a step forward for children with special educational needs and their parents only if extra resources are given to schools and the education and library boards to enable them to carry forward the process. It is important for the Minister to say what resources are being allocated to cater for special educational needs. In a parliamentary answer on 23 May 1995, the Secretary of State for Education told my hon. Friend the Member for Woolwich (Mr. Austin-Walker):

"the hon. Gentleman may not be aware that an Audit Commission survey in 1992 found that two thirds of head teachers did not know how much was in their budgets for special educational needs".—[ Official Report, 23 May 1995; Vol. 260, c, 695.]

She was critical, in her special schoolmistress style, of head teachers who did not know their special educational needs budget. It should therefore be easy for the Minister to tell us the size of Northern Ireland's special educational needs budget and how much it has been increased by the measure that we are considering.

Mr. Roy Beggs
(East Antrim)

Mr. Worthington
: I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman, who has raised a crucial point. It should not be necessary to ring-fence the resources, but unless we ensure that they are going to the children intended, teachers, parents and children will feel shortchanged by the measure, which promises a great deal without providing the necessary funds.

Teachers have many concerns. Will extra staff be provided to deal with the day-to-day administration of a school's special educational needs programme? Will there be scope for specialist staff to co-ordinate the programme in schools? Considerable documentation will be needed. The order places emphasis on statemented children, for whom there will be a formal system documenting their needs, enabling checks to be made on whether the programmes of schools and the education and library board are being carried out. 6 However, the majority of children with special educational needs will not be statemented. The extra needs of those children will be recognised, but they will not have access to the formal system. A serious approach to special educational needs must take account of those children and provide resources for them.

There is a chronic shortage of educational psychologists throughout the United Kingdom. There is little point in schools identifying needs that have to be assessed unless there are resources to provide educational psychologists or occupational therapists from health boards to cater for those needs. What action will be taken to back up the order? Without such back-up, the order contains only hollow words.

The issue is particularly important because there is a prevalent suspicion elsewhere in the United Kingdom that general financial constraints have put the specialist personnel in education authorities under pressure to find cheap answers for those with special educational needs. For example, under-resourced solutions to help children with autism or dyslexia are frequently put forward because the equivalent of the education and library board does not want to spend too much.

I should like to raise some specific points. I understand the wording of article 3(3) on page 6 which concerns children in Northern Ireland schools whose first language is not English and states:

"A child is not to be taken as having a learning difficulty solely because the language (or form of the language) in which he is, or will be, taught is different from a language (or form of a language) which has at any time been spoken in his home."

I appreciate that we should not have a special needs ghetto, in that a teacher can decide that, because a child's first language is not English, that child can be categorised as having special educational needs and differentiated from other children solely for that reason. However, a child whose first language is not English will have particular difficulties in the education system. As the order excludes such children, what resources and skills are available in the Northern Ireland education system to cater for them?

Article 5(2) on page 7 gives a freedom to DENI. It states:

"The Department shall consult such persons about the draft"—

that is the draft code, which will be one of the keys to the success of the order—

"as the Department thinks fit and shall consider any representations made by them."

I plead with the Minister for parent groups to be included in the consultation with DENI and that there will be no question of consulting only professional groups. DENI must identify relevant parent groups and ensure that they are consulted. I should like the Minister to confirm that today.

I am unhappy with article 7(2)(b)(iii) on page 8 which requires that

"the efficient use of resources."

are borne in mind when complying with the duty to secure education in their ordinary school for children for whom the board maintains a statement of special educational needs. That is the wrong phrase—it is the JPEG 7 language of the marketplace. There will never be efficient provision of resources for special educational needs, which are of course, expensive. Even if we amend the order today, I would be much happier if we could make it clear that we are not talking about the efficient use of resources, but the effective use of resources. The language of the marketplace has no role in such a measure. The stipulation that resources should be used efficiently is a get-out for any educational administrator who can say that the use of resources is not efficient by the general yardstick of how much we spend on children. What matters for the child is that the use of those resources should be effective.

I should like some information on special educational provision in institutions outside Northern Ireland, to which reference is made on page 11. Northern Ireland has a population of approximately 1.6 million. What are the shortfalls in provision? My background is in an education authority covering a population of 2.25 million and even an authority of that size could not provide from its resources all that was required for children with specific needs—perhaps one in 100,000. It would be interesting to know how much is spent on provision outside Northern Ireland, in Great Britain and possibly in the Republic.

Linked with that, but on a smaller scale, is collaboration. What provisions will exist for liaising with other education and library boards within Northern Ireland? What mechanisms exist? We still have five education and library boards in Northern Ireland, which are small in relation to the populations that they serve. There is no way in which an education and library board can provide from its own resources for every special need that will crop up. What are the budgetary mechanisms and the general role played by DENI to ensure adequate provision for all special needs in Northern Ireland?

Finally, I turn to the definition of special educational needs. That definition is fairly narrow and its language relates to learning difficulty and those with below average educational achievement. The document does not provide a perspective for dealing with gifted children. Does the Minister envisage the parents of a very gifted child being able to tell a special educational needs tribunal that their needs are not being met within the system?

The document does not provide a perception of special educational needs in terms of emotional and behavioural difficulties and it has been criticised for that. Does the Minister consider that it has been relatively easy for parents of children with emotional and behavioural difficulties to get special provision within the system?

Those are some of the issues that arise in part II of the order and I hope that the Minister will respond to them.

Before I deal with the specific points raised, I should like to make another general observation. I listened carefully to the hon. Member for Clydebank and Milngavie and was pleased for his sake that he had begun to adopt what might be called the new Labour approach to economics; he demanded considerable extra expenditure without suggesting how to fund it. The only difference between the new Labour approach in Northern Ireland and that adopted in the rest of the United Kingdom is that Northern Ireland operates a block system. Any increase in expenditure in one sector must come at the expense of another, so we have to make a judgment on the balance of priorities every year and assess the key areas for expenditure.

The hon. Member for Clydebank and Milngavie talked about the efficient use of resources, appearing to suggest that a market philosophy was at work that would damage the interests of special and other education in Northern Ireland. Using resources efficiently is important in many sectors. We must ensure that the resources available for Northern Ireland are spent efficiently and effectively, to the maximum advantage of all those involved in education. If the hon. Gentleman considers that more fully than he had when he reaised the point, he will understand that it is a sensible requirement for all expenditure in Northern Ireland.

I now turn to the specific points that have been raised. The hon. Member for East Antrim asked about lay inspectors. It is not our intention that they should be involved in the inspection of teaching and curricular issues. Their function is to bring a commonsense approach to other aspects of the school, such as finance and administration. A lay person with outside experience may be better placed to consider those aspects than those who are concerned solely with the inspection of education. They will consider issues such as financial management and staffing structures. Our intention was never that they should be involved in the inspection of educational matters.

10 As the hon. Member for Clydebank and Milngavie said, training such people to become inspectors in the fullest sense of the word causes the value of their outside experience to be lost. That is the reason for the order's provisions.

The hon. Member for Clydebank and Milngavie was concerned that the framework was top-heavy and legalistic, and that it would create problems. An earlier proposal to set up board-based local appeal committees was withdrawn, following strong representations that they would not be perceived as independent. That consideration was the main reason for setting up the tribunal in its present form. However, a key objective of all those involved will be to ensure that the tribunal proceedings are conducted with minimum formality.

The presence of lawyers at the new tribunal's hearings will be discouraged. To underline that, hon. Members will note that appellants are entitled to seek legal aid for the preparation of their cases, but that it is not intended to extend that to legal representation at the hearings. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will understand that the purpose of that is to keep hearings as informal as possible. Sitting alongside the chairman, who will be legally qualified, will be two lay assessors. Again, we hope that that will lead to the sort of informal hearing that the hon. Gentleman rightly wants.

The hon. Member for Clydebank and Milngavie queried article 15. The duty on a board to serve a notice on a parent that it intends to assess a child's special needs is not a legalistic mechanism; it is a means of ensuring that parents are fully informed of the board's intentions and of their rights to make representations. The hon. Gentleman will understand the value of informing parents of their rights.

The hon. Gentleman raised the issue of the experience of the tribunal in England, and suggested that the proposed framework might lead to more appeals. It is difficult to judge that possibility at this stage. In England, there was a considerable increase in the volume of appeals during the first year of that tribunal's operation, but numbers are expected to level out as precedents are established, both by the tribunal itself and by court judgments following appeals from the tribunal. It was, perhaps, inevitable that there would be a rush of appeals at the beginning, but we think that numbers will stabilise. We expect the same to occur in Northern Ireland.

On the questions of finance and resources, the hon. Members for Clydebank and Milngavie and for East Antrim asked how many educational psychologists would be available, and what special provision was being made for that. That is one area of special education for which funds have been earmarked or ring-fenced. As the hon. Member for East Antrim will know, special education has recently substantially benefited from the additional recurrent allocations of £2 million in 1994–95, £3 million 1995–96, and additional teaching staff to the tune of £1.6 million. In conjunction with other elements, those extra moneys have funded a significant number of additional psychologist posts, which is where the greatest JPEG 11 pressure is currently estimated to lie. The hon. Member for East Antrim asked whether we would continue to monitor the need for special educational psychologists, and I can assure him that we shall do so—we recognise the importance of that provision.

In more general terms, the full cost implications of the order cannot be quantified at this state. However, where a board considers that the provisions carry additional cost implications that cannot be met from existing resources, there are normal channels in the ordinary public expenditure process for presenting a case for additional resources. Provided that the bids are fully substantiated and quantified, I shall give them the fullest possible consideration within the constraints—as is always the case in a block system—of the totality of resources available and other competing pressures. It is helpful that the code of practice will not come into effect until September 1997 because that will allow a full year for resource implications to be considered.
