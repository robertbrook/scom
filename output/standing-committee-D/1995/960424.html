Draft Road Traffic Offenders (Northern Ireland) Order 1996

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Northern Ireland (Mr. Malcolm Moss)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Road Traffic Offenders (Northern Ireland) Order 1996.

The draft order provides a revision of Northern Ireland's road traffic law as it relates to prosecution and punishment for road traffic offences. Its provisions are modelled on those currently in force in Great Britain.

The order has three main parts. Part II makes provision for the trial of road traffic offenders, and deals with proceedings, evidence and the production of documents in court. Part III provides for sentencing of offenders, endorsement of driving licences and disqualification. In particular, it introduces a penalty points system. Under that system, motoring offences would be separated into endorsable and non-endorsable offences. Endorsable offences would carry penalty points which would be endorsed on the counterpart of a driver's licence. If 12 or more points were accumulated within a three-year period, the driver would be disqualified.

The system is aimed at rendering any person who repeatedly commits endorsable road traffic offences liable to a period of disqualification. That would be the most significant new feature of the revised law. The proposed order also specifies offences that would carry obligatory disqualification in their own right, and those where a court would have discretion to disqualify. Other important features of part III include a new extended driving test following conviction for certain serious road traffic offences, and an experiment in the use of courses for drink drivers.

Part IV deals with the fixed penalty procedures. It introduces dual-level fixed penalties. The present fixed penalty of £20 would continue for non-endorsable offences, and a second level of £40 would be introduced for endorsable offences. Those amounts are based on those currently in place in Great Britain and would be subject to review.

Five offences are created by the order. Those deal with failure to produce driving licences and certain information in court, false statements and the unauthorised removal of fixed penalty notices from vehicles.

The draft order was circulated to 162 individuals and bodies, and about 30 responses were received. Overall those indicate a general acceptance of and a welcome for the order's provisions.

4 The draft order follows the 1995 order by reviewing the prosecution and penalty provisions applicable to road traffic offenders. It is believed that its proposals will make a further valuable contribution to road safety and to achieving our target of a one-third reduction in road casualties. I commend the draft order to the Committee.

Mr. Jim Dowd
(Lewisham, West)
: I welcome you to the Chair, Sir James.

I shall be brief. There is nothing in the draft order to which we take exception. As the Minister says, it is largely a catching-up process—a process to which Northern Ireland legislation seems especially prone. The orginal changes in Great Britain legislation date back as far as 1988 and were amended in 1992, so Northern Ireland is now four years behind. I am surprised that the Northern Ireland Office does not take a more original approach, not only in respect of the draft order, but in respect of the delays that are often encountered when harmonising Northern Ireland legislation with Great Britain. Northern Ireland could be used more effectively to test developments in particular fields and to see whether those could be improved in advance of becoming law in Great Britain. I shall return to that point.

The title makes it clear that the order has to do only with offences and the treatment of offenders. As such, no new traffic offences have been created, other than minor ones related to non-payment of fines. The order is essentially about the administration of the process, rather than about matters relating to road traffic and safety, except for the introduction of speed cameras, as they are popularly called—I am not sure what the technical expression in the order is—and the admissibility of photographic evidence. Even my thin grasp of the subject suggests that a camera is necessary to produce photographic evidence.

We welcome the introduction of drink-driving courses for those convicted and willing to undergo them, the extended driving tests following disqualification and the introduction of penalty points, which the Minister mentioned. I am not sure how far behind GB legislation we are in Northern Ireland. To my certain knowledge, penalty points have been in existence in the United Kingdom since 1984 when I received my one and only speeding fine and three points. I hasten to add that that is my only motoring offence, apart from the odd parking ticket, to which motorists in London are prone. We welcome all those developments.

Following the changes to the penalty points system, and the subsequent abolition of parts of the 1981 legislation, will offenders be liable to the new penalty on the date when the offence was committed or on the date of conviction at court, assuming that they go to court? I understand that there could be an overlap between the two. In other words, the order could come into force after the offence was committed but before the offender was brought before the court. I should be grateful for clarification of that.

The evidence from the local authorities that have responsibility for the scheme indicates that it has prompted some offenders to pay so that they do not need to be taken to court. I want to spend a few moments considering the cost and staffing implications. Will the Minister assure the Committee that the full costs of both the drink-drive courses that have been mentioned and the extended driving tests will be borne by offenders, and that there will be no impact on the public purse?

Part 3 of the explanatory document that accompanies the order says that two additional staff will be required at the Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency but makes no mention of the additional staff required as a result of the increased activity of the court service. However, it does show that when the system is established, it will have initial costs of £1.2 million and annual running costs of £600,000 thereafter, against an estimated annual income of £3.2 million. That means that the Department will make £2 million in the first year and £2.6 million in every subsequent year. Some people might regard that as a nice little earner. Certainly, it would justify the institution of the system. However, depending on whether the penalties are £40 or £20, it shows that anywhere between 80,000 and 160,000 new offences would be committed every year. I wonder what will happen to the people who commit those offences. I assume they are being committed now without punishment. I cannot imagine that people's behaviour would change such that they would want only to stoke the Department's coffers.

With regard to financial and staffing implications, a major omission is the cost of installing speed/traffic light cameras. Perhaps that matter is not the responsibility of the Minister's Department. How will the relevant costs be met and who will be responsible for maintaining the facilities?

All road traffic and associated legislation is primarily about safety. Safety is especially appropriate in Northern Ireland, which has the worst record for accidents and for general road safety standards in the United Kingdom. That matter has been researched 6 recently. After our consideration of the previous order in October 1995, the Minister wrote to my hon. Friend the Member for Clydebank and Milngavie (Mr. Worthington) confirming that the Transport Research Laboratory had been commissioned to undertake comparative analysis and that it would report at the end of 1995. Its report has been available for some time. What action does the Minister propose to take in light of the report's conclusions and recommendations?

The Minister confirmed in response to a written question of mine that the number of road safety education officers in Northern Ireland would be cut, despite the fact that the Traffic Research Laboratory report praised their work highly. It stated:

"The relatively better casualty record for children of school age (eg 10–14 year olds) reflect more effective road safety education practice in Northern Ireland…. This probably reflects the Province's unique policy of deploying road safety education officers in Area Education Boards rather than Highways Departments as is usually the case in Great Britain."

It is a shame that the Minister sees fit to cut the number of road safety education officers, for whom there is widespread regard throughout Northern Ireland. There is clear evidence that they play a significant role.

The report also states:

"Another problem of road safety in Northern Ireland that has been indicated by the statistical analysis is the level of drink/driving, which appears to be significantly higher than on the mainland. The RUC breath tests far fewer accident-involved drivers than do the British forces. Experience shows that increasing the test rate to match the British average should help to reduce the level of drink/driving significantly."

We all understand that there are competing and conflicting pressures on the RUC, but the report makes a significant point. The chief constable of the RUC said that enforcing road safety and road traffic leglislation has, for reasons that we all understand, taken second place to other duties. Will the RUC be able to make appropriate progress? Does it have the resources to police and to enforce the order effectively?
