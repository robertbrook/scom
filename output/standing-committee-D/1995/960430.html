National Health Service (Optical Charges and Payments) Amendment Regulations 1996

4.30 pm

Mr. Henry McLeish
(Fife, Central)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the National Health Service (Optical Charges and Payments) Amendment Regulations 1996 (S.I., No. 582).

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the National Health Service (Charges for Drugs and Appliances) Amendment Regulations 1996.

Mr. McLeish
: I welcome the opportunity to discuss both orders, but in particular the order dealing with charges for drugs and appliances. The Minister nods sagely. I am sure that he understands why we want to discuss the matter. In 1975—indeed, on 1 April 1971—prescription charges stood at 20p. Today we are seeking the approval of the House to increase them to £5.50.

In a reasonable world, it could be argued that increases in line with inflation are acceptable. But it is obvious, given the hikes in precription charges in the past 17 years, that the Government have not sought to increase charges in line with inflation. In only two years—1988 and 1989—was the increase inflation-linked. Most of the increases have been savage and we cannot escape the conclusion that this is a Government-inspired tax on health. We have said it before, but it is worth repeating, that the Government have systematically and deliberately hiked up prescription charges in the past 17 years. They have gone much further than increasing charges in line with inflation; they have imposed an additional burden on people who require drugs to treat illness and disease.

The Government have taken such action in a number of areas. They do so unashamedly, so none of us should be surprised that they want to hike up hidden costs and pass them on to patients who seek improvements in their health. They pretend to be a prudent Government who do not want to impose any additional tax burdens on the public. It will be interesting to know from the Minister what cumulative charge has been imposed on patients in the past 17 years. What portion of that was inflation-linked and what portion was the difference between the charge and the inflation element? I think that the figures will be shocking. The Government have imposed that burden by stealth year in and year out.

4 In the early I 980s—after the massive 125 per cent. increase on 16 July 1979—the Government said that they would increase prescription charges in line not with inflation but with costs. The Minister will claim in his defence that the increases reflect the much higher costs faced by the health service. But such increases are unacceptable. In 1979. the increase was 125 per cent., in 1980 the increases were 56 per cent. and 43 per cent., in 1983 it was 30 per cent., and so it goes on. How do the Government justify putting a massive burden on sick people? That is a key question for the Minister.

Today's debate has become ritualistic. We consider these issues every year, either in Committee or on the Floor of the House. The Government should justify their actions. In the next 12 months they should explain to the public what they are doing about this tax on sickness. What evaluation have they made of it? They seem to hike up charges as if they were in a vacuum, with no regard to the implications. What have been the implications of vouchers for eye tests and—although we are not dealing specifically with these—of dental and prescription charges? What effect have those so-called marginal increases had? I hope that the Minister will tell us that the Department of Health has undertaken detailed studies that show what the implications have been, but I suspect that there have been no such studies or, if there have been, that they have not been published.

If the Government want to impose increased taxes. they must spell out the consequences to patients and to the public. Several concerns have been expressed in the press and in medical journals about what is happening to prescription charges. One of the key arguments is that the group of people who are not exempt but who are on low wages cannot afford some of the increases that the Government describe as marginal but which could be described as punitive for people on low incomes. What fact-finding exercise has the Department of Health done so that it can advise Ministers on the implications of hiking up their tax on health? Ministers may not want any objective valuation of what they are doing, but it is a valid question to ask.

Secondly, the Government tend to turn a blind eye to the predicament that doctors face when they have to give patients three prescriptions. for example. at a cost of £16.50, knowing that they might not he taken up because patients cannot afford them. What information does the Department of Health have on that? What is the Minister's advice and guidance on the implications for health of those charges? They are astonishing, and yet the Government preach prudence in matters of finance and taxation and say that they always do the right thing by the public. If that is the case, let us have a justification for some of the increases instead of the blind assertion that they provide valuable income to the national health service.

Thirdly. are people deterred from visiting their general practitioner by the high cost of drugs? That is another important consideration. I shall he objective. and ask whether there is a link between access and JPEG 5 charges. The Department of Health must have carried out a number of studies on that over the past 17 years. Cabinets must be full to the brim with objective evaluations of Tory tax hikes on health. I hope that the Minister will tell us about them this afternoon, or at least put them in the Library for us to read.

Fourthly, people who suffer from certain chronic conditions are exempt from charges, yet others with different chronic conditions are not. What studies are being done on the fairness of that? It is obvious that certain people with similar—at least from their own perception—conditions are given different treatments in terms of charges. That cannot be right. The Government must have an idea of the problems that they are creating and what they should do about them.

My fifth point concerns optical charges. The regulations will increase the value of the vouchers for them.

One thing is clear: the sustained increase is not in doubt and the Government should start to discuss how charges link not only to the quality of care but to access to it. The Minister cannot get off with the usual waffle that the increases are in line with costs, that 80 per cent. of people are exempt and that the prescription charge is only a proportion of the price of the drugs received. That is the well-rehearsed, insensitive and uncaring response that we are all used to.

First we abolished the free test, then we started charging people more for appliances. They have become more and more out of pocket. The vulnerable will suffer, which is a disadvantage of the measure and goes against the spirit of the NHS.

I hope that the Minister can answer my specific questions. I also hope that my colleagues and I have made our position on the two matters clear. We all have a duty to be as clear as possible for those who want us to come clean with our policies.
