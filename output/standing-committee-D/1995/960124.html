Goods Vehicles (Licensing of Operators) Regulations 1995

4.30 pm

Mr. Graham Allen
(Nottingham, North)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Goods Vehicles (Licensing of Operators) Regulations 1995 (S.I. 1995, No. 2869).

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the Goods Vehicles (Licensing of Operators) (Fees) Regulations 1995 (S.I. 1995, No. 3000).

Mr. Allen
: I make no apology for my intention to make a lengthy speech on this important matter. The statutory instruments are part of the Government's deregulation initiative, which is designed to end the need to renew heavy goods vehicle operators' licences every five years. Whether the overarching consideration within which the statutory instruments are framed is valid may be the subject of later discussion.

The Minister made a positive response to Brake. I was present with Brake representatives at a meeting when the Minister was attentive, helpful and listened to the group's concerns. I hope that he will be equally positive today. If he is unable to be helpful in the Committee, perhaps he will respond in writing to the points that Labour and, perhaps, Conservative Members raise. I understand that the Government Whip is traditionally unable to say anything on such occasions, but I pay tribute to the hon. Member for West Derbyshire (Mr. McLoughlin) for his stout support of the BRAKE campaign. I know that the group appreciates that support.

I should declare an interest. My constituency is sponsored by the Transport and General Workers Union, although I have had no contact with it on this issue.

I want to make it clear that the Opposition support the vast majority of people in the heavy goods vehicle industry—they are conscientious, hardworking and reputable. It is in their interests that we highlight the problems in the industry, which are exemplified by the statutory instrument and which are entirely brought about by the cowboy operators who force their drivers to work overlong hours, often with poorly maintained vehicles, tamper with tachographs to hide those facts and fail to run operating centres adequately. Those cowboys are damaging the industry but, sadly, they are not dealt with in the statutory instrument. Those operators—both licensed and unlicensed—from Britain and the rest of Europe are putting innocent lives at risk. In addition, they often make local people's lives a misery by running their operating centres badly.

The proposed changes to regulations are not acceptable without a commitment from the Government—which I hope the Minister can give today —to clamp down on 2 those cowboy operators. Indeed, the statutory instrument reveals the wrongheaded approach of the Conservative party in general. I will not go into that in any detail; I shall try, as far as possible, to preserve the cross-party atmosphere on a matter, that is important to so many victims and their families. However, I am sure that my hon. Friends will draw attention to the constant seeking after deregulation in an area of public safety, even if I, in my search for agreement, do not emphasise it.

The changes proposed in the statutory instrument include a change to the rules so that partnerships can hold operators' licences. That is welcome, but I am not convinced that those who are responsible for cowboy practices, whether an individual or partners, will be brought to book because the Government are failing to strengthen enforcement and penalties.

Ms Jean Corston
(Bristol, East)
: I apologise first, for being late, and secondly, for leaving soon because I am serving on a Select Committee which will sit shortly.

Does my hon. Friend agree that we should congratulate Brake, which has campaigned on HGV safety? Its meetings held in the House under the chairmanship of our hon. Friend the Member for Crewe and Nantwich (Mrs. Dunwoody) have given powerful testimony of the inadequacies of current remedies to deal with cowboy operators. I have in mind especially the accident at Sowerby and the terrible effect it had on families. I wish Brake more power to its elbow.

Mr. Allen
: I am pleased to put on record again our deep appreciation of Brake's work. My hon. Friend the Member for Bristol, East (Ms Corston) and, no doubt, other hon. Members—I have already mentioned the hon. Member for West Derbyshire—are active members of Brake.

Mr. John Carlisle
(Luton, North)
: On a point or order, Mr. Cook. Perhaps the hon. Gentleman might inform us exactly what Brake is and what it stands for.

Mr. Allen
: I am rather surprised that an hon. Member would come to the Committee to discuss HGV regulation without being aware of Brake. I am glad that the Minister, through the Whip, is now briefing the hon. Gentleman. Brake has campaigned with the support of all parties, organisations within the motor trade and the roads industry to ensure higher safety standards for HGVs. I am sure that it would be pleased to continue the hon. Gentleman's education by sending him literature, which I advise him to read closely.

Mr. Keith Mans
(Wyre)
: What does Brake stand for?

Mr. Allen
: I do not want to explain the ins and outs of a motor engine.

The Chairman
: Order. I am all in favour of a little laissez-faire in the Committee and I do not mind inquiries about acronyms, but I would hate things to become acrimonious. Please desist and allow progress to be made.

Mr. Allen
: Part II of the statutory instrument refers to procedural irregularities. We support traffic commissioners being given the opportunity to review a JPEG 3 decision to grant a licence because of procedural irregularities—for example, if the police passed on damning evidence about an applicant that was then wrongly filed. That is a welcome change and I congratulate the Minister for introducing it.

Later, in my speech, I shall press the Minister about when the Government will provide an effective computer network, as recommended by the Curtis report, which would remove any chance of a communication breakdown. Having spoken to him on the matter, I know that he is concerned about it. The sooner we have that computer network the better.

Those are my general points. It is important that, in what outsiders may see as the rarefied atmosphere of the Committee, we do not get lost in the detail. We must consider the human cost of the statutory instrument. I shall put forward a worst-case scenario. Let us say that an innocent person has been killed by a large goods vehicle that ran out of control down a hill due to ineffective breaks. It is discovered that the haulier has a history of dangerous lack of maintenance and other illegalities stretching back several years, yet the licensing authority has allowed the operator to continue. The cost is a life.

It may be thought that justice will be done, but although the Department of Transport vehicle examiners have proved that his badly maintained vehicle was responsible for the crash, it takes almost three years before the haulier is proved guilty in court. Even when he is charged on a range of courts, including defective brakes and steering, it is the victim who has to inform the licensing authority because there is no effective official communication channel between the courts and the traffic area officers.

Following the victim's representations, the licensing authority takes the haulier to court. The licensing authority listens to the haulier's argument that

"he was short staffed due to illness"

and decides that he is still of good repute and can keep his licence. Four years after the fatal crash, the Department of Transport discover that the vehicle examiners have imposed defect notices on his vehicles, but the haulier is still granted a licence.

Thank God that that is fantasy and could not happen—or could it? I am sorry to inform the Committee that that was not a fictional story. It is the all too sad but true story of Sue Williams, a 52-year-old physics lecturer who was killed in a crash in 1992 by a lorry owned by the haulage firm Herbert Pheasey. The latest incident in the saga happened in November 1995. It was not until this afternoon that I realised that Mrs. Williams' daughter is Mary Williams, whom the Minister knows well and who has campaigned tirelessly through her work with Brake, to end these tragedies.

That is just one example of the tragic flaws in the system. I hope that the Minister will not dismiss it as a one-off or claim that the system generally is working effectively. There are all too many such incidents and the system needs to be re-examined so that we can catch the problems before they cause fatalities on the roads.

This morning we met the parents of Charles Mitchell, aged 20, who was killed by a lorry last year on the A1 near Newark. The driver is being tried on 5 February at 4 Nottingham crown court on a charge of causing death by dangerous driving, so I shall not refer to that case in any more detail. This morning his parents, who are still grieving for their son, asked us to use this debate today to bring to the Minister's attention the need for tougher enforcement and penalties for operators who put lives at risk.

My hon. Friend the Member for Halifax (Mrs. Mahon) will wish to catch your eye, Mr. Cook, to talk about the Sowerby Bridge tragedy in Yorkshire. I shall content myself with saying that six people were killed by a truck with defective brakes. A battle is still going on in the High Court to bring about a judicial review of the Crown Prosecution Service's decision not to bring a charge of manslaughter against the company. The penalties in part VII of the statutory instrument will not help that at all.

The company's only penalty so far has been a £5,000 fine. Six people died that day. I leave hon. Members to draw their own conclusions about whether their lives are worth less than £1,000 each. Sadly, the incompetence of the CPS resulted in a wrongly worded summons being sent to the operator by name, rather than the company. By the time that the trivial mistake had been spotted, it was too late to reword the summons and charges could not be pressed. I hope that the Minister will join us in asking the Attorney-General to expedite the case and let the victims' families finally draw a line under that tragedy.

Again, part II.6 of the statutory instrument shows that it is not only a case of people who could well be guilty of manslaughter getting off with a trivial charge. When an HGV driver ploughed into the back of Tom Cowan's car in Nottingham, killing him—leaving behind his wife and three children—the driver was fined £400 for careless driving. We should compare that with what happened in the case of the fishing vessel Pescadoro, about which we have all probably read in the newspapers. That vessel was raised from the sea bed some years after it had sunk. The courts had no compunction about preferring charges of manslaughter against its owners, who had sent it out to sea in an unfit, unseaworthy condition.

The same should be able to happen to the case of heavy goods vehicles, but at the moment people can avoid serious charges. The statutory instrument does not address the matter in any shape or form. That is a great omission and needs to be put right as soon as possible.

I have cited just a few cases to illustrate the inadequacy of various parts of the order. I hope that the Minister, who has been extremely generous with his time on such matters, will use his remaining months in office—[HON. MEMBERS: "Hear, hear."]—I mean that seriously and do not say it simply to make a point. I hope that he will use his remaining time in office, however long that may be, to leave as an obituary or monument to himself some serious changes in the regulations on heavy goods vehicles, even if he cannot do that today.

The evidence reveals that a massive 24 per cent.—almost a quarter—of all heavy goods vehicle crashes involve unlicensed vehicles. We estimate that 4 to 9 per cent. of vehicles are unlicensed, so that tiny minority are responsible for a disproportionately high number of accidents. I am afraid that that illustrates again the inadequacies of the order, in which the stipulated penalties are so feeble that they can act as no disincentive whatever to HGV operators who operate without a licence.

JPEG 5 Parliament, whether through the present Government or through an incoming Labour Government, must act to end the shambles by introducing the power to impound unlicensed operators' vehicles. Nowhere in the order is that question addressed. I know that the Minister has some sympathy with that view, which we have put to him regularly. I hope that he will say something today that will ensure that, in extreme cases when people are driving HGVs without a licence, the ultimate sanction will be the impounding and possibly the sale of the vehicles involved. They are unlicensed lethal weapons on our roads.

The licensing authorities referred to in the order do not currently have sufficient powers to put cowboy operators out of business. According to Brake, many licensing authorities admit that they would rather not revoke a dangerous operator's licence because he may simply choose to run HGVs outside the system. Impounding would stop that problem and provide a real deterrent to those who operate without a licence.

We have been in touch with the industry, which also says that such people should be put out of business. The tiny minority who operate in that way undercut the great majority of good operators. They can undercut them on price because they send vehicles out with bad maintenance. They have bad garaging facilities and they pay low wages. They are the enemy of good practice, both from the drivers' point of view and from that of the responsible owners and operators who play such a vital part in our economic life.

I do not bring a partisan message to the debate between labour and bosses or Conservative and Labour. The Curtis report, published in June 1995, stated:

"One of the reasons why Commissioners are not tougher in the exercise of their disciplinary powers, and do not command greater public confidence, is that it is felt too easy for an operator to run vehicles illegally, that is without an operator's licence … More must be done to ensure that the potential penalties, particularly where businesses have already been convicted of illegal operation, or banned from operating by a court of Traffic Commissioner, are such as to be a real deterrent."

More recently, the Secretary of State for the Environment's United Kingdom round table on sustainable development—another highly authoritative source—also supported those calls, pointedly suggesting that there was a need for tougher and better enforcement. Its report on freight transport states:

"We agree with the Royal Commission on Environmental Pollution that the seeking out and prosecuting of those who operate unlicensed vehicles, and the impounding of vehicles where necessary, would be likely to reduce offences in all categories, since it is usually those who are operating illegally who are contravening other regulations, for example on vehicle maintenance, roadworthiness and overloading. The targeting of illegal operators would be much more effective if backed up by the power to impound, and if necessary dispose of, illegal vehicles."

That is the view of the United Kingdom round table on sustainable development, courtesy of the Secretary of State for the Environment. I call him as a witness in my case for having the power to impound heavy goods vehicles that are not licensed.

As the Minister knows, the freight industry's representative body, the Freight Transport Association, is calling for the impounding of unlicensed vehicles. It is also supporting other initiatives to promote high standards of driving, such as the good lorry code. On impounding, will the Minister tell us the results of the survey of 2,000 lorries that he mentioned when he went 6 to see him as part of a delegation some time ago? The hon. Gentleman promised to introduce the impounding of unlicensed lorries if the Department of Transport's survey of 2,000 lorries showed that between 2 per cent. and 5 per cent. are cowboy operators and that there is a significant problem.

Section 12, which is headed:

"Time of making objections and representations"

deals with the current controls on entry to the industry mentioned in the regulations. The current system for licence applicants is stuck, I am afraid, in the age of the quill pen. The Minister and the Department seem oblivious to the information revolution—sadly, in this instance, they appear to be chugging along in the slow lane of the information superhighway and encouraging a long tailback of HGV abuse behind them. The weakness of these requirements is compounded by poor communication between the police, the licensing authorities and the vehicle inspectorate. There is no shared information network which combines the intelligence from all three enforcement agencies. Brake claims that

"application forms for operator licences are hardly cross-checked at all by traffic area offices."

I am sure that the Minister will correct me if Brake is wrong about that. An operator with a string of convictions can claim that he has no criminal record and his deceit probably will not be picked up.

I have here a visual aid in the form of an HGV application for a licence. It is a self-certifying form—asked whether he has any previous convictions, an applicant can simply tick the box marked "No". For those who say yes, the form asks for a list of convictions. The applicant is asked about his professional competence and whether he is bankrupt. He need simply fill out the form, send the application to the traffic commissioner and then—nothing. He can be bankrupt and have a string of convictions, but no one bothers to check through another agency whether he is fit to run an HGV. I know that the Minister is concerned about that, so I beg him to reconsider the position in light of the survey which his Department is conducting but which is not in the public domain.

Mr. Gordon Prentice
(Pendle)
: There is certainly a lack of liaison between the courts, the police and the licensing authorities. But are not local authorities also left out in the cold? Local authorities are responsible for planning matters and often receive complaints from residents who live near operating centres; like the courts, the police and licensing authorities, local authorities are in a pivotal position but they, too, are being ignored.

Mr. Allen
: My hon. Friend makes a good point. We want local authorities to have some approved access to such information—if not directly, then from another source. I enter a caveat about people's civil liberties and about information being spread far and wide but believe none the less that when highly specific applications are being dealt with, highly specific checks through one agency or another are needed to ensure that local authorities are involved. People with long strings of convictions should not be able simply to fill out a form, get an HGV, set up an operating centre and rev up their lorries first thing in the morning and last thing at night JPEG 7 with bits falling off them and smoke belching out at the back, but that is what a tiny minority of cowboy operators are currently able to do.

Computerised enforcement intelligence such as that held on the police national computer is the only way to combat that dangerous fraud. The cases that I have described are examples not of road rage or of people overtaking and cutting one up but of crimes in which people are killed. We should use all the means of technology available—with the necessary human rights safeguards—to ensure that such people are brought to book. One speaks with a bit more vehemence on this subject after one has met the families of the victims. It is a shame that we cannot hear from them today.

The current system of intelligence relies on the names of applicants for goods vehicle licences being listed by traffic area operators in a publication entitled "Applications and Decisions, which is published fortnightly. In response to a parliamentary question on 9 January, the Minister said:

"Copies are sent to statutory objectors, including the police, to check the details of applicants for operator licences against their records."

In admitting that the current system was not adequate, the Minister added:

"Our longer-term aim is the development of a common database for operators and drivers for enforcement and licensing purposes, and which will provide links to enable traffic commissioners to receive information from other sources relevant to their licensing and discipline decisions."—[ Official Report, 9 January 1996; Vol. 269, c. 46.]

Why is that a "longer-term aim"? The Minister has conceded the case. Does the Department of Transport have insufficient resources to move into the information age? I could be petty and party political by suggesting that, with the recent salvaging of the roads programme, a little money might be reallocated to plug in one or two computers from A to B to ensure that such checks could be made not in the longer term, but in as short a term as possible. Will the Minister tell us the time scale that he has in mind?

When I asked the Minister to clarify how many people had successfully obtained licences and had subsequently been found to have a criminal record, he refused to answer. He argued that it would be too expensive to acquire that information. I should have thought that traffic area operators would be well aware when an application from a convicted person had slipped through the net and that the Minister could not argue in all conscience that writing to each traffic area would cost too much money. I suspect, however, that I did not receive a direct answer because there is a problem and because no one wants to admit it and no one wants the press and public to know its true extent.

In lieu of answers from the Department, I carried out a short piece of telephone research. Within a few hours, I discovered the case of an operator who applied for a licence in September 1994, and had failed to declare on the form previous convictions for illegal operation of heavy goods vehicle, illegal disposal of waste and handling stolen goods—just the sort of chap we want to push through the system quickly, and to provide with an HGV licence, of course. He continued to operate until June 1995, when a report on poor maintenance was 8 actioned. Only then did the convictions come to light. It turned out that the operator had held a licence prior to that issued in September 1995, and that that had been revoked because of maintenance problems. What is it necessary to do to be denied an HGV licence?

If an Opposition Member, with correspondingly limited resources, can find out such information, I should have hoped that the Department of Transport, with its huge resources and clout, would be able to provide us with more comprehensive, publicly available statistics on the extent of the problem. However, figures in the Government's Curtis report reveal that it is the general public, rather than statutory bodies, who raise issues concerning applications for operators' licenses. There is little in the relevant statutory instrument relating to the way in which such matters could influence the development of a safer HGV industry. The figures that are given for 1993–94, for the number of objections and representations concerning applications for HGV operators' licences, reveal that 5 per cent. were opposed. The vast majority of the representations came from the general public in the vicinity of the operating centre—a point that my hon. Friend the Member for Pendle (Mr. Prentice) pertinently raised. More than 7,000 such representations and objections were made on environmental grounds. I understand that the statutory instrument will provide for such complaints to be made at any time, and that is welcome. However, only 54 objections came from the police. They objected to only 0.14 per cent. of the 37,000-plus applications in 1993–94.

Perhaps that fact bears witness to the good repute of the applicants, but I am afraid that common sense tells me that some people with criminal pasts may be slipping through the net—and even filling out the form dishonestly, without being caught by a police check. An effective computer network would not eliminate those occurrences, but it would make them far less likely. Far fewer people with criminal convictions would slip through the net, obtain licences and operate HGVs illegally on the roads.

Improvements are also needed to the information flow between the agencies. The problem does not end with cross-checking procedures. Cases have already arisen in which operators have offended but the information has not been passed between agencies, because there is no computer network. I congratulate the Government on the setting up, at long last, of a trial scheme in south Wales and Scotland, so that traffic areas can be informed by local courts of hauliers' convictions for not possessing an operator's licence. The scheme must be expanded, but the relevant statutory instrument does not refer to it. Its expansion would allow the establishment of an up-to-date computer network, passing on information about all convictions relevant to lorry drivers or haulage bosses.

Ideally, the computer network should connect all the enforcement agencies and traffic areas. That is vital, because at the moment too little information is being passed between courts, licensing authorities and enforcement agencies. Additionally, of course, we should want the network to be connected, if possible, to the police.

The number of people carrying out enforcement has dropped, so that there is less information on cowboy operators. In considering the statutory instrument and discussing the return of licences and discs, we should be JPEG 9 aware that since 1991 the number of vehicle examiners, who are responsible for checking the physical safety of vehicles and maintenance levels, has dropped from 260 to 241. The number of traffic examiners, who check vehicle licences, has also been reduced, from 204 to 191.

I served on another statutory instruments Committee yesterday, in which we approved a marvellous statement of intent about marine pollution. Sadly, cutting back the Marine Safety Agency and the Coastguard Agency flies in the face of those fine sentiments. The fine sentiments in the order must be underpinned by adequate numbers of vehicle and traffic examiners.

We understand that that is not the end of the story. Further efficiency gains may be made by enforcement authorities, which have not been used to raise standards of enforcement. In a recent document, the Freight Transport Association said:

"efficiency gains made by enforcement authorities on safety checks for lorries should be used to improve standards rather than just to maintain existing levels."

It could have added, to reduce existing levels.

The order fails to give clear guidance to licensing authorities. The current system of autonomous traffic areas is producing an unreasonable amount of variation in decisions on the granting and revoking of licences. The authorities need clear guidelines. We need something clearer than the rather obscure drafting of the order, which may perhaps be necessary, but is none the less not easily accessible to members of the public, victims or their families.

Clear guidelines would help to remove the inconsistencies between the rulings of different licensing authorities and would tackle the problem of lenient rulings, which so enrage our constituents—and Members of Parliament—when they come to light. Such inconsistencies can ultimately lead to the sort of tragic deaths to which I referred.

Such detailed guidelines, which I understand would be welcomed by many of the licensing authorities, should include advice on who should give evidence at public inquiries. It is vital that the police, victims and their families and other relevant bodies be given every opportunity to state their objections to dangerous operators.

Finally, on the failure of the order and of present laws to ensure that companies that fail to maintain their lorries properly receive severe penalties, there is overwhelming evidence that companies that are directly responsible for deaths because of poor maintenance are literally getting away with manslaughter. I have already mentioned the Sowerby Bridge case and I am sure that my hon. Friend the Member for Halifax will speak about that later. It is an example of how the Crown Prosecution Service fails properly to address the tragic deaths caused by HGVs, despite the evidence that illegal practices are to blame.

The system is failing to hand out sentences that match the crimes—for crimes they are. For example, the 38-tonne tanker driven by Loretta Campbell, which crashed into a car on the hard shoulder killing three young men. She had exceeded her maximum driving hours and had probably fallen asleep at the wheel. The tachograph was nowhere to be found. Yet on 17 November, she was convicted of careless driving and fined £500—less than £200 per life.

10 The Mitchells, whom we met this morning, spoke of a tape recording in which the driver who was finally to kill their son in an accident on the A1 told his employer, "I am over my time. I am tired. I cannot do the next run. I have worked my hours." He was told in no uncertain terms that, if that was how he wanted to play it, he was effectively out of a job. That inference is caught on tape. I cannot say much more about it because the matter is to come to trial shortly.

The Minister will be aware that there is mounting concern about the weakness of sentences handed out to operators as opposed to drivers. In may cases the operators are not even charged. The statutory instrument will not correct that—it is wrong that operators are not charged with causing death by dangerous driving.

The Chairman
: Order. I ask for the Committee's complete attention on this point, as I must seek the assistance of hon. Members. There are different issues to discuss—we are talking about charges that could come before the courts. I am alert to the fact that we may do a little damage in trying to do a great deal of good. I ask hon. Members to be careful how matters are presented.

Some of the comments that have been made—I emphasise that this is not a rebuke—prompt me to ask hon. Members to be sensitive to the fact that, until an hon. Member reaches the punchline, I am unable to tell whether a matter might be sub judice. For Heaven's sake, please do not conceal such a fact until the end of a contribution. We do not want any Paul Daniels stuff here. Let us have matters out in the open. If there is any risk of something being sub judice, keep it out of the argument and use another form of logic.

Mr. Allen
: As always, Mr. Cook, your advice is good. I shall not refer to specific cases while concluding my remarks.

A lorry driver is in charge of a potentially lethal weapon and has responsibilities to drive with maximum care and attention. An operating company should have the same responsibilities, but I hope that I have shown that where drivers and, above all, companies do not take those responsibilities seriously, accidents often happen and operators can get away scot free.

The large amount of freight now travelling between European countries makes it essential that the Government, or an incoming Government, take steps to simplify procedures to ensure that it is easier to prosecute drivers and companies for offences not committed in the country of origin. I urge the Minister to use his power and to move quickly to save further lives. We will support the Government in Committee and in the Chamber, if they urgently take sensible action to address those problems. It is tragic that we are debating red tape when we could be discussing effective measures to prevent such tragedies. Such effective measures are not in the statutory instrument. I urge the Minister to bring them forward quickly.

Mr. Gordon Prentice
: My hon. Friend the Member for Nottingham, North (Mr. Allen) mentioned red tape. Will the Minister fill in the gaps between the Government's deregulation initiative, which bears heavily in this area, and the proposals in the statutory instruments?

JPEG 11 We need tougher regulations and better enforcement for the reasons that have been set out.
