4.30 pm

Mr. Malcolm Wicks (Croydon, North-West): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Child Support Departure Direction (Anticipatory Application) Regulations 1996 (S.I. 1996, No. 6335).

I do not intend to detain the Committee for too long, but I should like to ask the Minister some specific questions about the regulations. As an introduction to my brief remarks, may I say that the Government and Parliament are now struggling to bring some justice and fairness into the child support system? It is an administrative system which, in the eyes of some, does not bring the same justice to bear that a court-based system might.

As we know, Parliament has taxed itself for several years in considering the implementation of child support and the practice of the Child Support Agency. It is fair to say that the agency has come in for considerable criticism from both sides of the House of Commons. It was no surprise when the Government introduced the new Child Support Act 1995 to make amendments which, in general, Opposition Members welcomed and, indeed, had urged.

The regulations concern the piloting of what is known as the departure system, and will bring greater flexibility to it. It is easy to consider only one party—for example, the so-called absent parent or the parent with care—but it is much more difficult to get the balance right when considering the child's interests and maintenance between the parents.

Although at the beginning of the agency's life, most of the criticisms of it that came to me via my advice surgery were from the so-called absent parent—typically the father—I am now seeing a striking shift in the balance. Rather more parents with care—typically mothers—are coming forward. They say, "I thought Parliament had legislated for child support, but I've got two children and the father's never paid me a penny." The right balance must be achieved.

One of the departures concerns children who are not the natural children of one of the parents in their family—in common parlance, step-children. The departure regulations will bring some justice to their situation. First, will the Minister explain the approach of the Department of Social Security and the CSA to step-children? Which step-children are included in the regulations and which are excluded? How does the regime for step-children relate to the obligations of the child's natural father—the so-called absent parent—to support him or her? That is an important issue. In Col. 4 recent years. Parliament's attention has focused more on the one-parent family phenomenon—it certainly involves large numbers and issues—and less on stepchildren, and yet perhaps one in 11 or one in 12 children in this country live in step-families. The issue is therefore important in terms of the operation of the CSA.

Secondly, I note that the Government have not introduced a departure for child-care costs. Many would argue that for some so-called absent parents who have a second family, the costs of care for their children might be as important in terms of the family budget as, say, travel-to-work costs. Why has no departure been brought forward for child-care costs?

That brings me to my third point: travel-to-work costs. In the early days, one of the common criticisms of the CSA was that travel-to-work costs were not really allowed for. The departure concept endeavours to introduce a policy to allow for travel-to-work costs, and it would be helpful if the Minister were to explain what that policy is and how he envisages it operating in practice.

My fourth specific point concerns the further departure—the departure from the basic formula—in terms of a person who tells the agency. "Here is my declared income; it is low; I cannot afford much child maintenance", but who is perceived by some, including, perhaps, his ex-wife, to have a life style which seems, if not lavish, significantly better than would be suggested by his income. There is a departure to deal with that, which does not exclusively affect the self-employed, although in my experience, the self-employed are numerous among those who might use their company as a means to avoid child maintenance obligations. Anecdotally from advice surgery, women tell us of men who claim to earn only a certain income, but who, in practice, have a great life style, a great house and a smart car, and pay their new wife or partner a great salary in order to reduce the profits of the company—or whatever. It would be helpful if the Minister could explain the Government's approach to that situation.

We are aware that the CSA have been under enormous pressure administratively, to put it mildly. Although some of the indicators are moving in the right direction, we know that there have been problems of error and maladministration. However, the Government want to give the CSA quite substantial and sometimes complex new duties. Does the Minister assume that those will be fulfilled under existing resources, and can he give us some reassurance that the new duties will not make the agency's pretty poor record even poorer? In simple terms, can the CSA cope administratively with the new departure system alongside its other duties?

We are discussing pilot regulations—regulations that introduce pilots—and it would be helpful if the Minister could tell us something about the pilots: how they are being monitored, whether there are any independent monitors or researchers of the scheme and what form of report will be brought to Parliament Image Col. 5 so that we can all be satisfied that the issues have been piloted properly and that their outcomes are successful.

Mr. Anthony Steen (South Hams): I wonder whether the hon. Gentleman can help me with the term "pilot regulation". I have never come across it before. Are the Government introducing additional regulations—pilot regulations—that are not real regulations?

Mr. Wicks: The Minister will be in a better position than I to clarify the terms. The regulations essentially introduce pilot schemes. In other words, they will not operate throughout the country, so the term "pilot regulations" is possibly an abbreviation that is not altogether helpful to the astute among us. No doubt, the Minister will be able to clarify the issue.

Under the current appeals procedure, I know that there is scope in certain circumstances for a chairman alone to hear appeals without the two lay representatives. Does the Minister plan to use that mechanism on appeals regarding departures? It would be helpful could he clarify that point.

4.40 pm

Ms Liz Lynne (Rochdale): I apologise for being a few minutes late, Mr. Hill. We are debating the departure regulations because of the basic injustice of the formula initially. Even supporters of the Child Support Act 1991 and the original formula have come to believe that a degree of discretion is essential.

I am pleased that step-children for whom parents took responsibility before the Child Support Act came into effect will be taken into account. I should, however, like clarification of the position of stepchildren for whom parents took responsibility after it came into effect. At long last, some children and parents who have gone through misery might receive a little comfort.

We cannot, however, help people—including many of my constituents—whose relationships have broken down. I have spoken before about a man who had a good relationship with his son. When the Child Support Agency became involved, he could not visit the child any more, give presents or pay for clothes. His ex-wife used that as a stick with which to beat him and prevented the child from seeing him. She told the child, "Your Daddy doesn't love you any more", and when his father telephoned, he said, "I don't want to see you ever again" and slammed down the phone. We have all heard such cases in our advice surgeries, and they demonstrate the Act's emotional impact on families. In so far as step-children will be taken into account to some degree, I welcome the regulations.

I fear, however, that the proposals will be an administrative nightmare and that people will come to my surgery or write to me because they will not receive the answers that they need for the CSA and because their cases will not be dealt with as speedily as they should be. An answer to a parliamentary question showed that, until the end of November 1995, clearance for appeals took 41 weeks. The number of reviews is building up and there is a tremendous backlog. Will the departures lead to an even longer Col. 6 one? I need some assurance from the Minister. As he knows, I shall write to him with questions if the present situation continues. Although I welcome the proposals, they will not be good enough. The CSA is already struggling and could grind to a halt. As the Minister knows, we need to scrap the Act and start again from the beginning.

I am concerned about the strict conditions in part II. Someone who completes the departure application and is asked to provide further information must do so in 14 days. Of course, we want the assessment to be speedy, but we should remember that it takes the CSA 41 weeks to check the facts for an appeal. It therefore seems a little unreasonable to tell parents that they will have no case for departure if they fail to provide the extra information in 14 days. Is the 14-day period set? Will there be some leeway if parents have been on holiday or are unable to communicate? Will they have to provide the information in 14 days or face losing their right to the departure? Speed is essential, but is it possible?

One ground for departure is the cost of keeping in touch with the child, and I welcome the proposals in that respect because children have suffered for four years. A Joseph Rowntree Foundation report recently stated that the CSA and the Child Support Act had had an adverse effect on children.

I am concerned about the problems that the proposals might create. We currently have problems with the 104-night rule, which some parents use to ensure that the liable parent does not have the child for more than 104 nights. Could that happen under the system that we are debating? If the liable father—it is usually the father—does not see the child regularly because the mother with care has denied him contact, will the Child Support Agency be able to use that situation say to him that the departure no longer works because he has not seen his child as often as he had agreed to see him or her? We need clarification, because the CSA could get into a horrendous muddle. Vindictive parents with care may use the system to get their own back on the other parent, be it the father or the mother. A financial penalty would be imposed on the liable parent; probably the father. We need clarification and reassurance that we shall not get into the mess that we found ourselves in on some of the other departures.

4.56 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. Andrew Mitchell): First, Mr. Hill, may I welcome you to the chair?

The regulations pave the way for the trial of an important change in the calculation of child support liability and I am grateful to the hon. Member for Croydon, North-West for arranging our debate so that we can discuss them in detail. During my brief remarks, I shall try to persuade my hon. Friend the Member for South Hams (Mr. Steen), who rightly mentioned our strong interest in deregulation, that the regulations are in the finest traditions of the view that he is advocating and do not run across it.

The regulations allow the Child Support Agency to pilot new procedures that will introduce an element of discretion into the way liability for child support is calculated. I emphasise that they are pilot regulations and that it is important that we should pilot them, in view of some of the things that have gone wrong with the Child Support Agency. Under the provisions, the CSA will be able to take account of certain special expenses and other factors that the standard assessment formula is unable to accommodate. The new departure direction system is a major part of the Government's review of the way the Child Support Act 1991 has operated in its first three years. The result will be a more flexible and just regime which should do much to diffuse criticisms of the way the child support system currently operates.

Col. 10 It would not be helpful to the Committee to explain the content of the regulations in great detail. For the most part, they are self-explanatory, but it may be helpful if I outline the way that the departure scheme can be relied upon to produce fairer calculations of child support liability. First, a parent can cite six special expenses in support of an application. These are: high travel-to-work costs—I shall return to the point made about that; high costs in maintaining contact with the children; costs arising from the illness or disability of the applicant or a dependant; the costs of servicing debts incurred while the family were together; commitments entered into by a parent before his court order was replaced by a CSA assessment; and in certain circumstances, the costs of supporting stepchildren, a point to which the hon. Members for Croydon, North-West and for Rochdale referred and to which I shall return.

Secondly, a departure direction can replace the broadbrush allowance for property and capital transfers introduced last April with an accurate figure based upon the actual amount transferred. Thirdly, the departure scheme can deal with some of the avoidance methods that have been springing up, such as instances where an absent parent diverts part of his income to another party in order to reduce his liability. The hon. Gentleman asked me about that point, which concerns both him and me; it principally, but not solely, affects the self-employed and I hope that the pilot scheme will enable us to remedy that injustice. Finally, the scheme will deal with potential inequities in the way the formula calculation deals with housing and travel costs.

I shall address, in no particular order, the points raised by hon. Friends and by Opposition Members—if I discover from reading Hansard that I have overlooked any point, I shall write to the hon. Member concerned.

The hon. Member for Croydon, North-West said that his experience of the Child Support Agency suggested that the balance of complaints had changed from more men to more women complaining about its workings. I can help him by confirming that he is right. The majority of complaints from men are now about the use of the deduction of earnings order—the DEO—which the Child Support Agency imposes in appropriate cases. One has to work hard to secure a deduction of earnings order. Only when there is a lack of co-operation, which is unfair to the parent with care, are DEOs imposed.

The hon. Gentleman was also right to identify women's main complaints, which derive principally from those who know that other women are receiving support from the CSA and that they are not. I hope that DEOs, by ensuring that payments get through, will help to improve the situation over the coming months.

The hon. Gentleman also asked me about the position of the pilot scheme so far. He will appreciate that it has been running for only a few weeks, but the pilot scheme has been successfully launched, the relevant offices opened, the staff fully trained, the information technology system is functioning well and Image Col. 11 there have been no major operational problems so far. In the few days of the scheme's operation, there have been 4,000 responses and approximately 1,000 full applications with that number steadily rising.

The hon. Gentleman made a point about lifestyle—the asset-rich, income-poor fathers who by and large are seeking to avoid their liabilities are in the lead among the cases that have been referred under the departure scheme.

Mr. Steen: When my hon. Friend said that the staff had been trained, can he confirm that no additional staff are to be taken on as a result of the measure.

Mr. Mitchell: I am coming to the way in which the funding and staffing will be calculated in a moment if my hon. Friend will bear with me.

The CSA will be chasing about 300 cases to which it has been alerted either by the child support agency centres, the National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux, or Members of Parliament, through their constituency caseloads. Those cases appear to have special grounds for a departure and the CSA will pursue them.

I was asked about the way in which the information would be interpreted and used. That is a key function of the pilot scheme and we have let a research contract to interview people who have not responded to the approach on departures. That will help to assess how many are likely to apply when the scheme goes live across the country. It is important research that will help the effectiveness of the departure scheme.

The hon. Members for Croydon, North-West and for Rochdale asked about step-children. Where financial responsibility for supporting step-children was taken on before the existence of the CSA, it is reasonable to make allowance for costs. Anyone taking on a step-family after 1993 did so in full knowledge that the child support scheme would make no concessions to the additional cost. The departure system does nothing to alter that.

The first responsibility is to one's own children, even when step-children have no one to provide for them. When paying the standard formula assessment causes difficulty, however, the protected income calculation will ensure that sufficient disposable income is left after paying child support to meet the needs of all members of the household. The formula has always done that.

The hon. Gentleman asked about child care costs. I can tell him that child care is not an issue that has been raised with any frequency by clients of the CSA. That is because the formula already makes significant provision for child care costs through the inclusion in the maintenance requirement of the carer allowance. Where young children are in the family, it amounts to £48 per week. Similarly, parents with care returning to work and claiming family credit will get an allowance towards child care costs, which has been increased to £60 a week from April this year. That is a generous disregard.

Col. 12 The next point concerned travel-to-work. The majority of parents incur some cost in travelling to their place of employment. For those who face particularly high costs, there is already an allowance within the formula. However, the allowance is broad brush and might not accurately reflect the costs involved. For example, where a man lives and works on opposite sides of an estuary, the application of the rule, which requires the use of straight-line distances, is clearly to his disadvantage. He will, therefore, be able to apply for a departure direction based on the actual cost of his journey.

The hon. Member for Croydon, North-West next asked about appeals with one chairman only. We want to ensure that the independent tribunal service has the maximum degree of flexibility to manage that new area of work. The hon. Gentleman will appreciate that the regulations on appeal are not the subject of today's debate. They will be dealt with when the pilot scheme has been validated and we propose to go live. We will examine how the pilot scheme goes. We are interested in everyone's views on the matters and when we have made a decision, we shall return with appropriate regulations.

The final point made by the hon. Member for Croydon, North-West was to do with the level of costs—a point that was picked up by my hon. Friend the Member for South Hams. The departures scheme has a separate, ring-fenced budget which will be specifically tailored to the needs of the departures system.

Ms Lynne: The Minister says that the budget will be ring-fenced. Does that mean extra money on top of the money already being put into the Child Support Agency?

Mr. Mitchell: Yes, it does.

Mr. Steen: Before my hon. Friend leaves the issue, could we have some details of what the additional costs of the departure will be and why they are necessary? The Child Support Agency has an enormous staff, so surely it can move a few staff around.

Mr. Mitchell: My hon. Friend has touched on a most important point. That is precisely why we are discussing the pilot regulations today. The purpose of the pilot is to find out accurately how many staff we might need to service the departure system so that we do not waste public resources by not having an accurate assessment. When the pilot has been concluded—I hope at the end of July—we shall move to a firm scheme, going nationwide on about 1 December, and further regulations will come before Parliament and will need to be discussed. Those will be the departure regulations. At that point, as a result of the pilot scheme, I shall be able to give my hon. Friend very detailed costings and analysis. I hope that my hon. Friend wiil be on that Committee—I know that my hon. Friend the Member for Croydon, South (Mr. Ottaway), the Government Whip, is taking notice—so that he will be able to examine the regulations and determine their effectiveness and validity.

The departure system is designed to ensure that the key element of fairness is injected into those cases which cannot be adequately dealt with under the formula. The hon. Lady will recall that the all-party Select Committee did not recommend a departure system. We have introduced it because we have heard about cases with hard edges and are determined to address them.

Ms Lynne: I do not know whether the Minister will come on to this point, but I talked about the administrative nightmare that could be created by the departures. Piloting them will ensure that we avoid an administrative nightmare.

The hon. Lady's next point concerned the 14-day limit. I am happy to confirm that that will be reasonably applied. It is clear that there must be an element of discretion and common sense, and, indeed, there is.

Ms Lynne: Before the Minister leaves my remarks, perhaps he would answer my question.
