legislation Prisons and Young Offenders Institutions (Scotland) Amendment Rules 1966

10.30 am

Mr. Menzies Campbell (Fife, North-East): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Prisons and Young Offenders Institutions (Scotland) Amendment Rules 1996. (S.I., 1996, No. 32).

These important rules make changes to many aspects of the working procedures and practices in Scottish prisons. They include the introduction of random drug testing and alterations to the composition of prison visiting committees.

The instrument is subject to the negative resolution procedure and was laid on 15 January 1996. A prayer in the name of my hon. Friend the Member for Orkney and Shetland (Mr. Wallace) was tabled on 18 January, as a result of which the House decided on 13 February to refer the instrument to a Standing Committee. The statutory 40-day period for praying expired on 23 February, before a debate in Committee could be held. A motion for revocation, as opposed to annulment, was tabled by the official Opposition on 6 March, demonstrating the extent of concern about the content of the proposed changes.

Since the period of annulment has passed, our task is, by argument and any other means that we can muster, to persuade the Government that the rules are so flawed that they should be revoked. I am concerned in particular about the two issues which I have already mentioned—random drug testing and the changes made to the composition of prison visiting committees.

I shall illustrate my general observations on the changes to prison visiting committees by reference to the situation in the Borders, on which my hon. Friend the Member for Roxburgh and Berwick (Mr. Kirkwood) and my right hon. Friend the Member for Tweeddale, Ettrick and Lauderdale (Sir David Steel) have provided me with information and advice.

In the past, the rules governing membership of prison visiting committees allowed visitors to be drawn from the regions in which the principal prisons were located. Members from the Borders were allowed to serve on the visiting committee of Saughton prison in Edinburgh. That enabled Borders visitors to maintain connections with Borders people who were serving prison sentences there. It is well known that anyone sentenced in a Borders court is most likely to serve their term in Saughton.

There is abundant evidence that that opportunity was well used to the advantage of prisoners and their families. Several prominent councillors from the Col. 4 Borders distinguished themselves by the amount of time and energy that they devoted to maintaining a link with those from the Borders who found themselves in Saughton.

The Government have announced a change in policy, restricting the membership of prison visiting committees to those from the local authority areas in which a prison is situated. The Saughton prison visitors committee is now to be drawn from Edinburgh district and the region currently known as Lothian—that name is due to change on 1 April. Those in the Borders who have been affected by the change are disappointed. A subsequent change in policy means that, after 1 April, visitors on the committee for Saughton prison will be drawn from east Lothian and west Lothian, but not from the Borders.

I have used the link between Saughton and the Borders as an example, but such circumstances may well be repeated in other areas, with equally undesirable results. I therefore ask the Minister to reconsider the restrictive and unhelpful rule changes and to allow links with geographical areas and prisons to be maintained as they have been in the past.

I want to address my remarks primarily to the issue of mandatory drug testing. You may remember, Mr. Maxton, that we briefly touched on that matter on 15 January in the Grand Committee. My hon. Friend the Member for Orkney and Shetland intervened on the Secretary of State's speech. It is fair to say that for some of us the right hon. Gentleman's replies were inadequate. Indeed, there was more than a flavour of that most dangerous political motivation that one must be seen to be taking tough action irrespective of the sense of such action. On that basis, the original prayer against the statutory instrument was tabled so that the important change to mandatory drug testing could have proper parliamentary scrutiny.

It may help if I briefly outline the legislative history of this matter and how it comes to be before us this morning. Rule 88A makes provision for compulsory testing for controlled drugs. It derives its statutory warrant from section 151(2) of the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994 which inserts into the Prisons (Scotland) Act 1989 new section 41B. The rule

"requires a prisoner to provide a sample for the purpose of ascertaining whether he has any controlled drug in his body."

According to the rule, a sample will usually be a urine sample. But the 1994 Act and the rules curiously provide for what is described as a sample of any other description. That excludes a sample of what is described, rather archly perhaps, as a sample of an intimate nature, which is described in new section 41B(3) as blood, semen, saliva, pubic hair or a swab taken from any orifice. Where urine samples are specified and other samples of an intimate nature are excluded, but a further unspecified sample may be sought, we are entitled to ask the Minister what the other sample is and in what circumstances it may be required from a prisoner.

That is not simply a matter of the anxiety of lawyers or Opposition Members of Parliament to expose what they believe may be a lacuna or an unnecessary power in the legislation. Refusal to give a sample may lead to Image Col. 5 disciplinary proceedings, which is clearly stated in schedule 3. That is of considerable importance to a number of professionals operating within the drug testing field.

In a letter dated 14th September 1995, Dr. Bird, Dr. Sheila Gore and a significant number of medical and scientific co-signatories, wrote to the Secretary of State expressing concern about the introduction of mandatory drug testing in Scottish prisons. That letter came from the Centre for HIV Research in West Maines Road, Edinburgh, which is a division of the Medical Research Council, and is therefore written by people with considerable experience with the advantage of detailed information and understanding of what is involved.

The distinguished signatories raised several concerns. They referred first to the betrayal of prisoners' trust with regard to previous prison researchers; secondly, to the undermining of previous evidence based policies for prisoners and public health; thirdly, to a compromise of current schemes involving voluntary referral to in-prison agencies for alcohol support and methadone programmes, and, fourthly, to the excessive cost of mandatory drug testing, estimated by some as being twice what is required from an appropriate public health response to an already identified problem of injection drug use in Scottish prisons. Finally, they referred to the compromising of future co-operative participation in monitoring the prevalence of blood-borne viruses and risk behaviours in prisons.

The letter is written in robust language and two concluding paragraphs in particular warrant careful consideration by the Committee and the Government. The signatories say:

"We are concerned that mandatory drug testing significantly compromises the future of the innovative, widely disseminated, successful and public health-orientated research initiatives—in addition to WASH surveillance—"

—That is, willing anonymous salivary HIV surveillance—

"that have been pioneered, and until now acted upon, in the Scottish Prison Service."

Essentially, the signatories are saying that there are worthwhile programmes in operation but their anxiety, as those directly concerned with the implementation of the programmes, is that they might be compromised.

The letter continues:

"Considerable hidden costs will be incurred by the mandatory scheme because of disciplinary action that results for inmates who refuse or whose urine tests positive for cannabis let alone for Class A drugs. Mandatory testing diverts scarce resources away from the expansion and evaluation of drug education and rehabilitation initiatives which are so critical for the prevention of further outbreaks of blood-borne virus infection in prisons, and whose effect on recidivism should be formally assessed."

That is strong stuff and bears weight because those who signed the letter are distinguished in their field and, undoubtedly, have extensive direct experience of the issues.

Col. 6 A response to that letter, on behalf of the Scottish Prison Service, was written by Mr. Stewart, who is director of strategy and corporate affairs. Among other things, he said:

"Mandatory drug testing will also complement the constructive work that is being done in the context of reduction and detoxification."

Significantly, he then goes on to say:

"The Secretary of State agrees that drug misusers need support to enable them to recover from their addiction."

As a general statement of policy, I doubt whether any member of the Committee, or even anyone in the country, would argue with that but, in the way in which it is stated, it opens up an interesting issue. If the Secretary of State believes that drug misusers need support to enable them to recover from their addiction, it is essential that the Government spell out in advance what the consequences will be of detecting drug misuse. Will a positive sample lead inevitably to disciplinary action or will it lead to support being given to the prisoner? If support will be given, we should know what the nature of that support will be and what resources will be made available in every Scottish prison for that support to be delivered. Those are fundamental questions which go to the heart of the policy of mandatory drug testing.

I have no doubt that the Government will have drawn upon the experience of England and Wales, where there has been mandatory drug testing since February 1995. It does the Government no injustice to say that the introduction of mandatory drug testing in England and Wales was, to use somewhat emotive language, part of the war on drugs. If that was its purpose, however, the experience since February 1995 is inconclusive as to the success of the policy.

Initial figures released by the Home Office give cause for some alarm. I shall read out some statistical information for the benefit of the Committee. During the period February to May 1995, 1,089 random tests were carried out in prisons in England and Wales. Of those, 362, or 33.2 per cent., were positive for cannabis, and 44, or 4.1 per cent., were positive for opiates or benzodiazepines. From June to December 1995, 2,282 random tests were carried out. Of those, 663, or 29.1 per cent., were positive for cannabis and 168, or 7.4 per cent., were positive for opiates and benzodiazepines.

That analysis suggests that there was a movement away from cannabis and towards opiates and benzodiazepines.

That analysis suggests that there was a movement away from cannabis and towards opiates and benzodiazepines. Support for that view can be found in the evidence, no doubt based on experience, that was cited in an article in The Independent on 6 February 1996. The article was written by the crime correspondent of that newspaper and appeared under the headline:

"Prisoners use heroin to thwart drug tests".

That is a rather curious headline, but it is justified by the article. The article stated that the

"chairman of Dartmoor Board of Visitors said the switch was taking place"—

Image Col. 7 that is, from cannabis to heroin—

"because traces of cannabis can remain in the bloodstream for up to a month compared to about a day for the harder narcotics. He predicted that the change would lead to more inmates becoming drug addicts."

The chairman, whom we can invest, if not with infallibility, at least with the ability to make informed criticism and comment, goes on to say:

"'It's all because of the introduction of mandatory drugs testing. Inmates know that cannabis lasts about a month in the system. It is no longer flavour of the month because it is so easily detectable.'"

The chairman of the Prison Officers Association at Dartmoor, Mr. Horn

"confirmed there had been a rise in heroin and cocain seizures. `Inmates have said that cannabis lasts longer in the body—they know the score, they are not idiots'".

A spokesman for the Prison Service denied that there was any connection between those factors, as might have been expected. Yet that is powerful anecdotal evidence in support and corroboration of the statistical evidence that I have quoted. The article also quoted a spokesman for the national Prison Officers Association, who admitted—in terms that might give the Minister more comfort than they give me—that:

"it was extremely difficult to be certain about drug trends because of the difficulty of getting reliable evidence.


But a spokesman said, 'The mandatory testing on its own is not going to improve things without proper after-treatment. It will merely tell us what we already know—that a lot of prisoners take drugs.'"

There we have the experience in one of the prisons in England and Wales supporting the statistical trend identified in the Home Office figures. Those matters should give cause for concern before we embark on such a programme of testing in Scotland. There is a possibility that mandatory drug testing will lead prisoners to switch from cannabis, which has a half-life in the body of some 14 days, to heroin, which has a half life of three days, in an effort to avoid detection. Heroin misuse is significant because it raises the attendant problems of shared needles and the possible spread of HIV and hepatitis.

I want to ask the Minister today what scientific evaluation will be made of the results of random testing in Scotland if the rules pass into law, what study has been made of the figures from English prisons, and what evaluation has been made of the possibility of prisoners switching from cannabis to class A drugs in the light of the anecdotal evidence from Dartmoor to which I referred? If no such evaluation takes place, we are entitled to raise the possibility that the Government are pursuing a policy for public relations purposes without adequate attention to the effect on prisoners or public health in general.

The Secretary of State was challenged on those matters by Doctors Gore and Bird who have been assiduous in putting forward the point of view to which I referred. In their letter of 5 March 1996 he was asked what proposals he had concerning those issues, Col. 8 first, for evaluation and, secondly, in relation to the experience in England. As yet, no response has been made to that letter.

Mandatory drug testing has an attraction because it fills a gap in people's minds. They tell themselves that something must be done about drug-taking in prisons and derive some comfort from the fact that the regime will be more rigorous with an element of compulsion because they believe that good is bound to flow.

Sir Andrew Bowden (Brighton, Kemptown): I have listened carefully to the hon. and learned Gentleman and have learnt a lot, but if the mandatory power is in the hands of the prison authorities and they wish to test someone, for whatever reason, perhaps because they believe that he is a serious drug addict, they can compel him to take a test. If that power did not exist, would there be nothing to compel that person to take the test? Would it be for the Prison Service to decide how such a test is used?

Mr. Menzies Campbell: The hon. Gentleman is quite right. If there is no mandatory power, there is no compulsion. The point of Drs. Bird and Gore is that there has been much success with a voluntary system, and those who are concerned with the issue of HIV have achieved remarkable success by creating an atmosphere of confidence in which both doctors and prisoners can rely on each other.

I thought for a moment that the hon. Gentleman was going to argue that the mere existence of the power might operate as a deterrent. There is not much evidence of that and I am one of those who, based on my professional experience, some of which involved prosecution in the High Court in Scotland, believe that the deterrence of punishment is rarely as effective as the deterrence of apprehension and conviction. It is a remote possibility that the threat of disciplinary consequences for drug addicts in prisons if they are found to have ingested drugs following a mandatory test will stop them doing so again. If that were so, we would see some evidence of that outside prisons. We impose higher sentences in Scotland for possession and possession with intent to supply than in the rest of the United Kingdom and much higher sentences than in many other parts of Europe, but the incidence of drug taking does not appear to have been favourably affected by such a policy.

The prison visiting committees, to which I referred rather briefly, and, perhaps more significantly, the question of mandatory drug testing, should be discussed widely by the Committee. We deserve to have a clearer account of the Government's policies towards those two matters than we have been given so far.

Mr. John McFall (Dumbarton): First, I congratulate the hon. and learned Member for Fife, North-East (Mr. Campbell) on the way in which he moved the statutory instrument. This is an unsatisfactory way of changing the working practices and procedures in prisons.

Image Col. 9 I sat recently on the Committee that considered the Licensing (Amendment) (Scotland) Bill. That Committee took evidence about raves in Scotland. As a result of those representations and the co-operation of the Minister of State, Scottish Office, we were able to modify that three-clause Bill. One new clause of the three that we had tabled was adopted. The original drafting of the Bill suggests how out of touch the Government, and perhaps Parliament, were. When representations were heard about the Bill the Government agreed to modify it, on which I congratulate them. The statutory instrument contains rules and regulations that will interfere in many ways with the workings of the prison system, but we have been given only an hour and a half to discuss them.

The hon. Member for Brighton, Kemptown (Sir A. Bowden) mentioned mandatory drug testing. During the passage of the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1984 the Opposition moved an amendment that would require a governor who applied for judicial sanction to authorise a drug test, but the Government rejected the amendment. My experience, and, I am sure, that of many other members of the Committee, from visiting prisons and talking to governors is that the prison system can work only with co-operation and when incentives are provided. Co-operation should be at the heart of the matter. Mandatory drug testing is a serious matter, and the Government may apply for the relevant judicial sanctions. We will consider supporting them in that regard.

The hon. and learned Member for Fife, North-East discussed restrictive and unhelpful rules. If they did, the Col. 10 Government's policies would result in fewer people in them, but ever more people are going to prison. Prison obviously does not work. When the Secretary of State produced "Making the Punishment Fit the Crime", I asked what consultation he had undertaken. The resounding answer was that there had been no consultation. That is the most deeply worrying aspect of the issue.

I shall not go over the pertinent material produced by the hon. and learned Member for Fife, North-East, but rather consider the Government's proposals to make the punishment fit the crime. They intend to abolish parole. That will leave prisoners with no incentive. A contemporary consideration of drug testing in our prisons compared with practices elsewhere shows that incentives are necessary. We are not proposing going that far, but the present system takes that right away. Rule 88A, to which the hon. and learned Gentleman referred, introduces sanctions for a refusal to provide a sample, but with parole taken away, no incentives are left for prisoners. The mandatory drug testing regime makes chaos in prisons more likely, whereas there is relative tranquillity at the moment.

We should not ignore the comments of Dr. Sheila Gore and others at the Medical Research Council, who have written to us on the issue during the past few months. Their aim is to reduce drug taking by people in prison and to make prison regimes more tranquil. I am keen that the Minister should tell us what advice he has taken. If we do not follow good advice, we will have very disturbed prison regimes.

There is a danger that there will be minimal education and counselling to help people away from drug taking. The Secretary of State is giving us nothing more than rhetoric, doing an injustice to prison officers and governors who are trying to operate regimes conducive to good behaviour and fulfilment.

The diversion of scarce resources is an important issue. The Secretary of State has said that he is keen on private prisons. He told members of the Scottish Prison Officers Association that he would consider them for the management of such prisons. God only knows how that fits with the private finance initiative. The Opposition will not vote against the rules because there is no sense in doing so after such a short debate. We need a longer debate in order to consider all the issues. However, I plead with the Minister, for goodness sake, to consult governors and prisoner officers and then tell the House what progress has been made. The comments of the Medical Research Council Image Col. 11 were made in good faith by experienced people. It is sad that they are not being taken up. 11.6 am

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton): We have had an interesting debate. As hon. Members concentrated their remarks on visiting committees and mandatory drug testing, I shall address those subjects.

The hon. Member for Dumbarton (Mr. McFall), raised the issue of early release. That has nothing to do with the rules before the Committee. We are consulting on the proposals, and the outcomes will be laid before Ministers shortly for consideration. It is too early to say what the consultation exercise will reveal, but it has been extensive and it is certain that we will come back to the House.

Mr. McFall: Although the proposals are not directly relevant, does the Minister agree that prison regimes have to be co-operative and harmonious? If there are no incentives for prisoners, problems occur. The need for mandatory drug testing and incentives for prisoners is important. Does the Minister agree?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: It is important to establish a form of mandatory drug testing that will work effectively, and to be aware of potential problems and different circumstances.

Many points have been raised, and I shall address the subject of mandatory drug testing first. The hon. Member for Dumbarton raised the question of support programmes. Such programmes are very important, and I have seen one in progress at Saughton prison which was extremely effective. The appropriate support programmes will be put in place once the scheme is up and running throughout the Scottish prison estate.

The hon. and learned Member for Fife, North East raised the Bird and Gore concerns with regard to research. I confirm that the costs of mandatory drug testing are currently being assessed. There has not yet been any indication of loss of prisoners' co-operation. The results are to be assessed systematically with the overall target of reducing the prevalence of positive tests. The operations meeting is taking place weekly, and is monitoring the results. The meeting is chaired by the chief executive of the Scottish Prison Service.

Col. 12 Rehabilitation is extremely important and will go ahead. Increased emphasis will be placed on support programmes. On its own, mandatory drug testing cannot solve the problems associated with drugs in prisons, but it can contribute to the overall objective of reducing drug misuse as part of the Scottish Prison Service's wider drugs strategy. Mandatory drug testing in Scotland is to be introduced on a phased basis when establishments have appropriate support programmes in place to assist prisoners with drug problems.

The first phase of testing has already begun in Edinburgh and Cornton Vale prisons. Edinburgh was chosen because it is best placed to provide support to drug-misusing prisoners—it has the longest established drug-reduction programme. Cornton Vale has a full-time addiction worker, and a formal drug-reduction programme is about to begin to supplement existing treatment provided to prisoners with drug problems.

Drug testing will send a clear message to all prisoners that if they misuse drugs, the likelihood of that being detected will be much greater. An increased possibility of detection will help prisoners to resist the peer pressure often placed on them to become involved in drugs. Mandatory drug testing will help to identify all those prisoners who may need assistance to combat their drug problems, and appropriate support will be offered to individual prisoners. It will provide accurate and objective information on the scale and the trends of drug misuse, allowing prisons to manage and target more effectively their resources for tackling drug problems.

Mr. Menzies Campbell: The Minister said that in Cornton Vale someone has been appointed specifically to deal with addiction. Are there similar appointments in all other Scottish prisons, or is it an advantage enjoyed only by Cornton Vale?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: So far, there is a full-time addiction worker only in Cornton Vale. Saughton has an extremely good medical unit which gives the best possible advice to prisoners who were on drugs before entering prison and who have a problem while there.

All categories of prisoners are liable to be tested. Some 10 per cent. of prisoners will be chosen by a computer on a random basis. If a prisoner tests positive, he or she will be charged with a breach of discipline under the prison rules and could be liable to the usual range of disciplinary action in prison, including an award of additional days, loss of privileges and loss of earnings. He or she will be offered assistance to refrain from drug misuse which might range from provision of information to a place on a drug-reduction programme, depending on the needs of the individual. If a prisoner refuses to provide a sample, the amendments to the prison rules make that a disciplinary offence.

The hon. and learned Member for Fife, North-East raised the issue of what drugs should be included. A wide range of illegal drugs, including cannabis, will be covered and the list will be reviewed periodically. The move towards more sophisticated drugs is based on Image Col. 13 early data from the English Prison Service. It appears that such drugs are present in urine for a shorter time. In the long term, we believe that mandatory drug testing will help eradicate drugs in prisons.

The hon. and learned Gentleman mentioned samples. Those are analysed by an independent laboratory. If a prisoner disputes the result of a screening test, the sample will be given a confirmation test using gas chromatography and mass spectrometry. I have no doubt that the hon. and learned Gentleman will be an expert on such matters having dealt with them in court. Studies are taking place to determine whether that will be more reliable than urine, but as yet no firm conclusion has been reached.

I do not regard mandatory drug testing as a betrayal of prisoners' trust. All research on drugs in prisons was conducted anonymously and no prisoner was or will be targeted because of it. Mandatory drug testing will complement the Scottish Prison Service's challenge to drugs. Drug-reduction programmes are being steadily expanded, not reduced.

We have general measures to stop drugs getting into prison, including closed circuit television, surveillance of visits, searching, use of sniffer dogs and close, regular liaison with the police and other agencies. As for medical treatment, when a prisoner on a methadone maintenance programme is received into a prison on a short sentence or on remand, the prison medical officer, in consultation with the prisoner and the doctor responsible for prescribing for the prisoner when he was in the community, considers whether such a programme should continue. All prisoners receive a comprehensive information and training programme on HIV and AIDS within two weeks of admission. A refreshing reality is that the number suffering from AIDS, certainly in Saughton prison which had a high incidence of AIDS cases, has decreased. We shall make an assessment of the scheme and are considering how best to publish the results.

The hon. and learned Member for Fife, North-East referred, on behalf of his hon. Friend the Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire, to visiting committees. There has been consultation on the matter. The Convention of Scottish Local Authorities accepted the change in the rules, as did the Association of Visiting Committees for Scottish Penal Establishments, because in the past many of those on visiting committees did not attend, the committees were large and unwieldy and a change was called for. Scottish Borders is the only council to have made representations. Someone from the region currently chairs the Edinburgh visiting committee and because one third of the membership must come from outwith the elected membership, it will be open to Scottish Borders to operate services again when prison visiting committees are reconstituted under the amendment rules. It was generally considered that the change in the rules concerning visiting committees should go ahead.

I recommend the rules to the Committee. There are many of them but hon. Members have not raised many points and I need not weary the Committee with details that are non-contentious and simply bring the Col. 14 rules up to date. We have discussed mandatory drug testing, but I should also mention that there is a new complaints procedure. The 1994 complaints system provides, as foreseen in the justice charter, for an independent element through the creation of a Scottish prisons complaints administrator. I instituted a survey of prisoners for the first time in Britain. It was a successful project and a thorough analysis was made of the points raised by prisoners. One was that they wanted better circumstances for family visits and we considered what could usefully be done to satisfy prisoners without infringing discipline and order in prisons and to create a better working relationship between prisoners and prison officers.

The Scottish prisons complaints commissioner will be involved in internal procedures following referral to the Scottish Prison Service headquarters. Internal procedures and referrals must take place before the commissioner becomes involved. In the past that proved lengthy and unwieldy and the headquarters stage has now been removed from the complaints process. Once prisoners have exhausted the available internal avenues in the prison concerned, they can make their complaints direct to the complaints commissioner. Similarly, if they have complaints about orderly room decisions, they no longer need to make those to the Scottish Prison Service headquarters before approaching the commissioner. The commissioner will deal with them and make his recommendations to the chief executive. Nothing in that change should give hon. Members cause for concern. Prisoners will have swifter resolution of their complaints and improvement in their situation.

The other significant policy changes include temporary release with home leave to help to smooth reintegration into family life. That will be subject to strict criteria and must be earned by good behaviour. Other changes are so relatively minor that I need not go into detail.

I have covered the main issues raised by hon. Members. We shall monitor the situation closely in relation to mandatory drug testing, but there have been serious criticisms that far too many drugs have been getting into prisons and we felt it necessary to act in the public interest.
