Draft Stansted Airport Aircraft Movement Limit (Amendment) Order 1996

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Transport (Mr. Steve Norris)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Stansted Airport Aircraft Movement Limit (Amendment) Order 1996.

Stansted is the United Kingdom and Europe's fastest growing international airport and has been so for the past four years. Forty one scheduled destinations are served by direct flights from Stansted operated by 13 carriers. Air UK, a British airline, is the biggest operator there, with 20 routes, mainly to European destinations. The airport is extremely popular and the new terminal has won more than 25 awards for design, passenger comfort and environmental friendliness.

Stansted has outline planning permission for about 15 million passengers per annum, but when that permission was given it was decided that development should be phased in line with the rate of growth of demand and the development of the associated infrastructure. The first phase allowed development for up to 8 million passengers per annum—known as mppa.

If the draft order is approved, it will enable Stansted airport to reach an annual throughput of 8 mppa and thus to realise the full potential of the first phase of development. The order amends the existing Stansted Airport Aircraft Movement Limit Order 1987 by raising the air transport movement—ATM—limit there from 78,000 movements to 120,000 movements per annum.

The reason for the order is straightforward. In 1987, the purpose of the ATM limit was to support the planning condition for development of the first phase, up to 8 mppa. The key element in converting passenger throughput into a movements limit was a forecast of how aircraft loadings would develop. The ATM limit of 78,000 was calculated by comparing passenger loading with Gatwick airport and drawing a parallel that might operate at Stansted when that airport was handling 8 million passengers.

The science of aircraft loading is, however, inexact. The limit of 78,000 movements per annum adopted in 1987 assumed average loadings of about 100 passengers per aircraft. In fact, the airlines operating from Stansted use smaller aircraft than anticipated, so the average number of passengers has been rather lower. Current throughput is about 4 million passengers per annum and commercial passenger aircraft movements number just over 57,000. That means that the current ATM limit of 78,000 equates to 4 just over 5 million passengers per annum, which is likely to be reached by 1997. In addition, the number of movements at the airport means that stand space on the existing two satellites will become increasingly congested, and the development of a third satellite will soon be required.

In the light of those circumstances, the British Airports Authority applied to the Secretary of State for Transport in July 1995 to raise the ATM limit at Stansted from 78,000 to 150,000. The BAA assumed that 150,000 movements would be reached in about 2008, and envisaged that that would be the equivalent of 15,000 mppa. The Secretary of State consulted on that and a lower limit of 120,000 movements per year was agreed.

That lower limit was calculated by adjusting the existing ATM limit upward to take account of the lower than expected average aircraft loading, but retaining the link to 8 million passengers per annum, which was the core of the original agreement. On the basis of the latest information about passenger loading, an ATM limit of 120,000 would enable Stansted to achieve a throughput of 8 million passengers a year.

A total of 70 responses to the consultation exercise were received from a variety of bodies, including aviation groups, local authorities, district and parish councils and environmental groups. The responses were an important part of the decision-making process, and when all the factors had been carefully considered the Government decided to recommend to the House a revised limit of not 150,000, but 120,000 movements. That limit will meet the concern expressed by local authorities that the development of Stansted should be phased with the development of local infrastructure. It is also consistent with the conclusions of the planning inquiry that the timing of the further development of Stansted should depend on the rate of growth of demand, but it will allow the BAA to continue to develop terminal facilities as business grows.

The draft amending order contains only two articles. The first determines when the order will come into effect, and the second substitutes the figure of 120,000 for the figure of 78,000 in article 2 of the 1987 order.

I know that several members of the Committee participated in the 1987 debate, when the original order was made. I think in particular of my hon. Friend the Member for Saffron Walden (Sir A. Haselhurst), whose constituency interests have led him to take an extremely active part in matters regarding Stansted Airport, and of my hon. Friend the Member for North-West Hampshire (Sir D. Mitchell), who, as Minister of State at the Department of Transport, led for the Government in that debate—I am delighted that he has found the time to be with us today. On that occasion—a rather late night, as I recall—the House approved the order because it supported the Government's policy of phasing the development of Stansted Airport in line with the growth in demand.

JPEG 5 That is essentially the issue before us today. The revised limit of 120,000 movements will ensure that future development continues to be phased, but also gives the BAA sufficient assurance to proceed with the necessary investment. On that basis, I commend the order to the Committee.

Sir Alan Haselhurst
(Saffron Walden)

The order affects my constituency more than any other, although neighbouring constituencies are affected to some degree. However, it has aroused so much concern because the ATM limit introduced in 1987 was the only protection given to local people. My hon. Friend the Member for North-West Hampshire, when he was Minister, said that the

additional to the phasing of the airport at 8 million passengers a year—

"of regulating airport development, with the object of controlling the impact of that development on local infrastructure."—[ Official Report, 28 April 1987; Vol. 115, c. 263.]

My hon. Friend the Minister referred to the infrastructure keeping pace with the development of the airport. I suspect that the lively response to the consultation was the result of people's experience over the past nine years, which has caused them to doubt whether the infrastructure has kept pace with the development of the airport. It is hardly surprising that most of the official responses to the consultation suggested that ATMs should be limited to 100,000 a year, and some, including the Council for the Protection of Rural Essex, said that there should be no increase.

Certain benefits were supposed to flow from the decision to allow the major development of Stansted. There have been benefits in employment, and many people find it increasingly convenient to travel from Stansted airport. Those factors are on the plus side, but there is a negative side. The understanding, shared by the inspector who chaired the airport inquiry, was that the rail service to the airport would be enhanced. However, apart from the building of a spur into the airport, no increase in capacity on that line has been achieved. Indeed, the north-bound spur was built but the service has now been reduced to one train a week. That is no service at all—it is a ghost train, and known as a parliamentary train.

We have seen no improvement in the rail service; rather, those who commute or use stations from north of the junction into the airport have seen the service to Liverpool Street station deteriorate. The airport train service is fine for air passengers, but it is not much good for those who work at the airport because the trains are fast, stopping at few places. They do not stop at intermediate stations to collect the growing number of the airport's employees. The failure to do that inevitably increases the pressure for more housing to 6 be provided in the immediate environs of the airport. Those of us who value the rural nature of north-west Essex want to resist that pressure. So no promises on the rail service have been delivered.

On the question of the road improvements, it was understood early on that there should be a trunked and upgraded A120 running eastward from the M11, to the airport, and on to Braintree and the Al2indeed, BAA, the airport operating company, provided resources towards it. BAA has managed to build, at a cost of £436 million, a splendid new airport terminal with all the ancillary facilities, but the first sod has still not been turned on the A120. That is a hopeless mismatch of infrastructure in this major airport development. The fact that that improvement has not taken place is convenient for no one—whether connected to the airport, or trying to live ordinary lives around it. I should like assurances that a continued standstill on the road infrastructure will not accompany the increase in the ceiling on movements at the airport.

The third point I want to mention is BAA's extremely poor performance on compensating people for the loss of value of their properties. There is statutory right to compensation under the Land Compensation Act 1973. Claims are permissible up to 1998, but the way in which they are being dealt with defies all analysis; it is being done in an extremely slow, curmudgeonly way. All those people know that Parliament is considering an increase in the number of flight movements into Stansted, thus further adding to their woes—not that noise disturbance is the key thing in their minds—yet the process of compensation is falling miserably behind.

I hope that some pressure will be applied on the airport operator to improve its performance fulfilling its statutory function. Otherwise, it will be unfair for my constituents to see an increase in activity at the airport, but no commensurate increase in the pace at which their compensation is being paid. When one considers that there has been no improvement in the rail or road infrastructures and a niggardly performance on compensation, it is no wonder that there has been resistance to the proposal that the ATM limit should be increased.

One element of my constituents' suspicion is not perhaps justified and that is the fear that, if the Committee and the other place approve the order, an increase in the limit will automatically produce more business. Of course, there is no direct link between the ceiling on the number of passenger movements and the actual number of passengers who come through the airport doors the following day, week or month. However, the fact that an increase is signalled will inevitably heighten concerns about the additional number of night flights that might be expected. I hope that my hon. Friend the Minister will recognise the fact that, although fewer people live in the countryside around the airport, the disturbance of night flights is that much greater in the peace of the countryside than elsewhere. We need to be tolerant of the fact that there might be an increase in the number of night flights as a consequence of the proposal.

JPEG 7 Having relayed those fears to the Committee on behalf of my constituents, I hope that I shall receive a response from the Minister. I am glad that, after consultation, the Government have settled on the lower of the two figures that they proposed as the basis for consultation and that they are demonstrably saying that the order corrects the ATM figure in order to equate with their original commitment to limit the development of the airport to eight million passengers per annum.

I dare say that my hon. Friend the Member for North-West Hampshire and I will exchange the odd wry smile over the debate that took place in the House in 1987. I jousted with him about whether the figure that he put before the House on that occasion was the appropriate one. It is not often that we score a point against a Minister of the Crown, but history shows that it was game, set and match to me on that occasion. It also underlines how cautious we must be when making assumptions about the number of passengers on any particular flight and the number of flights that make up the total throughput of an airport.

Experience shows that the figure of 78,000 air movements is inadequate. Although the figure of 120,000 might be frightening to some, it is perfectly justifiable. I justify it simply on the basis that, as far as one can predict, the airport services that will use Stansted in the next few years are unlikely to be much different from those that have been using it since the new terminal opened in 1991. It is perfectly reasonable to predict that the number of passengers per flight will be in the region of 68 to 70. Therefore, to arrive at a figure of about eight million passengers per annum, one must assume that there will be 120,000 flights. Even though a lower figure may be more comforting to my constituents, I do not think that there is a better figure given what we know about the airport's likely development.

On a topical point, if the alliance proposed yesterday between British Airways and American Airlines is approved by both Governments, it is almost certain to lead to a further concentration of flights at Heathrow. That makes it even less likely that the pattern and type of flights coming to Stansted will alter significantly in the next few years.

I believe that the Government have adopted a reasonable position in proposing the figure of 120,000 and I shall give the order my support.

They want to make their businesses profitable, and the most profitable activity is to use capacity where new markets exist to try to gain additional revenues from the flow of additional passengers.

My hon. Friend the Member for Saffron Walden raised several issues about the airport, the first of which concerned night flights, which were also mentioned by the hon. Member for Nottingham, North. There is a night flight quota at Stansted, as there is at Heathrow and Gatwick and it is varied for winter and summer. The ATM limit that we are establishing will limit all qualifying movements at the airport. It will in no way undermine the limits imposed by the night flying restrictions. There is some capacity within the night flying limits which has not been taken up and therefore technically could be, but I want to emphasise the point—pertinently made by my hon. Friend—that raising the limit would not affect a single flight. Flights are not generated merely by raising the limit. As my hon. Friend knows, in practice, the volume of night flights flow from the type of business that is being generated by Stansted airport. The limit remains in place and is not affected by the order.

As for land compensation, I hope that my hon. Friend will accept that it would be inappropriate for me to comment on individual claims. The legal position is clear that it is for BAA to deal with such issues under part I of the Land Compensation Act 1973. I am equally concerned to learn that there has been a delay in settling applicants' claims. I shall pass that information on to my right hon. Friend the JPEG 15 Secretary of State who meets BAA from time to time. I am sure that the Committee sympathises with my hon. Friends constituents. It is important that, where a liability exists in a case such as this, it is adequately and promptly discharged. However, I shall not comment on individual aspects of that because it is very much a commercial matter.

With your indulgence, Mr. Cook, we have dealt with the four substantive issues raised by my hon. Friend which worry him and his constituents. I underline what he was kind enough to point out. The new limit takes effect as a result of the different background to calculating air transport movements. In other words, capacity per flight is rather less than was originally anticipated in 1987. That fate will not befall me on the basis of any prognosis I may have made this afternoon. I notice that my hon. Friend the Member for North-West Hampshire has left the Committee. I judge that to be a prescient move on his behalf. I commend the order to the Committee.
