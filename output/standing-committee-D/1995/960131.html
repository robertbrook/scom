On the question of how to improve the licensing arrangements for HGV or PSV licence holders. I have to say that, although such licences are not, strictly speaking, the subject of the regulations before the Committee, traffic commissioners are responsible for granting PSV operators licences and for enforcing the "good repute" requirement. That requirement relates not only to operators, but to PSV licence holders.

It is an offence for any driver to fail to declare relevant convictions when applying for a PSV licence. The police are statutory objectors and have the right to object to any licence if they have any reason to believe that the person concerned has illegally withheld information or not presented wholly accurate information.

I emphasise that any applicant for an operator's licence must declare any relevant convictions that he or any of his employees—I stress employees—have had in the past five years. The names of all applicants are published fortnightly in notices and proceedings in advance of the grant of a licence. That allows police to take up their statutory rights to object. The first of the two central points made by the hon. Member for Nottingham, North was that we should continue our serious efforts to improve the flow of information to traffic commissioners from courts and the Vehicle Inspectorate. I will not reiterate the debate that took place in this Room a week ago, but the hon. Gentleman must not underestimate the serious technical difficulties implied by that undertaking. A vast array of offences is involved. Simply, a person may be convicted of a serious sexual offence while he was working as a bank clerk and make a subsequent false declaration to obtain a PSV licence. No system related to information from courts will be able to pick that up retrospectively. It will be identified only when that person comes to the attention of the authorities. It would be entirely regrettable if he did so only when a further offence had been committed.

I agree that the hon. Gentleman's second point is important. He asked whether it would be practical to extend to the PSV licensing process the process of automatic checking with the police national computer. It is technically possible, but I am sure that the hon. Gentleman shares my concern not to underestimate the technical and logistical difficulties that that would involve. The Association of Chief Police Officers will certainly advise him of those difficulties. However, I assure him that I am keen to explore all available avenues to ensure that people with inappropriate criminal convictions are not put in positions in which others are at risk. I pray in aid of that commitment my own record over the years on PHV licensing in the City of London. As the hon. Gentleman knows, I have made the point, in concert with the Suzy Lamplugh Trust and others, over many years, that such checks are not currently available. It is regrettable that trade unions, including the Transport and General Workers Union, have not seen the value of that proposal and have hindered attempts to bring it into being by use of a Private Member's Bill or another method.

I accept that the hon. Gentleman's desire to eliminate risk is sincere. I, too, take that issue seriously and shall do what I can to ensure that any mechanism Image Col. 15 that can improve the traffic commissioners' ability to take effective measures to check the quality of individual applicants, and their criminal convictions, will be introduced.
