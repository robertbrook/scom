Clause 78
RELIEF FOR POST-CESSATION EXPENDITURE

Ms Hilary Armstrong
(Durham, North-West)
: I beg to move amendment No. 247, in page 78, line 34, after "tax" insert "at the basic rate".

When you called our amendment, Mr. Winterton, I was enjoying a discussion with the Financial Secretary about the really interesting business we should reach next week on self-assessment—although you, Mr. Winterton, might not think that that is the most exciting business to Chair.

Clause 78 attempts to correct a long-standing anomaly. Our amendment is a probing one to clarify what the Government expect the clause to cost. We will be discussing four clauses this morning that deal with tax reliefs. Opposition Members have been cautious to ensure that the Government do not allow any tax relief to turn quickly into a means of abuse and a tax loophole. Post-cessation receipts have been discussed in previous Finance Bills. However, this year we have a whole raft of new proposals. The anomaly addressed by the clause has been recognised by the Committee previously.

If it is appropriate, I should like to ask several questions, some of which are directly related to the amendment and some of which are more general. Why is the relief to be made available only to individuals and not to companies? Why is it applicable only to income tax and not to corporation tax? Why is it restricted to setting expenditure against income and capital gains in the year in which it is to be paid? We have received representations that that is inconsistent with the provisions regarding losses which, for individuals under self-assessment, can be carried back and set against income in the previous year of assessment.

Several groups, including the Faculty of Taxation, the Chartered Institute of Taxation and the Institute of Chartered Accountants in Scotland, have told us that the seven-year limit is inadequate. I want to make it clear that the questions I am asking have been put to us. I am not sure that the Opposition would support all the submissions, but I should like the Minister to deal with them. The groups explained that a person normally has six years in which to make a claim and that any payment is made several years after that claim is received. In cases 436 involving, for example, the construction or waste industries, up to 20 years may elapse before claims are made.

A problem arises when an executor makes a claim in respect of a payment made by a deceased taxpayer. Can the Minister confirm that the relief would be available in such a case?

The Government are attempting to correct an anomaly in a way that recognises that sometimes payments must be made even after trade has stopped. The clause addresses the question whether the liability should be the same as it was before trade ceased. I would be grateful if the Minister could answer those questions.

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Sir George Young)
: As the hon. Lady said, the clause has been widely welcomed because it puts right a perceived injustice. I shall explain briefly what the clause does and then go on to deal with the hon. Lady's questions. The hon. Lady asked what the cost will be—the answer is £10 million.

The clause introduces a new relief for expenditure incurred by individuals after a business has ceased, such as professional indemnity insurance premiums. I shall explain in a moment why companies are excluded. The intention is to relieve genuine trading expenses, which do not, at present, qualify for tax relief.

At present, no relief is available under the tax system for expenditure incurred after a business has ceased unless the expenditure is provided for in the final accounts or there are post-cessation receipts against which the post-cessation expenditure can be set. Relief against post-cessation receipts will continue. The new provision relieves certain post-cessation expenditure, closely related to the trading or professional activities while they were carried on, against income and capital gains where that relief cannot be given immediately under the present rules.

The new relief is limited to particular types of loss or expenses which can be seen to relate directly to the business activities—for example, the costs of remedying defective work done, goods supplied or services rendered while the business was continuing, and damages paid by the taxpayer in respect of such defective work, goods or services whether awarded by a court or agreed during negotiations on a claim.

Further categories of expenditure which will be relieved under the provision are: legal and other professional expenses incurred in connection with such costs; insurance premiums paid to insure against such costs or expenses; and the costs of collecting debts which have been taken into account as turnover in computing the profits of the trade before discontinuance. In addition, debts owed to the business which have been taken into account in computing the profits or gains of the trade or profession before discontinuance, but which have subsequently become bad, will be treated as an expense within the new relief.

The hon. Lady referred to the timing of the new relief and asked why relief would be given at the time the expenditure was paid, rather than set against the trading income prior to cessation. The answer is that that would mean re-opening assessment on the trading profits several JPEG 437 years after the assessments were settled, and neither the Revenue nor taxpayers would welcome that. Giving relief when the expenditure is actually paid is much more straightforward.

The hon. Lady then asked why the new relief is limited to a period of seven years after trade ceases. It may be that some post-cessation expenditure can be paid for more than seven years, but as the allowable expenses have to be related to the trading activities, it would be more difficult to see such a nexus the longer the time period that had elapsed since the trade ceased.

The seven-year limit is also close to the general limit of six years under the statute of limitations, which will apply to many of the expenses arising out of late claims against the trader or losses on debts which he may be entitled to pursue. There is also a certain symmetry in that pre-trading expenditure relief is limited to expenditure in the seven-year period prior to the commencement of trading.

The hon. Lady asked why the new relief is limited to individuals and excludes companies. I think that, on reflection, she will accept that an individual who ceases trading but continues to exist as an individual may have continuing liabilities, but when a company ceases to trade there are no continuing liabilities for anybody to impose on. To that extent, the parallel between individuals and companies is not exact. The introduction of the new relief into the corporation tax system would also introduce unwelcome complications because the relevant corporation tax rules are different from, and, on the whole, more complex than the income tax rules.

The hon. Lady then spoke—though without great feeling—to her amendment, which she happily described as a probing amendment. I have to say that it is not one that the Government would accept. She did not make clear why she woud wish to restrict the relief that I have just spoken about to basic rate when expenditure is set against other income. She also left out the option of setting expenditure against capital gains tax, which is an anomaly.

Apart from the structural weaknesses in the amendment, I am not persuaded of the case. It would introduce an unnecessary complication, inconsistent with the current scheme for relieving trading losses at a person's marginal rate of tax, be that the lower rate, the basic rate or the higher rate. If expenditure would have been relieved at marginal rates if incurred while a business was carried on, why should not it be relieved at marginal rates when incurred after the business has ceased?

I invite the hon. Lady not to press her amendment to a Division. I hope that I have given her some of the background reasons why the clause is in the Bill.

Ms Armstrong
: I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

The amendments are somewhat technical. However, they would bring this welcome clause into line with normal market practice. They would add a few bells and whistles to a good clause to make it perfect.

Sir George Young
: The clause has an impeccable pedigree. When the Finance Bill was debated last year, my hon. Friends the Members for Bournemouth, West (Mr. Butterfill), for Fulham (Mr. Carrington) and for Slough (Mr. Watts) tabled amendments that were welcomed by both sides of the Committee. My predecessor undertook to consult on making possible changes to the law and to consider bringing forward proposals for legislation in this year's Bill. That is what we have done.

The clause will introduce a new tax relief, which will start on 6 April 1995, for employees who incur expenditure on indemnity insurance and on certain uninsured liabilities such as legal costs that relate to their work. It thereby cancels out any tax charge when indemnity insurance or uninsured liabilities are paid for or provided by the employer.

Payments by employees or directors for indemnity insurance, such as directors' and officers' liabilities insurance—D and O, as it is known—or professional indemnity insurance do not at the moment qualify for relief. That is because the expenditure fails the strict schedule E expenses rule, by not being incurred wholly, exclusively and necessarily in the performance of one's duties.

Payments by employers to provide indemnity insurance, which protects employees or directors, are 440 taxable as a benefit-in-kind for directors and to those employees earning £8,500 a year or more.

Payments by employees or directors for uninsured personal liabilities, which arise from their activities at work—such as legal costs in defending an action that is brought against them—also do not qualify for relief. Payments by employers to meet employees' or directors' uninsured personal liabilities are taxable.

To take the debate forward from last year, the Inland Revenue published a consultative document in May. There was a strong response. We listened to representations, and have responded positively. The new tax relief in the clause is comprehensive. It will cancel out any benefit-in-kind charge on directors and employees whose indemnity insurance premiums are paid for by their employer. It will also ensure that no tax will arise on payments by employers to meet employees' uninsured liabilities that arise from their work, when the liabilities could have been insured against. In line with the elimination of tax on employer-funded insurance and uninsured liabilities, it will give employees relief for the cost of providing their own insurance or having to meet their own uninsured liabilities. Costs and liabilities that arise from criminal convictions, which cannot be insured against, will not attract relief.

The new relief will also have a deregulatory effect. It will enable employers to apply for dispensations and so avoid the need to provide the Revenue with details of indemnity insurance that is provided for employees or payments of uninsured liabilities. A dispensation will spare employers the task of apportioning indemnity insurance premiums between the employer and the employees, and between the individual employees, in order to arrive at the benefit-in-kind. I therefore hope that the clause is generally acceptable.

Government amendment No. 145 relates to the provisions that give relief to directors' and employees' liability insurance and work-related uninsured liabilities, as I have just explained. However, as drafted the provisions in clause 79 would give no relief for premiums for liability insurance policies that cover a director's or an employee's liability to the employer. Similarly, any payment of a director's or an employee's uninsured liability, when the liability is to the employer, would not qualify for relief. There would also be no relief under clause 80 when the premiums related to liability to a former employer. That is because, during consultation, we focused on liabilities to third parties rather than to the employer.

However, since the publication of the Finance Bill it has been put to us that the exclusion of liabilities to the employer would disqualify many existing policies from the new relief. That is not a result that we intended. It has also been put to us that the absence of relief where liability is to the employer, as opposed to a third party, is unfair. Liability might arise when, for example, shareholders pressed a company to take action against a director for a wrong investment decision instead of the shareholders bringing the action directly. Liability in those circumstances can be and is properly insured against.

We recognise the force of those arguments and propose, therefore, to ensure that relief is available where liability is to the employer, both for liability insurance policies to JPEG 441 cover that risk and for uninsured liabilities relating to the director's or employee's work. As my hon. Friend the Member for Fulham implied, the proposal would be welcomed by directors or employees who have liability insurance policies which provide indemnity for liability to their employers. I commend the Government amendment to the Committee.

The good news ends as we turn to the other proposals. Amendment No. 138 would allow a director or employee to have relief for expenditure on liability insurance or uninsured liabilities against income from the employment not only in the tax year in which the payment is made but, as my hon. Friend explained, against income from employment in past and future years without any time limit. It is not clear exactly in what order the relief would be given, but it might be given against income from other employments. I do not agree with the amendment's proposals.

The new relief introduced by clause 79 follows the well-established principle in schedule E of giving relief only for employees' expenses incurred out of their income in the relevant employment in the tax year in which those expenses are incurred. Expenditure on uninsured liabilities met by the employee not the employer could exceed the income from employment in any given year. However, such cases are likely to be rare. The Inland Revenue's consultative document, published last May, requested examples of where employees had met out of their own pockets, uninsured liabilities arising from their work without being reimbursed by their employer. Only one such example was given and, in that case, it was not clear whether the employee's expenditure had amounted to more than the employment income in the year of the expense.

Clause 80 proposes to go further by allowing relief to be given against the former employee's total income and capital gains. However, that would occur in the exceptional circumstances where expenses occur after employment has ceased and, therefore, there is no longer any income from the employment against which to set the relief. That accounts for the distinction between the provisions in clause 80 and clause 79. Clause 80—rightly, in my opinion—does not propose to give relief backwards or forwards against past or future income and gains. It gives relief against income and gains in the year in which the payment is made.

In addition, to give relief against an employee's income from employment in past and future years would add great administrative complexity to what is intended to be a relatively straightforward relief. If, as my hon. Friend proposed, relief were to be given backwards, it would be necessary to re-open assessments for previous years which would otherwise have become filed. It would also add to legislative complexity as another provision would be needed to give relief against assessments for previous years. The amendment would also place no time limit on the number of years against which relief could be given for past or future years.

I am sorry to say that, as drafted, the amendment does not set out the order of priority against which the relief would be given—for example, whether past years would have priority over future years and whether the immediately preceding year would have priority over the year before that. There is also a risk that the amendment 442 might allow relief to be given not only against the income from the relevant employment but against any other income from other employment.

My hon. Friend may have detected that we are not wildly enthusiastic about his proposal. I do not think that we should go beyond the general principle by giving relief against income from the employment in past and future years in what are likely to be exceptional circumstances.

I was born in Sunderland, which was known as the largest shipbuilding town in the world for several years after the war. At that time, my grandfather worked in those yards as a boiler-maker—the elite of shipbuilding trades. I dread to think how he would react if he could see me moving such an amendment, when this country's capacity to build ships has decreased so drastically.

    The Government's measure is welcome but, my goodness, how it reflects that the Government have abandoned a serious commitment to enable shipbuilding to remain a major part of the manufacturing industry in this country. One reason that the Government have given 470 for introducing the measures is the country's defence needs. We know all too well that when the country was in difficulty we had to call on the Merchant Navy and had to commission merchant ships to assist the defence industry because, on its own, the Royal Navy was unable to meet the country's needs. During the Falklands war the merchant navy once more came into its own. With no pleasure we face the reality that, in similar circumstances today, we would probably not be able to meet that need with British ships and we would be dependent on ships from foreign countries with different interests from ours coming to our defence. That is an appalling indictment of the Government's lack of stewardship of such an important activity.
