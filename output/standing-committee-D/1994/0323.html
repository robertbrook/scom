Clause 1
ATOMIC ENERGY AUTHORITY TO MAKE SCHEMES FOR

TRANSFER OF PROPERTY, RIGHTS AND LIABILITIES IF SO DIRECTED

Dr. Moonie
: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 8, leave out "or schemes".

The Chairman

No. 5, in page 1, line 22, leave out "person or persons" and insert "successor company".

No. 6, in page 1, line 23, leave out "or include one or more companies".

Dr. Moonie
: AEA Technology is important to the nuclear industry—for which it carries out an enormous volume of research—and to the country, because of the range and type of activities in which it is involved. In view of its strategic importance, the Government should consider changing their initial suggestion that a scheme or schemes be set up to facilitate the sell-off of AEA Technology. Amendment No. 2 would remove the words "person or persons" from clause 1.

AEA Technology would benefit greatly from being sold off—if it must be sold off—as a single entity rather than as a succession of smaller, single companies. I will briefly state the reasons for that view. It is a generally held assumption that a larger company is better than a group of smaller companies. There are constraints on that suggestion, but it holds for a company of the size that we are considering. For example, larger companies gain advantages in raising capital by virtue of their bigger muscle. They have greater synergy, and a wider range of activities and complementary skills among their work forces. A wide variety of sources have provided advice on the issue and I have a morass of papers to support our view.

On Second Reading, I referred to an editorial in the Financial Times—which, incidentally, supported privatisation. It stated that the ideal solution to AEA Technology's sale—

"must be a management buy-out. This would create a sense of partnership among the company's employees, and guarantee the AEA the independence to underpin its credibility as a consultancy.


The alternative would be flotation as a private company. This would not be easy, however, since investors would have to be sure that the AEA had a worthwhile portfolio of contracts, and that its principal assets—people—would not walk out of the door.


Only as a last resort should the Government consider the remaining option: sale in whole or in part to trade purchasers. This would be highly unsatisfactory. The AEA would lose its independence and, if broken up, its range of skills as well."

That is also the view of the trade unions that represent the majority of employees within AEA Technology and I believe that it is also the generally held view of its management. The Government would be well advised to heed that advice.

AEA Technology is a major research and consultancy group. It is the largest company of its kind in this country and one of the largest in the world dealing with decommissioning and research on nuclear facilities and JPEG 5 materials. During the past few years it has grown and diversified. There can be no argument about that, and it is a good thing. It is a model for many other agencies in the Government sector.

Depending on how one defines research and development, AEA Technology could, as a single entity, be listed in the top 20 British companies in terms of the volume of research and development that it undertakes. I cannot understate the value of that research and development to this country. The Government must not do anything that will prejudice the ability of AEA Technology to compete successfully in its chosen fields and to continue to grow and diversify.

It is still a major player in the nuclear industry. It works to improve the performance of nuclear reactors as well as the performance and longevity of plants and processors. Its processor technology has applications in many other areas and in turn, AEA's work is of great relevance to the oil and gas industries, where these technologies can be used.

AEA plays a major role in establishing the safety of nuclear sites. The situation in the world being what it is, it also has a major worldwide role to play. In eastern Europe it provides help to run existing nuclear installations and to decommission those due for that process. There is a range of such activities. They have in common that they are complementary to each other. From one piece of core technology, another one, two or three activities spin off and can be used to generate wealth. As a result, a clear assumption can be made that it would be better to keep AEA as a single entity, rather than to break it up and lose the present synergy between its constituent parts.

Other aspects of AEA Technology bear mention. It is the main Government body for environmental research. Last year it took over the operation of the Warren Spring national environmental research centre, formerly at Stevenage. It is also mentioned as the probable successful bidder for the National Physical Laboratory. I referred to these aspects on Second Reading so I do not propose to discuss them in detail. They are key national priorities and it is unthinkable that they should be split up and that their work should be lost or submerged in a break-up of AEA into its constituent parts.

Warren Spring has not been absorbed into AEA Technology as a single entity. Less than 50 per cent. of its staff—far fewer than I thought—transferred to the new joint operation. That has not been kept as a single entity and subsumed into other activities of AEA Technology across a wide area of its operations. We can argue whether such an important national priority should be in the public or in the private sector, but none of us would disagree that it should be a priority of Government to see that its work on the enforcement of environmental quality standards continues. Because of the way in which Warren Spring has been absorbed into AEA Technology it is essential that the company remains as one entity to allow its work to continue.

What if the same were to happen to the National Physical Laboratory, which is the agency responsible for standards of measurement in this country? Nowadays, we tend to take such matters for granted, but wars used to be fought over whether standards were properly applied in Europe. I am not suggesting that that would happen today, 6 but it underpins the importance of this area of work to our national interests. We should run no risk of breaking it up.

AEA Technology's value lies in its independence and its credibility as an agency that can be trusted to do the work that it is set. That depends on its size, knowledge, experience and work force. What message are we sending to the work force and to the customers of AEA Technology, on whom its future success depends, and to the management who have worked so well in the past few years to diversify and to turn AEA Technology into a successful entity that can stand on its own in the public or private sector? What message is clause 1 sending to them? It shows that the Government do not know what they are about. They would like to get rid of AEA Technology, sell it off and obtain the income—but they do not know whether they will sell it off as one part or in several little bits, some of which might be closed down. Frankly, that is not good enough. When the Government sell off an agency as important as this one, they must make their intentions clear. It is not just a matter of securing the maximum return in the shortest time.

If the Government make it clear that they will sell AEA Technology off—if it must be sold off—as a single entity and not as a group of smaller organisations, that will be good for the morale of the work force and of the management, which has to prepare the scheme. Such clarification would send a clear message to the customers of AEA Technology in this country and throughout the world. It would prevent cherry picking of the best parts of the company, and the other parts—that perhaps need a little longer to mature and give the company a sound base in other areas—would be given time to develop.

The Government should make their intentions clear. That is the purpose of the amendments.

Mr. Ken Purchase
(Wolverhampton, North-East)
: I will try to set out the background to another mistake by the Government. We have to go back a considerable time to a statement by the late Nicholas Ridley about UCS Shipbuilders, to find where the drive comes from for the type of privatisation now being put before Parliament—the breaking-up and selling-off of an organisation that has shown its value and worth to our country. In 1969, at the heyday of the so-called Selsdon man, Mr. Ridley—as he then was—was heard to remark and wrote in a memo, as hon. Members will recall as they will have read it 1,000 times, that the best way to deal with UCS was to break it up and sell it off cheaply. That is exactly the plan that is before us today, dressed up in more moderate language by, I hope, more moderate people. I hope that they are moderate enough to take the amendment on merit.

The privatisation of AEA Technology is unnecessary. It should be left together with the whole organisation. The word "synergy" was used. I understood that to mean, in simple old-fashioned words, that the whole was greater than the sum of the parts. That has been a useful expression over many years for recognising that vertically and horizontally constructed organisations produce additional value because they work together in companies.

It has been fashionable—but is now less so, I hope—for large companies to splinter and to get pieces of business done in the private sector, for the simple reason that they thus avoid most social security charges and reduce the power of working people's organisations. Such problems cannot be resolved by avoiding them. Where difficulties arise in organisations—either because of ossification or because chains of command grow too long—they should be met head on with modern management techniques, so that people work in partnership and with a spirit of co-operation.

8 The Government continue to take the short cut. They chop up, cut up and sell off cheaply. The nation made a great sacrifice in putting those organisations together—the AEA was formed by a Conservative Government whose counsel was wiser than that of the present Government. That organisation is to be broken up and sold off, and the Financial Times suggests that it will be valued at around £200 million. That is a paltry sum compared to the value of the company's work for the country.

It could continue that work, but what will happen when it is in the private sector? The amendments make it clear that part of the problem will be that the new company will have considerable difficulty raising the investment funds that it will need to continue the work that it has done for many years. It is claimed that the markets will decide, but it is a tactic of markets to ensure that so-called profitable sectors of companies are sold off. The money that is made is then distributed to shareholders. It is as though the company and the people who worked for it never existed—it is sunk without trace.

The Bill offers no certainty about the details in the Government's mind. Plurals are used instead of singulars. It is suggested that the sale may be made to a number of companies or to one company. The Government do not know what they want. The sale is driven not by a practical expression of the work undertaken but by the death rattle of privatisation. That ideology of selling off cheaply began in 1969, but no lessons have been learnt. It is being applied blindly, without detail or understanding, to the AEA. That criticism will be repeated many times in our debates. If the Bill is to pass, it must be tighter, more defined and better understood.

The Government are driven by dogma. Some members of the Committee are experts on privatisation and have been pushing it for years, but they are out of tune with modern thinking—even modern economic thinking. Not so long ago, a winner of the Nobel prize for Economics, Douglass North, spoke in his prize lecture about what he called the "new institutional economics." He is not a man dealing with way-out economics, but someone well-rooted in the Anglo-American neo-classical tradition of Adam Smith and all the characters that we know so well these days. Professor North went on to say that the pure market model has become

and is being implemented to the destruction of the very institutions that it ought to support. In his report to the Cato Institute, Professor North dismissed privatization as

I suggest to Conservative Members—although they might not have time—that before voting they look at that letter and at the work of that Nobel prizewinner.

The proposed privatisation is a classic example of dogma undermining a strong business institution. That is not just a crude stripping of assets—it is a refusal to stand up and ask questions about the impact of the decision. It is not enough to leave matters to a later date and see what happens. If we do that, it could be too late. If people start picking up different bits, they may choose the very segment that pulls the whole structure apart. The Government's approach is not conservative—it is destructive, and the Committee should not support it.

At least our amendment does not throw out the whole Bill, it simply tells the Government that they ought to think again more clearly and put their proposals on the table, so that we can all be clearer about future possibilities. Conservative Members say that workers are interested only in their pension schemes. I refute that. "We do not agree that the market should determine the research because it is very difficult to plan any longer than a few years."
