Clause 75
INTEREST PAYMENTS DEEMED TO BE DISTRIBUTIONS

Question proposed [this day], That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

Question again proposed.

The Chairman
: The hon. Member for Milton Keynes, South-West (Mr. Legg) was on his feet when we adjourned for lunch.

Mr. Barry Legg
(Milton Keynes, South-West)
: Before we broke for lunch, I was explaining that the clause makes the Inland Revenue responsible for making wholly judgmental decisions about the appropriate financing of substantial companies that have been established in the United Kingdom by overseas investors. My right hon. Friend the Financial Secretary made much of the arm's length principle. We are handing over all judgments on the arm's length principle to the Inland Revenue. Even if one has complete knowledge of a company, it remains a question of judgment what level of finance one believes would be provided at arm's length by a third party. As anyone in the corporate sector knows, if one shows various financiers and bankers a balance sheet, one receives completely different answers as to how much any financier or banker will lend to a company.

Furthermore, members of the Committee may be aware that, recently, Pentos allowed its subsidiary, Athena, to go to the wall. It was surprising that a public company allowed its subsidiary to do that. It does not often happen because it is usual for public companies to stand behind their subsidiaries. That is another factor that will be taken into account when the Inland Revenue, looking at groups of companies and subsidiaries, form a judgment on how much finance they may obtain. It will ask whether the parent company will stand behind its subsidiary and what the attitude of that public company's directors will be. There is a range of uncertainties and the Inland Revenue will be in the driving seat deciding what capital structure is acceptable for overseas investors.

I echo the words of my hon. Friend the Member for Stamford and Spalding (Mr. Davies), who referred to the insertion of section 808A into the legislation, especially when much of it is effectively spent. That is a good point because it adds to the uncertainty surrounding the legislation.

My hon. Friend was also good enough to refer to amendment No. 190, which I tabled, and which was not selected for consideration by the Committee. It set out safe harbour provisions, with which some members of the Committee may not be familiar. Basically, safe harbour 396 provisions set down firm guidelines for overseas investors so that they know how much debt they can put into an overseas operation in the United Kingdom and receive tax relief on the interest. Many countries provide such safe harbours, which are an added attraction to external investors.

Mr. Geoffrey Hoon

Mr. Legg

If they wished to exceed those provisions, they would have to justify the matter to the Inland Revenue, and prove that they were doing so on an arm's length basis and that the level of debt was the same as that which a domiciled company could achieve. If they could not agree with the Inland Revenue on a level of debt or if they went above the level that the Inland Revenue had decided on the arm's length basis, that interest deduction would be disallowed and it would be treated as a distribution.

Several of our competitors on the continent have such provisions. Germany is a particularly interesting example JPEG 397 because, in the case of holding companies, the safe harbour provision runs up to a nine to one basis. That means that a holding company set up in Germany can have nine times more debt than it has share capital. That relates to the point made this morning by my hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Smith) about tax allowances and balance in the economy. The possibility of such high levels of debt and interest charges in Germany does much to mitigate the high levels of corporation tax that apply there. The Government would do well to consider the safe harbour basis, because it provides a degree of certainty to outside investors. Most of us know from our dealings with Japanese or German investors that they appreciate such certainty and a system that is rule based.

I will be interested to hear what my right hon. Friend the Financial Secretary has to say and whether he can give us any details of what informal guidance ratios the Inland Revenue may use. Some tax practitioners believe that the Revenue works on an informal one-to-one basis. I do not know whether my right hon. Friend can confirm that or if he feels that some different informal level would be appropriate in the future. If the Inland Revenue is working informally on a one-to-one basis, it is in marked contrast to the acceptable debt levels among some of continental competitors to which I referred.

My hon. Friends the Members for Stamford and Spalding and for Beaconsfield raised interesting points about consultation this morning.
