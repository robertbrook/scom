: Thank you, Mr. Hogg. I shall be brief in speaking to clause 135, but before doing so, I shall declare an interest. I am the unpaid parliamentary adviser to the Association of Unit Trusts and Investment Funds. I am also adviser to a bank—the Saudi International Bank—which does not have any unit trusts under its management, but does take on fund management.

Clause 135 is very welcome. It brings the regulations for open-ended investment into line with the regime for unit trusts. The industry has been pressing for such a measure for some time and would like the regulations to be implemented as rapidly as possible. The clause is necessary because international competition in such trusts is becoming increasingly great. The fact that open-ended investment trusts are not treated in the same way as unit trusts has led to the shift of some business out of London to more tax-friendly regimes.

There is, however, one slight problem with the clause. I do not expect it to be dealt with this afternoon, but I believe that more discussion is needed between the industry and the Treasury on how it could be tackled in future. The problem is raised by clause 135(3)(b)—a somewhat technical paragraph which transposes the treatment of foreign income dividends currently applied on unit trusts into open-ended investment trusts. It has arisen not so much from the treatment of the open-ended trusts but from the treatment of unit trusts because of the way in which the foreign income dividend regime recognises the recovery of tax.

The problem is straightforward. Some owners of unit trusts are taxpayers and some are not—for example, foreign nationals who do not pay tax in this country. Trusts need to distribute the totality of their income, which means that they must distribute to a point at which their ability to recover advance corporation tax becomes questionable. The difficulty is that their treatment of unit holders has to be equal between those who pay tax and those who do not. Although there has been discussion with the Revenue, the industry has received legal advice which suggests that it cannot treat the average unit holder as being typical, but has to consider each unit holder in his own right. It cannot therefore discriminate against one unit holder as opposed to another.

If a unit trust distributes income in a way which does not prejudice non-taxpayers, taxpayers are automatically likely to be prejudiced. Consequently, it will be impossible to reconcile the tax treatment of unit trusts with the same treatment for open-ended trusts. The industry does not altogether see its way through the JPEG 627 problem and it may well be that the only answer is to create two distinct types of unit trust—those for non-tax-paying investors and those for tax-paying investors. That, however, has administrative and marketing implications and would create severe problems for the industry, perhaps causing some business to leave London and go offshore, which the clause is specifically designed to avoid.

I should like my hon. Friend the Minister to show his willingness this afternoon to discuss the problem with the industry and seek a solution to it. If that solution requires a statutory change it could be introduced in next year's Finance Bill, but I hope that it could be done purely by regulations addressing the conundrum of taxpayers and non-taxpayers in unit trusts and open-ended investment trusts.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory
: All will be revealed.

The clause does not affect the amount of tax paid by any taxpayer, but it concerns the way in which the Inland Revenue and Customs and Excise account for some of the payments that they make. In the normal course of their operations, both of those departments repay tax, and that may carry interest. The intention has always been that those payments should come out of the tax collected by those departments, with only the balance being paid to the Consolidated Fund. Recent legal advice says that, in some circumstances, that treatment is incorrect and that JPEG 651 payments should come out of the amount voted to the departments to cover their costs of administration.

I should make it clear that that problem does not concern taxpayers. They are still entitled to be paid any amount owed to them by the Inland Revenue and the Customs and Excise. Under the law as it stands, those payments should be made out of voted money, but that would not be a sensible result. The payments can be large and their size and timing may be unpredictable. If the two departments had to find the payments out of their costs of administration, the amounts voted to the Inland Revenue and Customs and Excise would need to be increased. Even then, their finances would be very difficult to manage.

The problem arises because of the wording of the Exchequer and Audit Departments Act 1866. Section 10 of that Act allows the Revenue and Customs to deduct tax repayments from the amounts, that they pay into the Consolidated Fund, but our legal advice is that the term must be construed narrowly and so does not cover all the payments that they make. The clause will extend section 10 to allow such payments to be deducted from amounts due to the consolidated fund. That means that the payments will not have to be made out of voted money. We have made the clause retrospective because, on the basis of recent advice, the Revenue and Customs are now seen to have wrongly accounted for such payments for many years. Those errors were inadvertent. Therefore, it is right to correct them retrospectively.

My hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield asked why the 1866 Act should not stand as justified and without the need for amendment. I remind him that in 1866, VAT was not even a gleam in the House of Commons' eye. Technically, therefore, incorrect payments have been made only since the advent of those two taxes. It is not the fault of legislators of more than 100 years ago that they did not foresee such eventualities.
