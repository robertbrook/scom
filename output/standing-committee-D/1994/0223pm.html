Friend the Member for Eastbourne argued for a longer period. Perhaps I can set out the counter-arguments to his suggestions and make the case for three years being the appropriate time scale.

The whole aim of the measure is to encourage reinvestment in the merchant fleet. The argument for the roll-over relief is simple. We accept that because of market conditions or delays in constructing a new ship, there can be a gap between the disposal of one ship and the purchase of another. Under the normal tax rules, a balancing charge can often arise on the sale of the old ship. The shipping industry argued, and we accepted, that that was a disincentive to reinvestment, which is why we introduced the roll-over relief. We chose three years because that gives adequate time for a replacement ship to be acquired. When we introduced the measure, three years seemed the right length of time and it still seems like the right length to me.

The three-year period has some secondary advantages. It coincides with the roll-over period for capital gains tax to which some ships may be liable on sale. It also means that the measure can be accommodated within the normal six-year period for settling a taxpayer's affairs. Both of those are administrative conveniences rather than compelling arguments for not going beyond three years. However, three years is central to the purpose of the measure and the point is to encourage reinvestment in ships. The problem with the longer period, be it five years or seven years, is that the point of the measure is lost. With three years, the measure gives a reasonable incentive for the old ship to be replaced quickly. With seven years, that incentive is very much weakened.

I understand the point made by my hon. Friend the Member for Eastbourne about cycles in the price of ships. With a seven-year roll over, we would move from the aim of encouraging reinvestment to assisting trading in future ship price movements. All investment decisions have an element of crystal-ball gazing, of course, but three years seems to get the balance right between encouraging investment and delaying it.

None the less, my hon. Friend the Member for Eastbourne made a plausible case with force and coherence, as one would expect from him, and I accept that the right period must be a matter of judgment, so if he wants to withdraw the amendment, I will consider further some of the points which have arisen during the debate.

Sir George Young
: I shall say a word about the context. Clause 84 proposes to introduce two new sections, sections 33D and 33E, to the Capital Allowances Act 1990. The two new sections define the expenditure to which the deferral of the balancing charge arising on disposal of the old ship is attributed and what qualifies as a new ship. The clause defines the expenditure that may be used to frank a deferred balancing charge arising on disposal of the old ship. The expenditure must be incurred on a ship that comes within the single-ship rules and has not previously belonged to the buyer or anyone connected with him. At time of purchase, the ship must also be regarded as a qualifying ship that will continue to be so for the next three years or until it is sold to an unconnected third party, if earlier. The clause sets out what is to be regarded as a qualifying ship for the purposes of the new roll-over relief. Qualifying ships will be those of a sea-going kind, of 100 gross registered tonnes or more, that are registered on a register maintained under the law of the UK, a member state, another state within the European Economic Area or a colony. The kind of ship normally used for sporting or recreational purposes will not qualify, nor will oil rigs or gas installations.

Within that context, the two Government amendments relax slightly the conditions under which a ship can qualify as a new ship against which a deferred balancing charge may be set. The legislation as presently drafted would not allow a ship to qualify if it had at any time in the past belonged to the present purchaser or a person connected with him. The legislation was so drafted to prevent groups of companies from using ships in their existing fleets to frank deferred balancing charges instead of making new investment. The measure is, after all, intended to encourage investment in merchant shipping.

In the amendments we recognise the fact that ships have a long, useful life and it is not uncommon for them to be owned more than once during that life by the same person, or by connected persons. By preserving a rule that a ship must not have been owned by a connected person in the previous six years, the amendments would still ensure that existing fleets cannot be used to frank deferred balancing charges.

Sir George Young
: As I have already said, the legislation was so drafted to prevent groups of companies from using ships in their existing fleets to frank deferred balancing charges instead of making new investment. My hon. Friend the Member for Eastbourne said earlier that ship prices fluctuate in seven-year cycles. Amendment No. 226 would allow a company to sell a ship at near its peak price in, say, the second year of that cycle, to obtain roll-over relief on the balancing charge arising and then, just short of seven years after the start of the cycle, reacquire the same vessel at a low price and use that disposal to frank any balancing charge arising in the mean time. The amendment would encourage speculation rather than investment. To guard against that, we recommend that a new ship should not have been owned by its purchaser or a connected person in the previous six years—a period which also coincides with the tax time limits. I therefore advise the Committee not to accept amendment No. 226, but to support the Government amendment.
