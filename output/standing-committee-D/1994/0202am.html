Mr. Denis MacShane
(Rotherham)
: I beg to move amendment No. 62, in page 9, line 21, at end insert—

"(c) a member state of the Council of Europe.".

 I have been a Member of Parliament for only eight or nine months. I read about the proposed new airport tax in the newspapers last year. The proposal filled my heart with horror. I spent a good chunk of my life working for international organisations and travelling around the world. I have visited many airports in different parts of the world—communist countries, and countries in the far east and in Latin America. One could tell that one was in a bankrupt, poorly run, inefficient, third-world, authoritarian, dictatorial state if one had to fork out for airport tax. One was back in civilisation in the United States, Europe, the Nordic countries or dynamic Asian countries, where airport taxes did not exist. I felt shame, as a British traveller—and as an executive card holder with British Airways—that the British Government proposed to impose a third-world tax on us.

Only in our country would such a pernicious, mean and reprehensible tax be allowed to crawl out of the Treasury. Tax on travel is the late 20th century equivalent of the 19th century tax on newspapers. I am sure that the Government will get around to taxing newspapers before their nine or 24 months are up. It is profoundly illiberal to tax travel at a time of globalisation. It is also inefficient. According to a note that I received from the Library, it will raise £90 million this year. That is less than the amount of money that could be raised if we abolished the £113 million that the Ministry of Defence uses to subsidise the private schools to which officers' sons—not their daughters—are sent. That is the sort of tax subsidy that no other modern country provides.

The proposed tax is regressive.
