I understand that the clause is mainly technical, providing that where a trust has more than one trustee, payments of tax, penalties, interest and surcharges may be collected from any trustee. The Faculty of Taxation wants trustees to have a statutory right to claim reimbursement from other trustees when the assets of the trust are not sufficient to meet the tax payments. What is the Minister's opinion of that? I shall keep returning to this issue. The first set of clauses that we shall deal with this morning amend the Taxes Management Act 1970 in preparation for self-assessment. But, despite the aim of simplification—an aim that we support—increasingly the recurring result is technically complex legislation. That point was made last week in respect of other clauses, but these clauses are designed to enable the self-employed and others to fill in tax forms much more easily, to save everyone money, to simplify the system and to enable people to enter into self-assessment happily and confidently.

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Sir George Young): This clause is a rather narrow one. It brings the rules for trustees in line with self-assessment. I shall deal with the question that was posed by the hon. Member for Durham, North-West (Ms Armstrong) about the liability Image Col. 503 of trustees. She also made some important general points which, to some extent, we rehearsed in a debate on the Floor of the House. I shall answer her question about staff training and technology, but it would be difficult to deal with all aspects of self-assessment in this debate. I shall begin by explaining what the clause will do and then respond to her question about trustees indemnifying themselves.

This clause and clause 101, which we shall discuss later, will introduce the provisions necessary to apply the new system of self-assessment to trusts where there is more than one trustee. Trustees are jointly liable for tax due on income and gains of the trust and each trustee is liable for all the tax due, not just a share of it. The clause will not affect that basic position. It is a mechanical provision providing for the assessment and collection of tax from trustees.

In practice, trustees usually arrange for one of their number—the principal acting trustee—to deal with the Inland Revenue on their behalf. The effect of the clause is that where the principal acting trustee notifies liability, makes a return of trust income or gains or makes a self-assessment, his actions will be treated as meeting the tax obligations of the trustees as a whole. That is convenient both for trustees and for the Revenue and it reduces compliance and administrative costs.

Conversely, if the principal acting trustee fails to meet any tax obligations and that failure is not remedied by his fellow trustees, the effect of the clause will be to treat all the liable trustees as having failed to meet them. In those circumstances, the clause will allow the Inland Revenue to recover tax, interest, penalties or surcharges from any liable trustee if, for whatever reason, it is unable to obtain payment from the principal acting trustee. The basic, underlying position is not affected by the clause, nor are the responsibilities of trustees. However, the way that the new system will work under self-assessment is changed.

We considered the question of trustees indemnifying themselves from trust funds in the 1991 consultative document. We considered the possibility of providing trustees with a statutory right of indemnity in the document on income tax and capital gains tax treatment of United Kingdom resident trusts. However, we concluded that it was sufficient to rely on the position under trust law, which generally allows trustees to recover tax and interest on tax that is paid late from the trust funds. In the case of penalties and surcharges, allowing trustees a right of recovery from the trust would be unfair to beneficiaries, so we did not proceed with it.

The hon. Member for Durham, North-West (Ms Armstrong) touched upon two important issues—training and information technology. She was right to point out that the introduction of self-assessment is an important reform which has implications for taxpayers and for Inland Revenue staff. Some 40,000 staff have already received training on customer service. There is a four-year training programme, which is designed to give our staff the skills that they will need to introduce self-assessment. That programme is being devised in consultation with trade unions and staff and the process is going well. Of course, Inland Revenue will also provide information to taxpayers through a publicity campaign and through the tax inquiry centres, of which there are 400. Their Col. 504 resources will also be available to help to keep taxpayers fully informed.

I shall deal specifically with the training programme. More than 30 separate courses are available to Revenue staff. Each one is designed to meet the specific needs of its target audience. They are geared to equip staff with the knowledge and skills that they will need to meet the demands that they are likely to face. For example, besides the basic training that is available to all staff, whatever their job, tax inspectors are receiving intensive training on the technical aspects of the changes that were introduced by the 1994 Finance Act. That, of course, will be supplemented by training on the changes that are being brought in this year. Likewise, other groups of staff will receive training that is tailored to meet their own specific needs.

As I said, it is an ambitious training programme which is kept under constant review. It is being developed in close consultation with Revenue staff and trade unions. It is designed to ensure that the Revenue can cope with the introduction of self-assessment, not only meeting its own needs but helping taxpayers meet their new obligations.

On the question of information technology, the Revenue expects the new system to be delivered on time and also that it will work. However, as part of its proper management of the risks inherent in any large project, there are mitigation strategies and fall-back plans. Those include identifying the most critical aspects of the new system—for example, accounting for taxpayers' money—and giving those priority while introducing the IT systems in tranches rather than all at once. The Revenue will be buying proven products where possible, not trying to develop a new system in-house.

Ms Armstrong: Does that mean that the Revenue will rely on the expertise of another country that has been involved in self-assessment?

Sir George Young: As the hon. Lady knows, the system has been developed by EDS, which will use the best technology available. Some of it may be introduced from countries that are further down the self-assessment track than we are. What is important is that the system is being developed in parallel with piloting the new system—the tax returns are being filled in by volunteers and the system will be introduced incrementally so that people are not confronted with a completely new system. The programme has been broken down into critical tranches. The technology will be proven and not new whenever that makes sense.

In fairness to the Revenue, it has a good track record of introducing computer-based projects on time, on target and within budget. I am taking a close interest in the development of technology associated with the programme. It is on track and I am confident that it will be available when needed.

The hon. Lady spoke in alarmist terms about an uprising. I hope that she is wrong. The piloting of the forms with volunteer taxpayers is going well. Many find it simpler. When the new returns were most recently tested, 90 per cent. of the volunteers filled in the forms in a way that the Inland Revenue could process. In the spring, another 5,000 volunteers will be filling in returns on the new tax forms. There is a progressive programme Image Col. 505 of amendment, improvement and adaptation of the forms so that when they are received through letter boxes in April 1997, the three or four-year programme of introduction and implementation will have made them as perfect as possible.

The clause amends the rules for trustees to bring them in line with self-assessment. I am happy to answer any other questions when we debate relevant clauses. Clause 93 might give us an opportunity to discuss some of the items mentioned by the hon. Lady, such as compliance costs for the self-employed and small business men. In the meantime, I hope that clause 90 is agreed to.

The clause sets out the procedures for making claims on tax returns. In particular, it says that claims can be made only for amounts which are quantified. We have tabled our amendments following representations from the Institute of Taxation about the problem that it feels that that causes. The institute believes that the procedure for making claims under self-assessment is too inflexible if the claim can be made only for quantified amounts.

In many practical situations it is possible to anticipate losses or the benefit of the carry-back of personal pension premiums before the return for a particular year has been filed, but as the amount of the claim cannot be quantified or supported by documentary evidence, such a claim will not be allowable under the clause. When a taxpayer has reasonable grounds to anticipate a highly likely reduction in tax liability, a claim should be allowed. From the Inland Revenue's point of view, such flexibility would be unlikely to lead to spurious claims, as the automatic interest and penalty provisions will apply.

If a sole trader incurs a loss in year two, he may claim, among other reliefs, to carry back the loss against profits Col. 526 from year one, thereby eliminating or reducing the taxable income and the tax payable. It is generally accepted that such relief provides a valuable cashflow benefit to businesses in temporary difficulty. We all know from constituents how important such flexibility is, particularly to small businesses.

Under present practice, it is usually possible, by agreement with the inspector of taxes, to anticipate the benefit of the carry-back in determining the amount of tax payable on year one's profits before it becomes due, rather than having to pay the tax liability in full and then seeking a refund of that tax a year later when the accounts for year two are available. That avoids hitting a company with a refundable tax charge at a time when it is evidently in difficulty. Such relief is fair and difficult to abuse—we are keen to avoid making it easier to abuse tax liability—because an overestimated claim would create a liability to interest on unpaid tax.

We have tabled the amendments to point out the important—though, I accept, fairly technical—consequences of shifting the year of liability and the impact that that will have, in the view of the Chartered Institute of Taxation, on the cashflow problems of some businesses. That is the spirit of the amendment.

Sir George Young: The amendment relates to one of the more obscure areas of the tax system—a regime currently operating that is, to some extent, anomalous. The present non-statutory practice of claiming relief in advance of the event that would give rise to the relief resulted from the current system of handling appeals in the collection of tax. The interest rules that apply to the existing system mean that there is an advantage in not paying tax on time, if it can be cancelled later by a claim to carry back, even though the claim cannot yet be made.

The new system will allow the claim to be made as soon as legally possible and relief to be obtained at that point, but we do not believe that it would be right to bring in interest rules that reproduce the current regime, which, as I said a moment ago, is somewhat anomalous.

The hon. Lady's amendment seeks to change the machinery for dealing with claims to carry back relief to earlier years. Typical examples are claims to carry back relief for payments made through personal pension plans and for trading losses. In both cases, taxpayers have the additional flexibility of being able to use the relief in an earlier year as well as in the year in which the payment was made or the loss accrued. The amendment would allow taxpayers to claim an amount that they have not quantified. That would not fit with the general principle of self-assessment, which aims to arrive at a quantified tax bill within the normal timetable for each year and by reference to the tax events of that tax year.

I can give some assurance about the way in which such claims will be dealt with. Subsection (1A) introduces the sensible and necessary requirement in a self-assessment system—that the claim must be in a specified amount. It would be difficult to arrive at the amount of relief due without that figure. The same is true of income and gains to be included in the return. They, too, must be quantified so that the taxpayer can arrive at the amount of tax due.

In both cases, however, there will be times when, with the best will in the world, the final figures are simply not available. In such cases, whether they are in relation to Image Col. 527 income or gains or claims to relief, the taxpayer will be able to submit his best estimate of the figure that should be included. There is nothing new about that and I say to the hon. Lady that, provided that taxpayers take due care in making their estimate, relief can be given. When the final figures are available, the taxpayer can then adjust the amount of relief that has been claimed.

The Revenue is discussing those matters with representative organisations and will be issuing guidance on the use of estimated figures in returns. That guidance will include reference to the use of estimates in claims to reliefs. As for the specific point, people can make their claim at the earliest opportunity and, if necessary, use their best estimate of the relief that is available. There is no need to change the legislation as proposed in the amendment.

Amendments Nos. 257 and 258 would change the way in which we propose to deal with the carry back of reliefs, by making earlier self-assessments contingent on later events. At present, that does not matter, but under the new system the amounts assessed for each year should be capable of being finally determined by 31 January following the tax year. That then determines the tax payable for the year.

As drafted, subsection (4) deals with carry back of relief by making it a claim of the year in which the relief arises. It will continue to offer taxpayers flexibility in their use of such reliefs and it will change neither the amount of relief that is available nor its tax effect. In addition, it will not change the time at which such claims can be made.

However, a self-assessment, unlike a current assessment, will not be able to be contingent on later events. In practical terms, that means that there will no longer be an incentive to delay payment of tax due at the payment date in the expectation that a claim will be made later. The tax shown by the self-assessment will remain the correct amount payable, and hence be acknowledged as such by both the taxpayer and the Revenue. That will give taxpayers greater certainty, and help to streamline the system.

The changes proposed in the amendments would reinstate some of the worst features of the present system, which is why I cannot accept them.
