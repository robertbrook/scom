Schedule 23
OBLIGATIONS ETC. IMPOSED ON UK REPRESENTATIVES

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Sir George Young)
: I beg to move amendment No. 204, in page 306, line 3, leave out "or" and insert "and".

This is a minor amendment to the provisions that set out the rights and responsibilities of branches and agents of non-residents under self-assessment. It corrects a drafting error in the definition of an independent agent. The definition was intended to require all three tests of legal, financial and commercial independence to be satisfied but, as drafted, any one of the three would have sufficed. The same definition is used in amendments Nos. 195 and 203, which would amend the provisions on investment managers acting for non-residents.

Sir George Young
: The amendments relate to the provisions which limit the tax chargeable on nonresidents from transactions carried out through investment managers in the UK. The rules include anti-avoidance provisions which exclude financial trades carried out through investment managers from the limitation of charge if the investment manager and persons connected with him have a beneficial entitlement to more than 20 per cent.of the trading income, for example through a carried interest in the capital of a managed fund.

The proposals in the Bill were developed in consultation with representatives of the fund management 544 industry, but concerns have emerged from within the industry that the 20 per cent. rule would work differently for different types of investment funds depending on their legal form. The amendments, which have been developed in consultation with, and agreed with, representatives of the fund management industry, will remove those differences.

The amendments will ensure that where investment management services are provided to a collective investment scheme, as defined in the Financial Services Act 1986, the 20 per cent. rule is applied by looking at the scheme as a whole. Without that amendment, the rule would have to be applied separately to each participant if the scheme were transparent for tax purposes—in other words, the income of the scheme is taxed on the participants in the scheme. The amendments provide that, where the scheme is transparent, the 20 per cent. rule is treated as satisfied by each participant in the scheme: where the scheme, if it were taxed as a separate entity, would not be regarded as carrying on a financial trade in the United Kingdom, whatever the level of beneficial entitlement; and where the scheme, if it were taxed as a separate entity, would be regarded as carrying on a trade, provided that the 20 per cent. rule is satisfied in respect of the beneficial entitlement of the investment manager and connected persons to the taxable income of the scheme.

The amendments will also ensure that wherever the 20 per cent. rule is not met, it is only the part of the income of the non-resident to which the investment manager and connected persons are beneficially entitled that is excluded from the limitation of charge. The limitation of charge will apply to the part to which they are not beneficially entitled, provided that the other conditions in the investment manager provisions are met. Without that amendment, the whole of the income would have been excluded in certain cases.

Amendment agreed to.

The hon. Member for Edinburgh, Central asked for an explanation of the clause's purpose. I am happy to provide that. The offshore funds legislation was introduced in 1984. Broadly, it is concerned with offshore roll-up funds, which accumulate income until investors realise their holdings. Gains arising on disposal are treated as income for tax purposes. Funds that distribute most of their income each year and meet various conditions can apply to the Inland Revenue to be certified as distributor funds. Gains made on disposal of the holding in a qualifying fund are subject to capital gains tax.

The clause does not change the general thrust of the legislation, but makes two relaxations at the margins where the Government have accepted that the current rules are unduly onerous. I shall deal with the second of the two changes first, because that is in no way controversial.

At present a fund seeking distributor status must broadly distribute most of its income each year, unless there is no income at all. That rule can be unreasonable where the gross income of a fund is very low and the administrative costs of distributing it to investors are disproportionate. The clause simply introduces a de minimis limit so that funds will be excused from making a distribution of the income available for distribution is no more than 1 per cent. of the average value of the fund's assets for the year in question. There is no difficulty with that change being made.

The other change is to the definition of a material interest in an offshore fund, so that the legislation will no longer apply to entities that are not defined as being collective investment schemes for the purposes of the Financial Services Act. At the margins, various commercial structures exist that are not intended as vehicles for rolling up income, but might appear to be encompassed by the very wide definition of offshore fund in the existing legislation. In practice those entities may already qualify for one of the exclusions that the legislation provides, but that can only be determined by specialist tax advisers, who must first apply a fairly complex series of tests to the proposed arrangements—a process that ultimately adds to the administrative costs of the proposed scheme.

By removing non-collective investment schemes altogether, it can be established at the outset by a simple test that such an entity is not an offshore fund. The clause will therefore save an unnecessary compliance burden in those cases. There is, of course, no change in the position of the mainstream offshore funds—and they are the great majority—which are collective investment schemes.

The clause also makes a consequential change to legislation concerning insurance companies, where the concept of "relevant interests in offshore funds" appears. These are anti-avoidance provisions, which have not been raised in the debate.

Tax relief should go, for example, to the shipyards of the north-east, where it would help to retain jobs.
