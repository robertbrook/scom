Gentleman for Rotherham is working. I am sure that the Opposition Front Bench team will want to reflect on that proposal and on whether they want to endorse it.

The clause and schedule make changes to the income tax rules which apply to the taxation of income from property. The hon. Member for Durham, North-West will be pleased to know that they simplify a complicated area of taxation and will make it easier for those liable to income tax to compute their income from property and to assess it under the new self-assessment system, which comes into force in 1996–97. My hon. Friend the Member for Milton Keynes, South-West was right to make that link.

The Committee will be pleased to learn that the changes have been welcomed by a wide range of representative bodies in consultation, and by a number of commentators and individuals as an example of real simplification. In order to prepare for the new system of self assessment, the changes will take effect from 6 April 1995.

The intention of the clause and the schedule is to move away from the present artificial approach, which defines what receipts are caught and what expenditure is deductible, and ring fences income and expenditure according to the type of lease. In future, the starting point for the computation of income from property will be the application of commercial accountancy principles, as mentioned by my hon. Friends. The commercial profit will be adjusted under the computational rules that apply to trading activities. That does not mean that they will be taxed as a trade, and I shall present the argument for that in a moment.

At present, income from property situated in the United Kingdom is taxed under schedule A, and income from furnished lettings is taxed under schedule D case VI. In future, for income tax purposes, it is proposed to bring all income from property situated within the United 166 Kingdom within the new rules for schedule A. Under the new approach, all that income will be pooled and expenditure incurred in earning that income will be allowed against the pooled income. That represents an enormous simplification and saving of effort for taxpayers.

The changes introduced do not apply for corporation tax purposes. I was asked why that was the case. The reason is that at present companies have scope for sideways relief against other income for some property-related expenses through the charges and management expenses routes. The new schedule A approach would mean that, in future, companies would have all their property expenditure ring fenced as a schedule A deduction. They would therefore lose the flexibility that they have at the moment, which is why we have restricted the changes to income tax payers. The flexibility has been welcomed by many companies that would be denied that flexibility if we moved to a ring-fenced system.

Sir George Young
: My hon. Friend is right in that there is logic in what he says. But there are powerful reasons for not abolishing schedule A, one of which is that it would cost the Government about £450 million and at present we are not in that mode.

The Committee may find it convenient if I mention in passing the Government amendment to schedule 6, although I shall move it formally later. The new schedule A regime imports the rules for computing trading profits. One of those rules is contained in section 82, which restricts the relief due to traders for interest paid overseas in certain circumstances. That provision would effectively make it impossible for landlords paying interest to nonresident lenders to qualify for a deduction against their rental income. It was not our intention to deny landlords a relief under the new schedule A regime to which they were entitled under the old regime, which was why the Government tabled the amendment. It is designed to restore the position and to ensure that rental businesses that borrow funds from abroad can continue to receive relief for the interest that they pay to non-residents.

On the subject of complexity, the pooling of income and expenditure so that property sources of income are no longer individually ring fenced for income tax purposes will make life simpler. We believe that the changes are a simplification, which will be welcomed by income tax payers—the changes have already been welcomed by a wide range of representative bodies and several commentators.

The purpose of the amendments moved by the hon. Member for Durham, North-West is to ensure that, when an individual carries on more than one business, but in different capacities—for example, alone and in partnership, or as a member of two different partnerships—those different businesses will be deemed JPEG 167 to be one business, chargeable to tax under schedule A. That, I believe, is the import of the amendments, which have received some support from Conservative Benches. The concept of new schedule A business is that any number of schedule A businesses that are carried on by a person on his or her own account should be treated as one business. If one individual has many properties they will be pooled and treated as one business. That is separate from any schedule A business that is carried on in partnership. The partnership business will be treated as a different business from the one that is carried on in an individual capacity. In that sense, the businesses that a person carries on in a single capacity will be treated as a single business under the clause. However, we cannot accept that businesses that are carried on in different capacities should be treated as a single business. They are different businesses, and it would be too great a friction to try to treat them as one. The approach that we adopted in section 21(4) follows the case I treatment of farming businesses, by which any number of farming businesses that are carried on by an individual are treated as one. However, if a farmer carries on farming activities in different capacities, he or she will be treated as carrying on a separate farming business in each.

If schedule A businesses were treated as one, losses arising from individuals could be offset against profits arising to a partnership, and vice versa. That would be more generous for schedule A than is the present treatment of losses that are carried forward either for farming or for case I businesses.

There are some technical deficiencies in amendments Nos. 115 and 117. They do not deal with the fact that business accounts may be drawn up on different fiscal bases, and those accounts may not necessarily tie up.

I dealt a moment ago with the point that was made by my hon. Friend the Member for Stamford and Spalding. He asked why we would not extend the changes to corporation tax. At present, companies have scope for sideways relief against other income for property-related interest which is relieved as a charge. Investment companies have similar scope for those expenses of management that do not come within the narrow old schedule A expenses rule. Those companies that are members of groups have the further opportunity of setting their unused sideways relief against profits of fellow group companies. If the Government were to adopt the suggestion of my hon. Friend, the new approach to schedule A would prevent companies from relieving property-related expenses other than through ring-fenced relief against their own schedule A income. Companies would not want to lose these facilities. That is why we have restricted the proposed changes to income tax.

The treatment of companies without the new schedule A regime is, overall, still more generous than that for individuals within the new schedule A because companies will retain the charges of management expense facilities.

My hon. Friend took his argument a step further, by suggesting that one could make income from property trading rather than investment income. The changes to schedule A are designed to simplify the rules for calculating profits in preparation for self-assessment. They do not address wider issues, such as the treatment of income from property as investment income. In any event, 168 the present treatment of landlords as investors of property reflects the long-established distinction in our tax system between earned income and investment income. I accept that a landlord might put much time and effort into managing property, but rental income is derived less from what the landlord does than from the property itself. The rental income is a return on the capital invested in the property in much the same way as a dividend is a return on shares or securities. I am afraid that I cannot travel that last mile, as my hon. Friend invited me to.

My hon. Friend the member for Stamford and Spalding asked a complicated question about mixed partnerships of companies and individuals. The short answer to a long question is yes. Individuals will be taxed according to the new rules and the company will be taxed according to the old rules. For a partnership, there will be two computations; one by reference to income tax rules, to calculate the share of profit chargeable on the individual partners, and the other, by reference to corporation tax rules, to calculate the share of profit chargeable on the corporate partners.

Finally, on the point raised by my hon. Friend the Member for Milton Keynes, South-West, I am not sure that he was right to say that non-resident companies have a choice of income tax or corporation tax regimes. They will have a choice only if they become resident or form a resident subsidiary. I agree that it is not ideal to have two sections with the same number, but the provisions make it clear which section applies to income tax and which to corporation tax. I hope that that is helpful to the Committee and not too complicated. I invite the Committee, if pressed, to resist the amendment.

Sir George Young
: As my hon. Friend has said, the amendment would shorten the Bill, but there are good reasons for retaining section 379A. The amendment proposes to delete section 379A on the treatment of schedule A income tax losses. Broadly speaking, at present, excess schedule A expenditure on unfurnished property can be carried forward to a subsequent chargeable period, provided that the period is within the currency of the same lease. The intention of the new schedule A income tax regime is to do away with those artificial ring fences and to pool income expenses on both furnished and unfurnished property. Any unrelieved schedule A losses under the old system and unrelieved case VI furnished letting losses at the changeover will be converted into a new schedule.
