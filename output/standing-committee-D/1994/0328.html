It may seem that I am making a big fuss about a small matter, but even small volumes of special nuclear materials are of critical importance. Their management is equally important in ensuring that there is no proliferation of nuclear weapons technology.

Over the past 10 to 15 years, a considerable number of JPEG 37 countries successively acquired fissionable material to make their own nuclear weapons. That happened in Israel, for example, which is now—as is well-known—a nuclear power with nuclear weapons at is disposal. There was the problem with Iraq before the invasion of Kuwait. Iraq attempted to secure various technologies to allow it to make fissionable materials to construct an explosive device, and it installed centrifuges to refine relatively dilute preparations of nuclear material. Significantly, not much material has to be present, provided one has the technology to extract and amass it.

Opposition Members were right to raise questions about the special nuclear material. AEA Technology currently performs a small amount of work on special nuclear materials—SNMs—owned by customers. Friend the Minister for Energy and Industry and I made it clear that overall responsibility for the management for the UKAEA's nuclear liabilities remains and will remain with the Government division. That includes overall responsibility for safety at the nuclear sites. The Bill will not affect that arrangement.

The UKAEA's Government division has the responsibility—which is strongly endorsed by the Department—to contract out the decommissioning of nuclear facilities to commercial contractors, wherever possible and on a competitive basis. As a general rule, the division believes that the best approach to the policy is for the division to retain the relevant nuclear site licence and overall safety responsibility. That approach was adopted in the decision announced on 13 March to appoint a site managing agent at Dounreay. It also underlies Government policy on current proposals for divestment or contractorisations for a small number of teams. However, that is the Government division's general approach—it should not rule out exploration of alternatives. The division is conducting such exploration in the case of stage 2 of the decommissioning of the Windscale piles.

UKAEA Government division is at an early stage of exploring how to structure the project. To help inform the process, it has asked other organisations to give their views on not only the conventional option of working under the Government division's nuclear site licence but an alternative involving the contractor holding a nuclear site licence. That approach would involve a departure from UKAEA Government division policy, but the division would be failing in its duty if it did not examine any method of achieving value for money for the taxpayer. However, it would only pursue that alternative if it were satisfied that safety would not be compromised and if significant cost savings could be made.

There is a crucial regulatory issue. Before any nuclear JPEG 41 site licence is granted, the nuclear installations inspectorate must be satisfied that the applicant has an adequate management structure and resources to discharge the obligations that result from it being granted a licence. The applicant also has to develop and submit a management prospectus that demonstrates commitment to health and safety. In addition, the licensee must present a satisfactory safety case for the facility.

Detailed control of all the licensee's activities relevant to safety is achieved through the licence conditions and continuous monitoring of compliance with those conditions by the nuclear installations inspectorate. The conditions are legally enforceable under statutory penalties, and failure to comply with them is a criminal offence.

The option of a contractor holding a nuclear site licence for the piles could and would, therefore only be implemented, if Government division and the nuclear installations inspectorate were entirely satisfied that the contractor would maintain high safety standards.

It has been suggested that private sector companies are not safe nuclear operators and should not be given nuclear site licences. They all hold nuclear site licences and operate safely under them.

Safety is ensured not by public sector or private sector control, but by regulation. The regulatory system ensures equally high safety standards whether licensees are public or private sector companies.
