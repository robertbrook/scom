The Chairman: I welcome all hon. Members to the first sitting of the 1995 Standing Committee on the Finance Bill. I speak on behalf of my fellow Chairman when I say that we are confident that it will be a constructive and well-ordered Committee.

That during the proceedings on the Finance Bill (except Clauses 2, 5, 8, 15, 52, 64 and 91 and Shedules 1, 4, 11 and 14) the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock and half-past Four o'clock.

As is customary, I warmly welcome you, Mr. Winterton, to the Chair. We all look forward to serving under your experienced and penetrating eye. I have already experienced an example of your skill—as, no doubt, will all members of the Committee in due course. I hope that it will in no way lessen the dignity of the Chair if I recollect that in the salad days of our parliamentary youths, both of us had something of a reputation for independence of mind, spirit and voting habit. I mention that only because the general delight that the Committee will feel at having you in the Chair will be enhanced in the Government Whip, who will be very pleased that you are in a position of such dignity and impartiality.

I congratulate you, Mr. Winterton, on chairing a Committee that is an historic first in breaking new procedural ground. This is the first time that Finance Bill Standing Committee proceedings have started in a morning. Sittings will be in the morning and the afternoon, Tuesdays and Thursdays, with the exception of this afternoon. The fact does not appear in the motion, for reasons which the distinguished and learned Clerk described as metaphysical. We all understand that it will be only this afternoon that we shall not be sitting.

The reform will enhance our proceedings. When the matter was raised on Second Reading, the shadow Chief Secretary, the hon. Member for Oxford, East (Mr. Smith), was kind enough to say that it would enable the Opposition and, indeed, Government Back Benchers to scrutinise with even greater rigour than is usual. I am certain that the reform will improve the quality of our debates, to say nothing of the working lives of hon. Col. 4 Members. It will also make a difference to the many outside bodies and individuals who want to listen to our deliberations, whether they are from the professions, the unions, industry, the press or another interest. Unless they are inveterate night owls they, too, will be pleased by the reform.

I pay tribute to the Opposition for making the reform possible by their co-operation. I commend the sittings motion to the Committee.

Mr. Andrew Smith (Oxford, East): I join the Chief Secretary in welcoming you, Mr. Winterton, to the Committee. As the right hon. Ms Dawn Primarolo (Bristol, South): Perhaps the Minister could answer some questions on the complex arrangements described in the clause. There still appear to be some anomalies in the Government's aims with this clause, which is about alcoholics—[Laughter.] No, it is not. Last night was a long night, Mr. Winterton. The clause is about relief for alcoholic ingredients and it introduces a new scheme for repayment of duty on alcohol used in preparing food, non-alcoholic beverages and vinegar. Relief is likely to be withdrawn only for products not intended for human consumption, but I note that, for example, beer shampoo is not to be covered. The Paymaster General (Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory): The clause would unify two separate systems of relief for the repayment of duty on alcohol used in the production of qualifying products. Although I believe the clause to be uncontentious, I am keen to explain it to the Committee.

Several differing products—some in the soft drinks and food industries and others in the pharmaceutical industry—use alcohol. The clause brings together two sorts of relief—that of the duty on spirits and that of the duty on beer, wine and cider. The reliefs currently operate Image Col. 7 under separate primary legislation and the clause combines them in a single, simplified system.

The sorts of products that the clause covers include liqueur chocolates, whisky marmalade, fruit in brandy, particular flavours of ice cream and soft drinks that contain small amounts of alcohol. The hon. Member for Bristol, South (Ms Primarolo) was right to say that the clause does not refer to products such as beer shampoo. However, a few uses that do not qualify for relief under the clause will be tackled in clause 4, which, under the new denatured alcohol scheme, provides for claimants to continue to manufacture their duty-relieved, denatured alcohol. Although products such as beer shampoo are not helped by the clause, they are covered later in the Bill.

Ms Primarolo: In clauses 9 and 10 and schedule 3, which we are now discussing, the Government propose to extend duty to games machines as well as to increase the duty on gaming machines where there is a cash prize. I want to deal with the two separate groups of machines; amendments that arise later in the schedule will pick up specific items.

The Government propose to introduce a tax on all gaming machines, regardless of whether they pay prizes. For example, video game machines—they are often referred to as games of skill—and pinball machines will be taxed. The Minister was good enough to write to me on 27 January explaining the Government amendments. I shall comment on that again at the end of my remarks.

Many Members of Parliament, as well as Members of the Committee, have been extensively lobbied by men and women from small businesses who run companies that supervise the machines. They are distraught at the Government's proposals. They have made representations to the Government—hence some of the amendments—but believe that the amendments do not go far enough. Many of the machines that the Government propose to tax do not raise sufficient revenue in the course of the year to make it viable for the machines to be maintained and run by the companies.

The association that represents the businesses in those areas asked a leading accountant to study the implications for its industry of the unamended proposals in the Finance Bill. It believes that there would be a reduction in corporation tax to the Exchequer of 28 per cent., or £10 million a year, from amusement arcades as a direct result of the withdrawal of machines because it would no longer be financially viable to run them.

There would be a loss of VAT receipts to the Exchequer of about £45.5 million. There would be a substantial Col. 12 reduction in, for example, video game machines, which the association believes would be reduced by about 60 per cent. It also believes that the number of pinball machines would be reduced by 39 per cent. Most importantly, it claims that as many as 4,600 people could lose their jobs. That would be a loss of revenue to the Treasury—let alone the personal misery and problems caused to the people who could lose their jobs. The association estimates that there could be a loss of £15.8 million, excluding the cost of social security payments to those who would be able to claim benefit. Therefore, for the creation of a revenue income estimated at £30 million the Government are taking steps that will undermine significant parts of the industry. That will be most heavily felt in seaside resorts where many of the machines are located. The distribution of the effect would fall more heavily on those areas that are already experiencing significant employment and tourism problems.

The industry recognises that there is a clear case for a proper and fairer tax regime for the machines. It has put to us, and we agree, that the emphasis of taxation should be shifted from game machines—such as pinball, video games and games of skill—and loaded on to games of chance. I do not play these machines so I am having problems in thinking of examples, but one arm bandits, fruit machines and machines that pay out cash prizes are very lucrative not for people who play them, but for the companies that run them.
