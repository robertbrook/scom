The two new clauses deal with group relief and foreign-connected parties. Again, I begin by declaring a sort of interest: as you know, Mr. Winterton, I have worked as a lawyer in the shipping industry for may years, albeit for few British shipping concerns—and the number is diminishing.

I reiterate what I said in a previous debate: that the shipping industry in this country warmly welcomed the announcement made be my right hon. and learned Friend 778 the Chancellor last year that the relief would be available to the shipping industry. That should be recorded again. The announcement was especially welcomed by bodies such as the Chamber of Shipping and the Baltic Exchange. The Committee must ensure that full advantage is taken of the relief, and both my new clauses are designed to achieve that.

New clause 23 deals with group relief. It is a fact of life that shipping companies in this day and age typically comprise, or are members of, groups of companies. Different subsidiaries of a large company may be concerned with separate shipping operations. New so-called clean subsidiaries may be required by banks and other lending institutions to acquire new tonnage, or existing ship-owning companies may be bought in order to acquire the ships that they own. That is typical of ship owning worldwide. The concept of the one-ship company—owning only one asset, which is a single ship—is widespread throughout the international shipping industry. Although that system is deprecated by commentators, the fact is that it exists throughout the shipping world. There would be no purpose in discouraging it in this country alone when other countries would continue to operate it.

The Government's proposal permits the company that has sold a vessel to use the roll-over relief for new investment in another vessel. That is unduly restrictive. Subject, of course, to safeguards to protect the taxpayer it would be appropriate to allow the relief to be used by other group companies. In any case, a relief that is restricted to investment in ships, and ships only cannot be used for other purposes.

One of the issues that has arisen in correspondence between the Chamber of Shipping and the Treasury is that ship finance institutions require new investment to be places in a so-called clean owning company, often a new structure established for that purpose. I am grateful to the Chamber of Shipping for giving me a copy of a letter from Mr. Michael Borch, vice-president of Chemical Bank, which is a major player in the business. On roll-over relief, the letter refers to

"the obvious requirement that any new investment to roll over the investment into, is made by the disposing company."

 It goes on to say:

"We would like to point out as a provider of finance for such reinvestments, why this strict rule will severely restrict … to act in these matters."

 Mr. Borch writes of lending to a company not known to the bank since its incorporation and says:

"Such a company might have any number of liabilities, either contingent or crystallised, which are not apparent after even the most thorough review. Such liabilities might be tax, litigation foreign court award etc. Any one of which would have the potential to imperil the security of the new asset that we are being asked to finance. It is therefore our policy to insist that such assets are owned in newly incorporated companies, formed just for this purpose by lawyers JPEG 779 acting on our instructions."

 I can testify personally, having spent half a lifetime arresting vessels around the world, that vessels have a habit of picking up liabilities of all sorts during their career as they make their progress around the world. That is therefore an important factor.

There are also often bona fide trading reasons for new investment being placed in a different company. For example, some companies allocate different types of ship to different group trading companies, sometimes as a condition of external commercial agreements or contracts. Many aspects of a shipping company's business for example, charter obligations, ship management arrangements, flag registration and manning and labour agreements—impact on the way in which a shipowner's business is structured and operated. Those are matters that should be addressed by the treasury. For example, if P and O bulk decided that it wanted to slim down its commitment to the bulk carrier market sold one of its bulk carriers and, within the time allotted for the relief, decided to buy a new ferry, it would be absurd that as a result of the restriction the ferry would have to be owned and operated by P and O bulk, not by P and O ferries.

A couple of other points have occurred to me relating to the reliefs. I understand that one of the Revenue's principal objections is that the original company might be put into liquidation or sold before the group informs the revenue that a replacement vessel is not to be bought, which could lead to difficulty in recovering the deferred balancing charge. However, the same objection could be used about any company using the roll-over relief or about a capital gain made in respect of a ship. If the gain is being held over into a replacement ship, it crystallises in the purchasing company 10 years after the ship was bought, thus a capital gain could crystallise 13 years after the old ship was sold, so there could be practical difficulties in any event.

There is a parallel between roll-over relief and capital gains tax. Roll-over relief has been allowed for some years, and clause 42 of this very finance bill contains a provision that seems entirely analogous. As the budget press release from the revenue said, the change would

"give statutory backing to a long standing practice concerning the availability of roll-over relief for groups of companies."

 The government are resisting some of our amendments, calling in aid the need to avoid a mismatch with capital gains tax, but I have given a clear example of group relief already being recognised under the capital gains tax regime in a different clause.

Mr. Quentin Davies
(Stamford and Spalding)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

There is no doubt that personal equity plans have been one of the success stories of the Government. All Conservative Members are proud and appreciative of that success in encouraging personal savings, often on behalf of people who are not necessarily very wealthy.

I have twice put forward in Finance Bill Standing Committees new clauses or amendments on the PEP regime. Three years ago, I made a proposal that I repeated last year, to transform the PEP into what I term a PIP—a personal investment plan. The difference would be that a personal investment plan would be able to contain any instrument, quoted or unquoted, thereby removing current distortions in the savings market as a result of limitations on existing PEPs. Secondly, the PIP could be managed by the taxpayer personally, thereby ensuring that the full tax benefit being provided by the Treasury, or, more accurately, by the generality of taxpayers, would accrue to the saver and would not have to be split between the saver and plan manager or intermediary as it presently is.

As will be imagined, I was extremely gratified to find that the Government had recently accepted a large part—the first part—of my proposals. They have extended the PEP regime to enable fixed interest instruments and hybrid instruments, such as convertibles, to be held in a PEP fund in future. That is very welcome and will achieve the purpose of providing a greater range of instruments for borrowers, which will be good for them. It is often much more suitable for small savers to have the lower risk but higher running yield instrument of a bond rather than the higher risk and lower running yield instrument of an equity. By the same token, that will increase the supply of those instruments, particularly to industrial borrowers, so I hope that it will help to promote an expansion of the 784 corporate bond market in the UK. All that is very welcome and I am delighted by it and I thank the Government and congratulate them on that liberalising move.

If I try today to have a second bite at the cherry, I hope that I will not be regarded as churlish or ungrateful. However, the second issue that I have tried to press before, and on which the Government have not felt it possible to move, remains important and should be drawn to the Committee's attention.

Put simply, a general principle of tax experience is demonstrated by the current PEP set up. If a tax incentive is available only via the intermediacy of some third party—in this case, a PEP manager—the tax benefit will, by definition, be shared in proportions that cannot, of course, be predicted exactly in advance, and which are often difficult to calculate accurately retrospectively. They will be shared between the actual saver and the intermediary or plan manager. That is the current case.

There have been changes in the fees charged by PEP fund managers over the past two years. However, for a small income taxpayer, who is a small saver not likely to achieve capital gains of more than £5,000 or whatever the personal allowance is per annum, the benefit of investing through a PEP is marginal. He will pay an annual charge of 1 to 1.5 per cent. If we suppose, realistically, that the typical dividend yield on an equity portfolio is 4 per cent., a standard taxpayer would pay income tax at 25 per cent. and would therefore lose to the Revenue 1 per cent. of his 4 per cent. dividend. In a PEP regime, he pays 1 or 1.5 per cent. of the value of his portfolio to the plan manager.

Therefore, instead of paying 1 per cent. out of the 4 per cent. to the Revenue, he pays it to the plan manager. He may obtain some value from that manager if it is a discretionary, managed PEP and if the standard of fund management is high. Nevertheless, it is a marginal calculation.

The object of the new clause is to give savers the choice of investing through a plan manager in the form of a PEP fund—it may be based on unit trusts, investment trusts or a specific purchase of self-selected shares held within a fund—or running his own fund and thus not having to pay money to an intermediary or plan manager.

Much publicity has been given recently to the lowering of the charges of PEPs. I hope that smaller investors are sceptical about that. It is true that the premium previously charged to people going into these funds has fallen from a standard 5 per cent. to a much lower figure and some managers have abolished the front-end charge altogether. However, we must look behind the headlines and see that, as entry charges have been reduced or abolished, exit charges have been introduced and that is a slightly sinister development. Management charges have not been substantially reduced below 1 to 1.5 per cent. per annum. Some enterprising brokers and fund managers, using good advertising to promote their work, offer a self-select fund without charges, except for commission, but that is very JPEG 785 high—a good deal higher than is generally available from transaction-only brokers, who are known as discount brokers in the United States. Happily, there has been a considerable growth in their numbers in this country.

The fundamental principle to which I drew attention still apply and Treasury Ministers would not argue with its validity: if a tax incentive can be obtained only by passing through an intermediary, that intermediary will cream off a significant proportion of the benefit. Therefore, the generality of taxpayers are not paying an incentive to the group of taxpayers which we hope may be a numerical majority who are saving money through a PEP, but they are passing resources to be shared between the saver and plan managers. That is a good deal for plan managers and is an expanding industry in the City; I shall not increase the number of my friends there by tabling this new clause.

I pay tribute to Mr. Malcolm Gammie, the tax partner of Linklaters and Paines who kindly drafted the new clause on my behalf. I asked him to do it and he was good enough to do so without charging me his normal fees, which would have taken me to the bankruptcy court. He is not responsible for the purposive or policy merit or otherwise of the proposal.

There are two subsidiary points. One of the great advantages of allowing savers to manage their own PEPs is that they would themselves gain the greater benefit fo tax advantage. They would therefore have a higher incentive to save and thus have a bigger impact on the aggregate savings ratio than at present, when a large amount of that benefit goes to the plan manager.

Another benefit, which I hope will appeal to Treasury Ministers is that it will contribute to shareholder democracy as, by definition, when people manage their own plans they will be registered as shareholders in their own name. They will be entitled to attend, and to vote at, annual general meetings or extraordinary general meetings, where mergers, acquisitions, rights issues and so on are discussed. It is important that individual shareholders should exercise their democratic property rights in that way. They will automatically receive the annual report and accounts of the company in which they have invested.

PEP managers are under an obligation to give their investors the option of exercising those rights. However, they invariably charge quite a lot of money for that privilege. Most PEP investors are now being pushed into nominee accounts, and their names are not appearing on the shareholders' register. It is not possible or practicable for those shareholders to exercise their rights. Unfortunately, therefore, the PEP system, as it is currently constituted, is contributing to the trend towards the disenfranchisement of the small shareholder. I am sure that I do not need to elaborate to the Committee—certainly not to Conservative members of the Committee—the unfortunate consequences that that tendency would have if it were pursued for long.

I expect that Ministers will say that one of the main problems with the proposal is that it will lead straight away 786 to tax evasion, because individuals cannot manage their own PEPs without cheating. However, that concession has already been made. Elsewhere in the Bill there are provisions for the introduction of self-assessment. People will have plenty of opportunities to cheat if they wish to be dishonest in their self-assessments. However, there are sanctions. Such a company is required to deduct tax from the gift and pay it to the revenue, but, because the company cannot get its funds out of the country, it has to find additional funds from within Britain to pass the tax to the revenue from where it is reclaimed later.

If a United Kingdom investor wants to give blocked funds to a UK charity operating in the country concerned, it has to obtain consent from the local government or central government to transfer the funds to the charity's accounts in that country. Generally, where that has been attempted, the company has not been allowed to repatriate the funds to enable the donor to comply with UK tax rules.

The impact of the system is that a company that wishes to make a donation from funds that are locked into a country abroad has to find additional monies in sterling to make the contribution.

I know that the committee is anxious to make haste, but I should like very briefly to go through some calculations. If a company in Britain wanted to make a donation of JPEG 811 £75,000, it would have to pay £18,750 in income tax. The company would receive 33 per cent. tax relief on the donation, which, on £75,000, is nearly £25,000. The cost of the company is therefore just over £50,000, although the charity would benefit by £75,000. If a foreign company wanted to give £75,000, it would have to find £25,000 in the United Kingdom to pay to the revenue. The company would therefore put in £100,000; it would get £33,000 on the 33 per cent. rule, so the donor's cost would be £67,000, compared with £50,000, which the British donor company would pay. The greater cost to the foreign donor is another disincentive.

Donors and charities try to get around the rules, and sometimes they secure successful donations of block funds. They can negotiate with the Government for special consent to commit the temporary relief on sufficient foreign currency to settle the UK tax liability on the condition that the charity provides a guarantee to replace the funds once it has reclaimed the tax from the Revenue. However, that solution has several disadvantages: it depends upon the co-operation of a foreign Government, and few countries are willing to cooperate; it requires protracted negotiations over many months, and, quite often, by the time that one is successful in obtaining consent, the company's circumstances may have changed; and the charity may not be able to give the guarantee.

Would the new clause make any difference? We shall not know until we have tried. However, the evidence in America is that, seven years after the introduction of a similar scheme, an extra $100 million was raised in charitable contributions. That is especially important in Britain, where charitable donations are in decline because of the national lottery.

The requirement for tax to be deducted from a company donation to a charity should be removed when donations are made from block funds. The proposal would cost the Exchequer nothing—the charities would lose money because they could not reclaim the tax, but at least they would get something.

We are anxious to ensure that the Revenue's concerns about the policing of donations should be sustained. For that reason, we have suggested in the new clause that a duty should be imposed on charities through a certification procedure for gifts of block funds. That would give the Revenue the details that it needs to administer the system. The new clause has been drafted with a view to implementing twin proposals: removing the requirements to deduct tax and introducing the certification procedure.

The Revenue fears abuse because companies could set up charities to launder money. I do not believe that there is any evidence of such abuse. The certification procedure would ensure that such abuse will not occur. The proposal would cost the Exchequer nothing, but it could bring great benefit to charities by releasing donations from block funds at a time when there is a desire among the Government and the Opposition to increase the role of charities in many realms of public life.
