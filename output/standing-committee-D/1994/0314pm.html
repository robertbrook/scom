Mr. Nelson
: Thank you, Mr. Winterton, for your guidance.

I was explaining before we adjourned that the tax approval rules for occupational pension schemes are very flexible and allow what is generally regarded as a reasonable replacement of income when people retire. That is why the maximum pensions that a tax approved scheme may pay are related to earnings in the period leading up to retirement.

The general limit on such pensions is two thirds of final salary. There is no magic about that figure, but given that the controls on occupational pension schemes relate mainly to the money that can be paid out rather than the money that is paid in, some limit is needed. Two thirds of final remuneration is generally considered to be reasonable. I remind the Committee—although I am sure that many members are aware—that the cost of the present reliefs on pensions amount to no less then £7 billion a year.

Mr. Quentin Davies
(Stamford and Spalding)
: Perhaps I should make plain, as I tried to do during my original speech on the new clause, that I do not quarrel with the two thirds rule. The question is, two thirds of what? Should the two thirds rule be two thirds of final salary, although the effort made by the pensioner during his working life would be better reflected by a higher level of earnings which he might have received before that period? The issue is whether the two thirds rule should be applied merely to the final salary or to the best years of employment.

Mr. Nelson
: If my hon. Friend will allow me to continue, I shall touch upon, if not answer, his detailed concerns.

The tax reliefs cost £7 billion a year. That substantial sum must limit the extent to which we could go—in mandatory or discretionary regimes of approved pensions—substantially beyond what is already provided for. It is worth drawing attention to that figure.

Tax law gives broad discretionary powers to the Inland Revenue relating to the approval of pension schemes. The tax approval rules have been adapted over the years to reflect changing patterns of pension provision. If they are now complicated, that is in part due to the willingness of the Revenue to adapt the basic rules to the needs of employers. In order that people know what is normally acceptable in the rules of a tax approved scheme, the Inland Revenue publish detailed guidance that explains, for example, that pensions may be broadly based on the best years' earnings in the previous five years before retirement or an average of any three or more consecutive years' earnings for a period ending up to 10 years before retirement.

Despite changing patterns of work, those definitions of final remuneration meet almost all situations. The first option is normally favoured by most occupational pension schemes, because it suits people with normal work and earnings patterns. The second option can help those whose earnings tail off in the final years of their career. Taken together, the options available under the tax approval rules cover most circumstances and ensure that benefits can reasonably reflect earnings in the period up to retirement, and so provide an appropriate replacement income.

My hon. Friends the Members for Stamford and Spalding (Mr. Davies) and for Milton Keynes, Southwest (Mr. Legg) mentioned a number of aspects to the tax rules that are of concern. I shall try to respond to their points. The position of people who suffer serious illness in mid-career is one that might give rise to such concerns. In such cases, and where a person can no longer continue his or her career, the scheme may allow early retirement. The pension may then be based on the normal definitions of final earnings that I have already described, and on the number of years service that the person could have completed up to the scheme's normal retirement age. In other words, benefits do not have to be limited to the period of employment up to the date of early retirement.

I shall now elaborate on that introduction. A person who has retired because of ill health may recover sufficiently to return to work. In such cases, it is normally a requirement that payment of the pension should be suspended if the person wishes to accrue further benefits JPEG 735 under the pension scheme. When the person later retires at the normal scheme retirement age, the pension can be based on total service completed and the earnings at final retirement. However, if the person would be worse off on that basis, there is no obstacle to the scheme providing a pension equal to the earlier retirement pension that was given up, increased from the date of suspension to the final retirement in line with the retail prices index.

There is also considerable flexibility for those who change jobs. Most pension schemes give benefits that accrue at one sixtieth of final salary for each year of service, so that the maximum pension would be earned after 40 years. With such a scheme, when a person changes jobs the accrued pension can be based on the length of service completed and on earnings received at that time. On moving to a new job, someone can join that employer's pension scheme and the tax approval rules will allow benefits to accrue at a faster rate of up to one thirtieth of final salary.

That is a very generous and, indeed, expensive level of benefits, which allows the two thirds of final salary pension to be earned after just 20 years service—half the normal time. The benefits, in keeping with the principle that the two thirds figure represents a reasonable benefit for a whole career, must then be adjusted to take account of pensions accrued under tax approval schemes of former employers, so that the total is kept within the two thirds limit. Given the costs of pension tax relief to which I have referred, that is not an unreasonable result. However, it is recognised that if a person suffers a reduction of income on moving to a later job, the pension could suffer. To overcome that difficulty, an occupational pension scheme can always give a pension of up to one sixtieth of final remuneration for each year of service, no matter how large the pensions from previous jobs might be.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Stamford and Spalding recognised, I cannot recommend acceptance of the new clause as drafted. As he rightly said, it contains technical flaws that would preclude it from having a meaningful effect. More seriously, it is possible that the definition would constrain the Revenue's discretion to use other definitions of final remuneration that might be more generous in many circumstances. Clearly, that would be undesirable.

I have given a brief description of what is possible under the tax approval rules for occupational pension schemes. It is, of course, for each employer to decide what benefits the scheme should provide, and how they should be calculated. Indeed, many pension schemes, for reasons of cost, provide benefits that are smaller than the tax rules would allow. Alternatively, there is nothing to stop an employer from being more generous. In such a situation, no special tax reliefs will be available, but that is not unreasonable.

I hope that my explanation of the tax approval rules helps my hon. Friends. We do not claim that the present system is perfect, but from practical experience it appears to work well in almost all situations, and it allows people to accrue a good level of benefits with tax relief without getting in the way of employers who want to be more generous.

Mr. Nelson

A derivatives trader may be burnt out in his 30s, a North sea diver may be redundant in his 30s and a pilot in the armed forces may be redundant in his 40s.

We do not make value judgments about people's activities, but it is recognised that different people have different peaks of activity and earning potential at different times in their careers. There is no intention to 738 deprive people of their entitlements at the end of the day because they changed job or have an earlier peak earning power.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Milton Keynes, South-West said, people can, on changing job, take out a personal pension. Contributions to such a scheme are limited. The final amount payable under such a scheme would not be restricted in the same way as entitlement under an occupational pension scheme, for the reasons that I gave, but the scheme input is limited to 17½ per cent., whether it is the employee or the employer who makes the contribution.

New clause 17
361A. LOAN TO BUY INTEREST IN A PARTNERSHIP COMPANY

"(1) After section 361 of the Taxes Act there shall be inserted—

  1. Subject to subsection (5) below and sections 362 to 365, interest is eligible for relief under section 353 if it is interest on a loan to an individual to defray money applied—
    1. in acquiring any part of the ordinary share capital of a partnership company; or
    2. in paying off another loan, interest on which would have been eligible for relief under section 353 had the loan not been paid off (on the assumption, if it was free of interest, that it carried interest);
    and the conditions stated in subsection (2) below are satisfied.
  2. The conditions referred to in subsection (1) above are that—
    1. the company is, throughout the period beginning with the date on which the shares are acquired and ending with the date on which the interest is paid—
      1. an unquoted company; and
      2. a trading company or the holding company of a trading group;
    2. during the year of assessment in which the interest is paid the company either—
      1. first becomes a partnership company; or
      2. is such a company throughout a period of at least nine months;
    3. the individual is an employee of the company throughout the period beginning with the date on which the proceeds of the loan are applied and ending with the date on which the interest is paid or, if at that date he has ceased to be such an employee, ending with whichever is the later of—
      1. the date on which he ceased to be such an employee;
      2. the date 12 months before the payment of the interest; and
    4. the individual shows that in the period from application of the proceeds of the loan to the payment of the interest he has not recovered any capital from the company, apart from any amount taken into account under section 363(1).
  3. For the purposes of this section a company is a partnership company at any time when more than 30 per cent.—
    1. of the issued ordinary share capital of the company, and
    2. of the voting power in the company, is beneficially owned by or on behalf of persons who are employees or former employees of the company and which has 'an employees' share scheme 'in which every employee, who has been an employee for a qualifying period not exceeding 5 years, is eligible to participate on similar terms'.
  4. Where an individual owns beneficially more than 10 per cent. of the issued ordinary share capital of, or voting power in, a company the excess shall be treated for the purposes of subsection (5) above as being owned by an individual who is not an employee of the company.
  5. Interest shall be eligible for relief only to the extent the amount on which it is payable during the year of assessment does not exceed the permitted maximum.
  6. In this section—
  7. 'employee' in relation to a company, means a person who works as an employee or director of the company or of a 51 per cent. subsidiary of the company;
  8. 'holding company' means a company whose business (disregarding any trade carried on by it) consists wholly or mainly of the holding of shares or securities of one or more companies which are its 75 per cent. subsidiaries;
  9. 'permitted maximum' means such sum as Parliament may determine;
  10. 'trading company' means a company whose business consists wholly or mainly of the carrying on of a trade or trades;"—[ Mr. Forman.]

New clause 17 seeks specifically to encourage all employees, not just some, to borrow with the aid of tax relief to buy shares in the company for which they work and to do so at any time, not only within the first 12 months after the original employee buy-out. That would put employees of what I have described as partnership companies on a par with professionals, such as accountants, solicitors and others, who borrow with the benefit of tax relief in order to buy into their partnerships. I estimate that the cost would be minimal in the early stages because, as with all other previous tax law schemes to encourage employee ownership, it is likely to grow gradually and organically. Therefore, I do not envisage a large cost to the Exchequer in the initial stages.

In order to take a belt and braces approach, the new clause proposes that the amount of eligible borrowing should be limited to, for example, a sum equal to 50 per cent. of annual salary with a total cap of, perhaps, £30,000 in any year. So the provision could not be used infinitely; it would have to be used in a capped and controlled way.

There are three key general points in favour of the new clause. The first is that it will facilitate the further broadening of individual ownership and contribute to solidarity in our society—a theme which should commend itself to Opposition Members. Secondly, it will help to create conditions in Britain's businesses in which all employees, whether managerial or shop-floor, feel that they are real stakeholders in the business, and thus enhance their commitment to the business and to corporate performance. Thirdly, the new clause will contribute to individual freedom and opportunity, thanks to the spread of personal wealth that employee share ownership, like any other form of share ownership, is likely to bring and, possibly, through the cascading of personal wealth down the generations, which, I believe, Conservative members of the Committee strongly support. With those general arguments in mind, and in the interests of brevity, I commend the new clause to the Committee.
