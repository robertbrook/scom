I do not understand that, because the VCT scheme appears to be based on the fact that tax relief will be available as an encouragement for people to invest in it. Tax relief surely means the right of people to avoid paying tax. The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Sir George Young)
: This is the third time that we have had an interesting debate on venture capital trusts. We touched on the subject on Second Reading of the Finance Bill; we devoted a substantial part of the Committee stage on the Floor of the House to VCTs; and we have had a canter round the course again today. Earlier deliberations of the Committee also touched on the matter. They have all been helpful debates and have revealed an element of consensus about the way forward.

The debates have mirrored the Government's actions over the past year or so with the industrial finance initiative. That initiative considered not only savings per se, but where the savings go. I was interested to compare and contrast what the hon. Member for Sheffield, Attercliffe said with what his hon. Friend the Member for Monklands, East (Mrs. Liddell) said on Tuesday about TESSAs. She was concerned that we should marry the need

"for productive investment in our economy with the encouragement of savings."— [Official Report, Standing Committee D, 14 February 1995; c. 276.]

She went on to say that the role of the small saver in the economy would be increased if there was a direct link whereby people could see how their money was being invested.

There is of course, as I said in that debate, a range of savings products on which tax relief is given. The VCTs, which we discussed this afternoon, meet spot on the very concerns that the hon. Lady touched on two days ago. We need a spectrum of savings instruments that reflect the needs of savers and the needs of investors who will make productive capacity available.

The hon. Member for Attercliffe said that the scheme was at the riskier end of the savings spectrum. He asked about the effects on the small investor of the VCT losing its tax approval. The answer is the one that he anticipated—that the investor would subsequently lose his tax relief. That has to be right. I hope that it will not happen often, but it is an inevitable consequence of the scheme as structured and it replicates provisions such as those in the business expansion scheme. If the small saver wants his money to be safer, he need not invest in a VCT. 332 He can invest in other products that have greater investor security such as PEPs—or even TESSAs, which are safer. He could have a spectrum of investments, some safer and some less so.

The hon. Gentleman went on to raise several other important issues. First, he asked why have a ceiling of £100,000 rather than a lower one, such as £50,000. The amount of £100,000, with tax relief of 20 per cent., is the figure in the enterprise investment scheme. The figure derives from the fact that tax relief at 40 per cent. was given on £40,000 investment in the business expansion scheme. That is the broad logic for the figure of £100,000. We did not slavishly adopt the figure in other schemes. VCTs, which will effectively be quoted investment-type vehicles, will have high costs for market analysis, listing, promotion and so on. We had to recognise that when fixing the subscription rate on which tax relief would be given.

That is not to say, as the hon. Gentleman implied in his speech, that all those who are going to invest will be well-off investors, perhaps investing £100,000. This morning I touched on the average subscription in the EIS scheme, where again the limit is £100,000, but the average subscription is £7,000 for the small to medium-range saver. It is not true that, just because there is a high maximum, only the big rollers are attracted.

Secondly, the hon. Gentleman asked what trades we were trying to encourage. Again, we have reflected the approach of the EIS, which is to exclude non-qualifying trades. In that way, we ensure that we do not exclude a company inadvertently because it does not fit neatly within the qualifying trades that would be specified if we did as the hon. Gentleman suggested. That method would exclude innovative companies that set up an entirely new trade. For example, who would have thought 10 years ago that companies might be set up to manufacture virtual reality helmets and related equipment? The legislation would have to be lengthy to include all the companies that the Government want to access VCT funding. The right way to proceed is to exclude rather than to include.

The hon. Gentleman's third question was whether we would monitor the development of those industries and the jobs that they produce. I mentioned earlier some of the uses to which the EIS has been put. I hope that in a year's time I will be able to report what activities the VCT scheme has produced. The Revenue will certainly be monitoring the activities because it will be approving them. I hope that it will produce a useful list for the House.

There has been a great deal of interest in the scheme. Last month, 300 people attended a seminar, jointly sponsored by the Inland Revenue and the British Venture Capital Association, to discuss the legislation. I addressed the seminar and answered questions on the scheme. I was particularly impressed by the interest and enthusiasm of those attending.

Contrary to what Labour members of the Committee might imply, they were not licking their lips at tax scams for the wealthy. They know that venture capital is to do with risk. The legislation will encourage people to take risks. Some will be rewarded; others may not be. However, what should come from the scheme is the creation of jobs, and I suggest that that is what is important to the Committee.

The hon. Gentleman asked about the costs of the scheme. We have said that the Exchequer cost will depend on take-up. Estimates are highly uncertain. Sir George Young
: Clause 68 and schedule 17 simplify the benefit-to-settlor rules. The context of the schedule is an initiative which we launched last year to tackle complexity in existing tax legislation. We invited the Law Society, the Institute of Taxation and the Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales to identify legislation in need of simplification and we involved them in working out proposals to achieve it. The benefit-to-settlor rules were a prime candidate for simplification as they were built up piecemeal over half a century. They are difficult to understand and apply, some are virtually redundant and there is overlap and duplication. The purpose of the changes was not to increase revenue. Much of the simplification achieved by the provisions on schedule 17 were warmly welcomed, but anxiety was expressed about one aspect of the proposals—the new rules that tax the settlor on the value of any loan between settlor and trustees that is interest free or at a beneficial rate of interest.

There was particular anxiety about loans made to a trust rather than from it. Some of that anxiety was misplaced JPEG 339 and arose from a misunderstanding of how the provisions operate. For example, initial comment suggested that the provisions apply to loans between individuals. That is not so; the rules apply only to loans to or from trustees.

It has become clear that there are more fundamental concerns about the application of the new provision on loans. In those circumstances we concluded that we should reconsider the proposals, which are intended primarily as a simplification, and it is important to ensure that any changes achieve that objective. It shows that those of us who are in favour of simplification do not have an easy road.

The Government amendments remove the new loans provisions from schedule 17 and reinstate the existing rules that they were designed to replace. Further consequential amendments may be necessary on Report. Those who were worried about the effect of the new rules will welcome the amendments.

The first part of the schedule deals with cases in which the settlor or the settlor's spouse has retained an interest in the settlement so that income or capital can be paid to them. In those cases, income arising under the settlement will be treated as the settlor's for all tax purposes. The new section 660A is based on the existing section 674A and most of the detailed rules that govern that section continue to operate. However, the new provision will apply to settlements whenever made, which means that a raft of earlier but similar provisions can be repealed.

When a settlement is in favour of a settlor's minor, unmarried child, a payment of income to or for the benefit of the child will be treated as being the income of the settlor. Payments of capital will also be treated as the income of the settlor for tax purposes, to the extent that they can be matched against unavailable undistributed income. Again, the new rules are based closely on those that they replace.

Finally, various supplementary provisions have been consolidated into a single set of rules governing the application of income tax charges. They remove unnecessary duplication.
