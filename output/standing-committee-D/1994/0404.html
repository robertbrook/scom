The amendments would allow employees to remain in their current pension schemes, irrespective of what happens once companies are split up and sold off. The provisions in the amendments are similar to those that were made for the employees of Amersham International when it was sold in 1981.

The amendments refer to existing employees who have accrued pension rights under the Atomic Energy Authority scheme. They would allow divested employees to remain in the scheme and would permit transfer back into it. The amendments would allow all employees to be treated fairly and flexibly, and that should be the objective of any scheme the Government set up. I hope that is clear. It is certainly as clear as any of the existing provisions in the schedule. I confess that my eyes glaze over when I read documents written in such excruciating detail. It is essential, however, that such provisions are worded accurately because of their legal implications—even if they are not entirely clear as a result.

No transfer scheme should just be a matter of saving money. The provisions in the amendments would reduce the short-term costs of restructuring. No reference is made in the Bill to the cost to the Government of a transfer of pension undertakings as a consequence of these proposals. Given that there are 4,000 employees, there is no doubt in my mind that if an accurate actuarial costing of the scheme were undertaken, it would show a cost greatly in excess of any moneys that are likely to be realised from selling off the companies. To seek to encourage greater costs is a strange way for the Government to proceed. Deferred costs JPEG 103 of allowing employees to remain in the scheme would ultimately have to be met, but at least that would hopefully be put off to a better day.

Trade unions representing employees have expressed concern about some of the Bill's provisions and the actuarial assessment of the comparability of pension schemes. The Government propose that pension schemes should be broadly comparable. The Institution of Professionals, Managers and Specialists, in a note on the subject, refers to the recent disposal

"of two groups of employees to the private sector by sale. These are Facilities Services Division with 950 employees to Procord Limited and Harwell Dosimeters with 7 employees to EMS Medical Limited. Employees in both groups were not allowed to remain in the UKAEA index linked pension schemes. Neither of the new schemes on offer from the new employers offered full index linking but both had been given the Government Actuary's Department certificate of 'broad comparability'.

The union sought independent actuarial advice from Union Pension Services Ltd, which concluded that

"the scheme offered in the Dosimeters disposal 'is not broadly comparable'"—

which would appear to conflict with the GAD certificate granted to the schemes. It goes on:

"Independent actuarial advice from the same source indicates that the FSD scheme"—

that is, the facility services division—

"would be 'broadly comparable' provided that certain assumptions about inflation were met. However it follows that if inflation were higher during the pensioner's lifetime the FSD scheme would not be 'broadly comparable'."

The IPMS adds:

"The fact that GAD can declare that schemes which do not have full index linking are 'broadly comparable' with UKAEA schemes that do, is a matter of great concern… It is equally alarming that UKAEA are now offering a pension scheme for one disposal"

that is considered by independent advice to be not broadly comparable. The institution continues:

"If this is happening now we can expect a similar approach from the Treasury to disposals constructed through schemes as defined in the current AEA Bill before Parliament. They will seek to minimise the transfer-in cost to new schemes and so worsen the benefits."

In summary, we believe that the scheme should be flexible, but should allow employees, many of whom have accrued a large number of years of service to the Government in the AEA, to retain pension benefits, which, as far as they are concerned, are a legitimate part of their package of employment. It is disgraceful that the Department is now considering removing those benefits from members of staff who have given years of loyal 104 service. Hence, we tabled amendments that seek to restructure the schedule in such a way that fairness and full comparability is retained.

I find it extraordinary that in this Bill, which seeks to democratise pension schemes and will appear in another place shortly after the Easter break, the Government are saying that the contingent liabilities fund—and therefore the transfer value—will be determined by the Treasury. At the very least, the Government should guarantee that such sums of money will be subject to testing by independent actuaries who have nothing to do with the Government. That is a matter of principle to which the Government should accede, irrespective of the outcome of our debates on these clauses.

There is also the question of what are the contingent liabilities. Under customary actuarial principles, they are either the sums necessary to protect all fund members if the fund were to collapse at an early stage as a result of the company going into liquidation or they may be the contingent liabilities carried forward for years into the future, to ensure that any individual receives his or her just deserts from the fund. Depending on the age profile of membership, that provides a wide range of possible figures.

Currently, the age profile of the existing membership is known and the fund liabilities are known. It would therefore be appropriate for the Minister to tell the Committee precisely what the contingent liabilities are as of today. If he cannot do so, that will put the Committee in serious difficulties. Without that information, the Committee will be taking a stab in the dark—protecting the interests of neither fund members nor taxpayers in our decisions.

Moreover, unless the Government spell out exactly what are the liabilities, no potential purchaser of the company can put a serious value on the company. In the case of Amersham International in 1981, the Under-Secretary of State for Energy said that the Government would continue to carry those liabilities. If it is the Government's intention, in privatising this company, to gain the best value for money for the taxpayer and if they have no satisfactory explanation for the change since 1981, it becomes impossible to justify how they are presenting the Bill.

The value of the company will be reduced as a function of the contingent liabilities.
