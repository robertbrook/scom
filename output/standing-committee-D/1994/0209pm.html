Finance Bill

Clause 38
CARS: ACCESSORIES FOR THE DISABLED

Amendment proposed [this day]:No. 81, in page 32, line 47, leave out

"designed solely for use by a chronically sick or disabled person"

and insert

"necessarily fitted to enable a sick or disabled person to use the car".—[ Mr. Nigel Jones.]

4.30 pm

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman
: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking amendment No. 125, in page 32, line 47, leave out from "designed" to end of line 48 and insert

"and related alterations necessarily installed to permit a chronically sick or disabled person to use the car".

Ms Margaret Hodge
(Barking)
: It was interesting that, in the period since the morning sitting, we have heard the Prime Minister assert that it is the business of Government to reduce inequality and it is appropriate in that context that we should be discussing a matter relating to people with disabilities. If the Government were generous in the drafting of the clause we could do something to tackle one aspect of the inequality encountered by people with disabilities.

The issue is important first, because working people who are chronically sick or disabled and require a company car are more dependent on such a car than are others because of the inadequacies of the public transport system. Secondly, the clause does not relate only to top executives. Many people working in the community, such as community nurses, are entitled to company cars. Such people do not earn high wages and depend on their car to do their job, so a proper drafting will be of benefit to them.

Thirdly, I—along with other members of the Committee, no doubt—have just come from hearing representations put to us by a lobby of people with disabilities. Those at the Remploy factory in my constituency, raised that issue in the context of their ability to get to work. As an aside, I should add that it appears that Remploy gives company cars only to the managers of particular factories, none of whom are disabled. No doubt that helps to increase the deficit which Remploy now faces and which may pose a threat to the jobs of some of the registered disabled. There appears to be a shared intent across the Committee on what we want from the clause, but there have been various interpretations of whether the current drafting of the clause meets that shared objective.

194 The hon. Member for Sutton and Cheam (Lady Olga Maitland) said this morning that to some people certain adaptations to a car were a luxury and to others they were a necessity. We agree with that, which is why we are anxious to know whether items such as automatic transmission or electric windows would be exempt from company car taxation. Such features may not be designed solely for the use of those registered as chronically sick or disabled, but they may be necessary for some such people to be able to use their company car. We are anxious that the wording should ensure that they get tax exemption on the additional costs of those features.

The other issue which is not clear from the drafting is whether disabled people have to have sole use of their company car. Several Conservative Members suggested that that was not the case, but we have difficulty accepting that in view of the current drafting of the clause. I hope that there will be no contention over the matter in the Committee. Several members of the Committee have asked whether the drafting meets the intent. If it does not, and if the amendment is not accepted, the clause should be redrafted in time for us to consider it on Report.

Mr. Mike O'Brien
(Warwickshire, North)
: There appears broadly to be a consensus in the Committee about what should be achieved. The question is whether the wording of the clause or the amendments achieves what we want to achieve.

The Government's proposal, which they put forward with good intention, contains difficulties. Problems have also arisen with amendment No. 81, and I accept that there are difficulties with amendment No. 125. There is a consensus about what should be achieved, but perhaps none of the options that we have discussed achieves it. I echo the call by my hon. Friend the Member for Barking (Ms Hodge) for the Minister to agree that there are difficulties in the drafting of the legislation. The best response that he could give to our constructive debate would be to say that he will take the legislation away and consider redrafting it.

My hon. Friend the Member for Sheffield, Attercliffe (Mr. Betts) rightly identified the difficulty with the wording of the clause. He pointed out that the Bill can be read either as saying "designed solely" or as saying "solely for use by". The two phrases have to be read separately, but they overlap in the Bill. I can well imagine arguments in court about how to interpret the clause. The words "designed solely" suggest that the benefit must be for the disabled, but the words "solely for use by" suggests that no one else may use the equipment. Indeed, it is not clear whether the car or the equipment is being discussed—from the wording, it would seem that just the equipment is involved. The phrase "solely for use by" excludes equipment that others may use, including, for instance, automatic transmission. That matter must be clarified.

Conservative Members have identified, to some extent, a difficulty in our amendment, but that difficulty also applies to the Government's clause. Amendment No. 125 states:

"related alterations necessarily installed to permit a chronically sick or disabled person to use the car".

However, a chronically sick or disabled person might never use the car, and might never have been intended to use the car. The equipment might be installed only to JPEG 195 increase the value of the car for resale. In that situation, the benefits in the legislation could be claimed, because the value of the car would be increased by the company that owned it. That would be permitted by amendment No. 125. However, the Government's clause would not prohibit that use either. The clause states:

"equipment designed solely for use by a chronically sick or disabled person".

One can imagine how alterations could be made to a vehicle, which is designed solely for use by a chronically sick or disabled person, simply to increase the value of that vehicle. No chronically sick or disabled person might ever use the vehicle.

Does the clause aim at what we want to achieve? Those who are chronically sick or disabled should have access to the equipment and car conversions that they need. Those adaptations should not necessarily be solely for the person who is to drive the vehicle. An able-bodied person might share a car with a disabled person and want to have the car converted for use by that disabled person. Would that fall within the parameters of the Government's intention in clause 38? Will the benefits in the clause apply to those sorts of conversions?

If the benefits do not apply, we are in danger of creating a discrimination between couples comprising two disabled people—who could both drive a converted car—and couples comprising one disabled person and one able-bodied person. In the latter case, the able-bodied partner would not be allowed to drive the converted vehicle. We are trying to reduce discrimination against disabled people, so we must be wary of the danger of creating a new discrimination. That issue must be addressed in the context of the wording of clause 38.

Given the high level of consensus on this issue among members of the Committee—we all know where we want to go and what we want to achieve. I hope that the Financial Secretary will accept that this constructive debate should result in his agreeing to re-examine the clause and, perhaps, return to the Committee with a better wording, which we all want.

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Sir George Young)
: This has been a positive and constructive debate. The architect of most of this part of the Finance Bill was my predecessor and we have been admiring the giant clauses and coming to terms with the vision of the design. As with many great buildings, some minor details are the responsibility of pupils and lesser artists. This little clause is one of them and it has generated a great deal of debate.

I shall begin by explaining what the problem was and putting the Government's solution in context. I shall then explain why I believe that our solution is preferable to the approach being pressed on us both in the amendments and by hon. Members on both sides of the Committee.

What problem does the clause seek to address? It introduces a change to the company car tax rules so that accessories designed for use by disabled people will not be included in the price of the car in calculating the benefit for tax purposes. Therefore, no tax will be charged on the value of the accessories.

If an employee has private use of a company car, he or she pays income tax on the benefit. That benefit is now calculated as 35 per cent. of the list price of the car, plus 196 accessories. Accessories include items specially fitted to enable a disabled person to use the car—for example, special hand controls for the clutch, brake and accelerator. In principle, and under normal tax rules, those items should be treated in the same way as other accessories and included in the price of the car in calculating the value of the benefit. That is because the benefit reflects what it would cost the individual to provide the car, plus accessories, at his or her own expense.

For that reason, as my predecessor explained when the treatment of those accessories under the new system for taxing cars was debated by this Committee in 1993, we did not immediately feel able to exempt the cost of the accessories from tax. However, subsequently it has been put to us that the necessary extra costs of adapting a company car for a disabled person are not the same as other accessories, for example, compact disc players, which are typically designed to enhance a driver's enjoyment. Without adaptations of the sort that we have been discussing, it simply would not be possible for a disabled person to drive a car on business or privately. However, because of necessary adaptations, a disabled person currently has to pay more tax than an able-bodied driver on what is otherwise an equivalent company car.

Those are the arguments on both sides. I believe—and it is clear that members of the Committee believe—that fiscal principles should give way to the need to ensure that disabled people do not face a higher tax charge simply because of adaptations to a company car, which are essentially to help them to pursue their employment. As a targeted response to reasonable representations, we believe that the right way forward is to exempt the charge for accessories designed solely for the disabled.

4.45 pm

There are two ways of approaching the matter. One is to focus on the accessories, which is the Government's approach. We believe that that is right because it is the accessories and the charge for the accessories that are at the root of the problem. The other approach, which is adopted in the amendments, is to focus not on the accessories but on the disabled person. There are difficulties, which I shall explain in a moment, in going down that route—in particular, the difficulty of definition. We have a precedent for VAT on accessories and that is the way to proceed.

I shall take the Committee through the argument. The clause is commendably short—one of the shortest in the Bill—as, indeed, are the amendments. We could have proceeded with an extensive attempt to define "disability", but the debate this morning has shown how difficult it would be to define that for the specific purpose of the exemption. The use of the wording "chronically sick or disabled" has been generally welcomed by the Committee. It has a familiar ring from social security legislation over the years. But that social security legislation leads on to longer definitions of "disability" and, in turn, to powers to issue guidance to local authorities.

There is nothing clear-cut in those definitions of the disabled that sheds much light on the debate today. The Government's Disability Discrimination Bill sets out a range of definitions which run to three pages. It is disproportionately lengthy for today's narrower purposes JPEG 197 and therefore is not appropriate. I am not sure that it would be a firm basis for tax legislation.

What we have done instead is to focus clause 38 specifically on accessories made to be sold to the disabled. That means that we can use the existing rules without more elaboration. The clause follows the approach in VAT legislation which zero rates goods and services for the handicapped, who are defined in VAT legislation as "chronically sick or disabled". That test is familiar to manufacturers and suppliers of accessories for the disabled, including car accessories.

I shall give assurances on some of the points raised. The hon. Members for Stoke-on-Trent, South (Mr. Stevenson) and for Sheffield, Attercliffe asked whether an accessory, such as a wide door which could also be used by the able-bodied, would be disqualified by the provisions in the clause. The answer is no. That is well established from more than a decade of VAT tribunal cases which have considered identical wording. Indeed, the general guidance to suppliers on VAT says,

"an article designed solely for the disabled remains eligible even though it may be suitable for use by other people too".

One hon. Member asked whether the disabled would have to prove their claims. In practice, the employer would return a reduced taxable benefit if he believed that the accessory qualified. It would then be for the Inland Revenue to raise any questions. In the great majority of cases, under the clause as drafted it would be obvious whether the accessory was a luxury aimed for the able-bodied or whether it was made for particular disabilities. That would be far less clear with the amendments. I am genuinely concerned that the Revenue would need to get involved in intrusive questioning about the employee's condition if we went down the alternative route of trying to link the exemption to the disability of the driver.

The hon. Member for Bristol, South (Ms Primarolo) asked whemer the Inland Revenue would be following VAT precedents. The answer is yes. Clearly, that is every advantage in adopting the same approach for the same problem.

Several hon. Members asked whether factory-fitted items such as electric windows and automatic gearboxes would be exempt. They would not. Those goods are largely marketed to able-bodied people and would not be zero rated for VAT nor would they reduce the company car tax.

The Government accept that such accessories, fitted by the manufacturer, would sometimes be an alternative way of suiting a car to a disabled person, rather man using a specialist conversion. But, unlike some hon. Members who have spoken, we do not believe that the car tax charge on such items should be eroded as it might be by the amendments—in particular amendment No. 81, which has no "chronic" test but relies on temporary claim of disability. We have stayed with the approach known to specialist manufacturers and their advisers.

Clause 38 has merits of brevity, familiarity and certainty. The amendments fall on the second and third counts.

If the amendments were accepted, there would be a risk of general relief for all sorts of luxury items because a hypothetical disabled person might have access to the car.

Mr. Stevenson

Sir George Young

Buying it without automatic transmission, taking out the gearbox and putting in automatic transmission seems to be a more expensive way of getting to the same end.

The hon. Gentleman's point is a criticism of amendment No. 125. It would cover the cost of alterations, but that is unnecessary. The car tax charge is on the cost of the car plus supplying and fitting accessories to it. There is no additional cost or specific charge on alteration costs. If the accessory supplied is exempted, its fitting costs and the costs of altering the car also fall out of the car tax charge.

The hon. Member for Monklands, East (Mrs. Liddell) asked for the yield of the clause. Most of the measures that are introduced by the Treasury have a yield, but this is a relieving measure. It costs. Because there are relatively few company car drivers, the total cost of the relief is small, but the benefit to the individual and the removal of a tax that is perceived to be unfair is significant. Hence the welcome for the clause.

Ms Hodge
: The right hon. Gentleman stated that the benefit to the individual is great but the loss of yield is JPEG 199 small. If someone with a disability needs power steering and automatic transmission, that could put another £2,000 or £3,000 on the cost of the car. That is quite a lot of money, especially if it is accepted that those who will take advantage of the measure will not, in general, be top executives. On the whole, the people who will take advantage of it will be those in the lower income bracket. Would it not, therefore, be worthwhile, if the Minister wants to measure the benefit to the individual, to amend the clause so that what are necessities to some but luxuries to others, are subject to tax exemptions?

Sir George Young
: I understand the hon. Lady's case, but if automatic transmission is exempted, for example, the mechanism that we are using can no longer be used for accessories. Questions must then be asked about the person driving the car, as we would not want to give relief on automatic transmission to someone who is able-bodied. That is why difficult questions must be asked about the definition of disabled drivers and disabled people. The Government believe that it is simpler to focus on the accessories and exempt from tax those accessories designed specifically for the disabled. That is the change, which was introduced in 1993, which has caused the perceived grievance.

The hon. Lady makes a case for going further, and there may be good grounds for it, but it is not the proposition before the Committee. It would cost more and it goes beyond the immediate purpose which is not to tax people for accessories which are essential for them to enjoy the use of a car. The Government's approach is less intrusive to enforce; it is easier for the tax people, for manufacturers and for employers. The VAT precedent is not over-restrictive—

Mrs. Helen Liddell
(Monklands, East)
: The right hon. Gentleman mentioned the loss of yield. Will he please quantify that loss?

Sir George Young
: As I said, the sums are small and I cannot quantify them, but, clearly, the bigger the exemption, the greater the cost, as one is reducing the tax charge by exempting more items. The sums are probably relatively small as there are few company drivers. The more that is exempt from tax—electric windows, power steering, automatic transmission, for example—the greater the loss of revenue, even though it may be from a small base.

Mrs. Liddell
: I am interested in the right hon. Gentleman's point. The global figure for company drivers will be minute. Is there scope to give us even a ballpark figure on the yields so that we can put it into context?

Sir George Young
: The costs in terms of the Financial Statement and Budget Report are negligible. It could be about £3 million a year if there were slightly more exemptions, such as automatic transmission, but, because we do not have much data about how many drivers there are, such estimates are necessarily speculative.

This is the right approach, focused on accessories using existing precedents. It avoids intrusive questions and difficult definitions and I hope that the Committee can now make progress and resist the amendment.

Clause 39
BENEFICIAL LOAN ARRANGEMENTS: REPLACEMENT LOANS

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Ms Primarolo
: I want to ask the Financial Secretary a few questions. It is an intriguing clause. It is a mystery because it was not mentioned in the Budget press release. It is primarily directed at closing schemes involving interest-free or low-interest loans from employers to higher-paid employees.

Under existing rules, an employee would be assessed on the benefit in kind representing the difference between the interest paid and the interest that would have been paid if a market rate had applied. Will the Financial Secretary explain what types of case the clause is designed to prevent? Although we expect the proper rate of tax to be paid on loans and do not want loopholes to appear, we are not clear as to the aim of the clause. It would be helpful if the Financial Secretary could tell us what happens now, but would not be permitted in the future.

Mr. Matthew Carrington
(Fulham)
: Perhaps I can help the hon. Lady. Those of us who are hardy annuals on the Committee remember that we debated this subject last year, which resulted in the then Financial Secretary agreeing to come back with the clause before us.

The clause resolves the problem that was described last year. There is general consensus in the House that companies should pay their employees a salary in cash and that they should not be encouraged to pay them in benefits. One of the benefits that has grown up largely because of quirks in the tax system is that of low-interest loans. They have been predominant in the financial sector.

JPEG 203 A problem arose in companies that wanted to stop providing beneficial loans at cheap interest rates to their employees. If they said that they would stop the scheme and would give the loans' portfolio to an outside lender whose business is providing loans to employees, the transference of the beneficial loans to the new lender would be at commercial rates of interest. However, those loans would still be treated as beneficial loans for tax purposes. I do not need to remind the Committee that the taxation rules for beneficial loans, even when they are provided at a commercial rate of interest, can be disadvantageous to the borrower. The Revenue will look at an assumed rate of interest on the beneficial loan that is frequently higher than the rate of interest on a commercial loan.

That is a complicated way of describing the problem. The clause is designed to reverse the position in which companies that wished to cease providing beneficial loans to their employees were encouraged not to do so. In fact, they had a financial disincentive against providing employees with commercial loans. This clause is complex because of all its anti-avoidance provisions. It allows companies to move their loan portfolios to a commercial lender and to remove themselves from the beneficial loan business without causing any financial disadvantage to their employees, other than that of losing a cheap loan.

I welcome the clause. I thank the Government for introducing it and I hope that is will not meet too much resistance in Committee.

Sir George Young
: The Committee is grateful to my hon. Friend the Member for Fulham (Mr. Carrington), not simply for supporting the provision, but for explaining, without notes or briefing, a complicated clause in terms that everyone in the Committee understood. I shall not try to replicate what he said. I confirm that the clause removes the possible tax obstacle to the provision of replacement loans in he circumstances that my hon. Friend described by replacing the anti-avoidance rule, which makes all replacement loans potentially taxable, with new rules that focus more precisely on possible avoidance. I commend the clause to the Committee.

Clause 40
RELIEF ON RE-INVESTMENT: PROPERTY COMPANIES ETC.

Mr. Alistair Darling
(Edinburgh, Central)
: I beg to move amendment No. 126, in page 34, line 7, leave out subsections (2) and (3).

The Chairman
: With this, we may take amendment No. 127, in page 34, line 18, leave out subsection (4).

Mr. Darling
