Interpretation of Part I

1.—(1) In this Part of this Schedule—"the 1988 Act" means the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988;

  • "the Corporation Tax Acts" has the meaning given by section 831(1)(a) of the l988 Act;
  • "the Gains Act" means the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992.

(2) This Part of this Schedule shall be construed as one with the Corporation Tax Acts.

General

2. If a transfer scheme transfers property, rights and liabilities to a successor company, then, subject to paragraph 3 and paragraphs 15 to 19 below, the following provisions shall apply for the purposes of the Corporation Tax Acts in their application in respect of any accounting period beginning on or after the date on which the transfer scheme comes into force, namely—

  1. any trade or part of a trade carried on by the Authority which is transferred in accordance with the transfer scheme to the successor company shall be treated as having been, at the time of its commencement and at all times since that time, a separate trade carried on by that company,
  2. the trade or trades carried on by the successor company on and after the date on which the transfer scheme comes into force shall be treated as the same trade or trades as that which, by virtue of paragraph (a) above, is treated as carried on before that date,

  3. all property, rights and liabilities of the Authority which are transferred in accordance with the transfer scheme to the successor company shall be treated as having been, at the time when they became vested in the Authority and at all times since that time, property, rights and liabilities of that company, and
  4. anything done by the Authority in relation to property, rights and liabilities which are transferred in accordance with the transfer scheme to the successor company shall be treated as having been done by the company.

Chargeable gains: general

3. Paragraph 2 above shall not apply for the purposes of corporation tax on chargeable gains; and no provision included in a scheme by virtue of paragraph 5(2)(a) of Schedule 1 to this Act shall have effect for those purposes.

4.—(1) Section 171(1) of the Gains Act (which makes provision in relation to the disposal of assets from one member of a group of companies to another member of the group) shall not apply where the disposal in question is a disposal in accordance with a transfer scheme from the Authority to a wholly-owned subsidiary of the Authority.

(2) In sub-paragraph (1) above "disposal" shall be construed in accordance with section 21(2) of the Gains Act (which relates to part disposals).

Chargeable gains: group transactions

5.—(1) For the purposes of section 179 of the Gains Act (company ceasing to be a member of a group), where any subsidiary of the Authority ("the degrouped company") ceases, by virtue of a qualifying transaction, to be a member of a group of companies including the Authority, the degrouped company shall not, by virtue of that transaction, be treated under that section as having sold, and immediately reacquired, any asset acquired from a company which was at the time of acquisition a member of that group.

      1. the transfer of any property, rights or liabilities of the Authority in accordance with a transfer scheme, or

  3. Expressions used in this paragraph and in section 179 of the Gains Act have the same meaning in this paragraph as they have in that section.

Mr. Page: We previously indicated that the Government would introduce a new clause relating to the tax requirements of the Bill. The new clause is the fruit of our considerations and it introduces the new schedule. Together they deal with certain taxation consequences arising from the exercise of powers set out in the Bill, and in particular the consequences of transferring property, rights and liabilities to a successor company or companies.

The provisions are technical and unavoidably detailed, but the broad aim, as with similar provisions in previous privatisation legislation, is to achieve "tax neutrality". Let me help the Committee by explaining what that means. Tax neutrality is about ensuring that the process of transferring AEA Technology from the public to the private sector does not, in itself, give rise to tax consequences.

The Bill envisages the transfer of certain property, rights and liabilities from one entity—the Authority—to another—the successor company—for no consideration. In normal circumstances, such a transfer would give rise to Col. 146 certain tax consequences. For example, a charge to capital gains tax might arise on the sale of shares in a successor company in respect of the transfer of assets to the successor company by the authority. Corporation tax consequences might arise from the cessation of one set of trading activities by the authority, and the commencement of trading by the successor companies. Stamp duty, and stamp duty reserve tax, would have to be paid on instruments transferring property under or pursuant to a transfer scheme.

Consistent with the principle of tax neutrality, the aim of the schedule, in broad terms, is to put the successor company in the same position for tax purposes as if it had always carried out the activities transferred to it from the authority. It is usual for such provisions to appear in privatisation legislation. However, as the Committee has discovered in its examination, the authority is not in quite the same position as many of the nationalised industries that have already been privatised.

As a scientific research organisation, the authority is currently entitled to and does claim exemption from corporation tax. It is also exempt from taxation on chargeable gains and certain types of income under section 512 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988 and section 271(7) of the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992. In practice, therefore, the authority currently enjoys exemption from corporation tax and capital gains tax.

Because of that exemption, the general principle of continuity of tax treatment cannot be applied without some specific provisions to deal with this particular set of circumstances. Some key elements of the picture are missing, and must be provided for.

I shall give the Committee an example of what the process involves by briefly outlining the provisions that relate to capital allowances. As yet, the authority does not have agreed values for assets that qualify for capital allowance purposes. Successor companies that inherit such assets would not be entitled to claim capital allowances in respect of them. The schedule provides a mechanism to determine such allowances, having regard to information concerning the market values for the assets concerned.

Mr. Ken Purchase (Wolverhampton, North-East): I agree with the Minister that these matters are extremely detailed and difficult to understand. Would not it have been helpful therefore if the Minister had prepared some examples of the effect the measures will have on a new comany? Those considering purchasing AEA technology would then be aware of their potential taxation commitments and would know that such matters had been discussed at this level. Examples would be helpful: does the Minister have any to hand?

Mr. Page: The hon. Gentleman wounds me because I have obviously failed to clarify the schedule's purpose, which is tax neutrality. The successor company will take over on a tax neutral basis and will not have to go through the anguish to which the hon. Gentleman rightly refers. The successor company will take over with a clean sheet. It will not have liabilities hanging over it, and uncalculated costs will not hit it. That is the purpose of the Bill.

Another consequence that flows from the authority's status is that, because the authority has to date been exempt from corporation tax and has never made taxable profits, it has never had to prepare tax accounts. In-so-far as it might have had notional trading losses, those have never Image Col. 147 been calculated. It is not the Government's intention to allow the successor companies to claim tax relief in respect of any such losses that might relate to activities transferred to them. There is no provision in the schedule for the successor companies to claim relief in respect of those notional losses.

The provisions are in standard form, and are a necessary part of setting up successor companies as independent trading entities. I commend them to the Committee.

Dr. Lewis Moonie (Kirkcaldy): Amendments (a) to (d), which I have tabled, may seem a little odd because they single out particular paragraphs in an otherwise unremarkable schedule. As the Minister said, the schedule is a normal part of such a Bill—although that does not necessarily make it easier to understand. The Minister has, however, made the provisions a little more enlightening than they were previously.

The amendments would wreck the tax provision and would, therefore, be unacceptable. The Opposition will obviously not press them to a vote, but they are intended to throw light on the process by which the Atomic Energy Authority will be divested of AEA Technology. The Opposition want the Government to explain how Government investment in the company—which has been heavy in past 10 years—will relate to the tax status of the new companies and how capital investment will be written off. The amendments also concern the vesting of past research, patents rights and know-how in successor companies and how the vesting of those relatively intangible assets relates to the tax status of such companies. In other words, it is about how to price years of research and people's endeavours over a long period. It also relates to the provisions that the Government will have to make for the new companies to be established.

The Government are expecting to make £200 million from the sale, so it seems pointless—to say the least—to write a cheque with one hand and to cash with the other.

Years of public investment in knowledge and research are going to be thrown away to the private sector, with no guarantee that an appropriate level of research activity will continue.
