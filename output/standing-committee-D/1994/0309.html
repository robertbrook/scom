I now turn to the five employee financial participation schemes, to explain how they ought to interface with the changing employment structure. The intention behind all those schemes is to encourage and motivate everyone whose work makes a business prosper and grow. That is why we encourage businesses to establish schemes by allowing them tax relief on their set-up costs, and why we give tax reliefs to employees and directors who participate in them. The schemes break down the barriers between employees and employers; they build an identity of interest between those who own the businesses and those who work in them; they improve the profitability of the nation's businesses; and, as a result, they benefit all shareholders, employees and customers.

The main cost of the schemes falls, quite properly, on those who chiefly benefit from them: the companies and businesses themselves. It is the shareholders or the partners who bear the major burden, not the customer or taxpayer.

I shall describe briefly the five schemes that are covered by the new clause. Approved profit-sharing schemes enable companies to give employees shares free, and free from income tax. The company sets up a trust and gives it money—typically from profits—to buy the company's shares. The trustees then allocate the shares to individual employees who must agree to leave them in the trust for a minimum of two years. After five years, employees can take their shares out of the trust completely free of income tax. In 1992–93 some 740,000 employees received free share allocations worth £310 million. A wide range of companies have profit-sharing schemes—Sainsburys is just one example.

Under approved savings-related share option schemes, employees agree to save money through the save-as-you-earn scheme and are granted options to buy shares in their employing company with the money saved. They contract to save a specific sum of between £10 and £250 a month over a five-year period and at the end of the term their savings earn a tax-free bonus. If they wish, employees may leave their savings for a further two years to earn a larger tax-free bonus. When the bonus date is reached, employees can choose to exercise their options using the tax-free proceeds of the savings contract or they may take the whole sum in cash. In 1992–93, some 590,000 employees were granted options over shares worth £1.88 billion. Again, a wide range of companies has such schemes—Cadbury Schweppes is one.

The third scheme is the registered profit-related pay scheme, under which tax relief is given on that part of pay which varies with changes in the profits of the business for which the employee works. PRP is tax free up to a limit of the lower of 20 per cent, of pay or £4,000. The scheme must be registered with the Inland Revenue before its start date and it must specify how the total sum to be distributed as PRP is to be calculated. It must also ensure that at least 80 per cent, of the employees in the business participate in the scheme. We discussed PRP at length last week. Some 2 million people in 8,000 companies are covered by PRP schemes.

Fourthly, qualifying employee share ownership trusts—ESOTs—are trusts which conform to statutory rules and are set up for the benefit of a company's Col. 662 employees to provide them with easy access to shares in the company. The trusts acquire shares and distribute them to employees, either directly or through an approved profit-sharing scheme. The tax relief available includes corporation tax relief for a company's payments to the trustees and capital gains tax roll-over relief on certain conditions for people who dispose of their shares to a qualifying ESOT Those four schemes cover some 3.5 million people, so the Committee will understand that new clause 5 has wide application.

I come finally to the fifth scheme. Under approved executive or discretionary share option schemes, selected employees are granted options to buy shares at a price fixed by reference to the market value of the shares at the date on which the option is granted. There are limits on the size of options that can be granted. There are limits on the size of options that can be granted to an employee— the value of the shares held at the date on which any option is granted must not exceed four times the amount of the employee's pay or £100,000, whichever is the greater. Executives must hold their options for at least three years before they can exercise them free of an income tax charge and at least three years must elapse between any tax-relieved exercise. If executives then sell the shares, capital gains tax is payable at 40 per cent., subject to the usual rules, on the difference between the sale and the option price. In 1992–93 some 80,000 employees and executives were granted options over shares worth £1.6 billion.

As I said earlier, the clause is designed to open those schemes fully to part-time workers. In introducing it, we have also had very much in mind the decision of the House of Lords last year in the case of Right v. the Secretary of State for Employment ex parte the Equal Opportunities Commission and another. It is appropriate that we are having this debate on the day after the International Women's day. The decision relates to the way in which European equal pay and equal treatment law affects the treatment of part-timers under United Kingdom legislation.

Current tax legislation relating to the schemes either requires or permits an employer to exclude part-time workers, as defined by reference to the number of hours worked, from participation in the schemes. The new clause removes those provisions and is designed to have the effect that part-time workers must be treated in exactly the same way as full-time workers in each scheme.

When we were planning the legislation, we understood that to treat part-time workers fairly and in accordance with the House of Lords' decision it was necessary to treat all part-time workers alike, whether employees or directors. That is why the new clause removes restrictions on the participation of part-time directors in the schemes. Since the clause was published, we have reconsidered that in the light of representations from the Association of British Insurers.

We accept that a there is a distinction to be drawn in this context between part-time directors and part-time employees. The role of non-executive directors is to stand a little apart from the day-to-day commercial issues of the company and to bring to that company a measure of independent judgment. They cannot easily do that if their personal interests are too closely tied to the company and its share price.

We therefore thought it right to stand back and reconsider the position of part-time directors. After doing so, we are satisfied that, within the spirit of the House of Lords' decision, we can properly take part-time directors, as distinct from part-time employees, out of the new clause altogether. As I have already announced in reply to a question, we propose to bring forward on Report, amendments which will ensure that the provisions in the new clause about the inclusion of part-time workers in all those employee financial participation schemes will apply only to part-time employees and not to part-time directors. Part-time directors will continue to be barred from participation in approved executive option schemes.

I must stress that, in terms of the executive option scheme, the amendment will apply only to part-time directors. We have no plans to exclude part-employees from executive schemes. There can be no reason of principle to discriminate against part-time employees as between one scheme and another. Executive share option schemes are already discretionary in the sense that employees can choose which employees, or groups of employees, to include in them and companies must be left to make their own judgments about where shareholders' interests lie.

I believe that we have now reached the right position in relation to part-time directors and that the new clause, when the necessary amendments have been introduced on Report, will be seen by Members on both sides of the House to be a satisfactory resolution which avoids discrimination and yet retains a sensible balance.

Ms Dawn Primarolo: (Bristol, South): I beg to move, that the clause be read a Second time.

I shall put the clause in context and remind the Committee of the Government's charter commitment on visits to firms. In 1992, the then Chief Secretary to the Treasury instigated a series of fundamental expenditure reviews—FERs—for each Government Department. This was, in our view, a necessary cost-cutting exercise because of the Government's economic incompetence. The key result of the Customs and Excise FER is that 4,015 jobs will be cut in the coming four years and that the number of collections—administration—regions will be cut from 21 to 14. The number of VAT staff will be reduced from 5,400 to 3,395—a reduction of more than 2,000 staff. That is relevant to the new clause because we believe that the cuts will undermine Customs and Excise's ability to continue effectively to collect outstanding VAT, and to ensure especially that the complex regulations can be operated properly.

Image Col. 695 Last week, the National Audit Office published its report on the Government's decision to write off more than £800 million of uncollected VAT in 1993, for various reasons. We think that the Government's policy of cutting the number of VAT officers will continue to frustrate effective collections.

Those cuts came on top of spending cuts in 1992, single market changes, and market testing—all of which have produced extremely low morale in Customs and Excise. The Government's plans to target VAT officers' visits to business are inadequate. Small traders—those with a turnover of less than £100,000 a year—generate 6 per cent, of the revenue collected, but absorb 22 per cent, of the VAT control effort. The FER therefore proposed abandoning altogether first visits to a large proportion of small traders and abandoning routine visits to small and medium-sized businesses that are thought to be compliant—that is, making their returns correctly.

That raises four specific issues. The first is the question of public service. A major part of the inspectors' work is helping businesses to comply with complicated VAT law. That is achieved by one-to-one visits and fulfilling the VAT charter commitment, issued by the Government in 1992, to visit every newly registered trader within three years.

The visits and, therefore, the charter commitment would be abandoned under the proposal in the fundamental expenditure review. Should that be followed through, it would again demonstrate the meaninglessness of the provisional charters and the inability of the Government to connect reality with their aspirations for fairness and openness.

The Government propose to replace the visits with what they call educational seminars. That is totally inadequate. It does not respond to what businesses want, but will increase compliance costs and reduce the level of service.

The Public Accounts Committee, in its report "The Cost to Business of Complying with VAT Requirements"—41st report, 1993–94—stated in paragraph 25:

"The Department have recognised the importance of early contact with newly-registered traders as being most likely to need advice and education and who often face proportionately the highest compliance costs".

It continued in paragraph 33:

"in 1992/93, some 11,000 newly-registered traders were overdue a visit by one month. Given the benefits of early contact, we"—

the PAC

"look to the Department to reduce the number of overdue visits as a matter of priority".

The clear advice is that early visits followed by regular visits to businesses assist with compliance costs and, most important, assist in the effective collection for the Government of the money that is outstanding through VAT.

A Customs report, entitled "The VAT Business Needs Survey", issued in November 1994, revealed that 89 per cent, of businesses wanted the same or more frequent VAT visits and that they would prefer a one-to-one consultation to a seminar. The Federation of Small Col. 696 Businesses also supported the principle of regular visits, yet the Government are going in the opposite direction.

First visits are highly effective, as VAT can be explained within the context of a trader's particular business and accountancy system. Seminars would not cater for continuing need, and, even if they did, staff reductions will mean that the closure of a large number of VAT offices will make attendance at seminars more and more difficult due to their location.

The second point is about tax yield. The proposed cuts are counterproductive. There is a clear link between the number of visits and overall compliance. Consequently, there is a considerable risk that evasion will increase significantly, undermining the long-term tax yield.

A recent experiment in Birmingham bears out that suspected link. Of a random selection of 300 businesses, half were visited and half were not. In the following year, the tax yield of the businesses that had been visited increased by 20 per cent., whereas the tax yield of those that had not been visited increased by only 8 per cent. That points to the obvious fact that visits have a preventive effect on tax evasion.

The Government say that they will maintain the tax yield by better targeting, but that ignores the point that the overall number of visits is more important than the result of one visit. Their plan is underpinned by a new risk assessment system, but they seem to have little or no idea of how that would work.

My third point relates to tax equities. The removal of controls creates unfair competition because those traders who are honest and who comply fully with the VAT requirements are undercut by those who are not honest and do not comply. That is a particular problem for small businesses, which often have to choose between committing the same fraud or going under because their businesses are not viable.

My final point relates to payments and accounts. It is claimed that the FER will remove the burdens on business. We do not believe that that is the case. The Government have two more VAT proposals: VAT annual returns and a simplified system. That would involve small businesses in assessment of turnover by customs and lead to filing returns and keeping records, which would make the system more complicated.

Our new clause would enforce the charter commitment of visiting every newly registered business within three years by making it a statutory duty. That would ensure VAT compliance and, therefore, protect the tax yield. It would also safeguard jobs, which is very important, and would provide for important prevention work in ensuring a sensible compliance with our tax laws.

I hope that the Government will accept the new clause as uncontentious and vote for it, because it would simply make the charter commitment a statutory requirement.
