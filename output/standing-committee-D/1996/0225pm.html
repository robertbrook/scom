The debate about the role of the Medical Practices Committee, in relation to part I of the Bill, flows naturally from this morning's long debate about the importance of evaluation. Conservative Members did not use their voting strength to uphold our argument, but we are sure that in their hearts and minds they were persuaded by it. We want sufficient strategic capacity, under the Bill, to ensure proper distribution of general practitioners. No disadvantage should arise out of whether a general practitioner's patients are in pilot or non-pilot areas. The strategic role of the Medical Practices Committee is well established and developed, and we want to ensure consistency in the quality control offered by the MPC under parts I and II of the Bill. Otherwise, no adequate quality checks will be possible on general practitioners' practice.

It is worth reflecting on the effectiveness of the Medical Practices Committee. Since its creation in 1948, by virtue of its verification of doctors' capabilities and the legitimacy of their qualifications, there has been only one bogus general practitioner. Other doctors, such as hospital consultants, do not undergo the double checking of which the Medical Practices Committee provides the second tier, and we read, not often but more often than we should like, of bogus doctors being appointed as locums or to other positions in hospitals. This morning, we clearly identified the considerable deregulation that the Bill proposes. We want to ensure that that deregulation Image Col. 121 does not extend to evaluating a doctor's ability to practise, or to the distribution of doctors, in a way that could jeopardise patients' safety.

Later in our proceedings, we will consider other aspects of the Medical Practices Committee's responsibilities for general medical services that the Bill amends. The Medical Practices Committee is worried that no progress has been made on the question of who places GPs on the medical list.

The Minister believes that health authorities, as responsible public bodies, can be expected to do that properly, but we fear that not all health authorities have the ability to do that. They do not make other medical appointments. NHS trusts appoint hospital doctors. Some health authorities have already suggested that if such a function were delegated to them, they would ask the MPC to act as their agent. In the light of such recognition by some forward-looking health authorities, why not enshrine the responsibility for placing GPs on the medical list in the Bill?

The MPC is not satisfied that health authorities will act in accordance with the Bill's intention to allow on the medical lists only those GPs whose prospective partners have received approval in principle. Without sanctions, the system will be open to abuse. For example, if the MPC does not approve in principle a request that is supported by a health authority for an additional partner, there is little to prevent the health authority from ignoring the MPC's ruling and granting the application.

The MPC is worried that that will happen sooner rather than later and that the proposed safeguards will be insufficient. Clearly, no one wants such an outcome. Therefore, we shall press our amendments to safeguard the Bill's requirements.

Government amendment No. 111 deals with central work force planning. The British Medical Association and the MPC share anxieties about it. The Bill creates two different work force planning systems. Under one system, health authorities—boards in Scotland—make decisions through the Secretary of State about doctors who join pilot schemes. Under the other system, the MPC, or its Scottish equivalent, has a remit for determining GP recruitment or replacement under the current GP contract.

It is clear that central work force planning is essential for the continuing nationwide delivery of high-quality care. That is linked to the importance of strategic oversight by the MPC. The British Medical Association believes that one overall body should be established to examine work force planning and distribution between health authorities and that that responsibility should not rest solely with the Secretary of State.

In response to the British Medical Association's concerns, not least on checking admissions to the medical list, there have been extensive discussions involving the GMC, the MPC and the Department of Health about maintenance of a national overview of the medical work force and its distribution. The Col. 122 Department's stated intention is to establish a national group to bring together the MPC and the resource allocation group to examine work force distribution between health authorities. That group is intended to advise the Secretary of State and the MPC on the boundaries that govern decisions on private schemes and on the appointment of part II GPs.

The national group, the MPC and the Secretary of State would all be informed by local work force plans developed by the health authority in conjunction with local medical committees. The MPC and the national group could each raise concerns about those plans with the Secretary of State where necessary. The national group would be charged with maintaining a national overview of the medical work force and its distribution as well as resources issues.

The new national group can, in the Department's, view, be non-statutory, but the statutory MPC would be expected to act within set parameters. That gives further cause for concern. We are convinced that emasculation of the Medical Practices Committee is intended. In the absence of any proven effective means of safeguarding the quality and safety of general practice in the tricky business of innovation and piloting into new waters, we believe that there may be a danger to quality of treatment and potentially to the safety of patients.

Our amendment on whistleblowing should be included in the Bill. It is essential that no doctor should find that they are less able to protect the interests of patients because of any new employment arrangements envisaged by the Bill. Doctors should be able to speak out if the conditions in which their patients are treated or are being cared for give cause for concern.

Doctors do not blow the whistle lightly. Almost inevitably, it involves revealing confidential information about a patient, a group of patients or the conduct of colleagues. In almost every case, the action is taken only after the most careful consideration.

Doctors' freedom to blow the whistle represents an affirmation of the traditional basis of medical practice that puts doctors' first loyalty to their patients and recognises their wider societal obligations as secondary to that. It also recognises that doctors must be free to exercise clinical judgment in respect of individual patients.

In its 1993 publication "Medical Ethics Today", the BMA observed that there can be no free or informed debate on health matters if those with an expert contribution to make are excluded from the right to contribute. That is also recognised in paragraph 330 of the Whitley council's terms and conditions of service for hospital, medical and dental staff, which stresses:

"A practitioner shall be free, without prior consent of the employing authority, to publish books, articles etc. and to deliver any lecture or speech, whether on matters arising out of his or her hospital service or not."

If general practitioners are not employed on those terms—we know that the trusts' contracts of employment vary considerably—that freedom is compromised.

As early as the early 1990s, whistleblowing became an issue for all health workers. It was brought to the fore by the sacking for gross misconduct of nurse Graham Pink who publicly voiced concerns about the welfare of acutely ill geriatric patients in a ward that he considered to be dangerously understaffed. In 1990, Mr. Pink was charged with a breach of confidentiality when he informed the media of the situation after the health authority said that it could not improve staffing levels.

The case raised anxieties for all health professionals, particularly as it coincided with the so-called gagging clauses introduced into the employment contracts of trust hospitals. That has important implications for the way health professionals support or isolate colleagues for whistleblowing.

The BMA considers it an ethical duty for doctors to take action against unsafe standards of care. It follows that doctors should also support, and not discriminate against, those who are brave enough to speak out about standards which are unacceptable. Although Col. 124 paragraph 330 of the Whitley terms and conditions of service remains in force for staff on existing contracts, the freedom of NHS trusts to negotiate contracts for new staff has in some cases led to an attempt by trusts to impose new types of clauses within contracts.

An example of those confidentiality clauses, the so-called gagging clauses, is as follows:

"in the course of your work you may have access to confidential information which should not be disclosed to any other person unless in the pursuit of your duties or with the specific permission of the employing authority."

It is patently ridiculous for a doctor or any other member of staff who wants to raise concerns about the treatment of patients in the hospital to have to seek the permission of the employing authority before doing so. We want to ensure that doctors still have that freedom to speak out as a result of the Bill.

It is also worth recording that the General Medical Council has now further clarified its attitude, making it clear that the first duty of the doctor is to the care of his patient. It adds that a doctor has a wider duty "to protect all patients". That duty to protect all patients is defined by the GMC as follows:

"You must protect patients when you believe that a colleague's conduct, performance or health is a threat to them.


Before taking action you should do your best to find out the facts. Then, if necessary, you must tell someone from the employing authority or from a regulatory body. Your comments about colleagues must be honest. If you are not sure what to do, ask an experienced colleague. The safety of patients must come first at all times."

There is a clear consensus in the medical profession about the importance of doctors' freedom to speak out. We could spend considerably more time addressing the kind of organisational issues that would obviate the need for this course of last resort. But I am sure that we will return to that on many future occasions.

The amendments can be seen as protecting the interests of patients in two ways. First, they ensure that the Medical Practices Committee continues to have proper oversight of pilots. Secondly, they ensure that the powers that GPs currently have as independent contractors to speak out on behalf of their patients are not removed from them should they become employed by NHS trusts.
