Mr. Malone: I am grateful to you, Sir Alan, for your explanation and for agreeing to take the starred Image Col. 43 amendments for the convenience of our debate. It makes a lot of sense to do so.

It might be helpful to the Committee if I set out the background to the amendment and those grouped with it. On Second Reading my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Health announced that we should table amendments in Committee to ensure that only members of the NHS family would be able to hold contracts with health authorities for the provision of services under the Bill. The amendments deliver that commitment with regard to pilot schemes.

Following discussions with the medical profession, I must give notice that we shall also table equivalent amendments relating to the more permanent arrangements that are established after the pilot schemes. That is a consequence of our policy. We shall also table further amendments in due course specifying who will be permitted to put forward proposals for pilot schemes.

The key provisions are set out in new clause 19 in respect of pilot schemes involving personal medical services and in new clause 20 in respect of schemes involving personal dental services. The new clauses state clearly that agreements, or contracts, for such schemes may be held only by specified individuals or bodies. The conditions, which are tightly drafted, are listed in subsection (2) and further defined in subsection (3) of the new clauses.

For the avoidance of doubt—although it is not strictly necessary to do so—we shall table additional amendments specifying that only those specified individuals or bodies and no others will be permitted to put forward proposals for pilot schemes. That will be done for the sake of neatness. It seems most unlikely that anyone who is not qualified to run a pilot scheme would bother to put together a proposal, but concerns about that matter have been expressed to us and we are glad to tidy up the matter.

We devised the list in the new clauses of those who comprise the NHS family in such a way as to ensure that key groups who work in the NHS are included and to put beyond doubt the fact that commercial organisations are excluded. In addition to general practitioners, general dental practitioners and trusts, the list includes individuals who contribute to NHS services as employees of general medical or dental practices or of trusts.

The new clauses will ensure that key healthcare professionals who are involved in the delivery of primary health care will be ale to put forward proposals and hold contracts. The most straightforward and talked-about example is practice nurses employed by GP practices. Other examples include community nurses employed by NHS trusts and practice managers who may want to put forward a proposal with a group of professionals or a group of GPs. The provisions also ensure that those people may form themselves into partnerships or companies, if that is convenient, as a vehicle for carrying out their duties and providing services under the Bill.

Col. 44 I assure the Committee that we have been extremely careful in the drafting of the amendments and new clauses to rule out the possibility that anyone other than members of the NHS family may have an interest in such a company, be that a beneficial or technical interest. There is no way out of the circle that we have made round the NHS family.

The principle that all professional groups encompassed by the definition of the NHS family should be treated equally by the Bill is fully respected in the amendments. Amendments Nos. 99, 100 and 101 do away with the provisions which until now have given a guaranteed right to GPs, general dental practitioners and trusts to have their proposals put forward, but not to others in the NHS family. That right has now been extended so that anyone who is qualified under the amended legislation before the Committee to put together a pilot scheme has the right to have that scheme assessed by the Secretary of State. That has been widely welcomed, particularly by the nursing profession and others who play such an important role in the delivery of primary health care.

We shall consider on their merits all proposals that meet the criteria that we have set, regardless of the professional group from which they come. We shall, however, expect all proposals to show how they intend to secure the medical or dental involvement that all pilot schemes will need. For a pilot proposal to be credible, it is important that the partnership and the team that will provide the range of services encompassed in the pilot proposal are clearly set out.

There has been concern about the possibility of commercialisation resulting from the Bill. That has always been an argument at the periphery of what the Bill is about. It was clear from the consultation exercises that preceded the White Papers, "Primary Care: Delivering the Future" and "Choice and Opportunity", that the mainstream of change that would take place in primary care would be provided by those who already provide it. Be that at the periphery or not, we have listened to those concerns about commercialisation and the amendments respond to those concerns. They bring back to the spotlight the purpose of the Bill—to provide opportunities for those who already deliver NHS primary care to do so in new and better ways. The amendments and new clauses have been discussed comprehensively with the General Medical Services Committee of the British Medical Association, which agrees that they are the most appropriate way of achieving our shared objectives. I welcome the fact that we now stand firmly on the same square as the General Medical Services Committee on the issue.

We have also had discussions with the Royal College of Nursing. I had such a discussion about the framework and detail of the amendments last week and I am glad to tell the Committee that it recognises the opportunities presented by our approach and that there will be a more level playing field among healthcare professionals who provide primary care.

Amendment No. 98 refers to NHS trust powers. It is our policy to enable NHS trusts to put forward Image Col. 45 proposals and to provide services under pilots. There was some doubt about the vires of that in terms of the way trusts are constructed and the amendment puts beyond doubt their ability to do so. It also enables trusts to provide piloted services as members of a company, with other members of the NHS family—although, in the spirit of what I told the Committee earlier, not with anybody else. In effect, the company must be totally owned by the trust and the other members of the relevant NHS family.

The Government amendments and new clauses meet completely the concerns that have been voiced in the public domain about the potential of commercialisation. Many such concerns have been vastly overdone. The issue was at the periphery of the debate about developments in primary care. I hope that the Committee can accept the amendments and new clauses as dealing comprehensively with any anxieties.

The Secretary of State introduced the White Paper "Choice and Opportunity" in the House last autumn. By opening up provision of primary care to the private sector, the Secretary of State sought to suggest that the provision of primary care by any other body was no different from provision under the independent contractor status that GPs have enjoyed since 1948. He may not have thought that there was a difference, but the BMA certainly did. At Committee stage in another place, the Under-Secretary of State, Baroness Cumberlege acknowledged that the Government:

So, in introducing the Bill in another place in Committee, the Government's conviction was clear: they want commercial provision in primary care. Why are the Opposition so opposed to that idea?

The first reason is that we believe that commercial companies operating as providers or procurers of primary care provide a threat to the basic principle of a publicly funded NHS, which is universal in its access. That view is shared by most GPs: when surveyed by a recent poll for General Practitioner magazine seven out of 10 were against commercial companies sponsoring their services.

The second reason is that the presence of the commercial interest creates what was well described by the BMA as a third party in the consulting room. It threatens to come between patient and doctor as another potential source of influence and conflict of interest.

For example, if I take my elderly mother to the doctor to see about treatment and bandaging of her leg ulcers, I do not want her to feel that the decision to prescribe a treatment is influenced by any consideration other than her clinical needs. I do not want her to be persuaded to use the products of the company that initiated the pilot that now provides her care. Nor does she want to feel that suggestions that she should go into a nursing home are motivated by the fact that the doctors who run the pilot have a nursing home and want to keep it full. Conflict of interest is inevitable when commercial factors are involved in primary care clinical judgments. It is unavoidable and works against the best interests of the patient.

The third reason for our opposition, has not been properly aired. There is a struggle to maintain equity between patients in primary care. The risks of cherry-picking—accepting onto lists of pilot schemes those patients who are young and fit and whose healthcare costs are low—would be irresistible where profitability became a driving consideration. The evidence for that can be seen in the selective criteria applied, for instance, to the membership of some health maintenance organisations in the United States. We know that the Government do not accept those arguments and are intent, for the time being at least, on appeasing the BMA.

The two new clauses before us this morning represent a clear retreat from the Government's earlier and what we believe to be their true position. It is not a retreat born out of conviction. They have introduced the redrafted new clauses only because the BMA threatened to campaign against them during and in the run-up to the election. The conviction that led to the inclusion of the earlier proposals in the White Paper has been wrapped up in expediency and simply kept out of sight for the time being. The truth is that, for all their fine words, the Government pose a profound threat to the founding principles of the NHS because they believe in the importance of commercialisation.

Col. 48 The parliamentary management of the Government's retreat has been bizarre. A raft of amendments were tabled to the Bill in another place just before Report. Half were withdrawn not to reappear, as had been intended, on Third Reading. Before Second Reading in the Commons, the Secretary of State said that the Government would be tabling an amendment in Committee to confine the pilots to the NHS family. The problem stems from a disagreement between the BMA and the Government about the definition of the NHS family. It is important for the purposes of understanding exactly what the Government are up to that we tease out what has been going on. The BMA defined the NHS family as NHS trusts and their employees and GPs, dentists, the nurses who work with them and the staff who work with the nurses. It was worried that the definition of NHS employees and pilot scheme employees kept the door to commercialisation of general practice wide open. New clause 16 defined an NHS employee as an individual employed or engaged under a contract for services by an NHS trust, a medical practitioner or a medical practitioner who is providing personal medical services in accordance with a pilot scheme. The phrase

"or engaged under a contract for services"

also appeared in the definition of a pilot scheme employee. In effect, that means that any company working under a contract for services with the NHS could be regarded as a member of the NHS family. So, for example, companies such as Rentokil, BUPA and PPP Healthcare would all be NHS family members under that definition.

So the phrase

"or engaged under a contract for services"

has been dropped in the new clauses that we are considering today, which are otherwise the same as new clauses 15 and 16.

We understand that the British Medical Association is now satisfied that the loopholes regarding the provision of personal medical services have been closed but it remains concerned that there is nothing in the Bill to prevent a private company from proposing a pilot scheme.
