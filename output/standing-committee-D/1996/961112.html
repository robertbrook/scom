legislation Draft Health and Safety (Repeals and Revocations) Regulations 1996

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Sir Paul Beresford): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Health and Safety (Repeals and Revocations) Regulations 1996.

On 23 October, I laid before Parliament proposals for the Health and Safety (Repeals and Revocations) Regulations 1996. The regulations are being made under section 37 of the Deregulation and Contracting Out Act 1994 which allows the removal, without replacement, of certain existing health and safety statutory provisions that have been shown to be outdated and to have no practical effect. The latest proposals remove 16 pieces of redundant legislation relating specifically to textile and agricultural workplaces.

I come first to the textile provisions, some of which have reached their 90th birthday and are long overdue for retirement, if not burial. They cover three subjects—artificial humidification, kiers and cotton shuttles. In the early part of the century, textile workers were exposed to unpleasant and sometimes dangerous working conditions caused by a combination of high temperatures and humidity. The steam injection systems used to humidify workrooms were controlled by hand. Regular thermometer and hygrometer readings acted as a check that humidification levels were being properly controlled, and the law required these readings to be taken in a prescribed manner at specified times during the working shifts. Steam is no longer used as a means of humidification and modern monitoring and control systems are automated. The related legislation is simply redundant.

I shall explain what a kier is for those who, like me until recently, were unaware that it is a big vat used for boiling textiles. It incorporates a system that allows water to percolate through the textiles. The regulations apply to kiers in print works, bleaching and dyeing works, or works where cotton or cotton waste is bleached. They address the risks from scalding by hot liquid or steam and lay down systems of work for routine entry to kiers for operational and maintenance purposes. Few, if any, kiers are still in use. Even if some still survive, the specific requirements set out in the regulations are now superseded by modern across-the-board health and safety legislation.

The regulations on cotton shuttles, prohibit the use of shuttles that are threaded by mouth suction in cotton cloth factories, because of the risk of Col. 4 contracting a serious disease such as tuberculosis. Fortunately, technological changes in the industry mean that the regulations are obsolete.

The agricultural provisions cover four subjects—field machinery, in which I include the provisions on threshers and stationary balers, ladders, workplaces, and poisonous substances.

The regulations on field machinery cover the sale and hire of machines as well as their use. From 1 January 1997, they will be duplicated by the Provision and Use of Work Equipment Regulations 1992 and the Supply of Machinery (Safety) Regulations 1992. They are simply not needed.

Regulations on ladders place an obligation on agricultural employers to ensure that ladders provided for use by their employees are properly constructed and used in a safe manner. The construction and use of ladders in all sectors of business is now covered by modern across-the-board legislation and manufacturing standards.

Similarly, the requirements in the regulations covering the safety of agriculture workplaces are duplicated by those elsewhere, particularly those in the Workplace (Health, Safety and Welfare) Regulations 1992. The more modern regulations deal with the working environment in all workplaces, including ventilation, temperature, cleanliness, safe movement around the workplace, and falls from a height. They also deal with welfare issues such as access to washing facilities and sanitary conveniences.

Much of the Agriculture (Poisonous Substances) Act 1952 has already been repealed. Those sections which remain refer to the taking of samples of substances to which the Act applies, either because they are listed in the Act or in orders made under it. The sampling procedures are no longer used by health and safety inspectors who have powers to take samples under the Health and Safety at Work, etc. Act 1974 instead.

The Health and Safety Commission has consulted widely with industry on the proposals. The responses received demonstrated that there was no objection from those affected, by which I mean organisations representing employers and employees, to the proposals, which I therefore commend to the Committeee.

Many of the regulations that we are considering affect the agricultural industry, which has serious health and safety problems. Last year, the Health and Safety Executive cut agricultural inspectors from 169 Col. 6 to 75, convictions have fallen to their lowest in more than five years, yet deaths in the sector have increased by more than one third. Rather than use parliamentary time phasing out some of these irrelevant regulations, perhaps the Minister will explain how he can sanction reducing the number of health and safety inspectors in agriculture while the number of accidents in agricultural work is so high. Quite literally, the cuts presided over by the Minister can damage people's health.
