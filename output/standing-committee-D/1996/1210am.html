clause Clause 15
title GRANTS TO PROMOTERS OF GRANT-MAINTAINED SCHOOLS

Mr. John Gunnell (Morley and Leeds, South): I beg to move amendment No. 154, in page 14, line 9, after "school" insert

"for which the authority is satisfied that there is demonstrable public support".

The clause will assist people who wish to promote a grant-maintained school. It is an open clause—in fact, it is one of the most generous measures that we might ever see from the Government. It states:

"The funding authority may, in the case of any persons proposing to establish a grant-maintained school, make grants to those persons in respect of expenditure incurred or to be incurred by them in connection with"

and then goes on to refer to the formulation of proposals and the preparation of particulars of proposed premises.

The clause deals with a grant, without any conditions attached to it, which can be made to anyone who wants to set up a grant-maintained school. It says that the funding authority can require the grant to be repaid at some point in whole or in part, if it wishes to, but it places no conditions on the person receiving the grant. Theoretically, any person who just fancies setting up a grant-maintained school can apply for a grant. As there are no conditions attached to the grant, is there any reason why the funding authority should not make it? It is one of the few really open invitations Col. 332 from the Government, via the funding authority, to anyone who wants to establish a grant-maintained school.

The amendment would insert into the clause the words:

"for which the authority is satisfied that there is demonstrable public support".

In order for a grant-maintained school to be promoted and for the Government to pay money, via the funding authority, surely there must be some idea that people want such a school in an area. The amendment is simple. It would introduce consultation—an element of democracy—into the Bill. At present, the measure will allow a super-quango to give away taxpayers' money, at the expense of LEAs, to promote the establishment of grant-maintained schools without there being any evidence of public support for such a school and without the authority having even sought such evidence.

The amendment is fair. I should have thought that the Government would have been well disposed towards it and would agree to attaching conditions to an award of money. The Government tell us that their raison d'être is to give people freedom of choice rather than to impose from the centre. Under those circumstances, they cannot object to an amendment that seeks to allow those in the community whom the new school is to be created to serve to be consulted before a decision is taken.

It is interesting to note that in clause 6(2), the Government have allowed grant-maintained schools wishing to expand or to change their provisions to carry out

"such consultation … as appears to them to be appropriate."

In something as fundamental as establishing a new grant-maintained school, it is logical that the Funding Agency for Schools should at least do what has to be done when a school wishes to expand.

I suspect that Ministers have not read clause 15 in detail. Perhaps it was written by officials and the Minister did not have the opportunity to think about it before it appeared in print, otherwise he would have seen its illogicality. I realise that Treasury officials dominate the thinking of all Departments; the Treasury certainly tells them what funding is available. Indirectly, it will tell the Funding Agency for Schools what funding it can have. I believe that the clause was not seen by Treasury officials and that departmental officials must have written it without recourse to the Minister.

The requirement now is that at least 10 per cent. of pupils must already attend grant-maintained schools in the area before the funding agency can establish a new one. Even that minimal proof of local support will now be jettisoned, along with the evidence that the vast majority of parents want schools to be accountable and open to the communities that they serve. It is patently obvious that the clause has nothing to do with extending parental choice. Indeed, it is the opposite; it seems to allow an ideologically driven quango to ride roughshod over the wishes of parents in the wider community.

Image Col. 333 New grant-maintained schools are likely to be proposed in areas where there are none. A new grant-maintained school might be justified if a large number of children in an area had to travel a considerable distance to a grant-maintained school outside the LEA boundary. Such a situation exists in my constituency. Morley has three comprehensive schools, but just over the boundary is Heckmondwike grammar school, one of the West Riding schools that I used to visit in the old days when I worked for the university.

The number of pupils who go from Kirklees to the comprehensive schools in Morley is very much greater than the number of children who choose to go to Heckmondwike grammar school, the nearest grant-maintained school. Even though we are near the boundary of a city with only one grant-maintained secondary school, not many people want to cross the border and there is no demonstrable public support for a change of status. Indeed, the funding agency might consider a new grant-maintained school in Leeds simply because it had no grant-maintained school. However, it would do so only if there was a public out there waiting for such a school—parents who hoped that some Leeds schools might have changed their status and who were still looking for a similar school for their children—but there is no evidence of that.

By accepting the amendment, the Government would be able to save quite a lot of money. Nothing could be worse than paying money out through the funding agency for someone to promote a school that was unwanted. We all want surplus places to be kept to a minimum.

Last Tuesday, the hon. Member for Bath (Mr. Foster) said that the expansion of grant-maintained schools would cause

"a reduction in the number of places in less popular schools",

which, although not sufficient for the schools to close, would

"considerably increase the financial burden"

on them and

"add to the total cost of the education system".

The Minister replied:

"There may be an element of truth in it in some circumstances".—[Official Report, Standing Committee D, 3 December 1996; c. 209.]

Despite the grudging nature of the Minister's concession, if that is true for expansion, it must be true for building an entire, additional grant-maintained school.

The amendment would mean that the provision of schools in an area would not be centrally imposed, which creates a market that is the worst of all worlds with no scope for the customer—the parent—to exercise choice or to shape the system. Under the clause, choice will be the prerogative of the few—of the funding agency and anyone who pops up to promote a school in the area. A Government quango is rarely a good vehicle for public will.

Col. 334 I hope that the Minister will recognise that there are already ways in which to establish whether there is public support in an area—by examining what has happened, for example. Even when less than 10 per cent. of people are prepared to send their children any distance to a grant-maintained school, one can discern a notable trend in the market. To set up a school without qualification, however, is a big mistake. The Government should find some way of establishing the degree of desire for a school among parents in the area. They should not allow the funding agency to put up good taxpayers' money—albeit recouped from the local authority—to promote a school unless they can determine from other evidence that there is a real need for it.

clause Clause 16
title RECOVERY FROM LOCAL FUNDS OF SUMS IN RESPECT OF START-UP GRANTS

Mr. Kilfoyle: I beg to move amendment No. 156, in page 14, line 38, leave out "determine" and insert

"consider, after consultation with the local education authority, corresponds to the amount which the local education authority itself would have expended during that period in respect of the initial costs of the school if it had been a county school".

The Chairman: With this we may consider amendment No. 66, in page 14, line 42, leave out from "year" to "shall" in line 1 on page 15.

Mr. Kilfoyle: The simple purpose of amendment No. 156 is to limit the amount of money recouped from the local education authority to the level that it would spend on the start-up costs of a county school. It is important at this stage to establish that there will be no repeat of the manifestly unfair situation that obtained with grant-maintained schools or of the ensuing arguments. I am sure that the Government do not want a rerun of those arguments.

The amendment seeks to limit the powers of the Funding Agency for schools to recover from the local education authority the costs of establishing a new grant-maintained school by limiting the amount recouped to the level that the local education authority would spend on any county school. The amendment's underlying principle is to ensure consistent treatment. Nobody would object to consistency in the treatment meted out to local education authority schools and grant-maintained schools.

The funding arrangements for new grant-maintained schools should be consistent with those that would apply in the case of a new school maintained by the local education authority, which would, in any other circumstances, be determined in accordance with the given authority's local management of schools formula. Local education authority's local management of schools schemes set out the detailed funding arrangements applicable to local education authority-maintained schools. The Col. 342 amendment aims simply to ensure that any recoupment from the relevant local education authority for the establishment of a new school is done in agreement with the local funding formula governing local education authority schools. Opposition Members would emphasise that that would be entirely in accordance with the Government's publicly stated objectives to underpin the funding arrangements for grant-maintained schools in relation to both the common funding formula and the annual maintenance grants. Those arrangements should refer to grant-maintained schools and local education authority schools.

The annual maintenance grant arrangements are based on the principle of replication of the area's local management of schools scheme. If it is accepted that the overall revenue funding of the new grant-maintained schools should honour that principle, it should equally follow that any recoupment from the relevant local education authority for the establishment of a new grant-maintained school should also adhere to that principle.

The Bill would give the Secretary of State unfettered discretion in determining the amounts that might be recovered from the local education authority for the start-up costs of a grant-maintained school. If the Government were to accept the amendment, it would provide for equitable treatment of grant-maintained and local education authority schools in the exercise of the Secretary of State's powers to recoup the funds from the local education authority.

In the current public expenditure climate, where the need to ensure value for money as well as effectiveness is accepted on both sides of the House to be an overriding priority, it is essential that the Government act, and are seen to act, fairly and consistently in all aspects of school funding policy. Parents, governors, pupils and the electorate all need to be assured that Government policy on school funding operates to ensure consistent and equitable treatment between schools, whether primary or secondary, grant-maintained, or local education authority-maintained. It would be unacceptable if local education authority schools were penalised by any measures that sought to claw back more funding than was strictly necessary or fair, to the benefit of a new grant-maintained school and at the direct expense of the majority of pupils attending local education authority schools in an area.

I stress that the amendment should not be seen as an attempt to place unnecessary or unreasonable restrictions on the power of the Secretary of State. It provides simply for appropriate reference to be made to the relevant local education authority's published formula for funding arrangements for its schools, and for any necessary consultation with the local education authority.

The LEA local management of schools scheme is agreed with the Secretary of State following consultation with schools in the given local education authority area. It is therefore transparent and commands the support of all maintained schools in that local education authority area.
