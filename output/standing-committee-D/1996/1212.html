Ms Morris
: The amendment makes sense, so I shall not spend much time on it. We all want governing bodies to take part in the writing of a code of discipline, but the clause puts an undue emphasis on the governing body writing the text. We wish the governing body to agree the text with the head teacher so that there is a common agenda. The law should not place an undue obligation on governing bodies to write the text.

The amendment would bring the clause in line with other obligations on governing bodies—for instance, their work on the curriculum and sex education. In both those instances, the law requires them to make a statement, but it does not oblige them to write the text. I am not sure why the wording is different on the matter of discipline from what it is in the two instances that I have mentioned. By no stretch of the imagination do I wish to remove the obligation on the governing body to be involved with making policy on discipline; I wish to make it clear that the governing body's obligation is to agree the policy with the head teacher and others.

I hope that the amendments will find favour with the Minister.

The Minister of State, Department for Education and Employment (Mr. Eric Forth)
: It is important that the head teacher and staff should be seen to be implementing and operating a behaviour and discipline policy with the full support of the governors. That is why we are placing a new duty on the governing JPEG 417 body to draw up, and periodically to review, a written statement of general principles upon which the head teacher will determine the school's discipline policy. By so doing, we shall increase the role of the governing body in promoting good behaviour and discipline with the school.

Many governing bodies already do that, but that good practice needs to be disseminated more widely. Clauses 20 and 21 will ensure that every governing body determines the broad principles for the school's behaviour and discipline policy.

The amendments are unnecessary. It is surely implicit in the wording of new subsections 154(2) and 306A(2) that in preparing a written statement of general principles the governing body as a whole has, by a majority decision, agreed that that should be the statement to be passed on to the head teacher. The word "agree" might lead to ambiguity, as it is not clear with whom the document should be agreed—for example, would it be among the governors or with another party, such as the head teacher? We do not accept that it would be a valid objection in law to say that "prepare" means only "to make ready". I therefore urge the Committee to reject amendments Nos. 134 and 133.

As for amendment No. 182, it will be important when the governing body is preparing its written statement of general principle that it should have access, as needed, to guidance on pupil behaviour and discipline. That will ensure that the governing body is as well informed as possible about those important matters.

Mr. Forth

I am advised, however, that there is scope to improve the wording on amendment No. 182. I therefore accept in principle its underlying assumption, and shall bring forward a revised proposal on Report to secure that aim. In doing so, we shall go one step further: we will also amend clause 21 to ensure that a similar requirement is made of the governing bodies of grant-maintained schools and grant-maintained special schools to consult the head teacher. Do the Government intend to place the onus on parents to make travel arrangements? If so, would not that undermine the efficacy of the new initiative? Non-co-operative parents will try to charge the school with the duty of care to ensure their child's safe return home if they have given proper notice that they cannot make satisfactory arrangments to collect their child.

Mr. Kilfoyle
: As my hon. Friend was speaking, a visit that I made to a school on the outskirts of Gloucester flashed through my mind. It is a denominational school with pupils from outlying villages and the only transport is that arranged by the school—small buses take the children home. Being conscious of rural poverty, I ask whether parents will be forced to arrange a taxi because there is no alternative transport to take the child home. Who will pay for the taxi?

Mr. Griffiths
: My hon. Friend draws our attention to a concrete example of the difficulties that could arise as the clause stands. Our amendment is designed to give some defence for the school, which will be able to argue that detention is reasonable and that the implicit safety of the walking distance has been considered.
