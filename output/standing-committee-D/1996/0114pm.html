As you have just assumed the Chair, Mr. O'Hara, I think that it is in order to wish you a good new year. I am sure that in your case it will be a very successful 1997. We shall look forward to the particular success that we might expect for you.

The new authority is the result of Sir Ron Dearing's work. In his review of what was available for 16 to 19year-olds, he recommended the merger of the National Council for Vocational Qualifications and the School Curriculum and Assessment Authority. They will become the new authority, the Qualifications and National Curriculum Authority. When Sir Ron Dearing proposed the merger, he recommended explicitly that the consultation process should involve consideration of how employment interests might best be represented in future arrangements to ensure that Col. 580 national vocational qualifications continue to be based on occupational standards and remain employment led.

The Confederation of British Industry submitted to the consultation process several proposals that outlined its members' views on how their interests could be represented on the merged authority proposed by Sir Ron Dearing. The CBI proposed safeguards that it considered were necessary to ensure that what Sir Ron suggested did happen. It suggested, in particular, that members of the new authority should have appropriate experience.

The amendment would slightly alter the make up of the authority. Will the Minister say why, when proposing the establishment of the Qualifications and National Curriculum Authority, the clause recommends that it consists

"of not less than 8 members nor more than 13 members"?

It will be smaller than the bodies that it replaces. Why are those figures different from those for the similar body that is proposed for Wales? Clause 43 states that the authority in Wales shall consist

"of not less than 10 nor more than 15 members".

The members of the new authority will be appointed by the Secretary of State, just as members of the present authorities are. Will the Minister confirm that, when appointments are made to the authority, they will be subject to the Nolan proposals regarding public appointments? I should have thought that Sir Leonard Peach, the Commissioner for Public Appointments, would be consulted about them.

The amendment sets out how the membership might be altered slightly to ensure that not less than a third of the body has experience of

"industrial, commercial or financial matters"—

the category included in clause 37(4)(c).

Under the amendment, those who have experience of

"the practice of any profession"

would be separated from those in the category under clause 37(4)(c). According to the present wording, it would be possible that no member of the body had experience relevant to those employing people in industry. Understandably, one would expect those with industrial experience to be concerned about such a gap; they are guaranteed no representation under the present wording of the clause. I am sure that the Minister will say that it is not the Government's intention to exclude those with industrial experience from the authority's membership.

The clause contains other curiosities, with which the amendment does not deal. For example, an authority could be set up of which no member had experience of the maintained education system, as it is far from clear who can be included in the category under clause 37(4)(a). The amendment would alter subsection (4)(c), producing two categories: one under paragraph (c), the other under paragraph (d).

The Minister must explain why the body is so small. Schedule 6, which deals with the functioning of the authority, mentions four other people who will be present at all its meetings, in addition to its members. It can be seen from how the authority will be set up that Image Col. 581 those whom it employs will have a large say in what happens. In the circumstances, it is understandable that employers want adequate representation on the body that agrees the curriculum and qualifications, especially those relevant to employment.

The Minister should give us some idea of the Government's thinking about who will be appointed to the body. I should like some assurance that those people will be appointed under what might be called Nolan principles, especially as the body will be small—it might have only eight members. Although four categories of people will be represented on the body, some people might fulfil the requirements of either paragraphs (a) and (b) or (c), or perhaps even (a), (b) and (c). Nevertheless, the body will be small and it might contain politically motivated people, so there must be a balance, such as that found in the Nolan proposals. One or two political appointments would be influential in such a small body.

I should also like to be given some understanding of the mode of appointment to the authority and of how the Bill would guarantee Sir Ron Dearing's initial reflections on the body's ability to respond to employers. I think that the amendment is necessary.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Education and Employment (Mrs. Cheryl Gillan): The hon. Member for Morley and Leeds, South (Mr. Gunnell) was brief in moving the amendment and I shall also attempt to be brief. I hope that I shall satisfy him on some of the points he raised.
