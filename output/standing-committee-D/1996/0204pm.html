Sex Offenders Bill

clause Clause 2
title EFFECT OF NOTIFICATION REQUIREMENTS

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 9, in page 4, line 4, at end insert—

"(6) The provisions of section (Code of practice on the use of information held under this Act by the police) shall apply to information notified to the police under this section.".

4.30 pm

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking the following:

procedure

New Clause 1—Access to information

—(1) The Secretary of State may, by order made by statutory instrument, specify those persons or bodies to whom a chief officer of police may supply details notified to him under section 2 of this Act.

  1. An order made under subsection (1) above shall be laid before Parliament in draft and shall be subject to approval by a resolution of each House before being made.
  2. An order made under subsection (1) above may permit a chief officer of police to provide information to—
    1. a local education authority, grant-maintained school or private school;
    2. a health authority or NHS trust;
    3. a social services authority;
    or any other person or body whom the Secretary of State thinks fit.
  3. Any person who, in the course of his official duties, has or at any time has had any information supplied to him under an order made under subsection (1) above, shall be guilty of an offence if he discloses that information to any other person other than in the course of his official duties.
  4. Any person who obtains any information notified to the police under section 2 of this Act by means of any fraud, dishonesty or bribe shall be guilty of an offence.
  5. A person guilty of an offence under—
    1. subsection (4) above, shall be liable on summary conviction to a fine not exceeding level 4 on the standard scale; or
    2. subsection (5) above, shall be liable on summary conviction to a fine not exceeding level 5 on the standard scale or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding six months, or both."

New clause 4—Code of practice on the use of information held under this Act by the police—

".—(1) The Secretary of State shall, following consultation, issue a Code of Practice setting out—

  1. the means whereby information notified to the police under this Act is to be registered and maintained;
  2. those persons who may have access to information so notified; and
  3. the responsibilities of the police and others to co-operate in using information so notified for the protection of the public, including, but not confined to, responsibilities for providing information and advice to members of the public and to statutory and voluntary organisations.
  4. (2) A Code of Practice made under subsection (1) above shall be laid before Parliament in draft and shall be subject to approval by a resolution of each House before being made.".

Mr. Michael Clapham (Barnsley, West and Penistone): When we broke for lunch, I was speaking to amendment No. 9 and new clause 4. I was explaining to the Minister, by way of an example from my constituency, why I consider that new clause 4, in particular, would be so important to the Bill. I was citing a case in which a widow with two young children married a man who was a paedophile. In the subsequent court case, after certain matters had come to light, it was pointed out that the marriage may have taken place only because the man wanted to get to the children.

If new clause 4 was accepted, the information on the register that will be maintained by the police would be available to other agencies, in particular to the social services, which could therefore have informed the unfortunate woman that the man with whom she was having a liaison was a paedophile and that he would be a danger to her children.

It is important that the Minister accepts the need for a code of practice as set in new clause 4. It is no use having a register that is to be maintained by the police, which, perhaps, may be made available to social services. Certain operational priorities must be in place, so that the information can be used to protect children. Therefore, the code would become the core of the Bill. I hope that the Minister is willing to accept new clause 4 or a similar clause that will bring into being such a code of practice.

Mr. Peter Thurnham (Bolton, North-East): I should like to speak to new clause 1 as well as to amendment No. 9 and new clause 4. I support the proposals for a code of practice. It would be a sensible way in which to proceed. However, my new clause 1 would make it an offence to disclose information to anyone to whom it should not be disclosed.

When discussing how the information should be used, we must consider what is happening in America under what is known as Megan's law. I understand that that law's provisions are being applied in several states throughout America. There is a requirement for all other states to consider it, although it may still be challenged in the Supreme Court on the technical issue of whether limitless retrospective and double jeopardy punishment breach constitutional rights. Megan's law is the result of the terrible tragic death of seven-year-old Megan Kanka. Her mother decided to campaign strongly because the paedophile who allegedly murdered her daughter had been living in a house on the other side of the road, a matter of which she was not aware, although other neighbours were aware of it.

Megan's case raises the question whether such people should be released in the first place—we are dealing here with the most serious offenders. A learned colleague was in contact with the father of Sophie Hook, who was sadly murdered, and he has written to me about how a measure like Megan's law could work in this country. He states:

"Refer back to what happened to Sophie Hook and to Mr. Hook's campaign to have a register introduced and to the courteous and interested hearing he was given"

by the Home Secretary.

"Since then nothing has happened, and the Home Office plainly wants to forget the whole idea as unworkable. But it is workable. Suggest a system based on a register containing 5 levels of registered Image Col. 45 information, ranging from Grade 1 serious sexual convictions to Grade 5 relevant allegations which have resulted neither in conviction nor formal discipline (e.g. conduct by someone dealing with children in a voluntary organisation against whom complaints are made). Provide a machinery through the county courts, which on the whole are quite quick, to enable proposed registrations to be challenged. Standard of proof to be balance of probabilities. Grade A registrations would be published in the area where a registered person lived, there being a requirement for registered persons to notify the police of any change of address, harassment of registered persons to be a specific and quite serious new criminal offence punishable by imprisonment."

That is the road down which the Americans seem to be going and some argue strongly that we should consider it here. I would look at it in another light, however. If people pose such a serious risk to the public that the neighbours have to be informed that they are living in the neighbourhood, we should ask whether they should be released in the first place. The Government have been making capital from their "two strokes and you're out" hard line, but one must ask whether one strike is enough for some of the people we are discussing.

This morning, I drew the Minister's attention to the report prepared by the Home Office interdepartmental working group entitled "Sex Offenders: A Guide to Inter-Agency Co-operation". It contains numerous references to the need for pre-sentence assessment, so that when a judge is determining what would be an appropriate sentence he could ask for an assessment of the offender's characteristics and what the chances were that the offender could safely be rehabilitated into the community. If the offender belongs to the category known as high deviancy, I suggest that a judge would be better to pass an indeterminate sentence, lasting until such time as a risk assessment shows that it would be safe to let that person out. The alleged murderer of young Megan was gaoled because he had left his previous victim for dead by a river but refused psychiatric treatment while serving his sentence in prison. We must ask why he was let out in the first place.

I believe that it would be a mistake for us to argue for the adoption of Megan's law in this country; instead, we should follow the recommendations in the Home Office report. I do not know whether the Minister would object, but if the Home Office cannot provide members of the Committee with a copy of the report, it should be placed in the Library, as it is so relevant to the points that we are discussing. On who should have access to the information, the report has a number of recommendations. Under paragraph 4.8, it states:

"Information should be exchanged for clearly defined purposes, as and when required. Particular care must be taken to ensure that information is restricted to those whose work properly requires access to it."

If information should be restricted, I believe that it should be an offence to pass on information further than it should be. I press the Minister to ensure that the Committee is given the information that is available to him in the Home Office, as that information is so relevant to the point that we are discussing.

Reference to the register does not appear in the Bill, but it is assumed in the workings of the Bill that there Col. 46 must be a register. The use to which that register will be put must be carefully controlled, so that it is primarily useful in the prevention of crime and not open to abuse leading to vigilante victimisation of offenders and their families.

On 24 January, Matthew Parris wrote in The Times:

"If we judge a person so dangerous that his neighbours must be warned about him, he should not be allowed back into the community."

That is the answer that I would give on whether we should have a Megan's law. The important issue is that there should be treatment and an assessment before offenders are released back into the community.

The same points were made on Second Reading by hon. Members, including the hon. Member for Stockport (Ms Coffey), who said that it would make sense if information

"were also available to other Departments that keep lists with the aim of protecting children."

The right hon. Member for Conwy (Sir W. Roberts) said that he was disappointed that that was not spelt out in the Bill and that information should

"be confined to those with a need to know—for example, head teachers of schools."

He continued:

"Notification must not become an additional punishment; it must be a preventive measure."

The hon. Member for Swansea, East (Mr. Anderson) said that

"responsible organisations should in certain circumstances be able to consult the register"—[Official Report, 27 January 1997; Vol. 289, c. 69, 48–9, 34.]

And the hon. Member for Cynon Valley (Mrs. Clwyd) said that direction must be given to the police on the usage of information.
