Education Bill

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Department for Education and Employment (Mr. Eric Forth)
: I beg to move,

That, during the proceedings on the Education Bill, the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock and half-past Four o'clock.

In doing so, may I on behalf of the Committee welcome you, Sir Michael, to the Chair. Committee proceedings are usually happy occasions but for whoever is in the Chair, they are difficult and testing times. I am sure that members of the Committee will not want to make our forthcoming proceedings difficult for you and the fact that your enormous experience, wisdom, fairness and firmness will be guiding us throughout means that, in all probability, we shall have a productive Committee stage. I also welcome Mr. O'Hara as your substitute Chairman, Sir Michael, who I am sure will guide us with equal firmness and sagacity.

I note that several veterans of the Committee that discussed the Education Act 1993 are members of this Committee. I played a small part in those discussions, as did my hon. Friends the Members for Wyre Forest (Mr. Coombs), for Erith and Crayford (Mr. Evennett) and for Rugby and Kenilworth (Mr Pawsey) who, I suspect, has been a member of every Education Bill Committee since 1979—and who does not look any the worse for that! Furthermore, the hon. Member for Bath (Mr. Foster) has probably been a member of Education Bill Committees since 1992. The hon. Members for Plymouth, Devonport (Mr. Jamieson) and for Birmingham, Yardley (Ms Morris) have also been members of such Committees. Some of the usual suspects are here and I should not be at all surprised if we make our usual speeches too.

The sittings motion provides for the Committee to follow the usual sitting pattern if that becomes necessary. However, it allows us flexibility to determine when we shall sit. I believe that it will be possible for us to do justice to the Bill by arranging flexible sittings that are agreed by all members of the Committee.

(Liverpool, Walton)

Member for Rugby and Kenilworth who is described in less polite circles as "flogger". I do not know why, but I understand that we shall discuss an amendment that he has tabled later in our proceedings.

I now turn to the importance of time in relation to the Bill. It contains many varied and important issues. We shall be considering a Bill of two halves, the first of which deals principally with selection and the extension of grant-maintained schools. The second half deals with issues that have been flagged up repeatedly by the Opposition, and we are pleased that the Government have decided to adopt many of those proposals. I am sure that we shall tease them out, and say whether a future Labour Government would find them acceptable.

As far as we can conceivably manage it, we intend to be constructive and pragmatic. Nevertheless, time is of the essence. I understand the Government's sense of urgency, but we must try to ensure full discussion of the various parts of the Bill—the contentious and the less contentious parts—and to balance the time given to them, in order to give the Bill our full consideration.

For instance, we have long stood against the principle of selection. We shall go into this in some detail when debating the various amendments, but we believe that selection is wrong in principle and that the outcome of selection is not in the broader educational interests of our children or in the national economic interest. Some of the national testing results to be announced this week and the forthcoming GCSE results and league tables will show that slection is not the answer to the country's needs—neither in terms of economic prosperity nor in terms of social cohesion.

I look forward to the publication on Thursday of the report of the Office for Standards in Education into exclusions from schools, and to all that it will imply. The extension of grant-maintained status, too, will require much debate. We would naturally argue that although about 1,100 schools have already opted for that status, about 23,000 maintained schools—the overwhelming majority—have not opted for grant-maintained status and that there is, therefore, no public demand for its extension.

We shall also need time to argue our case that some of the provisions governing grant-maintained schools will have a deleterious effect on the plans of local education authorities to ensure the appropriate provision of schools in their areas. If there is sufficient time, we shall argue that we need to explore the proposals in depth, particularly the definition in clause 7 of those grant-maintained schools requiring special measures. The Government will need fully to explain what they intend.

As we go further through the Bill, we shall come to provisions dealing with the impact of equal opportunities legislation. I am mindful of the celebrated case involving my son's school, the Liverpool Bluecoat school. The school faced great difficulties—rather, the Secretary of State faced great difficulties—when considering whether it could JPEG 5 become selective because there were insufficient places for girls in the local education authority area. That is an important point because, given time, the paradox of the increasing disparity in performance between girls and boys will become evident. We will argue that there are sound ways in which that can be tackled, following on from policies that we will advocate during the course of the Bill.
