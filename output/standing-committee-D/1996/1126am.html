Ms Morris
: May I welcome you, Mr. O'Hara, to your first sitting of the Committee. I know that my hon. Friend the Member for Liverpool, Walton (Mr. Kilfoyle) is pleased to have another scouser in the Committee, and we are pleased to have a Chairman who has a long record of interest in education and who, as we already know, will deal with our proceedings in a firm but fair manner.

Members of the Committee will be pleased that I do not intend to take a long time in speaking to the amendments because many of the arguments that were made when we discussed the previous amendment 84 apply to these amendments—although more strongly. Amendments Nos. 30 and 31 would remove from the Bill the right of a primary school to select. The Minister says that he regards the Bill as a permissive rather than prescriptive measure, but it would be the first, if it were made into law, to enshrine a primary school's right to select its pupils by ability or aptitude.

That would be a wrong move. All our previous arguments about the dangers of taking away parental choice, of labelling children too easily and of destroying the community aspects of many of our schools are even more forceful when we compare primary schools with secondary schools. Some Members of the Committee would argue that there has been a call from some quarters for a return to selection at the secondary stage, but I disagree with that. There has certainly been no call for the introduction of selection at the primary stage. There is no strong lobby of parents or others that selection should be introduced at the primary stage. The argument that there is cannot be advanced in favour of the clause.

Real problems exist about whether we can determine the ability or aptitude of a child who is as young as five or seven. At our previous sitting, the hon. Member for Rugby and Kenilworth (Mr. Pawsey) described how the system failed to predict his ability and how it deemed him an academic failure at the age of 11. He then went on to great things, which shows how difficult it is to test aptitude and ability at the age of 11. It would be even more difficult to test a child's aptitude and ability at the ages of five or seven. To label children and to put them into one form of schooling at those ages could damage them in their later years of schooling.

It is important that we regard schools as part of the community. They are sometimes the only institution in a district that can support and offer facilitites to the community. At the primary level, there is a strong notion of community. Most parents choose to send their children to the local school. The Bill will potentially deny them that right. If demand exceeds supply, criteria will have to be introduced on which children should have the right to go to a particular school.

The right of children to go to their local school is fundamental. We are talking about stability—the stability that comes from children going to school with their neighbours' children, from not having far to travel and from being able to identify with the school buildings. Taking that away from children at the age of five, six or seven would destroy that stability. It will do nothing to buttress the feeling of community. We talk a lot these days about the need to guard and strengthen communities. Taking away a child's right to attend the local school will do nothing to support the community.

Even if the provision remains unamended, I believe that few schools will choose to use it. There is no demand for it. Parents will jealously guard their right to send their youngest children to the local school, with their brothers and sisters and the neighbours' children. We should not underestimate the strength of feeling JPEG 85 for local primary schools. I believe that parents will do all that they can to make sure that that option remains for them.

However, as long as that provision remains in the Bill, schools might choose to deal with the problem of demand exceeding supply by selecting on ability or aptitude. We have a duty to protect the right of parents to send their children to the local primary school—the school that is nearest; the one that the child and neighbours identify with; the one that is easiest to travel to; the one that is part of the community in which the child will live during those important early years.

The amendments will ensure that parents retain the right to send their children to the local school. I therefore hope that they will find support in the Committee.
