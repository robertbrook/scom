Mr. Foster
: Although you were not in the Chair, Sir Michael, I wish to apologise to the Committee for my late appearance at our previous sitting and my rather abrupt departure from it. In return for disrupting the Committee in that way, I intend to be brief in speaking to the amendments, something on which I am sure the Government Whip will be keeping a check.

The Minister of State has said many times that he is delighted that the Bill is permissive. Given that he is so keen on permissive legislation, I am sure that he will support the amendments. I propose that we change the provisions in the Bill from being over-prescriptive to merely permissive. The amendments would remove from the Bill the requirement that governing bodies must consider annually whether they wish to change the status of the school from non-selective to selective. At present, governing bodies are extremely hard-pressed in their work and I want to reduce the pressure that is placed on them to undertake unnecessary tasks.

The current legislation in respect of grant-maintained status requires all governing bodies to consider annually whether their schools become grant maintained. Although that is a requirement, it might surprise Conservative Members—it certainly does not surprise Opposition Members—to know that despite such a requirement, in the past 12 months, only one half of 1 per cent. of all eligible schools decided to hold a ballot on grant-maintained status. Governing bodies have no desire to go down that route and the same is true in respect of schools that are currently non-selective, but which will be required to consider whether they wish to become selective. A large amount 168 of time will be wasted by governing bodies deciding what to do and then concluding, as I suspect that they will, that they have no desire to take their schools down a selective route.

The amendments would remove that requirement from the majority of our schools, which are currently non-selective. They would merely require schools that are currently selective to consider annually whether they wish to continue being selective and to explain why. The amendments are permissive; they would make it possible for the governing bodies of non-selective schools to consider annually—they could consider this each month if they wished—whether to become selective. The proposals would simply remove the requirement that they must consider changing their status when I am convinced, as I am sure are most people in the country, that they will have no desire to do so. I am sure that the Under-Secretary of State will welcome such a proposal.

We take the view that if one school decides to become selective, not only that school but others will change in nature and character.
