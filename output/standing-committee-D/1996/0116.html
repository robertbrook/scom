New Clause 22
SEN CODE OF PRACTICE

"A careers adviser shall, in making available careers education to a registered pupil who has special educational needs, have regard to any Code of Practice issued under section 313 of the Education Act 1996".—[ Mr. Win Griffiths]

Brought up, and read the First time.

10.30 am

Mr. Win Griffiths
(Bridgend)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

I move the new clause in the ardent hope that the Government will accept that it adopts a reasonable and responsible approach to how careers advisers should view the code of practice for the identification and assessment of special educational needs. Under the Education Act 1996, everyone involved in education must pay attention to the code of practice and everyone is told, in the time-honoured phrase, "to have regard to it". However, such a provision does not cover careers advisers, something which this Bill laudably seeks to deal with—at least, to some extent.

The draft leaflet on access that was published by the Department for Education and Employment mentions in its last paragraph the code of practice. The document is a draft leaflet, which is why I am sure that the Government will be prepared to differ slightly from the view that they have taken so far. It states:

"Schools and Careers Services should refer to the Code of Practice on Identification and Assessment of Special Educational Needs issued by the DfE in 1993."

I do not believe that I am being over-pedantic, but to be asked to refer to the code of practice is not the same as to be asked to have regard to it. Nor is being asked to have regard to the code of practice the same as being referred to a guidance leaflet that suggests that careers advisers should refer to the code.

We must also consider the amount of specialist training that will be required either for all careers advisers or for a group of advisers to whom anyone can have access in a particular area. Special expertise is needed to enable careers guidance advisers to give appropriate advice to pupils with special educational needs on their prospects. I hope that the Minister will say whether the Government anticipated additional financial implications in ensuring that those involved in careers guidance are properly equipped to provide such a service.

Most of all, I hope that the Government will accept the new clause or, at least, be prepared to consider it and return at a later stage with a similar proposal to 618 ensure that the careers service has regard to the code of practice and is not told in a guidance leaflet simply to refer to it when giving advice.

The Minister of State, Department for Education and Employment (Mr. Eric Forth)
: The Department's code of practice on the identification and assessment of special educational needs is an important source of advice that every school is already required, under law, to take into account when they take action on behalf of children with special educational needs. The current code includes sections on preparing pupils for transition at the age of 16 and provides advice on how careers services should assist with that preparation. Obviously, it is our intention that that advice be followed.

The Bill provides that careers education should become part of the overall educational responsibility of head teachers and governors, so the statutory duty to ensure the code takes account of that is already in place. Although careers advisers may contribute to careers education, it is for each school to determine a careers education policy that suits its particular circumstances and to decide how it can best be implemented. I believe that the schools themselves are best placed to ensure that the code is taken into account in the planning and provision of all careers education within their institutions.

It is for the schools to ensure also that any input from the careers adviser takes account of the code. We therefore share the view that careers advisers should take account of the code. The Department will provide a reminder of that in its good practice guide and in advice on implementing the legislation, so the legal duty to ensure that that happens is already sufficiently in place.

Quite apart from that, LEAs are required by law to invite a member of the careers service to the first annual review of a child's statement following his or her 14th birthday. A separate section in the code of practice, entitled "The Role of the Careers Service", clearly describes the sort of information and advice that members of the careers service are expected to provide in such circumstances. The amendment seeks to clarify the law. Again, I shall be happy if the Minister wishes to speak about existing provisions. The purpose of the amendment is to make it clear that the principal objective of pupil referral units is to ensure

"the satisfactory reintegration into school after a limited period of pupils referred to it."

Some provision for that already exists in DFEE guidance note 11/94.

In practice though, as reflected by the Ofsted report, pupil referral units can almost become detached from the rest of the educational system. People find it all too easy to refer difficult pupils to them and then forget them. The teachers who staff those units have the difficult job of dealing with those pupils and trying to offer them a hope of returning to mainstream education, overcoming the disregard in which they are held.

LEA schools and Government advice work towards the early return of pupils to mainstream schools. Good practice and the advice already provided try to ensure that that happens. I do not think that the Government can ever argue that that fundamental objective should be contained only in a circular because it may change over time, as can be argued for many other matters. 620 The fundamental objective of trying to reintegrate disruptive and difficult pupils into schools does not change.

DFEE circular 11/94, "The education by LEAs of children otherwise than at school", states at paragraph 4:

"The prime aim in respect of all children who are away from school should be to secure their return to mainstream education, including special schools, as soon as practicable."

At paragraph 21, it adds:

"Where children are out of school the aim wherever possible should be to plan for their return to mainstream schooling, in the meantime keeping their education in touch with that of their peers in schools."

We believe that that good advice would be better as a statutory objective in the Bill.

The circular gives further specific advice on the date at which a pupil's progress should be reviewed. We should not expect that on the face of the Bill because there may be arguments about the frequency of reviews—every term or half term, six-monthly, annually or whatever. Perhaps at least each half term might be appropriate, but that kind of advice can be contained in circulars.

We want a specific duty for pupil referral units, or other means of dealing with disruptive pupils—such as Cities in Schools in south Wales, which I have seen to be excellent—rather than a reliance on advice in guidance circulars. I cannot envisage any future change in the worthwhile objective of a return to mainstream education. It should, therefore, be a statutory obligation on the face of the Bill.
