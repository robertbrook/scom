Mr. Kilfoyle
: Happy new year to you, Sir Michael. I am sure that I say that on behalf of all members of the Committee. With your leave, may I extend the congratulations of the Opposition to the Minister of State on his elevation to the Privy Council? I know that he will not take offence when I say that that is the least he deserves after his years of labouring in education.

538 The clause arises from an idea that has been put into practice by some schools on an entirely voluntary basis. Many people, including the Labour party, have argued that such a provision should be more widely extended. The Government have proceeded from excellent intentions, but the drafting of the clause is ill thought out and could have unlooked for and damaging consequences.

The amendment is designed to improve the Bill by retaining those good and laudable elements to which I have already referred, while eliminating the problematic aspects of its current drafting. We all agree that parents should be given every encouragement to support the education of their children and the schools that they attend.

We also agree that schools should be clear about what they expect of pupils and parents and the ways in which they will help parents supports their children in the learning process. It is eminently sensible that such matters should be clearly written down in a document so that all parties know where they stand. However, there are worrying elements about a law that allows schools to choose whether they will offer such a document and which allows them to impose their version of what it should contain, with the sanction of refusing a place at the school if the parents do not comply. The Government are keen advocates of patental choice, but providing a charter for some schools to browbeat parents in such a way is hardly an enchancement of parental choice.

In the public debate that has surrounded the issue, there has been much talk of home-school contracts or partnership agreements. The Bill avoids reference to the terms "contract" or "agreement", and advisely so. I see that the Minister is nodding his head in agreement. Such words are associated with voluntary arrangements between parties of more or less equal standing, and they cannot properly be used to describe a situation in which one party can dictate terms to another. Yet we are faced with a Bill that is weighted in favour of shools, under which a school can choose whether to offer a partnership document and determine its content whereas parents must accept the school's decision.

However, the clause is not as simple as that. It recognises that there is a potential conflict between the ability of a school to refuse admission to a child whose parents will not sign an agreement and the right of access to free education. It makes it clear that admissions authorities can waive the requirement to sign. It gives the Secretary of State the power to strike down unacceptable conditions and to prevent certain kinds of requirements from being imposed on parents. The proposed new section 413B(6) states that the documents

"shall not be capable of creating any obligation in respect of whose breach any liability arises in contract or in tort."

 In short, it is conceded that the documents are legally unenforceable.

The document purports to be enforceable but, on closer inspection, its sanctions are revealed to be ineffective. What will be the practical result of that? The majority of parents actively support their JPEG 539 children's education and want to work with their children's schools. For them, the enactment of this part of the Bill will make no difference. Such parents would happily sign a statutory document, just as they would subscribe to an informal non-statutory one.

But what of the minority—the good but argumentative parents who are keen supporters of their children's education but who may object to particular sections of the partnership document; or parents who have a principled objection to being required to sign such a document? Such objections could give rise to disputes. The school could refuse entry to the children, or it might back down, waiving the requirement for the document to be signed. The parents' choice of school would thus have been defeated—for no particularly good reason—or the authority of the school would have been undermined. Neither outcome is desirable. In both cases, time and energy will have been expended, resulting in damage to the relationship between the school and the community that it seeks to serve.

However, less conscientious parents, who had less regard for their child's education, might readily sign the necessary papers to enable them to secure the child's place in the school but subsequently pay no heed to their responsibilities. Once the child was on the school roll, the sanction of refusing admission would fall and the school would be no more able to procure the appropriate behaviour of the parent than it was before. In most cases the Bill will have secured no practical advantage, but in a small number of cases it may be unhelpful or have damaging consequences. That is a poor basis for law making.

The beneficial effects of the amendments are several. The Bill would be shorter and more concise, something that is always desirable in legislation. The amendments would remove all connections between the partnership document and the admissions process. That would do away with some real administrative complexities and avoid potential conflicts between the Bill and other legislation, which would prevent those provisions being misused. The Opposition and the Government may have differeing views about the value of selection, but I should be surprised if many advocates of selective admission arrangements were entirely comfortable with the selective refusal of places in ostensibly nonselective schools, which is what the clause presently sanctions.

Most important, however, the amendments would require every school to draw up a statement of what it understood to be the mutual rights and responsibilities of the school and the parents. That would ensure that every parent knew where the school stood on important issues. This would provide an opportunity for mature dialogue between parents and schools, would allow all parents to know what the school could offer them and what it would expect of them, and would provide a vehicle to assist schools in encouraging parents to live up to their duties and responsibilities.
