Draft Local Authorities (Contracting Out of Allocation of Housing and Homelessness Functions) Order 1996

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Mr. James Clappison)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Local Authorities (Contracting Out of Allocation of Housing and Homelessness Functions) Order 1996.

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to discuss the draft Allocation of Housing Regulations 1996 (S.I. 1996, No. 2753), and the Homelessness Regulations 1996 (S.I. 1996, No. 2754).

Mr. Clappison
: It gives me great pleasure, Mr. Maxton, to serve on the Committee under your chairmanship. It may assist the Committee if I set out briefly the background to where the regulations fit into the scheme of the Housing Act 1996. They take forward its policies, the aim of which is to ensure that subsidised social housing is allocated on a fair basis to those who need it.

Three main objectives underly the policy. First, it will ensure that all allocations of long-term tenancies in social housing are made through a single mechanism—the new housing register. This will mean that everyone's needs are viewed together, whether they are statutorily homeless or on the waiting list. The second objective is to steer local authorities towards a broader view of housing need and homelessness. Under part VII of the 1966 Act, housing authorities will be required to ensure that advice and assistance are provided to all residents in their district who request it. This will help focus on the reasons why people lost their homes rather than dealing only with the effects of homes being lost.

The third objective is to preserve, through the Housing Act 1996, the essential safety net of homelessness legislation which has been in place since 1977. The Housing (Homeless Persons) Act 1977 introduced the duty to accommodate any family or vulnerable person who was unintentionally homeless. In the 1996 Act the definitions of homelessness and threatened homelessness remain the same, as does the definition of priority need. On the whole, we are building part VII of the 1996 Act on earlier legislation. In these respects, the system remains the same.

The interim duty to accommodate pending inquiries, essential for families who are thrown out of their homes, also continues to apply. The order differs from the 1977 legislation in that there is a new duty on local authorities to provide suitable accommodation for homeless families and vulnerable individuals for a 4 minimum period of two years. During this time, the longer-term needs of a homeless person or family will be assessed alongside those of others on the housing register. The difference between this and the previous system is that all needy households, the statutorily homeless, and others in need will be allocated long-term housing by the same criteria. The Committee may be familiar with the powers of local authorities when the two-year period ends and the duties that may fall upon them.

Much work has already been done towards implementing parts VI and VII of the 1996 Act. On 31 October my Department issued a code of guidance on those parts; on 30 October regulations on allocation of housing and on homelessness were made. These are the regulations that are being prayed against today. They set out detailed arrangements that give full effect to the provisions of the Act. It is essential that they be passed into law if local authorities are properly to implement the Act.

Mr. Raynsford

Mr. Clappison

The reviews were published last week and the changes in the implementation date are contained in legislation. The hon. I shall be happy to deal with any other points that he may wish to make, but I want to say something about the regulations as they relate to allocations—the main feature of part VI.

The Allocation of Housing Regulations do several things. They set out cases in which allocations provisions do not apply; they set out which people who are subject to immigration control are entitled to appear on the housing register; they set out that most people who are owed a duty under the homeless legislation have a right to appear on the register; they set out that certain other people from abroad do not have the right to appear on the register and they lay down the information which must be contained on the register and the procedure that must be followed before someone can be removed from it.

The Homelessness Regulations introduce several important measures. They make supplementary provisions for people who are subject to immigration control, but who are also eligible for assistance under the homelessness legislation; they specify other people from abroad who are not entitled to homelessness assistance; they set down the periods of notice which local authorities must give when they are to cease to accommodate someone beyond the two-year period; they specify a period during which the local authority cannot transfer responsibility for households which are placed out of the area to another authority. We debated that subject at length when we considered the Housing Bill.

Mr. Clappison
: Veterans of the Standing Committee which considered the Housing Bill will remember the comments that the hon. Member for Greenwich (Mr. Raynsford) made on the Bill's drafting. Those of us with non-technical minds found his comments interesting. I warn the Committee that if we seek improved drafting, we will not be able to conclude our proceedings in the time scale that we envisaged. On the JPEG 7 Housing Bill, the further drafting for which the hon. Gentleman called took some time to complete. Whatever the drafting, I hope that the hon. Member for Greenwich will accept that the measure will operate in the way that I described.

I move from the regulations on homelessness and on the allocation of housing to consideration of the contracting-out order. I hope that it will draw a warm glow of approbation from certain quarters of the Committee even though they may not approve of its drafting. The order will perform an important function.

The transfer of local authority housing stock—large-scale voluntary transfer as it is often known—is an excellent way to increase investment in local authority housing, and improve tenants' living conditions, by mobilising private finance. Since 1988, 53 authorities have transferred almost 233,000 dwellings, generating more than £3.7 billion in private finance. Transfer is the best way to get more investment in housing, to improve the condition of the stock and to offer tenants a higher standard of management. That all comes to pass when transfer takes place.

Tenants like transfers because they get an accelerated programme of repairs, guaranteed rent levels for up to five years and often closer tenant involvement in mangement. Local authorities also feel the benefits through large capital receipts.

We have introduced the order to enable authorities to contract out their executive functions under parts VI and VII of the Housing Act 1996. That will be particularly relevant to authorities that have transferred all or part of their stock to a registered social landlord under the large-scale voluntary transfer—LSVT—arrangements. It will also mean, however, that other authorities may participate in a common housing register run by another body if they wish.

In the case of an LSVT authority—an authority which has transferred its stock to a housing association or another registered social landlord—the registered social landlord has become, in effect, the main provider of social housing in the authority's area. The authority retains the strategic responsibility for ensuring that housing needs are met in its area and, in keeping with that, the order will enable an authority to contract out only executive functions.

For example, an authority will be able to contract out lettings under its allocation scheme, but it will itself have to set out the terms of the allocation scheme and decide which classes of persons are or are not qualifying persons entitled to appear on the register. Under section 72 of the Deregulation and Contracting Out Act 1994, local authorities remain ultimately reponsible for functions carried out by the contractor.

The order needs to be made before enforcement of parts VI and VII of the 1996 Act in order to allow authorities to enter into new agreements with their registered social landlords. In accordance with the requirements of the Deregulation and Contracting 8 Out Act 1994, we consulted local authority associations in England and Wales in October and we received no objections to the proposals set out in the order. Copies of the Department's consultation letter and a list of reponses have been placed in the Libraries of each House. Copies of the reponses have been placed in the Department's library.

The order is welcomed by local government generally and it is needed by local authorities who have transferred stock. I commend it and the two sets of regulations to the Committee.

Mr. Raynsford
: As the Minister has made clear, we are dealing with three statutory instruments. The link between them is that they all relate to homelessness. They arise from the Housing Act 1996, which made far-reaching changes to the framework under which local authorities have responsibilities to assist the homeless.

There is, of course, immense need for such provisions. The numbers of homeless people in Britain are currently recorded in official statistics at about 120,000. That is double the number officially recorded as homeless in 1979 when the Government came into power. Many people—mainly single and childless—are not recorded because they do not approach a local authority, but they are also homeless. The most extreme examples of them sleep rough on our streets.
