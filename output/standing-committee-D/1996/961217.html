legislation Draft Social Security (Incapacity for Work and Miscellaneous Amendments) Regulations 1996

4.30 pm

The Minister for Social Security and Disabled People (Mr. Alistair Burt): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Social Security (Incapacity for Work and Miscellaneous Amendments) Regulations 1996.

I am delighted to welcome you, Sir David, as Chairman of this Committee.

The Regulations before the Committee today represent the first changes to the legislation since incapacity benefit was introduced in April 1995. They include a number of tidying-up measures and points of clarification which we have identified over the first 18 months of operating and monitoring the new arrangements. In addition, we are taking this opportunity to respond to a recent judgment by the High Court. That decision concerned the beneficial provision contained in regulation 27 of the Incapacity Benefit (General) regulations, which allows people with certain conditions who would otherwise fail the all work test to be treated as incapable of work.

We are debating these regulations today under the agreed special procedures for parliamentary scrutiny which were laid down in the Social Security (Incapacity For Work) Act 1994. These require regulations made under certain powers in the Act within four years of Royal Assent, however minor, to be passed by affirmative resolution of each House. Opposition Members were central to the development of that procedure, which ensures that, while the new system is bedding down, any changes will be formally debated by Parliament.

The introduction of incapacity benefit last year represented a major reform of the benefits for people who are incapable of working because of the effects of an illness or disability. Experience has shown that when introducing any reform of this magnitude there will always be a need for some fine-tuning in the light of practical operational experience. To a large extent that is what these regulations provide. They introduce a number of changes to three sets of the regulations which contain the detailed rules for determining entitlement to benefits for incapacity: the Social Security (Incapacity For Work) (General) Regulations 1995; the Social Security (Incapacity Benefit) Transitional Regulations 1995, and the Social Security (Overlapping Benefits) Regulations 1979.

I should say at the outset that none of the amendments we are considering today introduces any significant changes to our established policy in this Col. 4 area. I have written to members of the Committee with a summary of what each of the regulations sets out to do, and I hope that that will assist our debate.

Included are amendments which are directly beneficial in nature; others, by clarifying the existing regulations, will ensure consistent application of the all work test procedures. I hope that they will be recognised as such and welcomed by all hon. Members. An example is the proposed amendment to the current provision which allows incapacity benefit recipients to care for a close relative without that being treated as work, which could affect benefit entitlement. The amendment broadens the definition of "close relative" to include, among others, spouse, grandparents and grandchildren.

Another beneficial measure concerns the long established provision under which people who refuse to undergo medical treatment which might improve their condition may be disqualified from benefit. We are restricting the circumstances in which that provision can be used, to ensure that it can only be considered in respect of treatment that has been recommended by the claimant's own doctor or hospital.

The new all work test of incapacity is, of course, the key to the reforms that we introduced last year. We have therefore been monitoring its application very closely, and have undertaken a number of exercises to assess the experience of the Benefits Agency doctors and adjudication officers who have been applying the test over the first 18 months of live running. The detailed activities and descriptors which make up the all work test, and which allow the claimant and the doctor to express the extent of someone's physical and mental impairment in specific functional areas, are set out in the schedule to the general regulations. Our monitoring has shown that, in a few areas, the wording of some of these activities and descriptors needs to be more precise.

Mr. Alan Howarth (Stratford-on-Avon): The Minister has said that he attaches great importance to the evidence obtained from doctors employed by the Benefits Agency medical service. Will he say why he has just announced that he is lowering the standards required of doctors recruited by that service?

Mr. Burt: We are not lowering the standard: we are widening the range of doctors who can be used by the Benefits Agency medical service. We want to broaden the criteria for the experience required of doctors before recruitment to the service. The previous regulations demanded five years' experience of general practice, which ruled out some doctors whose experience might have been in hospitals but who had sufficient knowledge of the areas of functional limitation to make them useful in dealing with the test. That is the reason for the change. I can say more about that later, but I do not want it to be thought that there has in any way been a lowering of standards.

Image Col. 5

Sir John Stanley (Tonbridge and Malling): I thought that my hon. Friend said that he had sent members of the Committee an explanatory letter about the regulations. I regret that I have not received one. Can he arrange for copies to be made available?

Mr. Burt: I am delighted to help my right hon. Friend as quickly as I can by giving him a copy of the letter.

Having identified the areas I have mentioned, we sought advice from the Department's lawyers and medical advisers as to how we might improve the wording. In doing so our aim has been to ensure that the amendments more accurately reflect the original policy intention. Hon. Members may recall that that was established in the light of a consensus agreed following the development work for the test, which included assistance from a panel of 80 experts appointed to help with its detailed design. I reassure the Committee that none of those improvements represents any tightening of the functional descriptors in the test beyond the original policy intention.

I can perhaps best illustrate the sort of amendment proposed by giving an example. One of the functional areas assessed under the all work test is manual dexterity. The activities specified in that area measure a person's ability to perform various tasks with his hands. We want to make it clear that the ability to turn a tap refers to an ordinary sink tap; and that the word "control" in the phrase

"control knobs on a cooker"

is intended to be an adjective, not a verb. That is the sort of detail into which the regulations go.

Several of the activities in the test relate to an inability to perform particular tasks with either hand, or with either arm. We want to make it clear that those references relate to disabilities in both hands or arms, rather than in one or the other. Separate descriptors are, of course, specified to assess people with disabilities in just one hand or arm.

We are also making absolutely clear the general requirement, already set out in the regulations, for the award of points under the all work test to be linked to the existence of a medical condition.

I hope that the Committee will acknowledge the importance of removing such areas of ambiguity in which a lack of precision in the original drafting might lead to an inconsistent application of the test.

I turn to the effects of the High Court decision to which I referred earlier. It concerned the application of the beneficial provision in regulation 27 of the Social Security (Incapacity for Work) (General) Regulations 1995. We recognised from our work in developing the all work test of incapacity that a minority of claimants who are clearly incapable of work could fail the test because their current condition causes only limited functional impairment. Regulation 27 allows a person who fails the test still to be treated as incapable of work if the examining doctor considers that one of the specified exceptional medical circumstances applies.

The question of whether not one of those circumstances applies is, of course, a medical one. That is why the application of the provision was conditional Col. 6 on the recommendation of the examining doctor. The High Court decided, however, that it was wrong to make the doctor's opinion binding on the adjudication officer. The practical effect of the judgment is that lay adjudication officers and social security appeal tribunals are no longer bound by the examining doctor's opinion on the application of regulation 27 and can take into account other evidence when considering whether exceptional circumstances apply.

We are amending the provision in the light of the court's decision to assist the adjudicating authorities in their determination of the question. The proposed wording more clearly defines the circumstances in which that exceptional provision applies. Adjudication officers will still be required to take account of the examining doctor's opinion. That must be right, given that we are talking about a specialist medical question; but they will also be able to consider other medical evidence—for example, that provided by the claimant's doctor—and give preference to such evidence if they consider that it outweighs that of the examining doctor.

The all work test arrangements are fair in principle and in application. The draft regulations introduce important fine-tuning to those arrangements and I commend them to the Committee.

4.39 pm

Mr. Henry McLeish (Fife, Central): I echo the Minister's sentiments about your being in the Chair, Sir David.

I am always slightly apprehensive when a Minister whom I greatly respect comes to the Committee suggesting that all that is on offer will be a significant improvement for claimants the length and the breadth of the nation. I suspect that when we look at the regulations in more detail, we may find that they will not help claimants, but will narrow criteria for eligibility for benefits.

Although the Minister talked about small changes, minor amendments and easing up the system, all the small changes that we increasingly see in regulations brought before the House add up, of course, to significant changes affecting claimants' daily activities in the benefits culture. It is important when we are dealing with regulations to put them in a wider context and recognise that small changes can add up to considerable developments.

First, the regulations before us today, and many that have emerged previously, shift the balance of advantage from individuals to the Government. In a democracy, we should be concerned about that. Secondly, such regulations create obstacles in the path of benefits claimants. The overwhelming majority are claiming benefits which they are fully eligible to obtain. But regulations are often aimed at narrowing the criteria for eligibility. Imperceptible changes, when viewed over a long period, can add up to significant change. Thirdly, regulations create more and more powers for Secretaries of States to intervene.

A fourth point which is causing me great concern is that regulations and legislation are based on an assumption that people are not deserving or genuine. Image Col. 7 If such a culture is created within the benefits system, people who genuinely need to claim can be discouraged from doing so because they do not want to experience the indignity attached to some aspects of the all work test.

It is important to provide the context for our specific concerns. There are three issues that I want to address. The first is regulation 27, the second is the amended physical descriptors and the third is the sensitive issue of mental illness.

Regulation 27(b) of the Social Security (Incapacity for Work) (General) Regulations 1995 covers exceptional circumstances where it says:

"Although a claimant may not satisfy the all work test, he or she may still be treated as incapable of work in specific circumstances, as approved by a doctor approved by the Secretary of State";

This applied when

"he suffers from some specific disease or bodily or mental disablement and, by reasons of such disease or disablement, there would be a substantial risk to the mental or physical health of any person if he were found capable of work".

In practice, the existing regulations allow claimants with progressive illnesses such as severe heart conditions to be treated as incapable of work. It was accepted that claimants with long-term, potentially life-threatening illnesses could not return to the work place. The proposed amendment to regulation 27 makes no mention of the risk to new claimants in a similar position.

An article in the Daily Mirror on 14 November illustrated that point. It is a dramatic piece, and I am not using the drama to suggest that all cases are like this. The headline read,

"Heart Man Dies After 'Back To Work' Order",

and the article said:

"A heart patient collapsed and died after being told he must find a job or lose his state benefits. Stricken David Holmes, 54, had another heart attack which friends believe was brought on by distress over his treatment by Government bureaucrats."

That gentleman was certainly not fit for work and died a week after undergoing the stress of the all work test. That is an extreme situation, but it underlines the fact that the regulations that we had before provided important protection. We have to go beyond a hard all work test to recognise that there are issues of people's general state of health. If we dismiss those, there could be serious consequences.

In the eyes of the disability lobby in particular, regulation 2(9) is an extremely damaging change to the exceptional circumstances in which a disabled person who does not pass the all work test can still be deemed incapable of work. There are currently four such exceptional circumstances, but the new regulations reduce them to three. They eliminate the condition of a person who

"suffers from some specific disease or bodily or mental disablement"

where it was deemed that

"by reasons of such disease … there would be a substantial risk."

The Disability Law Service argues for the retention of that condition because some disabling conditions might not allow a person to score sufficient points on Col. 8 the all work test, but might still make it impossible for him to work without his condition seriously deteriorating. The Government should bear that important consideration in mind when they make changes that seem minor on the surface, but which can be deadly for claimants.

The second issue that I want to stress is the amendment of the schedule of physical descriptors in 3(b) to 3(e). Original descriptor 3(b) awards 15 points against the scoring system when a person cannot

"sit comfortably for more than 10 minutes without having to move from the chair."

The amended descriptor reads:

"Cannot sit comfortably for more than 10 minutes without having to move from the chair because the degree of discomfort makes it impossible to continue sitting."

I sometimes think that I do not have the intellect to understand the regulations that emanate from the Department— [Interruption.] I am grateful and reassured that the Government Whip has kindly suggested from a sedentary position that I do have the intellect—perhaps other factors are at work, then.

That is just one of the changes, but alterations have been made to another four descriptors. Why is it that when the knife is put in, it has to be twisted? What is the game plan behind some of the regulations? Of course, the Government have to save money—that is the raison d'être behind the proposals. There has been a huge expansion in the regulations governing incapacity benefit and its predecessor, invalidity benefit. We are, however, dealing with real people, and I would be grateful if the Minister dealt with the slight changes before us, which could have devastating consequences for those involved.

The third issue that I want to discuss is mental illness. The original regulation 10 in the Social Security (Incapacity for Work) (General) Regulations 1995 covered people with a severe mental condition who should be treated as incapable of work. Regulation 10(2)(e)(viii) verified that a person shall be treated as incapable of work if a doctor who is approved by the Secretary of State certifies that he is suffering from "a severe mental illness". The amended and much expanded regulation stipulates that such an individual should be treated as incapable of work should he suffer from

"a severe mental illness, involving the presence of mental disease, which severely and adversely affects a person's mood or behaviour, which severely restricts the social functioning, or his awareness of his immediate environment."

I hope that the Minister will explain why there is such an expanded definition. Is it perhaps too prescriptive; and what is the definition of a mental disease? In their haste, the Government are pushing into regulations definitions which would be accepted by few experts in the field. Many of the lobbies that work day in and day out on behalf of claimants would not accept those definitions. Once again therefore we must deal with changes of words and small modifications that may have significant consequences for eligibility criteria.

Image Col. 9 In dealing with mental illness it is important to adopt a higher level of sensitivity. I appreciate that we are dealing with vast sums of taxpayers' money, but we are also dealing with people who have severe problems and who should be given maximum credibility.

The Minister should address himself to the definitions and the impact of the changes that he is about to implement in January. They will not enhance incapacity benefit but will detract from the Government's efforts to achieve the requisite fairness, equity and high degree of sensitivity.

Mr. Burt: Without being unnecessarily disputatious, I think that the hon. Lady will be aware that the success/failure ratio of appeals under the previous system was very similar to what it is now. Ms Lynne: Surely the Minister cannot be happy with the fact that almost 50 per cent. of cases are won on appeal. The Minister cannot complacently say, "Things are no different now from what we had before". He is the Minister responsible. The Minister ought to think again about incapacity benefit and the all work test generally, but he must specifically reexamine the regulations.
