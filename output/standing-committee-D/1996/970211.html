[MR. PATRICK THOMPSON in the Chair]

Draft Register of Occupational and Personal Pension Schemes Regulations 1997

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. Oliver Heald)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Register of Occupational and Personal Pension Schemes Regulations 1997.

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the Occupational Pensions Schemes (Winding-Up) Regulations 1996 (S.I. 1996, No. 3126).

Mr. Heald
: This is the first time that I have been in Committee with you as Chairman, Mr. Thompson. I am sure that all members of the Committee agree that it is a pleasure.

We have before us two separate sets of regulations which form an important part of the Government's pension reforms. I shall begin with the Register of Occupational and Personal Pension Schemes Regulations. The background to the regulations is that the register of schemes was established in 1991. It registers schemes, operates the scheme tracing service and collects the pension schemes' levy. The tracing service is particularly important because many individuals with preserved pension rights tend not to focus on them until they approach retirement. Someone who loses contact with a former scheme can contact the Registrar of Occupational and Personal Pension Schemes and have the scheme traced through the register database.

The Register of Occupational and Personal Pension Schemes Regulations 1990 govern the register's operation. They need to be amended to appoint the Occupational Pensions Regulatory Authority as the new registrar from April 1997 in place of the Occupational Pensions Board, which has been the registrar until now. We also need to amend the regulations because of the introduction of the general and compensation levies.

In practice, the register works extremely well, so while we have carried forward most of the original provisions, we have also taken this opportunity to simplify the registration process. We are extending from six months to 12 months the time limit for reporting changes to the registrar. That will mean that schemes will be able to notify changes at the same time as they make their annual levy payments, which will help schemes and the registrar.

4 There are also minor changes to the items of information required for the register, including a new piece of information about the nature of scheme benefits.

The regulations change the requirements about disclosing information from the register. At the moment, those engaged in research are allowed unfettered access to the register database. That has raised concerns about confidentiality, particularly in relation to data protection legislation. In future, only the registrar's staff will have the right to inspect the register. We recognise that the register is unique as a source of information about schemes and there is growing demand for such information, so the registrar will be able to provide information from the register direct to any organisation or individual specifically for statistical analysis.

As well as offering a free tracing service for scheme members, the registrar will continue to provide information free of charge to the pensions ombudsman and to trustees about their own schemes. The register will also be an important source of information to assist people in fulfilling their obligations under the Pensions Act 1995. That is why we have extended the provision so that information can go to the Occupational Pensions Regulatory Authority, the Pensions Compensation Board, insolvency practitioners and the official receiver.

At the moment, a breach of the requirements to provide information to the registrar can result in a criminal sanction. A court can impose a fine up to level 3 on the standard scale, which means up to £3,000. As a result of the Pensions Act and these regulations, the maximum fine will now be level 5—up to £5,000. OPRA has the power to impose a medium-level penalty on anyone who uses register information to market a product or service. That carries a maximum civil penalty of £1,000 for an individual, or £10,000 for a corporate body.

The regulations have been subject to extensive consultation with practitioners and others who have an interest. We have addressed the concerns and comments received. The regulations proivde an efficient and sensible way forward.

The Occupational Pensions Schemes (Winding-Up) Regulations are introduced by the Pensions Act, which contains a new statutory priority rule that will apply when a salary-related pension scheme winds up. That was debated at length in both Houses during the passage of the Bill. To recap the main issues briefly, the extensive measures introduced in the Act are designed to promote the security of pension funds for all members. It must be right to protect all the members when a scheme winds up not fully funded. That is what the statutory priority rule is designed to achieve.

I understand the concern, which was expressed when the Act was debated, that future increases to pensions would not have the same priority as they have now under scheme rules. But pensioners will still be well protected. Pensions in payment will be second only to additional voluntary contributions in the priority order. I have heard it argued that pensioners need more protection because they have no chance to JPEG 5 rebuild their pension rights, but those close to retirement can find themselves in a similar position. They, too, need protection. Even those who are not so close to retirement must be treated fairly so that they know that their plans to build a reasonable income for retirement are secure.

The transitional priority order relates to schemes that start to wind up in the 10 years from 6 April 1997 and meets a promise that was made when the Act was before Parliament. That will give high priority for pensions already in payment, including indexation, and to accrued rights for contracted-out benefits, including guaranteed minimum pensions. There will be no sudden change in the position of pensioners or those with substantial guaranteed minimum pension rights.

That links directly to the transitional introduction of the minimum funding requirement. A scheme that is fully funded under the MFR should have sufficient funds to cover pensions already in payment, including future indexation. Non-pensioner members should also get their fair share of the fund. Until the MFR comes fully into effect, there is obviously a greater risk that a scheme will wind up unable to meet all of its liabilities. That is why we have provided that transitional arrangement for the priority order.

Ms Liz Lynne
(Rochdale)
: I should be grateful if the Minister would tell the Committee what consultation he has carried out. The explanatory note on the winding-up regulations states that

"the Secretary of State has consulted such persons as he considers appropriate."

Which pensions organisations have the Government consulted?

Mr. Heald
: The procedure has been the same with each set of regulations that has come forward under the Pensions Act 1995. An outline of the regulations, but not the detailed drafting, has been sent to the entire industry—every organisation from the joint working group to the National Association of Pension Funds—and I am sure that my officials could supply a full list. A wide-ranging consultation also included individuals who expressed an interest, pressure groups and so on.

After the outline was considered and we took the comments back, we produced draft regulations, sent them round the same group of people—the industry—and took their comments on board and made changes as appropriate. The consultation was wide.

Ms Lynne
: I have been told that BT pensioners were not sent the regulations. Does the Minister know whether that is the case? Are there other pensions organisations which have not been provided with them? I have talked to solicitors and they think that this matter could be a minefield.

Mr. Heald
: I disagree that it is a minefield, although it has inherent complexity, as the hon. Lady and others will have realised. I keep in regular contact with the BT pensioners—I met them last year—and do not recall them raising that issue. The list of consultees includes, for example, the Confederation of Occupational 6 Pensioners Associations, the Confederation of British Industry and other groups which have an interest in the matter.

The list is long, and I am happy to let the hon. Lady read it. It also includes the Association of Pension Lawyers, which is well known to the lawyers in this field, and the Association of Pensioner Trustees, which includes many scheme board members. In fact, the list includes everyone we could think of who might be interested.

I shall tackle some of the other issues which are covered in the regulations, which deal also with several technical problems. They identify when a scheme begins to wind up for the purpose of the regulations. That is an important technical issue, which concerns the date of crystallisation of the liabilities.

The regulations cover the position of pensions in payment that were secured by insurance policies before 6 April 1997 and where surrendering the policies would reduce the value of the pensions—cases in which there is no money over and above the liability that is secured. In such cases, all the pension in payment will take first priority in the list after additional voluntary contributions.

The regulations require the method of valuing pension rights to be in line with the minimum funding requirement valuation method and actuarial guidance. Importantly, they allow a lesser or greater amount to be used to secure pensions in payment so that the current cost of annuities can be met.

The process of winding up a pension scheme can be complicated and lengthy, which is why the regulations provide clarity about where members come in the priority order at the beginning of the process.

The regulations also cover technical issues about the discharge of liabilities. They limit the trustees' ability to defer wind-up and deal with the complications that can arise in multi-employer and hybrid schemes. All the provisions concern the nitty gritty, but also the real problems of handling a wind-up.

The regulations are important. Without them we would have confusion or stalemate, neither of which would be in the interests of scheme members—pensioners and non-pensioners alike.

Some people complain that the regulations under the Act are over-prescriptive, but we have tried in all of them to pick up the real problems that the experts have raised with us in consultation. The regulations are a positive reaction to requests from thoses who have to deal with pension scheme wind-ups for us to clarify in legislation how problems should be dealt with. The registry regulations have been prepared in extensive consultation with those who use the registry, and the same is true of the winding-up provisions.

I hope that the Committee will recognise how important the regulations are in the overall scheme of things. I commend them to the Committee.

Mr. John Denham
(Southampton, Itchen)
: I am grateful to the Minister for his introduction to the two statutory instruments. I shall deal first with the winding-up regulations because they raise the greatest number of substantive issues.

The concern is clearly that the regulations could significantly disadvantage existing pensioners of occupational pension schemes or people who may soon retire and become pensioner members of such schemes. There is a contradiction at the heart of the regulations between the implementation of sections 73 and 67 of the Pensions Act 1995. Section 67 makes the intention of the House at that time perfectly clear: once a member has approved a right under a pension scheme it cannot be taken away without his consent. Under the regulations, however, someone who pays into an occupational pension scheme in the expectation—indeed, according to the way in which the scheme is drawn up, with the guarantee—that he will receive limited price indexation when he retires could, in circumstances that I shall outline in a moment, lose the right to that indexation, even though he has paid in for it and it is part of the scheme's basis. Effectively, rights that people have built up in the scheme are being retrospectively removed.

For the purposes of our discussion, the main change that will take place is that guaranteed increases in pensions in payment will have lower priority than pensions that are not in payment. If a scheme fails because of under-provision, the remaining assets will have to be distributed around the different categories of beneficiaries of the scheme. The payment of pension increases to existing pensioners will come right at the end of the queue and people will be least likely to receive that part of their pension.

At the extreme, for example, the regulations could mean that a higher priority woud be attached to returning the contributions of a 20 or 21-year-old person who had paid into an occupational pension scheme for only a couple of years than to the payment of increases to people who had retired already.

The regulations would also mean that, under what could be called the legislation's cascade principle, as soon as a priority was left unfulfilled all the money would be shared proportionately over a scheme's different claimants. Also, if there were any money to pay increases, the increases to pensioners would be only in proportion to the increases to those who had not yet retired.

I do not want to return today to the debates over the 1995 Act. I would rather examine the effect of the regulations, but I recall the comment of my hon. Friend the Member for Monklands, East (Mrs. Liddell) when the 1995 Act was in its Committee state. She said:

"By putting pensioners' increases at the bottom of the list of priorities, we are saying that they are much less important than those of deferred benefits members."

8 A little later in that debate, my hon. Friend made another important point:

"The Government must ensure that individual scheme members are aware of the consequences of the changes to wind-up. People might otherwise budget for their retirement but fail to take into account the fact that their pension might be frozen".—[ Official Report, Standing Committee D, 8 June 1995; c. 507–9.]

That is important. It is posssible that younger members of pension schemes who become aware of the impact of the Pensions Act 1995 and of these regulations will be able to make extra provision—if they have the right information and are sufficiently prudent. They might use additional voluntary contributions, which are the most protected category under the Act, to safeguard against the loss of indexation if their schemes fail. However, people who have retired already will not be able to adjust their planning for the future. For them, loss of pension increase could be serious.

The Minister spoke earlier about consultations, and he will acknowledge that anxiety about the regulations is widespread. That unease has been expressed strongly by the National Association of Pension Funds, which represents the great majority of occupational pension schemes. That association has said that the regulations are likely to deprive a number of current pensioners and their widows when they are very elderly.

When the regulations come fully into force in 2007, there will be many people with pensions in payment who are in their 70s, 80s and 90s. As time goes on, they are less likely to be original memebers of the scheme than those members' widows. Those widows will probably receive reduced payments from the scheme and they will suffer even more if they lose indexation.

That forecast has been made by the National Association of Pension Funds and by the United Kingdom pension scheme operated by Guinness, the GUD pension trust. The trust has some 18,000 pensioners—10,000 deferred members and 7,000 active members—and has also expressed fears about the retrospective loss of members' rights that have been paid for in the past both of those members and by the company.

What alternative proposals are available to tackle the problem of how the Act should be implemented? First, does the timetable have to be in place by 2007? It would be possible to protect the rights of existing pensioners by extending that timetable by 10 years. That would affect the way in which assets were distributed in schemes being wound up over that period of time but it would provide a longer period of protection for people whose pensions were already in payment and who are most at risk if their schemes fail.

The second possibility under section 73 of the 1995 Act seems to be that the regulations could make the Act apply only to new accruals. That would limit the pension rights of active members of the scheme only to accruals made from 1997 onwards. That would provide a much longer time before the full impact of the Act came into force. I hope that the Minister will provide a reasonable explanation of why those JPEG 9 alternatives have not been taken into account, since the implications for existing pensions might be considerable.

Let us consider an example. Someone may have retired this year at the age of 57 on a pension of £8,000 a year, which might increase to £14,000 per annum by 2008 as a result of guaranteed cost of living increases. Let us assume that the pension scheme then winds up when the member is aged 69 and that insufficient funds are available to cover indexation. It is not difficult to show that, under those circumstances, by the time the member was 80 the pension would still be fixed at £14,000 per annum, but if inflation averaged 5 per cent., an indexed pension would have gone up to £24,000 per annum. In other words, under the regulations a member would be living at only 58 per cent. of his former standard of living, which would worsen as he grew older. That represents a very significant potential impact.

I recognise, as the Minister said, that we also have to take into account the position of those approaching retirement who have an interest in the distribution of assets of the scheme. Working on a new accruals basis would be an easy way of protecting such people, so the Government should consider that option more carefully.

It was suggested earlier that there is nothing to worry about because schemes will be protected by the minimum funding requirement. I recall being told on a number of occasions when we were debating the Pensions Act that the Swan Hunter scheme, which had collapsed at that time, would have met the requirements of minimum funding. However, it was clearly unable to pay out the pensions that people had been expecting. Irrespective of that particular case, it is possible that the wind-up of a scheme could take place between the period when minimum funding requirement valuations had been carried out and the stock market fell, which could leave underfunded schemes that had apparently passed the minimum funding requirement test.

The company behind the scheme might also have hit severe financial difficulties. The fact that the shortage of funds in the pension scheme was technically a liability on the company might have no meaning. There are real possibilities therefore that even when the minimum funding requirement is fully in place, a scheme might not in practice have the assets necessary to meet the indexation of pensions.

I hope that the Minister will be able to say more about why the alternative approaches to the regulations have been rejected and about why existing pensioners are having some of their accrued rights removed by the regulations.

Let me turn briefly to the Register of Occupational and Personal Pension Schemes, which marks not the coming of age but the formal birth of OPRA as the pension regulator. Will the Minister deal with the position of schemes that are not yet registered with, or regulated by, OPRA but will be from 1 April. Anxieties are being expressed about the conduct of ballots for member-nominated trustees. Complainants are being 10 told that they should refer their complaints to OPRA, but OPRA is saying that, as it is not yet in existence, it cannot handle the complaints.

I have had drawn to my attention issues about the information provided in the recent GEC ballot, issues concerned with the company Thomas Broadbent and Sons and issues connected with Fullwood and Bland pension schemes. In each case, concerns were raised about information provided to members or the conduct of the ballot. There seems to be no body yet in existence to which members of those schemes who believe that the ballot has been conducted wrongly can take their complaints. I should be grateful if the Minister would tell us, on the record, to whom he thinks they should be able to take their complaints and what they ought to be able to do about them.

Mr. Heald
: We are aware of the comments made today about the new priority order on winding up, but we must think of the position of all members when a scheme winds up. The prescribed valuation method means that pensions will be valued by reference to the rate of return on gilts. On wind up, therefore, more cash will be attributed for pensioner liabilities than if assets were distributed to all members in proportion to their accrued rights.

The hon. Member for Southampton, Itchen suggested that it would be a retrospective reduction in rights; but it is not. The regulations do not change the rights that people already have. Those rights remain the same. What is important is the level of assets available to meet those rights. The priority order does not preclude the payment of indexation to pensioners; it ensures that funds are used first to secure basic pension rights for all members. Only then does it provide for indexation. We should remember that all members have rights, and that those rights should be protected.

JPEG 13 It was said that, the regulations would give pension increases a lower priority and that there would be less protection, but the regulations deal with the transitional period, not the principal change imposed by the Act. Ten years is sufficient for the transition, but I shall say a little more about that later.

It is wrong to say that, under the transitional arrangements, increases are at the bottom of the list. They are not. The list for the transitional arrangements that we are debating today starts, as everyone agrees it should, with AVCs. It then lists pensions in payment and next protects the rights that the national insurance rebate has paid for—the basic core rights of individuals in the national insurance scheme who contracted out and who are now entitled to a guaranteed minimum pension. The new rights, which will be acquired after 1997, not those over and above those rights purchased by the rebates, are the very core. The list then deals with issues such as increases.

It would not be right to say that that is not the right way to prioritise. Putting the most important rights at the head of the list reflects what happens now with the order of priorities in scheme rules. With the exception of increases, we are protecting first the rights that are most important.

Mr. Heald

In addition, at the end of the period, the new section 9(2)(b) rights will have been on stream for 10 years; those rights are all the rights that accrue under an occupational pension scheme.
