legislation Special Grant Report (No. 26) (House of Commons Paper No. 326)

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Sir Paul Beresford): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Special Grant Report (No. 26) (House of Commons Paper No. 326).

The report sets out the basis for the provision of financial support to the London borough of Tower Hamlets, Manchester city council and the Greater Manchester police authority in respect of the cost to those authorities of dealing with the aftermath of the terrorist attacks on London docklands and Manchester.

Hon. Members will share my deep regret that the report has been necessary. We were all appalled by the cowardly IRA bombing of docklands last February, which brought the ceasefire to an end and caused widespread damage and tragic loss of life. The equally atrocious attack on Manchester city centre in June was another cynical outrage which showed that there were no depths to which these criminals would not sink.

Both areas face an immense long-term rebuilding task, and the Government are paying their share. We have announced £6 million of additional estate action funding for the Barkantine estate in Tower Hamlets, which took the brunt of the docklands blast. We have also made £2.6 million available to the London Docklands Development Corporation for repairs to roads and pavements and for assisting the police and Tower Hamlets in providing closed-circuit television coverage to give greater security to everyone in the area. A similar sum has been provided to the docklands light railway for repairs to South Quay station, which serves businesses and the local community. My right hon. Friend the Deputy Prime Minister recently announced that the Government would provide up to £43 million over the next three years, in addition to the £20 million of European funds announced last year, towards the cost of rebuilding Manchester city centre.

We recognise that Tower Hamlets, Manchester city council, and the Greater Manchester police authority incurred substantial costs in the months following the attacks in clearing up the streets, safeguarding damaged and dangerous property and providing support and assistance to the affected communities. The two councils also suffered loss of income, directly or indirectly, as a result of the incidents.

Col. 4 All three authorities applied for help with those costs under the Bellwin scheme of emergency financial assistance to local authorities. Under that programme the Secretary of State may set up a scheme of assistance where an authority has acted immediately in response to an emergency, in order to safeguard life or property or to prevent suffering or severe inconvenience to local residents. Grant is paid on eligible net expenditure above a threshold. The Bellwin scheme is usually invoked following exceptional weather emergencies such as flooding and storm damage.

We considered the applications carefully and found that the nature of the costs resulting from the Docklands and Manchester bombs did not sit easily with the statutory constraints on the Bellwin scheme. The relevant primary legislation—section 155 of the Local Government and Housing Act 1989—permits financial assistance only in respect of

"expenditure on, or in connection with, the taking of immediate action".

This ruled out assistance in respect of loss of income, which formed a substantial part of Manchester city council's claim, or even small-scale permanent capital works such as replacing windows.

The statute also restricts our capacity to consider paying grant on all but the most immediate expenditure. However, the scale of the blasts and the resulting dislocation of activity caused relief effort to be unusually protracted. We found that after deducting the relevant thresholds none of the authorities would have received much, if any, assistance under the Bellwin scheme. We felt strongly that in view of the exceptional nature and scale of the incidents, the authorities should not meet those costs unaided. We therefore decided to use our powers under section 88B of the Local Government Finance Act 1988 to make assistance available to the authorities in the form of a special grant. The purpose of the grant is to provide support towards expenditure incurred as a result of dealing with the aftermath of the terrorist attacks and towards loss of income incurred as a consequence of the attacks.

As everyone in the House will be aware, resources are not unlimited, and we have had to cash-limit the total amount of grant payable. Nevertheless, up to £2.5 million will be made available. The actual amount payable to each of the three authorities will be determined once we have received their audited claims. The report, however, sets out the basis on which those calculations will be made.

In determining the manner in which grant payments are to be calculated, we have taken into account the representations that we have received from the three authorities. Annex A to the report sets out what we believe to be a fair and affordable basis for establishing each authority's entitlement to the grant.

Grant will be payable on relevant expenditure and income loss above a specified amount, equivalent to 0.075 per cent. of each authority's standard spending assessment. The specified amounts are far lower than the thresholds that would have been applicable under the Bellwin scheme, which was originally anticipated as being of use to the local authorities.

Mr. David Sumberg (Bury, South): As my hon. Friend will know, many traders in the centre of Manchester have lost a great deal of money as a result of the terrible bombing there. Sir Paul Beresford: I thank my hon. Friend for his point. I shall pick it up in my explanation. The special grant is not the only source of funding. Manchester has a Lords Mayor's fund that will also help. That fund has reached a sizeable sum.

The report defines relevant expenditure in a way that enables us to pay grant on additional uninsurable expenditure which was necessarily incurred by each authority in the six months following the two explosions as a direct result of responding to and clearing up after the incidents. The authorities will be able to claim for additional expenditure incurred to provide relief, to carry out works to safeguard life or property, to alleviate suffering or to prevent severe inconvenience. Such costs include, for example, the additional costs of clearing debris, evacuating people from dangerous structures and making those structures safe, providing emergency accommodation, supplying food and other key services to affected communities and counselling people affected by the explosions. Grant on relevant expenditure will be paid at the rate of 85 per cent.

The authorities will also receive some recompense for uninsurable losses of income where the loss, such as the loss of rental income, is directly attributable to the explosions or where it is indirectly attributable to the attacks. For example, Manchester city council suffered indirectly from loss of income from its city centre car parks when, in the aftermath of the explosion, fewer people visited the city centre. Given that the estimates of loss of income will always be subject to considerable uncertainty, grant on such losses will be payable at the lower rate of 42.5 per cent. I remind the Committee that such assistance would not be given under the Bellwin scheme.

We are not paying grant in respect of insurable costs or insurable losses of income. It would be wrong to undermine the operation of the normal insurance market in respect of those costs. It has long been the Government's policy that prudent individuals and organisations, including local authorities, should make proper arrangements for insurance and consider the need for the insurance that is available. We are not prepared to bail out those organisations that fail to take out adequate insurance, including insurance against terrorist damage.

When commercial reinsurers withdrew from the terrorism insurance market in 1993, the Government stepped in and set up the Pool Reinsurance Company Ltd., which is known as Pool Re, to ensure that everyone had proper access to full terrorism cover. It would be unfair on those who took advantage of those arrangements if we compensated those who, for Col. 6 whatever reasons, chose not to. Of course, we recognise that insurance policies normally carry an excess, and the special grant arrangements make allowance for that.

To avoid double counting with other grants and subsidies—police grant, for example—costs for which other grants have been or will be made, or for which credit approvals have been or will be issued, are not eligible for special grant under the report. Grant will also be net of any receipts to the authorities from charges levied for the provision of goods and services.

The amount of grant payable to each authority will be calculated, in the manner described in annex A, once we have received their claims. Those claims will, of course, be subject to audit and the report stipulates a deadline of 30 September 1997 for receipt of audited claims. We recognise, however, that some time has elapsed since the explosions in docklands and Manchester. Meanwhile, all three authorities have had to meet considerable costs in the present financial year. We therefore propose to make a payment on account to each authority of 90 per cent. of its estimated entitlement to special grant under this report before 31 March. Any necessary adjustment will be made in 1997–98 on receipt of authorities' audited claims.
