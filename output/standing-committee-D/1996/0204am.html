Sex Offenders Bill

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Timothy Kirkhope)
: I beg to move,

That during proceedings on the Sex Offenders Bill the Committee do meet this day and Thursday 6th February at half-past Ten o'clock and half-past Four o'clock.

I shall not detain the Committee for long, Sir Roger. May I say how pleased I am to be in this Committee under your chairmanship. We are looking forward to a constructive Committee stage, dealing with important legislation. The Bill is an important step forward in the development of protection against paedophiles and other serious sex offenders. It has two basic objectives. First, it will place an obligation on those convicted of paedophile or other serious sex offences to notify the police of their movements, thus giving the police in each locality knowledge of the offenders living in their area. That will enable the police to keep tabs on the movement of offenders. The Bill is not primarily about the disclosure of information, although that is an aspect in which others take some interest.

Mr. Kirkhope

I agree that it may be necessary for there to be some clear guidance or a code of practice in such matters, but I hope to deal with that in more detail later.

Secondly, the Bill will give our courts jurisdiction over acts of child sex abuse committed overseas. It is the final link in the chain to outlaw the despicable practice of so-called child sex tourism. Such matters are important and we want to press ahead with them as quickly as possible. That is why I am proposing that the Committee sits this afternoon.

Mr. Thurnham
: The amendments deal with the matter of retrospectivity. At present, the Bill will affect only those who are convicted in future or who are currently serving a sentence or under supervision. That has surprised the members of the public to whom I have spoken and the police. Last month, I met representatives of the Greater Manchester Police Federation; they were surprised that the Bill would not be retrospective, given the well-known fact that people with paedophile tendencies can continue to offend until late in life—their behaviour does not necessarily improve unless they receive proper treatment.

My general feeling about the Bill is that we should approach it from a commonsense point of view, which dictates that we need as good a source of information as possible. Where a known offender is currently residing at a given address, I see no reason why he should not be notified that he must tell the police if he changes 6 address. If the police already know where he is, it will not be a burden on them if the Bill requires that the change of address be notified—indeed, it would help them.

I understand that it would be difficult to notify every previous offender, whose whereabouts may not be known. I asked the Library to estimate how many people would be affected. In its note to me, it said that, on current figures for England and Wales—I do not have the figures for Scotland and Northern Ireland—it would affect 3,000 offenders per annum, plus a further 1,000 people who are cautioned. That is about 4,000 people each year, so if we went back 20 or more years the Bill could cover almost 100,000 people, including many tens of thousands of serious offenders—the Library thought that the figure would be in the upper tens of thousands.

Where the police already know of someone who might be a risk, I do not see what the problem is in their notifying that person. As I see it, the difficulty with the Bill is that it will not allow the police to register someone who had previously offended. The police will have to keep a list of people who offend after the date of the Bill's commencement, but will not be allowed to put on that register the names and addresses of previous offenders. That seems to go against all common sense.

The Minister might claim that the police will be allowed to keep a list of previous offenders, but I do not understand how the register would enable them to find out who had offended if the names and addresses are not included. It seems that the police will have to keep a separate register, which would apply only to those people covered by the Bill; it would not include someone who is well known to the police at his current address. It does not make sense to impose that requirement on the police.

There are already a large number of lists. The Minister of State has told me that the police already hold information on the Phoenix database and on microfiche records held by the national identification service. They may also retain information locally about alleged paedophiles. That does not make it clear exactly how the police would hold and handle that information—whether they would keep separate lists on paper or on computer and whether they would put that information into the Phoenix database.

Moreover, the National Criminal Intelligence Service maintains a database of known or suspected paedophiles; the Department for Education and Employment keeps a list known as list 99; and the Department of Health has a list known as the consultancy index, which covers people who have been convicted of an offence and enables the department to notify employers of the names of persons who worked with children and were dismissed or resigned in circumstances in which a child's welfare was put at risk. In addition, voluntary bodies such as the Scout Association keep lists.

Will the Minister clarify how the Bill will affect local authorities? Local authorities often have more information than other bodies; they know where there is abuse within a household and may require that unless a member of that household leaves the house the children will be taken into care. There may not be a conviction, JPEG 7 but local authorities know well where there are people who are more than vague suspects.

Paragraph 64 of the document "Working Together"—a guide to arrangements under the Children Act 1989 for inter-agency co-operation for the protection of children from abuse—which was published in 1991, states that local authorities should not hold a list of such people. It seems confusing that local authorities have a legal duty—established in the case of Devon county council—to use the information that they have, but guidance from the Government tells them that they should not keep a list. I should be grateful if the Minister would clarify how local authorities should co-ordinate the use of their information.

Virtually everyone who has made representations to me about the Bill has said that a common database is needed to hold all information relevant to paedophile offenders, both those who are convicted in criminal or civil courts and those who, for other reasons, are well known as suspects. There is a requirement for local authorities to keep a list of names and addresses of people who visit homes where children have been abused. Therefore, it is established that local authorities should keep information about suspects, but it is not clear how that information should be co-ordinated.

The public view is that, under the Bill, there will be a paedophile register, which will include all the information that is needed. In fact, the word "register" appears no where in the Bill. It is not obvious what will be done with the information supplied to the police. It would be most helpful if the Minister will clarify exactly how the information will be held and used and how the police can avoid having to keep several different lists in order to comply with the requirements of the Bill, under which they will not be allowed to put the names and addresses of previous offenders on to the register.

In the Government's consultation paper "Sentencing and Supervision of Sex Offenders", which was issued last June, there was a call for measures to provide better protection for the public. That protection is needed now. However, the Bill will not provide the protection that the public wants now. It might do so in decades to come, but for now it only nibbles at the problems—it is a mouse of a Bill given the large numbers of previously convicted offenders.

Offenders' addresses might already be known to the police, so the Government's existing databases could be used to follow up such people. I am not clear how that is done. Presumably, by using national insurance numbers it is possible to know where people work or, if people draw benefit, to know where they do so. It is important to know how all such measures will work.

There is a difficulty with the Government's view that such a requirement is a punishment. In his opening speech, the Minister said that these were draconian measures. I do not see them in that light. If people are still a risk to the public, they should be considered as people who require treatment—indeed, some people 8 might question whether they should be allowed out. If those people require treatment, the Bill should not be considered as draconian.

On the question of whether the Bill should be applied retrospectively, the Minister referred to the European Court of Human Rights. I understand that when ordinary civilians in most European countries move from one town to another—for example, from Dusseldorf to Berlin—they are required to register their change of address. That is normal in Europe; it is not seen as a draconian arrangement. It is common for civilians in many European countries to comply with such arrangements. This country is the exception—we do not have to register when we move from one town to another. It is a mistake for the Government to view this as a draconian measure; it should be seen as straightforward and in the interests of the offenders. I was pleased to hear the Minister say that the Government were not considering a Megan's law under which all the names would be pinned up on the door of the town hall. We must ensure that the information is used properly, not abused in any way.

Mr. Kirkhope
: In reply, I would like to elaborate on what I said earlier. At the beginning of his remarks, the hon. Member for Bolton, North-East (Mr. Thurnham) used the term "common sense". I do not suggest that he personally is lacking in common sense, but the amendments are.

The reasons for that are straightforward. If the amendments were accepted, we would have an unworkable piece of legislation. When we were formulating our proposals, we carefully considered whether we could apply the provisions to anyone who had ever been convicted of a relevant offence. We recognised that it would be virtually impossible to identify—and, more importantly, to locate—all those with a conviction for a relevant sexual offence. I presume that the hon. Gentleman would want those cautioned to be included. In most cases, we have no idea where those people are now living. A general search for such people would place an intolerable burden on the police, and would also be publicly unacceptable. We have tried to make the provisions practicable and realisable.

One question that the hon. Gentleman has not answered is, if the amendments were accepted, for what period would people who were found, having been convicted many years ago, be required to register? There are a lot of people in society who have in years gone by—especially since the Sexual Offences Act 1956—been convicted of an offence that would now fall under schedule 1.

The amendments are not practicable. Under the Bill's provisions, we are extending registration—as the hon. Gentleman said—to those who are still in contact with the criminal justice system or who are still in psychiatric hospital. Obviously, we shall then be able to notify the people involved of the requirement. After all, it is their basic right to be notified and it would be impossible if we did not know where they were.
