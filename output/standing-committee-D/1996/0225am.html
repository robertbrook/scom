Ms Jowell: I and my colleagues welcome you, Mr. Anderson, to our proceedings this morning. Col. 82 [Interruption]. The Minister would like to be included in our good wishes.

The amendments stress the importance that we attach to ensuring that the proposals for piloting, which form the principal changes in the Bill, are subject to proper evaluation; that the pilots that are approved by the Secretary of State are relevant to deficiencies and problems in services; and that there is an opportunity to scrutinise the quality of pilots and the benefits that derive from them. We hope to set out the case for systematic and proper evaluation.

The success of pilots must be judged against clear benchmarks. For example, will they take account of service shortages, will they achieve improved quality of care and will that improved quality, as judged by those working in the pilot to deliver those services, be recognised by patients?

The importance of examining the problems that are endemic in delivering consistency in primary care were clearly set out in the Government's White Paper "Choice and Opportunity", which recognised that

"although generally good, the quality of service for some patient groups and in some areas is not always high. New ways need to be found to address these problems, some of which are long standing".

That creates the justification for piloting as the approach to be adopted. The White Paper went on to observe:

"the rewards and incentives of the single national contract do not always have a clear link to the services required to meet local need or circumstances…similarly, the contract does not always promote the team approach often needed in primary care…fundholding and the move towards a Primary Care led NHS has placed wider responsibilities on GPs as well as offering wider opportunities."

It is important to record that fundholding has never been subject to systematic evaluation. The only assessment of fundholding was conducted by the Audit Commission, which made what was very much a mixed report.

In making the case for wider contractual choice, "Choice and Opportunity" continues

"the traditional structure of general practice based on partnerships, personal investment and long term commitments does not always meet the different needs or aspirations of existing, or possible future, GPs. Career breaks, part time working and changes in career or location are becoming increasingly common features and, alongside problems of GP recruitment in some areas, are challenging traditional models."

We more or less accept that analysis of the framework and the fact that piloting is a way to deal with some of those intractable problems.

We hope that the Minister will respond to our suggestions in the spirit of consensus. The need for good, systematic evaluation and for the Bill to recognise the importance of evaluation are not party political issues that divide us. The Minister in another place made it clear that she recognised that evaluation was an extremely important step in validating the pilots and assessing their long-term prospects.

The development of innovation through pilot schemes is a bold way to promote diversity, flexibility and new approaches to some of the intractable problems that we have identified in the consistent Image Col. 83 development of high-quality primary care. Opposition Members do not need to remind the Minister of the unacceptable variations in doctoring and the achievement—or, in many parts of the country, the underachievement—of, for example, even the most basic targets for immunisation and cervical screening. The Minister will agree that there is a correlation between the poverty of patients on a GP's list and the quality of a general practice. I do not suggest that patients who live in deprived parts of inner cities are necessarily treated by poor GPs. However, GPs often operate in extremely difficult circumstances, which makes it hard to provide a comprehensive service and to reach the immunisation and screening targets. That matter was established in response to a succession of parliamentary questions that I tabled.

No new national innovation should worsen the situation. It is reasonable to assume that the purpose of piloting is to reduce some of those inequalities. It is essential to improve the national standard of the national health service. The pilot scheme should not create further fragmentation. Some parts of the NHS have been reduced to little more than a geographical lottery. A health service that is national should acknowledge the importance of striking a balance between a national framework of guaranteed standards of care and local flexibility and diversity to meet local needs. The trade-off for innovation cannot be the collapse of clear, national standards of entitlement.

A failure to establish clear criteria and to write the importance of evaluation into the Bill will diminish the value of pilots if they are assessed only vaguely against criteria that are established from the start. That was made clear in "Choice and Opportunity", which I have already mentioned. That White Paper also made it clear that the Secretary of State would not approve any pilot that did not make its benefits clear.

Amendment No. 55 seeks to ensure that the evaluation and review of pilots is carried out according to criteria that are nationally defined and responsive to local schemes. That would strike the proper balance and establish the failure or ineffectiveness of a new approach.

The Minister's listening exercises—"Choice and Opportunity" and Primary Care: Delivering The Future"—set out a number of good starting points, which could link with the analysis of the shortcomings of primary care set out in the document. The Minister set out five characteristics of primary care: quality, fairness, accessibility, responsiveness and efficiency. We on the Opposition Benches would add a sixth characteristic: equity, which is lacking in so many aspects of the national health service.

If pilots are to be at the leading edge of new ideas, with the intention that their good lessons and their experience then be rolled out everywhere, we have to know not only what works but the circumstances in which success can be assured. No one would support the national health service becoming a multitude of Col. 84 local experiments—800 or 1,000, whatever the limit on the number of pilots might be.

Research and development bodies outside the House recognise the difficulty of such development and of the replication of initiatives from test-bed to generalisation throughout the service. One must understand, for instance, the significance in a specific locality of the hero or heroine innovator and separate the impact of that contribution from other factors. Without systematic evaluation, that becomes impossible. The health service has been denied much of the virtue of the great innovations in service development in various parts of the country because there has not been systematic evaluation of those developments and the knowledge has not been disseminated.

If a national framework for evaluation does not exist, the pilot is the property only of its locality, and does not contribute to a broader understanding of how to strengthen primary care across the whole of the national health service. Given that we all support the drive for evidence-based medicine, surely evidence-based policy-making is an essential corresponding discipline.

It is the Committee's purpose not to draft criteria but to set a broad framework of national criteria. Local criteria would be developed on a case-by-case basis and submitted as part of the bid to the Secretary of State. Before any pilot is approved by the Secretary of State, the means by which it is to be evaluated should be clearly established.

To touch on two other issues raised by this group of pilots—I mean amendments; these are not piloted amendments but tabled amendments—there is a problem if there is no proper framework within which to evaluate innovation as it unrolls. Amendment No. 50 sets a minimum length for pilots, and amendment No. 110 is probably overtaken by Government amendment No. 149, which sets out the maximum length for pilots. We welcome the fact that we have been able to reach agreement that pilots should have a maximum length.

I hope that the case for evaluation is compelling and that we do not need to enagage in too long a debate about whether it is desirable. I hope that, in the spirit of consensus, the Minister can help us make progress on that important point because it is essential that those who will be responsible for implementing the legislation—if it receives Royal Assent—feel confident about its competence. We believe that failure to include evaluation of pilot initiatives will severely compromise that confidence.
