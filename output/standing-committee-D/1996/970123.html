Cosmetic Products (Safety) Regulations 1996

10.30 am

Mr. Nigel Griffiths
(Edinburgh, South)
: I beg to move,

"That the Committee has considered the Cosmetic Products (Safety) Regulations 1996 (S.I. 1996, No. 2925).

Welcome to the Committee, Mrs. Dunwoody. We are privileged to have you as Chairman.

I am sure that the Minister is familiar with the background to these important regulations, but other members of the Committee might not be. Until 1995, the European Parliament sought to have regulations and laws to protect consumers who wanted to buy products that were not tested on animals. Indeed, the European Parliament went beyond that and sought to ensure that cosmetic products and toiletries would not be tested on animals. Those measures were supposed to become effective in 1995, but they have still not been enacted.

The regulations represent an attempt to enact part of those measures, as Ministers decided to remove from the European Parliament's proposals the complete ban on animal testing. Therefore, the regulations represent the outcome of the Government's deliberations on ensuring that information on whether products have been tested on animals is made available.

The crucial purpose of the regulations is to ensure that British consumers who oppose the testing of cosmetics on animals can make an informed choice when they shop. The regulations will allow leading British companies to continue to make and sell products with labels that state how the products have been made and tested.

We are debating a serious attempt to frame a law that will protect the rights of millions of consumers and enable them to exercise their free will to choose products that have not been tested on animals. Yet British manufacturers and retailers have advised us that the way in which the Government have drafted the regulations creates a major loophole which could completely undermine their ability to give consumers the information that they want about the goods that they buy.

We support 95 per cent. of the regulations. I do not want to press the motion to a Division and perhaps have Labour's position misconstrued. My purpose this morning is to articulate the concerns of millions of consumers, animal welfare organisations and British manufacturers and retailers.

4 We welcome all practical attempts to strengthen laws that end animal cruelty. I hope that the Minister can convince the Committee that the regulations are such practical measures. There are two sides to the argument: on one is Robert Vanhove, secretaire general of the European Toiletry and Perfumery Association, and on the other there are world-beating companies, such as The Body Shop and Yardley, and leading retailers, such as the Co-op, Boots, Tesco and J. Sainsbury. We are entitled to ask why the Government appear to side with the European association and ignore the powerful case that British companies have advanced.

The British public undoubtedly feel strongly about inflicting suffering on animals simply to improve the performance of shampoos, lipsticks and other cosmetic products. The Co-op's survey of 1,000 people, undertaken by National Opinion Polls, showed the strength of public feeling. There is an overwhelming desire to ban animal testing for toiletries and cosmetics once and for all. Four out of five consumers favoured banning such tests; only 7 per cent. endorsed continued testing.

The Co-op survey contains two other important findings. The public know that cosmetic and toiletry ingredients are likely to have been tested on animals in the past, but they are stunned to discover that such tests continue. Most shoppers—three out of five—get information about animal testing from product labels and two out of three shoppers say that they would consider boycotting products that have been tested on animals.

The first thing that we want from the proposed legislation is a clear instruction to ensure that the label on cosmetic and toiletry products lets consumers know whether a product has been tested on animals. The information must be written on the label. The Government are not making that a requirement, however. Our job today is to ensure that consumers can make an informed choice. The major continental cosmetic manufacturers have conspired to frustrate that choice.

The European Parliament sought a comprehensive ban long ago, which was in line with British public opinion. The Council of Ministers has now set a deadline for 1998, but I believe that it has let European manufacturers off the hook by saying that they can continue to test on animals until alternative tests have been developed. It has been left to manufacturers to develop those alternative tests because no time scale has been set, so 1998 will come and go. The overwhelming opposition to animal testing will be ignored. As the Co-op's survey of public opinion found, when consumers were told about the loophole allowing tests to continue, nine out of 10 people wanted the animal testing ban to be enforced by the target date of 1998. That will not happen, but that is a debate for another time.

Animal testing labelling is the focus of concern today. In July 1996, the Government proposed draft legislation to put the directive into effect. The Department of Trade and Industry sought the views of industry, consumers, animal welfare experts, such as JPEG 5 the Royal Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals and the British Union for the Abolition of Vivisection. They all warned that the wording of that legislation would harm—perhaps fatally—the pioneering work done on behalf of British consumers by British companies. The wording on which the Minister is insisting is:

"If a relevant reference to animal testing is made, suppliers are under an obligation to disclose any tests which they have established have taken place, and not just tests which were carried out by them or at their behest."

A cogent case was put by retailers such as The Body Shop for enacting the directive, but a key adjustment was urged on the Minister to ensure that the directive would give consumers the information that they want and need to choose products that have not been tested on animals. I shall let the British Union for the Abolition of Vivisection spell out the problem, rather than the manufacturers and retailers, who are in complete agreement with BUAV. It says:

"It would be virtually impossible for these companies to say that none of their ingredients had been tested on animals—as almost all ingredients have been tested at some time in the past."

I mentioned that the draft regulations fail to require companies to divulge if the product has been tested on animals. However, they hamstring the ability of British and other companies to promote their products that have not been tested on animals. Some leading British companies have a clear policy. They do not and never have tested their ingredients or products on animals. They refuse to buy supplies from any other company if they are, or recently have been tested on animals. Because animal tests were the norm for decades, many of today's products contain items that were tested on animals at some stage—often long ago. The Body Shop leads the field in that policy.

Let us consider a practical example. Coal tar is an ingredient in soaps and shampoos. It was last tested on animals in 1921. Under the draft regulations, a label stating, "We do not test any of our products on animals or allow our suppliers to do so either" would be illegal, even if only an element of the product was tested all those decades ago. The proposed legislation does not put an onus on companies that continue to test on animals to put that on the label. There could be two products side by side on a shelf, one of which has been tested on animals and the other which has not. Under the draft regulations, a claim that the product is cruelty free—not tested on animals—would have to disclose that one of its ingredients had been tested 76 years ago, but the other product, which had been actively tested on rabbits, need not mention one word about that. Surely that cannot be right.

There is a list of other ingredients that have been tested on animals. Dimethicone is a silicone lubricant used in the cosmetics industry; as far as we know, it was last tested in 1953. Under the regulations, even modern cosmetics, which are light years in advance of those in the 1950
s
, would not be able to carry the claim "We do not test on animals" if they contain a trace of that substance. Other examples include ozokerite wax, which is widely used as a base wax in lipsticks; I am told that it was last tested in 1962. Beeswax is commonly used in creams, lotions and balms; it was 6 last tested in 1970. Sodium laureth sulphate is a common base for shampoos; it was last tested in 1971. Lanolin was last tested in 1974. Water is used in almost every process as a dilutant, but if it has been tested on the skins of animals it would have to be declared as such on the label if a company makes any claim whatever about animal testing.

No wonder, then, that foreign competitors of United Kingdom companies are rubbing their hands with glee. Many of them sponsored the sixth amendment to the cosmetics directive to make it virtually impossible for companies to promote their ethical stand on testing on their products. That seems to have been swallowed hook, line and sinker by the Government and is reflected in regulation 4(4).

What I find astonishing is that the only beneficiaries of the Government's dogmatic insistence on not listening to The Body Shop or other United Kingdom businesses are foreign cosmetic and perfume manufacturers. French perfume manufacturers and the German cosmetics business can now send products here without a mention of cruel animal tests on the products that are offered for sale, whereas British manufacturers such as The Body Shop and Yardley, and retailers such as the Co-op, Boots, Tesco and Sainsbury, will be hamstrung—they will be unable to claim that their products are not tested on animals as, back in 1921, a trace ingredient was tested.

What is worse is that, as The Body Shop has told the Minister, the directive could easily open the way to industrial sabotage. The Body Shop does not carry out tests on animals. It produces a face cream that contains bananas as one of its ingredients. If a French rival company takes that Body Shop product and commissions unnecessary animal testing, The Body Shop would have to remove any claim on the label that the product was not animal tested or else face the humiliation of putting a warning on the product that it had been tested on animals. That cannot be right, but it is the effect of the regulations as drafted. The French are not rushing to implement them; they do not intend to do anything until further discussions on the directive are completed at some unspecified time in the future.

Given that the Roddicks have given the moral lead on animal testing, that The Body Shop has become an international brand name, that its 3,000 workers and its millions of consumers can say with pride, "We make and use cruelty-free products", that its export contribution to the United Kingdom economy is enormous and that it allows Britain to speak with authority against animal testing, is it not an irony that the only people who do not listen to its pleas and warnings are the very Government-the British Government—who benefit most from its contribution to the economy? The Minsiter must convince the Committee of why he and his civil servants are right and The Body Shop, Boots and Tesco are wrong.

The Committee has been lobbied by the European Cosmetic, Toiletry and Perfumery Association, which represents continential companies that are frustrating the will of millions of British consumers who do not like their products because they are actively tested on JPEG 7 animals. Those companies look enviously at our great export record. British companies export about £1.2 billion worth of cosmetics and toiletries every year. We import less than £750 million worth of them. Within the European Community we are net exporters—in 1995, nearly £700 million worth was sold to Europe, whereas £550 million worth was imported, even taking into account the highly lucrative and protective French perfume market.

The representatives of the French industry are saying that no company can truthfully state that products or their ingredients have never been tested on animals. Under the regulations of label that states, "We do not test any of our products on animals or allow our suppliers to do so", would be illegal, even if some element of the product was tested all those decades ago. The regulations do not place on companies that continue testing on animals the onus to make that claim on labels. It is therefore important that the Minister listen to the concerns of the industry whose firms support cruelty-free products and answer the grave charges made against him by British companies and retailers.

I shall return to that in a while.

If such companies wish through their annual statements to make their position on animal testing clear, they may do so: if through their public documents and in their shops they wish to state that they are against it and want it done away with, they are at liberty to do that. They will strike a chord with many purchasers among the British public on account of their position. They will gain commercially from that reputation, and why not? Britain is the fifth largest manufacturer in the world and the third largest in Europe. There are markets to be penetrated, not least in Europe, which is the most important cosmetics market in the world. Friends have always been friendly towards me, notwithstanding all my frailties, so I hope that they will be kind enough to allow me to read into the JPEG 9 record of our proceedings some significant remarks. The subject we are discussing is important; it has a European dimension, a consumer safety dimension and an animal welfare dimension. In the face of those three elements, I stand in a position of relative humility and I want a proper argument put into the record.

The United Kingdom has a long history of introducing measures to safeguard the health of the consumer. The proposition that cosmetics should be safe will, I hope, provoke no disagreement. Cosmetics are applied to the skin and mucous membranes throughout a lifetime. They are applied several times per day, every day. The existence of regulations in this area are of paramount importance to protect human health and safety. Far from being innocuous, cosmetic products have in the past contained some ingredients that are poisonous and others that are linked with cancer and birth defects.

The regulations implement the EC cosmetics directive 76/768 and all subsequent amendments to that directive. They consolidate the previous United Kingdom regulations and also implement for the first time the sixth Council amendment agreed during the UK presidency in 1992 and adopted in 1993.

Mr. John Gunnell
(Morley and Leeds, South)

Mr. Taylor

I naturally wish all member states to abide by the same general ground rules, which will help British exporters.

I wish to develop the argument that the application of cosmetics by people to themselves is rather more important than simply wanting to be beautiful, to smell nice and to be a nice colour. It goes further than that; of the three important factors that I mentioned earlier, human safety is not to be diminished.

It is important that the words that I shall use now should be put on record, Mrs. Dunwoody, although I do not normally use such language at home. I am bound to say that a cosmetic product is defined in the regulations as a product applied to the epidermis, the hair, the nails, the lips, the external genital organs, the teeth or the mucous membranes of the oral cavity to clean, to perfume, to change the appearance, to protect, to keep in good condition, and to correct body odours. The definition includes anti-caries toothpaste, anti-dandruff shampoo, and sunscreens to protect against sunburn—all products that are important to human health. Wow! I am glad to have finished that.

The regulations proscribe ingredients that must not be used in cosmetics. For example, until it was prohibited by regulation, mercury was used in mascara and also as a skin whitener in soaps. At present, 767 substances are banned from being used in cosmetics, including the poisonous elements of arsenic, lead and cadmium.

10 The regulations also list ingredients that are permitted subject to appropriate restrictions and warning labels. In the case of the more hazardous classes of ingredients, the directive prescribes approved or positive lists of the only preservatives, colouring agents and ultraviolet filters that can be used in cosmetics. Limits are placed on the use of other ingredients that are considered safe only in certain proscribed amounts and concentrations.

I said that although the regulations are consolidating regulations, they introduce a number of new provisions contained in recent directives. One is the requirement for safety dossiers. Suppliers will now have to hold sufficient information in safety dossiers to demonstrate the safety of their products and will have to make that information available on request to the competent national authorities.

Full ingredient listing is another new substantive provision, which forms part of regulation 6. Before placing a cosmetic product on the market in the European Union, suppliers are now required to label the ingredients used in cosmetics. To facilitate full ingredient labelling, the industry has compiled a common labelling nomenclature, which covers more than 6,000 ingredients.

The main benefit of full ingredient labelling is that it will help consumers to make a more informed choice when purchasing cosmetics, especially if they know that they are allergic to a particular ingredient. I say that with personal feeling because almost every aftershave product on the market brings me out in a rash. I am therefore sympathetic to people wanting to know the ingredients of the cosmetics products that they seek to purchase.
