Mr. Peter Kilfoyle (Liverpool, Walton): During the previous sitting, I referred to the fact that the Department for Education and Employment estimates that there are 883,155 surplus places. Given that figure, a relatively modest redistribution of pupils in favour of one or two schools could significantly undermine the position of several neighbouring schools. As we know, schools can swing in or out of fashion for a variety of reasons not always linked to the quality of education that they offer. The status of schools can change.

Surplus places are generally removed only by the rather cumbersome process of school closure and amalgamation, which is normally highly unpopular with the parents of pupils in the schools involved. There is a legion of examples of where that has happened much to chagrin of local parents.

The slow, lingering decline of undersubscribed schools, which often become trapped in a vicious circle of declining pupil numbers and a consequent reduction in resources, must be a damaging experience for the pupils and staff, many of whom have little or no real opportunity to move elsewhere. It is not a process to be encouraged lightly.

The introduction of a mechanism to allow grant-maintained schools to expand without proper regard to the potential impact on either surplus places or neighbouring schools could have a negative effect in some areas.
