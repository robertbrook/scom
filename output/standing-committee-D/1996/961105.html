4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Health (Mr. Simon Burns): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Special Grant Report (No. 22) (House of Commons Paper No. 16)

May I say what a particular pleasure it is for me at this, my first Committee, to have you in the Chair, Mr. Jones? We go back a long time and even if I did not agree with the electorate's decision those many years ago, it enriched the House of Commons for you, rather than me, to be here at that stage.

The report specifies determinations in respect of a grant that the Government propose under section 88B of the Local Government Finance Act 1988. The persons from abroad children's grant has been determined by my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Health and for ease of reference during our proceedings, I shall refer to it as the children's grant. It is designed to assist English social service departments with their expenditure from 5 February 1996 to 31 March 1997 in providing support for children in need, when that duty arises in consequence of changes to the entitlement of asylum seekers to social security benefits, or to support from local authorities in connection with their previous duties to support homeless persons.

The background to the report is that the Government recognised that withdrawal of benefits from certain asylum seekers could impact on local authorities expenditure. The Government, therefore, proposed providing assistance to social service departments following consultations by the Department of Social Security with the Social Security Advisory Committee and the local authoritiy associations, leading up to the reform of benefit arrangements for asylum seekers and other persons from abroad.

As a result, my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Social Security deferred implementation of the changes from December 1995 to 5 February 1996. A further outcome of the consultation was that my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Social Security decided that existing claimants would be given limited transitional protection. Benefit would continue to be paid until an unsuccessful decison about an asylum application. At the time, my right hon. Friend gave a commitment to assist local authorities in England with any relevant additional costs that they might incur in carrying out their duties under the Children Act 1989 and homelessness Col. 4 legislation as a consequence of the changes made by the Social Security (Persons from Abroad) Miscellaneous Regulations 1996.

The Committee should be in no doubt as to the scale of the problem. Between 1984 and 1994, the United Kingdom saw a 10-fold increase in the number of people claiming asylum. Yet in the same period, the proportion of those succeeding in their applications fell from 32 per cent. to 4 per cent. It is clear that the availability of benefits and other help has attracted people who are not in fear of persecution but are seeking to improve their economic position. It is an abuse of the asylum procedure and the Government had to act.

The changes that we made to the benefit rules in February were to focus on those with the strongest case for support from the taxpayer. Those who make their position clear at the outset will qualify for benefit while waiting for the initial decision on their application for asylum. Those who enter illegally, however, are denied benefit, as are those who enter by misleading others about their status and claiming to be able to support themselves.

The Government remain committed to helping the genuine refugee and have introduced provision for backdated payments of benefits, enabling refugees to reimburse those who supported them while they were without benefit.

Under section 17 of the Children Act 1989, local authorities have a general duty to safeguard and promote the welfare of children who are in need within their area and, so far as is consistent with that duty, to promote the upbringing of such children by their families by providing a range and level of services appropriate to those needs. Section 17 duties are for children under 18 years.

The Government have proposed the special grant to target compensatory funds to those local authorities with significant numbers of asylum seekers with children. The purpose of the children's grant is to provide compensation to local authorities for the unforeseen additional expenditure that they incur for the period from 5 February 1996 to 31 March 1997, in carrying out their duties under sections 17 and 18 of the Children Act. This is to support children in need and their families through support for the whole family or, when a child of 16 or 17 is without a family and is not already being looked after by social services, who are affected by changes in social security benefits and housing legislation for asylum seekers. The grant is to help keep the family together.

My right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Health has earmarked up to £25 million towards the grant, which will be available to all authorities in England that incur expenditure above the threshold and, therefore, qualify for grant support. Under the arrangements set out in Special Grant Report (No. 22), the children grant will cover 80 per cent. of the relevant expenditure incurred by local authorities above a threshold equivalent to £1 on council tax band D properties.

Image Col. 5 The grant will also cover relevant expenditure incurred after 19 August 1996, when the order under the Asylum and Immigration Act 1996 came into force, bringing housing authorities' duties under the homelessness legislation closer in line with the rules on eligibility for benefits.

In drawing up the grant scheme for support for asylum-seeking families with children affected by the changes in legislation, the Government have been guided by three underlying principles. First, the grant should provide reasonable compensation for unavoidable, unforeseen additional expenditure to those authorities that are most affected by the costs of providing services for children under sections 17 and 18 affected by changes in legislation.

Secondly, the grant should contribute to costs in a way that is fair on the basis of objective data and audited costs. Thirdly, the grant scheme should be clear and simple and not impose administrative burdens on claiming authorities that are disproportionate to the amount of money involved.

The Government have responded to the concerns of local authorities in proposing this grant and I wholeheartedly commend the report to the Committee.

Ms Ann Coffey (Stockport): This report sets out the arrangements for compensation for local authorities incurring extra expenditure on services for children in need as a result of recent legislative changes that moved entitlement to benefits and housing assistance from categories of people from abroad. It is an acknowledgment of the unfairness of local authorities having to pick up the costs of such changes. That acknowledgment is a start, but I regard the Government's response to the situation as inadequate and incoherent.

At present, London boroughs are supporting some 700 families with children in various settings. That number is expected to rise rapidly as and when the outstanding asylum claims, including an estimated 13,000 families with children, are determined by the Home Office. No commitment has, as yet, been made to reimburse the expenditure on the adult asylum seekers affected by Justice Collins' recent decision that social services have a duty towards destitute asylum seekers under the National Assistance Act 1948.

Local authorities have no control over the volume of expenditure, as the demand for services will be determined by the number of destitute asylum seekers in the country and the rate at which outstanding cases are determined. Local government did not seek the additional obligations for which it is incurring costs. The savings resulting from the new benefit rules introduced by the Asylum and Immigration Act 1996 will give the Department of Social Security an estimated £200 million in savings and it does not seem right that part of the costs of the changes should be transferred to local government without compensation.

Col. 6 No costs will be recoverable under the threshold to be determined for each authority using the formula set out in the report. For London boroughs in particular, that will mean spending up to a maximum of some £130,000 out of their annual budgets without compensation. The Minister has wider responsibilities for community care and I remind him that that sum would fund carers of elderly people for 400 weeks of respite care. Above that threshold, only 80 per cent. of the cost will be reimbursed.

The authorities under pressure are also capped, which inevitably means that other services will be affected. It is difficult to see how Government exhortations about financial planning by local authorities can be reconciled with the fact that the Government expect local authorities to pick up the costs of unplanned expenditure that has arisen because of their own inability to put competent legislation on the statute book.

I have a number of questions to ask the Minister for clarification. The report says that grants will be paid to local authorities

"whose relevant expenditure … exceeds the threshold for that authority".

"Relevant expenditure" is then defined as

"reasonable expenditure properly incurred by a local authority in providing services for children in need, under section 17 or 18 of the Children Act 1989".

We may all think that we know what is meant by "reasonable expenditure", but I should like to know what the Minister thinks it means. Will he assure us that it includes housing costs for the family?

Local authorities are currently in the process of setting their budgets for 1997–98. When will the Minister decide whether the arrangements set out in the report for the reimbursement of costs to local authorities will continue for 1997–98? Can we expect that decision to be made before the deadline for the legal requirement to set the budgets is reached? When can we expect a Government decision on reimbursement of costs to adult asylum seekers incurred as a result of Judge Collins's judgment?

The Minister proposes to limit the grant up to the issue of a deportation order, but it can take the immigration authorties several months to enact deportation, during which social services will continue to be obliged under the Children Act to provide services to children and their families. Will he take that into account for future arrangements, or does he propose to amend social service responsibilities in that area? The Children Act requires social services to provide continuing support to young people, so why have the Government limited reimbursement of costs of services to children under 18? I should be grateful if the Minister would clarify those points.
