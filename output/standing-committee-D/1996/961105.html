[SIR JAMES HILL in the Chair]

Draft Open-Ended Investment Companies (Investment Companies with Variable Capital) Regulations 1996

10.30 am

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Mrs. Angela Knight)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Open-Ended Investment Companies (Investment Companies with Variable Capital) Regulations 1996.

The regulations are being introduced under the European Communities Act 1972 to provide for the establishment in Great Britain of a new type of body corporate falling within the scope of a European Union directive. Separate regulations will be introduced to enable the formation of open-ended investment companies in Northern Ireland.

The European Union directive in this case is the 1985 directive, co-ordinating the provisions relating to undertakings for collective investment in transferable securities. It is known as the UCITS directive. It is a single market directive for collective investment schemes in which investors' money is pooled and invested in a way that spreads risk. Although the directive provides that schemes may take the form of open-ended investment companies, the only United Kingdom based schemes able to take advantage of the marketing passport have been authorised unit trust schemes. That is because there is no provision in United Kingdom law for open-ended investment companies.

Much business has been lost to Luxembourg and to Dublin because of the difficulties of marketing unit trusts offshore. One of the main reasons for the regulations before the Committee is to enable British investment companies to catch up with their offshore competitors by enabling them to compete on a level playing field with the rest of Europe.

There has been a long and thorough period of consultation about these regulations for what are commonly known as "OEICs". That may not be a particularly elegant term, but it is certainly unforgettable. The Treasury first consulted with policy proposals in 1984 and followed that with further consultations about draft regulations in 1995. The investment fund industry has responded constructively with comments and I am grateful for their thorough and careful consideration of the issues involved. A number of adjustments to the original proposals have been made to reflect messages from those consultations.

The regulations for OEICs establish the main elements of a separate corporate code for the new type of investment company. The regulations are the first 4 part of a package of measures to allow the formation of OEICs in Great Britain. OEICs will not be formed under the Companies Acts, because that traditional route is not appropriate for what will be a regulated collective investment scheme. However, a number of familiar concepts of company law are contained in the OEICs code and they are blended together with other features from unit trusts. For example, OEICs will have annual general meetings, shareholders will have the right to approve the appointment of directors and, if necessary, will be able to sack any of them.

Like an authorised unit trust, the OEIC will be authorised and regulated by the Securities and Investments Board under powers granted to it by Treasury regulations. Those powers are similar to those in the Financial Services Act 1986, under which the SIB regulates unit trusts. SIB regulations for OEICs will be the second part of the package of measures and will supplement the European Communities Act 1972 regulations to complete the code for OEICs. The SIB has consulted extensively about its proposed regulations during 1995 and earlier this year in parallel with the Treasury's consultations. A near final draft of SIB regulations was published for further comment on 22 October.

The third part of the package will be tax regulations to be introduced shortly by the Inland Revenue. The tax regime for OEICs will be broadly similar to that for authorised unit trusts, and the tax regulations will also provide some relief from stamp duty and stamp duty reserve tax for authorised unit trusts that convert to OEICs. In addition, the Government have already announced that the next Finance Bill will introduce relief from stamp duty for other mergers of authorised unit trusts for a period of two years.

An OEIC's business is investment in securities, such as the shares of other companies. It will issue shares to its investors and its capital, like that of authorised unit trusts, may go up or down as shares are issued or cancelled.

The assets of an OEIC—the shares that it owns—will be held by a depositary, who must also be authorised under the Financial Services Act 1986. The depositary plays a key role that is similar to that of the unit trust trustee and he or she must be legally independent of the directors of the OEIC.

The independence requirement is an important feature of investor protection and reflects a clear message from virtually the whole investment fund industry during consultation. Each OEIC will have a board that may consist of a sole authorised corporate director or several directors. Promoters of an OEIC will have a choice about whether to appoint independent, non-executive directors. That important flexibility was introduced following consultation.

An OEIC with a sole corporate director can be effective, like a unit trust with its fund manager. At the same time, many OEICs and, indeed, many investors may see advantages in a board with a number of directors, some of whom might be non-executives. When a board has more than one director, SIB regulations will require one to be an authorised corporate director.

JPEG 5 The OEIC will be a modern, flexible and transparent investment product. Regulators will supervise all its activities. The OEIC and its key players must be authorised under the Financial Services Act 1986.

Investors in this country will find a new product that is simple to understand, and has key features that are disclosed more clearly than ever before. They will have an opportunity to widen their ownership of shares without the burden of managing a diverse portfolio. Its corporate form will make it attractive to overseas investors too.

The Government have been careful throughout to maintain a balance between flexibility and a high standard of protection for investors. The regulations for OEICs introduce a new and forward-looking stimulus to collective investment schemes in this country, and I believe that, from 6th January next year when the regulations, if they are approved by the Committee, are due to come into force, they will enhance the City of London's reputation as a centre for excellence in financial services. I commend them to the Committee.

10.36 am

Mr. Mike O'Brien
(North Warwickshire)
: The Opposition accept that OEICs are desirable vehicles for enabling the City of London to expand its influence in international financial markets. Therefore, we support the broad concept of OEICs. We retain doubts and anxieties, however, about their detail and especially their implications for consumer protection. There is no opportunity to amend the regulations and we therefore seek reassurances from the Minister. I shall put a series of questions to her and I hope that she can provide information that will enable those who wish to use OEICs in future to do so with confidence.

I concur with the Minister's view that OEICs are desirable. It is important to enable the City to respond to changes in the international markets. There is a growing OEICs market in Dublin, Luxembourg and the Channel Islands. The City must be able to respond to the demand for OEICs, but suitable protection for investors must exist. The Association of Unit Trusts and Investment Funds helpfully set out in its brief the extent of the growth in OEICs, especially in Luxembourg and Dublin, in recent years.

I shall quote from that brief, if only to emphasise the importance of the City and the financial services industry, which employs 2.8 million people and accounts for 18 per cent. of our gross domestic product. Every Government should ensure that the City has the ability to compete effectively in international financial markets. The statistics show how many United Kingdom-based investment companies have moved to Dublin and Luxembourg and that is a matter of concern.

Luxembourg and Dublin have a 22 per cent. share of the European funds market. That figure has increased from 12 per cent. in five years. The United Kingdom share of the market remained more or less static in that period at just over 10 per cent.—it dropped 15 per cent. in the late 1980s. Funds under management in Luxembourg and Dublin have quadrupled, while 6 those under management in the United Kingdom have nearly doubled. The combined assets of United Kingdom fund managers in Luxembourg and Dublin increased from $3.3 billion to just under $20 billion between 1992 and 1995. In 1995, 63 per cent. of Dublin business was undertaken by United Kingdom fund management houses. In 1994, 757 funds from other European countries were notified to the United Kingdom authorities as being marketed here, but only 114 United Kingdom funds were similarly notified to the authorities of other European Union member states.

The aim of OEICs is to allow the United Kingdom to benefit from the employment opportunities and associated benefits provided by having funds as well as asset management established and accounted in the United Kingdom. As the Minister pointed out, OEICs also provide opportunities for individual shareholders to spread risk in the ownership of shares without encountering the many difficulties that can be presented when individual shareholders have to manage a complete share portfolio.

OEICs have been welcomed by the industry. A survey in June of Britain's 100 largest unit trust management companies by Coopers and Lybrand showed that 47 wanted to set up OEICs in the near future and that one in five would convert existing unit trusts to OEICs. OEICs may well replace unit trusts in years to come. Indeed, the Treasury might be aiming to encourage that by reducing stamp duty in relation to such conversions. Can the Minister confirm that that would be the Treasury's intention and, if so, explain precisely why that process is being encouraged?

OEICs will also provide benefits for investors by trading at one price and replacing what is, to some of us, the somewhat obscure bid and offer pricing structure of unit trusts. Slightly more controversially, it could be argued that OEICs are pro-European—unit trusts are almost unique to Britain and the United Kingdom wants to open the financial services single market in Europe. OEICs are, therefore, a welcome pro-European policy from a Eurosceptic Government.

I want to put some of the Opposition's anxieties to the Minister; some of them are quite important. The incidents at Morgan Grenfell concerning Mr. Peter Young and various unit trusts might well have implications for how those trusts operate in Britain. The extent of the implications and the detail of what happened is as yet unclear, but it might become clear fairly soon as reports come out. Would not it have been better to try to ascertain the difficulties in the current set-up before proceeding with a new type of financial instrument, which might replicate some of the problems of the Morgan Grenfell-type unit trusts? Why would it have caused problems to delay for a few short months to enable completion of investigations into the Morgan Grenfell incident and to ensure that any lessons to be learnt were learnt before introducing the new financial mechanism?

JPEG 7 Why is there no obligation for non—executive directors to be on the board? In her opening comments, the Minister said that there were advantages in non—executive directors and so there are. Increasingly, non—executive directors in Britain have been expected to play a supervisory role, protecting the investor. Why is that not necessary in open—ended investment companies.

Does the depositary, who performs a similar role to that of the trustee in unit trusts, provide enough protection for investors? We must ask that question because the problems at Morgan Grenfell have raised doubts. Such doubts might prove to be unfounded. It might be found that it was not the regulations that were at fault, but the means by which they were complied with. That remains to be seen, but we need some reassurance from the Minister that there is sufficient protection—it seems that the trustees were insufficient in the Morgan Grenfell case and if Deutsche bank had not been behind Morgan Grenfell, investors might have lost out heavily. Trustees appear to have provided little practical protection. Is the Economic Secretary sure that the risks being taken in introducing this depositary mechanism are sufficiently covered by the proposals?

Last Thursday in another place Lord Mackay said that the depositary plays a key role similar to that of the unit trust trustee. Indeed he does. Can the Minister confirm that the depositary will have all the fiduciary obligations of a trustee? Will she confirm that at least all those fiduciary obligations are present in the new role, given that it is set up under an entirely different mechanism more similar to company legislation than traditional trust law? How far do we need to go beyond the traditional fiduciary role?

Lord Mackay said:

"It is unfortunately the case that no system of regulation can entirely rule out the possibility of fraud or mismanagement, but it is important to set high standards of protection for investors."—[ Official Report, House of Lords, 31st October 1996; Vol. 575, c.513.]

I agree with that and I hope that the Minister can reassure us that that is the case. May we also seek reassurance that, once the Morgan Grenfell inquiry is complete, any additional protections that are brought forward for unit trusts will also be brought forward, at the same time at the very least, for OEICs?

I was somewhat concerned when reading the debate in another place that Ministers were almost seeking to have their cake and eat it over the Morgan Grenfell case. Various reassurances seemed to be given that the preliminary conclusions of that case—it was stresed that they were preliminary conclusions—had few implications for the proposals for OEICs. They are indeed preliminary in terms of their outcome and I therefore find it difficult to see how the Minister can say with confidence that it is unlikely that Morgan Grenfell will have any implications. Perhaps we could clear up the issue of whether the preliminary results from the inquiries that have so far been undertaken have any implications for OEICs. Either they do or they do not. If they do not, will the Minister accept that 8 there is no guarantee that when those inquiries are completed there will not be some implications which have to be taken into account?

Lord Mackay set out the various roles of the depositary. He or she is responsible for ensuring that the OEIC complies with the Securities and Investment Board's regulations on pricing, investment, borrowing powers and income allocation. If there is an authorised corporate director the depositary has a duty of oversight over that ACD to ensure compliance with the regulations that relate to the protection of investors. Those onerous duties go beyond the role that trustees have so far felt they have had in relation to unit trust. Will the Minister spell out the process for authorisation of the depositary and his or her qualifications? How can the depositary ensure the ACD's compliance?

"must be an authorised person".

We want to know precisely who can become an authorised person. That is a bit more than the qualifications. Who would the Minister consider to be a suitable authorised person? Under regulation 5(2)(b), the depository can entrust

"to a third party all or some of the assets in its safekeeping"

or can authorise

"the third party to entrust all or some of those assets to other specified persons."

What does that really mean? It means, it seems, that the depositary can pass assets on, perhaps to the bank. Or, perhaps it means that the depositary can pass them to a third individual, who may or may not be authorised. What are the authorisation procedures or qualifications for that third, fourth or even fifth party, to whom the assets may be passed? What form of protection is there for the consumer in this context?

If the assets were held by a depositary or a third party authorised by the depositary, what would happen if those assets were frozen in the event of insolvency, such as happened in the Barings case? Only last week Sir Andrew Large set out a programme of work that needed to be done to ensure that senior managers of investment firms understood the compliance requirements. His view was that there were still problems. SIB clearly believes that there is work to be done. Does the Minister agree and, if so, what work is needed? Will she assure us that that will have no bearing on the protection for investors in OEICs? If it does have some bearing on that, will she report to Parliament on the implications? I want the Minister's assurance that she will, at the first opportunity, place before Parliament a full report of any implications, in relation to the Peter Young affair, for OEICs and unit trusts. The corporate government aspects of both—but particularly of a new instrument such as OEICs—must be right. People need to have confidence. If they think that the provision has not been passed by the House in a proper fashion, confidence in OEICs will be lacking from the start, and that would be undesirable.

The Morgan Grenfell matter arose from difficulties in relation to unlisted securities. What measures will guarantee that the same problem will not happen in relation to OEICs? The Association of Unit Trusts and Investment Funds has called for voluntary restraint on JPEG 9 the holding of prelisted securities. Does the Minister agree that that is desirable and, if so, why do the proposals contain nothing, or insufficient provision, in that regard? What means will exist to enforce limits on the holding of unlisted securities, and what means will be used to value unlisted securities held through OEICs? We know that the Morgan Grenfell unit trusts were supposed to be low risk, but they became high risk, in a sense, through unlisted holdings. Why are there no rules to give investors further protection in relation to that?

We do not know all the implications of the various tax proposals that may come from the Government. Will the Minister be as candid as she is able in the circumstances? Parliament should have as much information as possible about the costs involved in this matter and, in particular, the tax implications. I hope that the Minister can confirm her answer to a written question 9 May, that she intended to include OEICs in

"an exemption for conversions of an authorised unit trust into an OEIC or a single sub-fund of an umbrella OEIC."

She continued:

"The regulations will also contain an exemption from stamp duty and SDRT, for a period of some two years, for conversions which involve a merger, such as the conversion of two or more authorised unit trusts into a single OEIC or sub-fund, or the merger of an authorised unit trust with an OEIC.


We also intend to introduce legislation in the next Finance Bill to exempt mergers between authorised unit trusts from stamp duty and SDRT if the merger is carried out after the Bill becomes law and within a period of some two years thereafter."—[ Official Report, 9 May 1996; Vol. 277, c. 204.]

Will the Minister confirm that that is still the intention and, if so, will she set out clearly the thinking behind it, and the reasons for the Treasury's view? I am interested in the policy implications and the Treasury's long-term objectives in adopting that tax policy.

I move on to bearer shares. At present, if a person has a share in a company, that share will be registered. But there will be no need to register all shares in relation to OEICs, as Lord Eatwell said in another place last week. He said:

"I am aware that bearer shares are common in many European countries and are not unknown in this country, particularly in some pre-war legislation. But their use was discontinued in this country, not least because they were a potent means of tax evasion. Introducing them into the heart of the financial services industry is contrary to the entire thrust of British companies and financial legislation for the past 50 years. It introduces a clear and present danger to the integrity both of financial institutions involved and of the fiscal system."—[ Official Report, House of Lords, 31 October 1996; Vol. 575, c. 515.]

Will the Minister comment on Lord Eatwell's observations, which express important concerns? I think that they deserve a rather more detailed answer than the noble Lord received on the day on which he made them.

What are the implications if nobody is registered as the owner of shares? Who might the owner be? Might the owner of bearer shares be a Nick Leeson, or a criminal, or some undesirable person with whom a potential investor might not wish to be involved in an OEIC, or in any enterprise? What investigations has the Treasury undertaken into the potential use of bearer shares for money laundering by criminals?

10 Those are serious questions, which the Minister must deal with. I accept that because considerable numbers of OEICs are available in Luxembourg, Dublin and elsewhere on the continent there is arguably a money-laundering facility available to those who wish to use it. But surely we must be concerned not to create more opportunities for that behaviour? What safeguards and reassurances can the Minister give to ensure that that does not happen and that such matters have been looked into in the required detail?

Will the Minister ensure that, in moving forward, the Treasury takes a view on whether it is desirable that OEICs should become involved in using bearer shares? Whether or not to use bearer shares is a matter for the OEIC. It may decide not to use them. Is the Treasury view that the use of bearer shares will increase in this country as a result of the regulations? If so, by how much? Does the Treasury think that the preponderance of shares for OEICs will be registered shares, not bearer shares? If so, to what extent, in the Treasury's view, should we be conscious of the implications of bearer shares for the future of the UK financial services sytem?

I have made a series of points on which I hope the Minister can provide some reassurance. In principle, we think that there is enormous potential for OEICs and that they are desirable. However, the assurances that we have asked for today would mean that those who seek to invest in OEICs in future may well be able to do so with greater confidence.
