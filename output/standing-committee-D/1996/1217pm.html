Education Bill

New Clause 1
CORPORAL PUNISHMENT LAWFUL WITH PARENTAL CONSENT

'For section 548 (no right to give corporal punishment), section 549 (interpretation of section 548) and section 550 (no avoidance of section 548 by refusing admission to school) of the Education Act 1996 the following sections shall be substituted—

" Corporal punishment lawful with parental consent.

548.—(1) Where a pupil at a school to which this section applies who has not attained the age of 18 is required on disciplinary grounds to be subjected to corporal punishment at his school, his punishment shall not be unlawful if his parent's consent to it has been given in accordance with subsection (2) and if the conditions set out in subsection (4) are satisfied.

  1. The admission arrangements for a school to which this section applies may include provisions—
    1. making it a condition of the admission of every child to the school that his parent gives the admission authority a signed parental declaration either—
      1. at the time of applying for a place at the school for the child, or
      2. if the child is allocated a conditional place, within such period as is specified in the arrangements;

        that he consents to the use of corporal punishment as one of the measures that may be taken with a view to regulating the conduct of pupils including his child; and
    2. authorising the admission authority to dispense with that condition to any extent in the case of a particular child where they are satisfied that there are special reasons for doing so.
  2. This section applies to—
    1. a school maintained by a local education authority;
    2. a grant-maintained or grant-maintained special school; or
    3. a city technology college or city college for the technology of the arts.
  3. The conditions referred to in subsection (1) are—
    1. that the head teacher of the school must have previously determined, and made generally known within the school, that corporal punishment is one of the measures that may be taken with a view to regulating the conduct of pupils;
    2. that the punishment must be imposed by the head teacher or by another teacher at the school specifically or generally authorised by him for the purpose; and
    3. that the punishment must be reasonable in all the circumstances.
  4. In determining for the purposes of subsection (4)(c) whether a punishment is reasonable, the following matters in particular shall be taken into account—
    1. whether the punishment constitutes a proportionate punishment in the circumstances of the case; and
    2. any special circumstances relevant to its imposition on the pupil which are known to the person imposing it (or of which he ought reasonably to be aware) including in particular—
      1. the pupil's age,
      2. any special educational needs he may have,
      3. the pupil's state of health, and
        4. 498
      4. the pupils's disciplinary record.
  5. Section 572, which provides for the methods by which notices may be served under this Act, does not preclude a notice from being given to a pupil's parent under this section by any other effective method.
  6. In this section—
  7. "admission arrangements", in relation to a school means the arrangements for the admission of pupils to the school;
  8. "conditional place", in relation to a child, means a place which is conditional on the child's parent giving the admission authority a signed parental declaration; and
  9. "parental declaration" means a declaration to be signed by a parent seeking the admission of his child to the school.

Inhuman and degrading punishment.

549. Where, in any proceedings, it is shown that corporal punishment has been given to any pupil in accordance with section 548 above, the punishment cannot be held to be inhuman or degrading.".'.—[ Mr. Pawsey.]

Reference is often made to matters that are cruel, degrading and inhuman and we are all assumed to know what that phrase means, but I am not sure that it takes us further forward.

I turn now to what I believe are much more substantial difficulties with my hon. Friend's new clause.

The first is connected with the European convention on human rights. If the new clause were to be implemented, it would be likely to lead to successful claims that the United Kingdom's obligations under the European convention on human rights had been violated. I think specifically of article 3 of the main treaty, on inhuman and degrading treatment, and article 2 of protocol 1, on respect for the philosophical objections of parents to corporal punishment. I should like to explain why.

Previous cases considered by the European Court indicate that article 2 of protocol 1 would be found to have been violated if a child had been denied admission to a school because of the parents' objections to corporal punishment. That would be the case even if a place was made available for the child at another suitable school nearby where such punishment was not used.

Furthermore, although the parents might agree to sign the requisite declaration to obtain a place for the child at a school, they would be free to change their minds subsequently and write to the school withdrawing consent for corporal punishment to be administered. It is clear that article 2 would be violated if the school was to administer punishment despite the parents' revised wishes, or if the school excluded the child because of the parents' principled objection to that form of punishment. In any case, such an exclusion could be lawfully effected only as a result of an appropriate disciplinary offence; the pupil could not be excluded simply because of the parents' views.

504 Proposed new section 549 provides that corporal punishment administered in accordance with proposed new section 548 could not be held to be "inhuman or degrading". However, although the punishment would have to be

"reasonable in all the circumstances",

in some circumstances, a fairly harsh beating in response to a pupil's serious misconduct might be "reasonable" under the amendment even though the European court had ruled it to be inhuman and degrading and hence a violation of article 3.

The new clause abolishes the requirement that corporal punishment administered to privately funded pupils at independent schools must not be inhuman or degrading. That would open the way for cases to be brought that such punishment had violated article 3. By repealing the existing provisions of the 1996 Act, publicly funded pupils at independent schools other than at city technology colleges or the city college for the technology of the arts would no longer be safeguarded. Corporal punishment without parental consent of pupils on assisted places would probably be regarded as having breached article 2 of protocol 1.

I have been through that in some detail because, in spite of the ingenuity of my hon.
