Ms Tessa Jowell (Dulwich): I begin by welcoming you, Sir Alan, to the Chair. As we made clear on Second Reading, the Opposition welcome many aspects of the Bill. However, a number of aspects are still insufficiently clear and we are opposed to some aspects of the original proposals.

During our proceedings, we shall take the opportunity to recast primary care and, in a measured way, to debate the way in which it will be recast. We welcome the Government's emphasis on phased implementation of pilots because it is essential that continuity of patient care is not jeopardised in the process of change.

As we have already made clear, a lot of pain would have been spared in the national health service if the approach taken in the Bill had been applied to implementation of the reforms in 1990. So much energy was expended in that reorganisation that the current demoralisation of the service is felt by many to have had its origins in that process.

In considering how much time is needed to debate the Bill, we need to ensure that we return to and fully explore the many points that my hon. Friends raised on Second Reading. That debate covered the essence and context of primary care. I hope that we will have sufficient time to revisit such issues as the recruitment of trainees to general practice. We know that, in many inner-city practices, there is what is approaching a crisis of recruitment and retention. We also want to reexamine the availability of women GPs. It is not acceptable that some patients who want to register with a woman GP cannot do so.

Perhaps more than anything else, we want to ensure that the Bill deals with how to confront and get rid of the two-tier NHS. The two-tier NHS has become a hallmark of primary care under the Conservative Government. The chief executive of the hospital that my constituents use—King's College hospital, which is only two or three miles down the road from here said last summer that he could no longer justify the two-tier NHS to the patients who came to his hospital.

During our debate on the Bill we must get to grips with why a two-tier NHS has emerged. There are a number of reasons, the most blatant of which is the extent to which GP fundholders can procure faster access for their patients to elective treatment than Col. 4 patients of GPs who are not fundholders. Across the river at the Lambeth, Southwark and Lewisham health authority, maximum waiting time for paitents of fundholders is 12 months, but patients of GPs who are not fundholders have to wait up to 18 months for elective treatment in hospital. That inequity, which overrides specific regional needs, results in a two-tier health service and is of enormous concern to patients and those who provide health care.

During our debate we are also keen to explore the regional inequity in the distribution of GPs which, again, creates access problems as well as difficulties for GPs in providing proper cover for what might be unacceptably large lists.

We also hope to discuss the crisis facing NHS dentistry—a crisis which the Government have so far refused to acknowledge, but which is clearly having an enormous impact in different parts of Britain in terms of the ease with which people can register with an NHS dentist. In towns throughout Britain it is virtually impossible to find an NHS dentist. That is another example of regional inequalities in the availability of primary care.

When the time comes, we want to ensure that, despite all the Government's instincts, the possibility of back-door or front-door privatisation of primary care does not arise.

The Opposition have exceptionally strong representatives on the Committee to ensure that the Bill receives thorough scrutiny. They include my hon. On Second Reading, we endeavoured to refer the Bill to a Special Standing Committee by means of a committal motion. I hope that some of the achievements that would have been accomplished by three sessions in a Special Standing Committee can be dealt with by the wide debates that we will need to have during the next week or so.

We are not satisfied with the Government's handling of the tabling of amendments. That was a specific problem in another place just before Report stage, when some 96 amendments were tabled, half of which were withdrawn, and not all reappeared on Third Reading. We are also concerned about the Government's exact intention in relation to other bodies and privatisation. We should have liked to debate the Bill in a Speical Standing Committee and to Image Col. 5 have had the opportunity to take evidence in such a forum because the Bill's provisions will work only if they attract the support and confidence of those who work in primary care throughout Britain. I refer not only to general practitioners, but to nurses, midwives, pharmacists and optometrists, whose joint effort enables primary care to be as strong and robust as it needs to be.

We support the Bill in many respects. We hope that, subject to the amendments that we want to secure, it will be possible for it to receive Royal Assent before the general election. However, our co-operation is entirely dependent on the spirit of compromise that the Government adopt. We believe that the Bill has significant flaws that will not serve to rebuild primary care. Over the next two weeks we hope to strengthen the Bill by removing those flaws.

Mr. Hugh Bayley (York): I hope that the Bill makes good progress, but not such good progress that we do not discuss some important questions about primary care, which I raised on Second Reading. The first matter that I raised was equity—the distribution of primary healthcare resources around the country and the distribution of GPs. Committee members will be aware that there is much inequality in the distribution of those resources. The Bill has the potential to reduce that inequality and to increase the number of GPs in areas that have less than their fair share of resources. Such a reform would make it possible for better-resourced areas to increase their distribution still further. I hope that the Government will reassure me that the Bill's purpose is to improve rather than to reduce equity in the distribution of resources.

I want to flag up another matter that I raised on Second Reading and to discuss it in slightly more detail. If we are to enter into a different system relating to contracts for primary healthcare services, the Government must be clear about how they will ensure that high-quality and effective services are provided and that those services are cost-effective.

I shall give one or two examples of issues that we must debate at some stage during our consideration of the Bill. When the new GP contract was introduced some time ago, an annual domiciliary visit to all patients aged over 75 years was required. Many GPs have complained to me that that is not a cost-effective practice. They understand the healthcare needs of patients whom they see regularly, in surgeries or in patients' homes. The need for an annual domiciliary visit in such cases is not needed on health grounds because GPs are already aware of patients' healthcare needs. However, there is a strong reason for an annual Col. 6 domiciliary visit to those patients who are not regularly seen by GPs. A GP's resources would be used more effectively if they were concentrated on patients whose records were not up to date and about whose healthcare needs the GP was unclear.

I hope that if healthcare services are contracted out to third parties—whether to a trust or a private organisation—the terms of contracts will require the providers of primary healthcare services to provide cost-effective services.

We should also discuss the advice that the NHS Centre for Reviews and Dissemination, which is funded by the Department of Health, gave about cholesterol screening. It advised that blanket screening was highly cost-ineffective because it consumed NHS resources and produced very little health gain, whereas screening of people at risk of heart disease was highly cost-effective. We would want to know that the NHS money going to the primary healthcare teams that had contracted to provide services was spent in accordance with the evidence-based medicine guidelines produced by the Department of Health. That is not clearly stated in the Bill.

Minor surgery is another issue that we must discuss. When the health reforms were introduced, the Government encouraged GPs to provide lumps and bumps operations within general practice in the belief that that would be more cost-effective than similar operations in hospital. Yet the growth in minor surgery in general practice seems to have been in addition to the amount of treatment given in hospitals, so those planned savings have not come to fruition. In letting contracts to third parties to provide primary healthcare services, it is important to ensure—my support for the Bill will depend on the Minister's response to this—that the services bought will make ill people better, and that we are not giving subcontractors a licence to spend NHS money, whether or not they provide a cost-effective service.

Ms Jowell: The purpose of the amendments is to ensure that general nurses, health visitors, midwives and community nurses can propose pilot schemes in their own right, instead of doing it through a GP or an NHS trust. On Second Reading, the Secretary of State appeared to suggest that the Bill already allowed for that. We might debate a Government new clause on the issue later in our proceedings, but there is a clear opinion among those who represent the interests of nursing in its broadest possible sense that the Bill does not make it possible for nurses to initiate and be in charge of pilots.

The barriers in the Bill to nurses acting independently of GPs and trusts to initiate pilot schemes are numerous. First, in clause 1(3), nursing services are identified as services which are not Col. 12 personal medical or personal dental services, but which may be provided under part I of the National Health Service Act 1977 or the National Health Service (Scotland) Act 1978. However, subsection (3) provides that pilot schemes may include arrangements for the provision of such services. Nursing services could be part of the wider pilot, but nurses acting alone cannot propose a pilot scheme.

The second barrier appears in clause 2, which sets out the duties of health authorities to consider pilot schemes that are submitted to them by qualified persons, and to prepare proposals for submissions to the Secretary of State.

Clause 2(7) defines "qualified person" as

"an NHS trust or a person who is"

a medical practitioner or a dental practitioner. Amendment No. 41 adds nurses to the list of qualified persons. Currently, nurses wishing to take up pilot schemes would have to do so via a doctor or an NHS trust, thus restricting both the potential of the Bill and, the Opposition believe, many of the opportunities for diversity in primary care that the Government intend.

Failure to amend the Bill in the light of those concerns would work against the grain of the provision of much that is best in nurse-led primary care at present. Not far from here we can find nurses leading a comprehensive healthcare project for homeless people. That approach could be generalised to the benefit of homeless people in different parts of the country.

For example, a team of health visitors, community mental-health nurses, school nurses and nurse practitioners could contract with the health authority to deliver a wide range of healthcare services for homeless families and individuals who, for whatever reason, are unable to register with a GP. The team could contract with or employ a medical practitioner as appropriate, and the range of services provided by nurses working in that way could include health promotion, family planning, social support and care for acute illnesses, as well as support with chronic conditions such as asthma.

On Second Reading in another place, the Under-Secretary, Baroness Cumberlege, paid fulsome tribute to the contribution of nurses. I hope that you, Sir Alan, will forgive me as I squint at my script because the font that I chose on my word processor turned out to be almost unreadable. The Under-Secretary acknowledged that

"The nursing profession already plays a major and growing part in the delivery of primary care."

She added:

"We want to encourage that trend and also greater integration of the different primary healthcare professions. Our proposals will enable nurses to develop proposals with GPs which include a range of services in which they take a lead, such as child health clinics or monitoring of asthma."—[Official Report, House of Lords, 3 December 1996; Vol. 576, c. 591]

Both White Papers that preceded the legislation—"Choice and Opportunity" and "Primary Care: Delivering the Future"—identify major new opportunities for the development of nursing for the benefit of patients. It is, therefore, inconsistent—we hope it is no more than an oversight—that clause 2(7) as currently drafted excludes midwives and nurses as Image Col. 13 qualified persons. In clause 1, there is no explicit mention of nursing in the opening definition of pilots. It is not enough simply to imply that nurses are eligible. The eligibility of nurses across the range of the discipline needs to be explicitly reflected in the Bill. That is the purpose of the amendments.

We know that the Minister for Health has been labouring under the impression that the shortage of nurses in the late 1990s is to do with their Hattie Jacques image. Perhaps he regrets that remark, which was no doubt made in the wake of the Christmas turkey.
