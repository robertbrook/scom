    Mr. Morgan
    : The amendment deals with the right to choose. That is a difficult issue because the British national health service has had a two-way tradition of non-intervention: when a medical practitioner wants to remove a patient from his list, he can do so without giving a reason. The same applies when a person wants to leave one doctor and go to another: there is no compulsion to give a reason or to make arrangements for a replacement to maintain the doctor's list. Patients have no responsibility for maintaining the list size and no reasons have to be given. It is a matter of free choice, which preserves the best doctor-patient relationships, unsullied by considerations of commercial practice, viability for the doctor or problems that the patient might have in finding another doctor.

    Certain problems have always attached to the tradition. In rural areas, for instance, there is no effective choice. More recently, doctors have been under greater pressure to be tighter about how far they are willing to travel and to take consideration of the fact that some patients are much more expensive to treat than others. That is the reason for the amendments, which deal with doctors' consent and patient choice. Can a doctor refuse a patient without giving a reason? Should there be a reason? In general, opinions have changed which is why the amendments are worth serious Government consideration.

    Over the past 50 years, it has generally been accepted that a doctor should not have to give a reason for not wanting a patient on his list. If a relationship breaks down, it has been considered to be better not to go into it or to give reasons, as that could colour the willingness of another doctor to take the patient on. By and large, doctors have not refused patients in rural areas because they know that there is no choice and that the next nearest doctor might be 20 miles away. Common sense has prevailed. Why has that JPEG 163 changed and why is the amendment now worthy of serious consideration?

    The reason is that doctors now are, perhaps, far more commercially driven. They might be fundholders with cash-limited instead of non-cash-limited drugs budgets, which means that they might have reasons not to take patients who have a high drug dependency—for example, people on Interferon being treated for cancer or those having expensive anti-AIDS treatments. If patients think that a doctor wants to get rid of them for a financial reason, should the doctor have to state that that is so—that the person is very expensive and is making an excessive call on the doctor's limited budget for drugs and hospital treatment? That causes problems because there may be restrictions on the doctor's ability to ask a patient to leave his list.

    The same applies with dentistry, which does not have the same universal coverage as medical practices. There is no obligation on dentists to accept someone on to their list. All Committee members recognise that dentistry must approximate in one way or another to the same universal coverage as medicine. Despite the Government's boast that NHS dentistry has expanded, there is still a problem. I suspect that even they recognise that the expansion of NHS dentistry is very patchy and that it is difficult for fee-paying adults to obtain NHS dentistry in vast swathes of the country. It is in the interests of NHS consumers to clarify the relationship, which the amendments would do. They would make it a matter of public policy for general dental practitioners and general medical practitioners to think more seriously about their obligations in the new climate of the NHS, of which they are a part. They are cogs in the wheel of the provision of universal coverage of medical or dental primary health care.

    If the Bill is eventually enacted, we shall move into an exciting new era that involves pilot schemes providing dental and medical services, but more safeguards are needed. The amendments would provide them for people needing NHS dentistry, which has been extensively withdrawn, or NHS medical care. Could a pilot go ahead in an area that has a select population of patients who are easy and cheap to treat? Other people might say, "That scheme is not in my interest. How come I am being left out of this exciting pilot?" That person might happen to have an expensive condition to treat, which would put burdens on his doctor through repeat house calls or night calls or expensive drug provision—if the GP is a fund holder—which might include Interferon or the various treatments for AIDS. I hope that the amendments will establish how the Government intend to ensure that patients as consumers will be given the rights that people probably always thought that they had.

    All Committee members know, through our constituency surgery experience, that there is much more tension between patients and doctors or dentists than there used to be. When I came into the House 10 years ago, it was rare for patients who had been asked 164 to leave a doctor's list to accuse the doctor of removing them for financial reasons, but that is not uncommon today. People commonly say to me, "My child has been ill frequently this winter, and I have had about about 10 house calls. Now the practice wants me to leave the doctor's list." We know that the only reason is the money that is involved.

    Such matters are poisoning the relationship between patients and doctors. The safeguards that the amendments contain would neutralise the poison and strengthen the belief among patients as consumers that they have rights and that doctors will not exploit in a one-sided manner the traditional right to ask someone to leave their list without giving a reason.

    We should strengthen access to NHS dentistry. For some people, a dentist's surgery may be 40 miles away. For example, in Pembrokeshire in west Wales, four people had to hire a minibus to travel 40 miles to the nearest NHS dentist in Fishguard. They all got seen, got back in the minibus and went home again. That was never what was envisaged for universal primary health care when the NHS started. We and the Government are talking about an NHS that is primary-healthcare led, yet people are making minibus journeys of 40 miles across a Welsh county to find that health care. That makes people feel that we have a primary-healthcare-withdrawing NHS, not one that is primary-healthcare led. That is already happening with dentistry.

    Consumers and organisations that represent them feel that their rights need to be strengthened. The Government need seriously to consider how to undo some of the side-effects of the changes since 1990 with the new GP contract: the breakdown of negotiations between dentists and the Government in the mid-1990s, and GP fundholding.

    The Bill's provisions could also increase patients' fears that they are not getting a fair crack of the whip and are being taken off lists without explanation—although they suspect that money is behind it all. In reality when someone ceases to be on a doctor's list, we are talking about the breakdown of the doctor-patient relationship. If people can be assured that money is not causing doctors to remove them from the lists, they would be reassured about doctor-patient and dentist-patient relationships. However, dangers have occurred largely since but not entirely because of the 1990 contract and the breakdown of the dentist contract negotiations in the early 1990s. There are far greater tensions in dentists' and doctors' surgeries when someone is removed from the list now than there were when I became a Member of Parliament in 1987.

    If the Government do not accept the amendments, will the Minister explain how the Government intend to remove that increasing tension in the relationship over the issue of which list a patient is on?
