Clause 23
VARIATION OF LIMIT ON FIXED-PERIOD EXCLUSIONS: ALL MAINTAINED SCHOOLS

Mr. Peter Kilfoyle
(Liverpool, Walton)
: I beg to move amendment No. 198, in page 21, line 3, after 'school', insert—

'(a) after subsection (1) there shall be inserted—


"(1A) Where the headteacher of a school exercises his power to exclude a pupil for a period of more than three days, he shall secure that the educational needs of that pupil continue to be met"; and (b)'.

The amendment is designed to clarify and strengthen the Bill's provisions on excluding pupils from school. We believe that there should be no doubt about a school's responsibility for the continuing education of pupils who have been temporarily excluded. The amendment does precisely that.

A clear distinction exists between a fixed-term exclusion and a permanent exclusion. The distinction is better expressed, in old terminology, by the words suspension and expulsion. When pupils are expelled they are removed from the school's roll. The connection between school, pupil and parent is severed and the school no longer has a duty to educate the pupil. That duty remains with the parent and it reverts to the local education authority to secure suitable provision. However, when pupils are excluded for a fixed period—suspended—the school still retains its responsibility to that pupil.

The sanction of fixed-term exclusion is part of a school's tool kit for upholding its disciplinary policies. Members on both sides of the Committee agree on that, but the weight of professional opinion is that a fixed-term exclusion should not be seen as a punishment in itself. From society's point of view—and school is a microcosm of society—withdrawing the opportunity to be educated from troublesome individuals is a self-defeating policy. The lack of a successful education is closely associated with socially damaging behaviour later in life. Rather, temporary exclusions are a device to facilitate other methods of dealing with disciplinary problems.

It may be necessary in the wake of serious incidents to separate the protagonists to provide a cooling-off period and allow investigations into the facts of the 458 case. Such a situation also emphasises the need for parents to become involved; we believe that suspensions, or temporary exclusions, should remain in force until a forward-thinking strategy has been agreed with them.

During the first day or two of a fixed exclusion it may not be practical to require the continuation of the excluded pupil's school work. The exclusion may have been imposed rapidly while important matters, such as physical violence, are dealt with urgently. However, if the exclusion is allowed to persist longer than the time required for urgent action or for a brief breathing space, it is important that the school should take positive steps to ensure that the excluded pupil's education continues while he or she is out of school.

The advice and guidance set out in the Department for Education and Employment circular 10/94 makes it clear that that is Government policy. Paragraph 51 states:

"The head teacher of the excluding school should wherever practicable make arrangements for the pupil who is excluded for a fixed period to receive school work to do at home and to have it marked until he or she returns to school. The governing body should keep these arrangements under review."

That is not made explicit in the Bill, however, and it is possible to misread section 19 of the Education Act 1996—which provides that LEAs should have responsibility for excluded pupils, but does not draw a distinction between temporary and permanent exclusions—so as to believe that the guidance does not apply.

The amendment would put the matter beyond doubt by setting out the duty of the head teacher to prevent a temporary exclusion from being a complete interruption to a child's education—we want to ensure that that appears clearly in the Bill. The amendment would strengthen the Bill by underlining and reaffirming an existing element of Government policy that we fully support. I hope that the Minister recognises the worth of the amendment, which would enhance, rather than inhibit, Government policy.

CORPORAL PUNISHMENT LAWFUL WITH PARENTAL CONSENT

'For section 548 (no right to give corporal punishment), section 549 (interpretation of section 548) and section 550 (no avoidance of section 548 by refusing admission to school) of the Education Act 1996 the following sections shall be substituted—


"Corporal punishment lawful with parental consent.

548.—(1) Where a pupil at a school to which this section applies who has not attained the age of 18 is required on disciplinary grounds to be subjected to corporal punishment at his school, his punishment shall not be unlawful if his parent's consent to it has been given in accordance with subsection (2) and if the conditions set out in subsection (4) are satisfied.

  1. The admission arrangements for a school to which this section applies may include provisions—
    1. making it a condition of the admission of every child to the school that his parent gives the admission authority a signed parental declaration either—
      1. at the time of applying for a place at the school for the child, or
      2. if the child is allocated a conditional place, within such period as is specified in the arrangements;

        that he consents to the use of corporal punishment as one of the measures that may be taken with a view to regulating the conduct of pupils including his child; and
    2. authorising the admission authority to dispense with that condition to any extent in the case of a particular child where they are satisfied that there are special reasons for doing so.
  2. This section applies to—
    1. a school maintained by a local education authority;
    2. a grant-maintained or grant-maintained special school; or
    3. a city technology college or city college for the technology of the arts.
  3. The conditions referred to in subsection (1) are—
    1. that the head teacher of the school must have previously determined, and made generally known within the school, that corporal punishment is one of the measures that may be taken with a view to regulating the conduct of pupils;
    2. that the punishment must be imposed by the head teacher or by another teacher at the school specifically or generally authorised by him for the purpose; and
    3. that the punishment must be reasonable in all the circumstances.
  4. In determining for the purposes of subsection (4)(c) whether a punishment is reasonable, the following matters in particular shall be taken into 482 account—
    1. whether the punishment constitutes a proportionate punishment in the circumstances of the case; and
    2. any special circumstances relevant to its imposition on the pupil which are known to the person imposing it (or of which he ought reasonably to be aware) including in particular—
      1. the pupil's age,
      2. any special educational needs he may have,
      3. the pupil's state of health, and
      4. the pupil's disciplinary record.
  5. Section 572, which provides for the methods by which notices may be served under this Act, does not preclude a notice from being given to a pupil's parent under this section by any other effective method.
  6. In this section—
  7. "admission arrangements", in relation to a school means the arrangements for the admission of pupils to the school;
  8. "conditional place", in relation to a child, means a place which is conditional on the child's parent giving the admission authority a signed parental declaration; and
  9. "parental declaration" means a declaration to be signed by a parent seeking the admission of his child to the school.
