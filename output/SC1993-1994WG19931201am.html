Implications of the Budget for Wales

10.30 am

Resolved,

That the Committee do meet again this day at Four o'clock.—[ Mr. Redwood.]

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. John Redwood)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of the implications of the Budget for Wales.

Yesterday we saw a virtuoso performance from my right hon. and learned Friend the Chancellor. This year's Budget is a great Budget for the United Kingdom and a great Budget for Wales. It is a Budget for jobs, a Budget for economic recovery and a Budget to curb the deficit. [Hon. Members: "Whose jobs?"] Opposition Members do not understand the essence of the Budget; they do not want to believe that the recovery will grow stronger and continue year after year as the economic forecast suggests. I must disappoint them: the recovery will do that, and that will mean that the next election in a few years' time will be fought against an extremely good economic background. That is why Opposition Members are so glum and do not want to believe what is happening: they can see clearly that there will be low inflation, high growth, more prosperity and more jobs, in Wales as in the rest of the United Kingdom.

Mr. Alex Carlile
(Montgomery)
: May I interrupt the rivulets of rhetoric and ask the right hon. Gentleman where the Budget makes provision for jobs to replace the jobs that will be lost as a result of the savage cut in hill livestock compensatory allowances that was announced yesterday as the Budget was being delivered?

Mr. Redwood
: It is a bit rich for the hon. and learned Gentleman to intervene so early in the debate when he spent much of our recent debate on the floor of the House deliberately preventing me from dealing with issues that are of concern to hon. Members and to people in Wales. The fact that I gave way to him so early in my speech shows what a generous spirit I have. The hon. and learned Gentleman made an erroneous point, taking into account only one element of support for hill farming, rather than all the elements of support from the common agricultural policy. He does not want to know that hill farming incomes have been increasing and that the common agricultural policy is giving more generous treatment. Instead he puts his finger on one small part of a much broader issue and will not admit—he will never admit—that things are getting better. Many hill farmers have been experiencing rising incomes this year and will be pleased by the increases announced under the common 4 agricultural policy in the recent statement on public spending.

Amid the welter of information produced in connection with the Budget, one aspect has received too little attention. Page 16 of the Red Book states in a rather technical language and even, perhaps, in a little jargon:

"gilt sales will be some £6.8 billion lower than indicated by the normal operation of the full fund policy. This will help to ease the exceptional degree of cash shortage in the money markets."

[Hon. Members: "Eh?"] The Opposition do not understand that that is perhaps the most important item in the Budget. Either they have missed it or they do not understand it. What that statement means is that we are serious about recovery and will ensure that money conditions are right so that business can expand and so that jobs are created. That follows the great success of our policy in recent months, cutting interest rates from 15 per cent. to 5.5 per cent. As a result, there has been a pick-up in lending, a pick-up in business activity and a pick-up in jobs.

Mr. Redwood

The essence of the Budget is that the deficit is being brought down by economic recovery, which will generate more tax revenues, as well as by a judicious mixture of tax increases in the two Budgets this year and sensible reductions in public spending, which were also much needed.

: The fact that the right hon. Chancellors have repeatedly assured us that the tax decreases would lead to greater investment by business.

Mr. Redwood
: Last year, the Chancellor made optimistic forecasts and they proved to be right. We have had a year of growth, a year of progress and even a year of diminishing unemployment, which I hope the Opposition will welcome. Of course, we must correct the ravages that the recession has wrought on the public finances and we need more jobs. Central to the Budget is the creation of conditions for enterprise and prosperity to ensure that those jobs come forward. Jobs are already coming forward: we predicted last year that that would happen and some have come, and we predict it again this year and there will be more.

Mr. Peter Hain
(Neath)
: On the question raised by my right hon. Friend the Member for Llanelli this morning, surely the Secretary of State will agree that there is no problem funding the deficit. That is why the Government are in such trouble. May I also refer the right hon. Gentleman to p.97 of the Red Book and ask why the figures given for Welsh Office spending in this year and the coming years are different from those in the Welsh Office press release issued yesterday?

Mr. Redwood
: The Committee will find that there is some rounding of the figures in the Treasury booklet, which accounts for any divergencies that the hon. Gentleman may have noticed. On his more substantial point, it is true that an element of the deficit is the result of the recession, and that that element will reduce as employment and activity pick up and as tax revenues arise from the recovery.

It is also true that quite a lot of the deficit is to finance large programmes of public capital expenditure. The tables in the Red Book show, on different definitions, between £22 billion and £29 billion-worth of fixed domestic capital formation or capital investment in the public sector, which will be financed by borrowing. It is perfectly reasonable to finance capital investment by borrowing. If the projects are good, if there will be a good return from them, and if there are future revenues. If they are other types of investment, one must be careful, however. If there is no revenue to match the interest charges, one has to consider the impact on taxation in future years of meeting the cost of such projects, even though they are of a capital nature.

They are always suggesting that fuel should be made more expensive and that motorists should be penalised. We have come up with sensible measures which take into account the fact that fuel prices have been falling in real terms. What we have suggested is a good compromise between the desire to fulfil our green commitments and not making prices unrealistic for people in rural areas. Were the Opposition parties ever let loose, motorists would be clobbered severely and great damage would be done to people in rural areas as a result. The Opposition parties are always telling us, "Cane the motorist, put up the taxes on all kinds of fuel." They believe that we should be greener than we have suggested.

Friend the Secretary of State for Transport's proposals will make it that much easier to have a competitive, hungry, decent railway which successfully competes for more business, from both freight and passenger traffic.

Mr. Redwood
: I agree that that is an important project. Gentleman can rest assured that I shall recognise the importance of the project when I announce my decision.

I was discussing the favourable impact of lower interest rates before a series of interventions diverted the Committee's attention. To remind the Committee, big interest rate reductions have given a huge injection of money to British business—£12,000 million extra a year—and a welcome injection to many household budgets. The average mortgage holder is £170 a month better off as a result of the big reductions in the main base rates and the mortgage rates related to them.

The impact of that can already be seen in retail sales, which are moving ahead nicely. That is feeding through to demand for goods from factories in Wales and elsewhere. Improved retail sales and higher disposable incomes for those with mortgages have been accompanied by a change in tone and mood throughout Wales and the rest of the United Kingdom.

JPEG 7 The trend of share prices is a reliable indicator that politicians cannot arrange or fix. New highs have been registered for many months, reflecting the confidence of investors at home and abroad in British industry and showing that the City believes that profits are rising and will rise strongly. That is good news for investment and for jobs.

Land prices are beginning to stabilise or move ahead in areas of the United Kingdom in which they have been hard hit. There are growing signs of confidence in house building and in commercial property. My recent discussions with house builders and with commercial property specialists in Wales and in the rest of the United Kingdom have been very different in tone from those of a couple of years ago. The questions that now are being asked concern land availability, planning permissions, looking into the future and creating new opportunities, whereas a year or two ago they concerned high interest rates, the lack of tenant demand and the impact of the recession on such businesses.

Property development and redevelopment will be an important part of building the new Wales. We need the confidence and support of the people involved in that business to ensure that the valleys initiative is seen through to a successful conclusion, that the Cardiff Bay development corporation also makes good progress and that, throughout Wales, modern business is properly equipped with the right buildings.

Economic progress has been most visible in the past year. Unemployment has fallen throughout the United Kingdom, by 129,000 for the United Kingdom as a whole and by 2,400 in Wales in the past month—a most encouraging figure. I should like to remind the Committee briefly of the position in March 1986, when unemployment was last at very high levels. In those days, Wales had 13.8 per cent. unemployment. In the rest of the United Kingdom, unemployment was at 11.2 per cent. Today, the United Kingdom and Wales both have an unemployment rate of 10.2 per cent. That rate is below the unacceptably high levels of 1986, but in Wales the fall has been much greater. Unemployment in Wales is now in fine with the rest of the United Kingdom and below the European Community average. Welsh and United Kingdom unemployment is falling while unemployment in most of the rest of Europe is going up.
