Local Authorities (Alteration of Requisite Calculations) (England) Regulations 1999

4.30 pm

Mr. Paul Burstow
(Sutton and Cheam)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Local Authorities (Alteration of Requisite Calculations) (England) Regulations 1999 (S.I. 1999, No. 228).

I am pleased to welcome you to the Chair, Mr. Atkinson. I look forward to serving on this Committee considering these regulations this afternoon and to our relatively speedy progress. At the same time, I hope that members of the Committee can consider the issues that the regulations raise because they raise serious questions about the way in which our local government finance system operates and the gap between the Government's stated intention in opposition and what they are now putting into place.

Although these regulations are entitled the Local Authorities (Alteration of Requisite Calculations) (England) Regulations, which sounds a straightforward, albeit technical measure, on closer examination it becomes clear that they constitute a major shift of the balance in our local government system which breaches some important principles. I want to take a little time to consider what these regulations mean in practice and to air some of the concerns that have been raised by local authorities, by community groups, by the Local Government Association and by others in the House.

Some of those issues have already been debated in the Standing Committee that considered the Local Government Bill some weeks ago. My hon. Friend the Member for Taunton (Jackie Ballard) took an active part in those discussions, and I read the exchanges that took place with some interest, not least because the Minister, knowing that this debate was going to take place, was understandably not entirely forthcoming about all aspects of the issues concerned. Indeed, some notable speeches were made by Opposition members of the Committee which outlined only too clearly the damaging effects of breaching these important principles and what that would mean for local authorities.

I was at my council budget setting meeting on Monday night where we had a debate about our council tax level which, in my London borough of Sutton, will cross the threshold that these regulations set. Labour members of the council urged us to go just a little further on the grounds that, with the council tax benefit subsidy limitation that the regulations introduce, only an additional 19p would be added to a band D council tax in my constituency. They argued 4 that the regulations and the issues relating to council tax benefit subsidy limitation should not give cause for concern because they were not having much effect. That did not seem a sound argument. It concerned me greatly that Labour councillors who had been briefed on the issue thought that it was enough just to look at the figures and how they were being applied in the current year and not be too concerned about the principles behind the measure.

Jackie Ballard
(Taunton)
: Does my hon. Friend agree that as Sutton is in the relatively prosperous south-east, his council's increase in council tax above the threshold will have much less impact on his council tax payers than the same increase will have in poorer areas of the country? The Government will say that they have included some safeguards, but they are not enough to stop poorer areas losing out more than wealthier areas of the country.

Mr. Burstow
: My hon. Friend is absolutely right. That is one of the points that had passed my Labour colleagues on the council by. The dampening mechanism set out in the regulations will work in such a way that the poorest of our communities will pay the most. That seems perverse, and is a reason why, although we are moving the regulations, we wish to move them into a siding rather than onto the main track; we do not wish to see them implemented by this or any other Government.

Perhaps I should explain a little more about the regulations. There are two types of authority that are affected by them: the billing authorities, which collect our council taxes and send out the bills, and the precepting authorities—the county councils. County councils are subject to the legislation now making its way through Parliament and will therefore also be captured by these regulations. The council tax collecting authorities will, unless that legislation goes through, find themselves in an invidious position, in that their collection funds will be penalised by the way in which this system will work. Attempts were made in the Committee considering the Local Government Bill to question the Minister as to whether, if the relevant clause of that Bill did not get through the Committee— and it still has to get through on Report—the Government would pull these regulations. I hope that the Minister will return to that point later. If Parliament decides that the measures set out in the primary legislation currently under consideration are unacceptable—a view shared by a growing number of Members in all parts of the House—and that the principles raised by them are ones to which Labour Members would not subscribe, I hope that the Government will then withdraw the regulations. Perhaps the Minister will tell us how the Government might approach that matter.

Council tax benefit is intended to assist those who cannot afford to pay the whole of their council tax bill. Under the existing arrangements, the Government provide 95 per cent, of council tax benefit through the subsidy scheme. Local authorities currently provide 5 per cent., but the regulations will give the Government powers, for this year and subsequent years, to claw JPEG 5 back council tax benefit subsidy paid to local authorities that set a council tax increase that is deemed excessive. The regulations set out the criteria by which that will be judged.

The scheme will apply to both the billing and the precepting authorities, and each tier will be responsible for meetings its share of the clawback arising from the regulations. The Standing Committee considering the Local Government Bill paid attention to those matters, and to the changes to the law necessary to provide for the precepting authorities to be caught by the clawback regime.

The regulations contain a complex algebraic formula, a seminar on which the committee might find entertaining. During the Committee on the Local Government Bill, the right hon. Member for Skipton and Ripon (Mr. Curry) gave details on the way in which the formula operates. Since then, he and others outside that Committee have rightly pointed out that the Secretary of State's estimate of the council tax yield for each local authority—or the average that is assumed, which is 16 per cent.—is crucial to how the measure bites. As my hon. Friend the Member for Taunton has already said, it bites disproportionately on the poorest local authorities and on the poorest communities in those local authorities.

This year, the threshold to which the rules will apply is last year's council tax plus 4.5 per cent.

4.53 pm

On resuming—

Mr. Burstow
: Before our little trip downstairs, I was explaining that the regulations operate through a complicated algebraic formula. Unfortunately, the blackboard that we requested in order to go through the formula has not arrived. Therefore, we cannot dwell on all the details, although I am sure that other hon. Members want to enmesh themselves in the formula.

The regulations provide that, this year, the threshold to which the rules apply will be calculated by adding 4.5 per cent, to last year's council tax. When the threshold is passed, a system of tapers determines the clawback that specific councils have to pay. For example, if a council spends only 1 per cent, above the 4.5 per cent, increase, an extra quarter of the increase will have to be paid out of local resources. That will mean reductions in services and a shuffling of financial resources. The sum will ultimately be paid by council tax payers. One way or another, it will affect communities. If, however, the rise is a little more—if it is 3.5 per cent, above the 4.5 per cent, threshold—all the increase will fall on the council tax locally. None of it will be met from the council tax benefit subsidy from the Government.

I referred just now to the algebra underlying the regulations and set out in the schedule. The crucial term of the formulae is B, which pops up in one or two places. Hon. Members who have not yet had a chance 6 to study all the regulations may want to know that B is the proportion of council tax yield from benefits estimated by the Secretary of State, or 16 per cent. That 16 per cent. comes into play only if the real figure or the Secretary of State's estimate turns out to be higher. That is an important aspect of the working of the formula, because of the way it affects authorities in relation to their level of gearing.

The range of gearing for local authorities is quite diverse. The average is around one to four, so that to increase spending by £1 it would be necessary to put up council tax by £4. For some local authorities it can be significantly higher and for others significantly lower. The key determinant of the gearing effect is the extent to which the local authority's expenditure is supported by national non-domestic rates and revenue support grant. The authorities that tend to be most supported by those means are the poorest, which do not have a sufficiently large or well distributed revenue base to support services locally. All too often those are also the local authorities with the most band A and band D properties and the ones that suffer the greatest impact.

Quite simply, some of the poorest communities will pay more as a consequence of the regulations that we are considering, and will be disproportionately affected in comparison to some of the wealthier local authorities. As my hon. Friend the Member for Taunton pointed out, my local authority is not significantly affected, because it is relatively wealthy, with a relatively low gearing ratio. Those with high gearing ratios—often the poorest authorities and communities—will suffer most.

(Mole Valley)

He may have noticed that the different definitions of SSA for this and the following financial year are such that, essentially, SSA means what Ministers want it to mean. That is a very unpleasant imponderable.

Mr. Burstow
: I read the exchanges between the hon. Member for Mole Valley (Sir P. Beresford) and the Minister in the Standing Committee, which went to that very point. He is right that this is a case of words meaning what Ministers want them to mean to suit particular convenience and arguments. I should be interested to know what definition will be thought applicable to today's debate, as compared to the debate in Standing Committee a few weeks ago.

A number of disparities will arise, which will get worse in time. Because of the way the formula works, there will be a link between the settlement established this year and future settlements, with respect to the working of the threshold. That is an attempt to prevent people from manipulating the system to their advantage, but undoubtedly it will in turn disadvantage many of the poorest local authorities and result, in time, in a shifting of the burden of council tax benefit from the Exchequer to local people through council tax bills. That localisation by stealth of council tax benefit is an unacceptable breach of the JPEG 7 principle that national Government should support such welfare expenditure. The Government are shifting that burden by the back door, just as they are shifting the burden generally with respect to council tax policies, back to local council tax payers, but in a way that blurs accountability.

Sir Paul Beresford
: The hon. Gentleman is using some very nice words to describe what I consider to be crude universal capping. That is all it is.

Mr. Burstow
: The hon. Gentleman anticipates my peroration, perhaps; I may return to that. I have no doubt that the evidence introduced by the regulations is crude universal capping. It is dressed up with technical phrases, but quite simply that is what it is. It will have the greatest impact in areas with the most low-value properties—bands A and B—which often have the greatest number of benefit recipients. The effect will be greater in areas such as inner London and Merseyside, where needs are often greatest, and council tax payers in the poorest areas will be hit 10 times as hard as those in the least poor areas.

During the debate on the local government settlement on 4 February, the hon. Member for Normanton (Mr. O'Brien) raised a point of concern, fairly, about the way in which the regulations might affect some of the poorest communities. He said:

"If a council in the poorer group of local authorities tried to increase its council tax over its budget requirement to sustain services, the application of the council tax benefit subsidy limitation scheme would be devasting, because of the clawback of the rate support grant.


The council tax benefit is payable to help people who cannot afford to pay their council tax. It is a social benefit and, I believe, it should be the responsibility of Government to meet the cost. Such direct welfare costs should not be a charge on the resources of local authorities. I emphasise that I am speaking of the poorer council group in local government."—[ Official Report, 4 February 1999; Vol. 324, c. 1152.]

That is what we are talking about as we attempt to reject the regulations which will have a direct impact on the poorest in our communities.

The Labour party, when in opposition, rightly aligned itself with those who opposed the breaching of this very principle in respect of housing benefit and opposed the previous Government's introduction of a regime that effectively put in place a mechanism for clawing back housing benefit subsidy from local authorities. In an exchange on 25 February in the Standing Committee of the Local Government Bill between the Minister for Local Government and Housing and my hon. Friend the Member for Taunton, the hon. Lady's response was simply that it was all right to oppose the clawback of housing benefit. She said:

"Only other council tenants have to accept the burden of additional housing benefit."—[ Official Report, Standing Committee B, 25 February 1999, c. 549.]

But surely only other council tax payers have to share the burden of picking up council tax. I fail to see the distinction; if there is one, it is incredibly fine. The reality is that poor council tax payers will, as a consequence of the targeting of this measure on areas 8 with the highest number of bands A and B properties, be footing a disproportionate part of the bill to pay for the very poorest in their communities. We believe that the regulations are inappropriate.

One point of great concern to the LGA is the pace at which the change has been introduced. The association is anxious because it has not had an opportunity for adequate discussions with the Government. It has made clear its opposition to the regulations, but has also made it clear that it still seeks to continue discussions with the Government if the regulations go through today, because it wishes to know whether Ministers will be prepared to undertake a fundamental review of the way in which the new regime will operate during its first year and what its impact will be in practice rather than what Ministers say it will be. I hope that the Minister will give the LGA some comfort by undertaking to conduct a review with the association, thus building on the partnership about which the Government so often speak when they discuss their work with local government.

It is our view that the combination of the gearing effect, the tapers set out in the regulations, and the arbitrary nature of the way that council tax yields are being measured will affect the poorest communities disproportionately. Not only that, it will reduce accountability still further. Liberal Democrats have consistently opposed the application of crude and universal capping as well as this more sophisticated and sneaky capping now being introduced. The reality is that this even cruder device, which harks back to measures introduced by the previous Labour Government in their system of controlling local government expenditure, will further blur accountability and make it harder for local citizens to know who is responsible for their council tax increase. Is it the Minister, with her decisions on thresholds, or is it their local council? Whatever the answer, the consequence is that the poorest will pay. I hope that the Committee rejects the regulations because they should not be allowed to go through.
