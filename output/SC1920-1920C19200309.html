CLAUSE 1 ( Persons to be insured against unemployment) ordered to stand part of the Bill.

CLAUSE 1
(Persons to be. insured to unemployment benefit.)

Every person who being insured under this Act becomes unemployed and in whose case the conditions laid down by this Act (in this Act referred to as "statutory conditions") are fulfilled shall be entitled, subject to the provisions of this Act, to receive payments (in this Act referred to as "unemployment benefit"), at weekly or other prescribed intervals, at such rates and for such periods as are authorised by or under the Second Schedule to this Act so long as the statutory conditions continue to be fulfilled and so long as he is not disqualified under this Act for the receipt of unemployment benefit.

Sir R. HORNE
: I beg to move to leave out the word "becomes" ["becomes unemployed"] and to insert instead thereof the word "is."

This is a purely drafting Amendment. The draftsman takes the view that it makes the position clearer to use a simple word rather than a word which indicates the operation of becoming unemployed.

Amendment agreed to

CLAUSE, as amended, ordered to stand part of the Bill.

CLAUSE 3 ( Exemptions) ordered to stand part of the Bill.

CLAUSE 4.
â€”(Power to extend Act to excepted employments.)

(1) The Minister may with the approval of the Treasury, by order provide for including among the persons employed within the meaning of this Act any persons engaged in any of the excepted employments specified in Part II. of the First Schedule to this Act, either unconditionally or subject to such conditions as may be specified in the order.

(2) Before any such order is made a draft thereof shall be laid before each House of Parliament for a period of not less than twenty days during which the House is sitting, and if either House, before the expiration of that period presents an Address to His Majesty against the draft or any part thereof, no further proceedings shall be taken thereon, but without prejudice to the making of any new draft order.

Mr. WILLIAMS (Clerk)
read the Resolution of the House of 5th March as follows:â€”

"That, for the purposes of any Act of the present Session to amend the Law in respect JPEG 11 of insurance against unemployment, it is expedient to authorise the payment out of moneys provided by Parliament of a contribution towards the cost of unemployment benefit and any other payments to be madeout of the unemployment fund established under the Act, and also of such other sums as may be required for making any other payments (not being made payments in respect of the cost of unemployment benefit or of benefits payable under any supplementary scheme) as may be authorised under the Act:


Provided that the contribution towards the cost of unemployment benefit and other payments aforesaid shall not exceed one-third of the aggregate amount of the contributions which would be received from employers and employed persons in any year if those contributions were at the rate in the case of men of six pence and in the case of women of five pence per week, or at such higher rates not exceeding in the case of men eight pence and in the case of women seven pence, as may be fixed under any provisions of the Act for securing the solvency of the unemployment fund or relating to the periodical revision of the rates of contribution."

Sir R. HORNE
: I beg to move, in Sub-section (1), after the word "Act" ["First Schedule to this Act"] to insert the words "or any class or description of the persons so engaged."

The intention of this Amendment is to make provision to the effect of excepting not merely a whole trade where that is thought advisable but also to give an opportunity of excepting parts of a trade. For example, at the present time agriculture is exempted from the Bill. This Clause gives an opportunity of bringing in an exempted employment, but it would be unfortunate if that were to infer that the whole employment of agriculture, including forestry and horticulture, could only be brought in as one whole, when in point of fact you might wish to bring in one portion of so great an industry as agriculture without bringing in it all. The intention of the Amendment, accordingly, is to provide that there may be brought into the Bill not merely one of the excepted employments referred to in the Bill but also any class or description of the persons so engaged in any employment.

Lieut.-Colonel A. MURRAY
: Will the right hon. Gentleman explain how any branch of an employment would be brought within the scope of the operation? Would it be by order of the Minister, or would legislation be necessary?

Sir R. HORNE
: That is provided for in a later Clause of this Bill. The pro- 12 vision is that it is to be by Order, an Order to be laid on the table of the House, to which objection may be taken, so that the matter really comes before Parliament before the Order can be pronounced.

Amendment agreed to.

Clause, as amended, ordered to stand part of the Bill.

CLAUSE 5.
â€”(Contributions by Employed Persons, Employers, and the Treasury.)

(1) The funds required for providing unemployment benefit and for making any other payments which under this Act are to be made out of the unemployment fund established under this Act shall be derived partly from contributions by employed persons, partly from contributions by the employers of those persons, and partly from moneys provided by Parliament.

(4) Except where regulations under this Act otherwise prescribe, the employer shall in the first instance be liable to pay both the contribution payable by himself (in this Act referred to as "the employer's contribution") and also, on behalf of and to the exclusion of the employed person, the contribution payable by that person, and subject to any such regulations shall be entitled to recover from the employed person, by deduction from his wages or otherwise, the amount of the contributions so paid by him on behalf of the employed person in accordance with the rules set out in the Fourth Schedule to this Act.

(5) Contributions in respect of employed persons shall cease to be payable on their attaining the age of seventy.

(6) Where it has been decided by the Minister in manner provided by this Act that contributions under this Act are not payable in respect of any person or any class of persons, and that decision is subsequently revised or reversed on appeal so as to make contributions payable in respect of that person or that class of persons, contributions shall be so payable only as from the date on which the decision was so revised or reversed.

(5) The Minister shall, on the application of any employer made in the prescribed manner within two months after the termination of any calendar year or other prescribed period of twelve months or after such longer time as the Minister may prescribe, refund to such employer as soon as practicable out of the unemployment fund the sum of three shillings in respect of each workman in respect of whom he has paid not less than forty-five contributions during the period of twelve months. For the purpose of meeting any change in the period for JPEG 13 which any refund of contributions is to be made under the foregoing provisions, or for the purpose of making provision for any period which may elapse between the date on which contributions commence to be payable under this Act and the date on which the first period for the refund of contributions under the foregoing provisions commences the Minister may, so far as necessary for the purpose, apply the provisions of this sub-section to any period less than twelve months, subject to such proportionate reduction of the number of contributions required as the Minister directs, and this sub-section shall take effect as regards any such period of less than twelve months as so applied.

Sir R. HORNE
: I agree that it is very-important that we should do everything we can to prevent unemployment and give an inducement to employers to keep men at employment rather than allow to go out of work men who, with some little zeal upon the part of the employer, could be kept at work. The Clause which the hon. and gallant Member has moved is in terms a repetition of Clause 94 of the previous Unemployment Act. That Clause was enacted with the intention to which the hon. and gallant Member has referred, but it has been entirely futile for that purpose. If he considers it he will see that it offers no possible inducement to an employer to continue a man at work rather than discharge him. The proposal is that the employer should be given 3s. a year in respect of each man for whom he has paid forty-five contributions in the year. It offers no inducement to an employer to keep a man on even for an extra day if you offer him 3s. a year for a man costing 15s. a day to employ. However good the object you cannot achieve it by this method. The fact is that you could not except at the most 14 inordinate expense offer an inducement to an employer which would be sufficient, in works of any dimensions at all, to induce him to keep on men when work became scarce. There must be some other inducement, either affecting his own pocket or his view of public philanthropy, or the interest that he takes in the personal welfare of his men, towards keeping employment going at a time when perhaps it is difficult, but you can never induce an employer by simply paying him 3s. a year for a man who costs him many shillings a day.

In the past it has cost Â£118,000 a year to pay the employers the money which they were entitled to under the section of the previous statute, and that Â£118,000 has been spent absolutely without result. At that time there were only 1,500,000 people insured. The provision did not apply to the extended insurance scheme of 1916. It only covered the original 1,500,000 who were insured under the initial Act. Accordingly there is to that extent precedent for not pushing it further or keeping it alive now. We propose now to insure 12,000,000 people altogether, and instead of costing Â£118,000 a year this would cost according to our calculations at least Â£600,000 a year, which would wipe out the whole balance of your fund. The result would be that you would have to put up the contributions of employers and workmen to give the benefit which we at present provide. So far from this 3s. allowance to employers producing any beneficial result, it would only lead to a more expensive scheme for workmen and employers without any corresponding advantage. I hope therefore that the hon. and gallant Member will not press his amendment.

What has been the experience of friendly societies in relation to men over 70? It is that it really becomes impossible to distinguish between the cases in which a man is either ill or unemployed through incapacity and the cases in which he is unable to work or to move about owing to the ordinary debilitation of age. Accordingly 16 the matter becomes an entirely practical one. Whatever provision legislatively may be made, or ought to be made, for people over the age of 70, it cannot very well be done in this fashion, because what insurance becomes under such a provision as this is really an adjunct of the old age pension. You may say, if you like, that people ought to get larger sums in the way of pensions than they get now. That is an entirely different matter. In practice it really does prove impossible to distinguish between cases where a man is unemployed through old age and where he is unemployed through the vicissitudes of trade.

I would like to emphasise that point, because men, who may be working on different sides of a street, and may be living next door to each other, may both be affected by a dispute, and one can take Unemployment Benefit and the other cannot. I am sure the right hon. Gentleman in charge of this Bill will see how this kind of thing will rankle in the minds of those adversely affected as a very serious injustice.

The points raised as to the difficulty of administration can be met, I think, as was suggested by the Mover of the Amendment. The administration of Unemployed Insurance has been largely one of difficulty right from the very beginning, and, as experience has come along, means have been devised whereby the various difficulties can be met. In this respect, if not on the lines suggested, at least by some other method, it will be perfectly easy to lay down lines of demarcation between the man who is unemployed as the result of his own action, and the man who is unemployed through no fault of his own. The point about the non-unionist hardly has the application the right hon. Gentleman may think. He, at least, has an opportunity of having a voice in the question, and if he is a skilled worker in a foundry or a factory, he has the right of belonging to the Union which has got the question under consideration, which may develop into a strike or lockout. But the other man has no chance or opportunity. He is outside the thing entirely; he has no right to attend meetings at which these things are discussed. He is helpless right from the very beginning, and has no responsibility whatever for his unemployment, which might result from a 38 dispute in connection with another class of labour with whom he may be to some extent associated as an assistant. Having in mind these facts, I do hope the Minister will reconsider this point. Trade Unions, if left to themselves, by their own schemes have always solved the difficulties. There is no reason to introduce restrictive Regulations such as are in this Bill. Trade Unionism can do what is required in its voluntary schemes, and with much lower contributions for the rate of benefit in this Bill. If they can manage schemes on these lines, and keep their schemes solvent and satisfy the members it ought not to be impossible to do something of the kind in this Bill.
