Mr. Andrew F. Bennett
: At our last sitting I was trying to develop the argument about the need for the widest possible consultation regarding any changes, that the different groups needed to be consulted and that there should be different weighting. But as we have printed copies of our last proceedings there is no need for me to recap.

I had started to argue the need for consultation regarding pupil-teacher ratios. One of the most disturbing features of the old system of secondary modern schools and grammar schools was the disproportionate allocation of resources. More money was given to the grammar schools and they were able to enjoy a better pupil-teacher ratio than secondary modern schools. In going back on those proposals, it is important that the local authority makes it clear to parents that it does not intend to discriminate against either type of school, that in a secondary modern 60 School system pupils will enjoy the same pupil-teacher ratios.

In the allocation of resources for books people have been disturbed that local authorities have either reduced or not increased the amount allocated in line with inflation. We hear of pupils with insufficient text books who either have to share or use photocopied pages from text books, which is unsatisfactory. It seems, from my researches, that the situation is worse in the secondary modern schools.

Local authorities should state that they are not trying to make the secondary moderns second-class Schools and give a clear commitment that, in the allocation of resources, they will give as much to the second modern as to the grammar schools. It may well be that the number of text books needed in the secondary modern schools is not as great as the need for other materials—such as audio-visual aids, which are expensive.

There should also be more information about truancy rates. Conservatives have criticised the truancy level in some comprehensive and secondary modern schools. But there are often remarkably high truancy rates in some grammar schools. That is because one or two people are playing truant. Considering the selective nature of the intake, no one should play truant from such a school. Parents should be assured that pupils selected for grammar schools, who find the education offered not to their liking, will be able to transfer.

As a transfer may imply demotion and a loss of status there is a tendency for pupils not to transfer, although it would be in their best educational interest to develop a more widely based course; the type of education available in a comprehensive school but not in a selective school. The question of transfer downwards should be discussed fully. In a transfer upwards, it is implied that the pupil has done very well, but in the transfer downwards there is a problem and the child's reaction may be to truant.

The next point concerns one of the more crucial areas—namely, the proportion of children who will be selected for grammar schools. Very few local authorities actually spell this out. The method tends to depend on how many places are available. The grammar school has a three-stream entry, so 90 children can attend the grammar school, and if that represents 20 per cent., 25 per cent. or 30 per cent. of the number of children in that year, that is the pro- JPEG 61 portion which goes. We therefore have the very unfair situation in which in one year a child is selected for a grammar school while, perhaps, two or three years later a child of exactly the same ability does not get selected because there happen to be more children in that age group. The proportions of children to go to grammar school and to secondary modern school ought to be spelled. out. However, one of the major weaknesses of the selective system is that no one can Say, whether the ideal proportion should be 20 per cent., 35 per cent. or any figure between them. I think that this area should be fully discussed.

A further point that should be discussed in consultation concerns the real questioning of examination results. The Government are very pleased to question some of the results in the newer comprehensive schools, but what surprises me is that they are not so willing to question the results that have emerged in some of the grammar schools and often in some of the independent schools or former direct grant schools. Some hon. Members boast about examination results of 90 per cent., 95 per cent. and sometimes higher percentages, but the real question is, why was the figure not 100 per cent?

If the school has a selective intake and if the selection process is correct, all its pupils should be reaching O-level and a large proportion should achieve successful A-level results. It is a matter of comparing examination results not with other schools but with the predicted ability of the children who attend that school. If a school maintains, as some of the independent schools did, that it had the top 5 per cent. from a particular catchment area, then the real measure of that school is whether any child failed to achieve satisfactory examination results; it should be said not that the school has done well because 85 per cent. of the pupils passed but rather that it had done badly because of the I5 per cent. failure rate. There should be far more discussion of a school's examination results in the context of the likely achievement of the children who attend.

I argue that there should be far more consultation in that respect, and I hope that the Government will accept either amendment No. 3 or my amendment No. 4.
