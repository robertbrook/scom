The purpose of the Amendments is to improve the lot of the invalidity pensioner by the introduction of the principle of earnings-related benefits into the State scheme to cover the needs of long-term incapacity. These proposals are in line with Labour Party thinking on the subject. During the last Parliament, the Labour Government introduced the National Superannuation and Social Insurance 88 Bill, which was wholly based on the principle that, if men and women made earnings-related contributions, they should be entitled in return to earnings-related benefit.

Present Ministers have carried with them into office the beliefs they held in Opposition, and those beliefs are now unfortunately in the White Paper, Strategy for Pensions. The Government are still floundering along with the idea that, in future, the work force will be prepared to pay earnings-related contributions and receive in return only flat-rate benefits. If the average man and woman were asked to accept such conditions in the private sector, one would be entitled to describe such a scheme approaching a swindle, and one could hardly envisage that there would be many customers for it if it were advertised in the daily or the week-end Press. However, I do not wish to exacerbate any feelings of tension or disagreement between the two sides of the Committee. I seek only to achieve agreement in this limited area in order further to assist the long-term sick and invalidity pensioners.

The present system of State benefits provides that a person with an adequate contribution record who becomes sick and is unable to work is entitled to an earnings-related sickness benefit in return for his contributions for the first six months of incapacity. After that, however, he returns to flat-rate benefit, which we now call the invalidity pension. The only items in the Bill which are new are the term "invalidity pension" or sickness benefit and the addition of the invalidity allowance which we discussed on the earlier Amendment.

Even with the increases proposed in the Bill, with the level of benefit standing at £6.75 for a single person, with higher benefits for a married couple and the trinity of invalidity allowances in addition, 220,000 people will receive only 35p. invalidity allowance and 75,000 will have no invalidity allowance whatsoever. Therefore, very large numbers, perhaps almost all on invalidity pension, will receive a benefit recognising their chronic sickness, their long-term inability to work, but at the end of the day, even the increased rates proposed by the Government are still recognised by the State as being inadequate to live on without recourse to some other form of income. The result is that a large proportion of JPEG 89 such sick people will have to depend in the future, as in the past, upon means-tested supplementary benefit in order to have enough to live on.

On this side of the Committee, we believe that as a matter of priority we should seek to push back the area of means testing as much as we can. I know that the Minister will explain that, as the result of the higher allowances for the children of invalidity pensioners, some pensions could be taken off means-tested benefit and could have enough to live on without means-tested benefit. Nevertheless, large numbers of people who have only the income laid down by the scale rates for invalidity pension will still have to go to the Supplementary Benefits Commission.

On the question of supplementary benefits, there are some contradictions in the argument on either side. It depends upon the attitude taken towards means-tested benefits. The general view of my party is that we should seek to get as many people as possible off means-tested benefits. But the invalidity pensioner or retirement pensioner could well complain under any system, as will happen under this system, that as a result of the pattern of child allowances and the invalidity allowances, when there is an increase in the invalidity pension the net result is that their supplementary benefit has been reduced and in total they are little or no better off. I am not taking about not a change in the rates but about a change in the scheme. As a primary principle, one of the reasons we seek to introduce the concept of earnings-related benefits in return for what are becoming increasingly earnings-related contributions—and with the introduction of the Government's proposals, and if the computers in Newcastle are fully operational by that time, we will move over fully to earnings-related contributions—people will be getting inadequate flat-rate benefits, and for many of them they will not be enough to live on without recourse to means testing.

That illustrates the two major reasons why we seek to introduce the principle of earnings-related benefits for invalidity pensioners. First, we do not want them on supplementary benefit, and, second, we think that there is an important principle here which should apply not only to the private sector but to the 90 State scheme, that; there should be a relationship between contributions and benefits. When he begins his working career, no one assumes automatically that his health will break down permanently. People look forward to a build-up of credits in the State scheme, and, when they can, in a private scheme through private insurance, in order to accumulate income for their retirement. What is so sad about the State scheme as it now stands is that there is no real provision for earnings-related benefits or any extra benefits on top of the flat-rate allowances and pension if a person's health breaks down irretrievably.

Perhaps I may be allowed to deal a little more with the general background of the principles involved. The earnings-related supplement was introduced by the Labour Administration in 1966. It meant that most people who were sick or unemployed for over 12 days were entitled to an earnings-related supplement on top of a flat rate of benefit for a period of six months, provided that their earnings were sufficient to entitle them to the supplement. This did not apply to people with comparatively modest earnings. At the end of six months, people on the supplement dropped back to the flat-rate benefit to which they were entitled, plus the invalidity allowance and any other allowance which we have been able to introduce in the last two years. The idea of the Opposition when they introduced that scheme was to provide a cushion—to try to ensure that there was not a big drop in income for people who went sick or became unemployed. Inevitably, this meant that the higher paid benefited much more than the lower paid.

We have continued the earnings-related supplement in our proposal in the Bill and in last year's legislation but, in addition, we have concentrated the additional resources available on the long-term sick, those sick for over six months. This is the idea behind the invalidity pension and the invalidity allowances discussed at our last Sitting, and is equally the idea behind the allowances JPEG 97 which give help to the children of invalidity pensioners and to working wives. The concept was not on the basis of earnings but on the basis of likely need, which we felt could best be defined at this stage at the age of onset of disability.

I should like to say a few words about long-term aspects. The advantage of concentrating the additional help through the national insurance scheme is that it achieves immediate maturity. This is the contrast between the national insurance scheme, which is based on a pay-as-you-go principle, and almost any form of occupational scheme, whether run by the State or by a firm or company in the market. However much accelerated maturity may be put into an occupational scheme, there is a period of maturity, and people who need benefits have to wait some time before the benefits mature. This appears to us to be a strong argument for concentrating the benefits at this point of time, when we have a backlog to make up, in the national insurance scheme where maturity is immediate.

That does not mean that we are neglecting the longer-term prospects through occupational pension schemes and the like. I answer the point on this aspect put by the hon. Member for Rotherham by saying that we have had discussions with the occupational pension interests and with others concerned and are anxious to remove any barriers which may exist to breakdown pensions. There may be some barriers on the tax side, but we shall ensure that any difficulties which may lie in the way of firms which wish to provide breakdown pensions are removed. Equally, we should encourage everybody who runs a pension scheme, and who is able to to do so, to introduce some element of this kind within the scheme. Equally important, we shall ensure that the Government as employers will set a good example in this respect.
