Confiscation of Alcohol (Young Persons) Bill
Clause 1
CONFISCATION OF INTOXICATING LIQUOR

10.30 am

Mr. Michael Stern
(Bristol, North-West)
: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 9, leave out

"or any other relevant place"

and insert "relevant place or nearby".

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to take amendment No. 2, in page 1, line 11, leave out

"or any other".

Mr. Stern
: The purpose of the amendments is not so much to attach the principles of the Bill, but to amend its wording, which grants to the police more extensive powers than are necessary to achieve its aims. I hope that my hon. Friend the Member for Castle Point (Dr. Spink), the promoter of the Bill, can reassure me that the powers in the Bill are no more than are necessary.

Clause 1(1)(b) deals with the confiscation of intoxicating liquor, when it is purchased by an adult to be consumed by a person under the age of 18. I am concerned about the width of the suspicion that could be open to the police. Under the wording of clause 1(1)(b), it would be possible for a constable to arrest an adult who was purchasing alcohol at a supermarket in Westminster if the constable had reasonable suspicion that it would be passed on to a young person in Southampton. My amendment would restrict severely the range of suspicion that could be allotted to the police under paragraph (b).

As I said on Second Reading, I believe that the Bill is intended to cover circumstances where there is a suspicion that alcohol is being purchased to be handed to someone immediately outside the store. That is why I want to introduce into the clause the word "nearby", rather than leave the Bill open to a complete lack of geographical limitation.

I tabled amendment No. 2 because the Bill grants the police powers that are wider than necessary. For example, if the Bill allows a constable to take action because he suspects that someone in a public place has recently consumed intoxicating liquor, are we not giving the police the power to arrest a young person simply because he is drunk? He may be drunk when he is seen by the policeman, but he may have consumed the alcohol elsewhere. I doubt whether the purpose of the Bill is to add to the public order powers; it is concerned with the confiscation of alcohol. Clause 1(1)(c) would add a power to arrest a person for drunkenness to the otherwise admirable provisions of the Bill. I do not think that such a power is necessary. The police already have powers to arrest for public order offences, but the power to arrest merely on 4 suspicion that a young person is drunk is going a little too far.

Dr. Robert Spink
(Castle Point)
: It is a source of great pleasure and comfort that you, Sir James, are taking the Chair for this important Committee. The Bill will have an effect in every village, town and city in the country. It will help young and old alike; it will make our communities more pleasant, and our streets safer.

I am grateful for the opening remarks of my hon. Friend the Member for Bristol, North-West (Mr. Stern). I know that he is keen that the Bill should quickly become law. I know that he recognises its benefits, not least in preventing the onset of public disorder by preventing young people from coming under the influence of drink, making a nuisance of themselves and getting criminal records that will follow them round for the rest of their lives. I know that my hon. Friend does not want to obfuscate the Bill's powers, and I am grateful to him for bringing forward for debate such matters as this, which will help us.

I am sure that hon. Members will agree that the Bill provides a simple, down-to-earth solution to what is unfortunately an increasingly common problem. If the power to require the surrender of alcohol is to work, it is essential that the police should not become engaged in unnecessary, fruitless arguments, particularly arguments on the streets with young people, who are quite devious and will do whatever they can to avoid being controlled by the police. Simplicity, clarity and certainty are essential in all law—not just in the Bill—if it is to be easily understood and therefore effective.

I shall ask my hon. Friend to withdraw the amendment, because it makes the Bill less clear and therefore less effective. The Bill allows a police officer to confiscate alcohol from a person of 18 or over if he reasonably believes that that adult has given, or is likely to give, the alcohol to a young person to be consumed in a relevant place—that can be the same place or any other relevant place.

Subsection (6) defines "relevant place" as being

"(a) any public place, other than licensed premises; or


(b) any place, other than a public place, to which the person has unlawfully gained access".

The Bill thus enables constables to require the surrender of alcohol in all places to which the public have a right of access. That would include such places as shopping malls and streets, public parks, cinemas and, as my hon. Friend the Member for Sutton and Cheam (Lady Olga Maitland) said on Second Reading, trains.

If a person had unlawfully gained access to a property—these problems occur in private gardens, derelict buildings and even school grounds—it would not be possible, under the Bill, for the adult to avoid the requirement to surrender the alcoholic drink by taking the cans of lager or the alcopops, doing a swift bit of legwork down the street or into a nearby garden and claiming that he was not in the company of the underage person. If the constable reasonably believed that that person had supplied a young person with alcohol, he could require the surrender of any remaining alcohol.

If amendment No. 1 were accepted, an officer could require the surrender of alcohol from a person of 18 years or over only if he were in the same place as the JPEG 5 young person or in a nearby relevant place. If amendment No. 2 were accepted, the surrender of alcohol could be required of a person of 18 years or over only if its consumption by an under-age drinker had occurred in the same relevant place. It would not be possible if it had occurred in any other relevant place. The amendments would therefore create a problem for officers seeking to enforce the law. They would need to judge how far apart people should be in order to be considered as being in different places.

The example given by my hon. Friend—of someone purchasing alcohol in Westminster and taking it to Southampton to give to a young person there—is not practical. A police constable in Westminster could not reasonably ascertain what that person would do when he got off the train or out of his car in Southampton, so I reject that analysis.

Dr. Spink
: I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman for raising that point, because it gives us the opportunity to consider the essence of the operation of the Bill. It is right that we consider that now, so that it is absolutely clear.

The police officer will go to the young people whom he sees or suspects of consuming alcohol and ask them to surrender that alcohol to him. Usually, they will do so, and that will be the end of the matter. The police officer may well report the young people to their parents, and their parents will do what they have to do, but that will be the end of the matter in the eyes of the police.

The policeman will take the alcohol away. I hope that he will pour it down the nearest drain, rather than take it back to the station for a party. I am sure that chief constables will lay down proper instructions for their constables. The police officer will physically take away the alcohol from the young person.

If a young person attempts to conceal the alcohol or refuses to give it up, the police officer will arrest that 6 young person and take him to the station, where he will be dealt with. The police officer will have no power to search the young person on the street, in the park or behind the bicycle shed. We thought it better not to give police officers that power, because we want to avoid confrontation between the police and groups of youngsters on the street. We know that youngsters on the street often respond to a policeman with bravado in front of their friends. They show off and act big, but when they are taken to the privacy of the police station and faced with the custody sergeant telephoning their mum and dad, they usually get very sensible very quickly and co-operate, which means that the matter can be dealt with summarily.

The police must be given the power to deal with the matter, because to walk away and allow a young person to keep and drink alcohol and they to get up to mischief would be to betray that young person and put him in grave danger. I believe that 93 per cent.—or some similar percentage—of first use of drugs occurs under the influence of alcohol. Alcohol leads young people into petty crime and serious crime; they take cars, joyride and kill themselves and their friends. Tragedies occur in our communities because of alcohol and its influence on delicate young minds. It is right that we give the police full powers to deal with the matter in a preventive manner. I am grateful to the hon. Member for Littleborough and Saddleworth (Mr. Davies) for allowing me to make that clear.

I return to my earlier point about distance, which was raised by the amendment tabled by my hon. Friend the Member for Bristol, North-West. If the supplier of the alcohol and the young person were separated by, say, 50 m, would they be in the same relevant place or in nearby relevant places? What if they were half a mile apart? We might agree that they were not in the same relevant place, but would they be in nearby relevant places? Those are difficult questions, which would cause problems of drafting and legal interpretation.

The Bill is a simple, straightforward and commonsense measure that can be easily applied by the police officer on the street. That officer will generally be the community beat officer who will know from walking around the young lads and girls on the street. Our police officer will not have to pace out distances on the street, checking mileages or determining what is a nearby place; he will simply take away the alcohol. That is the purpose of the Bill and what we want to happen.

Would people be in different relevant places if one person were in a public street and one was in a private garden to which he had unlawfully gained access? They would be in different kinds of relevant place, but they might be separated by only a few feet.

I suggest that the amendment would be most unhelpful. It would lead to lengthy arguments; it would wreck the Bill's purpose and, I suspect, make it unworkable. We must avoid allowing youngsters to retain the liquor by the simple act of passing a can across a garden wall or hotfooting it down the street. I know that my hon. Friend is keen to see the Bill on the statute book so that his constituents can benefit from it. I JPEG 7 hope, therefore, that he will agree to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Stern
: I am most grateful to my hon. Friend the Member for Castle Point for his explanations. I fear, however, that he has not entirely met the purposes of my amendments. We are dealing with two entirely different matters, and I shall deal first with relevant place.

My hon. Friend was right; the amendments are probing and they are technically deficient for the reasons that he pointed out. However, it is not satisfactory to say that because the amendment is technically deficient we must accept the arguments for what I suspect is the equally deficient wording in the Bill.

There is huge difficulty in defining what is a relevant place and what is nearby. Probing amendment No. 2 is designed to elicit whether we are giving the police the power to arrest and charge young people because they may have been drinking at some other time in some other place. I am not prepared to allow the Bill to provide that power.

The more serious question relates to clause 1(1)(b) and the confiscation of intoxicating liquor that has been purchased by an adult. Here, we are no longer talking about the involvement of any young person, except when the police constable "reasonably suspects" that an adult is buying alcohol for young persons. My hon. Friend did not like the example of alcohol purchased in Westminster for consumption in Southampton, but an example that he may find more reasonable is alcohol purchased by an adult in a supermarket in Westminster when there is a rave in Hyde Park nearby. A constable may have the reasonable suspicion that the adult is purchasing the alcohol for a young person to consume at that rave.

I accept that there are difficulties of legal definition. Indeed, I may need to call on my hon. Friend the Minister to assist me in the matter, as the Bill nowhere describes what makes it reasonable for a police constable to believe that alcohol is being purchased by one person for consumption by another. We may be setting up a feast for lawyers, who will seek to determine whether the constable had reasonable belief at the time and place considered in the court some months afterwards. I am reluctant to import such a process into the law. Given that we are talking about giving constables the power to confiscate private property from adults on suspicion, we are entitled to seek a slightly tighter definition in the Bill of the circumstances in which adults can be forced to give up their property.

Dr. Spink
: I am grateful to my hon. Friend for raising that issue, which is the essence of the Bill. There are few occasions for us in the House to reinforce parental responsibility, to put the control of youngsters back with the parents, where it belongs, and to take it away from the police and the courts. I see the Bill as a measure to help the youngsters themselves, rather than the wider community, although it will do that too.

We cannot forget the link to drugs, which is one of the great dangers of our time, and the protection that we need to give young people. Parental responsibility should be reinforced at all possible times. As a parent, I would be appalled if I thought that someone knew that my children were getting up to mischief or putting themselves in danger, but had not formally told me on each occasion, so that I could exercise my duty and responsibility to control and guide them.

I hope that all members of the Committee agree that parents have a vital role to play in ensuring that children 12 grow up in a responsible way and with a responsible attitude to drinking, in particular. I know that the vast majority of parents would be appalled to discover that their children were involved in drinking binges in public places, but I know that many of the children of my constituents are because I go out on the streets in my constituency. Recently, I have been taking round radio and television crews every weekend. I know exactly where to find the children. Those youngsters tell me that they agree with the Bill. They will not like having their drink taken away, but they know why the Bill has been introduced and agree with it.

The other day, I asked a 12-year-old who was drinking a can of lager, "What would your dad say if he knew?" He replied, "He'd kill me." The boy was exaggerating; I do not think that the father would have killed him, but he would have taken action to control him. Under the Bill, that father would know, as he should, what his 12-year-old son was doing, so that he could stop him from becoming a 14-year-old drug user, a 16-year-old delinquent and a 20-year-old criminal with no life in front of him—with a destroyed life. Alcohol plus young people in public places is a recipe for disaster, and we must do everything to prevent it.

The "Do you know where your children are?" campaign that was run in Weymouth last year gave us the information that we needed about parental control. It dealt with 139 children, whose parents were informed about what they were doing. Only eight of the 139 have come to the notice of the police a second time. That suggests that parents are, by the large, responsible. They want to and will do the right thing and control their children—all they need is the information, which gives them the power to take the right action. We can give them that power; it is our duty to give them that information so that they can carry out their responsibilities more effectively.

The Bill properly avoids criminalising young people. Its aim is to keep young people out of the criminal justice system, thereby saving police and court time as well as saving the young people the problem of having to live with a record. It is about teaching young people to behave responsibly. That must start and end in the family—in the home with the parents—and we are failing if it takes place in the courts with the police.

One strand of the Bill is to enable the police to confiscate alcohol when they see youngsters drinking it in public places. The power is discretionary; the police need not use it if they think it inappropriate. However, I hope that they will use it as a rule, as it is one of the best bits of the Bill and one of the most essential measures that we can provide.

There may be occasions when it would wholly irresponsible for the police not to inform the parents. On Second Reading, I gave the example of a group of 80 youngsters, including nine-year-olds, getting tanked up on alcohol in a school playing field. I would have been absolutely furious if I had been the parent of one of those nine-year-olds and not been told by the police who had caught them so that I could control little Johnnie, help him along the right way and correct his actions. I would have thought that the police were taking too much on themselves and taking away my ability to control my own children.
