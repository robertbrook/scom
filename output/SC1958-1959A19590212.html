Mr. R. H. S. Crossman
: I beg to move, in page 1, line 13, to leave out from the beginning to the end of line 10 on page 2.

It might be convenient if I first told the Committee what our aim is in this Amendment. The effect of the Amendment would be to substitute a uniform flat rate system of contribution, with neither ceiling nor floor, and apply it not only to pensions but to all social service contributions and benefits.

We are taking the present Government scheme, which is a mongrel combination of the flat rate system and the graded system, and seeking to knock out the flat rate element altogether. That is the effect of the five Amendments which we are introducing. We delete the provision introducing any reference to the flat rate and at the end we leave the figure of 4¼ per cent. and make that the substantial proposal. I would point out that that would mean that no Schedule would be required, and we assume that we should have to delete the Schedule as well.

We are proposing on this Amendment to discuss the whole basic principle of the flat rate as against graded contributions. I should like to make two comments before I come to my main argument.

First, we would have infinitely preferred in our own proposal to limit graded contributions solely to old age pensions. We found it impossible in the terms of this Bill to re-write it to suit us, and we had to deal with the Bill as it is and make a practicable Amendment. If we want JPEG 5 to have universal graded contributions in the Bill we cannot limit them to old age pensions, and they have to be extended to sickness and unemployment benefit. Of course this is not Labour Party policy, and we are putting it forward today because we wish to discuss in principle the whole issue of the graded contribution as against the fiat rate contribution. Much the same applies to our decision to leave the 4¼ per cent. as the amount, of course. We realise that if we were to switch to graded contributions for all social services we should not be able to get the kind of rate of benefit which we should like to see for old age pensions from a 4¼ per cent. contribution spread over all the other services.

However, we thought it wise to leave this figure untouched and we have made calculations which indicate that even if we were calculating for the whole of our social security benefits, graded contributions levied on all incomes from top to bottom, without ceiling or floor, would be completely adequate to cover the benefits and give the Minister an amount of money above what he would get from the basic flat rate plus the grading.

I now come to the principle which we are discussing, namely the question of the flat rate. I think that the Committee will have Jess disagreement about the principle than it would have had two years ago. In the simple and admirable explanation of the Government scheme published by the Conservative Party there is a very interesting section on page 5 in answer to the question:

"How will the scheme help those workers not covered by occupational schemes … ? "

The answer is:

"The new scheme makes it possible for these workers to start earning a pension more in line with their earnings before retirement."

There is then this striking sentence:

"Nothing like that is possible with a flat rate scheme under which nobody can pay more (or receive more) than the lower paid workers can afford."

I think that we shall also get agreement from the other side of the Committee that what is wrong with the flat rate scheme is that it makes it impossible ever to reach an adequate level of pension. It is pegged to what the lowest paid worker can afford. So any flat rate scheme cannot be far above the poverty line.

6 It is worth while asking why it was that when the Beveridge scheme and the whole of our social security arrangements were talked about in 1944 the flat rate was accepted to easily. I think it was because we were looking back to the poverty and mass unemployment of the thirties, and we had our sights fixed very low. Everyone felt that it would be a saving miracle to have even a little for everybody—fair shares in adversity.

It is, I think, a very encouraging factor that as the result of the growing prosperity since the war we have been able to raise our sights and we now realise that in dealing with old-age pensions, what we require is a contribution rate and benefit rate linked to wages. This, I think, is essential if we are to achieve in old-age pensions what we want.

What do we mean by a subsistence old-age pension? Subsistence cannot be defined as exactly the same in £ s. d. for everyone. What we mean by subsistence is the smallest possible decline in our purchasing power when we retire from work; not the catastrophic fall in the standard of living which must come if all that we have is a flat rate pension. Roughly there is a 75 per cent. fall in the standard of living of the average worker if that is all he gets. I think that there is considerable common ground on both sides of the Committee on the principle that pensions must be wage and salary related.

What puzzles me is how the Minister can defend introducing the principle of graded contributions and graded benefits and then limit it at the bottom and at the top, cutting it off so that it applies only to a wage and salary band from £9 to £15 a week. What is the defence for a Bill, which goes so far as to admit the principle of grading and then applies it in this extraordinary way?

The result, in our view, of what the Government are doing is that the new graded pension will not be available where it is most needed. We have no doubt whatever—I think that there is no disagreement in the Committee on this—that, by and large, the better-off members of the community have for a long time looked after their old age with considerable skill, whether they do it individually by insurance policies or collectively by superannuation or top hat policy.

JPEG 7 If we look at the spread of the occupational pension scheme, we find that it is first the executives in a firm who get it and then it spreads to the industrial workers. The problem of the better-off was in the process of being solved by private occupational pension schemes. The gap arises in that most of the lower paid workers have no possibility or prospect of occupational pension schemes from private sources. We are looking forward to some growth of occupational schemes in the next ten years but it is unlikely that more than half of the employed workers will be in occupational schemes at the end of ten years. Even then, all the lowest paid workers will be left out. If nearly all the lowest paid workers are left out of occupational pension schemes on the private side, how can the Government defend leaving out everyone under £9 a week from the benefits of the new State graded pension scheme?

That is the essence of the Government's plan, to leave the flat rate pension and contributions for everyone under £9 a week. That excludes not only nearly all women workers but also many agricultural workers. The agricultural worker is an example of someone who needs Government help, yet the agricultural worker, unless he is lucky and earns more than £9 a week, is excluded from this scheme altogether. He merely pays a flat rate contribution for a flat rate benefit.

I am discussing the principle hereof excluding the lowest paid worker. The Government scheme leaves out the people who most need Government support and assistance in superannuation. This is why we feel that this Bill will do so little to bridge the gap, the gap of the two nations in old age, the gap between those with occupational schemes in addition to national insurance pensions and those without. That gap is widening. I do not see how the Government scheme, even if the contributions were better, would very much help us to narrow that gap when millions of workers are excluded from it because they do not earn more than £9 a week.

Let us turn from the bottom to the top of the Government plan. Here is my second question. We have not had an adequate explanation from the Minister as to why it is that wages and salaries 8 above the £15 band are excluded from contribution. Here I should like to quote the figures which we worked out for ourselves in a very rough and ready way as to what would be the effect of bringing in these people. Our Amendment would bring them all in. If we introduce an Amendment under which everyone pays 4¼ per cent. we can see the difference between the Goverment's pension scheme on the basis of a flat rate contribution plus grading from £9 to £15 and a simple graded scheme on the basis of 4¼ per cent. from everyone.

We have worked out figures very roughly. I hesitate to put them before the Minister as I know he is sceptical about figures, and I hope that if they are inaccurate he will correct them for us. We have put the Government scheme and ours side by side. Dealing first of all with self-employed persons, and taking 1961, if we are right the Government scheme will be collecting £402 million while under our scheme the Government would collect from the employers £500 million; and whereas the insured persons would pay £362 million under the Government scheme, they would pay £475 million under our scheme.

Then we have made calculations for the self-employed, on the assumption that they are brought in, for later we are going to move a new Clause in which we shall propose the inclusion of self-employed persons. If those are included, under our scheme the amount brought in will be £115 million in contributions whereas the Government scheme produces only £32 million where there is the flat rate benefit. In 1961 the total collected under our scheme would be £1,096 million and under the Government scheme £802 million. In 1971 our scheme would produce £1,316 million while the Government scheme would produce £1,015 million; and in 1981 our total would be £1,617 against £1,219 under the Government scheme. We have made similar calculations for employers and employed and for self-employed persons for those various dates and we should like to put those on record and to ask that the figures shall be looked at later to see whether we are correct. We cannot expect an answer today.

There is no doubt that if these figures are approximately accurate they indicate JPEG 9 the astonishing increase in income achieved by including in the scheme incomes above £15 per week. Once those incomes are included we get that big difference. We make our top limit £2,000—four times the average national wage, If we add that to the Government income we really begin to collect an amount which enables us to pay adequate benefit. What interests us most of all is to elicit from Her Majesty's Government why people with £16, £17 and £18 and up to £47 per week are excluded from their scheme. Is there an explanation of that in the Conservative Party's own pamphlet? Here is the answer given to the general public on page 7 of that pamphlet:

"Why does the graduated contribution not extend to earnings above £15 a week?"

The reply given was:

"The object of the new scheme is … to make better provision for old age in the light of our rising national prosperity. But it would not be right for a State scheme to make people contribute more for their old age than is reasonably necessary. For that would amount to dictating to a person how to spend his money during his working life. Such State dictation would be rightly resented and would undermine people's sense of responsibility for their own affairs. A person may wish to make private provision for his own old age."

The more I look at that answer the more curious it is because there is no doubt that dictation is imposed upon those with earnings from £9 to £15 per week and, as we shall show, the amount levied by the State on the £9 per week person is an outrage in view of the benefit he gets. Yet it is a compulsory levy. According to Conservative philosophy, that is no infringement of liberty. Yet, suddenly, above £15 per week this levy becomes an outrage on human liberty. That is so completely baffling to us and we should like to ask the right hon. Gentleman what happens at the level of £16 per week to make compulsory contributions an infringement of liberty.

I notice that private occupational schemes do not feel it a great infringement of liberty to inflict contributions on people earning over £15 per week. On the contrary, it is the higher-paid people on whom there is imposed sometimes a 5 per cent. and sometimes a 10 per cent. levy on income, as a condition of service. That is regarded as excellent, healthy, and non-inflationary. It is the perfect thing we all desire—when it is done by I.C.I.; but when it is done by 10 the Government we are told that it is an infringement of liberty. We do not understand why a national scheme of this kind should be considered such an infringement when a scheme, possibly more ambitious, levied on upper ranges of income is regarded as in conformity with freedom.

In our view the State should not do the total saving for old age, and I do not think that the half pay for the average worker which we want is by any means all which some would desire. If one could get to two-thirds by private savings, so much the better. We are not pretending that the State ought to do everything, but we are saying that the State should provide a firm foundation; and the kind of foundation at which we aim is universal half-pay on retirement. That, roughly speaking, is the sort of thing which should be done through a State superannuation scheme, if it is not already provided by a private scheme. If that is done, those at the bottom can be helped by making the top level pay its share; for it is quite clear to me that the real reason for the unsatisfactory Government benefit rates is the exclusion of those with wages and salaries above £15 per week. By excluding those people Her Majesty's Government have made it impossible to provide any adequate rate of benefit. It is simply a method of making the State scheme an inadequate, second-rate scheme.

This combination of flat rate plus a little grading proposed by Her Majesty's Government seems to get the worst of every possible world. It does not keep those who most need help. It does not make those who are well off pay then-fair share. It provides benefit which has no relation to contribution, even on the middle ranges. But it transfers the burden of paying the existing flat rate old-age pensions on to the shoulders of the middle range, those with salaries of from £9 to £15 per week.

If I could sum up this discussion of the principle—and on this Amendment we are dealing chiefly with principle and not details—it seems to me that Her Majesty's Government and ourselves differ on two main issues. We assert that a scheme of this kind should be universal and should bring in everybody. If we are to have grading, then everybody should JPEG 11 be graded and all incomes from the bottom to the top should be graded. That is my first point—how many shall be brought in.
