Mr. Graham Page
: When we adjourned I was starting to raise a number of points on which I am sure the Minister will be able to assist us.

The first point to which I want to draw attention is that in the penultimate line of subsection (1) one sees the words:

"…if the Board are satisfied".

With those words in, the whole clause seems to be left to the discretion of the Board, which shows how unconvincing was the argument against our proposals in some of the amendments that the Board should have discretion in a number of cases.

There was, however, a difference between what we were proposing at that time in the amendments and the discretion given to the Board here. The discretion given to the Board in subsection (1) seems to be a discretion as to whether facts are facts That is to say, if certain facts are put forward on the conditions set out in subsection (2) the Board can merely express an opinion that it is not satisfied that those facts are true and no appeal will arise at all from that.

The chargeable person cannot appeal if the Board merely says it is not satisfied, unless, of course, it decided it on a completely wrong basis of the colour of the applicant's hair or something like that. In the normal way there will be no appeal from the Board's decision that it was satisfied or dissatisfied on any particular point of fact.

We discussed this earlier, and I ask Treasury Ministers to see whether, having given an undertaking on a previous occasion that these words would be revised so that at least one could appeal from the Board to the Commissioners, they could be looked at again in the context of subsection (1).

996 I come now to the point about the relevant interest—again referred to in subsection (1)—having been acquired. As I understand it, it can be acquired at any time. We are not here tied to 12th September 1974, or any other date except that, of course, when two years have run out after the Act comes into operation we are dealing only with acquisitions after the Act comes into opeation, that is to say from the appointed day. Having looked at the last line on page 27 and the first two lines on page 28, I cannot imagine a more difficult way of expressing what I think is the intention.

"and, for the purposes of paragraph ( b) above, in any case where the owner acquired the relevant interest before the appointed day, it shall be assumed that the appointed day fell (and this Act was in force) before the time of his acquisition."

That sounds to me very like Dunne's theory of relativity of time, and I suppose one could explain it by the limerick:

"There was a young lady called Bright Who travelled much faster than light; She set out one day In a relative way And arrived back the preceding night."

That seems to be what is intended by the two lines:

"…it shall be assumed that the appointed day fell (and this Act was in force) before the time of his acquisition."

Could that not be expressed a little more simply? It does not matter what time the acquisition took place, and we do not want to have a fiction of this sort.

Secondly, may we have some assurance about the appointed day? I turn at once to the interpretation clause, Clause 46 and see that

"'appointed day' means such day as the Treasury may by order made by statutory instrument appoint",

which statutory instrument is not laid before the House, nor is it subject to any procedure in the House. One assumes that that is the day of commencement of the Act. We have been informed that 1st August is the probable date.

Under the provisions of Clause 46—and I apply that to Clause 18 about which we are talking—that appointed day could be any day. In fact, it could be some past day, certainly in accordance with the theory of relativity of time, as embodied in Clause 18. May we, therefore, have an assurance at this stage that the appointed day will be in JPEG 997 the future from now when the Bill comes into operation, and not some fictitious day for the purpose of different clauses in the Bill?

I turn to subsection (3) about which we had some considerable discussion on amendments, I do not intend to repeat that discussion. The Minister said at the time that he was resisting an amendment to make it more specific that there would be directions to the tax offices about the operation of this subsection, that is about the Board asking for further information if it was not satisfied with the information that had been given to it before it rejected the application.

I do not think that directions to tax offices will be sufficient. The public must know what the practice is to be. To that extent, I believe that a better way of informing the public is by a statement of practice rather than merely by directions through tax offices, which may not reach the public who have to try to operate under subsection (3). May we have something between the Board and the tax offices—not merely direction between the palace at Middlesbrough and the inspectors of taxes throughout the country, but something that is issued so that the public know what is happening?

Subsection (3) states

"…if the project were to be begun forthwith".

I am sure that the Minister has not a clue what is meant by "forthwith". I do not know what it means here. Does it mean on the very day? The subsection says:

"…the Board shall notify the owner whether, if the project were to be begun forthwith, they would or would not be satisfied…"

Does that really mean immediately at that time? Or how is the Board to interpret the word "forthwith"? I hope that that will go into a statement of practice.

I move down the page to subsection (5)( c), which says:

"immediately before the beginning of that project, there is a deemed disposal of the interest to which the notification relates".

From reading that on its own one might assume that before the project is started one must get rid of the property. But I turn back to Clause 2(1), where we find that a deemed disposal is assumed to take place

"Immediately before a project of material development."

998 It is a little confusing when read in the context of this subsection. It seems to be a matter of applying the theory of relativity again, that the person gets rid of the property and then carries out the project of material development.

Those matters come within the clause, but two matters are not mentioned in the clause, and I should like information on how they are affected. I shall take together partnerships and trusteeships. Although elsewhere in the Bill we are told that each partner is assessed separately, I assume that the partnership, and indeed a trust with trustees, will be treated as one entity or unit under the clause, so that the partnership, or the trustees for the time being, who happen to have applied for this clearance will if the partners or trustees change, find that the benefit of the notification will not exactly run with the land but will run at least with the partnership or the trust. That is not mentioned in the clause. It may be brought in by other clauses relating to partnerships and trusts.

The other matter which does not appear specifically in the clause but might come in by implication, I suggest, is that it is only the interests of the person who applies for the clearance which are affected. There may be other persons with interests in the land who will not get the relief under the clause. In Clause 2 we see that there are major interests which can be affected by a deemed disposal, and I assume that it is possible that under Clause 18 there will be interests other than that which was acquired and in respect of which a development is intended within the two-year period, which may be subject to the tax. Again, I use the phrase that it runs with the person seeking the clearance and not necessarily with the land itself—that is, that it does not affect all interests in the land, only the interests of the person who has put in the request for a clearance.

I hope that I have explained my problems sufficiently clearly for the Minister to deal with them. If not, and I have swung them on him too fast, I hope that he will let us have his reply later.
