Holocaust Denial Bill
Clause 1
HOLOCAUST DENIAL

10.30 am

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Mike Gapes (Ilford, South)
: First, I must draw attention to the fact that the Bill was introduced to the House as a ten-minute Bill on 29 January 1997. Its intention was to make it a criminal offence to claim in writing or orally that the policy of genocide against the Jewish people committed by Nazi Germany did not occur. The Bill received it First Reading on 29 January, it was published on 24 February and had its Second Reading, without opposition, on 28 February.

I explained my reasons for introducing the Bill on 29 January when I made it clear that many other countries in Europe, including Germany, Austria, Switzerland, France and Lithuania have already legislated against Holocaust denial and that such proposals are being considered by other countries. The difficulty is that our legislation is based on the Public Order Act 1986. Under that Act, material that is deemed to be offensive and which incites racial hatred can be prosecuted against, but such measures do not apply to Holocaust denial material—something that I shall say more about later.

Critics of the Bill have said that it is not necessary to have a specific Bill on Holocaust denial. That criticism has been met by the fact that the Bill is proposing an amendment to the Public Order Act 1986, whereby it will come within the scope of that Act's provisions and be covered by the safeguards and penalties of that Act.

I have been pleased by the wide interest and publicity that has been shown in the Bill. It has received extensive coverage on radio, television and in the press. I have had an interesting post bag. I have received letters of support from Holocaust survivors and their close relatives and I have also had my share of vicious, nasty, anti-Semitic hate mail. I am not Jewish, but it is interesting that most of the people who write such hate mail assume that one has to be Jewish to be concerned about the Holocaust and anti-Semitism.

Some critics of the Bill are well intentioned and have argued that it was necessary to introduce proposals that would amend the 1986 Act. That 4 argument has been met by the Bill. I refer, in particular, to the criticisms that were made last month by Mr. Anthony Lerman of the Institute for Jewish Policy Research and Geoffrey Bindman, who wrote an article about it in the Jewish Chronicle last week—criticisms that, I believe, are met to some extent by the Bill.

Some people wrote to me asking why the original, short version of the Bill referred only to the policy of genocide against the Jewish people. I believe that that criticism is answered by the drafting of clause 1, which refers to

"the policy of genocide against the Jewish people and other similar crimes against humanity committed by Nazi Germany".

We should not in any way minimise the evil crimes committed against the Jews, gipsies, homosexuals, Serbs or other opponents of Nazism in the areas occupied by the Nazis and their allies during the 1940s. It is estimated—it is difficult to be precise—that 11 million people died in the Holocaust at the hands of the Nazis and their allies. Six million of those were Jews, who were systematically murdered on the basis of an ideology and what came to be the search for a so-called final solution.

I draw the Committee's attention to another matter that has arisen since the Bill was published. During Prime Minister's questions on 11 February, the hon. Member for Harrow, East (Mr. Dykes) raised the general issue of Holocaust denial and mentioned the Bill. The Prime Minister said:

"It might be right to introduce a specific offence…However, I am dubious about the practical effect of doing so…At present, that material is subject to prosecution under public order law".—[ Official Report, 11 February 1997; Vol. 290, c. 137.]

Following that statement, I wrote to the Prime Minister on 14 February. I said:

"I was intrigued by your reference to holocaust denial material being 'subject to prosecution under public order law'. I am attaching a copy of a letter from the Attorney General to the Rt. Hon David Hunt MP dated 19 July 1996 in which he makes clear the view of the Crown Prosecution Service that although Holocaust Denial material is regarded as 'undoubtedly offensive and its content untruthful it is not "insulting" within the meaning of section 19' of the Public Order Act 1986. This conclusion is confirmed in the letter from the Solicitor General to the Rt. Hon David Hunt MP dated 26 September 1996.


In view of this, I am somewhat perplexed. If it is the case that Holocaust denial material is subject to prosecution under existing public order law, why are there no prosecutions of such material on that basis and why are the Law officers writing letters stating that prosecutions will not be brought because the material is not defined as 'insulting'?

JPEG 5

I would personally be very happy if Public Order Legislation in either its existing or an amended form was to be used against Holocaust Denial material. My purpose in bringing forward my bill was to highlight the loophole in our existing legislation and get this loophole closed leading to effective legal action against Holocaust denial material in future."

Last week, I received a reply to that letter from the Prime Minister. In his letter of 4 March, the Prime Minister stated:

"I have to say that I am not convinced that it would be the right approach to change the legislation in this area, nor that such legislation would have any practical effect. There is no evidence to suggest that the British people are taken in by the lies of those who seek to deny the Holocaust, and Holocaust denial receives very little mainstream attention in this country."

We could argue about whether that is right or wrong, but the Prime Minister continued:

"You asked about public order legislation. As you know Part III of the Public Order Act 1986 covers the circulation of racially inflammatory material. Prosecutions can be mounted against those responsible for material which is threatening, abusive or insulting and which is intended or likely to stir up racial hatred."

In my letter, I referred to a leaflet called "66 Questions and Answers on the Holocaust", which was circulated to schools in Hemel Hempstead in 1996. In his letter, the Prime Minister stated:

"You cite the case of the leaflet…As you know, after careful consideration, the CPS concluded that this particular material was not 'insulting' within the meaning of the Act."

It was claimed in that material that the Holocaust was a fabrication, that there were no gas chambers, that they were built after the war, and that Jewish people were seeking international sympathy. It was particularly obnoxious material.

The Prime Minister continued:

"The Attorney-General has given his consent to prosecution in other cases where the material which formed the substance of the prosecution contained Holocaust denial as part of a wider antisemitic message."

Therefore, I assume that the Prime Minister's and the Government's position is that material which is generally anti-Semitic is liable to prosecution, but that material that denies the holocaust is not liable to prosecution. That is an interesting distinction. Does that mean that prosecutions are taking place against anti-Semitic material?

I have done some research and contacted the Library. The legislation is specific to England and Wales, but I shall deal with Scotland later and perhaps other hon. Members will refer to Northern Ireland. Since 1979, there have been only about 80 prosecutions involving any form of incitement to racial hatred in England and Wales. That figure includes prosecutions involving material against all sorts of ethnic minorities as well as the publication of anti-Semitic material. On average, there are about three or four prosecutions a year.

In an answer to a parliamentary question asked by the hon. Member for Crewe and Nantwich (Mrs. Dunwoody), the Attorney-General stated that three people were prosecuted for incitement to racial hatred in 1992; two people in 1993; two people in 1995; seven people in 1995; and four people to October 1996. He stated: 6

"During the last five years, I have declined to give my consent in a total of two cases.—[ Official Report, 31 October, 1996; Vol. 284, c. 181.]

So, there are not many prosecutions in general. Given the amount of generalised anti-Semitic and other racial hatred material that I and other people have drawn to the attention of the police and the Crown Prosecution Service, why are there so few prosecutions? We must question how the Public Order Act 1986 is being implemented.

My father arrived in this country in 1940 as a refugee from the Netherlands. He left behind an extended family in Amsterdam. On returning to the Netherlands in 1945, he went back to the streets where his family and all their relations had lived; he was met by an eerie silence. They had all gone, never to return.

To give the Committee an example of the impact of the Holocaust, I shall give but one example of the kind of event that the documents we wish to prevent try to deny. At the age of two, a cousin of my father's was picked up in the middle of the night and taken away in a canvas-covered lorry with his mother, his father and his grandparents. His mother was pregnant. They were held in what could be called literally a concentration camp in north Holland, prior to deportation on those dreadful trains to the east. His mother gave birth and, because she suffered from a minor but highly contagious ailment, she and her two sons were taken away to a local hospital under German guard so that the contagion would not spread within the camp. When the trains were ready to go east, the Germans came to collect the mother and her two infants. A Dutch nurse had the presence of mind to give the German security guards the dead body of a two-year-old boy in place of my father's cousin. The nurse strapped the child to her back underneath her cloak, taping his mouth to keep him silent, and rode out of the hospital on a bicycle. She tok him to a relative of hers in the countryside who was a woodcutter. By chance, after the war my father spotted that child's name in a register of displaced persons and managed to find him. I had the privilege of meeting him—he is now a 50-year-old architect—0a few years ago in Israel.

I have the dubious honour of keeping a register of my extended family. Fifty-six members of my family perished in the Holocaust. That is not a figment of imagination: the records of Yad Yashem in Israel document all those deaths. I have documents showing that relations of mine perished in such places as Auschwitz, Sobibor, Mauthausen and Buchenwald. There is no doubt that the Holocaust happened.

It is a gross travesty that any document can be passed on to the young people of the United Kingdom that would give any idea that the depths to which humanity can sink simply did not happen. What happened is a lesson to us all; it should never happen again. I fully support the Bill because misrepresentation that has racial consequences should not be allowed to stand.
