It is all a question of balance between the advantages and disadvantages.

I should like to tell the Committee a little about the other procedures which are involved in bankruptcy proceedings because that will help us to get the right balance. If the public examination were the only or even the main way of eliciting information, it would be a very serious step to do away with it. But in fact the processes are such that even before one reaches the stage of public examination a number of other steps have been taken, which in any case which did not have unusual features would probably elicit most of what needed to be elicited, and would certainly give the experts in the case the " feel " of whether this was a case in which a public examination was necessary.

We know that it all starts with a bankruptcy notice, and if that is not complied with there is the petition and a hearing. If the hearing is successful, a receiving order is made. From then on, the person against whom the receiving order is made is very much obliged to put himself and his information at the disposal of the Official Receiver. This is one of the respects in which bankruptcy is so much more effective than administration orders, to which we shall come later, because JPEG 215 in bankruptcy the Official Receiver has such a strong position vis-a-vis the bankrupt once the receiving order has been made.

In case hon. Gentlemen are not aware of it, the first thing that happens is that an examiner, who spends his whole time examining, then examines the bankrupt, or person against whom the receiving order has been made. I am assured that that is always a very thorough examination. At the same time, the Official Receiver has advertised for other creditors, and though they cannot take part in the examination, when they see the advertisement calling for other creditors to inform the Official Receiver of the money owed to them, they can write to him and I am told that he pays great attention to all such communications. The examiner then follows up any leads given in those communications. The likely next step is a first meeting of creditors, and at this there will be considerable scope for discussing the sort of matters which should have a bearing on whether it is necessary for the matter to proceed to a public examination. After that, the Official Receiver applies for an adjudication under Section 18, so that is another occasion on which the effects are gone into, albeit perhaps not very fully.

It is not as though those of us who are saying that public examinations should be ruled out in a substantial number of cases are advocating a line of action which would cut down the investigatory processes as much as one might imagine if one thought that was the only substantial occasion on which there was an opportunity to carry out an investigation into the assets and conduct of the bankrupt. As I hope I have indicated to the Committee in that brief resume, there are in the ordinary processes of the bankruptcy all those stages in which relevant information can be obtained and considered.

    I now turn to the very normal part of the provisions of this new clause and I will also try to deal with some of the basic thinking which has caused us to come to the conclusion that this is a desirable course to adopt.

    Subsection (4) provides for all bankrupts who have been adjudicated bankrupt more than five years before the coming into force of this clause to be absolutely discharged on the coming into force of this clause, or on the 10th anniversary of the date of the adjudication, whichever is the later, provided that they have not, by that relevant date, already been absolutely discharged or had the adjudication annulled.

    The fact is that there are something like 100,000 undischarged bankrupts at the present time, or at least that will be the figure when the Bill comes into force. Those whose bankruptcies are less than five years old when Clause 6 comes into force will be dealt with by review under that clause. Their bankruptcies will be looked at for the purpose of discharge during the sixth year following the date of the order of adjudication.

    However, it is the others which present the real problem—that is to say, those over five years old. They are too many for review to be a realistic proposition. The only way in which it could be done JPEG 255 would be over a very long period of time and that in itself is unsatisfactory.

    Therefore, the choice that lay before me in this respect was either that they could be left to apply for discharge under Section 26 of the Bankruptcy Act 1914—and there is an argument for that because, after all, they went bankrupt under the old law and maybe they should seek discharge under the provisions of the old law as well. Alternatively, they could be granted an automatic discharge after a fixed period. We have to face it that there will be some rogues caught up in that, but I think one should have regard primarily to the vast majority of these people who are left under very serious and at the moment perpetual civil disabilities.
