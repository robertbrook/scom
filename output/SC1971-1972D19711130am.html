Mr. John Silkin
: May I briefly sum up what I was trying to say on Thursday last? I think I have taken the entire Committee most of the way with me on the basis that a reform of local government requires that there should be no political remoteness at least between the electorate and the councillor. I endeavoured to show that under the existing system, with its multiplicity of local authorities, which we have inherited in a patchwork way over the centuries, there could still be remoteness—a lack of communication between the electorate and between the councillors. In a sense, neither the unitary system nor the present proposals set out to remedy this particular basis, with the proviso that in the Maud proposals the community councils were a reasonable basis for complaints and awareness, and in that sense went some way to provide communication between the electorate and their local authorities.

Having said that, I had also taken the point of view that while there are those who complain of geographical remoteness in the Maud proposals, and the unitary system, there are occasions—some of hon. Friends have mentioned them—when there is as much, if not more, remoteness in the present proposals. However, while I disapprove of geographical remoteness, it is not as important as political remoteness.

That was the basis of my argument last Thursday. I should, however, like to mention one final point—a final method of evaluating all these factors. Do we believe that an all-purpose authority, with all services under its control, is more likely to serve the community, the local electorate and local interests, than a multiplicity of authorities where powers are divided and unclear, where the functions are divided and not clear, and where the authorities themselves are diverse and do not, as far as the ordinary individual is concerned, possess the clean logic that a unitary authority would possess?

Reform of local government is one of those ideas that everyone in theory supports, provided that the lash falls on someone else's back. Hon. Members will know that there is, and has been for some considerable time, a Royal Commission sitting to consider that question. Given that we are trying to lay down a lasting reorganisation of local government in the Bill, have we the right to dismiss any tier of government or any organisation of government at local level 58 before we have seen the proposals of the Crowther Commission for the reorganisation of provincial or regional government—whatever one may wish to call it—that tier of government which is larger than purely local and which will be reported on next year? I believe that we have no such right, and I hope the Minister will not, therefore, have a closed mind to the desirability or otherwise of unitary authority.

As the Minister of State at the Welsh Office will know, this question has been raised in a Welsh context on a number of occasions. He has briefly sought to show that because my right hon. Friend the Member for Huyton (Mr. Harold Wilson) said three years ago that there was no need to hold up local government reform until the report of the Crowther Commission, that is therefore conclusive of the matter. Of course, it is not because time is everything. The analogy I gave in the Welsh Grand Committee was: one takes more trouble to hurry for a bus if one knows there is not another bus for an hour than if one knows another bus will be along in two minutes' time.

Basically, what we are doing now in 1971, is to pre-judge the Crowther Commission Report by no more than a few months. Therefore, in our haste to push through any measure of reorganisation, we may be pushing through the wrong measure, because it is quite clear that the Royal Commission—I hasten to add, the Welsh Royal Commission—in analysing this position, says that boundaries cannot reasonably be divorced from functions or from finance. That, quite simply, is the problem here. Until it is known what functions a provincial council will have, until it is known what functions will therefore reside in the local authorities, the boundaries which the local authorities ought to have cannot possibly be known nor can it be known whether they ought to be organised on a unitary basis or on a two-tier basis. To say that we can judge this in advance of the Crowther Report is negative, and I think it would doom this Bill to early and fairly constant revision, in order to allow it room to expand in the next half century.

Indeed, the Secretary of State for the Environment, speaking on 16th November, seemed to regard some of the functions at present proposed for local government as being more appropriate to regional government. Dealing with the Birmingham overspill—and here we come JPEG 59 back to the point raised by my hon. Friend the Member for Bromsgrove (Mr. Terry Davis) and to other points that were made on the West Midlands—the Secretary of State said:

"I accept that such a regional approach does entail that the region as a whole has to cope with this problem".—[OFFICIAL REPORT, 16th November, 1971; Vol. 11, c. 238.]

In other words, what he is saying is that there must be a measure of regional government to ensure that land use planning is as efficient as possible. Yet he does not follow out the logic of his own argument, and say that until we know the pattern of such regional government we cannot possibly seek satisfactorily to allocate the functions contained in this Bill.

I hope, therefore, that the Minister will consider again this particular problem, because if boundaries depend on functions, and if functions, as the Secretary of State for the Environment suggests, must depend to a certain extent on what Crowther says, then we cannot decide here whether unitary or two-tier authorities are best until we know what the pattern of provincial government in England and Wales will be. I think that this problem is no less relevant to England than it is to Wales, although we have, as I have said, already made representations upon that point.

The Bill as it stands leads to all sorts of inconsistencies. England and Wales are indissolubly linked so far as local government reform is concerned. We are aiming to do away with the separation of the big towns from their hinterland. I remind the Minister that the Welsh proposal separates Cardiff exactly from its hinterland. There is, therefore, no principle of universal application, even in this Bill. If my right hon. and hon. Friends see fit to divide the Committee upon this unitary principle, I will vote with them, not because I think that for Wales unitary authorities are necessarily the right solution—they may or may not be, depending upon the pattern—but because I believe that it would be premature and rash to pre-judge the argument between unitary and two-tier authorities before we have the report of the Crowther Commission.
