Mr. LOVAT-FRASER
: I wish strongly to enforce the remarks of the hon. Mem- JPEG 1297 ber for Westhoughton (Mr. Rhys Davies) with regard to the inadequacy of this Clause. Many of us are profoundly disappointed that the Clause does not deal with this matter to which he has referred. It is now recognised by all who have given attention to the subject, that in order to deal with the child delinquency, there ought to be some system for the examination of children. Efforts ought to be made to find out how the child came to commit the offence with which it is charged, with a view to discovering the cause of the child's action and, dealing with the child, so that a repetition of the child's action is impossible. One of the greatest experts in this matter—I was going to say the greatest expert—is Mr. Cyril Burt, the psychologist of the London County Council. May I quote briefly some remarks of his, which bear out strongly the attitude taken up by the hon. Gentleman who has just spoken and myself? He says:

"The psychologist is a detective. But what he seeks to detect are not persons but motives. The question he has to answer is not 'who committed this offence,' but 'what made the offender attempt it.'"

He is speaking, of course, of children. He goes on to say of the psychologist:

"He learns that crime, like fever, is not a disease but a symptom. To punish the naughty child for his actions by scolding him, by whipping him, by locking him up under restraint, is like shutting a feverish patient in a chilly room in order to reduce his high temperature."

He goes on to stress the fact that it is very often lack of intelligence which causes criminal action in a child, and that the child is not responsible for lack of intelligence. Intelligence is born with a person or it is not. Mr. Burt says:

"In the hereditary equipment of the youthful criminal, the most serious handicap is not lack of morals, but lack of intelligence. Among the juvenile delinquents in London as many as 80 per cent, are below the average level in general ability. The ordinary criminal when grown up, still shows the mental age of a boy of only 10 or 11. Most rogues are also fools."

In order to deal with the situation shadowed forth in these quotations, you must have a psychologist and places, call them clinics, or observation centres, or whatever you like, where childden can be examined in order that those who have to deal with them, may do so on wise lines. Children often suffer from obscure complaints which only careful examina- 1298 tion can discover, and those obscure complaints lead them to commit offences. Mr. Alexander Paterson, one of the Prison Commissioners, speaking the other day, amused his audience by asking, "Why are so many burglars bow-legged?" He went on to say:

"His theory was that bow legs and crime were closely allied because of their mutual origin in rickets. They were two different results of the same cause, which was to be found in the over-crowded homes of poor parents living in a neighbourhood where there were few playing fields."

That is the kind of thing that must be inquired into by experts and psychologists if the child's future is to be wisely dealt with. Not long ago I had a Press cutting of a case in which a woman, speaking to a magistrate about a girl who stole, said, "The girl cannot speak the truth, because she has adenoids." There was an immense amount of truth in that. That is the sort of thing that you have to investigate. There is a well-known judge in a children's court in America who begins with every child, in trying to discover why it committed an offence, by asking about its teeth. Teeth, he says, produce indigestion, indigestion produces bad temper, bad temper produces quarrelsome parents, and quarrelsome parents produce criminal offenders. We are far behind other countries in this matter; America has been doing wonderful work for years. Some of the American universities have rendered most valuable service in the matter—California and Colorado—and even universities in our own Empire, Sydney, for example, has been co-operating in this work of psychological examination. I consider this Clause really inadequate, and I hope even at this late hour that the stony heart of the Under-Secretary will be softened and that he will really do something to meet the very general expectation that this Bill will deal with the matter of which I have spoken.

This is a mere matter of machinery. The Clause already provides that a true copy of a diverting order of an affiliation contribution shall be evidence of it. By this Amendment we extend that provision to the affiliation order on which the diverting order was based. As matters stand at the moment, it might be that an affiliation order had been made in one court and the diverting order in another, and it would be necessary to get from each court this certified true copy, although before the diverting order could have been made the affiliation order would have had to be produced in court. CLAUSE 40.
—(Appeals to quarter sessions under this Part of this Act.)

Mr. TURTON
: I beg to move, in page 30, line 11, at the end, to insert the words:

"Provided that if such court of summary jurisdiction is satisfied that the appellant is unable, through poverty, to pay the costs of an appeal the recognisance, or deposit, or other security shall not be conditioned as to the payment of such costs as may be awarded by the court of quarter sessions and the court of quarter sessions shall not order the appellant to pay costs."

The terms of the Clause are that an order made by the court of summary jurisdiction, which includes the juvenile court, may commit a child or young person to the care of a fit person, or send a child or young person to an approved JPEG 1303 school, or, inter alia, make other provisions, and in respect of these cases, the Sub-section ends up by stating that

"the child or young person or his parent or guardian … or the person required to enter into a recognisance, or the person required to contribute, … may appeal against the order to quarter sessions."

At first sight it would appear that you are giving those children a right of appeal to quarter sessions. That is a right of great importance when the Committee realise that by Clause 14, we have taken away the right of a child to demand that he shall be tried by quarter sessions instead of by the justices in the court below. It may well be that a child has a legal argument to put forward and prefers to be tried by a full court of quarter sessions rather than by a juvenile court. By this Clause you have given the right of appeal to quarter sessions, but I wish the Committee to observe the restrictions put upon that appeal. The archaic provision of the law says that there is right of appeal to quarter sessions and that the appeal shall be made within the prescribed time,

"unless it shall be provided in the prescribed manner that the appellant shall, within the prescribed time, enter into a recognisance before the court of summary jurisdiction with or without a surety or sureties, as the court may direct, to appear at the said sessions, and shall abide by the judgment of the court of appeal therein."

Any Member of this Committee would regard that as unjust. Obviously a man or child should be bound over to appear at the trial of his appeal at quarter sessions, but it goes on:

"and he shall be conditioned to pay such costs as may be awarded by the court of appeal, or the appellant may, if the court of summary jurisdiction decide, instead of entering into a recognisance, give such other security by a deposit of money or otherwise as the court shall deem sufficient."

It means, in all these cases, that not only is he bound over to appear at the court, but to be in a sufficient position to pay the costs of appeal. The court can, and usually does, make an appellant deposit a large sum of money in order to provide for the costs of the other side, should he fail in his appeal. I submit that that is a most unnecessary and unjust provision in the case of appeals by children from the juvenile court to the court of quarter sessions.

1304 I regret very much that my hon. Friend the Member for Huntingdon (Dr. Peters) is not in his place. He said that he could not be present, but he told me of his experience last week, and assured me that I could intimate it to the Committee. He was appearing last week, in the role of solicitor, in the trial of three men before a court of summary jurisdiction, and at the end of the trial the men were found guilty. They wished to appeal. My hon. Friend made an application before the justices for such an appeal, and the justices said that they would demand, as security for the appeal, a sum of £210 in respect of those three men. He objected, and the justices reconsidered the matter, and in the end graciously allowed the men to appeal, if they would put down a deposit of £105—£35 each.

Mr. TURTON

I am not asking to take away the right to bind over a child or a parent to appear at quarter sessions, because that is a reasonable and fair provision in the law. But I regard as unreasonable and unfair the provision that a child should have to pay a deposit before it can get its appeal to quarter sessions, as a sort of guarantee of the costs spent by the other side in connection 'with the appeal. It may be said that these appeals will not be used a great deal. I do not believe that they can be. At the present moment there are, when one looks at the whole system, JPEG 1305 few appeals to quarter sessions. It will be seen from the criminal statistics of 1928 that in that year there were 228 appeals to quarter sessions from courts of summary jurisdiction, and of those appeals only 100 cases failed. In 128 of the 228 cases the appeal succeeded either in part or in whole. You get injustice in all those courts of summary jurisdiction, and in this case it may be an injustice against the child or an injustice against a grown-up person in the matter of the contribution to the child or in the payment under an affiliation order. They are all cases where legal objections may well be taken, and cases where, if there is any possibility of appeal, real hardship can be occasioned to the appellant.

I think I know what the Under-Secretary will say. He will say that it is wrong to make this change in the law merely as regards children. Against that I would say that surely we ought to start by giving justice to the children, and then perhaps if it succeeds we can go on and give greater justice in respect of appeals from courts of summary jurisdiction to quarter sessions. In these appeals you do not have the difficulty that you would have if you allowed the free right of appeal. Cases of bastardy orders will not arise here. It is only in cases of poverty connected with children that appeals will be allowed. The Committee will realise that I have not made the same provision in the Amendment as was made in the Amendment which I had the good fortune to introduce earlier, where the costs of counsel and solicitor's were paid out of the rates instead of by the appellant. In this case it is fair and right that there should be no mention of the brief fees for counsel or solicitor. I hope that we shall not be briefed in all these cases. There are a number of open courts where such applications can be heard. Appeals from juvenile courts should not be a matter of legal appeals, but more in the nature of applications, perhaps, to be taken before the court of quarter sessions when it has got through the rest of its business.

I want the Under-Secretary to consider the matter well before he turns down the Amendment. He might find it difficult to make a real reform of the whole system of appeals to quarter sessions. No one would meet with more respect from all branches of the law if he did away with the archaic provisions in regard to the 1306 security for costs. We are not dealing with rich people, but with poor children who cannot possibly put down £35, or even £5. In no case is the security for costs less than £5, and a child could not pay such a sum.
