INCREASE OF RENT AND MORTGAGE INTEREST (RESTRICTIONS) BILL

OFFICIAL REPORT
CLAUSE 2.
—(Permitted increases in rent [1915. s.1 (i), (ii), (iii), and (iv.) 1919. s.2 Rep. 6–6, 25 and 26].

(1) The amount by which the increased rent of a dwelling-house to which this Act applies may exceed the standard rent shall, subject to the provisions of this Act, be as follows, that is to say:—

( c) In addition to any such amounts as aforesaid, an amount equal to fifteen per cent. of the net rent:


Provided that, except in the case of a dwelling-house to which this Act applies but the enactments repealed by this Act did not apply, the amount of such addition shall not during a period of one year after the passing of this Act exceed five per cent;


( d) In further addition to any such amounts as aforesaid—

  1. Where the dwelling-house is one to which section fourteen of the Housing, Town Planning, &c, Act, 1909, applies, or where in any other case the tenant is not under any express liability for repairs, an amount equal to twenty-five per cent. of the net rent; or
  2. Where the tenant is under an express liability for part and not the whole of the repairs, such lesser amount as may be agreed, or as may, on the application of the landlord, be determined by the county court to be fair and reasonable having regard to such liability.


(2) At any time or times after the date of any increase permitted by paragraph ( d) of the foregoing Sub-section the tenant may apply to the county court for an order sus pending such increase, and also any increase under paragraph ( c) of that Sub-section, on the ground that the house is not reasonably fit for human habitation, or is otherwise not in a reasonable state of repair.


The court on being satisfied by the production of a certificate of the sanitary authority 176 or otherwise that any such ground as aforesaid is established, and on being further satisfied that the condition of the house is not due or not wholly due to the tenant's neglect or default or breach of express agreement, shall order that the increase be suspended until the court is satisfied, on the report of the sanitary authority or otherwise, that the necessary repairs (other than the repairs, if any, for which the tenant is liable) have been executed, and on the making of such order the increase shall cease to have effect until the court is so satisfied.


(3) Any transfer to a tenant of any burden or liability previously borne by the landlord shall for the purposes of this Act be treated as an alteration of rent, and where, as the result of such a transfer, the terms on which a dwelling-house is held are on the whole less favourable to the tenant than the previous terms the rent shall be deemed to be increased, whether or not the sum periodically payable by way of rent is increased, and any increase of rent in respect of any transfer to a landlord of any burden or liability previously borne by the tenant where, as the result of such transfer, the terms on which any dwelling-house is held are on the whole more favourable to the tenant than the previous terms, shall be deemed not to be an increase of rent for the purposes of this Act.


(4) On any application to a sanitary-authority for a certificate or report under this Section a fee of one shilling shall be payable, but if the authority as the result of such application issues such a certificate as aforesaid, the tenant shall be entitled to deduct the fee from any subsequent payment of rent.


(5) For the purposes of this section the expression "rates" includes water rents and charges, and any increase in rates payable by the landlord shall be deemed to be payable by him until the rate is next demanded.

Colonel NEWMAN
: I beg to move in Sub-section (1, c), at the end, to insert the words

Provided also that tenants who could have claimed protection under the Act of April, nineteen hundred and nineteen, had they been able to use their rateable value alone as a test, shall be charged an increase of five per cent. only on their net rent for the first year under this sub-section.

The case which this Amendment is intended to cover is such an obvious injustice that I imagine the Government will have to meet it in some way or other. When passing the 1919 Act we did our business very badly in the House of Commons. We meant to enact that where the standard rent or rateable value was under a certain limit, the tenant should get the protection of the Act. We failed to do that. We have this anomaly at present. 178 You have houses under the 1915 Act, if the rent or rateable value did not on the 3rd of August, 1914, exceed the limit of £35, £30 and £26, but when you come to the 1919 Act, the position is somewhat reversed. A house is exempt from the Act if the rent or rateable value on the 3rd of August, 1914, exceeded £70, £60 or £52. So in one case the house is within the Act, if the rent or rateable value did not exceed, and in the other case the house is excluded if the rent or rateable value did exceed. That was an anomaly which was quite unjustifiable, and it was commented on by the Salisbury Committee. On page 10 of their report they remark:—

It was held in Isaacs v. Tittlebaum, 149, L.T. 84, in reference to the rental limit of £70, that rent meant the whole sum which the tenant agreed to pay as rent, and not merely the balance after deducting so much as was payable in respect of rates.


The effect of this is, supposing there are two houses exactly similar in all respects, if one was let at (say) £55, the tenant paying rates, it would be within the Acts, but if the other was let at £75, the landlord paying rates, it would not be within the Acts. We think that this anomaly should be removed, and that the protection of the Acts should be afforded to a dwelling-house, if either the rent or rateable value is less than the prescribed limit. As the law stands at present, if the standard rent exceeds that figure the house is outside the Acts, whatever its rateable value. It may be observed that in making this recommendation we are reverting to the method of fixing the limits adopted in the Act of 1915.

Imagine a man occupying a flat at a standard rent beyond £70, but an assessable value below £70. He was excluded from the 1919 Act against the wishes of Parliament. The result was that the landlord acting within his rights was able to raise that man's rent from £75 up to, we will say, £95. Now this particular occupier comes within this Bill, and the landlord will be able to raise his rent from the present £95 to whatever the limits of the Act are. That will mean, say, that his rent will be put up from £95 to £125, or in less than two years his rent will have gone up from £75 to £125. Because we have made that mistake, we want to rectify it and enact in some shape or other that the landlord shall not be able to increase that man's rent by more than 5 per cent. the first year as set out in paragraph ( c) of Clause 2. I would like the Government to give us some idea as to how they intend to justify this injustice, if they desire to justify it, which has been perpetrated under the 1919 Act.
