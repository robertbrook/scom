INCREASE OF RENT AND MORTGAGE INTEREST (RESTRICTIONS) BILL.

[OFFICIAL REPORT.]
CLAUSE 5.
—(Restriction on right to possession.)

(1) No order or judgment for the recovery of possession of any dwelling-house to which this Act applies, or for the ejectment of a tenant therefrom, shall be made or given unless—

( d) the dwelling-house is reasonably required by the landlord for occupation as a residence for himself, or for some person in his employment or in the employment of some tenant from him, and (except as otherwise provided by this subsection) the court is satisfied that alternative accommodation, reasonably equivalent as regards rent and suitability in all respects, is available;

and, in any such case as aforesaid, the court considers it reasonable to make such an order or give such judgment.

The existence of alternative accommodation shall not be a condition of an order or judgment on any of the grounds specified in paragraph ( d) of this subsection—

  1. where the tenant was in the employment of the landlord or a former landlord, and the dwelling-house was let to him in consequence of that employment and he has ceased to be in that employment; or
  2. where the landlord gave up the occupation of the dwelling-house in consequence of his service in any of His Majesty's forces during the war; or
  3. where the landlord became the landlord before the thirteenth day of September nineteen hundred and seventeen, or, in the case of a dwelling-house to which this Act applies but the enactments repealed by this Act did not apply became the landlord before the twentieth day of May nineteen hundred and twenty, and in the opinion of the court greater hardship would be caused by refusing an order for possession than by granting it.

(4) Notwithstanding anything in section one hundred and forty-three of the County Courts Act, 1888, or in section one of the Small Tenements Recovery Act, 1838, every warrant for delivery of possession of, or to enter and give possession of, any dwelling-house to which this Act applies, shall remain in force for three months from the day next after the last day named in the judgment or order for delivery of possession or ejectment, or, in the case of a warrant under the Small Tenements Recovery Act, 1838, from the date of the issue of the warrant, and in either case for such further period or periods, if any, as the court shall from time to time, whether before or after the expiration of such three months, direct.

(5) An order or judgment against a tenant for the recovery of possession of any dwelling-house or ejectment therefrom under this section shall not affect the right of any subtenant to retain possession under this section or be in any way operative against any such sub-tenant:

Provided that any sub-tenant so retaining possession shall upon the making of such order, or the giving of such judgment against the tenant, be deemed to become the tenant of the landlord.

The object of this Amendment is to do something to remedy a state of affairs which has arisen largely in regard to ex-service men. As the Bill stands, it is possible for a landlord to claim the house for his own occupation, but nothing is said in regard to the case of a landlord who only wishes to occupy a part of the house. There are many houses occupied by families, which, in the present stress of housing conditions, are not fully occupied, and which could quite well be occupied not only by the tenant but also, possibly, by the landlord. There would be no fear of overcrowding in such cases, because the matter would lie within the discretion of the Court. In paragraph (ii), provision has been made for ex service men, but, as I read that paragraph, it only means that, where an ex-service man has been the owner of a house in which he was living, and, owing to the war, has ceased to occupy it, it will not be necessary, if he still remains the owner, for him to prove the existence of alternative accommodation. There are a large number of ex-service men who may have been owners of houses which they sold when they were called up, because it was not possible for them to continue as owners; and there are also a large number of ex-service men who were only tenants of houses, who gave up their tenancy automatically when they were called up, and have since purchased houses which they are now unable to occupy, because, in their case, the provision that it is not necessary to prove the existence of alternative accommodation does not apply. Therefore, there is a legitimate grievance, and, if anything can be done for these men who went to serve their country, I think it ought to be done.

NEW CLAUSE.
—(Sub-tenants.)

If a tenant sub-lets any part of the premises without previous consent of the owner, 25 per cent. of any rent obtained by the tenant shall he due and payable to the owner for additional wear and tear upon such premises.—[ Mr. Lorden.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. LORDEN
: I beg to move, "That the Clause be read a Second time."

This Clause has been given to me by a Member of the House and I approve of it, because I think that the sub- 334 tenants have been the people who have been the difficulty very largely all the way through. There is no doubt that the tenants have let to lodgers—not lodgers in the ordinary sense—but they have let best part of their house and have only kept a very small portion for themselves. I think they should be penalised in some way so that this matter can be dealt with in a proper manner and that the landlord should get something towards the extra wear and tear. I know of my own knowledge of cases where houses with six or seven rooms have been let to five families where there is no authorisation whatever to let at all. I know of a case where a house was let at 10s. a week and where the tenant received by sub-letting 35s. a week. Therefore the profiteering has been very largely with tenants. I move this Clause because I think while it will probably not have a great deal of effect other than a deterrent, it will be a great deterrent upon tenants letting their houses out in rooms and in apartments which they are not authorised to do. At this late hour I am not going to detain the Committee longer but will move the Clause as handed in.

Dr. ADDISON
: I take it that this Clause will have the following effect. If a tenant sub-lets any part of the premises without previous consent of the owner, 25 per cent. of the proceeds must go to the owner in respect of additional wear and tear. Two circumstances might arise. First, the tenant might be legally entitled to sub-let, in which case I am not sure that the owner has any claim at all.

Mr. LORDEN
: I have provided for that circumstance.

Dr. ADDISON
: The next thing is that the tenant might very improperly let, in which case my hon. Friend says, "Well, he is doing a wrong thing, but, at all events, the landlord shall have some share of the plunder." It can only mean either of those two things. The conditions under which a tenant may sub-let, the conditions applying to furnished lodgings and so forth, are set out in the JPEG 335 Bill, and the procedure of the law must be to secure that those conditions are met. If any wrong or improper thing is done, the person ought to be dealt with accordingly by the process of law. There is no particular reason why any particular individual should benefit thereby. It is quite wrong that he should. I can see no justification for this. As long as the provisions of the Statute are obeyed, well and good. If they are not, the person should be dealt with according as the Statute prescribes and in no other way. No member of society is entitled as such either to be damaged or benefitted by anybody else's wrongdoing.

Mr. LORDEN
: This is not going to be a benefit to the landlord.

Dr. ADDISON

I do not for one moment suggest that where he has the consent of the landlord, or has obtained consent to the sub letting, that he should in any way part with some of the rent that he has obtained, but I do say, and I say it emphatically, that this sort of letting of houses means an enormous amount of wear and tear on 336 the property, and it is only reasonable that he should make some contribution towards the repairs which he has created.
