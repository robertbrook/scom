Mr. SHORTT
: I agree, Sir. This is a Clause which is intended to secure that there should be absolute fairness of dealing between the Ministry and any undertaking which is affected by the exercise by the Minister of his powers under the Act. The proposal is that there should be as far as possible uniformity in dealing with any claims by owners of undertakings, and for that purpose for all claims the same tribunal is provided, namely, the tribunal of the Railway and Canal Commission. Claims, of course, can arise in various ways. A claim may arise in respect of something done by the Board of Trade under their powers previous to the creation of the Ministry 1118 and the taking over by the Ministry of the powers of the Board of Trade. It might equally be a claim arising under the Act of 1871, or under the Railway Agreement if it is a railway company, or in any other way. Therefore it is provided that, no matter how the claim may arise, if there is a dispute as to the amount or any other circumstances, then the Railway and Canal Commission shall be the tribunal to deal with the matter. The Clause not only provides for the payment of compensation to an undertaking which is depreciated, but it also provides for the payment by an undertaking of enhancement of the value. If the Ministry expends money upon an undertaking, and that results in the enhanced value of that undertaking, then the undertaking will be liable to pay the Ministry the equivalent of compensation if there had been instead of enhancement, depreciation in. value. It is proposed that the value should be based upon the revenue-earning basis. It seems to me, and I mention this because there is an Amendment to leave out the words "on a revenue-earning basis," that the only way in which you can ascertain the value of such undertakings as those which are dealt with under this Act, is on the revenue-earning basis. Take a dock or railway, and if you can imagine such a thing as the purchaser of a railway in the ordinary sense, he would calculate what is the revenue which that undertaking will earn, and the same with a dock. The only value of a dock is its revenue-earning capacity. Nobody would buy it for any other purpose. No company would acquire the dock or a railway or a siding for any other purpose than in a revenue-earning capacity. There are two provisos. The first proviso is with regard to claims. It is provided that

"no claim in respect of any loss alleged to be due to any direction issued by the Minister shall be entertained if the direction was issued with the concurrence of the owners of the undertaking."

What is intended there is this: to meet the case where an undertaking sees that some alteration, some improvement, some extension, or whatever it may be, is for the benefit of the undertaking. They consider it is so, but they are not in a position to carry it out themselves. They cannot, perhaps, acquire the land or have not got the money available. Therefore they come to an agreement with the Ministry. The Minister would say, "This would be a good JPEG 1119 thing for you to do." They might say, "Yes, it would, but we cannot do it," and the Minister might say, "Very well, if I take control, and give you the power, you will then be able to do what you could not otherwise do. You can acquire the land, and we can advance the money, and so you can carry out the undertaking." In a case of that kind, it is only fair that the chances, whether for good or evil, should be undertaken by the undertaking itself. If there is a profit, they will acquire it, but if there is a loss, they must bear it. It is not intended to mean by concurrence that the mere obedience of a direction or the carrying out of the direction of the Minister is in itself concurrence. There can be no damage by providing that what is fair should be done. Suppose an undertaking were ordered by the Minister to do something, but the directors of the undertaking said, "It is a bad thing, but we will carry it out, if you insist," that would not be concurrence. They would concur in the sense that they carried out the direction, but it would not be concurrence as meant here. Therefore, in that case there would not be concurrence, although the direction of the Minister was loyally carried out by the owners of the undertaking, and if there was a loss the owners would in that case be entitled to be recouped under the provisions of this Clause. The next proviso is for the protection of the Treasury, and it provides that supposing an undertaking is being acquired by the State, and something which the Minister has done has enhanced the value of that undertaking, the owners of the undertaking are not to be entitled to go to the Tribunal and say, "We do not care who did this; it is perfectly true that the Minister did it, but here is our undertaking with that done, here is its value with the extension or improvement or whatever it might be, and we claim as of right that it should be valued on that basis." That would be manifestly unfair, and therefore the provision is that if the Minister has done something which has enhanced the value of the undertaking and the undertaking is about to be purchased by the State and valued by the Tribunal, they are not as of right to claim the value of the improvements carried out by the Minister.
