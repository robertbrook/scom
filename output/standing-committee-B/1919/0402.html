MINISTRY OF WAYS AND COMMUNICATIONS BILL.

SECOND DAY'S PROCEEDINGS.
CLAUSE 1.
—(Appointment of Minister of Ways and Communications.)

For the purpose of improving the means of, and the facilities for, locomotion and transport, it shall be lawful for His Majesty to appoint a Minister of Ways and Communications (hereinafter referred to as the Minister), who shall hold office during His Majesty's pleasure.

Amendment proposed [ 1st April], to leave out the words,

"It shall be lawful for His Majesty to appoint a Minister of Ways and Communications (hereinafter referred to as the Minister), who shall hold office during His Majesty's pleasure,"

and to insert instead thereof the words

"(1) there shall be established a Board of Ways and Communications charged with the superintendence of matters relating to transport in the United Kingdom;

  1. the Board shall consist of a president and six other members, who shall be appointed with reference to their special knowledge of the matters included in the Bill;
  2. the president and members of the Board shall be appointed by His Majesty and shall hold office during His Majesty's pleasure;
  3. the Board shall be deemed to be established on the appointment of the president thereof."—[ Mr. Joynson-Hicks.]

Question again proposed,

"That the words proposed to be left out. down to the word 'Ways', stand part of the Clause."

The MINISTER (Designate) of WAYS and COMMUNICATIONS (Sir Eric Geddes)
: This Amendment and others which follow it tend towards setting up a board of some kind, or several boards. The Amendment provides, so far as one may judge from it, for a board which should share with the Minister the responsibility for whatever action is taken under the powers given by this Bill. A board of that kind must either be a board upon which the Minister sits as the superior authority or upon which the Minister sits as an equal, or it must be a board which sits above the Minister. If a board of that kind sits above the Minister it appears to me that we destroy at once the responsibility of the Minister to Parliament and the Crown and we get an absolutely impossible state of affairs. We do not know who is responsible. On the other hand if it is a board which is subordinate to the Minister you have this position that the Minister must have the right to say what is to go to that Board; it will be impossible to lay it down in the Bill. The JPEG 345 Minister, if the board is subordinate to him, would have the power, subject to the Crown, of appointment to, and dismissal from, the board. That is the position in the board of Admiralty to-day.

If the board is not to be articulate I think—and I speak with some authority of the Board of Admiralty and some other boards—it serves no more useful purpose than the Minister consulting the various heads of Departments. At the Admiralty, which was cited by one of the members of the Committee as an example, the First Lord has the right, under the Crown, of appointment to, and removal from, the board. He has the right to settle what items shall appear upon the agenda of the Board and he has the right to distribute among the members of the board such duties as he thinks fit. Those are the prerogatives of the First Lord, and if you eliminate the naval element—and the naval element or anything comparable to it will be absent from this Ministry—it really gives you under this Amendment a conference with heads of Department. Such a conference I should think any reasonable man at the head of the Ministry such as this would always hold. If you constitute a board of that kind, where you have not the necessity for laying down a naval authority as you have in the Board of Admiralty, it does not seem to me to serve any more useful purpose than is served than by consulting your heads of Department.

If you set up the third type of board alongside the Minister to watch him, and you allow the board to be articulate, and either under this Amendment or any other Amendment to watch him with a separate spokesman, either in the House of Commons or the House of Lords, again you arrive at a most unconstitutional position. Therefore, it seems to me that you are driven back to a board which is subordinate to the Minister which can be appointed by the Minister, which can be removed by the Minister; this Minister can say who shall be the members of it, and he can say what is to go on the agenda of the board; for there must be some authority to say what is to go on the agenda, and you cannot lay it down in the Act, so that you must have consultation with the heads of Department, senior officials or otherwise. What kind of board can we look to? The Board of Trade is not a very good example. I think it was said that the Archbishop of Canterbury is one of the most distinguished members of that Board 346 I do not think that Board ever meets, but the President of the Board of Trade does consult the permanent officials regularly. The Board of Admiralty exists so that there may be an authority to give an order to the Fleet. That is really what the Lord High Admiral does in commission. Through members of the Board and the secretary he can give a legal order to the Fleet. Apart from that, the Lords of the Admiralty are the heads of the various Departments. They meet as and when the First Lord summons a meeting, and they deliberate upon such things as he puts upon the agenda. The responsibility for anything but a direct order to the Fleet must be the direct responsibility of the Government. That is what you get by having a consultative meeting every morning or every third morning with the heads of Departments.

When you consider this proposal to have a board under the Minister of Ways and Communications you are driven back to the conclusion that you must have a responsible Minister who must be responsible to Parliament as a member of the Cabinet, and you must have the direct responsibility of the Minister which must be shared by no one else. And, indeed, I think that the House and the country would wish that there should be someone who would be responsible, and that the intention of the Committee is that there should be some sort of guarantee that the Minister, whoever he is—and I will say at once that we are not looking at personalities in this at all—should have somebody to whom he could go and talk.

I will tell the Committee, if I may, exactly how I view that suggestion. I confess, indeed, it is on the face of the Bill, that I have not yet made up my mind, and I do not think that anyone on the Committee can have made up his mind, as to exactly how this very tangled skein of transportation is to be organised in future. It may be desirable to link the organisation of canals with docks, or to keep roads and light railways together. I do not know. It may be desirable in some sort of area commissions, local commissions, which is what I have in mind, to bring the railways and docks up to the quay edge under one commission authority—I do not mean to manage them, but I mean for general control. I do not say that that is the right thing, I merely say that that is one of the things which one might consider, and leave the water areas JPEG 347 separate. Therefore I am very reluctant at this stage to commit myself or my successor to any regular system of boards. At the same time I would like to assure the Committee that it is my intention to consult throughout these two years—I would ask the Committee to recollect that these two years stand by themselves, and that are a temporary period—accredited representatives of the big interests so vitally affected by this Bill.
