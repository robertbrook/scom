Parish precepts are important. However, clause 24 does not refer to them. The scheme will ensure that the cost of setting higher tax levels will generally fall on the local taxpayer, rather than the national taxpayer. When a council sets a tax level above the Government's guideline increase—which is 4.5 per cent, for the year 1999–2000—the cost of the resulting additional council tax will be met locally.

Currently, the loss of income that a council suffers as a result of council tax benefit is made good by the benefit subsidy that is provided by the Government. The Government contribute only 95 per cent, of the cost, so there is already a minor clawback. In Colchester borough council in my constituency, the balance is absorbed by the billing authority.

Under the new scheme, the cost of council tax benefit will have to be paid for by any authority that increases its council tax above the guideline. For example, in my constituency, if Colchester borough council were to increase its budget above the guideline, the loss of subsidy would be made good either by further increasing the council tax or by absorbing the extra cost within the budget. Currently, Colchester is planning to set a council tax for the next financial year that is no higher than the 4.5 per cent, guidline. However, the parish precepts are not in the control of the borough council, although they are included as part of the council's budget requirement for tax-setting purposes. I fully admit that that was a consequence of the Conservative Government's capping legislation.

Standard spending assessments are the means by which central Government grant is distributed. They take account of differences in population needs and the differing demographic and social characteristics of an area.

Ms Armstrong
