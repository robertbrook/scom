JPEG 87 I refer to a useful book that I recommend her to look at, "Law and Institutions of the European Union", Sixth Edition, by D. Lasok. In the chapter "Competition practices contrary to article 85" there is a section on public undertakings, which says:

"The Member States of the Union practise a 'mixed economy' in which the State participates alongside the private interest."

That clearly applies in the case of best value. It goes on:

"the state controls the 'public sector' in which it operates through public undertakings of different types … They exhibit, however, a common tendency towards monopolies and in that sense enjoy by law a 'dominant position' which the Union purports to contain within the concept of the Common Market."

It is referring to activities that may or may not be undertaken by the state, which are incorporated in the provisions of the Common Market, to which there is a specific reference.

The key case was as early as 1997, the van Ameyde v. UCI case, which was heard by the European Court of Justice. In that case an employment exchange, which we might regard as a purely state activity—a jobcentre—was characterised as an undertaking because

"finding people jobs is an economic activity; and the fact that such an activity is normally entrusted to a public service does not affect the economic nature of the activity since it is not always and not necessarily carried on by a public body."

Virtually any local authority activity, such as emptying dustbins, cleaning streets, providing computer services or management services, might be termed an economic activity and therefore could be included in the provisions of the Common Market. There is, therefore necessarily a relationship between those who are commissioning the services and those who are providing them. Unless we make that clear at this stage of the Bill, we care complicit in trying to defy the spirit of the European Community legislation and trying to blur the distinction.

We know what one of the major motivations of the abolition of competitive tendering is all about and why so many local authorities—local authorities very much dominated by the Minister's party—have sought to subvert the spirit of competitive tendering and to get round its provisions. It is because they want the producer interest to be dominant. The spirit of the Common Market is to put the consumer and the customer at the heart of the process rather than the producer interests.
