    : The purpose of clause 15 is to apply some procedure to the orders in clause 14. The purpose of the amendments is to circumscribe those procedures to provide some control over the way in which the Secretary of State may exercise the orders under clause 14.     354 I fully anticipate that the Government will suggest that the requirement to assess any trading advantage is beyond the remit of the Audit Commission as is the requirement to assess the benefits of such a proposal in accordance with clause 3(1). Clause 3(1) deals with the fundamental tenets of best value: efficiency, effectiveness and—I have said them so often that I have forgotten what they are. [Hon. Members: "Economy."]

    The purpose of adding the amendment is to avoid the situation where a local authority is given additional power to enter into a trading arrangement with another company and a cosy, anti-competitive relationship between them is created. The original purpose of open and competitive tendering was precisely to prevent those unaccountable relationships from forming.

    If there is to be a power in the Bill to allow the Secretary of State to allow new kinds of relationships and partnerships to develop, they must not confer an advantage on one contractor over another, permanently or temporarily. Otherwise we are opening the door to all kinds of improper relationships between a public authority and the private sector. Secondly, and this seems a simple and honest requirement, they should be for the purpose of achieving the objectives of best value.

    The onus must be on the proposers of any new power under clause 14 to demonstrate that it will achieve greater economy, efficiency and effectiveness, and is not just for the convenience of the council in question. The amendment would require the Audit Commission to produce a view on the matter.

    After consultation under clause 15, the Secretary of State lays an order and is required under subsection (2) to set out his proposals as a draft order and to provide details of the consultation. We propose in addition that the Secretary of State should include a copy of the application received under a proposed new clause. My drafting may be deficient but the power under clause 14 will not be exercised unless a local authority makes proposals and applies to do something that it would otherwise not be able to do.

    My proposed subsection 3(A) in amendment No. 214 does not refer to such an application, but where a change in the law is sought by a local authority, the draft order— and consultation on it—should include a copy of that local authority's application. As consultation is being made, it would then be possible to view the germ of the idea from first principles and understand the motive behind it.

    In amendment No. 213, we propose that the consultation should include a copy of assessment by the Audit Commission as specified in amendment No. 211. Thus consultees could see for themselves the basis on which the commission had decided that the local government function met the criteria of economy, efficiency and effectiveness.

    The concern must be that, because the arrangements under clause 14 are not just for implementation but are a permanent feature of best value and because the primary legislation might be amendable at any time for an indefinite period, proper processes are in place to ensure that the power is used only for the purposes intended by the Bill.

    JPEG 355 In our unwritten constitution, power is a fluid commodity. Sometimes powers not used for a long time become altogether unusable; but it is not unknown for powers brought into existence under one form of legislation to be used for completely different and unconnected purposes. It cannot be the Government's purpose to create powers so wide that they could be used for purposes other than best value.

    The amendments would ensure that the procedures governing orders under clause 14 were bound tightly to the original purposes of the Bill and would prevent businesses from pressing local authorities for a change in the law that would work to their own advantage and to the detriment of their competitors, the efficiency of the local authority function and best value itself. The onus would be on the Secretary of State to be convinced that best value was the prime and only purpose of a change in the law before it could be made.
