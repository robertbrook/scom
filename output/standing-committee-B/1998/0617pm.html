Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

Mr. John Whittingdale (Maldon and East Chelmsford): I am pleased that we have had this opportunity of debating economic and monetary union. Until now, the Government have been anxious to avoid discussing the matter at almost any cost and during the recent European election campaign they were happy to talk about any issue other than Europe. After publication of the national changeover plan, to which the clause directly relates, they refused all the requests of my right hon. Friend the Leader of the Opposition to debate the matter on the Floor of the House. Our only previous opportunity to do so arose when the hon. Member for Twickenham (Dr. Cable) was fortunate in obtaining a Wednesday morning Adjournment debate and I had the opportunity of debating the matter with the Economic Secretary. It is notable that no Government time has been available to debate one of the most important issues facing the country.

I had thought that the clause might not be debated because the Government might withdraw it following recent events. Since publication of the Bill, there has been a continual decline in the value of the euro—it fell yet again against the pound yesterday—and last week the British people voted overwhelmingly to reject the single currency. However, we are being required to debate a clause that will issue a blank cheque. We were told that the Government would exercise tight control over public expenditure and the Chancellor prided himself on how he would allow expenditure only if it was scrutinised and justified. Yet the explanatory notes state:

"The Revenue Departments may need to spend some tens of millions of pounds on preparations".

The clause will provide them with the ability to do so.

My right hon. Friend the Member for Wells (Mr. Heathcoat-Amory) referred to the matter in his speech. It is simply not good enough for the Committee to be asked to approve such sums with no further indication of precisely how much we are talking about. The Economic Secretary's reply was inadequate in that she did not even attempt to answer the questions asked by my right hon. Friend. I hope Col. 688 that she will now tell the Committee the precise sums involved.

The clause relates to the expenditure which Customs and Excise and the Inland Revenue may have to make to prepare for the possible introduction of the euro. The explanatory notes state that that may require

"tens of millions of pounds".

If it will cost Customs and Excise and the Inland Revenue tens of millions of pounds, how much will it cost British industry to prepare for an event that is unlikely to happen? Again, the Government have refused to try to quantify how much it will cost to go into the single currency.

Estimates have been made and I want to put on the record some of the evidence that we have received about the sums involved. The British Retail Consortium has estimated that the total cost of preparation is between £1.7 billion and £3.5 billion. Eurocommerce suggests that the cost to the European retail sector is around 1.8 per cent, of annual turnover, or up to £25 billion which equals approximately £40 per head for each of Europe's 372 million citizens. Marks and Spencer has estimated that it will cost roughly £100 million to install new systems and technology. That implies a cost to the UK retail sector as a whole of about £2 billion.

The sums involved are huge, yet the Chancellor is asking British industry to start spending without knowing whether it need do so. Our amendment would show that the Government have made it clear that we should not participate in the euro unless a referendum records a positive vote. It is unrealistic and absurd to ask the taxpayer and British industry to start spending such sums without first holding a referendum. The amendment would require that a referendum take place before any expenditure is incurred.

The failure to debate this issue is causing huge concern in all quarters of the House. I draw the Government's attention again to early-day motion 185, which calls on the Government

"to publish a White Paper on the constitutional, economic and political implications of the United Kingdom joining the European single currency."

It was signed by more than 100 Members of Parliament, including Liberal Democrats, Ulster Unionists and a number of leading Labour Members. By an extraordinary coincidence, the Committee of Selection appears to have overlooked those signatories when deciding on the composition of our Committee.

The Government have embarked on a policy of taking this country into the European single currency by stealth. The clause forms part of that objective.

Mr. Derek Twigg (Halton): Given that the Government have made it clear that there will be a referendum on entry into the single currency, how can the hon. Gentleman make that ridiculous comment?

Mr. Whittingdale: The whole point of our amendment is to show that the Government are committing tens of millions of pounds without a referendum. If the hon. Gentleman is committed to the idea of a referendum, I invite him to vote for the amendment, which would ensure that a referendum takes place before such expenditure is incurred.

I draw the hon. Gentleman's attention to a letter sent to accounting officers, authorising expenditure on what can only be described as propaganda in favour of the single currency, and to a letter sent to national health service trusts, which threatens that a contract with a supplier might not be made unless it demonstrates an intention to make its software euro compliant.

A vast exercise is being undertaken to create the impression that participation in the European single currency is inevitable. Before lunch, the Economic Secretary suggested that British industry fully supports that objective and, in doing so, quoted Lord Marshall. I shall quote the Director General of the Institute of Directors, who said:

"Not content with having frittered away £7.5 million of taxpayers money in advertising the euro, the Government now wants the public sector and business to spend millions of pounds preparing for something that may not be right for the economy nor have the support of the British people. Neither Government nor business should invest precious resources in preparing for the euro unless first, the Chancellor's five economic tests are met, and second,"—

this is the critical point—

"the British people vote for the introduction of the euro in a referendum. The Government's changeover plan is a classic example of putting the cart before the horse."

Furthermore, Sir Stanley Kalms, the chairman of Dixons, said:

"Changing our systems to accept the euro would cost £40 million. If the public say no to the euro, which seems likely, that money would be wasted".

The Government are asking the Committee to authorise the expenditure of tens of millions of pounds of taxpayers' money, without first bothering to ask the British people whether that is what they want.

Col. 690 Last week we saw evidence that the overwhelming majority of the British people are opposed to British entry into a single currency. If the Government really believe that it is in the interest of British industry, the British economy and the British people that we prepare for entry, let them have the courage to accept the amendment, so that if expenditure of this nature takes place it will do so with the authority of the British people in a full referendum.

Mr. Nick St. Aubyn (Guildford): This could be called a tit-for-tat clause: in return for giving the Bank of England an element of independence, the Treasury is getting its own back by taking over management of the national debt. This is a technical clause to achieve that. Throughout its history, the Bank of England has successfully managed the country's debt, despite the problems of many Labour Governments in the past, and I regret that the Bank is to be deprived of that role.

In recent weeks we have seen a specific reason for the Bank to keep that role. While it dealt in the market in the business of the national debt, it had a good and open reason for a long and strong dialogue with the market. That dialogue could extend to all aspects of debt and finance, and it both informed the Bank's opinions and improved its view of what was happening in the world. It enabled the Bank to ask questions without the market suspecting its true agenda.

The other day we had an example of how that could have been useful when the Government announced that they were selling half their gold reserves. I would like to think that they took advice only from the Bank of England, because there is a risk in discussing the matter with the market, but there is concern that people in the market might have got wind of that sale. Some major trading houses and international organisations in the City of London have a short position in gold today, and there is a reason for that. The risk of the Government inadvertently showing their hand will be far greater when the management of their operations in the London market is under the control of the Treasury instead of the Bank of England.

The clause is regrettable. It is also very complex. The way in which interest will be paid on discount securities, as described in the explanatory notes, shows that the shift from the Bank of England was ill thought-out. The change was quite unnecessary and has caused many technical problems, which the Bank is trying to resolve. The argument that the Bank of England should no longer control debt issuance because it now controls interest rates is spurious. As the Bank has shown, its decisions on interest rates are as transparent as possible. As I said this morning, when the Select Committee commented on EMU, it showed great appreciation for the transparency of an independent Bank of England.

It is absurd to argue that a transparent Bank of England that controls interest rates should not also control the national debt, but that is the rationale behind the clause. I look forward to hearing any Image Col. 697 defence of the clause that the Economic Secretary might muster.

My right hon. Friend the Chancellor's decision in May 1997 to give the Bank of England operational independence for monetary policy has given this country an intensely successful monetary policy framework that is delivering the lowest interest and mortgage rates for more than 30 years. The clause Col. 698 completes the process of securing the new and successful monetary policy framework by separating from the Bank the debt management function, ensuring that there is no possibility of actual or perceived conflict of interest, thus reinforcing the success of the new monetary policy framework.
