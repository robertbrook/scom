The Minister for Public Health (Ms Tessa Jowell)
: Perhaps you will confirm, Mr. Hood, that the Opposition said that they did not intend to press amendments Nos. 56 and 57 to 59, and that we were focusing the debate on amendments Nos. 126 and 131.

It is important to make it clear that we are dealing essentially with powers of last resort and that the only difference between amendments Nos. 126 and 131 is semantic. The principle behind both amendments is that the reasons for the directions should be published. Subsection (8) makes it clear that reasons must be given and it would be difficult to imagine circumstances when directions could be published without explaining why the power of direction had been used. It is important to underline the extent to which this is very much a power of last resort—the nuclear option after every other course of action has failed. We want to be as certain as we can that appropriate legal safeguards are in place, allowing Ministers to issue directions in the event of serious problems with the agency. One example relates especially to the implementation of European Union legislation. About 90 per cent. of food safety legislation emanates from the EU. Were that legislation not acted on, the UK Government would face infraction proceedings, which would involve the agency as well.

284 Ministers must have the power to issue directions in the event of serious problems. That is an important element of democratic control and accountability. It is also important for the Committee to remember that the power relates to serious failure in specified circumstances and is, therefore, tightly constrained. It cannot be used by Ministers simply to tell the agency what to do on a routine basis. It would be wrong to dismiss the power as general or draconian. The directing authority would need to ensure that it was exercising the power fully in accordance with the terms in which it is couched, otherwise the authority would be subject to judicial review. It is likely that the wording of any direction will have to say clearly why that step was being taken. It is difficult to conceive of a direction being given without the directing authority offering some explanation, otherwise the direction could appear arbitrary.

I have some sympathy with the ideas that have been expressed in the amendment and I see no difficulty in writing into the Bill an express requirement on the directing authority to publish the reasons for issuing a direction. That is consistent with our position on other parts of the Bill. In principle, therefore, I am prepared to accept the first part of the amendment, and I shall return with suitable wording on Report.

The second element of the amendment would strengthen the duty placed on the Minister to ensure that those affected by the direction were told about it. The current drafting is more specific in that the authority has to decide how to get that information to those affected. I suspect that there is not much between us, and I am prepared to consider further whether the amendment would more successfully achieve the intended effect.

I hope, in the light of that clarification and my willingness to take on board those key points, that the Opposition will agree to withdraw the amendment. Incidentally, the hon. Member for Meriden (Mrs. Spelman) explained this morning that she would be absent this afternoon.

Clause 25 enables Ministers to make an order to lift any statutory restrictions on disclosure of information to the agency. However, under the clause as drafted, a lacuna may exist in relation to common-law restrictions on disclosure, as they are not covered. I do not have any examples to share with the Committee, but the amendment is a preparatory provision to cover the event of a common-law restriction or bar being discovered in future. We want to lift such a bar for the agency, and it would not make sense to return with primary legislation because, as hon. Members know, securing a slot in this place is not easy. We want the agency to have access to all relevant information, and to be able to publish it if necessary. JPEG 287 4.45 pm

Clause 25 provides the Secretary of State and other appropriate authorities with the power to make orders to relax or override prohibitions on disclosure. The Bill deals with the problem of statutory bars but does not cover restrictions in common law. Amendment No. 129 is a simple technical amendment to plug that gap to cover any that might be discovered in future. In drafting the Bill, we examined all the statutory bars that we could find. One has only to look in the Division Lobby and at the volumes of legislation that this place has passed in recent years to know that such a search is no mean feat. We think that we have discovered them all, but we cannot be absolutely certain, hence clause 25. However, we had not contemplated the possibility of bars in common law.

I regret that we do not have an example to give the Committee, but the spirit of what is intended is to provide under the clause even more power to lift bars to information being published. It is therefore an advance on opening up information, not an attempt to close it down. Given that it moves in that direction, and in the spirit of compromise shown in our consideration of the Bill, I hope that the Committee will accept the amendment.
