Clause 5
ADVISORY COMMITTEES

Question proposed [ 1 July], That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Question again proposed.

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Jeff Rooker)
: I shall do my best to respond to the key points that were made in last Thursday's debate without encroaching on future debates on schedule 2. However, of necessity, I shall have to refer to certain issues that were raised by the hon. Member for Cotswold (Mr. Clifton-Brown).

The idea of setting up advisory committees for Scotland and Wales is one that the Opposition have probed, and rightly so. We defend their establishment on the grounds that there are differences between the devolved areas, especially Scotland. Under the general committee structure, scientific advice will be given on a United Kingdom-wide basis, but it will refer occasionally to the devolved areas.

The regional committee structure that we are proposing will play a useful role in providing sources of local expression and will be a channel of advice to the agency. I do not have the figures to hand, but it is well known that there is a greater prevalence of problems associated with E.coli O157 in Scotland than in England and Wales.

A year ago, I sent all hon. Members a copy of our Food Research News, No. 4, 1998, which made it abundantly clear in one of its charts that food poisoning in Scotland is twice the rate per head of population as that in England and Wales.

Such factors go right across the divided committee structure, but the two examples that I have cited show good grounds for devolved areas to have their own advisory committee to concentrate on local issues and 114 for them to be a channel by which to transmit issues from, say, the Scottish Parliament and Executive to the Foods Standards Agency executive director for Scotland and, hence, to the other United Kingdom bodies. That is an effective argument for having such advisory committees, particularly in Scotland. The argument could be put for Wales on the grounds of devolution and equality, but I do not think that there are grounds for arguing for a separate committee for England.

That is not to say, however, that different policies could not be imposed on specific areas. The answer may be yes, but a UK-wide agency should reflect our differences and not impose totally uniform procedures. For instance, a different law has been in place in Scotland since 1983 for the drinking of raw milk. That is a classic example of the law being dependent on a decision made in Scotland. It will be nothing to do with the UK agency. It will be a matter for the devolved Parliament, which will obviously receive advice and consultation. Just because it is UK-wide agency does not mean that its decisions are uniform.

As a result of negotiations in Brussels, our obligations to the European Union are another reason for needing a UK-wide agency. However, the Bill provides that the devolved bodies have the right not to follow the agency's advice. The Scottish Parliament has only just assumed its powers, but from early debates it seems that it wishes to have the benefits of a UK-wide agency that respects the devolved Administration. Perhaps I should not call them regional committees, but they will have a useful role as a source of local advice and information. The agency can therefore touch base with Scottish and Welsh opinion in a way that would not otherwise be available to it.

As I said in an earlier debate, except for the chairman, the deputy chairman and the devolved JPEG 115 members, the members of the board are not English members but UK members.
