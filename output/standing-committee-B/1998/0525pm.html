Clause 48
SCOTTISH PARLIAMENT AND DEVOLVED ASSEMBLIES

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory
(Wells)
: The clause deals with the tax treatment of the new devolved Scottish Parliament and the assemblies for Wales and Northern Ireland. The Government propose to extend a tax treatment which many regard as a tax privilege. There may be good reasons for doing so, but when such steps are taken the relevant conditions need to be tightly defined and the good reasons need to be stated.

The explanatory notes state that the clause will

"treat Members of the Scottish Parliament and the devolved assemblies for Wales and Northern Ireland in the same way, for tax purposes, as Members of Parliament".

Many employees of other public bodies and in the private sector have to work away from their homes. They have to engage in extensive tours of duty away from their main residence but they are not treated, for tax purposes, differently from other people. We in the House have a special tax treatment in that regard. To discuss whether that status is valid or not is beyond the scope of our debate, but we should be sure that it is right to extend the proposal in one leap to the Scottish Parliament and to the two assemblies, which differ markedly from the Westminster Parliament in their proposed duties and in the scope of their responsibilities.

We should also consider the obvious point that they cover much smaller territories.

Who in future will be responsible for setting the level of the relevant allowances? We might base the tax treatment on the assumption that the allowances and payments are made for broadly comparable duties, but 426 that arrangement would be upset if, at some future date, the bodies in question started to set their own rules, which they might make much more generous and which they might base on the erroneous assumption that they would be taxed on the relevant allowances.

To illuminate the issue, I turned to a written reply that the Prime Minister gave in March 1999 to the Review Body on Senior Salaries. On the proposed allowances for the devolved bodies, he said:

"It is not yet possible to know exactly what the allowances will need to cover … this is best done by the Scottish Parliament, the National Assembly for Wales and the Northern Ireland Assembly themselves. We would expect them to put in place as soon as possible an appropriate but closely controlled system, mindful of the need for a prudent approach and the need to justify to the taxpayer any significant divergence from the recommendations made by the Review Body."— [Official Report, 31 March 1999; Vol. 28. c. 774.]

There is clearly an intention that the allowance system, covering subsistence, travel and so on, should, in the Prime Minister's words, be "appropriate but closely controlled", but there is no assurance that it will be.

Mr. Gibb
: Although clause 50 is seemingly a technical clause, confined merely to reversing Privy Council offences—the New Zealand Wattie case—it is widely seen by those in commerce and industry as a change in the law. It is an extension of the tax base and, in effect, another of the Government's infamous stealth taxes—the tax rises that the Government hoped no one would notice, which will raise £20 million in this tax year and £50 million a year thereafter.

The Government clearly regard property as a rich source of additional tax revenue. They have raised stamp duty three times, abolished MIRAS and now they want to tax reverse premiums. That fits into another perceived rich vein of additional taxation—the nation's pension funds, which are already taxed by the Government to the tune of £5 billion a year.

The measure in schedule 6, involving the taxation of reverse premiums, is likely to be borne to a large extent by pension funds, which are heavy investors in property. The taxation of the receipts by the tenant will mean that tenants will require correspondingly higher reverse premiums than would otherwise have been payable, which will be at the expense of the returns of the pension funds.

The Institute of Directors feels strongly about the matter. It states:

"This measure will effectively raise tax from pension funds. The payer of a reverse premium will often be a pension fund, exempt from tax, so that any deduction that it might be able to obtain for the payment of a reverse premium would not be tax-effective … the effect of this measure will be to narrow the tax exemption available to pension funds by passing part of the tax burden on to them."

The Institute of Chartered Accountants is also concerned that there was no consultation with it, or with any other sections of industry, before the provision was introduced. For a Government who pride themselves on consultation, that is quite surprising.

Before the Wattie case, there was an element of uncertainty in the taxation of reverse premiums. For most tenants, there will be capital receipts without the 432 disposal of a capital asset. Thus, for tax purposes, it constitutes a nothing, and technically it is not taxable. The Inland Revenue, however, will try to find a link between the receipt that the tenant receives and subsequent rent levels that the tenant will pay. That will then be taxed as a revenue receipt. Paragraph 528 of the Inland Revenue's inspector's manual recognises that there can be circumstances when there is no such link and, as a consequence, the receipt is not taxable.

The Wattie judgment said that such receipts were not taxable, even when there was a clear link with subsequent rent levels. The Government are trying to reverse the effect of that case.
