Mrs. Spelman
: We come now to the vexed question of interests. That important matter was referred to briefly in some of the amendments discussed this morning.

In the context of the amendments, it is important that the Food Standards Agency is seen to be as independent and transparent as possible if it is to gain the trust and public confidence that the Government expect, but that will be inextricably linked with the interests of the members of the agency.

The Bill has had a lengthy gestation and I have examined the report of the Special Select Committee that considered the draft Bill. I was not a member of that Committee, but some of my hon. Friends were and it identified a possible weakness concerning members' interests. It stated:

"We recommend that at second reading the Minister should make explicit the criteria by which members of the Commission are selected and interests are declared."

I proceeded to pay great attention to what the Minister said on Second Reading and sought a response on the Special Select Committee's recommendation. The Minister stated:

"All applicants will have to declare all personal interests or connections, and the appointment panels will be charged with probing potential conflicts of interest very carefully."

He went on to say something that is important to our discussion of the amendments this morning. He said:

"I must make it clear that there are no specific interests— commercial or public—which would automatically exclude any candidate."—[ Official Report, 21 June 1999; Vol. 333, c.787.]

That is important because the position concerning interests remains unclear. The explanatory notes contain some contradictions and we hope that they will be resolved during discussion of our amendments.

78 The explanatory note on clause 2 refers to financial and other interests and gives the example of someone with a shareholding in a food manufacturing company. That seems in line with the Minister's position, but does not mean that any such interest will automatically disqualify a person from appointment as a member. That sits awkwardly with the advertisement that we discussed earlier this morning which states clearly that integrity and freedom from conflict of interest will be essential. Our view is that potential applicants for the posts of chairman, deputy chairman and ordinary board members might be deterred because it is hard to guarantee complete freedom from conflict of interest if someone's interest pertains to the subject.

The thrust of our amendments is to reinforce the Minister's position that someone with financial or other interests should not be debarred from applying to become a member of the Food Standards Agency. That is all the more important because people who have relevant skills and expertise might be put off applying. The vexed question of whether interests should be permitted was highlighted when the Minister for the Environment suspended members of a statutory body on genetically modified food because of their connections as scientists with certain companies.

80 Amendment No. 11 should be read with amendment No. 10. For the integrity and transparency of the agency to be publicly recognised, there must be a proper register of interests. I draw the Committee's attention to paragraph 9 of schedule 1, which refers to the register of interests, because it makes clear our perception of the weakness. It states:

"The Agency may regulate its own procedure"

"a system for the declaration and registration of private interests".

That is too vague. A system of registration is not the same as a clear, statutory register of interests, and the amendment is designed to toughen up that provision. The register should be refreshed annually. Board members' interests could change during the course of their appointment to the agency, so it is important that the register is updated. If the Government are unwilling to accept the amendment, we would like to know what they consider to be a system of registering interests. The public will want to know what is meant by such a system. One could envisage a system remaining cloistered, with members of the agency telling each other their interests, without that information reaching the public domain. It is important that a register of interests should be freely accessible to members of the public, and to all groups with an interest in food standards and food safety.
