Clause 5
BEST VALUE REVIEWS

Question proposed, That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Bernard Jenkin
(North Essex)
: I wish to speak against clause 5. It will be useful to discuss the clause as a whole, not least because our amendment was ill framed. We wanted to consider the performance of best value authorities and framing best value authorities and framing best value reviews.

We thought that the Bill was meant to reflect the White Paper's provisions on fundamental performance review. I shall recap, and give the Committee our interpretation of the relevant provisions of the White Paper.

Paragraph 7.18 of the White Paper provides the most concrete statement about the purpose of performance reviews. It states:

"The principal outcome of each review will therefore be performance targets which take account of national requirements for the service (s) concerned—covering strategic objectives, cost and efficiency, effectiveness, quality and fair access—and an action plan demonstrating how those targets are to be achieved."

The White Paper goes on to explain the four key processes that will help authorities to form a best value review. Paragraph 7.19 states that they include the challenge of providing services,

"in entirely new ways. New technology or best practice may meand that traditional ways of providing a service are no longer viable or acceptable".

That is reflected in paragraphs (a), (b) and (c) of subsection (4), which provides for an authority,

"to consider whether it should be exercising the function; to consider the level at which and the way in which it should be exercising the function; to consider its objectives in relation to the exercise of the function".

Paragraph 7.20 of the White Paper deals with comparison. It considers national performance indicators and benchmarking clubs. I am a bit suspicious of benchmarking clubs because they are mutually comfortable places where people can compare their attributes without the threat of having 168 to change anything. It is encouraging that the White Paper recommends that comparisons should go beyond local authorities,

"to embrace the experience of other public and voluntary bodies and the private sector."

Clearly, comparison should be fruitful for many local authorities. However, I am not sure whether it would give them the impetus to do something about failing public services.

The third process that the White Paper describes is consultation. The Bill provides for making comparisons, but mixes up comparing and consulting. For example, subsection (4) (f) requires local authorities,

"to consult other best value authorities and commercial and other businesses about the exercise of the function".

However, the White Paper treats the matter as one of engaging with local communities and carrying out reviews. I do not want to hark back unnecessarily to a previous debate, but the White Paper states that the consultation should be in the review process, whereas the Bill suggests that the only consultation would be with other best value authorities.

The real question that we want to raise is that of competitiveness. The Bill gives little indication of how competition should be provided for in conducting a best value review. Of course, competition was what so many authorities most resented on the introduction of compulsory competitive tendering. Getting rid of those rules was in many ways the motive for the Government and many of their supporters in introducing the best value process.

Subsection (4)(e) refers to assessment of the competitiveness of an authority's

"performance in exercising the function by reference to the exercise of the same function, or similar functions, by other best value authorities and by commercial and other businesses".

but that is all that it says. It is difficult to understand, harking back to what my right hon. Friend the Member for Skipton and Ripon (Mr Curry) is always going on about, what that provision means to someone who gets in at 9 o'clock in the morning and must provide for competition. Is it just another form of benchmarking—in which case those concerned can all go back to bed again after 5 o'clock without doing anything about it—or is there a degree of compulsion? The whole Bill is, of course, deeply imbued with the philosophy of central compulsion from Whitehall, as the hon. Member for Bow and Poplar— [Interruption.]—for Bethnal Green and Stepney, or rather the hon. Member for Bethnal Green and Bow (Ms King), the boundaries of whose constituency have obviously been subject to a best value review, will acknowledge.

The clues about what those concerned will have to do are in the White Paper. There is lots of rhetoric about providing

"a competitive economy with a flexible labour market, underpinned by the fair treatment of those affected"

and we should all sign up to that. The document contains the ritual complaint about

"the imposition of rules which invite avoidance"—

JPEG 169 that is a criticism of compulsory competitive tendering—and there is preference for an

"approach based on partnership rather than confrontation".

Competitive tendering always worked best where local authorities undertook it in a spirit of partnership. Where it was undertaken adversarially it was—and will remain—pretty easy for local authorities to frighten away potential bidders for contracts, whose reaction would be, "We don't want to work with them; they look like a nightmare to work for." That is how competitive tendering failed to achieve the results that we wanted.
