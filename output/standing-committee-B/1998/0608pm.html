Schedule 10
SHARING OF PENSIONS ETC. ON DIVORCE OR ANNULMENT

Question proposed this day, That this schedule be the Tenth schedule to the Bill.

4.30 pm

Question again proposed.

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Ms Patricia Hewitt)
: When we adjourned, I was responding to the questions of the hon. Member for North-East Hertfordshire (Mr. Heald) on pension scheme members who wished to rebuild their benefit entitlements following a divorce or pension-sharing arrangement. I explained that no specific legislation was needed to implement the easement for moderate earners who will be able to rebuild their pension benefit entitlements without taking account of the pension debit that has given effect to the pension sharing. Most occupational pension schemes choose the discretionary tax approval route, which offers more generous terms. Schemes that need approval under the strict criteria in section 590 of the Taxes Act 1988 relate primarily to controlling directors of companies such as investment companies, where the Inland Revenue would not be prepared to exercise its discretionary approval powers. In any event, controlling directors are excluded from the easement. The fact that it is not given statutory force in schedule 10 does not undermine its impact.

The hon. Member for North-East Hertfordshire also asked why schedule 10 made no provision to give effect to an easement for holders of personal pensions and retirement annuity contracts—[ Interruption]

The Chairman
: Order. The hon. Member for Glasgow, Govan (Mr. Sarwar) must take his seat.

Ms Hewitt
: Thank you, Mr. Butterfill.

The easement relates to occupational pension schemes that are approved under the discretionary powers which are controlled by the limits on benefits and the contributions that an employee may pay. Personal pensions and retirement annuity contracts are controlled solely by the limits on contributions. For example, the contribution limit for older people in a personal pension rises to 40 per cent of capped earnings, whereas the limit for someone with a retirement annuity contract rises to 27½ per cent of uncapped earnings. Those limits should provide ample scope for most people to make extra contributions to rebuild their benefit entitlement because very few people pay anywhere near the maximum contribution.

We do not think that it is right in principle to create special tax reliefs for extra contributions that are made by pension sharers. That would be unfair to other 520 scheme members, including those who are married and stay married, and those who divorce but do not pension share and are unable to pay the extra tax relieved contribution.

The hon. Member for North-East Hertfordshire also asked why we limited the easement to people on moderate earnings, for whom the threshold is £22,650 a year. We think that it is right, especially in the case of additional tax relief, to help people with modest earnings. They most need help with their savings and that is where we want to target the tax relief. The proposed threshold, which is a quarter of the current limit for the earnings on which benefits can be accrued in an occupational pension scheme, will cover the majority of occupational pension scheme members. It will target help to those who need it most. The threshold will be linked to the normal rules for people who change jobs and are able to ignore pension benefits from earlier employment. If the threshold for the latter changes, it will extend automatically to pension sharers.

The hon. Member for North-East Hertfordshire also asked whether scheme members should be allowed fully to rebuild their lost pension rights. We do not believe that that would be right, for the two reasons that I gave: First, that the help should be concentrated on the less well off; and, secondly, that it would be wrong to give greater pension tax reliefs across the board to people who divorce and then pension-share, as distinct from those who divorce and do not pension-share or those who stay married.

The final point raised by the hon. Gentleman was whether there should be a limit on the additional pension that the ex-spouse can earn, in either the same or a subsequent pension scheme. There was a suggestion that, as there would be some limits on the scheme member to rebuild his pension rights after a divorce and pension-sharing, there should be equivalent limits on the ex-spouse. I believe that that is wrong. The ex-spouse will take a share of the pension, in the form of a pension credit or a pension share, from which she—it will generally be the woman—would otherwise have benefited, had she remained a wife or subsequently become a widow. The benefit to which she is entitled from her husband's pension is separate from any pension that she has earned, or might earn in future, from her own employment. It would therefore be unfair to count the shared pension that she received following a divorce against any other pension that she had earned, or might earn in the future, from her own employment. Any restriction on the ex-spouse's pension could result in a fair financial settlement being thwarted, and unjustly affect any pension based on her own employment.

I hope that I have responded to the questions raised by the hon. Member for North-East Hertfordshire. Schedule 10 gives effect in the tax regulations to the pension-sharing provisions of the Welfare Reform and Pensions Bill. The hon. Gentleman began this morning's debate by saying that he welcomed the provisions and I, in turn, welcomed his support for them. However, I am now advised that there has been a policy change on the part of the official Opposition. : As my hon. Ms Hewitt
: I am grateful for the opportunity to explain why we are giving statutory backing to the orderly unwinding of an extra-statutory concession that dates back to 1967. There are several reasons why the extra-statutory concession is no longer appropriate.

First, the arrangements that, in some respects, treat companies within a single group as if they were one company—for administrative purposes—are incompatible with the obligations that are imposed under corporation tax self-assessment. Secondly, corporation tax self-assessment means that, from the point of view of the Inland Revenue, there is no longer any great administrative benefit to be gained from the simplification, which was one of the reasons for JPEG 523 providing the extra-statutory concession in the first place. Thirdly, since the end of March 1996, paragraph 12 of schedule 9 of the Finance Act 1996 has allowed no-gain no-loss intra-group transfers of loan relationship assets. A significant part of the extra-statutory concession is therefore redundant.

4.45 pm

Fourthly, concern has been expressed about the response of some groups to the arrangements. There is no doubt that they provide tax planning opportunities that are not permitted by statute or by case law generally, and are not available to other large commercial groups. As the right hon. Member for Fylde (Mr. Jack) indicated, non-statutory arrangements of this kind, especially when they apply widely, are open to justifiable criticism for undermining Parliament's authority over the taxation system. It was therefore judged no longer appropriate to maintain in the system a basis for taxation for which there was no statutory cover.

In terms of the arrangements for notifying insurance companies, the transitional provisions recognise the need for an orderly unwinding of the concession. They allow for tax-free intra-group transfer to continue until the end of December 1999. Insurers will therefore be able to reorganise their business this year. The insurance companies affected—only a limited number of groups are concerned—were notified of the Revenue's decision to withdraw the arrangement before the change was made. As indicated, transitional arrangements are being offered to deal with the difficulties that might arise from the change to a statutory basis.

The Inland Revenue is in discussion with the Association of British Insurers—the trade body for the industry—on an orderly ending. Large amounts are involved, and the Government were worried that companies might try to argue, in relation to obligations in respect of earlier years when the extra-statutory concession was in force, that the undertaking to return the tax on profits no longer applied. Because the amounts involved are large, we felt it right to underpin, with a statutory provision, the undertakings that the groups have already given to pay the appropriate tax on profits on intra-group transfers when the assets are transferred out of the group. That provision will require companies to return their profits on investment on the basis of the original group cost.

That is all that the clause does. It is long and complicated because the interaction between the arrangement and recent legislation—for instance, on loan relationships and the foreign exchange gains rule—is complicated. If companies abide by the undertakings that they gave in the past, however, there will be no need whatever to examine the legislation in great detail. I commend the clause to the Committee.

Question put and agreed to.
