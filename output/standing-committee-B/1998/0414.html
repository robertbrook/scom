Fur Farming (Prohibition) Bill

Clause 5
COMPENSATION FOR EXISTING BUSINESSES

Amendment proposed [ 31 March]: No. 24, in page 3, line 35, at end insert—

"() Any compensation scheme made under this section shall include provision for the payments of the reasonable fees of the professional advisers of persons making claims.".

The Chairman
:I remind the Committee that with this we are considering the following amendments:

No. 25, in page 3, line 35, at end insert—

"() Any compensation scheme made under this section shall include provision for the payment on account where requested by the claimant of 90 per cent. of the amount of compensation agreed between the claimant and the appropriate authority, or in any other case of the amount of compensations that the appropriate authority estimate the claimant is entitled to under the scheme within 3 months of the submission of the claim.".

No. 26, in page 3, line 35, at end insert—

"() Any compensation scheme made under this section shall include provision for the payment of interest, to be calculated on a compound basis, on the total of payments agreed at final settlement or as may be determined by arbitration."

No. 27, in page 3, line 35, at end insert—

"() Any compensation scheme made under this section shall include provision for the cost of clearing the land in question of buildings or other fixed equipment utilised by the business.".

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

Mr. Andrew Hunter
(Basingstoke)
: We are greatly privileged that you are presiding over our proceedings this morning, Mr. Benton. I apologise in advance for a discourtesy that I might commit this morning. My private Member's Bill will enter Committee at 11.30 am, and I shall feel obliged to leave in good time to prepare for that. I apologise if I leave before our proceedings are completed, but I am sure that that will be a relief to some members of the Committee.

When we drew stumps at the previous sitting, I had spoken to amendment No. 24, and I propose to summarise in only two or three sentences what I tried to argue then. My argument was that claimants under the land compensation code are entitled to have their reasonable professional advisers fees paid for them by the acquiring authority, and that that should apply to mink farmers as well. I expressed the hope—and I do so again—that the hon. Member for Liverpool, Garston (Maria Eagle) will 38 accept the logic of that. Her Bill will create a unique situation for a specialist form of farming, so it is essential that those concerned have access to good quality professional advice when negotiating their compensation claims.

I was mid-way through my argument for amendment No. 25 when the Committee adjourned. It is an irrefutable fact that since 1973, claimants for compensation under the land compensation code have been entitled to request advance payments on account of 90 per cent. of the agreed compensation, or—this is more usual—of the acquiring authority's initial estimate of their entitlement. The reason for that is self-evident. It can take months—sometimes years—before a final settlement is reached in a compensation case. That is why there is provision under the land compensation code for an advance down payment of the stated proportion. The argument for that is that it is reasonable for people entitled to compensation to be enabled to reorganise their affairs—for example, by setting up another business—by receiving up front a substantial proportion of the compensation due to them.

I ask the hon. Member for Garston to address that argument. Surely it is only fair and reasonable that provision for advance down payment under the land compensation code should be matched by the compensation scheme to be set up under the Bill. Moreover, if the hon. Lady and the Government want mink farmers to go earlier rather than later, the mechanism that I propose would enable that. My amendment would simply extend to fur farming the same compensation rights that exist for land compensation, and it would be unfair and unreasonable to reject it.

Amendment No. 26 would add a new paragraph between subsections (2) and (3). The argument for it is straightforward and uncomplicated, but that does not diminish its strength. I call this a natural justice amendment, and I hope that the Committee will agree with that assessment.

It is self-evident that, until a claimant receives the compensation award that he has negotiated, he cannot put that award to use. He cannot invest it or earn interest from it. The amendment would make provision for the calculation of the interest that the final settlement would have accrued between the day that the recipient became entitled to it and the day that he eventually received it. The recipient of the compensation would receive that sum in addition to the final settlement.

The precedent is well established. Claimants under the compulsory purchase code are entitled to claim interest on their outstanding compensation. My argument is simple: the same arrangement should apply to fur farmers who are put out of business by the Bill. I can see no reason why that should not be the case. If the hon. Member for Garston and the Minister disagree and reject the amendment, I will be interested to learn how they justify treating some victims of compulsory purchase differently from others. I speculate that they will find that a difficult position to justify.

Amendment No. 27, the last in the group, would insert another new paragraph between subsections (2) and (3). Another unique feature of the Bill, and one of the unique developments that will arise from it, relates to the capital JPEG 39 investment in mink farming. No professional opinion has been given that mink farms' specialised buildings and equipment can be converted to any other type of farming or business use; they will become useless. Amendment No. 27 would address that fact.

If fur farming is banned, the land on which fur farming is practised will have to be cleared. The estimated costs of clearing land to bring it back to green-field site status that I have received are £98,000 for a 6,000—female farm, and £52,000 for a 3,000—female farm. Those are substantial sums. However, land clearing must happen before the land can be put to any other farming or business use.

Amendment No. 27 is another natural justice amendment. It would ensure that the costs of clearance were recognised in the compensation schemes. It would be wholly unreasonable if that were not so. I hope that the hon. Member for Garston will incorporate that provision in the Bill and so ensure that the costs of clearance become claimable under the compensation scheme.

To summarise, amendment No. 24 would allow the inclusion of the reasonable fees of professional advisers in the compensation award, as is the case under the land compensation code. Amendment No. 25 would entitle the recipient of compensation to payment on account of a proportion of the acquiring authority's initial estimate within three months. Again, such entitlement applies under the land compensation code. Amendment No. 26 would entitle the recipient to compound interest on the compensation award, as is the case under the compulsory purchase code. Amendment No. 27 would ensure that the compensation award includes the cost of land clearance. I commend the amendments to the Committee.

However, the figure of 90 per cent. is rather high, particularly in the case of a disputed sum. It is difficult to accept the principle of paying 90 per cent. of a disputed sum, and I do not think that we could accept an amendment to that effect. If there were a discussion on the compensation award, we would have no objection in principle to an interim payment. We are certainly prepared to consider the issue.

On amendment No. 26, we are not against the principle of interest if there is an unreasonably long delay in settling a claim. Obviously, such a provision would need a clear start date for interest payments, and the amendment does not cover that. Again, if the matter went to the Lands JPEG 41 Tribunal, it might want to make a declaration on it as part of its proceedings. As a general principle, however, we are prepared to consider whether interest should be paid in the case of unreasonably long delays.

Amendment No. 27 deals with compensation for the cost of clearing land. We do not dispute that putting a site back into use or making it available for alternative use may have to involve the demolition of some of the buildings and fixtures. I can give the hon. Member for Basingstoke an assurance that, as a general principle, we are prepared to consider the costs of demolition in relation to a claim.

I should, however, make it clear mat the hon. Gentleman underestimated the possibility that some buildings could be put to alternative business use. In that respect, it would not be unreasonable for us to consider claims on their individual merits. Individual fur farmers would need to demonstrate that the costs of demolition were absolutely essential to reclaiming the site. They would also have to demonstrate that they had properly considered whether there was an appropriate alternative use for the buildings or fixtures on the site. That is not unreasonable, and we would have to be persuaded that those issues had been considered. If the case were made that demolition was absolutely necessary, we would, again, be prepared to consider the implications of that.

The clause provides for prohibition and powers of forfeiture and entry to take effect on 1 January 2002. The purpose of the delay is to give farmers still in the fur farming industry an opportunity to adjust their affairs and to wind down their businesses in advance, should they wish to do so. The power to make a compensation scheme will come into force two months after the Bill receives Royal Assent. That provision enables the Minister and fur farmers to get on with dealing with the compensation issue well in advance of the date on which the Bill will take effect. I hope that the clause provides maximum flexibility for farmers to move out of the industry as soon as they wish and not to have to wait until the date on which fur fanning is formally banned.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 7 ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Bill, as amended, to be reported.
