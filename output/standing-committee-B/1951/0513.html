Clause 1.
—(POWER FOR SHOPKEEPER TO SELL UNCOLLECTED GOODS RECEIVED FOR REPAIR OR OTHER TREATMENT.)

The Chairman
: I should like to call attention to the fact that adequate notice should be given of Amendments, as moving them without notice causes inconvenience. As a general rule, I do not intend to call manuscript Amendments.

Motion made, and Question proposed, "That the Clause stand part of the Bill."

Miss Elaine Burton
: I do not wish the Committee to agree to this Motion, and it may be of assistance if I explain very briefly the reasons for various Amendments which appear on the Order Paper in my own name and the names of the other sponsors of the Bill.

The Government have allowed me the services of their advisers, who, while preserving the substance of the Bill, have redrafted it in terms which may be of more precise legal meaning, and I should like to explain this when we come to the actual new Clauses. While the substance of the Bill has been preserved, certain provisions which I shall describe later have been added in order to safeguard customer's interests.

Mr. Coldrick
: It may be in order for me to give an indication why some of my hon. Friends and myself have put down certain Amendments. We did so in order that we might receive some clear explanation of what is now contained in the Clause. I will give a general welcome to the Bill, because I think it will render a very useful service, but, when we put down these Amendments, we did so for purposes of clarification.

1326 First of all, we want to know precisely what was meant by the term "shop," and if the term was sufficiently extensive in order to cover factories, laundries, and places of that nature. I take it, for instance, that if there is a business which has a repair factory, and boots and shoes are delivered to the shop and then collected from the shop and taken to the factory, later being returned to the shop and collected by the customer, these people are covered in this Bill.

Then, there is a practice which is quite common in the Co-operative movement, and I know much more about that than certain other practices, in which we find that a laundry is attached to the business of the society. Instead of the articles being delivered to the society, they are for the sake of economy taken direct to the laundry and returned from the laundry to the customer.

In such cases, we were not certain, as the Bill was originally drafted, whether that could be considered as coming within the definition of the term "shop," although, obviously, it is connected with the same business and the practice is very similar to what happens with boot and shoe repairs, except for the fact that the goods do not actually take the same course of passing through the shop. Obviously, the work has been executed according to the customer's requirements, and I take it that the whole purpose of the Bill is to see that people who have carried out repairs at the request of another person shall be paid for the work actually done, and it was on that point that we merely wanted some clarification.

Lastly, in the same connection, we frequently find in the Co-operative movement—and I suppose it is common to all businesses—that, in the case of a small society or even a number of small societies, where it would be obviously impossible to set up a boot repairing factory or a laundry to meet the requirements of a limited number of members, these local societies federate for that purpose, and we find that they operate a local factory covering a number of societies. Again, the goods are deposited directly with the boot repairing factory or laundry, and, after repairs have been executed the goods are returned directly to the customer.

JPEG 1327 In so far as these practices are covered by the Bill, we shall be happy to withdraw our Amendments, but I should like to have an assurance upon this particular point, because, as I have read the Bill, it seems to me that these practices may not be covered.

Miss Burton
: In reply to my hon. Friend, may I say that the point which he has made is now covered in the new Clauses.

NEW CLAUSE.
—(POWER OF BAILEES TO SELL GOODS ACCEPTED FOR REPAIR OR OTHER TREATMENT BUT NOT REDELIVERED.)

This Act shall apply in relation to the bailment of any goods accepted (whether before or after the commencement of this Act) by the bailee, in the course of a business, for repair or other treatment on the terms (express or implied) that they will be re-delivered to the bailor or in accordance with bailor's directions when the repair or other treatment has been carried out and on payment to the bailee of such charges as may be agreed between the parties or as may be reasonable.

  1. Where goods accepted as aforesaid are ready for re-delivery but the bailor fails both—
    1. to pay or tender to the bailee his charges in relation to the goods; and
    2. to take delivery of the goods or, if the terms of the bailment so provide, to give directions as to their delivery, 1328 the bailee shall, subject to the provisions of any agreement between him and the bailor and to the following provisions of this section, be entitled, while the failure continues, to sell the goods.
  2. The bailee shall not be entitled by virtue of the last foregoing subsection to sell goods accepted by him for repair or other treatment unless the following provisions are complied with, that is to say:—
    1. where the goods are so accepted after the commencement of this Act, a notice is, at the time of the acceptance, conspicuously displayed in the part of the premises used or appropriated for the acceptance for repair or other treatment of goods of the class to which the goods accepted belong, indicating that the acceptance by the bailee of goods of that class for repair or other treatment is subject to the provisions of this Act and that this Act confers on the bailee a right of sale exercisable in certain circumstances after an interval of not less than twelve months from the date on which the goods are ready for re-delivery;
    2. after the goods are ready for redelivery, or after the commencement of this Act, whichever is the later, the bailee gives to the bailor a notice that the goods are ready for re-delivery, being a notice complying with the requirements of subsection (6) of this section;
    3. after the expiration of the period of twelve months beginning with the date of the giving of the notice that the goods are ready for re-delivery and not less than fourteen days before the sale of the goods, the bailee gives to the bailor a notice of his intention to sell the goods, being a notice complying with the requirements of the said subsection (6),
    and the bailee shall not be so entitled to sell the goods otherwise than by auction;
  3. Provided that where the notice of the bailee's intention to sell the goods states, in addition to the matters required to be contained therein by the said subsection (6), the lowest price which he is prepared to accept on a sale of the goods by virtue of this section, he may sell the goods for not less than that price otherwise than by auction.
  4. Where at any time before the giving of the notice of the bailee's intention to sell the goods a dispute arises between the bailor and the bailee by reason of the bailor's refusal to pay the sum which the bailee claims to be due to him by way of his charges in relation to the goods, or to take delivery thereof or give directions as to their delivery, on the ground that the charges are excessive or that the bailor is not satisfied that the repair or other treatment of the goods has been properly carried out, the bailee's right to sell the goods shall be suspended until the dispute is determined.
  5. A dispute shall be treated for the purposes of this Act as having been determined if, but only if, the bailee at any time after the dispute has arisen gives to the bailor a notice (hereinafter in this Act referred to as JPEG 1329 a "notice to treat the dispute as determined")—
    1. stating that, unless within the period of one month beginning with the date of the giving of the notice, the bailor objects thereto, the dispute will at the expiration of that period be treated for the purposes of this Act as having been determined; and
    2. in other respects complying with the requirements of subsection (6) of this section,
    and within the said period of one month the bailor does not object to the notice; and where the dispute is treated for the purposes of this Act as having been determined, subsection (3) of this section shall have effect in relation to the goods which are the subject of the dispute as if paragraph ( b) thereof were omitted and as if for the reference in paragraph ( c) thereof to the notice that the goods are ready for re-delivery there were substituted a reference to the notice to treat the dispute as determined.
  6. A notice required or authorised by this section to be given by the bailee to the bailor must contain a sufficient description of the goods to which the notice relates and a statement of the sum which the bailee claims to be due to him by way of his charges in relation to the goods, together with—
    1. in the case of a notice that the goods are ready for re-delivery or a notice to treat the dispute as determined, a statement that if the bailor fails, within the period of twelve months beginning with the date of the giving of the notice, both to pay the said sum and to take delivery of the goods or give directions as to their delivery they are liable to be sold in accordance with the provisions of this Act;
    2. in the case of a notice of the bailee's intention to sell the goods, a statement of the date on which the notice that the goods were ready for re-delivery or, as the case may be, the notice to treat the dispute as determined was given and a statement that if the bailor fails, within the period of fourteen days beginning with the date of the giving of the notice of the bailee's intention to sell the goods, both to pay the said sum and to take delivery of the goods or give directions as to their delivery they are liable to be sold in accordance with the provisions of this Act.—[ Miss Burton.]

Miss Burton
: I beg to move, "That the Clause be read a Second time."

As I explained at the beginning of our proceedings, we have been fortunate in having the services of the Government's advisers on the drafting of this Bill. Furthermore, I stated that although the wording has been altered the substance of the Bill has been left very much the same.

10.45 a.m.

I might perhaps explain here that the advisers have thought it necessary to use 1330 the technical terms "bailment," "bailor" and "bailee," which have a precise legal meaning. I am informed that a bailment is a delivery of goods by one person called a bailor to another person called a bailee, for a specific purpose with a view to the re-delivery of the goods to the bailor after the purpose has been fulfilled. The bailor may be described as the customer and the bailee as the trader. It may be clearer to the Committee, and certainly it will be easier for me, if subsequently I refer to the bailor as the customer and to the bailee as the trader.

The particular kind of bailment or transaction with which this Bill deals is a delivery of goods by the customer to the trader for repair or other treatment with a view to re-delivery after the repair or other treatment has been completed and after payment of a charge agreed between the trader and the customer or a reasonable charge if there is no agreed charge.

Subsections (1) and (2) of this new Clause provide that where a trader has accepted goods from a customer for repair or other treatment the trader may, if certain conditions are fulfilled, sell the goods if the customer fails both to pay or to tender the amount due to the trader or fails to take delivery of the goods or arrange for their delivery. The Clause applies whether the goods are handed in by the customer at the premises of the trader or sent to or collected by the trader, and to goods accepted before or after the Bill comes into operation.

Subsection (3) states the conditions which must be fulfilled before the trader may sell. These are, first, that a notice must be displayed in the premises of the trader where goods may be handed in for repair or other treatment calling the attention of customers to the fact that ( a) this Bill, when it becomes an Act, applies to goods of that kind accepted by the trader, and ( b) that the Bill confers a right of sale in certain circumstances. This condition, of course, does not apply where the goods were accepted before this Bill comes into operation.

The second condition is that the trader has given the customer notice that the goods are ready for re-delivery. The third condition is that not less than 12 months later the trader has given the customer notice that he intends to sell the goods, and at least 14 days have elapsed from the giving of that notice.

JPEG 1331 The reason for requiring the display of a notice on the trader's premises is to let the customer know when he hands in his goods that this Measure gives the trader a right to sell those goods in certain circumstances. This will not help customers who, because their goods are either sent or collected, do not go to the trader's premises, but I think it would be difficult for the trader in those circumstances to call the attention of customers to his rights under the Bill, and it would be unreasonable to require him to do so.

On the second point, the reason for requiring a notice to be given to the customer that the goods are ready for redelivery is to ensure that the customer is informed that the work on the goods has been completed and that they are ready for him. The reason for requiring a notice of intention to sell the goods at least twelve months after notice that the goods are ready is to give the customer both an ample opportunity to pay what he owes and what is, in effect, a final reminder. It is clearly necessary to allow 14 days after giving this notice so as to allow the customer to act upon it if he wishes. The goods must be sold by auction unless the trader has included in his notice of intention to sell them the price at which he is prepared to sell them—in which case he may sell them otherwise than by auction, provided he does not sell them for less than that price.

Subsection (5) states that as a dispute may arise between a customer and a trader as to whether the work has been properly carried out or as to whether the charges are excessive, the Clause provides that if such a dispute arises before the trader has given notice of his intention to sell the goods, the trader's right to sell is suspended while the dispute continues.

The dispute can only be ended for the purposes of the Bill, and the trader's right of sale revived, by the trader giving to the customer a notice to treat the dispute as determined. If the customer does not object to that notice within one month the dispute is then treated as ended. This notice takes the place of the notice informing the customer that the goods are ready for re-delivery, and the 12-month period which must elapse before the giving of the notice of an intention to sell the goods runs from it. 1332 Subsection (6) provides that the notices must contain sufficient information to enable the customer to know the rights of the trader under the Bill.

Perhaps I should stress here as a general point that the Bill will not apply automatically to goods of any kind accepted by a trader for repair or other treatment. It will not apply at all unless ( a) in the case of goods accepted after the Bill comes into force the trader has taken the preliminary step of displaying a notice calling the attention of customers to the fact that the Act, as it will then be, applies to the acceptance of goods of that kind and that it confers a right of sale in certain circumstancea; and ( b) in the case of goods accepted before the Bill comes into force, if he takes the preliminary step of sending out a notice, in accordance with the requirement of the Bill, that the goods are ready for redelivery. In addition, the Bill will not apply if the customer agrees with the trader that it shall not apply.

NEW CLAUSE.
—(NOTICES.)

(1) In this Act the expression "notice" means a notice in writing.

  1. A notice required or authorised by this Act to be given by a bailee to a bailor shall, where the bailor is a corporation, be duly given if it is given to the secretary or clerk of the corporation.
  2. A notice required or authorised by this Act to be given by a bailee to a bailor of the bailee's intention to sell the goods shall be given by sending it by post in a registered letter.
  3. A notice required or authorised by this Act to be given by a bailee to a bailor, other than a notice of the bailee's intention to sell the goods, may be given either—
    1. by delivering it to the bailor; or
    2. by leaving it at his proper address; or
    3. by post.
  4. For the purposes of this section, and of section twenty-six of the Interpretation Act, 1889, in its application to this section, the proper address of a person to whom a notice falling within the provisions of subsection (3) or (4) of this section is required or authorised to be given shall, in the case of the secretary or clerk of a corporation, be that of the registered or principal office of the corporation, and, in any other case, be the last known address of the person to whom the notice is to be given.—[ Miss Burton.]

NEW CLAUSE.
—(PROCEDURE AFTER SALE.)

(1) Where goods are sold by virtue of the provisions of this Act, any amount by which the gross proceeds of the sale exceed the charges of the bailee in relation to the goods shall be recoverable by the bailor from the bailee and any amount by which the said charges exceed the gross proceeds of the sale shall be recoverable by the bailee from the bailor.

  1. Where goods are so sold, the bailee shall, before the expiration of the period of seven days beginning with the date of the sale of the goods, prepare a record in relation to the goods containing the following particulars, that is to say—
    1. a sufficient description of the goods;
    2. the method, date and place of the sale;
    3. the name and address of the buyer;
    4. the amount of the gross proceeds of the sale; and
    5. a statement of each item of the charges of the bailee in relation to the goods and the transaction for which each item relates,
    and shall, during the period of six years beginning with the date on which the record is prepared, keep the record together with a copy of the notice of the bailee's intention to sell the goods and the certificate of posting of the letter in which the notice was sent, and shall at any reasonable time during the said period of six years, if so requested by or on behalf of the bailor, produce the record, copy and certificate, or any of them, for inspection by the bailor or a person nominated in that behalf by him.
