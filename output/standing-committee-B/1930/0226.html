AGRICULTURAL MARKETING BILL.

clause Clause 1.
title —(Submission and approval of schemes.)

(1) A scheme regulating the marketing of an agricultural product to which this Act applies by the producers thereof, or of any kind or variety of such a product by the producers of that kind or variety, may be submitted to the Minister by the persons specified in Part I of the First Schedule to this Act, and the Minister may, subject to the provisions of this section, approve the scheme.

(2) A scheme submitted and approved as aforesaid may be applicable to Great Britain or to any part thereof, and nothing in any scheme shall apply to any product, in so far as it is produced outside the area to which the scheme is applicable.

(3) Before approving a scheme, the Minister shall cause to be published, in the Gazette and in such other manner as he thinks best for informing persons affected, notice of the submission of the scheme, of the place where copies thereof may be inspected, and of the time within which, and the manner in which, representations with respect to the scheme may be made.

(4) The Minister, after considering any scheme duly submitted to him and any representations duly made with respect thereto and after holding such inquiries (if any) as he thinks fit, may make such modifications in the scheme as he thinks proper:

Provided that—

  1. no scheme shall be modified so as to be applicable to any area to which it would not have been applicable without modification; and
  2. before making any such modifications, the Minister shall give notice of the proposed modifications to the persons submitting the scheme, and if within three weeks after notice has been so given those persons notify the Minister that they desire to withdraw the scheme, the Minister shall take no further action under this section.

(5) If the Minister,, after making such modifications (if any) as aforesaid, is satisfied that the scheme will conduce to the Col. 1122 more efficient production and marketing of the regulated product, he may, after consultation with the Board of Trade, lay before each House of Parliament a draft of the scheme, and unless either House, before the expiration of a period of twenty days on which that House has sat next after the draft is laid before it, resolves that the scheme shall not be approved, the Minister may make an order approving the scheme in terms of the draft, and the scheme shall come into force on such date after the expiration of the said period as may be specified in the order, and the making of the order shall be conclusive evidence that the requirements of this Act have been complied with and that the order and the scheme approved thereby have been duly made and approved and are within the powers conferred by this Act.

(6) A scheme may be amended or revoked in accordance with the provisions of Part II of the First Schedule to this Act.

The object of the proposed new Clause is to get over the mechanical difficulty which the Minister raised in answer to my right hon. Friend the Member for Stafford (Mr. Ormsby-Gore) and I submit that it gets over that difficulty completely. The procedure suggested is that a scheme shall be approved by the Minister in the way proposed in the Bill; that it shall be laid before Parliament and approved by both Houses, and that it shall then come into force. Then all the producers will have to be either registered or exempted, but no interference with the business methods of the registered producers will take place unless they themselves, on a poll, by a majority of 75 per cent. of the producers and the product, declare that they wish to put into force the coercive parts of the Bill. That would give us what we are asking for, and would, I think, also give the Minister what he is asking for. It would enable the boards to be set up and the boards could then start on a voluntary basis. They could prepare their lists of producers and the registered producers would be separated from the exempted producers. They would be empowered to start marketing the pro- Image Col. 1127 duct just as co-operative societies have done in the past—just as the Scottish Milk Pool or English Hop-Growers Limited, have done. They could get grants from the Government and advances from the bank against the value of the crop; they could have their special mark and they could decide the manner in which their produce was to be graded and packed. They could buy manures and implements wholesale and allow their members to have those articles cheaply. In short, they could get into their stride.

Furthermore—what is even more important—they would have several months in which to work out their compulsory marketing scheme in detail. They could put their proposal before their members who would have ample time to consider it. The members would know the personnel of the board. They would presumably have acquired confidence in the membership of the board, or else they would have changed it because the board would have been elected by the registered producers. Thus an agreed scheme, as it were, could be laid before the members of the marketing organisation and if 75 per cent. of the producers and the product were in favour of it, then the compulsory and coercive part of the scheme could be put into force. Gentleman has to carry agricultural opinion with him in this matter.
