Clause 1.
—(INCREASE OF ROAD HAULIERS' RADIUS OF OPERATIONS.)

Motion made, and Question proposed, "That the Clause stand part of the Bill."

Mr. H. Hynd
: I am sorry that you have not seen fit to select the first Amendment, Mr. Diamond, which would seek to leave out the radius of 60 miles and to insert one of 26 miles. I do not propose to contest your Ruling in any way, of course. Clause 1 raises the fundamental issue which we are here to discuss; it brings us straightaway to the vital part of the Bill and to its real purpose. If by some mischance it were to become law it would completely destroy the Transport Act, 1947.

The core of the matter is the suggestion that we should extend the range of private operations from 25 miles to 60 miles and that, of course, is quite contrary to the provisions of the original Act, the purpose of which was said to be:

"To provide or secure or promote the provision of an efficient, adequate, economical and properly integrated system of public inland transport."

A subsidiary object of the Act was that this transport should be conducted on a self-supporting basis, and my first submission is that if the Bill were accepted that would not be possible. The objects of the promoters of the Bill are obvious; indeed, I would say that if the Transport Act is to survive in any real sense at all for road haulage, then we must not accept the figure of 60 as the radius of private operations. A radius of 60 miles would mean a diameter of 120 miles and that, in turn, would mean that by choosing certain selected centres throughout the country the private operators could take the whole of the industrial work of the country, could cover the whole of the industrial area and thereby could take the cream of the traffic and leave the Road Haulage Commission as a quite ineffective organisation.

In order to discuss this question properly we must consider why the figure of 25 was selected in the first place as being a reasonable radius beyond which traffic would be regarded as long-distance traffic. In the course of the proceedings on the 1947 Act the Minister said quite clearly and quite frankly that 25 was an arbitrary figure—that there was nothing sacred about it. He gave fairly lengthy 744 reasons, which I do not propose to repeat, as to why the figure of 25 was eventually selected as a fair compromise or, at any rate, as the most suitable figure for the purpose. On 19th March, 1947, speaking on this very question, he said:

"In the main [it was] as an administrative convenience to cover the bulk of local transport that takes place."—[OFFICIAL REPORT, 19th March, 1947; Standing Committee B; c. 2081.]

He went on to point out that he had provided in the Bill a certain amount of elasticity. It is laid down in the 1947 Act that permits may be given for journeys beyond that radius for suitable purposes. Unfortunately—and I say this in passing—the object of the Act has subsequently been largely, if not destroyed. at any rate considerably changed by the generosity of the Minister about C licences. In my opinion he has given far too many of them.

This 25-mile radius gave an operating area of some 2,000 square miles. I will not argue over that figure, for I remember the arguments which took place before as to whether it was 2,000 square miles and I remember that some statistician stated that it was 1,875. In any event, it is something in the region of 2,000 square miles. The proposal in the Bill would increase that area to something over 11,000 square miles—a very considerable increase which, I submit, would completely destroy the purpose and the effectiveness of the Road Haulage Commission's work.

Dealing with the point of what is a suitable figure at which to draw a line between short-distance and long-distance traffic, I would draw attention to some evidence which was given before the Royal Commission on Transport in 1930 by Sir Maxwell Hicks, Chairman of the Long-Distance Road Haulage Committee of Inquiry—a Committee which I understand consisted of the principal firms in the industry. He submitted a Memorandum to that Royal Commission in which he said:

"For the purpose of this Memorandum and of the investigations made by the Committee, a long-distance haulier has been taken to be a person, firm or company who is the owner of mechanically-propelled vehicles of any weight who for his own profit utilises those vehicles for the transport of goods by road belonging to other persons for distances in excess of 30 miles from the base from which such vehicles operate."

JPEG 745 That was the opinion of one of the principal men in the industry who regarded the figure of 30 miles as the division between short-distance and long-distance traffic.

In 1946 the Road Haulage Association issued a booklet about the shortcomings of road transport control in Germany and in that booklet they defined long-distance road haulage as a distance—not a radius —"in excess of 50 kilometres," which gives us just over 30 miles.
