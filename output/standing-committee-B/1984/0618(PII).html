New Clause 33
SPONSORED SUPERANNUATION SCHEMES

"Section 226 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1970 shall be amended by inserting after subsection 10 the following subsection—

'(10A) Restrictions on reliefs and covenants payable under a sponsored superanuation scheme shall not apply where the service related to the scheme is the building or construction industry and the scheme in question is the building and civil engineering benefits scheme.'.".—[ Dr. McDonald.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Dr. Oonagh McDonald
(Thurrock)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

I hope that the drafting of this new clause is sufficiently tight for the Government to consider making this concession, and tight enough to exclude misuse by making a double tax claim on contributions to pension schemes. I understand that we may not have succeeded in our purpose, but I hope that we have.

Let me give some of the background. In the early 1980s, and by 1982, the employer and trade union organisations in the building and construction industry had negotiated a lump sum retirement benefit scheme to provide an industry-wide retirement benefit for its workers. The scheme took account of the highly mobile and casual nature of employment in the construction industry. Such a scheme could only be an advance on anything that had gone before in an industry where employment conditions are notoriously poor, The employers and trade unions were anxious to ensure that the scheme continued.

The provisions of the scheme form part of the standard terms and conditions of employment for building and civil engineering workers employed under the national working rules, which are agreed by employers and trade unions. The scheme is run in conjunction with the industry's "holidays with pay" arrangements. It includes a "death in service" benefit, and it is called the building and civil engineering benefit scheme. Currently it covers about 500,000 workers, of whom between 350,000 and 400,000 are active members, and they are employed by about 15,000 participating employers.

The scheme, which began in April 1982, is not contracted out of the state earnings-related arrangements. It provides a modest lump sum payment on retirement, based on the worker's weeks of construction industry service from the commencement date, multiplied by a 622 retirement benefit rate. Employers pay weekly contributions, the level of which is determined jointly by both sides of the industry. The retirement benefit rate is determined by the trustees acting on actuarial advice, and any increase is subject to the prior approval of the Superannuation Funds Office.

Benefit is payable, whether or not the worker is still employed in the construction industry, on his 65th birthday, and the benefit rate is that applicable at the date of retirement, irrespective of the worker's circumstances at that date. In this way cover and preservation for early leavers, who are not penalised if they leave the industry permanently or temporarily, is provided. Workers do not contribute to the scheme, but it is an approved arrangement under the Finance Act 1970. The fact that workers do not contribute to the scheme has given rise to part of the problem. Clearly, such a scheme is necessary because people go in and out of the building industry, depending on the work available. Obviously it is more difficult for the older workers to obtain work and, curiously enough, the more skilled a worker is at his trade, the more difficult it can be to find reasonably well paid work.

The scheme is exceptional and designed to deal with the industry-wide problem of the employment of a large and rapidly changing labour force. It does not provide a pension, and after 40 years' service the lump sum would amount to no more than the annual wage current at the date of retirement. The lump sum payable on retirement is often small—only a few hundred pounds.

Before the scheme was introduced in April 1982, several workers had the section 226 policy on which tax relief was given on the annual premium. However, the Inland Revenue classifies that scheme as a sponsored superannuation scheme for the purpose of section 226 of the Taxes Act 1970. That ruling means that individual building and civil engineering workers are no longer eligible to pay, or to continue to pay, contributions on retirement annuity contracts. That gives rise to the difficulties.

We do not know how many workers are contributing to the personal retirement annuity contracts—that is an opportunity that is now denied to them. It is thought that only a small proportion of the total work force is affected by this, but the construction industry thinks that this interpretation of the 1970 Act is most unjust in that it discourages workers from making further retirement provision. They do not get a pension, but only this small lump sum paid on retirement. The building industry seeks a limited concession for these workers because it needs to provide some industry-wide coverage.

There are no contributions to the scheme from operatives, and so it seems unreasonable to deny a worker who will benefit from this scheme tax relief on any personal pension plans that he may make.

I sought to ensure that the new clause was drawn as tightly as possible, but I understand that there has been considerable discussion on it between the industry and the Inland Revenue, and that the Inland Revenue hoped that it would be possible to bring forward an amendment which the Government would accept.

Of course there may be two reasons for this hesitation. The Government are planning to change pension schemes in general, and it may be necessary to wait for that. Alternatively, the Inland Revenue may be hesitant to introduce an amendment that could allow people tax JPEG 623 relief on more than one pension scheme. I understand that hesitation. That is why we have drawn the new clause in this way.

Our proposal may not be successful, and the Minister may consider that it will not be successful. If so, perhaps he will consider and find a solution to the problem for building workers of being in and out of work and working for one employer, then another, often in unsatisfactory conditions, without decent pension or holiday pay provisions, a problem that does not face such a large number of workers in other industries. Employers should produce some basic scheme—it does not seem an enormous amount—for their employees when they reach retirement. It would give them some protection, and I am pleased that the industry and the unions have succeded in coming to agreement on a scheme of this kind.

The Government should look sympathetically at the problems of the building industry and find some way to ensure fair treatment for building workers without opening up loopholes for others to use. The Minister has heard enough from the Opposition to know that we do not favour tax avoidance loopholes of any kind, and are only too anxious to assist in closing them. I trust that the Minister will look at the problems of the building industry and ensure that the new clause, or some suitable version of it, is accepted to protect this superannuation scheme.

Mr. Hirst
: The purposes of the new clauses is to correct an anomaly in the provisions relating to group 628 relief. It is not now possible to obtain relief for a tax loss partly as group relief and partly as consortium relief. For example, where a consortium company makes a loss it can surrender it up to other members of the consortium by way of consortium relief, or down to its subsidiaries by group relief. In either case the surrender may be for part or all of the losses. However, a consortium company cannot surrender part of its loss up to consortium members and part of it down to the subsidiaries on group relief.

A similar situation arises where a consortium member makes a loss. That loss can be surrendered by way of group relief to other companies in the same group or by way of consortium relief down to the consortium company, but that canot be surrendered partly by consortium relief and partly by group relief.

That restriction is both illogical and unnecessary. Furthermore, it can lead to real hardship where there are insufficient profits to relieve the loss solely by group relief or solely by consortium relief, and part of the losses may have to go unrelieved.

The consortium is of considerable commercial importance. Equipment leasing, major construction projects, cable television, shipping, electronics and space development are just some of the vital industries that can depend on the consortium's structure. Many highly successful developments have taken place through a consortium. Indeed, many new projects are of such a scale that they can be financed only through a consortium but it is not fanciful to think that consortia or their subsidiaries will not make tax losses and there fore will require the opportunity to seek relief.

New clause 4 is a fairly straightforward way of correcting the anomaly that I have outlined. New clause 5 is an alternative method that is slightly more complicated. Either way, there is an unjustifiable anomaly that will be potentially harmful to the development of the whole principle of consortia. I hope that my hon. Friend recognises that point when he responds to the new clause.

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. John Moore)
: I congratulate my hon. Friend for Strathkelvin and Bearsden (Mr. Hirst) on his brief but excellent introduction to an area which, as the hon. Member for Sedgefield (Mr. Blair) said quite legitimately, is extremely complex. I congratulate the hon. Member for Sedgefield on his analysis of the interlocking dual problems that linger through new clauses 4 and 5 as well as the different kind of attempt in new clause 30 to answer the problems. It would be helpful if I tried to go through carefully this area, which is not only complex but important to the outside world and thereby provided an answer to most of the points raised.

Last year we made major changes to the conditions for consortium relief. We increased the maximum permitted number of member companies in a consortium and allowed some of its members to be individuals or non-resident companies. These changes which were widely welcomed, expanded the scope of consortium relief, but they did not tackle the problems to which my hon. Friends' new clauses draw attention. My hon. Friend the Member for Croydon, South (Sir W. Clark) who, sadly, is not a member of the Committee this year has been a persistent and ardent advocate of the consortium relief changes which we are considering tonight. His name is on the original new clauses.

Under the present rules, and in contrast to a joint venture undertaken in the form of a partnership, a company jointly owned by a consortium is, like any other company, assessed to tax in its own right. Any profits or losses arising cannot be attributed for tax purposes to the companies which own it.

But, it is possible, under the group relief provisions, for the current losses and certain other excess reliefs of a jointly owned consortium company to be surrendered to the companies which own it for relief against profits arising concurrently; that is what the hon. Member for Sedgefield was describing and it is known colloquially as "consortium relief. Likewise, the consortium member companies may surrender losses to the jointly owned consortium company if that company itself has profits.

However, there are two particular limitations at present which can restrict the benefit of these group relief 630 provisions to consortia. Both concern the situation where a company is at the same time in group relationship with one company, and is also either a company jointly owned by a consortium, or is one of the joint owners of such a company. First, if a company of that kind incurs a loss and so on, that loss or other amount may be surrendered either to members of its own group as group relief, or as consortium relief, but not partly as one, and partly as the other. That is the problem addressed by new clause 4.

Secondly, if a member of a jointly owned consortium company cannot use its share of the consortium loss and so on, perhaps because it too has losses, then that share can only be carried forward by the jointly owned consortium company. It cannot "flow through" and be claimed as group relief by other companies in the consortium members' group. Similarly, flow through is not at present permitted in the reverse direction, where the losses arise in the consortium members' group, and it is the jointly owned consortium company which has profits. This is the problem addressed by new clause 5. New clause 30, in turn, is concerned with both problems.

New clause 4 seeks to allow "mixed claims", provided the consortium leg of the relationship exists and satisfies the conditions for consortium relief for the whole of the corresponding accounting periods of the claimant and surrendering companies.

9.30 p.m.

I think the intention here is to avoid the enormous complications that can arise where a company joins a consortium or group in the same year. It is in these circumstances that we have to be careful not to allow excessive relief. However, this new clause recognises only one part of the problem—the bit which arises when the company becomes a member of the consortium. We are left with the possibility of over-relief in the circumstances where the company becomes a member of a group in the year in which the loss is incurred.

Moreover, the purpose of the existing rule in section 263, as I am sure that the hon. Member for Sedgefield knows, is to stop a member of a consortium from getting too much relief in the case where it is the jointly owned consortium company which has the losses and where that consortium company itself has a subsidiary with profits. The right result in this situation would be for the consortium member to be entitled to no more than his proportionate share in the overall losses incurred by the jointly owned consortium company and its subsidiary. The new clause—even without its technical defects—does not tackle this need to ensure that too much relief is not given.

The object of new clause 5 is to allow the relevant share of a loss incurred by a company jointly owned by a consortium to "flow through" and be relieved anywhere in the member company's group, and not just by the member company itself. I assume that the intention is also to allow flow through of losses in the reverse direction—where the group companies have losses and the company jointly owned by the consortium has profits.

However, the clause has a number of technical defects and does not achieve these results. The anomaly has been identified, but the results sought are not achieved.

New clause 30 seeks to overcome these difficulties JPEG 631 by introducing a completely different approach to the taxation of jointly owned consortium companies. Instead of treating the jointly owned company as a person taxable in its own right—which it is—the clause would treat the profits or losses of such a company for tax purposes as accruing to the member companies which own it. These profits or losses would then be treated in the hands of the member companies in the same way as any other profits or losses arising from the companies' own activities.

The clause has a number of technical defects and does not achieve this result. And in some respects it goes much wider than I am sure my honourable Friend intends.

Thus, for example, because the appropriate share of the losses of a jointly owned consortia company would be treated as a loss of the member company itself, that loss could be relieved more widely by the member than if it had claimed them instead as group relief. For instance, the losses could be set against profits of the member company arising in the preceding year. By contrast, the group relief provisions, under which losses of one company can be set against the profits of another, allow losses to be relieved only against current profits, and not those of earlier periods. The clause would therefore be inconsistent with the existing group relief provisions.

There could be other problems, for instance where a member company left the consortium. In these circumstances, any losses attributed to the member company which remained unrelieved at that time would be lost, and, unlike under the present rules, these could not be carried forward by the jointly owned consortium company itself.

We recognise that there is a good case in principle for allowing both mixed claims and flow through of losses. But we have to find solutions which ensure that neither too much nor too little relief in total is given, and which also cater for the various further complications that can arise, for example, as a result of the provisions having to apply to different kinds of losses, and as a result of companies joining or leaving groups or consortia part way through their accounting periods. In short, the problems here are those of finding workable solutions that do not involve excessive legislative and administrative complexity. Moreover, it is not possible to consider either of these problems in isolation; we believe they should be considered together.

Inevitably, action to implement these proposals will entail adding further legislative and administrative complexity to what is already, technically, a very complex part of the tax code. For this reason, we decided that first priority in this year's Finance Bill had to be given to completing the business tax reforms begun last year and to getting the taxation of capital gains on to a sounder footing.

To make these changes possible, however some new rules and some tightening up of certain existing rules will be needed. We propose that the changes should take effect for accounting periods commencing on or after 1 August 1985.

The overall effect of these proposals will be to allow those companies that are, at the same time, in group relationship with one company and consortium relationship with another, greater flexibility in the use of group and consortium relief. New clause 7 provides an opportunity for a brief debate. I appreciate that in drafting it I have not addressed some of its technicalities, and it may be defective. I wish to cover three aspects—first, what an industrial revenue bond is; secondly, why it will benefit the economy; and, thirdly, what it will cost and how it should be paid for.

First, it could be more properly termed an industrial development bond rather than an industrial revenue bond, the name by which it has been known in the United States for many years. It is proposed that principally small and medium-sized companies should issue bonds. They are bonds in that they would be secured on the issuers' assets. In the case of weak credits, they would be guaranteed by a third-party institution such as a pension fund or insurance company. In the United States, it is the practice for commercial banks to guarantee the security of bonds through letters of credit, but that arrangement would probably be inappropriate here.

The bonds would be long term in that their maturities would be perhaps 10 years. They are distinct from the traditional way of financing by overdraft which applies to much of industry in this country. The bonds would be largely fixed-rate rather than floating-rate. The interest on them would be free of taxation to the personal recipients, although interest would not be allowed free of tax to institutional or corporate holders.

The bonds are designed to provide a tax incentive to individuals. Companies that issued bonds would be entitled to deduct the interest cost for the purposes of corporation tax. The coupon benefit—the interest rate benefit—would in practice be shared between the issuing company and the individual lenders.

The practice in the United States is to share the tax benefit approximately equally. Such an arrangement means that if a company could borrow long term in the debenture market at, say, 15 per cent a coupon, the actual issue cost of the tax-free instrument might be, say, 10 per cent. However, there is a serious question mark about whether companies could borrow in that way because we do not have the long-term debt market for small and medium-sized companies that exists in the United States.

I envisage that the bonds would be packaged by funds such as banks or Investors in Industry and retailed to individuals. It would not be practical to assume that individual companies could afford to distribute their own bonds broadly in the retail market. The bonds would have to be packaged in funds rather like a business expansion scheme. The bonds would be trade-able. There would be a secondary market in them. What is the advantage to the economy of such bonds? The tax advantage has to be targeted specifically on certain types of capital investment. In Kettering, we have an industrial estate which does not enjoy enterprise zone status, and it is very difficult for small and medium-sized companies there to attract sufficient finance not only for the infrastructure work within the industrial estate, but simply to put up the buildings and to buy the plant and equipment with which to create the new jobs. The big advantage of industrial revenue bonds is that they create a long-term bond, where the principal is repaid over a significant number of years. The cash cost to the borrower—the interest cost and the portion of the principal that is redeemed each year—is probably much less than it would be if the company was borrowing from the bank through an overdraft or was borrowing over a comparatively short period. The cash burden of the borrower is minimised and spread over a longer time. If there is a hurdle rate for the discounted cash rate of return that a company needs to achieve, it is easier to achieve that with long-term, comparatively cheap borrowing. Businesses are then in a better position to expand and to create employment.

The scheme is modelled on the tax-exempt industrial development bonds which have been issued in the United States by the states and local authorities. The federal tax exemption was originally intended for bonds issued by the states to fund public projects such as roads and schools, but increasingly the states and local authorities have used the federal tax exemption to raise funds for private sector use as well.

President Reagan's recent tax reform plan includes a proposal to withdraw the exemption, except for bonds confined to public authorities' own use. The Administration's view is that the exemption has proved to be inefficient because for every $2 of interest costs saved by the borrower, the federal Government forgoes more than $3 of tax revenue. So at least one third of the benefit remains with financial intermediaries and higher income investors. The exemption has lowered tax receipts and so caused serious erosion in the income tax base, and has had distortive effects on the economy. In the light of the United States findings and plan to withdraw the exemption, I suggest that we should be cautious about introducing a similar exemption in this country.
