: It is a pleasure to welcome you as Chairman, Mr. Leadbitter, for the duration of the spring incarceration of the Finance Bill. The Opposition were probing the basic rationale of the Government's priorities for imposing duties on the different beverages, such as beer, spirits, table wines and so on. I agree with my hon. Friend the Member for Stoke-on-Trent, Central (Mr. Fisher) that we are dealing with the Government's old fashioned game of "We need the money" syndrome. That phrase was coined so graphically by the Chief Secretary to the Treasury in 1982. It is noticeable that he has been less graphic with his phrases since then because it was such a telling indication of what the Government were about. They need the money. They will get it from anywhere. They will get it by flogging off public property, spreading the burden of taxation in all sorts of insane and unnecessary directions, and from the annual notch up on the taxation on the pleasures of the people.

The Government, which promised, mendaciously, that they would reduce taxation, are now increasing taxes in all possible ways. In their effort to show that they have reduced direct taxes, they have shifted the burden onto indirect taxes and sold public property to make ends meet. It is a gambler's progress. It is a Government of fiddle, fudge and flog.

The Minister's reply cast a strange light on the way in which such matters are decided in Government. The Government have a chance to adjust and influence social consumption patterns. The Government have the chance to plan a strategy towards industry, leisure and towards people's pleasures, but they have only compromised—a little bit on here and a little off there. The Minister said that the Government have to negotiate cleverly round Europe. Fortunately, the Euro-fun party is not present to hear him say that. Its policy towards Europe is to lie down on its back and have its tummy tickled. The Minister intimated the need to adopt a devious approach towards Europe and the need to adjust taxes without incurring displeasure. The Government have given a bit of pleasure to the Scotch whisky industry. There is an adjustment here and a trim there. That is all that there is to it. There is no rational decision about priorities.

It is regrettable that the Government have not taken the opportunity provided for within the framework of taxation to offset the tendency to concentration which 42 gives the big brewer a stranglehold on the trade and is inimical to the real interests of the people whom we represent.
