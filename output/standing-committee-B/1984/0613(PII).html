Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Terry Davis: I should be grateful if the Economic Secretary would explain what is involved in the clause, which appears to involve the abolition of stamp duty on certain instruments concerned with fixed exchange rates for foreign currency.

What is the cost to the Revenue of the clause? Also, why is this provision to take effect at a different date from the other clauses in this part of the Bill? As we have progressed through the clauses, we have become familiar with the date 26 March 1985. Why are we now being asked to legislate for 1 August 1985?

Mr. Ian Stewart: Clause 82 does not provide that duty shall not apply, but that

"Section 12 of the Finance Act 1899 … shall not apply".

I must explain to the hon. Member for Birmingham, Hodge Hill (Mr. Davis) that the clause therefore has no cost; it is not a financial measure.

Clause 82 makes a minor change in stamp duty law. It abolishes—from 1 August 1985—the fixed rates of exchange at which many currencies have to be converted into sterling for stamp duty purposes. From that date, all foreign currencies will be converted at the rate in operation at the time that the instrument was executed.

Fixed rates may have been suitable when exchange rates were relatively stable. It is clearly unsatisfactory that people are now making fortuitous gains or losses on stamp duty simply because market rates have changed since the rate was last fixed. It is a simple proposal and helps to simplify stamp duty law.

There are at present two different rules for converting foreign currency to sterling. With most currencies, the basic rules are provided by the Stamp Act 1891 and the conversion is at the rate applying on the date of the instrument. That will now apply to all transactions.

One of the provisions of the Finance Act 1899 imposed a fixed rate of exchange for 21 foreign currencies which were in active use at the time. They do not, of course, coincide with the 21 currencies most actively used today.

The hon. Member for Hodge Hill asked why clause 82 is to take effect from 1 August. It was thought that that date would be convenient to those involved in currency trading, as it would allow a little notice of the change. The clause does not change the duty, only the way in which it is calculated. The number of cases affected by the clause is relatively small.

The Finance Act 1899 imposed on the Board of Inland Revenue a duty to vary the rates. However, before the changes can be made, the proposed new rates must be advertised for two successive weeks in the London Gazette. With rapidly moving exchange rates the effect is that new rates can be virtually out of date by the time they are published. Therefore, we thought it sensible to make this change.

Mr. Eggar: The amendment relates to the question of the charge to capital transfer tax on the loss of conditional exemption relating to works of art. Recently, much concern has been widely expressed in the media and the other place about the inadequacy of the Government's arrangements for retaining nationally important works of art in this country. There are two procedures for making retention easier: acceptance in lieu of CTT and private treaty sales.

I think that it is generally agreed that the present arrangements do not operate as well as they might. The reasons for that are somewhat complex and relate to the attraction of what is known as the "douceur", which is the abatement of the capital tax due on the sale of an exempted item, which applies when one or other of the two procedures is used. For the "douceur" to have maximum effect, the object must have been exempted from CTT.

Few owners are advised to exempt works of art if they can pay the tax when it is due, which makes it more likely that they will be sold later on the open market.

Conditional exemption is not being taken because the basis on which tax is charged on a subsequent loss of exemption is unfair, and because of the roll-up effect of CTT, it can lead to a very heavy tax charge.

A similar situation existed in the 1930s in reverse, when values of works of art dropped and people were assessed for estate duty at a higher level than that at which they would be assessed if they decided to sell their works of art.

The amendments seek to revert to the pre-1930s position, when the value for tax was taken as the value at the last death. For CTT, it seeks to apply the lower of the current value and the value at the date of the last conditionally exempt transfer.

The amendment is radical but important.
