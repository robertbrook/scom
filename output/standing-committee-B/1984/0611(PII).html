The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Ian Stewart)
: May I say a few words at the beginning of this clause stand part debate, since it is the first clause in chapter IV of the Bill which deals with the the accrued income scheme? That scheme is set out in clauses 69 to 73 and schedules 19 and 20. It embodies a simple concept, which is, unfortunately, difficult, technical and complicated to put into legislation. The concept is that whereas before the introduction of the scheme a person would be charged to income tax on interest actually received in respect of securities while he held them, after the scheme is in force a person will be charged to income tax on the interest that accrues in respect of those securities on a day-to-day basis during the period of ownership.

The purpose of the scheme is that in practice in recent years an increasing number of holders of fixed-interest securities—particularly gilt-edged securities— have sold their investments at a time when the price included a significant element of accrued interest. The advantages to the taxpayer of taking that course have been that the sale price gives rise to a charge not to income tax but to possible capital gains tax. In the case of fixed-interest securities, particularly gilts, it gives rise to no capital gains tax if the gilts have been held for more than one year, because such securities are exempt. Within one year such securities would give rise to a charge to capital gains tax, but only at the capital gains tax rate of 30 per cent which is equivalent to the basic rate and not to the higher rates. of income tax.

The consequence is that the Revenue has been losing a considerable amount of tax which would have been collected if the accured income scheme had been in force. There is nothing intrinsically immoral about the sale of securities under existing legislation. In fact, it has been difficult, under the old regime to sell securites without selling some accrued income with them, as that is how their price is quoted. Virtually every disposal except a few which in timing are closley related to the payment of dividends gives rise to some form of bond washing, whether deliberate or not being dependent upon the circumstances.

It appears to the Government to be right to deal with increasing exploitation of this opportunity, which was 480 at a cost to the Revenue, and that is why we have introduced the scheme. It is complicated in detail, but the concept is simple: of the interest that accrues on a fixed-interest stock between one dividend date and the next, that part which falls within the ownershop of the vendor is to be charged as income to him, and that which falls within the period of ownership of the purchaser will be charged to the income of the purchaser.

There are a number of amendments to schedule 20, and I shall not say anything in detail until we reach that schedule. I shall merely assure hon. Members that none of those amendments involve any change of policy or objective. They are merely designed to make the scheme work more efficiently, largely because of the complexity of relating the accrued-income scheme to the existing provisions for income tax and capital gains tax. Accrued income as defined in the scheme is not technically income. If it were ordinary income it would automatically fall within the normal provisions for income tax. But because it is deemed to be income, special provisions are needed for a range of technical circumstances.

I cannot convince myself—and I do not think that I should be able to convince the Committee—that an event which does not take place until next year and giving rise to tax can be retrospective. Ordinary incomes and claims are apportioned back in the third year to the first and the second, but that is not to say that future tax regimes should be apportioned back automatically to previous years for Lloyd's alone.     Clause 85
    CHARGEABLE PERIODS RELEVANT TO LIMIT ON TAX PAYABLE AND EXPENDITURE SUPPLEMENT

    Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

    Mr. Moore
    : It might help the Committee if I were to say what is the purpose of the clause. The need for the clause arises from the relatively new practice of carrying out extended production tests. Its purpose is to correct two unintended consequences of the rules for PRT safeguard and uplift reliefs which could now arise as a result of such tests. I am sure that every member of the Committee is fully informed about the nature of the interrelationship between PRT uplift, safeguard and. payback. One works in favour of the taxpayer and the other against. Both are anomalous and need to be put right.

    As the changes proposed by the clause are technical, it might be helpful if I explain the two problems and the way in which the clause sets out to solve them.

    First, by way of background, I shall say a word about the PRT system to set the two problems in context. PRT is charged—for chargeable periods of six months—on the receipts from oil and gas production less the expenses incurred in finding, extracting, bringing it ashore and putting it into a saleable state. The expenses allowable include an uplift of 35 per cent on certain capital expenditure, the purpose and scope of which I shall describe a little later.

    If, after deducting any loss carried forward or backward from another period, this computation shows a net profit, an oil allowance representing up to a quarter of a million tonnes of oil is given for the chargeable period of the half year. For offshore fields outside the Southern Basin approved after 1 April 1982, the allowance has been doubled. Any PRT payable after 506 deducting oil allowance is subject for a limited period—this is crucial—to the automatic protection of safeguard relief. The rate of PRT is 75 per cent, and the tax so charged is then deductible in calculating the company's liability to corporation tax.

    Safeguard is intended to give companies a certain degree of additional protection in the early years of a field's life when its economics are most vulnerable.

    Safeguard relief sets a ceiling on the amount of PRT payable for a limited period after first production from a field. Where a participator's adjusted profits for the chargeable period—broadly, income less expenditure other than upliftable expenditure—represents a rate of return of less than 15 per cent on capital, his profits are exempt from PRT. Above this level PRT is limited to 80 per cent—the safeguard taper—of the excess if this is less than the liability under the normal rules.

    The duration of safeguard is governed by the number of chargeable periods between the first chargable period and the chargeable period in which payback is reached. Payback is defined as the chargeable period in which cumulative field income first exceeds cumulative outgoings. Safeguard relief applies for the whole of these periods plus half as many again.

    The problem comes because the first chargeable period is the period ending at the end of the half calendar year in which cumulative production from the field first exceeds 1000 tonnes. That does not usually happen until full production starts, and it follows that normally the safeguard period itself does not begin until full production starts; that is after the field has been developed. However, an extended production test carried out before either a development decision or the incurring of field development expenditure can result in considerable oil production and so trigger the period. Where that happens, there may then be a considerable length of time—possibly some years because oil field development is a lengthy business—where no oil is being produced, but which nevertheless is taken into account in calculating how long safeguard lasts after development costs are recovered. It was never intended that a potentially long unproductive period should be taken into account when the formula for limiting the period of safeguard was introduced in 1981.

    The solution to that problem is in subsection (1). It corrects the anomaly by providing for chargeable periods in which little or no oil is produced to be left out of account for the purpose of calculating how long safeguard lasts after development costs have been recovered.

    I turn now to the second anomaly with which this clause deals. It concerns the determination of the time of payback. This is the time when, broadly, a participator has recovered his development costs and first makes a profit. It is important because payback governs the length of time for which uplift and safeguard reliefs are available. I have explained safeguard in connection with the first anomaly and it may be helpful if I explain uplift. Uplift is given at 35 per cent of qualifying expenditure which, broadly speaking, is initial exploration and development expenditure incurred before the end of the period in which payback in a particular field is reached. Relief is thus given on 135 per cent of the amount of qualifying expenditure. The uplift is compensation for the fact that interest and other costs of financing are not allowable expenses for PRT purposes.

When payback is reached during the period of an extended production test because the value of the oil produced exceeds field expenditure to date, there is a risk that both uplift and safeguard reliefs will be prematurely cut off. Subsection (3) removes that risk by providing that payback is not to be regarded as reached until a field has received development consent and produces, in a chargeable period ending after such consent has been given, more than 1,000 tonnes of oil. That will ensure that the carrying out of extended production tests will not put at risk uplift in respect of the major field development costs and will not have the effect of unduly curtailing the length of a safeguard period.

    Clause 86
    QUALIFYING ASSETS; EXCLUSION OF LAND AND CERTAIN BUILDINGS ETC

    Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

    Mr. Moore
    : I shall explain the clause briefly because there are those outside who are more than interested in our deliberations.

    The purpose of this clause is to remove a charge to PRT which would otherwise arise on any tariff or disposal proceeds arising from land and certain buildings and structures on land—such as a company's head office. This is a limited class of assets in respect of which the acquisition or erection costs, if any, are not allowable against PRT.

    The broad purpose of the charging provisions of the Oil Taxation Act 1983 was to bring into charge to PRT incidental receipts such as tariffs or disposal proceeds in respect of assets for which PRT relief is available for the related costs. This clause concerns a particular class of assets in respect of which some costs are allowable against PRT, and some are not.

    The Oil Taxation Act 1975 provides that the cost of acquiring land and buildings onshore—other than those 510 used for specified activities such as the initial treatment or storage of oil—are not allowable against PRT. An example would be a company head office used for the administration of North sea activities. But, because the day-to-day operating costs—such as those incurred on rates and maintenance—at least partly qualify for relief because the building is used for a PRT purpose, any receipts from the asset—for example, if it is sold or rented out—would be charged to PRT even though the capital costs of the building had not been allowed. This is clearly unfair and the clause therefore removes this limited class of assets from the scope of the PRT charge.
