If it had been stamped unnecessarily, there would be no question of stamp duty being payable. It would have been paid.

Adjudication is provided by section 12 of the Stamp Act 1891. It is the term by which we describe the process when the commissioners of Inland Revenue are formally requested to determine the liability to duty of an instrument and the amount of duty, if any. An adjudication stamp is conclusive evidence that a document has been correctly stamped. If the commissioners decide that a document does not need to be stamped and that it is not chargeable, it can be stamped

"Adjudged not chargeable with any stamp duty."

That is the formal phrase used in such cases. If the commissioners decide that a document is chargeable, they assess the duty and, when the instrument is stamped for assessment purposes, they further stamp it "Adjudged duly stamped".

The adjudication process is especially used for complicated transactions and takeover provisions clearly fall under that definition. Adjudication establishes whether an instrument is properly exempt under the clause or, if it is partially exempt, the degree to which it is partially exempt. Such adjudication is necessary to ensure that stamping for duty purposes is correct.
