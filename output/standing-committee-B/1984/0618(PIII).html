Many businesses require to restructure their finances in order to take account of industrial and economic changes, in some cases by continuing with high real rates of interest. The purpose of new clause 8 is to enable businesses, especially agricultural businesses which are heavily in debt, to respond to the pressures when it is economically sensible to do so, by being able to sell assets in order to reduce debts without suffering the capital gains tax penalty.

Roll-over relief already exists to ensure that the capital raised from the sale of business assets and redeployed within the business by the purchase of new assets is provided so that a business can survive and prosper. However, it is just as important for a businessman to ensure that his business debt is at a level which can reasonably be financed.

The level of debt of many farmers, and traders in some other industries, has become excessive in recent years. Probably the only solution to protect and preserve the businesses will be to sell off assets in order to reduce the debts and, hopefully, be left with a more viable, though smaller, business.

The problem is that the capital gains tax may not be rolled over unless the proceeds are reinvested in new capital assets. The fact that the business disposing of assets in those circumstances may have to pay capital gains tax could threaten its future viability.

New clause 8 would enable a business, whether agricultural or not, to realise part of the assets and devote the proceeds to reducing the debts, without experiencing the cash flow disadvantage of having to part with capital gains tax on sale. This is not a relief of capital gains tax, but merely a postponement until the remaining assets of the business are sold.

JPEG 641 I can illustrate the need for the clause especially well in relation to agriculture, although it has equal application in other industries where the general level of debt gives rise to concern. The majority of agricultural businesses are small family businesses. Between 1970 and 1984 farm incomes fell in real terms by some 36 per cent. In recent years the proportion of farm income which has been taken by interest payment has risen by 15 per cent to 32 per cent.

In the immediate past quotas on milk producers and other changes in agriculture have not made matters any easier. The future requirements of agriculture to pay attention to environmental and conservation requirements make it unwise to assume that the trend in debt and interest payments will be reversed, except perhaps by a partial disposal of the assets of a business which would, of course, be subject to capital gains tax.

The need for roll-over relief was recognised by the previous Labour Government when they extended capital gains tax, and it has been continued by the Conservative Governments. Mr. William Powell
(Corby)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

Let me begin by declaring an interest. Hon. Members probably know that I act as an adviser to the unquoted companies group. The new clause is designed to benefit the hundreds of thousands of unquoted companies which account for well over 50 per cent of all activity in our economy.

I must pay tribute to the Government for the reliefs which they have already introduced for capital transfer tax—the abolition of lifetime accumulations, the extension of business reliefs on minority holdings and the reduction of the top rates from 75 per cent to 60 per cent. But they are not enough. Capital transfer tax has been little, if at all, eased for the larger unquoted companies. That is of particular significance to the new companies, started up in the past few years, which have been a major part of the Government's strategy aimed at regenerating the economy. Many such companies have been started up in my constituency, and, no doubt, in those of other hon. Members. But if those companies grow and prosper, they will find that the present structure of capital transfer tax will force them to become quoted companies.

They may choose in due course to become quoted companies because it is in their interest to do so: that is all well and good, but to force them to do so by means of an unjust tax is surely inequitable and contrary to the Government's policy. It will have a very damaging effect on the economy, all the more so because the assets of such companies are by nature illiquid. Liquid assets can be manipulated. It is possible to make provision against the incidence of tax. That is done all the 646 time, and the tax system recognises it. But it is quite impossible to do that for illiquid assets.

The impact of the tax as it is at present falls on the shareholder, but its incidence falls on the unquoted company. It therefore amounts to an additional tax paid only on unquoted companies. If it applied to the quoted companies sector, every 30 years or so all the assets of a company such as ICI would be subject to capital transfer tax. That is the distinction between the two sorts of company.

I advance this course for several reasons. Unquoted companies are specifically deserving of our approbation. They are specifically deserving of proper tax provision which eliminates the current unfairness in the system. The new jobs that will come in the future—as almost any view of the source of new jobs will affirm— will come from the smaller companies. Every survey has shown that new jobs are likely to be created in companies with fewer than 5,000 employees. To my knowledge, not one unquoted company in the country employs more than 5,000 people.

Those companies are likely to be the growth companies for employment. The capital transfer tax has become the most serious problem imposed upon them by Government. Small unquoted companes face many problems, but the most serious, and the one which lies in the Government's hands, is capital transfer tax. The current structure of the tax makes it impossible for companies to make the provision required to preserve the family element when tax becomes payable. The incidence of the tax forces companies to become public. That is not a just way to treat the sector of the economy that is of fundamental importance, as it always has been and always will be.

The Committee will be aware of the study published in 1984 by two Oxford academics Mr. Morris and Mr. Hay. They demonstrated conclusively that by virtually any criteria, the unquoted company is more efficient than the quoted company. There are good reasons why that should be so. The study proved beyond doubt that the profit returns on assets are better, the growth of net assets is greater and that almost any criteria of efficiency shows that the unquoted companies achieve a better rate of return than the quoted companies.

Unquoted companies have to finance their own losses. There cannot be cross-subsidisation. Profits are reinvested over generations rather than distributed in dividends. Such companies can take a long view. The Committee will be aware that Lord Pilkington, who was head of an unquoted company for many years, said that one of his major innovations would never have occurred if he had had a quoted company. Shareholders of a quoted company could not take the long-term view required for substantial investment over decades. Such investment is important to research and development.

The unquoted companies sector, employing well over 50 per cent of labour in this country, deserves our special attention. If anything in the tax system makes it more difficult for the unquoted companies sector to survive, it should be eliminated.

The impact of the tax that I have suggested would be on the shareholders, but the incidence would fall on the company. If the bill is substantial—it will be for any JPEG 647 unquoted company that has grown through inflation as so many have in the past two decades—the company will have to fund an enormous burden of capital transfer tax. Hon. Members may know family companies in their constituencies which have had to restrict their growth because of the danger that the incidence of the tax imposes if those companies had to pay substantial capital transfer tax. The nature of such businesses is that the time when the tax is payable cannot be forecast. One-third of the members of the Institute of Directors die of a heart attack before they reach pensionable age. The strains of running a growing business and securing the necessary finance are considerable. It is not possible to forecast the time when the tax may become payable. That itself poses a further threat.

I propose in my new clause that 100 per cent business relief should be allowed, and that it should apply to both minority and majority shareholders. The argument for relieving minority interests at a lower rate, as happens at present, confuses two different discounts— the discount for non-controlling interests is a commercial discount, reflecting market realities. In any particular case it may be a larger or smaller sum than the difference between 50 per cent and 30 per cent business reliefs that currently exist. But a controlling interest is normally valued by the market at significantly more per share than a minority interest. However, it is in the nature of many such companies that there are not just two interests—a majority interest and a minority interest—but a collection of minority interests which mesh together to run the company. It is wrong to draw a distinction between a minority and a majority interest.

What is the cost of my proposal? On 1985 figures it is difficult to quantify it, and I shall be interested to hear what the Minister says about it. As far as I am able to judge, the cost of increasing business reliefs—as the new clause is drafted, that would apply to all businesses, not just industrial but agricultural—would be about £30 million. If we divide that in two, between the agricultural and industrial sectors, that would be £15 million each. On top of that, the full cost of the increase in business and agricultural property relief from 20 per cent to 30 per cent was put at £5 million in the 1983 Financial Statement and Budget Report—£2.5 million for each agricultural and industrial sector. On that basis, the cost of increasing 30 per cent existing business relief to 100 per cent would be about £17.5 million. The remaining sum would affect those areas which are accounted for in discretionary trusts—about £17.5 million. In all probability, the cost would be about £50 million.

The return to the nation by enabling unquoted companies to grow to their full potential, particularly in the light of the great number of new companies starting up at present and over the past few years, would quickly repay any concession that was made. It is the best way to secure the growth of employment in this country that we need.

We appreciate very much the courtesy of the Chief Secretary, other Ministers and their officials throughout the Committee proceedings of the Bill and the willingness that they displayed in listening to our arguments. This year I learnt that the Chief Secretary, or whoever sits in his place, and the hon. Member who leads for the Opposition share a perk, which may be news to my hon. Friends. At each sitting of the Committee we receive a ballpoint pen, and no other member of the Committee is treated so generously by the staff of the House.
