The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. John Moore): I beg to move amendment No. 146, in page 34, line 22, at end add—

"(4) In paragraph 14(2) of the said Schedule 7 (power by order to. prescribe qualifying lenders for the purposes of the deduction scheme) for the words from 'with effect' onwards there shall be substituted 'with effect from such date as may be so specified'.".

It may be helpful to the Committee if I start by saying something about the clause itself as well as the Government amendment.

The clause itself does two things. First, it fixes the mortgage interest relief limit for 1985-86 at £30,000. Under the 1974 legislation, the limit is fixed for only one year at a time, so that the House has the opportunity to review it annually. As in previous years, we have considered the limit carefully along with other competing claims for further tax relief. The limit was raised from £25,000 to £30,000 two years ago, and we decided that it should remain unchanged this year. At £30,000, the limit is still sufficient to cover the great majority of borrowers. But we will of course be keeping the limit under review from year to year in framing the Budget.

Secondly, the clause requires MIRAS lenders to bring new loans above the £30,000 limit into the scheme from April 1987. Loans above the limit are slightly more complicated to handle under MIRAS because only part of the interest qualifies for relief. So, when the scheme was introduced, lenders were given an option to bring these loans into the scheme but were not required to do so. Now that the scheme has settled down, it should be easier for lenders, given suitable notice, to start bringing these loans into the scheme. This will be simpler for borrowers than getting relief through PAYE, and will mean administrative savings for the Revenue.

The amendment makes a minor change in the arrangements for bringing new lenders into the MIRAS scheme. The aim is to make the arrangements work more flexibly. At present, those new lenders who have to be prescribed by Treasury order can enter the scheme only from the following 6 April. In many cases, that is convenient because, Col. 276 where existing loans are receiving relief through PAYE instead of through MIRAS, it is simpler to let relief continue under PAYE until the end of the current tax year. But in some cases the 6 April rule is now proving inflexible. For example, it prevents a newly prescribed lender starting to bring new loans into the scheme before the next 6 April. It also means that if, for example, a banking group is reorganised and the mortgage lending is transferred to a different company in the group, the new company will have to wait until 6 April before coming into MIRAS. By allowing greater flexibility over starting dates, the amendment should be helpful to the lenders and their borrowers, and I hope that the Committee will agree that it is a useful change.

In most professions, when a firm puts clients' money temporarily an deposit the client is still beneficially entitled to the interest, and a deposit would be either inside or outside the composite rate scheme according to whether the client was an individual or a company. When the money is placed in a separate or designated account in the name of a particular client, the client is entitled to the interest, and composite rate will apply in the normal way if the client is an individual.
