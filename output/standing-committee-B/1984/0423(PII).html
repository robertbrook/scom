On resuming—

Dr. Marek
: I am concerned about clause 33 because it gives a lot of money to the rich, who do not need it, at the expense of millions of citizens whose standards of living have been lowered by the Government's policies. At the last count there were 3,200,000 officially unemployed, although we all know that the real number's more than four million, because many people do not bother to register as unemployed and people who used to work full time but now work only part time are also not included on the register.

I am worried about the gap that is opening up in our society. It worries Opposition Members and it should also worry real Conservatives in the House of Commons. There are not many real Conservatives in the House now but I hope that, although the number of Conservative Members will be fewer after the next election, a true Conservative party will then re-emerge.

I support entirely what my hon. Friends have said about the clause. It pushes up the higher rate bands and gives money away to those who do not need it. Examples have been given of the salaries of some captains of industry, but it has not been proved that if one gives more money away to those who do not need it, they will work harder because they realise that greater benefits are available. In fact, the reverse may be true. They may work less hard because they reason that to keep up their standard of living they do not have to work as hard since the higher rate tax bands and other giveaways make it unnecessary to do so.

Arguments could be advanced for having penal rates of taxation instead of giveaways to the rich so that they would all have to work harder if they wanted to maintain their standards of living. Conservative Members talk about initiatives and incentives, but if it were tougher at the top it would be an incentive to the rich to work harder to keep up their standards of living.

The case has not been proved one way or the other, but during the past four or five years of the Government's policies there has not been beneficent investment in manufacturing or record profits in our industries, nor are we seen to be leading the way as an industrialised nation. That shows that the Government's policies are wrong.

I do not want to spend longer on the clause as time is getting on and we all want to get away, so I shall take only three or four minutes more. Besides their salaries, directors are entitled to share option schemes, 250 which we dealt with in last year's Finance Bill. The Government thought that share option schemes would provide an incentive for employees to partake in the ownership of their companies.

We can now see the first results of share option schemes. My list of share option schemes shows that the salary of John Harvey-Jones, chairman of ICI, whose salary is £170,000 a year, had a notional share option gain of £486,950 at 24 January 1985. I know that he cannot necessarily realise that gain now, but if that is his notional share option gain, it makes his salary pale into insignificance.

Sir Nigel Broackes, chairman of Trafalgar House, has a salary of £105,000, and his notional share option gain, at the same date, was £423,342. Dick Giordano has a salary of £771,600—an increase of 48 per cent—over the previous year—and his share option notional gain was 080,000. I would not say that Mr Giordano could do without his salary, because his share option gain is less than the others.

I have six further figures, and I shall not give them all to the Committee. Brian Smith, another ICI director, whose salary is somewhere between £110,000 and £170,000, has a share option gain of £326,860. A huge gain of £2,881,664 has been made by John Beckett, chairman of Woolworth Holdings. He cannot cash his shares yet—only when the price is right—but even if the price is not right, he will still have made quite a large gain. Why do companies bother to pay their chairmen salaries when they can cash in their options and live on the proceeds?

I should have thought that some Conservative Members would be worried by such goings on. In the light of those figures, I do not think that the Government can say that they have a progressive tax system. The DHSS has been helping the Government with their usual policy of taking away from the poor. I shall not give a long detailed list of measures, but shall start by mentioning the break in the link between the rise in a pensioner's income and the rise in average earnings and the rise in inflation. That was the beginning of a long process whereby the needy in society have been done down by the Government.

At the other end of the scale we have a long list of measures giving to the rich, who do not want the money. I shall not go through that list, either, except to recount the first thing that the Government did which was to knock down from 83 per cent to 60 per cent the rate of tax paid on the top band of earned income. My second list culminates in last year's measures in favour of share option schemes.

This year, even more higher rate bands are upgraded. That really annoys me. This may not be the time to bring up the subject, but it is symptomatic of the way that the Government are seeking to divide the country and have divided the country. They will rue the day that they began to follow such policies.

They may not see themselves earning the dizzy salaries that were outlined, but once they have studied what Opposition Members have said in this and other debates, they will realise that the tax policies that we are delicately teasing out of The Opposition in debate after debate mean that they will be living in a no-hope country.

That is not the philosophic basis on which we operate. Today we are debating some very modest increases, by reference to rates of inflation, for people earning £15,400 and more. It is an elementary measure of justice and good sense that these bands should be indexed. On that basis, I commend the clause to the Committee.

I want to pick up one or two points in the Chief Secretary's speech. He referred to my article about China and the reintroduction of an incentive system there. The range of monthly salaries following the introduction of an incentive system is from 70 yuan—the basic rate for a factory worker; it can be increased by bonuses—to 300 yuan for university professors. There must be many factory workers at British Oxygen or shop assistants at Marks and Spencer or clerical workers at the Prudential who would be glad to have half, a third or even a quarter of the wages earned by their bosses. The Chief Secretary and the hon. Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Smith) made much play of the rise in real take-home pay. As the Chief Secretary has chosen to look at the increase in real net earnings for those on half average earnings and so on, I shall take one or two similar examples that illustrate the cause of our complaint.

10.15 p.m.

The term "real net earnings" means earnings after income tax, national insurance contributions, contracted-out contributions and child benefit where appropriate. Childless married couples on half average earnings have seen a 7.9 per cent increase in their real net earnings since 1978–79 and married couples with two children have seen a 9.4 per cent increase.

As it would be confusing to take too many examples from the table, I shall take only one other, by way of contrast. Childless married couples on ten times average earnings have seen a 61 per cent increase in their real net earnings while married couples with two children have seen a 59 per cent increase.

While those percentage figures help us to see the size of the differential, we must remember that a 9 per 272 cent increase in real net earnings for someone on half average earnings means far less in terms of buying power than a 61 or 59 per cent increase in real net earnings for someone on ten times average earnings. In the latter case the increases are enormous.

The gap between the two groups is enormous and that is our main complaint. We are talking about the contrast between the lot of the rich and the poor—the deliberate distortion of the tax system in order to benefit those on above-average earnings. Whether we consider the increase in the tax burden or the increase in real net earnings, that distortion is perfectly obvious, and the unfairness involved is our main complaint.
