Clause 17
INTEREST ON TAX ETC. RECOVERED OR RECOVERABLE BY ASSESSMENT

4.30 p.m.

Mr. Tony Blair (Sedgefield)
: I beg to move amendment No. 41, in page 17, line 20, leave out subsection (6).

As this is my first speech in the Committee, may I say at the outset what a pleasure it is to appear before you, Miss Fookes?

The amendment is designed to enable us to understand the thinking behind subsection (6). The clause deals in general terms with the interest on tax recovered or recoverable by assessment. Subsection (6) deals with the situation in which a trader who is not an authorised person within the meaning of clause 14(2) issues an invoice. Under clause 14(2) the definition of persons not authorised to issue invoices includes those not registered under the principal Act. If someone not registered for VAT issues an invoice showing a certain amount of VAT due, clause 17(6) allows the Customs and Excise to charge interest on the amount shown to be due.

I have some difficulty with that, which is why the amendment is really an inquiry. Perhaps I have misunderstood the definition of an unauthorised person. However, it seems to me that someone not authorised because, say, he is not registered under the principal Act, would not be entitled to issue an invoice requiring a VAT payment. If so, it strikes me as odd that such a person can be required to pay interest on the amount.

That is my question to the Minister. If no tax is due because the person is not authorised to ask for tax, it strikes me as somewhat peculiar that interest could be payable on it. Presumably if a VAT payment is requested by someone not authorised to request it, the person asked to pay cannot properly be liable to do so, because no authority to make the request is vested in the person issuing the invoice. If that is so, even if the unauthorised person received the VAT that he was not entitled to receive, surely he would be liable to pay the money back to the person to whom the invoice had been issued. If restitution were due in that manner, it is difficult to see how the Customs and Excise would be entitled to levy interest on the amount.

There may be a reasonable explanation involving the definition of authorised people. I await the Minister's reply with interest.

The Minister of State, Treasury (Mr. Barney Hayhoe)
: I first welcome the hon. Member for Sedgefield (Mr. 154 Blair) to the discussions in Committee. I share his view that the language of clause 17 in its entirety is not of such blinding simplicity that there could be no difficulty for anyone in understanding the inter-relations between all the provisions. However, I am happy to try to explain the purpose behind subsection (6), which the amendment would exclude. As the hon. Gentleman said, that would exclude from liability to default interest those amounts shown as tax on invoices issued by unauthorised persons.

Hon. Members might ask how an unauthorised person could issue an invoice. I am advised that from time to time people invoice customers improperly with a bill that includeds VAT when they are not registered as VAT traders. The law provides that if such a demand for VAT is made by an unauthorised trader and is collected from the customer, it must go to the Revenue, because it has been paid by someone who thinks that he has paid VAT and therefore comes within that direction.

If a person behaves improperly by hanging on to the money, accumulating it and not giving it to the Revenue, having charged his customers VAT, the money must be remitted to the Revenue. The purpose of the clause is to ensure that if the money is not remitted, default interest can be charged. The sort of person who might do that is the moonlighter or someone on the fringe of the black enconomy. If an honest and law-abiding citizen has been charged VAT and, thinking that he is doing the right thing, pays it to an unauthorised person, such a person must remit it to the Revenue.

Mr. D. N. Campbell-Savours
(Workington)

Mr. Hayhoe

If, at the end of the year, he finds that he ought to be registered, he must take steps quickly to register. But that goes way beyond the clause and is not affected by it as far as I know. The requirements for registration are in other legislation and are not at issue here.

Mr. Campbell-Savours
: Is it not possible that a person may decide to write a VAT invoice on the basis of believing that it is likely that within 12 months he will exceed the VAT threshold and that Customs and Excise may have refused to provide him with VAT registration on the ground that he would not do so, so he would be setting out to ensure that he fell within the law?

Mr. Hayhoe
: If that were so, and the individual was desirous of falling within the law, he would remit the VAT to the Revenue in the proper way. The basis of the hon. Gentleman's question is that if a person sought eagerly to ensure that he was behaving totally lawfully, there would be no question of his withholding payments that he had received.

Clause 18
THE DEFAULT SURCHARGE

Mr. Blair
: I beg to move amendment No. 42, in page 19, line 26, leave out subsection (5).

The Chairman
: With this, we shall take amendment No. 93, in page 19, line 37, leave out from "by" to end of line 39 and insert " 15 per cent.".

Mr. Blair
: Amendment No. 42 seeks to delete clause 18(5). It is a somewhat clumsy amendment, as consequential amendments would have to follow it to make sense of it. Many of my arguments are perhaps more amenable to debate on clause stand part rather than on the limited subject of subsection (5).

Two issues arise on clause 18. First, whether default surcharge is right—and we shall debate that on clause stand part. Secondly, even if we accept the principle of automatic surcharges in clause 18, is the type of surcharge introduced in the clause in itself too harsh? Clearly, subsection (5) must be read in conjunction with subsection (4). The effect is that there is either a fixed penalty of £30 or a specified percentage of the outstanding tax for any period. The trader will have to pay whichever is the greater of those two amounts.

I have some experience of drafting and I tried to simplify clause 18(2)(b). At first I thought that it seemed an incredibly complicated way of saying one year, but "within one year" does not quite express what is meant. It is a slight variation on Keith but, as I understand it, a default in any two accounting periods where a year overlaps with the next, or within one year, means that surcharge provisions will come into effect.

The surcharge provisions will be the specified percentage or the £30. There is then, under subsection (5), a series of staggered increases in the percentage, starting with 5 per cent if there is one prescribed accounting period in which the surcharge applies. Am I correct in thinking that a trader must have two defaults within that period of one year, or the overlap of a year, before the surcharge comes into operation? For any one accounting period when the surcharge comes into operation the liability is 5 per cent for one default rising to 10 per cent and then 30 per cent. Therefore, for the first surcharge accounting period the amount payable will be 5 per cent or £30; 10 per cent or £30 for the second; and up to a maximum of 30 per cent for subsequent years.

Mr. Hayhoe
: These three linked amendments deal with a fairly narrow point, and we shall come to some of the wider issues later. Between the publication of the exposure clauses and the Bill, the word "order" has been replaced by "written instruction".

The hon. Member for Sedgefield (Mr. Blair) and others have wondered why that has happened. Customs and Excise was a computer system to deal with the three million or so claims for repayment of VAT that it receives each year. In clause 9 of the consultative paper, the word "order" was used. The change was made on the advice of parliamentary counsel that it could not be right in law that a trader's entitlement should depend upon a fact of which he could have had no knowledge: the date of the computer run when the order was issued. I am advised that "ordered to pay" can be construed in a technical sense to mean the issue of a negotiable instrument or cheque.

Therefore, it is not feasible for individual orders to pay to be made. A written instruction directing the making of the payment will be issued. That will permit a responsible Customs and Excise officer to initiate the repayments on each occasion on which they are to be made. The date of the written instruction will be printed on the payable orders—the cheques—and on the ad-vices issued when payment is effected through the banks' automated clearing system. Traders will, therefore, know when the authority to make the repayment is given and whether in each case an entitlement to repayment supplement has arisen. That is a protection for the trader. I shall come to the timing later.

I hope that the Committee will feel that the written instruction will be fair to the traders concerned and I hope that the amendments will not be pressed.
