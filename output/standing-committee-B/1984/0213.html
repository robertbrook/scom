Education (Corporal Punishment) Bill

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Education and Science (Mr. Bob Dunn)
: I beg to move,

That during the proceedings on the Education (Corporal Punishment) Bill the Committee do meet on Wednesdays at half-past Four o'clock.

In moving the motion, may I say how pleased the Committee must be to be under your guidance and Chairmanship, Mr. Morris. Even-handedness and fairplay is your hallmark. During the sittings on the Bill we look to proceed in the cheerfulness and amiability which education Bills have begun to attract in recent years. I am sure that all members of the Committee will join me in wishing you a happy reign as it were, over our proceedings.

Mr. Andrew F. Bennett
(Denton and Reddish)
: I too welcome you to the Committee, Mr. Morris. I hope that we need not spend long on the sittings motion.

The Opposition made it clear to the Government that if they were willing to meet us in a few respects we could speed up the Committee's proceedings. First, we said that sight of the regulations that will arise from the Bill would be helpful to us. To some extent the Minister has tried to help us. I understand that members of the Committee who collected their post after 12 noon today will have found therein a set of notes on clauses and a copy of the regulations. It would have been much more helpful if we had had a little time to read through the documents and consult others, but it is not too much of a disadvantage. I appreciate that Ministers have great difficulty in persuading civil servants that it should be automatic for us when discussing legislation, to have sight of regulations that will be made under it. Therefore, although we were a little disappointed not to have the documents sooner, the concession is useful.

Of the other points on which we hoped that the Minister would meet us, in particular, we would have liked exclusion from corporal punishment for pupils suffering from physical or mental handicaps and for pupils over 16. We would also have liked some agreement about limiting the frequency and extent of corporal punishment and an opt-in rather than an opt-out system. The regulations seem to be a little better—they edge a little more towards our aims than I had expected. I welcome that. We had also hoped that the Minister would make it absolutely clear that local authorities which have already abolished corporal punishment cannot have it reintroduced in their areas because of a decision taken in a school.

So far, the Minister has been able to meet us only on the regulations. If the Government could meet us on some of the other points, our deliberations could be speeded up. We have no intention of deliberately delaying the Committee, but if there cannot be abolition of corporal punishment, which is what we believe in, we want a Bill that limits it as far as possible and meets the spirit of the European ruling. I support the motion.

Mr. Bennett
: It should be obvious to the Committee that this first group of amendments is designed to produce an abolition Bill rather than the mismatch of a Bill now before us. It was clear on Second Reading that a large majority felt that we should not continue to have corporal punishment in this country and, added to that, a large number said that the Government's Bill was not workable. While some wanted to keep corporal punishment, they felt that the bureaucracy and the invidiousness of treating one pupil differently from another rendered the scheme unworkable. Therefore the Opposition's first group of amendments seek to ascertain whether the Government have actually taken any notice of those views and whether, even at this late date, they might not be prepared to accept that it would be far better to abolish corporal punishment in all our schools.

I want to repeat the point that I made on Second Reading—that I used to be a teacher and that, sadly, I sometimes used corporal punishment. I regretted that I had to do that on those occasions and I very much regret it now.—[Hon. Members: "Why?"] I do not believe that one can teach effectively in an atmosphere of fear. There is ample evidence that successful teachers and successful schools can manage adequately without any consideration of corporal punishment. If we are trying to teach young people the importance of self-control and self-discipline, it is totally inappropriate—even immoral—to threaten them or to try to coerce them to do something that they cannot be persuaded to do other than by employing violence.

I also believe that not only is corporal punishment immoral and wrong but that on far too many occasions it has proved actually physically damaging to pupils. There are fairly regular instances of pupils ending up with broken fingers or wounds as a result of corporal punishment, which is unacceptable in an enlightened education world.

Some pupils subjected to corporal punishment get fairly hardened. They get used to it and some of them achieve a certain measure of notoriety and show bravado because such punishment makes no impact on them. Some youngsters can be damaged even when they are unlikely to be subjected to corporal punishment. If it is threatened, or if they witness teachers using corporal punishment on other children, some children can be damaged.

Mr. Bennett
: It could be argued that a person is not successful in disciplining a child if he has to smack the child. One should not be proud of having to smack a child. It is not a measure of success. It shows failure and incompetence. Some parents, because of their incompetence, are driven to smacking a child. However, that does not mean that someone trained as a teacher should admit that he is also lacking in competence. I accept that some teachers have occasionally used corporal punishment. I have done so. I admit that it was due to my own incompetence and the situation in which I was placed in the school. However, corporal punishment cannot be justified just because a parent does it.

Mr. Bennett
: We should all strongly object to the use of violence. There is no justification for the use of deliberate violence. It is perhaps because the parent has failed in other ways that he is pushed into using violence. The use of violence in all walks of life is evidence of an individual's failure or of society's failure. If we wish to believe that humans have attractions in their characters, we should try to organise society so that we can eliminate violence. I do not see how it can be argued that one sort of violence is wrong while the sort of violence we are talking about is right. We should aim to try to create a society in which violence is not necessary.

I accept that society is not perfect. Violence is inevitable on occasions. However, I still believe that we should try to create a society where it is unnecessary. I believe that a parent should try in all circumstances to avoid violence, but I accept that however hard parents try not to, they will sometimes lose their tempers or resort to violence. It cannot be said that that is a good thing. It happened because the parent was unable to find an alternative. I do not suggest that parents should act in that way on purpose. Ideally, they should avoid such circumstances.

I believe that the first requirement for parents is to ensure that children behave themselves because they want to. Parents set an example and so, by their arguments and actions, they can try to persuade their children to act in certain ways. Children are then more likely to respect JPEG 7 their parents and—because they accept their parents' authority—will do what they might prefer not to do. If parents fail in those respects, they may be tempted to coerce children by a slap.

It is, of course, easy to discuss such matters in abstract in Committee. It may be quite different and much harder for an extremely tired parent with a crotchety, small child to live up to that ideal. However, parents should aim for that ideal and not rely on violence.

Mr. David Young
(Bolton, South-East)

The real problem is that the Bill establishes several different punishments for exactly the same offence. Does my hon. Natural justice is at the heart of the problem created by the Bill.

Mr. Bennett
: I certainly accept that argument. I thought that I had made that clear when I outlined why the Government—having secured a majority for the Bill to proceed to Committee—should reconsider it and examine our amendments. Many of those who favour corporal punishment believe that because the Bill could treat children differently it is unacceptable. I hope that my hon. Friend will develop that argument.

I was referring to the emotional damage caused to some children who never receive, or are ever likely to receive, corporal punishment in schools. They become upset, for instance, because a teacher may have said that if a class acts in a certain way, pupils will suffer the strap or the cane. Children can still fairly frequently be heard to say that they do not wish to go to school; that is often because they have been frightened by a general threat to the class from the school system or an individual teacher, even though those particular children are unlikely ever to be subjected to such punishment. I shall not go into detail, but there are many instances of children being disturbed by, for example, a blanket threat that they could be subject to corporal punishment.

There are many connotations about the damage to individuals' personalities and sexuality by the misuse of caning. We have all seen on bookstalls objectionable publications such as the "Joys of Spanking", "The Art of Caning" and so on. That is perhaps a minor but unfortunate effect of the use of corporal punishment.

My strongest argument is that corporal punishment is anti-educational. One may be able to condition people—or animals—with such punishments, but if we want to educate people we should not do it with force of that kind. We should try to create an atmosphere in school where young people want to learn and where they learn one of the most important things—self-discipline. I can see little point in coercing pupils in school until they are 16, if at the end of the day one has not actually taught them anything or instilled into them self-discipline. Therefore, the whole idea of corporal punishment is anti-educational.

It is also counter-productive. There is much evidence to show that in schools where corporal punishment is used a great deal, particularly where it is used excessively, it breeds an atmosphere of violence and it breeds bravado in 8 pupils, because they are subjected to corporal punishment and say that it does not hurt. There is nothing more damaging to a school than when corporal punishment is held in contempt. There is quite a lot of evidence to show that pupils from schools which breed violence become violent in later life. I believe that the amount of child abuse—child battering—and abuse between husband and wife is the result of the belief held by some schools that total discipline can be imposed through violence.

My final point is that corporal punishment is unnecessary. It amazes me how many countries in Europe either have never had corporal punishment or abolished it many years ago. No one so far, either on Second Reading or in any of the correspondence that I have seen, has been able to tell us that it is impossible for the schools to operate in Greece, Italy, Iceland, Luxembourg, Poland, the Netherlands, Belgium, Austria, France, Finland, the Soviet Union, Turkey, Norway, Romania, Portugal, Sweden, Cyprus, Denmark, Spain, West Germany, Switzerland or even Ireland. All those countries have managed to get rid of corporal punishment; no one has come to me and said that the schools in those countries cannot operate, that education is defective without it.

With those fairly brief introductory remarks, I hope that we can persuade the Government to abandon the convoluted proposals in the rest of the Bill even at this late stage and to turn clause 1 into an abolition Bill, and perhaps finish at an early time at our first sitting.

Mr. Martin Flannery
(Sheffield, Hillsborough)
: I have been against corporal punishment since I was a child, because I went to a school where it was the order of the day. It was very rare to get through a day without counting about six that occurred on most hands in the school. It was unbelievable that anybody could be so silly as to do that. It made me realise that it was so counterproductive to education that as a teacher I never used it, and I found myself very unpopular on occasion in staff rooms when arguing against it on educational grounds.

I feel sorry for Conservative Members. I think that most of them do not believe in the Bill. I have spoken to some of them, and the fact that they are here, defending the indefensible, is almost alarming, because they do not have the heart for it. The Bill is surely the most ridiculous that any of us have seen, even those who have been here for many years. It has so many anomalies. Hon. Members have received documents from many sources. One from the Childrens Legal Centre lists 17 such anomalies, and most reasonable and even unreasonable human beings would agree with its representations. But what is even more important is that from those anomalies further anomalies bristle. The Bill is pregnant with absolute nonsense. It gives birth almost by the second to issues which have nothing whatever to do with education.

Having listened to the Second Reading debate and read the Bill, I cannot but believe that the only answer to this silly piece of legislation is the complete abolition of corporal punishment in schools, which has already happened in most other countries. Corporal punishment lingers in our schools like imperialism. Imperialism is now dying, yet the imperialist countries try to keep a toehold. Similarly, those who want corporal punishment JPEG 9 in schools cannot face getting rid of it, so we have to mobilise a head of steam to do so.

Mr. Flannery
: I think that the hon. Gentleman can follow it very clearly and is merely making a debating point. He must understand what I have said because it is so simple—children cannot be terrorised into good education. Any experienced teacher knows that. Sadly, however, oversized classes, too few books and education cuts have led them to resort to that method of ending, if only for a short time, their own frustrations while deepening the education problem which they face.

Some of the anomalies to which my hon. Friend has referred are appalling. The vast majority of schools in this country use corporal punishment, although many pretend that they do not, and they violate necessary rules all the time. Usually what happens is that a frustrated teacher calls out young Johnny and thrashes him in front of the class. How will that affect the sensitivities of the other children in the class? The result of such a stage act—a teacher beating a small child—is to close the channels of learning that the teacher was trying to open or to keep open, and the essence of the beating is anti-educational.

Moreover, nearly all such beating go unrecorded, although they go on all the time and have nothing to do with education. Clause 1(1) shows that a teacher beating a pupil in loco parentis is really acting in loco draconis. He will terrify the child. One thing worse than an individual losing his temper and striking another person is organising for that.

The Bill is bristling with unfairness and anomalies and it will produce endless problems. It is not only unfair, unjust and silly, it is totally unworkable. Unless it is abandoned, as it should be, it will produce such problems that teachers will have to ask the Government to withdraw it because it cannot work.
