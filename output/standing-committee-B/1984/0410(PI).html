Mr. Terry Davis

My earlier remarks were intended as a probing speech. The Chief Secretary did not respond to that probe, but I am sure that he will respond in writing with his usual courtesy because I made a serious point. I wish to know whether the Government will consider the matters that I raised earlier.

This is a probing amendment. I make no apology for saying that. My hon. Friend the Member for Kingston-upon-Hull, West (Mr. Randall) and I share a keen interest in the cider industry. My hon. Friend was born in Devon and now lives in the county of Hereford and Worcester. I come from,Worcestershire—as it was formerly known—and spent many happy years in Somerset, which is also well known as a cider-producing county.

Our previous experiences and present residence give us a deep interest in the health, prosperity and employment propspects of the cider industry. If the Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Transport catches your eye, Miss Fookes, I know that he will wish to associate himself with those remarks because he, too, represents a constituency in the county of Hereford and Worcester.

We are talking not only about cider, but about perry, which is the drink in the county of Worcestershire. Not for nothing was a pear the badge of Worcestershire. Perry is constituted in a similar way to cider, but uses pears instead of apples. I have not forgotten the day when, much smaller and younger, I wore a badge from the scouting movement which had a pear on it.

Cider came to this country with William the Conqueror and a liking for it replaced the Anglo-Saxon devotion to the predecessor of beer. It was boosted by the Hundred Years War and became a popular drink. It was overtaken in the 19th century by beer, which has enjoyed much greater sales and therefore greater production than cider in recent years.

However, there has been a big increase in cider consumption in the past few years. The Minister of State told the Committee a year ago that cider consumption has increased by about 6 per cent. a year, and in 1982 there was an increase of 20 per cent. In 1983, it increased by 11 per cent.

A year ago, the Government increased the tax on cider disproportionately compared with the increase in the tax on beer. Although I do not think that brewers had reason to lobby out of concern for sales, because beer consumption still exceeds that of cider at a ratio of 25 to one, I know that the tax increase on cider was considered exceptionally high 12 months ago.

Workers in the cider industry and hon. Members who represent cider-producing constituencies have expressed fears about a fall in consumption. We made this point a year ago and were told by the 8 Minister of State that he did not believe that there would be a fall in consumption as a result of the exceptional increase in the cider tax in 1984.

Nevertheless, that increase means that a pint of cider became more expensive that a pint of beer. We have tabled the amendment to probe and to ask the Government to tell us of their findings in the past year. I have no doubt that they have kept a close watch on the industry, which is concentrated in rural areas. We are conscious of the fact that there is little alternative employment if people in the industry lose their jobs.

Our amendment appears to seek to prevent any increase in the tax on cider. We will not press it to a Division. We merely wish to give the Government an opportunity to tell us, with the benefit of hindsight, what has happened during the past 12 months.
