These amendments seek to leave out the words " or understanding" in subsection (4), where the Bill refers to " a promise or understanding ". I have looked to see what "understanding" means, and find it extremely vague. It strikes me that it would 4 be sensible to have a more precise word instead of " or understanding ", which might give rise to difficulties of interpretation.

The Minister for Health (Mr. Kenneth Clarke)
:The hon. Lady has very fairly moved her amendment more by way of a query, and I quite understand her raising the question. I do not always instinctively leap to the defence of the drafting of Bills as they first arrive before a Standing Committee, but on this occasion I think that the wording that is used is very near that of ordinary, day-to-day, conversational English. I cannot see how it could be improved or made clearer. If we simply left out the words " or understanding" it would weaken the force of the Bill.

My understanding of the meaning of that phrase as it stands is that a promise has a certain binding quality about it, if only in honour. If a person promises that he will do something, he is committing himself to carrying it out. An understanding is something short of that. It applies in cases where one has not actually promised to do something but the other person has been left in the expectation that something will be done.

If these words were omitted there might be arguments before the courts on the question whether a certain arrangement was sufficient to amount to a promise do not want to invent the kind of thing that might amount to an understanding, but if the woman in question were left with the feeling that she would be all right, or would be looked after, or that something would be done for her, and it did not go beyond that, it is conceivable that later, when the matter came before a court, there would be a great deal of argument whether such a thing amounted to a promise.

The Bill attempts to catch any arrangements where there is an underlying belief that a payment is likely to be made in the end. I think that that makes the Bill more effective. To leave out the words " or understanding " would weaken it, and one can imagine arguments whether certain forms of words did or did not amount to a promise on the part of one person to hand over money to another.

So far, so good, I hope, if I made it clear. The only question that then arises, certainly in my mind, and the question that the Government think they would like to consider further, is whether it is actually the opinion of the House given the choice, that absolutely every feature of a surrogacy arrangement, even a voluntary one, should be made illegal.

I hazard my personal opinion. I would have thought that it should be made unenforceable that the mother should be compelled to hand over the child. Nobody wishes to see a surrogacy contract enforced with a surrogate mother who changed her mind being obliged to hand over the child. That is straightforward. I certainly think, and it is consistent with the Bill, that any agreement to pay a fee to the surrogate mother for her services—for the leasing of the womb—might be made unenforceable. Some people might object to that, but I think most would agree with it.

If, however, as a result of some purely voluntary arrangement—let us take the least harmful of all, in the opinion of some—there has been an agreement between sisters that some kind of medical expenses are to be paid on behalf of the surrogate mother, and there has been an agreement that the surrogate mother is to give up her job, so that the would-be parent is going to pay for the costs suffered during the pregnancy by the surrogate mother giving up her job in order to bear the child, we are not entirely sure that we would wish to make that unenforceable.

It is possible that the amendment, moved in the terms that it was by the hon. Member for Leeds, West (Mr. Meadowcroft), makes each and every aspect of an arrangement of that kind completely unenforceable, with resulting hardship. The problem will obviously be totally resolved if, when we get to what we are all describing as the full Warnock Bill, the House decides to make illegal all surrogacy arrangements, voluntary or commercial, or whatever. I imagine that the House will then go rushing on to say that such arrangements should be unenforceable in every respect. But if, in the end, we decide that there is scope for some voluntary surrogacy, we must be careful about exactly what we are saying should be unenforceable in the courts.

I probably have not been very helpful to the hon. Member, because I merely rehearsed the difficulties that stop me from immediately agreeing to the amendment. What I will undertake is that we will consider the point. There are obvious essential points that most people would like us to sort out and put in statutory form. If we can produce a satisfactory solution, either in this House or in another place, we shall be happy to put forward 8 amendments that will put the matter beyond doubt, without giving rise to any harmful and unintended side-effects.
