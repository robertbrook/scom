clause Clause 5
title BLENDING OF CERTAIN WINES TO CONSTITUTE PRODUCTION OF WINE

4.30 p.m.

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Terry Davis (Birmingham, Hodge Hill): I wish to oppose the clause—[Interruption.] Before I hear too many comments about being out of ratio, may I say that I shall be joined shortly by my hon. Friends who all have a passionate interest in public order. They were fully confident that I would be able to tackle these odds alone.

This clause is known to my hon. Friends as the Joan Collins clause. It arises from a court case that involved the commissioners of Customs and Excise and the firm of Cinzano (UK) Ltd, whose product was advertised so effectively in a series of TV commercials featuring Joan Collins and the late Leonard Rossiter. It concerns vermouth. It is not only Cinzano, because other importers of vermouth, such as Martim, are affected by the clause. Indeed, it concerns all fortified wines, the others being sherry and port, in that those fortified wines are also blended to some extent in this country. All the drinks are imported, but not necessarily as finished products; sometimes they are imported and then blended to make the vermouth or sherry that is sold to consumers.

A year ago, the Government changed the arrangements for the excise duty on fortified wines as part of the package that was introduced as a result of interference by the Common Market. There are four rates of excise duty, depending on the strength of the imported wine. People working for Cinzano noticed that if they imported vermouths of different strengths, paid duty according to the strengths of those imported wines, and then blended the imported wines in appropriate proportions, the finished product would be identical to the vermouth that they were then importing for sale in this country. Moreover, the duty paid on the blended vermouth—that is, the duty paid on different strengths—would be less than the duty paid on the same vermouth if it were imported as such. In other words, the blended vermouth would be the same, whereas the blended duty would be lower.

There was nothing illegal in that idea. The people working for Cinzano spotted a loophole in legislation that was introduced by the Government a year ago. They were perfectly entitled, as I am sure the Treasury Col. 88 will admit, to use that loophole to reduce the tax they had to pay as excise duty.

The development was a godsend to Cinzano (UK) Ltd. I should explain that Cinzano has had a bottling plant for several years at Telford in Shropshire—a new town. The plant was opened in 1977, at a time when the demand for vermouth was increasing rapidly in this country. That has changed, and the demand for vermouth is now declining. I understand that the total market for vermouth has fallen by 20 per cent in the past five years, and that is a dramatic fall in anyone's market.

The trade attributes the decline in the sales of vermouth to the increases in the amount of excise duty imposed on fortified wines by this Government during the past five years. The increases in tax on vermouth and other fortified wines has been greater than the rate of inflation. Indeed, the increases have been far greater than the increase in tax on wines. That is important, because it is believed that the switch in consumer preference has been from the fortified wines of vermouth and sherry to lighter wines, which are imported as table wine. Consumption of table wine in the United Kingdom has grown at the expense of fortified wines of all kinds.

Over the five years of this Government the increase in excise duty on fortified wines has been no less than 79 per cent, compared to an increase of only 21 per cent on table wine. Prices of table wine cover a broad span, but among products of roughly the same price, there has, not surprisingly, been a switch from fortified to table wine.

For companies specialising in fortified wine, such as Cinzano and Martini, which have other products but specialise in vermouth, and companies with much business in the sherry trade, the picture has been bleak. The Cinzano company has experienced a real decline in its market, and that has put the Telford bottling plant into real difficulties. I gather that a year ago the management of Cinzano was very worried about the future of that plant, sited in a new town and employing about 25 temporary workers and 50 full-time permanent employees.

Serious discussions took place about the possibility of closing the plant, but changes in excise duty last year saved it and the jobs in it by encouraging the blending, instead of only the bottling, of vermouth. The Government do not deserve too many thanks; last year's loop-hole was not deliberate. I do not believe that it was in the minds of Treasury Ministers on last year's Standing Committee that an arithmetical quirk in excise duties would save 50 permanent jobs in Telford. Neverthless, that was the effect; it was a happy accident for Cinzano employees.

By importing vermouths of different strengths and blending them at Telford, the company reduced the excise duty that it had to pay by £3 million. That would, of course, make a significant contribution towards the cost of the plant, and the switch was made from bottling to blending and bottling.

The response of the Commissioners of Customs and Excise was not a happy one; they immediately tried to persuade Cinzano to pay extra tax. No one suggested that the company had done anything illegal, but the commissioners tried to persuade Cinzano that the act of blending was an act of production and that it should Image Col. 89 pay extra tax; Cinzano, naturally, resisted. The case went to court. At the first hearing, in the High court, the Commissioners won their case. The management of Cinzano was not so easily put off, and went to the Court of Appeal which found for the firm, against the Commissioners of Customs and Excise. Recognising their obligation to maximise revenue for the Government, the Commissoners took Cinzano to the House of Lords where the decision was unanimous and the judges concurred that Cinzano was right, and that the act of blending was not an act of production.

In effect, the Government have not accepted the decision, and that is the purpose of clause 5. They intend to use their majority in the House of Commons to reverse the decision of the House of Lords, the highest court of appeal, so clause 5 defines blending as production.

Clause 5 will double the taxation on vermouth blended in this country, although it will not affect vermouth imported and sold in the shops. Clause 5 introduces double taxation. I should have had some sympathy if the Government had come to the Committee and said, "Congratulations to the managers of Cinzano. They are sharp-eyed fellows and clever chaps. They got away with it for a year, but we do not think that they should continue to get away with it and have the advantage of this loophole any longer. Therefore, we shall ensure that they pay just as much tax by importing and blending as they would have paid if they had imported the finished product." I would have understood and been less critical if that had been their aim.

However, the Government are not telling Cinzano and the other importers that they will make sure that they do not make a profit, or a contribution to the cost of blending by importing wines of different strengths and then blending them here. They are not saying that they want the amount of tax that they would get if the wine were already blended when imported, or that they want their tax, regardless of where the blending takes place. They want more tax if vermouth is blended in this country than if it is blended in Italy and then brought to this country.

That is nonsense. It is double taxation. The Government want to charge excise duty on vermouth of different strengths which has different rates of duty when those wines are imported. Then, when Cinzano has blended and bottled the wine and sent if for sale, the Government will tax it again at the full rate of the excise duty on the new strength. Heads the Government win, tails they win twice. They think that they will make extra money if Cinzano, Martini and the other importers blend in this country.

That will inevitably mean a loss of jobs at Telford as it will make the plant uneconomic according to the managers. It was a bottling plant but it is now a blending and bottling plant. The firm has already given redundancy notices to the temporary or part-time employees, and has already had discussions with full-time employees to explain the effects of the clause. Of course other importers of vermouth such as Martini, will also be affected.

Importers and sellers of other fortified wines will also be affected by the clause. The larger part of the fortified wine market is in sherry—more sherry is sold than Col. 90 vermouth—and small amounts of port are also involved. Sherry is also blended in this country if it comes from some other countries. Some sherry is imported and sold in the same condition in which it left its country of origin.

However, I am told that Cyprus sherry in particular is usually blended in this country before it is sold. A large share of the Cyprus sherry market is held by the Co-operative movement; indeed, I believe that the Cooperative wholesale society accounts for some 27 per cent of all sales of Cyprus sherry in this country through its outlets.

The Co-operative wholesale society has imported sherry from Cyprus and blended it in this country for as long as anyone can remember. The society goes back a long way, and memories go back a long way, but I cannot find anyone who can tell me when it began to import and blend sherry here. It is blended at Irlam, near Manchester, and 150 people are employed at the plant. I do not want to exaggerate. Those people are engaged in blending other drinks and in bottling, so I am not suggesting that the jobs of 150 people are dedicated to blending Cyprus sherry. However, it has an effect. A large part of the company's business is blending and bottling Cyprus sherry. To remove that part of the business would put some of those 150 jobs at risk.

The Bill insists that the Co-operative Wholesale Society or its agent shall pay excise duty at the time of importing Cyprus sherry at the appropriate rates for their strengths and also on the finished product at its appropriate strength. That double taxation will increase the prices of Cyprus sherry in this country by significant amounts.

The most popular type of Cyprus sherry sold by the Co-operative Wholesale Society is a cream sherry. The increase this year in what I shall call the duty bands—the increase mentioned by the Chancellor and described in the press release from the Customs and Excise—is 10p a bottle. The increase in duty in what I shall call the blending tax is an extra 20p a bottle on Cyprus cream sherry and 30p on dry sherry. The total increase from tax changes would be 30p on cream sherry and 40p on dry sherry.

Those are significant increase. We are talking about a retail price of £2.19 for a bottle of the Cyprus cream sherry supplied by the Co-operative Wholesale Society. A total increase of 30p puts up the price to £2.49.

If the Minister chooses to deploy the argument about bonding despite my assertion, I shall go into the matter in greater detail.

Because of their vendetta against Cinzano the Government are introducing a blending tax on all fortified wines—vermouth and sherry. Many people have now been caught up in that and are likely to lose their jobs.

At best, the government are exporting jobs in the blending trade to Italy; at worst, they are increasing unemployment in both Italy and Cyprus, as well as in the United Kingdom. The Government's international aspirations know no bounds.

Col. 92 The clause blocks a duty loophole—as the hon. Gentleman put it—by making clear that the blending of two or more wines of different dutiable strengths constitutes the production of wine. I am advised that the amount of revenue at risk is about £10 million.

As the hon. Gentleman said, the 1984 Budget reduced the duty on light wine in order to comply with the judgment of the European Court of Justice in the wine and beer case that we discussed at length last year. The result of that was to widen the duty differential between light and higher-strength wines. Companies—Cinzano in particular—realised that if they could blend a high-strength wine, containing 18 to 22 per cent alcohol, with a low-strength wine, containing less than 15 per cent alcohol, and pay duty on the items to be blended, the end result would be a total duty of some 20p a bottle less on the finished product. To prevent the potential revenue loss, Customs and Excise ruled that such blending constituted production, and, as the hon. Gentleman, said the matter was challenged in the courts.

As it was pretty clear what the judgement would be to those who attended the hearing, the Chancellor decided to act in anticipation of the Law Lords' adverse decision and to prevent a loss of duty of more than £10 million per annum. The new provisions were incorporated in his Budget and brought into force on 26 March by Budget Resolutions.

The hon. Gentleman seemed particularly exercised by the prospect of double taxation. That did pose a problem at first. Wines came into the pipeline with the ditty paid and ready for blending, but the blending was not carried out, or was only partially carried out. To deal with the problem of stocks of wine for blending which were being held or in transit when the new provisions came into force, the Excise Duty (Wine) (Temporary Relief) Regulations 1985 provide for duty paid on the constituent wines to be set off against duty paid on the blended products. The regulations apply to wine blended before 1 July this year.

However, neither the Customs nor the Government intend to impose double taxation after 1 July. If the blending is to be done in this country, it will be possible to bring the wine in and not pay the duty and there-fore not incur the bank charges—until the wine leaves in its blended condition. We want to ensure that the duty is the same on the blended product whether it comes blended from overseas, is blended in this country or indeed is not blended at all but is of equivalent strength. That seems a fair arrangement, and one which I hope will be practicable under the existing law. However, if problems arise I shall want to be told about them immediately.

In March I met representatives of Cinzano with my noble Friend Lord Thorneycroft and my hon. Friend the Member for The Wrekin (Mr. Hawkesley), and we discussed the matter then. I should be happy to meet them again if they think that the law will impose a condition other than that which I have described.
