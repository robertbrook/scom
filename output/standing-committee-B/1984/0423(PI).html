clause Clause 28
title PENALTY FOR FAILURE TO COMPLY WITH DIRECTIONS ETC. OF TRIBUNAL

Mr. Tony Blair (Sedgefield): I beg to move amendment No. 60, in page 28, leave out lines 26 to 29.

The amendment has the effect of deleting subparagraph (2) from the new paragraph 10 which is to be added to schedule 8 of the Value Added Tax Act 1983.

The Keith committee very sensibly recommended in volume 2 of its report, chapter 24.6.5, that where the special commissioners or the VAT tribunal made an assessment of tax, or penalty, or surcharge it should be recoverable in the High Court as a debt in the same way as any other award made by a court. The purpose was to ensure that there was no need to take separate action for recovery.

Can the Minister say whether clause 28 follows Keith in the way that was intended? I assume that the purpose of the clause is to implement the Keith committee's recommendation, but I am worried that there may be a mix-up in the wording, and that is why I want to ask the Minister a question by way of a probing amendment.

New paragraph 10(1) states:

"A person who fails to comply with a direction or summons issued by a value added tribunal … shall be liable to a penalty not exceeding £1000."

Col. 218 Subparagraph (2) provides that the penalty under subparagraph (1)

"may be awarded summarily by a tribunal notwithstanding that no proceedings for its recovery have been commenced."

Is it the Bill's intention to limit the power to award summarily and to institute proceedings for recovery to the penalty for failure to comply with the directions or summons of the VAT tribunal? Or is the intention to cover the original assessment of the VAT tribunal or special commissioners, and provide that the penalty need not go through the process of special recovery but may be awarded by the VAT tribunal?

I hope that I have made the distinction between the two alternatives clear. I thought it worth putting the question to the Minister as, if we are to tighten up the enforcement powers fairly, the Keith recommendation seems sensible.

The Chief Secretary to the Treasury (Mr Peter Rees): If the hon. Gentleman will allow me to do so, may I say that he has made his point with perfect clarity? He should be able to judge from my answer whether I have understood his question.

Although we have, to a measure, adopted the Keith recommendation, we have diverged slightly. The proposal here relates not to the original assessment but to those cases where, for example, a person called before a VAT tribunal has ignored a witness summons or an order to produce documents. Prior to the Keith recommendations and prior to enactment of the Finance Bill—assuming that it finds favour with the House—a curious situation existed. The powers of the VAT tribunal with regard to such matters as witness summons and orders to produce documents had been enforced for the tribunal by Customs and Excise under what one might call criminal procedures. That was thought to be rather cumbersome and inappropriate.

The Keith committee recommended that the matter should be dealt with by a contempt of court action, which would involve going before the High Court. We thought that also rather cumbersome, so it is proposed that the tribunal should have the right, subject to appeal—the provisions for which are set out in new paragraph 10(3)—to impose a monetary penalty of up to £1,000.

The provision relates only to those narrow areas where, to be effective, the tribunal must have powers to ensure that proper evidence comes before it and that a witness crucial to the case obeys a summons. Originally, before the Bill, such matters were enforceable by the Customs and Excise, at the behest of the tribunal, by criminal procedures. Instead of that method, and instead of the Keith recommendation of going to the High Court for a contempt of court action, it was decided that it would be more appropriate to give the tribunal the power to make the conduct of its affairs, the cases heard before it, effective—subject, I emphasise, to the proper appeal procedure set out in new subparagraph (3).

I hope that I have answered the hon. Gentleman's question and reassured the Committee.
