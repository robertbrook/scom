May I remind the Committee of the purpose of clause 53, which follows extensive discussions between the Inland Revenue and representative bodies? It seeks to remove the doubts—as the hon. Member for Sedgefield said before dinner—that have been expressed about the date on which expenditure is incurred for capital allowance purposes. The date is important, as it may affect the timing of allowances and during the phasing out—the critical period—of first-year and initial allowances, their amount.

On the Inland Revenue's view of the present law, before these changes, allowances become due when the purchaser of an asset is required to pay for it. However, under accountancy practice, which has generally been followed by accountants and others when compiling capital allowance computations, the expenditure has commonly been regarded as incurred when the obligation to meet it arises, that is when a firm debt comes into existence.

The consultations with representative bodies, which were held in 1984, showed that there was no single, all-embracing, authoritative statement of the date when expenditure is to be treated as incurred for "normal accountancy purposes". That makes a simple solution very difficult to find. It may be, and commonly is, the date on which an invoice is issued, but other dates can be and are used, such as the date when the asset is delivered or the date when the invoice is received.

Image Col. 403 The approach adopted by clause 53 is to describe the date on which the obligation to pay the capital expenditure has a sufficient degree of certainty to require it to be reflected in business accounts. The clause, therefore, refers to the date on which the obligation to pay "becomes unconditional"—that is, the vendor will have completed his part of the contract and the purchaser's obligation to pay will be unconditional and absolute, though not necessarily immediate if the vendor is allowing a period of trade credit. I shall come to the period of credit later.

This approach covers both expenditure before delivery, such as deposits and progress payments, and expenditure on or after delivery. With one exception, to which the hon. Member for Sedgfield referred—and I shall come back to it in a moment—it will mean that the date on which expenditure is incurred for capital allowance purposes will fall at the beginning of any trade credit, rather than at the end, which is the focal point of difference between accountancy practice and the Revenue's view of the law as it stood before this Bill.

The one exception is where the period of trade credit exceeds three months. Here, the date for capital allowances will be the date or dates which are prescribed for payment by the contract. That will apply mainly to those contracts, likely to be in a small minority, where the asset has been delivered and the balance of the purchase price is payable in instalments, perhaps over several years. Just as he does now, the purchaser will receive his capital allowances for each of the years in which he is due to make the instalment payments.
