Mr. Hayhoe
: Amendment No. 214 seeks to achieve consistency in the clause. In two instances the words "at least one year" are used, and in another instance the words " one year " appear, the "at least" having been left out. There should be consistency throughout the clause, an damendment No. 214 achieves that end.

The next group of amendments—Nos. 206 to 210, and Nos. 211 to 213 relating to schedule 17— deal with two issues on which the Bill as published did not quite achieve what Ministers intended. Under the terms of the existing relief, which was introduced in 1965, an individual can get relief on such a sale of shares only if he is a full-time working director of the company whose shares are being sold, but for many years the Inland Revenue, by a published extra-statutory concession, has allowed relief if the company in which the shares are sold is a member of a group or commercial association of companies and the individual is a full-time working director of any one of those companies, or of the companies taken together. The amendments ensure that the new statutory relief will reflect that concessionary practice.

The amendments also relate to directors of family companies who retire " partially ", in the sense that they cease to work full-time. They get relief for sales of shares when they filially retire altogether, so long as they were over 60 when they ceased to work 444 full-time and the part-time working involves at least 10 hours a week. Those are important conditions. Under such circumstances the amendments seek to ensure that such an individual will get the full relief at an appropriate time.

I emphasise that although the amendments have a relieving provision compared with the clause as it stands, they do not add to the total relief that an individual can claim of the £100,000. That is inviolate and cannot be changed.

I seek to ensure that retirement relief is available in relation to assets which are held outside the business when there is a partial retirement from that business. As my hon. Friend the Minister of State, Treasury will know, there is a distinction, for capital gains tax purposes, between assets of the business and those which are used for the purposes of the business, but do not form part of it; for example, the partnership assets. It is common for land to be farmed by a partnership, but only one of the partners may own the land and it may not be held as a partnership asset. If, with increasing age, for example, that land-owning partner decides to withdraw from the business, he may dispose, or seek to dispose, of only part of that interest in the partnership. He will be eligible under existing law, and under the Bill's proposals, for relief on that proportion of the partnership's assets which is attributable to him. For those assets which, although used for the purpose of the partnership do not actually form part of the assets of the legal partnership, he will not qualify for retirement relief.

I see no reason why the relief should be restricted by what is allowable at the moment. The question whether particular assets should be held as part of the partnership or retained outside it is a matter for the partners themselves. In certain circumstances, it may make economic sense for the assets to be held by the partnership and in other circumstances it might be the reverse.

It is wrong that tax consequences should have to be borne in mind when the legal form rather than the substance of a business organisation is under discussion. There can be no fundamental policy objection to relief being available on a partial retirement, because that is permitted, in any event, on a partial disposal of the business. I see no fundamental policy objection to relief being extended in some cases to assets which are held outside the business.
