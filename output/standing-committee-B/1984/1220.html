Mr. Chris Smith
(Islington, South and Finsbury)
: I beg to move amendment No. 59, in page 4, line 40, at end insert—

"(cc) The national Film and Television School, in whose case a minimum annual grant of £2 million, up-rated annually in line with the Retail Price Index, shall be made."

The amendment relates to the future of the National Film and Television school and reflects the great anxiety felt by Opposition Members, which I am sure is shared by Conservative Members, about the welfare and future of the school. The amendment simply seeks to place a duty on the Government to make a minimum annual grant of £2 million to the school.

I shall outline some of the background of the National Film and Television school. Its existence has been guaranteed so far by the 1970 Act, which is being repealed by this Bill. The Act stipulates that there shall be a national school. At present, the school has three-year courses specialising in training producers, directors, writers, lighting and camera operators, editors, animators, art directors and sound recordists. About 25 students are admitted each year, with five places reserved for overseas students. The average age of students is 25 years. There are also short courses for retraining.

However, the repeal of the 1970 Act by this Bill, which does not name the National Film and Television school, would rob the school of any legislative existence. The Act stipulates that there shall be a school. This legislation makes no mention of the school. So part of our reason for tabling the amendment is that we feel that it should do so.

The White paper and the Minister' ads speech on Second Reading merely outline the agreement that the Minister reached with the BBC and independent companies and—I believe that I quote the Minister correctly—the cinemas, to provide funds. In our view, that is not a sufficient guarantee of the school's existence. I believe that the agreements between the Government and the companies which, apparently, have agreed to put money into the school, are in the form of letters. The agreements are due to last only for five years, and there is no guarantee beyond that five-year period. Our major concern, therefore, is about the removal of legislative existence and the existence of guarantees only in the form of agreement between the Minister and particular companies or organisations, and only for a period of five years.

I understand that the letters from the BBC and the independent television companies, agreeing to their contribution for the future of the National Film and Television school, specifically agreed to make that contribution on the understanding that there should be no levy on the showing of films on television. That caveat, which was inserted into their agreement to the future funding arrangement, to which the Minister referred on Second Reading and which was included in the White Paper, has been removed by the Committee by an earlier decision. So that raises a further question mark about the degree of security that we can place on the agreement so far entered into.

I shall outline the present sources of money for the JPEG 231 school. It receives funds from three principal sources—the Eady subvention, through the British Film Fund Agency; the Office of Arts and Libraries, which is part of the Department of Education and Science; and the BBC and independent television companies. The approximate contributions in the year ending April 1984 were £95,000 from ITV companies, £45,000 from the BBC, £500,000 from the Eady levy, and just under £1 million from the Office of Arts and Libraries. That makes a total income of about f1,640,000.

The 1984–85 figures, some of which are at present merely requested or required figures not yet confirmed, show that the Office of Arts and Libraries has again given just under £1 million, that the Eady levy has produced £600,000, that ITV is scheduled to give £160,000 and the BBC £65,000, and that income from fees is approximately 00,000. The scheduled income for the coming year is slightly up on that, although the figures for ITV and the BBC are roughly the same.

For the continued existence of the school and to ensure that it can continue the excellent work that it currently does, it is essential that it be guaranteed the present level of funding in real terms. We are doubtful whether the agreements that the Government have so far entered into and the lack of security involved in those agreements will enable that amount of funding to be procured.

If we assume that the Office of Arts and Libraries continues to provide the £1 million or so that it does at present—at our last sitting, we heard that, although part of it is for one-off capital reasons, an increase in the coming financial year is projected—the school needs from the trade some E750,000 or £800,000 in order to ensure that it can continue at the same level.

With the abolition of the Eady levy, the Government's current proposals are that the trade should contribute only about £600,000 a year, and that is less than the school needs this current financial year to fund its existing operation. Not only that, it is less than it actually needed last year to fund its operations. Therefore, we can have little confidence that the funding level currently being projected by the Government—taking E600,000 from the trade to put into the National Film and Television school will he sufficient to enable the school to continue to do the same work, and offer the same courses and the same standard of teaching as at present.

We should also note that the three main contributors have done very well out of the Bill. I understand that they have each agreed to put in £200,000, making the figure of £600,000 that the Government are projecting. For the cinema companies, that is less than half their current contribution through the Eady levy. The BBC and the independent companies, although they will be paying more than they are at the moment, are doing so in the belief that they will be avoiding a levy on films shown on television, and I hope that the Minister will address that point when he replies.

Three other points are worth making. First, it is not clear from either the White Paper or Second Reading whether the £250,000 which the Government propose to contribute to improve the school's television facilities is included in or is in addition to the Office of 232 Arts and Libraries' amount. I hope that the Government will give a specific response on that point.

: I beg to move, that the Clause be read a Second time.

The Clause relates to a very specific problem which is none the less of great concern. I raised this problem during the Government's initial statement on film policy and also on Second Reading. The Minister said on both occasions that he shared my concern. I have tabled the new clause to try to find out how the Government intend to tackle a real and practical problem in our national film archives.

The problem arises because until 1951 virtually all film was made on a base of nitro cellulose. It can therefore be described as nitrate film stock. It produced excellent photographic quality but it is subject to inevitable and irreversible chemical decay as well as becoming readily and dangerously inflammable. It therefore has to be stored carefully in extremely secure conditions. Moreover, if we wish to preserve the images that are recorded on it, the nitrate stock has to copied.

The period after which deterioration sets in is variable and the instability pattern of nitrate stock can be difficult to predict precisely. However, most experts agree that the lifespan of even the very last nitrate stock produced just before 1951 is unlikely to last much beyond the year 2000. Copying on to video tape does not provide an adequate alternative. The results tend to have poorer quality and there are doubts about the permanence of video tape. That has been bourne out by the experience of Visnews which transferred the old Gaumont, Universal and Paramount newsreel footage on to video tape and found the results much less satisfactory than the original images. The solution recommended by most people in the industry, and certainly by ACTT, which has produced an excellent paper on the subject, is that the images at present on JPEG 245 nitrate stock should be reproduced on to acetate. That process requires significant resources.

It is worth pausing for a moment to remember that this stock of film is not just part of our film heritage, but recalls a large amount of our history in the early years of this century. It would be a tragedy to lose that enormous cultural reserve. There are three main holdings of nitrate stock in this country. The largest single holding, by a long way, is that of the British Film Institute in the National Film Archive, which was set up in May 1935 with a duty to maintain a national repository of films of permanent value. It now has a collection of over 75,000 titles, including features and fiction films, shorts, documentaries, newsreels, amateur films and television programmes ranging from 1895 to the present day.

I shall return to the National Film Archive in a moment. The other two major holdings of nitrate film stock are in the Pathe Film Library and the Movietone Library, and it is to those two libraries that the new clause is specifically addressed.

The National Film Archive might be regarded by the Government as the logical mechanism for ensuring that film held by Movietone and Pathe should be copied and thus preserved. However, the archive is now working overtime to ensure that its existing stock can be preserved. The duplication rate required to preserve its entire stock is some 7 million feet per yard up to the year 2005, and it is clear that its programme of preservation—which is excellent, and much praised by everyone in the industry—is at full stretch. The facilities, let alone the funds, to take on any additional work for stock other than that already held by the archive simply are not three.

Paragraph 4(1) and (2) set out the requirements for a film to be a British film. The maker of the film must be either a resident of a member state or a company that is registered and whose central management and control is in a member state throughout the making of the film. By contrast, any studio used in making the film must be in a Commonwealth country—which, of course, includes the United Kingdom—or the Republic of Ireland.

With regard to feature films, the definition is identical to that which at present applies to the registration of British films under the Films Act 1960.

When the United Kingdom entered the European Economic Community, amendments were made by section 8 of the European Communities Act 1972 to extend the definition to include citizens of or companies incorporated in any member state. That 252 extension was necessary to comply with the obligations imposed on all member states by directives issued by the Council of the EEC requiring the removal of restrictions on the right of establishment and the freedom to provide services in the film industry. However, the requirements in respect of the location of the studios used were not amended by the 1972 Act because the use of national studios is accepted as a justifiable requirement from a cultural point of view and provides the security of skilled labour and the know-how essential to the atmosphere of a film.

The provisions in respect of labour costs therefore provide that not less than a stated proportion of the labour costs of making the film, such as, fees, salaries or wages paid to directors, producers, actors, actresses, technicians, and so on, is a necessary requirement. That is to prevent, for example, American labour counting towards the qualifying labour costs of films that could be certified under schedule 1.

That is the background in which the United Kingdom has demonstrated greater Community spirit than some other member states. To put the clock back some 15 years later, and re-erect the barrier against other member states, would be pointless and futile. Sooner or later—probably sooner—such a provision would be held by the European Court of Justice to be inconsistent with the fundamental right of establishment and the freedom to provide services.
