Clause 47
COVENANTED PAYMENTS TO CHARITY: INCREASE OF EXEMPTION FROM EXCESS TAX LIABILITY

Mr. Andrew Hunter
: I beg to move amendment No. 109, in page 45, line 12, after "companies", insert—

"in sub-paragraph (1) of paragraph 3 (power to apportion amounts deducted in respect of certain annual payments) for the word 'distributable' there shall be substituted the word 'relevant' and".

I shall try to combine brevity with doing justice to the weight of argument that lies behind the amendment. The purpose of the amendment is that, by substituting "relevant" for "distributable" in schedule 16 to the Finance Act 1972, trading income of trading companies paid out in charitable donations will not be subject to 368 apportionment. At present, charitable donations paid out by unquoted close companies are thus subject: the argument is simply that they should not be.

Individuals and companies that support the amendment point to five strands of argument, and I shall refer to them, rather than develop them in detail, because they will be well known to hon. Members.

The first and essential point is that the existing provisions of apportionment are a deterrent to charitable donations by the shareholders of unquoted companies. That deterrent, self-evidently, is undesirable. Secondly, it follows that by there being a deterrent against charitable donations from one sector, the Treasury is guilty of schizophrenia not least because of the speeches made by the present Foreign Secretary when he was Chancellor of the Exchequer in 1980 about the importance that the Government attached to charitable donations. So we have declarations of acclaim and intent on the one hand and a pocket of tax legislation that works counter to it on the other.

Thirdly, and perhaps the major argument in favour of the amendment, is that a fundamental anomaly now exists. The provisions that the amendment seeks to change were drawn up to ensure that no income that could be apportioned was lost to the apportionment mechanics. But since 1980, the trading income of trading companies has not been apportionable, so the anomaly arises of the initial income not being apportionable except in so far as it is donated to charities by unquoted close companies. The argument is that there is no logical reason why, if the original income can no longer be apportioned, charitable donations from it should be thus subject.

The fourth strand of argument is that the Inland Revenue, and perhaps the Treasury also, has indulged in a fallacious parity argument put forward to justify the present anomaly. It is argued that parity should exist between individuals and the shareholders of close companies. The counter-argument is that there should be parity between close and non-close companies. Charitable donations of non-close companies are not subject to apportionment.

Finally, and equally briefly, another argument is put forward by those who support the amendment and wish to see it implemented. No other EEC country or western industrialised country has such provisions, and it is therefore hard to see why the anomaly prevails in the United Kingdom.

Those, in essence, are the arguments for the amendment. I believe that they carry a great deal of weight. I hope that the Chief Secretary will comment on them.

The Chief Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Peter Rees)
: May I respond to the points so ably and succinctly made by my hon. Friends the Members for Baskingstoke (Mr. Hunter), for Corby (Mr. Powell) and for Slough (Mr. Watts), on the amendment standing in their names and that of my hon. Friend the Member for Staffordshire, South (Mr. Cormack), who does not have the privilege of being a member of this Committee!

It is in line with Government thinking that charitable donations should be encouraged. Our record in this respect—in income tax, capital gains tax and capital transfer tax — amply demonstrates where our heart lies.

Before I deal with the issues that were raised, I am tempted—but I shall reject the temptation—to point out that the amendment is defective. It would not achieve the purpose for which it is intended, because relevant income is arrived at after allowing deduction for annual payments. So to provide, as the amendment seeks to do, an apportionment for amounts deducted in arriving at relevant income would give exactly the same results as at present. In other words, there would be an apportionment of the annual payment. That, I think, is the opposite of what my hon. Friends seek.

I shall deal now with the underlying theme. The amendment raises issues of substance which deserve serious treatment. My hon. Friends have invited me 370 to assimilate the treatment of close companies to the treatment of un-close companies. I shall not say "public companies" or "quoted companies", because private, public and quoted companies can themselves be close companies, but I am sure the Committee understands what my hon. Friends propose.

It is more exact to assimilate close companies to a partnership or individuals, that being the treatment that has been accorded. The increase in the limit that we have imposed for individuals for relief on covenanted donations to charity will apply in this case to each individual member shareholder of a close company to whom the covenanted donation by the close company might be apportioned. That person will be able to get relief up to £10,000 a year. So clause 47 will benefit members of close companies, although I agree that it does not go as far as my hon. Friends want.

In our view, it is fairer to assimilate close companies to partnerships and individuals. That thinking underlies the concept of apportionment, which has been around since 1922 or thereabouts.

My hon. Friends may say, "Why not lift it altogether?" That is tempting. But if we did that we should then be told, "But why not lift it altogether for individuals?" At present, we prefer to rest on the undoubted relaxations that we have introduced since 1980. I remind the Committee that we first initiated relief for higher-rate tax for individuals for the first time since the 1940s. I think it was Dr. Dalton who first deprived individual taxpayers for relief from higher-rate tax for covenanted payments to charity. We have gone back to what happened before that time. First, we gave relief to £3,000 per annum, then to £5,000 per annum, and now it is £10,000 per annum. So we can be acquitted of a lack of generosity.

My hon. Friends say, "Remove your gaze from the position of individuals, and take it to that of non-close companies." But there is a fundamental difference. A non-close company has a wide spread of shareholders, and its directors would need to satisfy themselves that the donation to charity was appropriate in the circumstances of that company's case. By definition, a close company has a rather close-knit group of shareholders, who might not be over-concerned with what I shall call commercial reasons. They could even set up a company as a vehicle for charitable donations.

So for the moment, it is not necessarily the policy of the Government to take the matter further. We feel that considerable relief has been given to individuals, and that clause 47 and its precursors have demonstrated a great appreciation for the position of charities. I realise that my hon. Friends do not entirely accept that we should assimilate the case of close companies to that of the individual shareholders rather than assmilating non-close companies. However, I hope that my hon. Friends will not press the amendment to a Division.

The comparison with foreign countries can often be misleading. One must know and understand, as I am sure that my hon. Friends do, the provisions of a country's tax legislation. There would be no advantage in adopting that route in the United States or Canada because there the individual would not get relief if he made the payment. Foreign analogies do not necessarily advance the argument.

Although I understand the intention underlying my hon. Friends' amendment, it is defective. I hope that JPEG 371 they will feel that the broad increasing measure of relief offered by clause 47, which will apply in the case of close companies, does adequate justice to the cause of hon. Members on both sides of the Committee.
