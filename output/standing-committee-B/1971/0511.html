Mr. Rost: We are now coming on to what I regard as one of the most important Clauses in this Bill. My Amendments which we are now debating, No. 27 coupled with Amendment No. 28, draw attention to the feeling in a wide section of the private enterprise industry which will be directly affected by the functioning of the corporation that there is a need for greater competition and freedom to sell gas produced within the private sector. The present legislation does not allow the oil companies to sell gases produced as by-products in the refining of crude oil at United Kingdom refineries without the Col. 948 consent of the area boards. This has only rarely been given and the oil companies feel that there is a case here for some Amendment.

If these Amendments are not accepted, oil refineries may be compelled to utilise gases less economically, and potential customers near to refineries may be denied the right of purchase.

Under subsection (4) of this Clause the oil companies have the right of appeal to the Secretary of State if consent is refused, but the question arises as to what criteria will be adopted by the corporation or the Minister for giving or withholding consent and how this discretion would be applied. The oil companies on the whole feel that they should be allowed the opportunity to sell by-product gases direct to bulk industrial customers within a reasonably narrow area of the refinery.

I should apologise for the somewhat sloppy drafting of the Amendment, which I can only attribute to my Parliamentary inexperience in these matters, but I do not believe that that in any way detracts from the sense or the meaning of the Amendment. If anything it clarifies it.

The purpose of the Amendment is to allow the oil companies to sell by-product gases to bulk industrial customers within a radius of, say, 10 miles by mutual arrangement, and it is my belief that such a very modest infringement of the monopoly powers which are being incorporated into this legislation would be consistent with a national fuel policy and would also be within the spirit of the legislation.

There are four reasons why I would hope that my hon. Friend would give these Amendments serious consideration. In the first place, these refinery gases, which, it is hoped, there would be discretion to sell direct to industry, are not compatible with the natural gas which the corporation sells, and it may well be far more economical to pipe these gases direct to industrial customers who are prepared and suited to burn those gases without having, through this legislation, to be forced to convert these by-product gases into a natural gas equivalent. This to me seems to make sense.

Secondly, by our allowing this modest infringement of the monopoly powers, the refinery companies would be enabled to balance their refinery operations far more economically than they would be able to do if they were restricted unduly.

Image Col. 949 Thirdly—and this, I accept, may be politically controversial, but I believe the Committee ought to consider it—I believe that these Amendments would provide a modest stimulus to competition for the British Gas Corporation without seriously offending the monopoly powers which will be enacted.

Fourthly, the Amendments, if adopted—and this I hope will appeal to hon. Members opposite—could well accelerate the development of industrial sites near refineries, and these are mostly in development areas where industrial development and employment are urgently needed. The industrial complex would be more economically unified through the facility of providing these by-product gases direct to industry. Milford Haven and Teesside are two areas where this could happen. This is an important consideration, because the restrictive nature of the Clause may hamper this type of industrial development.

I firmly believe that if these Amendments are accepted in principle they will not in any way encroach on the gas industry's primary function, which is that of a public utility providing gas to the consumer. It is entirely consistent that at the same time this modest concession should be granted so that there would be slightly more flexibility, a little more competition and the obvious advantages of industrial development.
