Poverty and Opportunity

10.30 am

The Chairman
: The first item of business is a statement on tackling poverty in Scotland. There is no time limit for statements and subsequent questions. However, statements eat into the time allowed for the major debate and I shall therefore act to ensure that the Committee has time for that debate.

The Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. Alistair Darling)
: With permission, Mr. Maxton, I shall make a short statement on the Government's progress in tackling poverty in Scotland—progress that depends upon our working closely with the Scottish Executive. Our objective is to tackle poverty and social exclusion by dealing with their symptoms and rooting out their causes. Tackling poverty and providing opportunity for all are essential planks in our policy. The Government and the Scottish Executive are working hand in hand to halve child poverty within 10 years and to eradicate it within a generation.

The report "Opportunity for All", published in September 1999, sets out the Government's strategy to tackle poverty and social exclusion. That report was complemented by the report launched last November by the First Minister of the Scottish Executive entitled "Social Justice". Those reports set out strategies and targets for tackling poverty and social exclusion. We are taking children out of poverty and we are moving people out of poverty and into work. There are now 700,000 more people in work. In Scotland, 2.3 million more people are in jobs—the highest level since 1966. Youth unemployment is down by nearly three quarters, thanks to the new deal and other measures. We are also reforming pension provisions for the future as well as tackling the poverty of the poorest pensioners.

Income, however, is only part of the picture. As well as tackling poverty of the pocket, we are determined to fight poverty of opportunity and poverty of expectation. Social exclusion is caused by more than poverty. It occurs when other factors—unemployment, poor housing and poor health—combine with poverty to trap individuals in a spiral of disadvantage. It needs joined-up government to grasp those problems effectively. The Scottish Executive has responsibility for many of the issues that are vital to tackling those problems at the root, and the Government are working in partnership with the Executive in order to make real progress.

We are committed to halving child poverty within 10 years and eliminating it within 20 years. From April 2000, the rate of child benefit for a first child will be £15; for the second child and subsequent children it will be £10. That change will benefit 1 million Scottish children. We have introduced the working families tax credit, which ensures a minimum weekly income of £200 for 140,000 Scottish families. As a result of the 4 various measures that we have taken, about 60,000 children in Scotland will be lifted out of poverty. Families will, on average, gain by about £740 a year.

As I said, we are dealing not only with incomes, but with opportunity—which will be the result of better skills and qualifications. The Government's work to combat child poverty and social exclusion is being complemented by the work of the Scottish Executive. From next year, total spending on school education will, in real terms, be at an all-time high. For example, the early intervention programme, which will be backed by an investment of £56 million between 1997 and 2002, will help children in the earliest years of primary school who have problems with literacy and numeracy. That will benefit at least 5,000 additional children by 2002. More than 50,000 four-year-olds currently benefit froma nursery place, and all three-year-olds will have part-time nursery places by 2002. It is the sum of those measures, not any one of them, that will ensure that Scottish children, wherever they live, get a good start in life.

Tackling the poverty of children is often about tackling the poverty of their parents. More than 20 per cent. of working age households in Scotland are without work. Although unemployment is declining, many families are still without work. Between 1979 and 1996–97, the proportion of the population in workless households doubled.

We have introduced changes to make work pay. More than 100,000 employees in Scotland are entitled to higher pay as a direct result of the national minimum wage. On average, that raised the incomes of those who benefit from it by 30 per cent. The 10p starting rate of income tax and the working families tax credit will improve the incomes of the poorest.

We are helping those who can work to get work with the new ONE service and the new deals. More than 20,000 young people in Scotland moved into work with the help of the new deal. There is a ONE pilot in the Clyde coast and Renfrew district, and an employment zone prototype in Glasgow. We are seeing the results. Employment in Scotland has increased by 38,000 since 1997. We need to do more, of course.

Complementing the improvements, the Scottish Executive are tackling poor housing and homelessness. The Minister for Communities has announced a package of £12 million in grants to local authorities to combat homelessness and rough sleeping. Last week, 400 new homes were opened in Castlemilk as part of a £23 million project to provide new homes for rent and for low-cost ownership.

We are making reforms for pensioners, too. We are restructuring pensions so that the low paid, carers and disabled workers will do better—18 million people will gain across the country. We are helping people to provide for a retirement to look forward to. We are also tackling pensioner poverty now. We are setting a minimum baseline for the incomes of the poorest with the minimum income guarantee, which will help more than 150,000 Scottish pensioners. Winter fuel payments of £100 to each pensioner household—paid, for the first time, last year before Christmas—benefit 900,000 pensioners in Scotland. The payments will now be made every year.

The oldest and poorest pensioners will each gain more than £500 a year from the improvements that we have made—winter fuel payments, changes to VAT on fuel, the minimum income guarantee and the JPEG 5 introduction of free television licences, which benefits 250,000 Scottish households.

We must help those who were left out during the past 20 years, and address the inequalities and the deep divisions that we inherited. We cannot hope to make a difference for people if we consider their problems as separate issues. Some of the problems are about worklessness, but they are also about poor health, poor housing and poor education. Social exclusion is the sum of those difficulties. The fight is against poverty of opportunity and poverty of expectation, as well as against the poverty of income. We shall fight those evils. We are determined to deliver change and a better future for society fit for generations to come. I commend the statement to the Committee.

That is real help for the poorest people in the country as well as carers and disabled people. Some 18 million people will benefit from the changes that we are making to the state second pension. We are making long-term pensions changes, helping those who cannot save and assisting moderate earners by increasing the money that they receive to put into their occupational pensions. Crucially, we are helping the oldest and poorest pensioners—the very people who lost out under the Tory Government.
