It will be within the recollection of the Committee that two Clauses of the Bill have at different times been described as the most important or even as the most controversial. Those are Clauses 1 and 11. In my view, Clause 19, while it may not be the most important, is certainly the most controversial. It has two purposes. First, subsection (1) restricts the rights of teachers to the enjoyment of certain procedures and securities in the matter of dismissal to registered teachers and withdraws those rights from certificated teachers.

Secondly, subsection (2) has the effect of preparing the way for the extension of compulsory registration to university 610 teachers and teachers in further education. The hon. Gentleman the Under-Secretary shakes his head at this point, but I hope he will be able to elaborate on it, because we have had little or no information about the implication of subsection (2). However, I now wish to concentrate in the first place on subsection (1).

We should understand what are the rights which it is proposed to withdraw from the certificated teacher and to confer upon the registered teacher. The relevant provision of the Education (Scotland) Act, 1962, the parent Act, lays down, firstly,

"No resolution of an education authority for the dismissal from their service of a certificated teacher other than a teacher who has completed forty-five years of first class service … within the meaning of the Teachers (Superannuation) Regulations shall be valid unless—

  1. written notice of the motion for his dismissal shall, not less than three weeks before the meeting at which the resolution is adopted, have been sent to the teacher and to each member of the education authority; and
  2. not less than one-half of the members of the education authority are present at the meeting; and
  3. the resolution is agreed to by two-thirds of the members so present."

Section 85(3) states

"If at any time within six weeks after the adoption of a resolution … a petition is presented to the Secretary of State by the said teacher, praying for an inquiry into the reasons for his dismissal, the Secretary of State shall make such inquiry as he sees fit …"

These are the rights which it is proposed to withdraw from certificated teachers and to extend to registered teachers in their place. We should be clear that these rights are written into the contract of every teacher who was in employment with an education authority in Scotland prior to 1st April last. The proposal to withdraw them raises fundamental issues. The Government are, however, going very much further. They are at present engaged in evicting and, indeed, hounding out of employment a handful of teachers who have still declined to register with the General Teaching Council, and are doing so in a manner intended to cause the breaking and disruption of existing contracts of employment.

JPEG 611 There is a revealing contrast between all the wonderful things the Government say in documents like "In place of Strife" about contracts of employment and what they themselves are doing about teachers in Scotland who decline to register. Before we agree to the Clause we should consider how the Government propose to treat the teachers. In January, the Government decided to issue default orders against certain local authorities which had failed to agree to issue dismissal notices to teachers in their employ who had declined to register.

On 7th February, there was an interesting report in The Glasgow Herald explaining how the orders would work. It said:

"The authorities have been given until March 28 to comply with the provisions of the School (Scotland) Code that govern the employment of teachers in the schools…. Orders were made yesterday by Mr. Ross declaring these authorities to be in default and requiring them to comply with their statutory duty. An official of the Scottish Education Department explained last night that the orders mean that by March 28 the authorities must no longer be employing unregistered teachers. It was not a matter of teachers being given notice of dismissal by that date. Asked how this would affect individual contracts that teachers may have with the authorities, such as one month's or two months' notice, he said:—'It is for the authorities to work out what steps to be taken'."

That statement has never been corrected, contradicted or altered by the Government. The implications are clear, that local authorities on which default orders have been served and which have binding contracts of employment with teachers in their employ, such contracts not expiring till after 28th March, are required by the Government to break them. This is unforgivable.

The Government have been reluctant to admit what they have been doing and what they propose to do. Time and again I have asked the hon. Gentleman what local authorities were to be required to do about teachers whose existing contracts would guarantee them the right to continue to be paid after 28th March. In the County of Angus, only a tiny handful of teachers have not registered, but it seems certain that they will not receive notices of dismissal which could enable them to run out their contracts by 28th March.
