This amendment to a series of insurance provisions constitutes the culmination of a process that began with the Finance Bill in 1987 and an undertaking made by the then Chief Secretary to reveiw the basis of the taxation of insurance. At the time, a process was set in train, the intention of which was to take a general look at the tax arrangements for life assurance which had developed piecemeal over a long period. In due course, that review was introduced and the Finance Bill of 1989 contained the first tranche of its recommendations. The measures that we shall discuss this afternoon were announced in December of last year.

Our attitude to the review was marked by our anxiety to ensure that the British insurance industry is competitive, especially as we approach 1992 and the implications that that will have for the economy and for the insurance industry in particular. Given the implementation of EEC directives on freedom of services, we are anxious that the competitive position of the insurance industry is not undermined by the Government's proposals. We want to maintain a fair balance of interests between ordinary taxpayers and policy holders and to ensure that an undue burden does not fall on them.

Over the years, the tax burden on the insurance industry had increased faster than its rate of growth. That is not out of line with the experience of other savings institutions. After measuring the Government's performance in introducing the proposals against that criterion, we welcome them as a basis for the future taxation of the industry, but we have several specific concerns about schedule 4. We seek certain assurances from the Government that the worries of the industry will be met.
