DRAFT AGRICULTURAL LIME SCHEME (EXTENSION OF PERIOD) ORDER 1974

10.30 a.m.

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. E. S. Bishop)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the Draft Agricultural Lime Scheme (Extension of Period) Order 1974.

I think the Committee will welcome this extension of the scheme, and I do not think there is any need to go into it too deeply. The purpose of the order is to continue for a further five years, from the end of this month, the authority to make payments to farmers in the United Kingdom towards the cost of liming agricultural land.

As the Committee will know, liming is a vital farming operation. We believe that this subsidy is an investment in the structure of the soil, as without adequate liming the soil would be impoverished and our national resources depleted. Farmers know the value of liming, but a lime subsidy has played an important part in farming policy since 1937. We propose, therefore, to continue the lime subsidy in its present form at an estimated annual cost to the Exchequer of £5 million. I therefore invite the Committee to approve the draft order accordingly.

10.32 a.m.

Mr. Michael Jopling
: I want to speak only briefly on this order. The Committee will recollect that this scheme was terminated, or notice was given that it was to be terminated, some time ago. I think the reason that announcement was made by the previous Government was that it was held at that time that the scheme was in contravention of the terms of our accession to the European Economic Community. As I read the Treaty of Rome, it seems to me that a lime scheme is in flagrant violation of Article 4 92 of the Treaty of Rome. But I understand perfectly well that the way in which the treaty is dealt with in Brussels and the attitude of the Commission to countries such as ours which find themselves in difficulties is one of benevolence and a certain degree of flexibility. Perhaps the Minister would confirm that, on the face of it, this type of scheme is in contravention of Article 92 of the treaty, which does not allow State aids which distort competition.

With regard to the use of lime and its advantages in agricultural practice, I agree with what the Minister said in his opening remarks, but I remember that when I was a student at university my professor of agriculture used to trot out an old farming adage:

"Lime and lime and no manure


Makes both farm and farmer poor"

The truth is that at the turn of the century, before modern agricultural science took on its present sophisticated and advanced knowledge, there were many farmers who put lime on their fields year after year, but did not put on much manure, and the fertility of the soil rapidly decreased.

That leads me to ask the Minister whether it is now accepted by the Commission and the people in Brussels who look after such things that a lime subsidy is legal. Is it not true that a fertiliser subsidy could be brought back? If it is possible to introduce a lime subsidy, is there any technical objection to bringing back a fertiliser subsidy as well? That is a fair question, and I should be grateful if the Minister would deal with it.

10.34 a.m.

Mr. Bishop
: In reply to the two questions asked by the hon. Member for Westmorland (Mr. Jopling), I think I can say that the subsidy will be considered in the usual way by the EEC Commission in its programme of harmonising State aids to agriculture. Articles 92 to 94 of the Treaty of Rome prohibit any aid which distorts, or threatens to distort, competition in trade between Member States, and the hon. Gentleman has rightly referred to that. The provisions require Member States to notify the Commission of plans to grant or alter State aid.

The lime subsidy will come up for review in the Commission's programme. I cannot anticipate what the result may JPEG 5 be but, as the hon. Gentleman says, there is enormous advantage in liming. The application of lime has been running at about 4½ million tons a year, which is the level estimated to be sufficient to maintain the existing situation.

With regard to the comparison between fertilisers and lime, the hon. Gentleman may recall from his college days that lack of lime can increase the acidity of the soil and result in a loss of fertility and a reduction in crops. This is, therefore, a long-term investment in soil. I am not sure of the Commission's attitude in this matter. It may say that fertilisers give a seasonal or annual boost to crops and this may distort competition between the Member States, but it may well take the view that lime is long-term investment in a different category.

Mr. Jopling
: I hesitate to attempt to blind the Minister with science, but some people might argue that the premise on which he is now working is contentious. Liming has to be a regular practice. The level of acidity in most land tends to rise over the years unless it is corrected by regular applications of lime. On my own farm, we apply lime every four or five years, right round the farm.

One could say the same thing about fertilisers. I quote as an example potassium, the level of which tends to drop over the years and has to be boosted regularly. Potassium is not leached out of the soil. It tends to stay there and be removed only by crops. It does not tend to be lost in the same way as nitrogen, about which the Minister is perhaps thinking. Therefore I think that it is valid to refer to the similarity between lime and fertilisers.

Mr. Bishop
: I am grateful to the hon. Member for that short technical dissertation. Some of his points will, of course, be borne in mind by the Commission, but the fact is that liming affects the range of crops that will be grown in the next four or five years. Liming is a longterm investment in the soil structure, compared with fertilisers, which have a shorter period of effectiveness. These are matters for the Commission. The United Kingdom has operated the lime scheme and subsidies since 1937, and we would have a strong case before the Commission for its continuation. I appreciate the welcome which the hon. Member has given 6 to the continuation of the scheme, and we think that it will be welcomed by the farming community generally.

Question put and agreed to.

Resolved,

That the Committee have considered the Draft Agricultural Lime Scheme (Extension of Period) Order 1974.

10.41 a.m.

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. E. S. Bishop)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the Hop Gardens (Replanting and Restructuring) Scheme 1974.

I think that the Committee and agriculture as a whole will welcome the scheme. It is made under Section 29 of the Agriculture Act 1970, and enables grants of up to £280 an acre to be paid to growers for replanting their hop gardens with improved varieties of hops, for renewing JPEG 7 the structures on which the hops are grown or for a combination of these operations.

The scheme will help growers to adapt their production to varieties with a high content of bittering acids, which are being increasingly demanded by brewers and the public and to improve the structures on which they are grown. It will provide assistance for our own hop growers similar to that already available in the other hop-producing countries of the Community. There are, however, certain differences. EECRegulation No. 1696 of 1971, which provides for the grants to be made, permits them to be paid only to recognised producer groups. We have the Hops Marketing Board, as Members know, whose valuable contribution to the well-being of the industry is acknowledged, but we do not have producer groups as defined by the regulation. It was therefore necessary, as I am sure the hon. Member for Westmorland (Mr. Jopling) will appreciate, for us to seek a derogation from the strict provisions of the regulation so as to pay these grants to individual producers rather than to groups, and this we have succeeded in doing, though it took quite a bit of negotiation.

Although our own hop growers have an advantage in that they can be considered individually for grant aid, they are at a slight disadvantage in that the provision of the Act of Accession under which we secured the derogation limits it to the period to 31st January 1975. The regulation itself permits payment of these grants until 31st December 1975. Because of the unavoidable delay in securing the derogation to which I have referred and the limited period of availability of these grants, we propose, and the scheme provides, for them to be paid on work done during the whole of the two years provided by the derogation, even though this dates from 1st February 1973. I should perhaps stress that the scheme is designed to encourage the improvement of hop gardens and not the expansion of hope production, and it contains provisions to this end.

In conclusion, I need not remind hon. Members of the important part played by our hops in the brewing of British beer. The scheme that we are considering will help in no small measure—I think that 8 is an appropriate term—to maintain this predominance by helping our growers to meet the demands of the users of the hops and the consumers of the beer. This must have a popular appeal, and I hope that the Committee will accept the scheme.

Mr. Bishop

The scheme gives grant aid for replanting with certain varieties, and these are named as Bullion, Northern Brewer, Wye Target, Wye Northdown, Wye Challenger, and Keyworths Midseason. In order to encourage the planting of material of the highest quality, a higher rate of grant will be paid for plants bought from a propagator registered as A plus under the certification scheme operated by the Ministry.

My reply to the comments of the hon. Member for Westmorland (Mr. Jopling) is that any permanent equipment used for complete rewiring or repoling will be eligible for a restructuring grant, but replacement of parts of wire work under normal maintenance procedures will not be eligible for a grant, nor will labour costs.

The hon. Member made a valid point when he questioned our policy of going back in time to cover eligibility for the grant. EEC Regulation 423 /74 provides a special derogation from the main hops regulation EEC 196/71 so that the grant may be paid in the United Kingdom to individual producers, and I have already referred to this. The derogation runs from 1st February 1973—the date on 12 which the Common Agricultural Policy became operative in the United Kingdom—for two years, which is the maximum allowed under the Treaty of Accession. The derogation was not obtained until early in 1974, and although it is not usual to make a scheme to pay grant on work which in many cases will have already begun, and in some cases finished, it is considered acceptable in this scheme, first, because it was impossible to make provisions for implementing the scheme until the Community had granted a derogation—I think that that is important—and, secondly, because it is reasonable that we should make available to our own producers, as far as EEC regulations permit, a benefit that has been enjoyed by competitors in other Community countries. In the existing circumstances, it will not be possible for a grant to be paid in the United Kingdom for work carried out after the final date specified in the derogation, that is, 31st January 1975.

We have been in consultation with the Community for a long time in seeking to get a derogation, and interested hop growers will have been notified of this. I have questioned the Department on the point, and I understand that detailed information is available within it about aspects of grant aid which have appeared in the last two years.
