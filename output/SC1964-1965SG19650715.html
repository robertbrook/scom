Mr. Patrick Wolrige-Gordon
: It is a great honour to speak from the Front Bench of the Conservative Party, but it is an even greater honour to speak on the fishing industry. Other industries are larger—numerically, perhaps, more important. Each one has its men of calibre and each has its particular fascination. But those who work at sea and those who handle their catch—often early in the morning at an hour when this House of Commons has begun to consider that it is time to go to bed—are among the best in Britain, and I am glad to speak on their behalf.

It may be because the fishing industry is so much smaller than other industries—particularly agriculture, with which its product is in most direct competition—that it has fewer champions, less concern and less money. There is need to examine and to clarify the position of the industry for that reason alone, apart from the fact that I believe that the fishing industry has men of the quality and the calibre who can demonstrate to the country how years of petty division and mistrust can be answered, and the ensuing accord used as a basis for radical improvement in the years ahead.

The fundamental division in the fishing industry is between land and sea. Fishermen spend their lives on the water, and it is true to say that when they get home they have neither the time nor, sometimes, the energy to see properly to their interests. In consequence, it has often been easy in the past for unscrupul- 272 ous elements on the landward side to exploit the fishermen's labour, and make easy money at its expense. In return, it is not unknown for fishermen to fish to a quota to keep prices high, or in any other way open to them to embarrass the home market.

All this has bred bitterness and misunderstanding which are still there, although, perhaps, not in quite as acute a form as in the past. Nevertheless, it can flare up at any moment in any section, as, for instance, we have recently seen in the berthing dispute in Aberdeen. I believe that the abolition of that feeling is the prime requirement of the industry. It has bedevilled operations for too long, too much.

Let me turn now to questions of policy. The hub of the Scottish fishing industry is in Aberdeen. In 1963, 2,014,677 cwt. of fish, of a value of £6,691,276 were landed there. In 1964, 2,217,487 cwt. were landed. The next heaviest landings at any other port in Scotland were at Fraserburgh in 1963–514,197 cwt.—and in 1964, 431,108 cwt.—in other words about five times less than than Aberdeen.

The importance of Aberdeen to the Scottish fishing industry is therefore unassailable, and its importance to that part of the country is also beyond disputs. Yet I have a letter from the Secretary to the Harbour Board in Aberdeen in which he states that expert advice has been given

"that the condition of the wharves is such that they may have to be closed down completely at short notice."

If that happens, the fishing industry in Scotland will have to limp along, presumably, in any way it can, but it will have suffered a crippling blow.

It is not as though people do not know. There is general agreement about the serious condition of some of the wharves. The argument centres on who is responsible for doing something about them, for finding the money, etcetra. That is plainly the wrong point. I feel responsible, and I think that anyone genuinely concerned with the welfare of Scotland and its fishing industry feels responsible, too. I am sure that the Minister does, and I would urge him to provide help, amend the legislation, instill some urgency—anything to get the job done. I would far prefer to see the Secretary JPEG 273 of State grappling with a repair fund than with a disaster fund. We must prevent Aberdeen fish market disappearing down the Dee. That is one of my suggestions on policy.

The Aberdeen fish market raises a general question affecting not only Aberdeen but many other ports—the question of handling and selling the fish on the quayside. Let us take boxes as an example. In its Report this year the White Fish Authority mentions regulations aimed at stopping the transportation of fish in dirty or unsuitable boxes—regulations which, incidentally, the Government have not been able to confirm. The use of dirty boxes can damage all the other efforts in all the other sections of the industry to present a clean quality article to the housewife. It must end.

Many aspects of the fishing industry have been greatly modernised—catching methods, packing methods, distributive methods, cold storage. I am impressed, too, with the fact that most fishermen are prepared to concede that the selling and distribution of fish is a highly commercial and competitive process, and that the differential between the firsthand sale price and the retail sale price is usually a fair one. Nevertheless, the high standard of modernisation and advance is not uniform, and far from obvious on every quayside. Conditions in Aberdeen itself are far from good. I cannot commend too highly those who seek to bring them up to date or urge too strongly those who use such conditions as an excuse for slackness or inefficiency to participate more effectively.

What sort of fleet, and how large a fleet, can we expect to have in the future? At the moment, a trawler owner can build a new ship if he scraps an old one. He can build a freezer trawler as he scraps two, or possibly three. Inevitably, this means a smaller fleet. But the position is worse than that. In a good year, Aberdeen trawlers can earn enough money to cover the liabilities of the present fleet—just; some 10 of them have had to be sold already. But even the fortunate ones cannot earn enough to cover the provision of a new fleet, and in ten or twelve years' time a new fleet will be urgently needed. At the same time, under present arrangements, the subsidy scheme for new building will 274 have disappeared. What is to happen? I can only tell the Committee what I think should happen. The grant and loan scheme should be reviewed. Trawler owners should be allowed to modernise on a one-for-one basis, and we should aim for expansion in the industry.

What about the inshore fleet? The position here is only slightly better. One or two new boats are being built, but very few. Costs of new building are growing, and it is becoming more difficult for fishermen, and particularly young fishermen, to find the 15 per cent. capital deposit they require. Again, I should like to see the country aim for a larger industry. Research has been put in hand to provide the information needed on which to build a new fleet of the right size, but it needs little research to know that we are already importing a tremendous amount of fish. I would far prefer to have that fish caught by British vessels.

It can be argued that already there is difficulty in obtaining crews, not only for trawlers but for inshore vessels. The fact is that other countries, whose fishing industries have expanded while ours has remained static, back their efforts with considerable attention to recruitment and training in the fishing schools. We have only just begun to tackle that point. If we accept, as I do, the need for growth in our industry we must accept, too, the need to attract the right young people, and give them the training needed for highly-skilled and difficult work.

Unhappily, the Government's policy seems to lie in the opposite direction. It was announced last week that they had cut the subsidy scales for herring and white fish by 10 per cent. This confirms the forebodings about their intentions which prompted the associations concerned, as long ago as the beginning of May, to take the unprecedented step of asking for a meeting with the Secretary of State in order to discuss the position before the new regulations were laid before Parliament. I hope that the right hon. Gentleman will meet them.

In the meantime, what is the aim of hon. Gentlemen opposite? Do they want a fishing industry, or do they think that it may be cheaper to buy our fish abroad rather than to support the industry at JPEG 275 home? That is an economic theory which they have already applied, with, if I may say so, impressive innocence, to aircraft. Is the fishing industry to be treated in the same way as the aircraft industry? We are, it seems, to obtain our defence increasingly from America. Where shall we get our fish?

So far, apart from the 10 per cent. cut in the existing scheme, we have had very little action from the Government. The minimum prices scheme will involve Treasury participation—there has not been a move. As I have already said, the fishing industry is not remarkable for its unity, but it is united about the minimum prices scheme. Indeed, the Scots have been after it for years, and for this reason, that the day must come when men stop breaking their backs to bring in fish which no one wants and see it go to waste. They day must come, too, that when the fish are there, and needed, the fishermen will bring them in at a price which the processors can pay and still be able to compete in a highly-competitive and very tight world market. I therefore urge the Government to get a move on here. It will help to bring more stability and confidence where both are greatly needed—on the fish market as well as on the fishing boat; and, too, I think it will promote some measure of co-operation between them.

The farmer receives a 33⅓ per cent. improvement grant for his farm. The boat is in exactly the same relation to the fisherman as the farm is to the farmer. Is there any reason why it should not be treated in the same way from the point of view of improvement? Have the Government considered having all inshore fishing boats paid either a daily rate of subsidy or a stonage rate of subsidy, at any rate, paid on the same basis?

I want now to refer to conservation of fish stocks and scientific co-operation with the industry. The White Fish Authority is beginning to co-ordinate scientific research, and I welcome this. Not always are the scientists loved or even understood by their fellow men. So it is in fishing. The failures make more impact than the successes, but the greatest, and I believe it to be a needless failure, is failing to win the co-operation of and to enlist the aid of the industry which they are there to serve. I hope that the White Fish Authority and all the scientists concerned will bear that in mind when, for example, the Discovery next goes to the Faroes and that someone will take the trouble to explain to the industry why it is going, and what it is that is being learned. Otherwise there will be misunderstanding and suspicion as to the value of that kind of work, and yet that work can be invaluable.

We need to know far more about the sea which covers 70 per cent. of the world's surface. It is an unlimited treasure chest if we learn how to use it aright. At present it supplies only about 1 per cent. of human food and 12 per cent. of the protein component of the world's food, and yet even now it is estimated that it could provide enough protein to meet 10 times the need of the world's present population. Fish farming is an important example of what could be done. Apropos what I have just said, I was glad to see that the White Fish Authority took the trouble to explain to Ardnamurchan what it is trying to do with fish farming in the Highlands and what it sees for the future. I have no doubt about the importance of that work. I think that it will grow greatly and greatly increase the industry's productivity.

Conservation is more an international question and will take time to solve. This is another example in this world of where common sense is so obvious and so necessary and yet so much neglected. I believe it to be an important part of good husbandry, and I suggest that this country applies it as far as we can, that is, in our territorial waters.

There is sometimes heard the idea of banning netting for fish within our JPEG 277 territorial waters up to a certain limit, say, six miles, so that anyone who wants to fish in those waters would have to use lines and rely on the quality premium which such fish would fetch in the market. Anybody else would have to go outside the limit to use nets. That is the kind of idea which it is much easier to discuss than to put into practice. It would mean great difficulties for certain sections of the fleet and for a few years it might upset the balance of supply badly. Certainly it could not be introduced without great care and without enlisting the co-operation of the industry and others concerned, but it is a suggestion which ought seriously to be considered.

Transport is an issue which has rightly caused much publicity in the Press over the last weeks. I take the opportunity again to urge on the Government the absolute necessity of a good transport service for the small ports and the small consignments, often of highly valuable fish.

I should like to conclude with what I want to see from the fishing industry and an indication of how we can achieve it. I want to see the industry at peace, united within itself, efficient, prosperous and growing at small cost to the nation, but giving an infinite return. So far as we in Parliament are concerned, this can be achieved through the industry's administration. I spoke about the division and suspicion and distrust which are never far beneath the surface in the industry, not only between the various sections, but often internally in the same sections. The only people far enough removed from the centres of the storms to deal effectively with this are the administrators, in this instance the Scottish Office, the Herring Industry Board and the Scottish Committee of the White Fish Authority.

So far, they have not done so and they have failed for the same reason that those more intimately concerned with the industry, on whichever side, have failed—because they have hopelessly underestimated the lengths to which they have to go in order to win the confidence and the co-operation of those on either side.

In the winter of 1963, thanks to a fair amount of hard work and great Ministerial efficiency on the part of my 278 right hon. and hon. Friends, it was discovered that for some three months the Herring Industry Board had been making a mistake about its funds for fishmeal and oil in Shetland. The herring fleet had to lie up for weeks in consequence and it is typical of the Board's attitude that this event, which caused great hardship to the herring fishermen who were unable to work and not able to obtain unemployment benefit, was described as an unfortunately mistaken interpretation of the scheme. There was no admission of regret, or even apology.

That is not now the point, although some such admission would have done the Board credit and gained it much support. The point now is the situation which I disclosed in the House on 22nd July last year, in which, while in 1946 there were 622 Scottish boats mainly concerned with herring fishing and in 1963 there were 189, in 1946 the Herring Industry Board employed 34 non-industrial staff and, in 1963, 68 non-industrial staff and about 114 industrial staff. Even excepting the industrial staff, this represents a rise of double the number of staff to deal with 433 fewer boats. I asked the Scottish office what the reason for that was, and the reply I had was that an inquiry was being made and its result would probably be made public in the Report of the Herring Industry Board. There is no such publication that I have been able to discover in the Board's Report or anywhere else. It is this kind of practice in administration which makes it so difficult to encourage efficiency, enthusiasm or even honesty in any industry.

So far the regulations in force prescribe the minimum meshes for trawls and seine nets, and the minimum sizes of the prin- 286 cipal species of white fish which may be caught and landed. It would take a long time to deal with all the other aspects of the Department's fisheries research, but I should like to say a few words about fish farming, to which the hon. Gentleman referred. This is an idea which has caught the imagination of a great many people. Fish cultivation has long been practised in certain countries. There are well-established trout farms, in the United Kingdom mainly for stocking, and, of course, oysters and other sendentary species are cultivated where sea conditions are favourable, but for most of the sea fish which we eat we are still at the beginning.
