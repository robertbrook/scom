Licensing (Amendment) (Scotland) Bill
Submission by Crew 2000

Over the past ten years there has been an escalation of teenage drug misuse which is unparalleled since the 1960's. This has been fuelled by a rebirth of interest in the so-called "psychedelic" drugs of the 1960's; Cannabis, Ecstasy, Eve, LSD as well as Amphetamine Sulphate.

The use of psychedelics and stimulants by young people in Scotland, as in England, is largely associated with other cultural events and activities; going to "raves", club events, attending football matches etc. The users are predominantly under twenty-five, of no particular class background, usually in employment. Very few are involved in crime (other than the crimes they commit in purchasing and consuming controlled substances) and most do not use drugs on a daily basis. This group of young people have separate needs to those traditionally seen by specialist drug services. Indeed this group would not generally define itself as problematic, would rarely attend such services and are generally contemptious of opiate users. Crew 2000 was established in Edinburgh in 1992 in response to this developing situation as a loose coalition of local young people active in the club scene, supported, but not led by drug workers. The coalition is committed to a peer education and harm reduction approach to young peoples drug misuse and works predominantly in the club and rave scene in Scotland. In a Crew 2000 survey of young people in Edinburgh in 1994, 67 per cent. said they used drugs in the last six months with only 10 per cent. saying they had experienced problems related to their drug use. The data showed high levels of recreational drug use and experimentation. However, there were also very low levels of knowledge about the drugs being used with 37 per cent. answering correctly to questions about Temazepam and only 2 per cent. answering correctly on Ecstasy.

Widespread use of recreational drugs such as Ecstasy has caused concern principally because of an increasing number of cases of serious toxicity and even death when the drug has been used in a "rave" or club environment. However, as Dr. Russell Newcombe states "MDMA related deaths are often the result of heat exhaustion, which requires such additional contributory factors as hot environment, a lack of water intake, vigorous exercise for long periods, and probably biological predisposers as well"

1

This evidence is backed up by Solowij in her review of the MDMA literature (1992, p412) who concludes that vigorous dancing by ecstasy users at rave parties and nightclubs is a key factor in ecstasy-related deaths: "It appears that a combination of sustained physical 2 exertion, high ambient temperatures and inadequate fluid replacement compound a direct pharmacological effects of MDMA on thermo-regulatory mechanisms, leading to fulminant hyperthermia."

2
In the majority of cases of Ecstasy-related deaths, the deaths were caused by the way in which the drug was used and not specifically from the drug itself. This would suggest that if aspects of the environment where Ecstasy is used were controlled then risks would be reduced.

Over the last few years it has been demonstrated that the demand for raving cannot be reduced by repressive action from the authorities. Therefore policy and strategy should be aimed at reducing the harmful effects of the use of dance drugs at clubs and raves. At this point it is worth noting that at events where adequate safety measures have existed there have been no fatalities and a reduction of casualties. This can be illustrated through the experience of the Hardcore rave event Rezerection which is organised at Ingleston outside Edinburgh every three months. The event attracts 5000 people and the promoters Kitchenware rely on agency co-operation to ensure the safety of their events. The Red Cross, Scottish Ambulance Service, Police, Environmental Health and ourselves Crew 2000 work together to create a safe environment based on a harm reduction approach. The conditions attached to holding such large events are stringently regulated and enforced by the local licensing board. Obviously the same conditions do not presently apply to smaller clubs and while a lot of clubs owners are responsible and are concerned about the safety of their customers, many put profit first and go unregulated.

The "Guidelines of Good Practice at Dance Events" compiled by the Scottish Drugs Forum offers a code of practice for clubs which will help to provide safer venues. The guidelines offer practical harm reduction advice which will help to reduce the risks associated with using dance drugs in a club environment.

Over the last two years Crew 2000 have worked in clubs around Scotland distributing information on reducing the risks associated with drug use. This has developed to providing an outreach service on-site in clubs providing advice, basic first aid, work with people suffering from drug-induced paranoia. Many young people take responsibility for their drug use in educating themselves about the dangers of the drugs they are using. Education on its own is insufficient if the venues used by drug users are unsafe and club owners are unaware of how to improve the safety of their venue.

It is our experience that drug use is a part of life for many young people and efforts to totally eradicate drug use from clubs have failed. The extension of powers to local licensing board will enable them to impose guidelines on clubs to provide a safer environment. This is a crucial step in attempting to reduce the risk of fatalities through drug use. Additionally through the development of a Safer Dancing programme, drug agencies providing outreach services at clubs will have an ideal opportunity to work directly with young drug users to inform and educate them on the risks attached to using drugs.
