Now we are reaching the fourth stage. What we are concerned with, Sir Stephen, as you will point out to me if I go too far from the main point, is a Local Government Bill. We are concerned with nothing but whether, bearing in mind those three previous stages, somebody of 18 should be entitled to stand as a councillor in local government.

I do not necessarily share the belief of the hon. Member for Shipley that there will be a sudden, dramatic drop in the age limit of our councils. It may not make all that much difference. It may be that here or there one will find a bright 21, 20 or 19-year-old who will, in fact, become a councillor if that Amendment succeeds. It will then be up to the electorate to decide whether they think the candidate too young or should be rejected on other grounds. That is all it does. It tidies things up.

Quite what rules there are in the Sixth—or whatever Republic we have now reached—I am afraid I do not know. Certainly it was true that in the past there was an age differentiation; and I have no doubt that this is true in many other countries in the world.

What we are concerned with, however, is local government. We have all of us agreed that we want local government to be allied with local democracy, to come nearer to the people in doing so. We are confessing, frankly, that local government has failed the people a little over the years, perhaps because of the change in events, but we certainly want to make a new start if we can now.

If we succeed and if the Amendment, which I heartily support, succeeds, we shall have done two things. First we shall have approached much nearer to the ordinary electors. For that reason I should like to give my support from this side of the Committee to the Amendment.
