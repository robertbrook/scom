Mr. Howard
: May I take the opportunity, before answering the specific point raised by my hon. Friend the Member for Bournemouth, West (Mr. Butterfill), to say a word about the concepts underlying chapter XII.

The chapter applies a different system of competition scrutiny from that in the existing legislation. Perhaps the most important point is that it replaces the judgment of the restrictive practices court by decisions of the Secretary of State. The Secretary of State or agency may recognise, or give leave to recognise, an SRO, an investment exchange or a clearing house, and he may JPEG 785 delegate powers to a designated agency if all the relevant criteria for recognition or delegation are fulfilled and it appears to the Secretary of State after advice from the director general that the rules of those bodies do not have the effect, or are not intended or likely to have the effect, of significantly restricting, distorting or preventing competition.

If the rules do appear to have such an effect, the Secretary of State must refuse to recognise, or give leave for the recognition of, the relevant body unless satisfied that the investor protection requirement is met. The same applies mutatis mutandis to delegation to a designated agency. This special regime has been introduced because the normal competition provisions do not provide a suitable basis for reaching decisions on the sensitive balance between competition and investor protection. Such decisions should not be taken by a specialist body such as the restrictive practices court. So the Bill reserves all decisions on the balance to the Secretary of State.

I believe it to be the intention of my hon. Friend the Member for Bournemouth, West to require the Secretary of State to consult both the Securities and Investments Board and the governor of the Bank of England as well as the director general of fair trading before deciding whether a self-regulating organisation, investment exchange or clearing house should be recognised, or indeed, before he makes a delegation order. The status of the director general in chapter XII, which deals with the prevention of restrictive practices, is clear: his function is to consider the relevant rules and regulations, guidance or arrangements, and advise the Secretary of State on the competition implications. Clause 105 gives the director general certain powers to enable him to carry out this function. Clause 104 makes explicit provision for the Secretary of State's consideration of the director general's report.

The Secretary of State is required to have regard to that report, but he is not bound by it. It is for him to consider the effects on investors. When the Secretary of State is considering the director general's report, he is free to consult whoever he wishes about the contents of that report. I am sure that he will want to consult. He could consult the board, the governor of the Bank of England or any other body or person concerned.

I can understand the desire of my hon. Friend to see that the Secretary of State's decisions under the legislation should be as fully informed as possible, but the existing provisions allow for that and the amendment is not necessary for that purpose.

I want to add one note of caution on the singling-out in the amendment of the chairman of the board and the governor of the Bank of England. It is extremely important that, in considering the balance between competition and investor protection on the advice of the director general of fair trading, the Secretary of State should not be regarded as having a particular duty to give effect to the interests of the City in terms of what has sometimes been described as the cosy club when he has wider considerations to take into account and a wider duty to fulfil. By singling out and mentioning specifically only the board and the governor there would be a danger that that impression might be created. I am anxious that that impression should not be created.

786 The role of the Secretary of State in the matter is as the guardian of competition and of the consumer. Of course, that has to be balanced against the need to protect investors, but the Secretary of State should consult widely—as I am sure any Secretary of State would in the circumstances—before taking any decisions on the advice of the director general. Therefore, I am anxious not to give the impression that his consul tations should be channelled in one direction.

The power in the clause is more than sufficient, as it allows the Secretary of State to consult whoever he wishes, so I hope that my hon. Friend will withdraw the amendment.
