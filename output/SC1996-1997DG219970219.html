Draft European Communities (Definition of Treaties) (Euro-Mediterranean Agreement establishing an Association between the European Communities and their Member States and the State of Israel) Order 1997

4.30 pm

The Minister of State, Foreign and Commonwealth Office (Mr. Jeremy Hanley)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft European Communities (Definition of Treaties) (Euro-Mediterranean Agreement establishing an Association between the European Communities and their Member States and the State of Israel) Order 1997.

The association agreement with Israel is one of a series of agreements that the European Union is negotiating with mediterranean states as part of the Barcelona process. That was launched in 1995 to promote co-operation between the European Union and the mediterranean region. Euro-med agreements are an important mechanism for strengthening economic links with the region. Their trade liberalisation provisions and the long-term goal of a Euro-med free trade area by the year 2010 will enhance the economic prosperity of the region. By doing so, it will also improve the conditions for political stability.

The agreements are also clearly in the United Kingdom's interests. They allow for the dismantlement of tariffs and the opening up of the economies of the region. Our trade with Israel is significant and has expanded rapidly in recent years. Last year, bilateral trade topped £2 billion, making Israel one of our top 25 export markets. The agreement allows United Kingdom companies greater access to Israeli markets. We are also a strong supporter of inward investment into mediterranean states. We shall host a major conference next month to promote investment into the region as part of the Barcelona process.

Negotiations on the other Euro-med agreements are at different stages. The United Kingdom has ratified the Tunisia agreement, the Morocco agreement should come before the House this summer and we hope that agreements with Egypt, Jordan, Lebanon and Algeria can be concluded soon. The signature of the interim agreement with the Palestine Liberation Organisation on behalf of the Palestinian authority is due at the General Affairs Council on 24 February. We welcome the progress on all those agreements. In political terms, the Palestinian agreement is particularly important in complementing the European Union-Israel agreement.

The EU-Israel agreement covers mainly trade and investment issues. It will create opportunities for further development of our already thriving trading relationship 4 with Israel. It also provides for enhanced political dialogue and co-operation in economic, social and financial areas. The agreement represents an important factor in our close relationship with Israel—a relationship that goes far beyond the sphere of trade. The state visit of President Weizman to the United Kingdom next week—the first by a President of Israel—symbolises the closeness of those links.

There are also strong political arguments for the agreement. Support for the middle east peace process is the cornerstone of United Kingdom and European Union policy in the region. Although the Barcelona process is separate from the middle east peace process, the two cannot be seen in isolation. Liberalisation of trade will contribute to prosperity and thus to regional stability. The progress that has been made towards peace between Israel and its Arab neighbours in turn has allowed them to enjoy better relations with Europe. Our consideration of the EU-Israel association agreement comes at a significant time. The Government of Mr. Netanyahu signed up to the Oslo process in last month's agreement on Hebron. That agreement reaffirmed both sides' commitment to the Israeli-Palestinian interim agreement of 1995. We hope to see momentum maintained by tiny, further redeployment of Israeli forces as envisaged in that agreement, and progress in negotiations on other outstanding issues. The Palestinian side must continue to do all that it can to suppress terrorism and help to ensure Israel's security from attack.

The EU's agreement with Israel and the PLO will help to increase prosperity in the region. As I have said, prosperity makes an important contribution to peace. For that reason, we are concerned by the restrictions that remain on the Palestinian economy. Although we recognise Israel's right to impose measures that are necessary for its security, we believe that the damage done to the economy of the west bank and the Gaza strip since 1993 by restrictions on the movement of people and goods is a threat to the peace process.

The Palestinian economy must be allowed to realise its potential if popular support for the peace process is to be maintained. We have urged the Israeli Government to make rapid progress in a number of areas—in easing restrictions on the passage of goods across the Jordanian and Egyptian borders, in making arrangements for free passage between Palestinian areas, in allowing the opening of Gaza airport and the construction of Gaza port, and in tackling a range of other outstanding economic issues. We welcome the easing of those restrictions over recent months and the recent encouraging agreement on the payment of outstanding debts between Israel and the Palestinian authority, but there is still a long way to go.

Nowhere do restrictions bite more severely than in east Jerusalem. We have been concerned to see what appear to be attempts by the Israeli authorities to change the demographic balance of the city, not only by the closures, but by the construction and expansion of settlements around east Jerusalem, the withdrawal of Palestinians Jerusalem residency permits, and other discriminatory administrative policies, such as the constraints placed on building licences for Palestinians. Apart from humanitarian concerns, pre-empting the JPEG 5 final status negotiations in that way cannot but damage the peace process.

By entering into the agreement with Israel, we intensify its political dialogue with the EU and the scope for discussing issues such as these in a practical manner. The agreement will be a positive step in bringing Israel closer to our thinking on a range of subjects and encouraging the Israeli Government to follow the policies which we believe are essential to the peace process.

Mr. Hanley
: In any speech of this kind on a subject of this nature, naturally I would have come to the issue of human rights before I had finished. Without a doubt, the subject of human rights, on both sides of the Israeli and Palestinian discussions, is vital to confidence in the peace process as a whole. Before I respond to the point—and I shall answer the question about article 2 and monitoring in due course—I should explain that the European Union, with strong United Kingdom support, is pressing ahead in discussions to establish a parallel agreement with the Palestinians. Both agreements show the commitment of European states to a constructive and long-term engagement in the region.

Human rights and democratic principles constitute an essential part in all association agreements. Human rights provisions in association agreements require partner countries to respect human rights and democratic principles. They commit partner countries to meet established standards. We believe that the EU-Israel agreement will provide further scope for raising human rights concerns with Israel, providing as it does for enhanced political dialogue.

The hon. Member for Great Grimsby (Mr. Mitchell) may say that we are losing a bargaining counter by accepting the agreement, but in return we are gaining a 6 mechanism in the agreement that could be used as a further item of pressure which, if terms of the agreement were broken, would give rise to a breach of the agreement. If those terms were serious enough, the agreement could be withdrawn. I regard that as an equal bargain.

The European Union continually monitors human rights in Israel. As we speak, the heads of mission are monitoring human rights in other countries. It is vital to ensure that monitoring is as effective as possible. The human rights provisions of the association agreement reinforce that requirement. I shall raise the issue of official monitoring with those who will examine monitoring and decide how a mechanism can be established.

We will continue to do all that we can to support the peace process. The EU-Israel agreement and other Euro-med agreements can help to underpin that process. They grow partially out of a recognition of the steps that have been taken towards peace and prosperity. UK ratification of the agreement will be timely—the peace process is recovering momentum after a period of stagnation and we are on the eve of a state visit by the President of Israel. The agreement will be a welcome boost to the UK's important trading relationship with Israel.

If I catch your eye later, Mr. Lord, I shall be happy to answer specific questions. I said that I would mention article 2 and other human rights issues. However, it might be best to hear contributions from members of the Committee before doing so.

Mr. Derek Fatchett
(Leeds, Central)
: The Opposition will support the statutory instrument and the agreement that has been reached between the European Union and Israel. The Minister made several arguments with which we would agree, but those arguments lead to points and implications that members of the Committee will want to explore in greater detail.

The Minister's basic proposition, which virtually everyone in the Committee will accept, is that trade arrangements offer the ability not just to enhance economic prosperity, but to bring political stability to the region. He is correct to say that the Israel agreement is not unique; other agreements form part of the broader perspective of maintaining or increasing economic prosperity and improving political stability. We share those objectives and recognise the difficulties involved in achieving them. We acknowledge the opening of trade as a means of improving economic prosperity and we understand the difficulties for some of the countries mentioned in achieving the political stability that the Minister and every member of the Committee wants.

The trade arrangement before us raises other questions. The Minister and I, who have been involved in earlier debates on trade arrangements, should be delighted that the instrument raises broader implications because, for almost the first time, we are not alone in the debate. On previous occasions, it has almost been just the two of us. Today, other hon. Members are present who have a clear interest in the subject. We have got rid of our lonely vigil on trade arrangements and we are delighted about that.

JPEG 7 The broader implications are the source of this afternoon's debate. The Minister is right that, in many respects, this trade arrangement is not the same as the others because it feeds into the middle east peace process. Although the two processes are not wholly related, there is a clear connection between them. That is why there is so much interest this afternoon.

The Minister made several points, which I should like to tease out a stage further. First, he talked about economic prosperity creating the conditions for peace and progress in the middle east. That is correct. It is why it is crucial that developments in the economic relationship occur along the Palestinian track as well as with the Israelis.

If the peace process is to work, we must be able to prove to the average Palestinian, as well as to the average Israeli, that there are gains to be made from the process. For the average Israeli, such gains might be made through economic prosperity and through security. We are right to stress that and must continue to do so. For the average Palestinian, the gains are made through the achievement of a set of personal and national aspirations and through being able to prove that the peace process brings an important economic dividend to Palestine—to the west bank and Gaza.

The economic figures since Oslo make depressing reading. The argument that peace brings prosperity does not apply with any great merit if we examine the experience of people who live in the west bank and Gaza. According to the best available estimates for the west bank and Gaza—they are likely not to be as good as the unemployment figures that we might argue about here—unemployment is between 35 and 50 per cent. Roughly speaking, one person in three or two is unemployed. I suspect that those figures are ambitious, especially in relation to Gaza. According to recent figures, average domestic wage levels in the west bank and Gaza fell by 20 per cent. in real terms in 1995. From 1992 to 1995, the real gross national product of the Palestinian economy fell by 23 per cent. and it was expected to fall by 6 per cent. last year.

Those figures show that there has been no immediate economic dividend from the peace process. I am sure that all Committee members will want to discuss trading difficulties for those who live in the west bank towns and Gaza as well as the problems that they have in taking up employment in Israel. Such issues are crucial to the economic well-being of the people who live in those areas. Although we recognise that there are security considerations, there must be a balance. One of the messages that the Committee would want to give to Israel is that while the security balance is struck, so the prosperity balance has to be struck in the other direction, too—those who live in the west bank and Gaza must have the prospect of having an opportunity to share in the dividends of the peace process.

The Minister pointed out that the process has been made easier since the recent agreement on Hebron. That is clearly the case and it has changed beneficially the atmosphere, but the process must continue. The crucial point about the deal was that it recognised the nature of the middle east peace process. I am sure that certain 8 hon. Members think that a better deal could have been struck, but its crucial point was that it maintained the process and progress.

The Israeli Government signed up to that process with the support of three-quarters of the Israeli people and a large majority in the Knesset. That is important progress, but we now need—and the trade arrangement is our way of saying that this is even more important—to continue the peace process and to ensure that the next stages of withdrawal from the west bank and the final phase negotiations begin, so that we reach a real, lasting and just peace.

Achieving that will be a difficult task, and no one here will fail to recognise the complexity of the issues—but the Minister was absolutely right to say that no additional hurdles should be placed in the way of that process. I warmed to his words when he warned of the risk of opening more settlements on the west bank and in east Jerusalem. Opposition Members share that opinion. Settlements would be provocative and are unnecessary in this context. They would make it much more difficult to reach the agreement that is crucially important for all sides in Israel and in the west bank and Gaza.

Reference has already been made to human rights. That is an important issue and, as my hon. Friend the Member for Great Grimsby said, members of the Committee have been lobbied on that issue. There is no point repeating the comments that my hon. Friends have already made. Human rights concerns do exist and the Minister rightly acknowledged that those concerns do not relate only to Israeli actions because human rights violations have been carried out by parties on both sides.

I have been concerned for some time about how we monitor the human rights provisions of the trade agreements. When we make agreements with countries whose human rights records do not match up to those of European Union countries, it is crucial that we take a firm line, not only in words but in monitoring the process. I applaud the fact that the Government have tried to take a lead in Europe on that issue. It is crucial that we continue to take a lead because the trade arrangements will be used for purposes for which we do not wish them to be used if there is no strict control of the human rights records of the countries with which the European Union makes trade arrangements.

I hope that when the Minister replies to the debate, he will speak about the importance of the human rights monitoring process and the need for the standardisation of such agreements.

Mr. Menzies Campbell
(Fife, North-East)
: I declare an interest: I am the chairman of the Liberal Democrat middle east council, from which I receive certain financial assistance towards research. I have made arrangements for that to be entered in the next published Register of Members' Interests.

Since 1991 the European Union has made new or expanded co-operation agreements, such as that which we are discussing, conditional on an undertaking by all parties to base their relations and their internal and external policies on respect for human rights and democratic principles. It would be surprising if the European Union had adopted any other policy. Those are important conditions and they must be universally applied and respected, not just by Israel but by Egypt and the Palestine Liberation Organisation, which the Minister described in his opening remarks as likely to be the subject of a similar agreement in the future.

Article 2 of the agreement with which we are concerned stipulates that

"respect for human rights and democratic principles constitutes an essential element"

of the agreement. The words "essential element" have a particular resonance in the present context. A breach of an essential element of an agreement is a material breach of the agreement itself. The Minister responded to an intervention by saying that there might be circumstances in which the agreement would be withdrawn, following such a breach. Could its withdrawal be achieved in the European Union by majority voting, or would it require unanimity? That is perhaps a technical point, but it might be of value to the Committee to know.

Without a clear signal that the conditions to which I have referred are more than a token gesture, there is little to suggest that the agreement would add JPEG 11 substantially to the continuing efforts of the European Union to promote human rights in the region. Failure by us to make clear the intention to enforce human rights conditions in the present case would seriously devalue this and all other agreements made by the European Union with other countries. That is why I support the view already expressed that the extent to which the agreement's conditions are implemented should be actively monitored. It is necessary to establish a clear intention to throw political weight behind the conditions. A monitoring mechanism, with a clearly defined mandate, seems to be the only effective way to ensure that the agreement is fully implemented.

All members of the Committee are, I am sure, keen for relationships in the middle east to improve. The recent agreement in relation to Hebron is clearly welcome and the European Union must do all in its power to help to nurture and assist the fragile peace. I should like to acknowledge the most recent release of prisoners by the Israeli Government. The release of female prisoners was a courageous and constructive gesture, which should be acknowledged by those of us who will argue in this debate that Israel has an obligation to implement the human rights conditions of the agreement.

As recently as December, the European Council declared its

"support for the fundamental principles of a just and lasting settlement in the Middle East, notably land for peace and self-determination for the Palestinians, with all that this implies."

By making the will of Parliament clear on the issue, on this occasion, we shall not be imposing any new measures or additional burdens on the Israeli Government; we shall simply be attempting to underpin an existing agreement, and international obligations that have already been freely entered into.

All parties who were present at the 1995 Barcelona conference to which the Minister drew our attention undertook to respect human rights, democratic principles and the promotion of equitable regional economic co-operation and development based, inter alia, on the removal of barriers to the free circulation of goods. Yet the Israeli Government and judiciary have recently formally sanctioned higher levels of what is described as "physical pressure" against Palestinian detainees. Amnesty International has voiced serious concern over what it calls that "judicial authorisation of torture".

The use of force against the civilian population of southern Lebanon in order to apply pressure on Hezbollah amounts to a violation of protocol 1 of 1977, which is additional to the Geneva convention. The protocol is against

"acts or threats of violence, the primary purpose of which is to spread terror among the civilian population."

The confiscation of land and the expansion of illegal settlements are in violation of articles 42, 43 and 53 of the fourth Geneva convention. The Government and the European Union reaffirmed the illegality of those settlements most recently in the Luxembourg and Dublin declarations. Indeed, in the past month in an exchange on the Floor of the House during Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs Question Time, the Minister 12 confirmed to me that it is still the view of Her Majesty's Government that Israeli actions on settlements are illegal in terms of international law.

The Minister referred to closures. They violate the economic rights of those living in the occupied territories and jeopardise the establishment of a stable Palestinian economic base. Only if that base is achieved will political stability follow. The hon. Member for Leeds, Central (Mr. Fatchett) also came to that conclusion in his remarks. In a sense, our self-interest is at stake. Closures frustrate the considerable investment by this country, bilaterally and with the European Union, that was made to assist Palestinian economic development. The actions that I have described are unacceptable. The terms of the agreement are such that if those actions are persisted in, the agreement could, in the words of the Minister, be withdrawn.

Progress towards peace can be maintained only with the confidence and support of the Palestinian people and of the people of Israel. By emphasising Israel's obligations under the agreement, we not only lend weight and credibility to this and other agreements but assist the delicate process of peace in the middle east. That is the contribution that the Committee can make to the critical task of confidence building. The agreement should be ratified, but it should be ratified only with the clear signals attached to it that I have drawn to the Committee's attention.

Sir Mark Lennox-Boyd
(Morecambe and Lunesdale)
: I am most grateful to you, Mr. Lord, for calling me. It demonstrates that my contribution will be in order even though I am not a member of the Committee. I was in some confusion about that. Because I came to the Committee thinking that I would not be able to speak, I have not prepared extensively so I will—as everyone will be happy to learn—inevitably be rather brief.

I am happy to follow the hon. and learned Member for Fife, North-East (Mr. Campbell). I agree with everything that he said and with all the other reservations that have been expressed. I support the order for exactly the reasons given, but I have some reservations, to which I shall come.

It is important to approve the order because, as my right hon. Friend the Minister said, it will strengthen the trade relationship between the European Union and Israel and will afford opportunities for even more developed monitoring of the agreement. Breaches of the interim agreement have taken place, and we are likely to face breaches of this agreement. If a proper monitoring mechanism is developed on top of the existing one—I hope that my right hon. Friend will deal with this point later—it will afford an opportunity for the Council of Ministers to take action on trade matters by qualified majority voting. That is the best form of diplomatic pressure that can be brought to bear if the European Union thinks that action is appropriate. Such pressure may be achievable by the European Union because of qualified majority voting.

There are two substantive matters in the agreement that affect the points about which I am concerned—the human rights clause and the trade clauses. Israel, of all JPEG 13 countries, has the most legitimate concerns about its own security. I hope that those who know Israel better than I do, and know the Palestinians less well than I do—I have known them for 30 years—will accept that when I say that as a friend of the Palestinians, I really mean it. I abhor all acts of violence and I earnestly support the Oslo accords in the hope that the peace that has so far eluded the middle east can be achieved.

The balance that other hon. Members have mentioned must be struck because—apart from reasons of natural justice or good conduct—if the measures taken to maintain security are so strong that they seriously inhibit Palestinian economic activity, the very security that the Israelis seek will be dramatically and horrifyingly undermined. I believe that that has happened in recent years. The hon. Member for Leeds, Central (Mr. Fatchett) spoke of the appalling decline in the Palestinian economy in the past year. When I went to Jerusalem, Jericho and the west bank last year, for the first time in many years, I found that those places were definitely poorer than when I used to visit them 30 years ago.

The febrile, fragile atmosphere of support for the agreement is not very sound. The Palestinian leadership is always mindful that it may be attacked by its supporters for being too accommodating of Israel's demands in negotiations. It is under pressure, which is why it is extremely important that the economy of the Palestinians should be built up and not put at risk in any way. It is certainly true that it has been undermined in the past year. We must build it up because the European Union is committing substantial aid support for precisely that purpose. It would be ironic if the EU were to commit aid support but not monitor the agreement, with which it is able to exercise clout to ensure that the economy is not undermined by oppressive retaliation or action by the Israelis.

The movement of Palestinians, from Gaza to Jerusalem and to the west bank, and from the west bank to Jerusalem is fortress-like. That has the most serious implications for trade, and is a principal reason for the Palestinian economy suffering so badly in the past year.

It has been alleged that the restriction has been imposed to teach a collective lesson: I assert that that is true. At whatever level the decision to give that message to the Palestinian people was taken, it is an element of collective punishment. All the restrictions suffered by those people since March 1996 have seriously undermined their economy.
