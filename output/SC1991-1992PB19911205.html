Clause 21 [ Annual statements of accounts]:

Viscount Mountgarret
moved Amendment No. 19A:

Page 19, line 21, leave out ("£10,000") and insert ("£25,000").

The noble Viscount said: In moving this amendment I shall speak also to Amendments Nos. 42A and 48A.

These amendments relate to the level of income of a charity before it should comply to the provisions in the clauses referred to in these amendments. Amendment No. 19A refers to the requirement of a charity to produce accounts in a certain form as prescribed by the Secretary of State. Amendment No. 42A relates to the annual reports which are required in a certain form and Amendment No. 48A relates to annual returns.

While the amendments affect all charities, I am speaking principally on behalf of the almshouse charities. I declare an interest in this matter as I run a small family charity at home which I am extremely worried about. If any of the amendments in my name were to be accepted, that would be of the greatest benefit to my charity. However, I should not like the Committee to feel that I am being parochial about this matter because I believe that it is an important point of principle for all small charities.

It is clear that the Government recognise that if a charity's income falls below a certain limit - and they have the figure of £10,000 - there is an added burden and cost, possibly an unnecessary burden and cost upon those charities. I am glad that the Government realise that.

However, I believe that the figure set is too low. As regards almshouses, I believe there are 2,200 almshouse charities in the country of which 1,600 have an income of £10,000 per year or less. If the average number of almshouses in a group is six, seven or eight, which is about average, a figure of £10,000 per year is not a great deal of money to have available for all the necessary on-going repairs, painting, decorating, gardening and the provision of all sorts of things which the inhabitants expect and which one wishes to give them. I hope that that will be borne in mind when my noble friend replies.

The other amendments will increase the burden as regards the amount of work carried out by the trustees concerned. I fully applaud the requirement that when trustees take on a job they should have their eyes fully opened to the responsibilities and the necessary and important work that is done.

The trustees of small charities - and I class small charities as those with an income of less than £25,000 92 - are bona fide people who are from the local parish and community and are not well versed in the technicalities of trusteeship and so on. They take on the responsibilities in a spirit of goodwill and do the work in their spare time. The requirement that they should stick their necks on the chopping block and take on that additional administrative paper work is heavy handed. It could well be that people will not wish to take on the responsibilities of trusteeship.

On the other hand, there are occasions when people have no choice as regards trusteeship. There are deeds which state that by virtue of a certain appointment Mr. X, Y or Z is automatically a trustee. Often that is the local vicar and so on. Sometimes the trustees do not have much say in what they are taking on.

Perhaps we can discuss the principle of whether the sum set is rather low and then take matters further at a later stage. I shall listen with care to what my noble friend says.

Lord Swinfen
: It may be for the convenience of the Committee if I speak to Amendment No. 20, standing in my name, which is grouped with my noble friend's amendment.

I agree with everything that my noble friend said. I plumped for the figure of £35,000 because that is the figure at which value added tax accounting comes in. At that figure charities need to be involved in a certain amount of additional accounting. Therefore, it would be convenient to bring in both sides of the picture at the same time.

There has been considerable discussion about the figure with the Charity Finance Directors Group. Various figures have been put forward. It is important that I should point out that, as far as I know, all the directors are in favour of the increase of the sum in the Bill. The figure of £10,000 is impractically low. Some would prefer £25,000, some would prefer £50,000 and some would prefer £100,000. I believe that perhaps the Minister, as my noble friend suggests, could take the matter away and give it further consideration. We may then come forward on Report with a more satisfactory figure.

Lord Richard
: There are two points here. First, are we satisfied that £10,000 is the right figure? The totality of evidence submitted to me and representations made lead me to believe that there is grave anxiety in the charities world that that figure of £10,000 is pitched too low.

If we are satisfied that it is too low, we should ask what is the correct figure—should it be £20,000, £25,000 or £30,00? Like the noble Lord, Lord Swinfen, I chose the figure of £35,000 because of the coincidence of that figure with the imposition of VAT. On the other hand, like the noble Lord, if the Government show some flexibility on the fact that £10,000 is too low I should not go to the stake in demanding the figure of £35,000 as opposed to £25,000. We need a justification of the figure of £10,000 from the Government which, as yet, we have not had.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Earl Ferrers)
: I assure the Committee that we do not wish to JPEG 93 overburden the trustees of small charities. We realise that those trustees will not be able to afford expensive assistance in preparing their accounts, nor do we wish them to incur unnecessary expense. It is important that a charity's accounts contain sufficient information about its expenses to give the reader the details for which he is looking.

The philosophy behind the Bill is that charities should be accountable to the public who have put their money into the public domain. The whole philosophy behind the Bill is that once the money is in the public domain the charities should be accountable. It is not very onerous to produce statements of account to say what you have done. My noble friend Lord Mountgarret said that local people are often trustees and they are not well versed in all these matters. That may well be perfectly true but they are nevertheless taking on positions of responsibility and trust. Therefore, it is right that they should be accountable. I do not believe that that is heavy handed.

We wondered what would be the right figure to put in. I am the first to accept that as regards any figure that is chosen someone will always say that it should be higher or lower. I believe that setting the level at £10,000 shows that we are erring on the generous side. About 70 per cent. of all registered charities are believed to have an annual income of less than £10,000. That means that with the figure in the Bill three out of four charities will be able to prepare a more simplified form of accounts.

If the threshold were to be raised to £25,000, as my noble friend Lord Mountgarret suggests, that would include a further 5 per cent. so that 75 per cent. of charities would be able to produce simpler accounts. Acceptance of the figure of £35,000 suggested by my noble friend Lord Swinfen would mean that about 80 per cent. of charities would be entitled to produce the simpler form of accounts. That is a very substantial number of charities which would be allowed to provide the simpler form of accounts. That is the reason why I believe that £10,000 is the right figure and even a generous one.

One must also remember that many of the smaller charities rely on endowments for their income. An endowed charity with an annual income of £35,000 or one with only £25,000, could be a fairly substantial undertaking with assets worth possibly £500,000. I do not believe that it is proper for a charity of that size to prepare accounts in the form which is suitable for very much smaller organisations. For example, the receipts of payments account would not show something as elementary as to whether a surplus or a deficit had resulted from the year's activities.

For all those reasons I hope that the Committee will agree that at £10,000 we are allowing far simpler accounts to be produced by a very large proportion of charities. To make the figure higher would be unreasonable.

Lord Swinfen

To my mind my noble friend's argument does not 94 change the principle behind this group of amendments. I hope that he will give some further consideration to the matter.

What the noble Earl did not JPEG 95 do—I expect he cannot—is say why exemption for 75 per cent. of the charities is a worse exemption ratio than 70 per cent. Earl Ferrers
: This is almost like a horse trading exercise. The Government have come forward with a figure and my noble friend Lord Peyton then said "I wonder whether the Government will think a little further about the figure and move up a little from £10,000 to a higher figure". The noble Lord, Lord Richard, then said that if £25,000 means that 75 per cent. of the charities are to be exonerated and £35,000 means that 80 per cent. will be exonerated, why is that so much worse than 70 per cent.? The noble Lord has fallen into a trap of his own making. We fixed a figure at £10,000 because 70 per cent. of the charities are to be released from the obligation. We believe that that is a very generous figure.

I return to the principle. There has to be accountability. All those charities at the lower end of the spectrum will be removed from arduous accountability. The Committee is trying to remove from the more arduous accountability requirements those at the higher end of the lower part of the spectrum. That is not reasonable. What is it that the charities are being asked to do? Let us look at Clause 21. It provides:

"The charity trustees of a charity shall…prepare in respect of each financial year … a statement of accounts complying"

with various requirements made by the Secretary of State. Subsection (3) requires that,

"Where a charity's gross income in any financial year does not exceed £10,000, the charity trustees may, in respect of that year, elect to prepare … a receipts and payments account, and a statement of assets and liabilities".

That is not an arduous responsibility.

If the Committee chooses the figure of £35,000, it may be releasing from the obligation to provide a better form of accounts those charities which have substantial capital. We believe that the figure of £10,000 is the right one because by that we are 96 releasing a large proportion of charities from the heavier undertaking. I hope that the Committee will agree that that is a reasonable figure.

"Subsection (2) applies to a financial year of a charity ('the relevant year') if the charity's gross income or total expenditure (or both) in any of the following, namely—

  1. the relevant year,
  2. the financial year of the charity immediately preceding the relevant year (if any), and

"Subsection (2) [the audit subsection] applies to a financial year of a charity if the charity's gross income or total expenditure in any of its preceding financial years (but not earlier than its last three financial years) exceeds £100,000".
