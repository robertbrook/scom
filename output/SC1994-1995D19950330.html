The amendments would delete what could reasonably be termed a catch-all power, which is what the clause gives the Secretary of State. I wonder why the draftsmen felt it is necessary to include that level of power for the Secretary of State. Given that the Atomic Energy Authority is not prepared to call upon the House for any form of ratification of anything it intends to do in future, perhaps it is necessary for the Secretary of State to have a catch-all power. It is incumbent on the Minister to spend a minute or two telling us what power might be used under the clause, and what action the Secretary of State could envisage taking as a result.

The clause gives sweeping powers to the Secretary of State and we have not yet had an answer to the questions that we posed at our previous sitting on the handling and 68 safekeeping of special nuclear materials and on the individuals involved in that process. I hope that this provision was not included in the Bill to cover that, because the Opposition still feel strongly that specific reference must be made in the Bill to those issues. It is not a matter of "may" or of empowering the Secretary of State to deal with nuclear safety and security in relation to the nuclear non-proliferation treaty. It is incumbent on the Secretary of State to come before us with detailed, prescriptive provisions that put a duty on any successor company within the public sector and the private sector to comply with all the regulations that currently apply to those materials within the United Kingdom Atomic Energy Authority. Anything less than that would be unacceptable.

I do not know whether the Minister has had time to reply to our questions—it is only two days since our previous sitting, so I shall not press him too hard today. At some stage, however, he must tell us which provisions of previous Acts apply to companies or other groups involved in the handling, distribution and storage of such materials. For example, how does the Official Secrets Act 1911 apply and how will its provisions affect future employees? That is important, as employees may be handling material relevant to national security. Will the employees of successor companies be positively vetted? Will companies have to apply for list X status? Those questions must be answered. It is unacceptable to have a catch-all clause that gives the Secretary of State all the powers that he needs. We cannot be expected to be able to sleep comfortably at night in the knowledge that the Secretary of State has our best interests at heart. That is simply not good enough.

What exactly are the powers that the Secretary of State seeks under the clause? The Minister should tell us. Moreover, if the Minister can answer our questions about the handling of special nuclear materials—I admit I have not had the opportunity to read the Official Report of Tuesday's sitting—we should like those answers now. If the Minister would prefer to reply in a letter, will he assure me that he will send a copy of it to other members of the Committee who are interested in his response?

UKAEA Government division and Atomic Energy JPEG 69 Authority Technology have agreed arrangements to ensure that all employees of AEA Technology, or of a successor company or companies, who deal with special nuclear material will continue to be vetted in accordance with UK Government standards.

UKAEA is at present required, by direction of the Secretary of State under the Atomic Energy Authority Act 1954, to apply Government standards of personnel security. That obligation applies equally in relation to employees of AEA Technology, and will continue to apply as long as AEA Technology remains part of UKAEA. I urge the Committee, therefore, to reject the amendment.
