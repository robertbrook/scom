Mr. Brazier

Mr. Evans
: I am aware of my hon. Friend's recognition of the role that is performed by family court welfare officers. He has told me that that structure is working well in his area and that his primary concern is the way in which it operates in other parts of the country. Although we shall discuss that substantive issue in due course, mechanisms are already in place that 236 involve talking to children about their wishes and feelings.

If obtaining the welfare report means that additional time is needed before the court can consider the issues under dispute, it already has the power to adjourn the proceedings as suggested in amendment No. 32. When the court is seriously concerned about the welfare of a child to the extent that it believes that it may be appropriate for a care or supervision order to be made, the court may direct the appropriate authority to investigate the child's circumstances under section 37(1) of the Children Act. Such a process is already designated as specified proceedings under section 41 of the Act, and a guardian ad litem may be appointed in those circumstances.

Children can make an application or apply to become a party to the Children Act proceedings. In both cases, they are eligible to apply for legal aid. The court, in considering such an application, must decide whether the child is of sufficient understanding. Such principles were set out in the famous Gillick case. There is concurrent jurisdiction between all tiers of court in Children Act matters. A direction of the president of the family division specifies that in all cases when an application is made by the child, the proceedings should be transferred to the High Court where the services of the official solicitor are available, if that should be necessary.

All those services are already available to ensure that the welfare of the child is protected and that children's views are heard. It is at such cases that the resources of legal aid, the court welfare services and guardians ad litem are rightly targeted. We must remember that the primary responsibility for the upbringing of children rests with parents. Indeed, the court will not make an order under the Children Act unless it is satisfied that to do so will be better for the child than not making an order at all. If parents and children are fully involved in the decision-making process outside the court room, the chances of workable arrangements lasting long term are much better than if the arrangements are imposed on families.

That is why the Government are introducing provisions for the state funding of mediation. Mediators will always remind parents of their parental responsibility and of the need to take into account the views of their children when reaching an agreement on their upbringing. The family will be making decisions together. Research that has been undertaken by a group in Newscastle shows that, as a result of mediation, parents generally feel more able to talk to their children about what is happening in the divorce process. They are more able to listen to their children's concerns and help them cope with the divorce, taking into account what really lies at the heart of such matters. We therefore need to consider what extending the provisions for the separate representation of the child would mean in practice.

I am not clear how far amendment No. 31 seeks to extend the provision for separate representation, because it would amend a clause dealing only with directions in relation to meetings with mediators. In principle, separate representation means that children may need to attend court more often, that they may have to take sides for or against a parent and that they JPEG 237 may be asked to explain that view to a court. That is an onerous burden to place on a child, which is why the current provisions oblige the court, in determining whether a child should be allowed to make an application, to consider whether the child understands the consequences of its actions.

The Bill's purpose is to reduce acrimony and hostility. I am worried that the amendments may inadvertently increase them. We should be careful not to encourage children to believe that they can prevent their parents from parting when the breakdown of the marriage may be irretrievable. In such cases, children may feel guilty; they may believe that it is they who have failed and that the breakdown could somehow be attributed to them. We need also to remember, however much we may wish it not to be the case, that what children want and what may be best for them are not always the same. What of the parent who is subjected to regular physical abuse that is witnessed by the child? The child may love both parents despite the violence and may want them to stay together.

To place children in a position where they may be asked to defend one partner or to accuse the other party may not have been intended. Thus, although I understand the spirit in which the amendments were moved, I believe that they could increase acrimony in proceedings, dilute available expertise and be more distressing for children. I am sure that that was not intended.

Amendment No. 33 would require the court, when considering whether to adjourn proceedings connected with the breakdown of a marriage, to have as a paramount consideration the interests and welfare of any child of the family. Clause 13 is about the power of the court to adjourn. The court can adjourn to allow the parties to comply with a direction to attend a meeting to explore the benefits of mediation and to take advantage of mediation if they wish, or so that disputes between them may be resolved amicably.

When considering whether to so adjourn, the court is required to have regard to the need to protect the interests of any child of the family. That must be right, and it will mean that the court is required to assess whether the additional delay imposed by an adjournment would be detrimental to the interests of any child of the family, or whether the harmful effects that could result from such a delay were outweighed by the benefits that might be obtained by the parents being given the opportunity to give serious consideration to mediation or another method of amicable resolution of their disputes.

I believe that the wording of the Bill is right. We are talking about procedures relating to the divorce of two adults. The effect that the parent's actions will have on their children is important, but it is not appropriate to say that, in relation to decisions made by the court about the dissolution of the marriage, the children's interests override those of the parents in all circumstances.

Mr. Evans
: I have dealt with separate representation; I said that, although the children's interests were important, they should not override the interests of the parents. I ask the Committee to focus on that core issue.

Under clause 13, the court will not make orders relating to the residence of, or contact with, children. When the court makes such orders under the Children Act, the welfare of the children, as the hon. Member for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy (Mr. Llwyd) well knows, is paramount—that provision is contained in section 1 of the Children Act.

Where it can be shown that the legal dissolution would result in substantial financial or other hardship to a child, the court may—as we were reminded during our debate on clause 10—make an order, under that clause, barring divorce. In such cases, therefore, the interests of a child would prevail. I do not think it right, however, that where a marriage has irretrievably broken down an empty legal shell of a marriage should be preserved unless it is necessary to do so to protect one of the parties or the children by involving the hardship bar.

Of course, it is important to minimise the trauma and bitterness for children that marital breakdown will inevitably cause; that is why the general principles clause states that regard must be had to that fact. However, research shows that children suffer at the time of breakdown in their parents' marriage and at separation. What causes suffering for the children may not be the dissolution, but the breakdown and separation. By the time of legal dissolution, the damage has already been done. Preserving a dead marriage in those circumstances, so that the parties can continuing warring, does nothing at all to help children.

There is a serious misunderstanding about how the divorce court operates when exercising its function under section 41 of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973. That has been the subject of much discussion between officials in my Department and representatives from the various children's organisations. In exercising its function under section 41, the court is not making decisions relating to children. Likewise, when the court is dissolving a marriage, it is not making decisions relating to children. When the court makes decisions under the Children Act 1989, it is, of course, making decisions relating to children, and, under that Act, the welfare of the child is of paramount importance.

It may help the Committee if I explain how the section 41 system operates. I would be grateful if Committee members kept in mind that we are talking about a procedure that acts as a bridge between the divorce procedure and procedures under the Children Act. A judge will consider the matters that are specified under section 41(1) of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973, in accordance with the provisions of family proceedings rules. Rule 2.39, made in 1991, provides for the judge to certify that he is satisfied that there are no children of JPEG 239 the family to whom section 41 applies, or that there are such children but that there is no need for the court to exercise its powers under the Children Act or give any direction under section 41(2) of the 1973 Act.

The rule continues:

"Where the district judge is not satisfied as mentioned in paragraph (2) … he may, without prejudice to his powers under the Act of 1989 or section 41(2) of the Act of 1973, give one or more of the following directions—

  1. that the parties, or any of them, shall file further evidence relating to the arrangements for their children (and the direction shall specify the matters to be dealt with in further evidence);
  2. that a welfare report on the children, or any of them, be prepared;
  3. that the parties, or any of them, shall attend before him at the date, time and place specified in the direction; and that the party shall be notified accordingly."

It will be noted that the court has power to require parties to file further evidence and also to order a welfare report or require the parties to attend before the judge. If the circumstances suggest that the court is likely to have to exercise its powers under the Children Act, the matter will be taken further under the Children Act, whereas I have said that decisions made by the court must proceed on the basis that the welfare of the child is paramount. I am aware that there is some considerable misunderstanding about the function of section 41. It is worth repeating that the court is not, under section 41, making decisions relating to children—that is, making or confirming orders in relation to residence or contact. Those decisions are made under the Children Act.

I am aware, from what the children's charities consortium has said to officials in my Department, that there is concern among the children's organisations about the fact that the section 41 procedure does not always alert judges to problem cases. We have asked the organisation for evidence of such cases, and we have also discussed with them ways in which the court might be put in a better position to discover whether a particular case should be looked at further and dealt with under the Children Act.

We have told children's organisations that we will examine the documentation and any other evidence that is placed before the court under section 41 to see whether it might be improved further, so placing the court in an even better position to decide whether or not there is a problem as regards the children.

On the matter of consulting children, hon. Members will be aware that the Government have tabled amendments to clauses 8, 11 and 24 that emphasise the importance of the welfare, wishes and feelings of children.
