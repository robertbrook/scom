Debate resumed on Clause 41.

[ Amendment No. 52A not moved.]

On Question, Whether Clause 41 shall stand part of the Bill?

Lord Peyton of Yeovil
: I wish to make a short point. As I understand it, the purpose of Clause 41 is to assist the trustees of small charities and make their administration both effective and efficient. The powers of trustees come under Sections 22 and 23 of the 1985 Act, subject to certain restrictions. As I understand it, the purpose of this clause is to tidy things up and make the application of the law wider and more uniform.

The trustees will still be required to act by unanimous resolution and with the commissioners' concurrence, and to give reasonable public notice of their intentions. I wonder whether the laudable purpose of the clause is not being defeated by subsection (1) (a), which states,

"This section applies to a charity if … its gross income in its last financial year did not exceed £5,000".

I did not table an amendment because I did not wish to enter into competition with my noble friend Lord Ferrers, which seems rather vulgar—rather like a Dutch auction or some other auction—and I did not want to guess at the figure. However, I suggest that the figure of £5,000 is too small.

Being eminently reasonable—I hope my noble friend concurs with that point—I am asking him on bended knee whether he will be good enough to reconsider the figure. I suggest that the purpose of the clause—to help trustees of charities—would be better served if the low level of £5,000 were to be changed.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Earl Ferrers)
: As usual my noble friend is enormously persuasive. He did not actually produce any reason why the figure should be above £5,000 or whether he thought £10,000, £15,000 or £20,000 would be better.

Obviously when one has a criteria such as this it is always possible to suggest another figure. I shall certainly reconsider the figure to see whether or not it is reasonable. My noble friend will realise that the provision applies to small charities. It could be argued that those with an income of £5,000 or so could be considered not entirely small, but once one goes up to £7,000 and £10,000 one is looking at much bigger figures. My noble friend will be aware also that the figure can be amended by order of the Secretary of State if it is considered to be too low.

Lord Peyton of Yeovil
: That is one of the points to which I attach some importance. When we are being invited to fix a figure it gives us little comfort to be told that someone of greater wisdom may subsequently 182 change it. We have an opportunity to change it now when my noble friend has available to him, without any difficulty at all, the collective wisdom of the Committee.

Earl Ferrers
: It is difficult to satisfy my noble friend. He says the figure is too low, and we say, "Yes, we have included a provision in the Bill to enable it to be increased". Then my noble friend, instead of saying, "I am so glad the Government are not being rigid", says, "No, we should alter it now".

My noble friend will be aware that when the matter was considered originally and the White Paper produced, the figure of £1,000 was suggested. Various representations were made so we upped the figure to £5,000. I shall certainly reconsider it to see whether or not it should be higher, but I cannot give my noble friend much hope.

Clause 41 agreed to.

Clause 42 [ Small charities: power to spend capital]:

Baroness Faithfull
moved Amendment No. 53:

Page 38, line 32, leave out ("for any useful purpose") and insert ("for a suitable and effective application").

The noble Baroness said: Amendment No. 53 seeks to sharpen up the words, "for any useful purpose". It does not much alter the meaning, except that "for any useful purpose" to be achieved could be anything, whereas we want the application to be "suitable and effective" and related to the purposes of the charity.

I shall not divide the Committee but my noble friend may agree that it would be sharper and nearer the truth to use the words "for a suitable and effective application" rather than "for any useful purpose".

Viscount Astor
: The purpose of the clause is to help the trustees of some very small charities which have capital that by law cannot be spent, to put the whole of their charity's assets to better use. The clause will be of assistance where trustees hold an investment, other than land, which must be treated as a permanent capital endowment and which cannot itself be spent. Many older charities have their capital invested in ways which give them a low yield, so that in modern terms the income is small compared with the amount of capital invested to produce it. As the years go by the amount of real help that the income can give the charity's beneficiaries is eroded by inflation since the capital itself rarely grows.

There comes a point where the trustees find themselves asking whether the amount of income they have available to spend each year is really achieving any useful purpose. In other words, is it actually relieving a need or is it merely a gesture? That is what the words, "any useful purpose" mean: is the money able to relieve any need? If it is not, the trustees will not be able to fulfil any of the purposes of the charity, since to give out benefits which, while they may be welcome to the persons receiving them, do not relieve any need would fail to fulfil the charity's purposes—not, of course, through any fault of the trustees.

Where that is the case, the trustees will be able to take advantage of Clause 42, provided that the charity's income is under £1,000 and that it has no JPEG 183 land as part of its permanent capital endowment. They will be able to resolve to spend the capital as though it were income.

I fully understand the point raised by the noble Baroness and I can assure her that the clause already means what she is trying to make it mean. There is the additional element of the trustees' opinion. It is at their discretion that they decide whether any useful purpose is achieved by spending income alone, and they must make that judgment in accordance with the specific policies they adopted within the terms of the trusts, in their efforts to apply the charity's funds as best they can in fulfilment of the charity's purposes.

Baroness Faithfull
: I thank the Minister for his reply. The wording may allow some of the small parochial charities for the poor to put the income to some purpose which does not help the poor. Surely it would be better for the wording to read "effective and suitable".

Perhaps I can cite an example. I know of one small charity which was supposed to be for the relief of the poor. The trustees used the charity income to build a bench in the village on which anyone could sit: the wealthy also sat on it so it was not really for the poor. It was useful, but it was not suitable according to the original terms of the charity.

Lord Brightman
: My understanding of the subsection is that it does not enable the trustees to alter the trusts in any way whatever, except to allow them to use the capital for any purpose for which income is applicable. It is not a provision enabling trusts to be altered, but merely to enable capital to be expended.

Viscount Astor
: The noble and learned Lord, Lord Brightman, is correct. The trustees must always use their assets for the purposes given in the trust deed. The use of the word "purposes" refers to that.

Lord Richard
: I briefly support the noble Baroness on this amendment. I am bound to say that it seemed to me that "for any useful purpose" means just that. The noble Viscount says that it does not, so I would be interested to hear the basis for it. On the face of it the phrase is too wide. The Government should look again to see whether the amendment has been drafted in an overwide fashion. Perhaps it could be tightened up in the way that the noble Baroness suggested.

Viscount Astor
: As I said, the phrase "for any useful purpose" must be within the purpose of the trust. I take the noble Lord's point. I believe that we are all trying to do the same thing. I shall be very happy to look again at the drafting of the clause. However, we believe that it means what we intend, although I certainly take the point made by the noble Baroness.

Baroness Faithfull
: I thank the noble Viscount. He believes that the amendment means what it says, but many of us do not. I also believe that a great many of the public do not agree, either. The noble Viscount has been generous enough to say that he will look again at the amendment. In those circumstances I beg leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 44 [ Person acting as charity trustee while disqualified]:

Viscount Astor
moved Amendment No. 54AA:

Page 41, line 39, at end insert ("(other than an exempt charity)").

The noble Viscount said: The commissioners do not have general jurisdiction over exempt charities and their trustees. The purpose of this amendment is to keep the trustees of exempt charities out of the commissioners' jurisdiction under Clause 44(4). As drafted, that clause will allow the commissioners to oblige a person who had acted while disqualified under Clause 43 as trustees of an exempt charity to repay sums of money to a charity from which he had received any expenses or remuneration in the course of acting as a trustee while disqualified. That would represent an unworkable extension of the commissioners' jurisdiction over exempt charities since the assets and property of exempt charities are not within the commissioners' jurisdiction. The commissioners are not permitted to inquire into the affairs of exempt charities under Section 6 of the 1960 Act.

The withdrawal of the commissioners' confidence would not therefore leave the charity without remedy in the circumstances. I beg to move.

The Lord Bishop of Worcester
moved Amendment No. 54AB:

After Clause 44, insert the following new clause:

("Limitation of liability of trustees

  1. Subject to the provisions of subsection (3), the liability of any charity trustee or trustee for any charity, arising out of any obligation lawfully incurred as trustee, in aggregate with the liabilities of any other such trustees of that charity, shall be limited to the value of all assets of that charity.
  2. Subject to the provisions of subsection (3) hereof any charity trustee or trustee for any charity shall be entitled to be indemnified in respect of any claim against them arising out of any obligation incurred as trustee, out of any assets of the charity.
  3. Subsections (1) and (2) hereof shall only apply where —
    1. the conduct of the trustee was in all the circumstances giving rise to the liability reasonable; and
    2. the trustee had, at the time the liability arose, complied with all statutory requirements as registered charity.").

The right reverend Prelate said: I am very glad to move the amendment in the name of my right reverend friend the Lord Bishop of London. I wish that the amendment had been in my own name because I believe that it is a very important amendment which will have much backing from all sides. The object of it is to encourage people to be trustees and to encourage them to be so without putting their entire assets at risk. Members of the Committee will know that at the JPEG 185 present time the trustees of a charity contract and deal as individuals and they are thereby personally liable without limitation. Where they are acting within the terms of the trust they are entitled in most cases to be indemnified out of the assets of the trust.

However, if the assets of the trust that are available are insufficient the trustees are jointly and severally liable for any shortfall. This present unlimited liability is a real problem. Those who have the skills and resources to be trustees take advice as to whether or not to be trustees of a charity, but they are increasingly unwilling to do so. It would have been highly desirable to have many of those people as trustees of certain charities.

It should be made clear that the purpose of the amendment is not to limit the liability of trustees where there are breaches of trust. The position as regards liability in those cases will remain unchanged by the amendment. I believe that noble Lords can easily imagine the kind of circumstances in which such liability can arise. For example, on proper advice trustees might enter into a building contract, but something goes wrong with the site or in some other way the contract is much more expensive than originally planned. The trustees are liable for any sum that the charity is unable to meet.

It is particularly unrealistic today to expect day-by-day supervision from volunteer trustees who, by virtue of being trustees, often have quite a large number of staff working in the project for which they are responsible. Yet at the moment the trustees are liable for the faults of their staff to the whole extent of their personal assets. I could if I wanted to, but I do not intend to do so, refer to an incident of this kind in my own county which is tragic in its consequences. There are other community projects which involve not only the church. I am glad that I am speaking not only on behalf of the Church but on behalf of many different charities.

The position can be further complicated because a number of the trustees are ex-officio. They may be people of quite slight means. The purpose of the amendment is to remove doubt as to whether or not endowed assets of a charity are available to meet these liabilities.

There is the question of the third party. The amendment says that the third party can only recover to the extent that a charity has assets. That is the position which the public would assume in any event to be the case. Many people would be unaware of the fact that if a charity has assets worth £20,000 and the cost of a building project is £50,000 then the poor trustees have to find £30,000. Is that fair to the third party? First, it is unlikely that any third party currently believes that he can claim anything other than the assets of the charity. Secondly, for the first time the financial position of a charity is publicly available including overdrafts, which in the past were not revealed. Banks are increasingly taking the view that an overdraft should be regarded as a liability of the trust that should be known.

The provisions of the Bill provide that there should be a completely open approach to the assets and the liabilities of a trust. The amendment is intended 186 simply to offer some limitation to the liability of trustees where they have behaved properly and reasonably and met all their statutory requirements as a registered charity. I beg to move.

At page 30 of the White Paper it is recognised that the company structure was not wholly appropriate to charities and suggests that there might be a need for a new corporate structure with limitation of liability. The point raised by the right reverend Prelate does not come as any surprise to the Government. As was pointed out, it would not limit the liability for breaches of trust.

The problem is that of striking the balance between the protection of charity trustees and the protection of members of the public. There are already some signs of people refusing to take on that responsibility. Unless some change were made along the lines of the amendment there is an increasing risk of people refusing to act as trustees.

In the way that it is worded it looks as though it is intended to cover only innocent errors of judgment made within the law.

I shall be interested to hear what my noble friend the Minister has to say, but this is a branch of charity law which has so far been dealt with by the courts and not by statute. However, there is one exception to it being dealt with by statute, and that is to be found in Section 23 of the 1960 Act which gives power to the commissioners. It is a long section and I shall not read it all, but it states:

"Subject to the provisions of this section, where it appears to the Commissioners that any action proposed or contemplated in the administration of a charity is expedient in the interests of the charity, they may by order sanction that action, whether or not it would otherwise be within the powers exercisable by the charity trustees in the administration of the charity; and anything done under the authority of such an order shall be deemed to be properly done in the exercise of those powers".

That limited degree of protection already exists in the 1960 Act. I remember that at the time we dealt with that Act a somewhat similar view was expressed. Of course, there were no right reverend Prelates in another place. I do not believe the view was expressed on behalf of the Church or religious charities. My JPEG 187 recollection is that it was vaguely expressed by a Member of another place who had some experience of charity work.

It is a big subject which needs thinking through, because it has great implications. I merely utter those rather vague words of caution.

Lord Brightman
: It does not seem to me that Section 23(1) of the 1960 Act meets the point. As I understand that subsection, it is directed towards authorising trustees to carry out some administrative action which might or might not be strictly within their powers.

As I read it, the amendment is intended to give protection to trustees somewhat similar to the protection which company directors, for example, would have when they enter into a contract on behalf of the company. Directors would not be liable beyond the assets of the company. As I understand it, the suggestion here is that trustees who are acting fully within their powers should not be personally liable beyond the assets of the charity. With great respect to the noble Lord, I do not believe that Section 23(1) is of great assistance in this context.

Lord Renton
: I did say that it had limited application to the matter which arises here. I agree that at first sight it does not have a wide implication, but it undoubtedly has a limited one.

Lord Swinfen
: I support the amendment. The last major contract of the charity for which I work was for well over £2 million for a building development. If we had had to rely upon trustees who were personally capable of settling that amount if anything went wrong we should have had an extremely short supply of trustees.

Where the trustees have acted lawfully, and something has gone wrong that is outside their control—for instance, settlement occurring during the contract that had not been foreseen or was not foreseeable incurring considerable additional cost—the trustees should not be personally liable. The cost should come from the charity's funds. After all, the contractor can look at the charity's latest accounts and would be able to take up financial references for the charity as a contactor can do for any commercial operator. The amendment is worthy of support.

Lord Richard
: Perhaps I may follow the noble Lord, Lord Swinfen, on one point. One of the historical arguments that one has heard to justify the greater legal liability of the trustee, as opposed to the liability of a company director, has always been that in relation to a charity those dealing with the charity did not have access to its accounts or information about how it was going to be run.

One of the objects of the Bill is to make more information about the charity available to the general public and those who might be dealing with it; and so, in a sense, the justification for maintaining that distinction between the liability of company directors and that of trustees of a charity has been considerably weakened. I see no reason why, in common sense, or, 188 indeed, in equity, a trustee of a charity should be personally liable for, in effect, innocent errors which might result in some loss.

Directors are not so liable, and I do not see why charitable trustees should be once the Bill is passed. I hope that the Minister can be forthcoming on this matter. It is clear from the mood of the Committee—if I may put it that way—that this is an issue which needs looking at carefully and fairly urgently.

Lord Mottistone
: I am the patron of a trust for raising funds for the completion of the cathedral at Portsmouth which was opened by the Queen Mother about a week ago. It required £3 million to be raised. It was a crucial affair. The Bishop, the Provost and the chairman of the trustees, who happened to be a retired rear-admiral—I like to think that he was a courageous chap, being a personal friend—were courageous and went ahead with £1 million still to be raised. We are now at a stage where I think there was only about £200,000 yet to come. They were courageous but they needed some protection, which they did not have, as I see it. This is what I believe the right reverend Prelate is asking for on just those occasions.

I hope that if the Government do not have an immediate answer they will take a sympathetic view of this. It is an important point.

Lord Brightman

Earl Ferrers
: The right reverend Prelate has brought in an amendment which is extremely important and has far-reaching implications. I understand the anxiety which noble Lords have expressed about the situation. My noble friend Lord Renton said that this is a big subject and has interesting implications. He is right. I wonder whether I may put the Committee into the picture as we see it. To the extent to which charity trustees of non-incorporated charities can be exposed to personal liability in contract when acting for or on behalf of a charity is not straightforward. The general position is that trustees act in a personal capacity on behalf of the trust. But provided that they have acted properly, they are entitled to an indemnity out of the trust assets, either under Section 30(2) of the Trustees Act 1925 or under the general law.

Where the indemnity is available, problems may arise where there is not enough income to meet the liability or where the only recourse is to the permanent JPEG 189 endowment of the charity. In other words, one might have to sell the village hall in order to reimburse the trustees.

Problems could also arise if the charity has insufficient assets to meet its liabilities. In those cases trustees may well be personally liable to third parties. The noble Lord, Lord Allen of Abbeydale, said that there should be some form of limited liability. He said that the White Paper stated that the Government would explore this. We did so to see whether it was possible to obtain some form of incorporated body with limited liability. That has not been implemented because when we explored the possibility it proved difficult to fit such a form into the existing insolvency provisions relating to the Companies Act. It is also difficult to protect the rights of third parties. That is the reason why we did not incorporate that suggestion into the Bill.

A universal right of indemnity could strengthen in certain cases the existence of a charity where its only property was its permanent endowment which was, for example, used for the purpose of the charity. An obvious example of that is an almshouse. The trustees concerned could sell the property in order to satisfy their indemnity and they could effectively thereby bring the charity to an end.

Under the present law, trustees could have recourse to the charity's permanent endowment in this way only by order of the commissioners or by order of the court and provided that it was in the interests of the charity.

One also has to consider the rights of contracting parties. My noble friend Lord Mottistone referred to Portsmouth Cathedral. He said it would cost £3 million and they had only £2 million. They were courageous and went ahead and did it. Perhaps they signed the contract for which they would have to spend £3 million even though they had only £2 million. I believe the contracting parties have a right and an interest in that.

The right reverend Prelate's amendment would severely limit the ability of charities to enter into contracts. It seems to me it would adversely affect the position of third parties. Contracting parties simply may not be aware of the extent of the assets of the charity at the time of the contract and when any liability falls to be paid. They could avoid contracting with unincorporated bodies knowing that the trustees' liability will be limited to the assets of the charity but not knowing what the extent of those assets is.

I realise that charities are concerned about the question of the liabilities of trustees when they enter into contracts on behalf of the charity. For this reason the Charity Commissioners have recently considered the question of charities with trustees taking out insurance against liabilities. They have agreed that in appropriate cases it would be proper for insurance policies to be taken out to cover the trustees for personal liability for acts either properly undertaken in the administration of a charity or where they were undertaken in breach of trust by honest mistake. Such policies could in appropriate circumstances be taken out at the cost of the charity.

190 Of course that form of insurance would not cover someone being wilfully negligent, and I do not believe anyone expects it to do so. However, it would protect the trustees from work which they had undertaken for which they were not personally liable. If one could give an example—it is always dangerous to do so—it might be decided to paint the village hall. The trustees know it would cost about £500, but when they start to do it they find it has dry rot and they have to remove the dry rot before they can paint it. The bill instead of being £500 comes to £2,000. That is the kind of situation where the difference would be able to be paid out of the funds of the charity. But if it meant the sale of a property which was the charity's assets or that the trustees would be personally liable for something they could not have foreseen it could be covered by insurance.

I accept that it is a difficult part of the law and many trustees are anxious not to be personally liable. However, if we were to go down the path of the right reverend Prelate it would create quite a lot of difficulties.
