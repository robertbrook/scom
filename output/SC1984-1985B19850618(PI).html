New clause 29
FOREIGN EARNINGS OF SEAMEN AND AIRLINE EMPLOYEES

"Where a seaman or an employee of an airline who is resident or ordinarily resident in the United Kingdom performs duties outside the United Kingdom for at least 30 days of absence, there shall be given a deduction in charging to tax the emoluments for the duties performed abroad equal to one quarter of their amount, whether or not the 30 days are continuous.".—[ Mr. Randall]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Stuart Randall
(Kingston upon Hull, West)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

I am pleased to move the motion because I have great concern, as do my hon. Friends, about the future of the merchant shipping industry and the people who work in it. Since the Government came to power in 1979, the neglect of the industry has been remarkable. The decline has been accentuated beyond all reasonable expectations since the Government came to power.

Particularly as a result of last year's Budget, the industry, which has its back to the wall, is having to carry higher costs arising from taxation policies. The Government reduced the tax benefits of seamen and airline staff, which has put pressure on their employers to compensate for that loss of income.

The industry is in a serious plight. It has declined and there has been a dramatic drop in the number of people employed. In addition, it is having enormous difficulty in coping with foreign competition—foreign flags and other countries, especially in south-east Asia.

Tonnage has dropped by 50 per cent and the number of vessels have fallen by just under 50 per cent since 1979. At the beginning of this period, there were 1,200 United Kingdom-owned and registered merchant ships. These comprised about 36 million tonnes deadweight. However, by the end of 1984 there were fewer than 700 ships, which amounted to 18 million tonnes deadweight.

That shows the seriousness of the decline in the industry, yet the Government fiddle while Rome burns. It is appalling that that has happened.

Amendments have been tabled by hon. Members, and the Government have also attempted to do something to arrest the decline. That was demonstrated in the Minister's comments on the proposals for reducing the write-off period for second-hand ships. Although it would be unreasonable not to welcome that attempt it 588 does not seem to be part of a coherent strategy. On one hand the Government seem to be saying, "Let us encourage owners to take on more merchant vessels by reducing the capital cost," but on the other, through their taxation policies, they are increasing the current revenue cost. So there seems to be no strategy at all; the Government are simply sitting back and watching the decline.

During our interesting debate on reducing the write-off period for the second-hand vessels, the hon. Member for Tatton (Mr. Hamilton) tried to justify the proposal in a strategic sense. He did not define the meaning of "strategic sense", but I presume that it includes the military aspect such as another Falklands war. Those who are far more knowledgeable about the merchant fleet than I am have said that we could not sustain another Falklands war as we did because of the decline that has taken place in our merchant fleet.

At a previous sitting we had a discussion surrounding subsidy. During their period in office of almost six years, the Government have created the impression that "subsidy" is a dirty word. I do not believe that it is. Sensibly placed subsidies can be of immense value. The more one looks at foreign industry and foreign competition, the more one realises that nowadays subsidy is an essential tool in ensuring that our industries can compete fairly. Our merchant fleet cannot do that.

The pattern of taxation on employees in the industry shows that in 1974 a resident of the United Kingdom who worked abroad for 365 days or more could claim tax relief on 100 per cent of his earnings and if he worked fewer than 365 days abroad he could claim tax relief on 25 per cent of those earnings. In 1977, the system changed and tax relief was given on 25 per cent of the earnings if a person spent 30 days or more abroad. Last year, that tax relief was reduced to 12½ per cent of earnings and, as from April 1985, all such benefits disappear altogether.

People in the industry, like all of us, have commitments, such as mortgage repayments. The Government have made a foul swipe at their earnings. Seamen on low earnings have lost £4, £5 or £6 from their pay-packets and the masters of tankers have had as much as £20, £25 or £35 a week docked from their wages. That loss is serious and unfair.

The consequences of those tax changes should be examined in the context of the biggest group of workers—merchant seamen. On the 1984 figures they stood to loose an average of £10 a week each on earnings of about £158 for a 68-hour week. Such losses are not only bad for the workers. They are bad for employers as well. Employees' incomes have been reduced almost at a stroke over a two-year period, yet they have long-term commitments such as mortgages. Who can blame them for putting pressure on their union for wage increases above those generally accepted by the Government?

In 1985, pay increases have averaged 8.2 per cent to 8.6 per cent although the industry has its back to the wall. Such increases are above the rate of inflation, though only just, if the earlier part of the year is taken into account and weighted a little. There is no need to say more about their effect on competitiveness.

What has happened is unfair and has aroused tremendous bitterness in the industry which I cannot stress strongly enough. In the beginning, many people in the JPEG 589 industry thought that anti-avoidance procedures would not affect seamen and airline staff. Both sides of the House support anti-avoidance measures. No one supports the fiddlers of the system. Unfortunately those who work in the industry and who live abroad have to pay tourist prices. They bear the extra costs associated with living abroad but they are not always in this country to use the services here.

New Clause 31
PARLIAMENTARY CONTROL OF EXTRA-STATUTORY CONCESSIONS

"(1) Extra-statutory concessions granted by the boards of inland revenue and of Customs and Excise shall be subject to approval by a motion containing a list of all such concessions that either—

  1. involved the boards of inland revenue and of Customs and Excise in a cost of more than £100,000 in the previous financial year; or
  2. have been in existence for more than five financial years.

(2) Such concessions shall be accompanied by an explanatory memorandum laid before the Commons House of Parliament not later than 1st March of each year.".—[ Mr. Fisher.]

VALUE ADDED TAX: MITIGATION OF PENALTIES

"(1) The Commissioners or, on appeal, a Value Added Tax Tribunal may, if they think fit, mitigate or remit any penalty, interest or surcharge for which a person is liable under sections 12 to 18 above.

  1. In determining the extent of the mitigation (if any) under subsection (1) above, or whether to remit any penalty, interest or surcharge, the Commissioners or Tribunal may have regard to the following matters:
