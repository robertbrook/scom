It is a shame that we are not meeting tomorrow which will be even brighter. We are meeting on the eve of St. David's day and we look forward to intertwining our daffodils with our roses and we welcome the victor from Pontypridd.

The amendment deals with uprating. At present benefits fall into three groups: those which by law have to be uprated, at least in line with prices, those which in practice are uprated more or less in line with prices and those which lose value from year to year because they are not uprated in line with prices. The latter category concerns us here. Even when benefits go up in line with prices as earnings and other incomes tend to rise in real terms, benefit rates fall steadily begind other incomes. Those on benefit fail to gain from the relative increase in prosperity that the rest of the nation is enjoying. If benefits cannot be increased in real terms it is better to have them increased in line with prices but the worst problem is that their real value is eroded by inflation.

The Secretary of State is already required to list in his annual uprating order all the benefits listed in section 63(1) of the Social Security Act 1986. But he does not have to specify which are not being increased or are rising by less than the inflation rate. The amendment requires him to do that and to explain his reasons for not increasing those benefits in line with prices and the consequences of that.

Benefits which are not rising with prices this April include the £1,000 widow's payment. That was discussed at some length in the debate on uprating on 20 December. My hon. Friend the Member for Derby, South (Mrs. Beckett) referred to the Government's illusionist's trick—first we see the benefits, then we do not. The proposal to substitute the lump sum payment for weekly widow's allowance was first announced in 792 June 1985 under the misnomer of a social security reform. Since then, including this autumn's uprating, other benefits have risen by about 22 per cent which means that widows should now receive £1,220 instead of £1,000. Women who were foolish enough to imagine that they stood to gain from this "reform" will now have been disillusioned. The widow's payment is worth almost £600 less than the uprated benefit it replaced—widow's allowance paid weekly for the first six months.

Child benefit will doubtless be covered at great length elsewhere. It has already been debated on the Floor of the House and I do not want to spend too much time on it now. However, it is worth noting the effect that freezing child benefit has on the value of other benefits. Widows are among those who have been short changed. This year a widowed mother receives £15.65 for each of her children, consisting of an £8.40 dependency increase to a widow's benefit, and the £7.25 child benefit. From this April she will receive £16.20—the £8.95 dependency increase and the £7.25 child benefit. That represents an actual increase of a mere 3.5 per cent.

The Government claim that long-term benefits, including widow's benefits, are going up by 5.9 per cent. The same is true of invalidity benefit. The long-term sick with children are getting a smaller increase than those without children. Like widowed mothers, they are not even being compensated for price increases over the past year. The earnings limit is a matter that continues, justifiably, to receive a great deal of attention. Paragraph 3 of the schedule is a rationalisation of that motley collection of provisions, it will empower all the adjudicating authorities to obtain medical and non-medical expert reports on such matters as medical conditions, valuations of stocks and shares, land and property and assessments of business accounts. Similarly, those adjudicating authorities who hold oral hearings or inquiries will be able to arrange for assessors to attend those hearings and inquiries. As now, they would provide advice to the authority but would take no part in the decision-making process.
