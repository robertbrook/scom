Aid System for Flax and Hemp

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Ms Joyce Quin)
: It is a pleasure to be here under your chairmanship, Mrs Michie. I look forward to our debate and welcome it for a number of reasons. The subject is complex, but there are some clear issues at stake relating to the working of support regimes under the common agricultural policy and how they affect different member states. This occasion is timely because the matter has been discussed by the European Commission and in national ministry official circles and it will be subject to political debate at forthcoming meetings of the Council of Ministers, when decisions must be made. It will be helpful to Ministers to have the opportunity of hearing hon. Members' views.

The European Union flax and hemp regime was introduced in 1970 and remains one of the few support regimes to escape significant reform. In recent years, the regime has run into difficulties, partly due to the stimulus to production from the rates of aid. Until the early 1990s, almost all EU flax and hemp was grown in France, Belgium and the Netherlands for the so-called traditional textiles market, which uses long-fibre flax. That market has remained small and static, but the price of the fibre has fluctuated according to fashion and, after 30 years of EU support, the market remains reliant on EU aid for its survival. However, in the early 1990s, interest arose in using the crop for industrial purposes and short-fibre flax is sufficient for that. On a smaller scale, there has been renewed interest in the growing of hemp in the EU, including the United Kingdom. In 1999, the United Kingdom hemp area was around 1,500 hectares, although that figure is likely to increase this year as new markets, particularly in the motor industry, begin to open up.

Concern has been expressed throughout the past decade that short-fibre crops have been grown primarily for the purpose of receiving the generous rates of European aid. To combat the threat of subsidy hunting, the Commission introduced more stringent requirements, such as processing contracts and minimum yields. Those measures have had some success.

In the United Kingdom, the flax area has fallen in recent years from 20,000 hectares in 1996 to 15,000 hectares in 1999. Production closely reflects demand for the new flax products. However, there has been an explosion of flax growing elsewhere in the European Union, especially in Spain, where the flax area rocketed from a very low level to some 126,000 hectares last year. Regrettably, the image of the European short-fibre flax and hemp industries has suffered as a result of abuse of the regime by some who have grown flax primarily for the aid.

4 The Committee will be aware that the Government's policy for United Kingdom agriculture is to secure a more competitive and sustainable industry, responsive to consumer wishes, environmentally responsible and with a much stronger market orientation. Therefore, in general, we support initiatives, and we are often in the vanguard of intiatives, for further reform of the CAP in pursuit of those objectives.

As I noted in my explanatory memorandum dated 14 December, the Government broadly support the Commission's proposal to bring flax and hemp into the arable area payment scheme. The move to a single area payment accords with our view that growers should make planting decisions in response to the market rather than to the stimuli provided by the CAP.

The expansion in United Kingdom flax and hemp has been driven by a Government policy to encourage the development of alternative crops. We are interested in flax and hemp because they are environmentally friendly crops. In addition to requiring low levels of chemicals and fertiliser treatments in comparison with mainstream crops such as oilseeds and cereals, they produce renewable raw materials that can displace synthetic fibres derived from petroleum and other mineral resources. The signs are that that is no passing fad. For example, the car industry, especially in Germany, is showing increasing interest in utilising flax and hemp products.

Flax and hemp have also provided opportunities for some of our farmers to diversify. It is therefore no accident that much of the interest in flax and hemp has arisen in Wales and the south-west of England, where livestock farmers have sought alternative uses for redundant grassland. We support such diversification, provided that markets exist to utilise the crops that are grown.

Some £7 million has been invested in the UK flax industry, of which more than £500,000 has been funded by the Government and slightly less by the European Union via structural funds, especially the objective 5b programmes. Clearly, we do not wish these investments to be made redundant by measures that are detrimental to UK industry.

In our view, the Commission's proposals for reform of the flax and hemp regime are fundamentally flawed and in need of significant amendment. Hon. Members may have noted from my supplementary explanatory memorandum dated 10 February that the Government share the European Scrutiny Committee's view that the Commission's economic justification for different treatment of the long-fibre flax sector and the short-fibre flax and the hemp sector is, to say the least, unconvincing.

As I have said, after 30 years of high-level support, the traditional long-fibre flax sector shows no sign of moving away from its reliance on European Union aid and productivity shows no sign of improving. The Commission's attitude seems to be to shore up a traditional activity in isolation from economic and market realities. On the other hand, it believes that the emergence of the new industrial uses sector of flax and JPEG 5 hemp is largely opportunistic and not worthy of support. We believe, however, that the two sectors should be treated the same and should attract the same levels of European Union aid, which should be reduced in parallel over the medium term until it matches that available for other arable area crops. The Government therefore oppose the discrimination between long and short-fibre crops contained in the Commission's reform proposals.

I want to explain the Government's position on a number of other points, some already raised by the Scrutiny Committee in response to the explanatory memoranda.

The first is the key point of land eligibility, which has been raised in briefings by the National Farmers Union and others. I hope that the members of the Committee have seen by now the draft regulatory impact assessment of the reform proposals. It makes it clear that the proposals are likely to discriminate against the United Kingdom flax and hemp industries, for both growers and processors. About 95 per cent. of UK flax and 85 per cent. of UK hemp is grown on land ineligible for arable area payments. Without some concession on the question of land eligibility, those crops would be likely to disappear, leaving processors with no domestic supply of raw materials.

We have considered carefully how best to deal with the problem. The Commission has acknowledged that it needs a solution. One possibility would be to seek the reclassification of land on which flax and hemp have been grown in recent years to become eligible for area payments. The problem with that approach is that there would be no guarantee that flax and hemp would continue to be grown, rather that other eligible crops such as cereals, unless eligibility were to be confined to flax and hemp.

Another problem with that approach is that the door would be closed to new growers, preventing the industry from expanding should the market so allow. That would affect areas that have experienced expansion in recent years, such as Wales and the southwest of England. A third problem would be the likelihood of increased base area penalties under the arable area payments scheme, which could reduce returns to growers across the arable sector.

We have, therefore, suggested an alternative two-pronged approach to the Commission that aims to avoid those problems. First, we have suggested that arable base areas should be adjusted by taking the average area on which flax and hemp were grown between 1997 and 1999, rather than between 1989 and 1991 as proposed by the Commission.

Secondly, we have suggested that the land eligibility rules should be disregarded for flax and hemp, so that any land could be used for those crops. With that approach, expansion would still be constrained by base area limitations, but the suggested approach for their adjustment would allow production to continue at least up to current levels without triggering base area penalties.

The timing of the reform is an issue that we and other interested parties have raised. The proposals envisage 6 the reform applying with effect from the 2000–01 marketing year for arable crops, which would include flax and hemp crops to be grown this year and planted during the current planting season. The final paragraph of the Commission's explanatory memorandum covering the proposals stated that a Council decision on the reform would have to be reached no later than the beginning of March in order to be implemented in time for the 2000–01 marketing year. As this deadline has now passed, it is too late for the reforms, if agreed, to apply to this year's crops. That has caused anxiety among UK growers, who need to know whether crops that they sow this year on land ineligible for arable payments will qualify for aid.

We have argued that, in the absence of a decision from the Council, the existing regime rules must still apply. We have received oral advice that growers who plant on what might be designated ineligible land under the Commission's proposals would have a legitimate expectation of receiving aid in relation to those crops this year, regardless of the outcome of the reform. However, the Commission has not put that in writing, and that omission forms part of our representations to seek clarification and to bring certainty to our growers. We have passed on the oral advice to growers' representatives and to flax and hemp processors. The Committee may find that a frustrating way of proceeding, but it is not unusual in discussions on reform proposals in the Commission. The Committee will be aware of recently proposed changes relating to hedges and it took a long time to obtain the clarification for our farmers in the current year.

We have questioned the lack of proposals for flax and hemp aid in this year's price-fixing proposals. It seems most unlikely that reform could be agreed by the Agriculture Council before the arable area payments scheme deadline for applications on 15 May and, even if it were, detailed Commission regulations would have to be agreed before new arrangements could come into force.

Another concern about the effect of the flax and hemp reform proposals is the added complexity that they would bring to the regime. The initiative to bring flax and hemp into the general arable regime is a step towards simplification, but the proposals for a new tonnage-based processing aid would create a whole new tier of support with new problems of administration and control. In effect, the commission is proposing to replace one area-based support mechanism with three, because, in addition to area-based support for growers, different processing aid arrangements are proposed for long-fibre flax, short-fibre flax and hemp. That goes against the Agriculture Commissioner's views on simplification of the CAP which he and others, including ourselves, have expressed continually in recent months.

We would like discrimination between the sectors to be removed. We are sceptical about the need for separate processing aid, but, if it is to be established, the rates of aid available to the two sectors should be unified and both should be phased out in the medium term. We also have reservations about the proposal for national guaranteed quantities, which would make it difficult for new factories that are being developed to JPEG 7 operate successfully. The proposals for processing aid and the national guaranteed quantities system are riddled with technical difficulties. For example, processors will need to achieve production of fibres with less than 5 per cent. impurities to qualify for the aid. That high level of purity is not necessary for all industrial applications of flax and hemp, so is not justified. Other problems include the transfer of fibres from one member state for processing in another and the lack of definition of what constitutes long-fibre and short-fibre production. The majority of member states, including the United Kingdom, would prefer any processing aid to be area-based, not tonnage based. I am pleased that the Portuguese Presidency has agreed to put together a paper for future discussion.

The proposals contain new restrictions on the growing of hemp. They are largely inspired by a United Nations initiative on drug production and control. The Commission is concerned about the image of the common agricultural policy. It understands the need to increase the minimum level of checking of EU hemp crops and to require member states to implement a system of prior authorisation for the growing of hemp. However, the new measures appear to be excessive, because there have been no reports of drug varieties of hemp being grown among European Union-supported hemp crops. The United Kingdom already has stringent controls on the growing of hemp, including prior authorisation through a licensing system administered by the Home Office. The new measures should not lead to significant problems for us, but we would be concerned if they changed an effective system.

The proposals envisage the compulsory denaturing of hemp seeds, other than seeds for sowing, imported from outside the European Union. The Committee may have noted from the draft regulatory impact assessment that there is a small United Kingdom industry which uses hemp seed in the manufacture of foodstuffs, non-prescription medicines and cosmetics. Most of the seed used by that industry is sourced from within the European Union, but some is obtained from third countries. That supply would be effectively blocked, as the denaturing process destroys the seed's essential properties, thus making it unusable. We have questioned whether there is a need for this further regulation in the absence of evidence that a loophole exists that needs to be plugged.

I am sure that the Committee will find it helpful if I give a brief update on the negotiations for this proposed reform. As I said, the proposals have yet to progress beyond discussions at official level. There have been five Council working groups, and the reform has been discussed twice at the special committee for agriculture. Much of the discussion to date has been about the technical details of the proposals, but the fundamental points of concern cannot begin to be addressed until the matter goes to the Agriculture Council.

It is worth pointing out that we are far from alone in having reservations—in our case strong reservations—about the proposals. Indeed, almost all member states have voiced criticism. Even those with traditional long- 8 fibre flax industries are unhappy. For example, Francehas a substantial hemp industry that may be jeopardised by the proposed reform. Member states which, like the UK, have emerging industries in the industrial fibres sector—Germany, Finland, Austria and Denmark—share our concerns that the proposals could damage the industry before it has had time to become fully established. Non-growing member states are also unhappy, because the national guaranteed quality arrangements would prevent them from moving into flax and hemp production in the future.

It is possible that the reform will appear on the agenda for the May Agriculture Council, but that will depend partly on progress with the Presidency's paper on area-based processing aid.

The European Paliament is due to give its opinion on the reform proposals in April. We have seen a working document in which the Parliament gives a provisional view of the proposals. The initial reaction of the Committee on Agriculture and Rural Development is that the proposals should be rejected in their present form on the grounds that they would not meet the Commission's stated objectives and would create problems for different parts of the sector. We await the Parliament's full opinion with considerable interest.

Clearly, there is a long way to go before agreement can be reached on the future of flax and hemp support. The Government support further reform of the CAP and see no reason why flax and hemp should be exempt from that, but believe that the proposals are deficient in the ways that I have described. Our aim is to pursue reform of the sector, while ensuring that the framework of support does not threaten the viability of the UK flax and hemp industry or adversely and unfairly discriminate against us.

I look forward to the questions and discussion in the Committee, but I hope that members of the Committee will feel able to support the Government's strategy.
