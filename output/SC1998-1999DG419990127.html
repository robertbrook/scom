Draft Legal Aid (Prescribed Panels) Regulations 1998

4.30 pm

The Minister of State, Lord Chancellor's Department (Mr. Geoffrey Hoon)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Legal Aid (Prescribed Panels) Regulations 1998.

May I say, Mr. Jones, how pleased I am to see you in charge of this Committee, knowing your keen interest in matters to do with the legal system? I am sure that you will be much entertained by these regulations, whose purpose is to establish the clinical negligence franchise panel as a prescribed panel for the purposes of section 32(7) of the Legal Aid Act 1988.

The clinical negligence franchise panel epitomises the philosophy underlying the Government's reforms of legal aid. The reforms are designed to direct legal aid funding to those who need it most. We want also to channel legal aid funding through providers of legal services who have demonstrated a recognisable standard of administrative efficiency and specialist expertise.

There is no doubt that medical, or, as it should now be known, clinical, negligence cases require a particular expertise. Few people—professional or not—would argue with that. Expertise is gained by doing the work and doing it regularly. Firms that only occasionally undertake specialist cases such as clinical negligence claims are unlikely to develop the experience and skill that a firm with a regular case load has. They are also unlikely to achieve the results for their clients that an experienced firm would. That is a proven fact. Results obtained in cases funded by legal aid demonstrate conclusively that lawyers who are members of the Law Society's medical negligence panel or the Action for Victims of Medical Accidents panel achieve much better results. They win more cases and obtain higher damages. In 1996–97, panel members achieved on average damages of about £48,500 compared with only £21,800 achieved by non-panel-member lawyers.

It is therefore important for clients that clinical negligence claims are directed only to franchised firms with a contract. That will not reduce access to justice. Contracts will be granted to a sufficient number of firms to ensure that the expected 6,000 clinical negligence claims a year are properly dealt with. In rural areas where the population is widely dispersed and people find it difficult to get to a contracted firm, it will be part of firms' contracts that, where necessary, the solicitor will travel to the client to take instructions.

There will be no difficulty in new lawyers entering the field of clinical negligence litigation. New and inexperienced lawyers can work for a solicitor in a 4 franchised firm. Having gained the experience necessary for admission to one of the accredited panels, they will then be eligible to be included in the prescribed panel and to obtain contracts themselves.

To let the public, the legal profession and other interested parties know about the new clinical negligence category, there will be a comprehensive publicity campaign. The campaign will let people know where and how they can obtain the advice and assistance that they need. Posters and leaflets will explain the necessity of seeing a solicitor who is a member of the panel. This information will be made available in the usual public places, as well as in citizens advice bureaux, law centres and other legal advice agencies. Information about the Legal Aid Board's free phone-in service, which will provide information on which solicitors' firms have clinical negligence contracts, will also be provided. Solicitors are also being informed of which law firms have contracts, so that new cases can be referred to the right place.

Firms with members on the panel will be granted contracts to undertake work on behalf of assisted persons with clinical negligence cases from 1 February 1999. After that date, only firms on the panel will be able to conduct such cases. There is one exception, for the 2,000 firms that currently have a franchise contract to conduct personal injury cases. The personal injury franchise has until now included clinical negligence work. Those 2,000 firms will be able to continue to work on new clinical negligence claims until 31 July. After that date, they will need to qualify for a clinical negligence franchise to be able to take on new clinical negligence cases.

The regulations give effect to the decision that I announced on 16 July 1998 in a written reply to my hon. Friend the Member for Worcester (Mr. Foster), following the response to our consultation paper "Access to justice with conditional fees".

The regulations are required because section 32(1) of the Legal Aid Act 1988 creates a general right for a person receiving advice, assistance or representation to select the legal representative to advise, assist or act on his or her behalf from among the legal representatives who are willing to provide advice, assistance or representation under the Act. That right may be qualified under regulations made under section 32(7), so that the person receiving assistance may select assistance only from legal representatives who are currently members of a prescribed panel.

Regulation 1 provides for the regulations' description and the date—1 February 1999—on which they are to come into force, subject to approval by Parliament.

Regulation 2 interprets the important terms used in the regulations. I will not deal with all of them, but I should explain why the term "clinical negligence" rather than the previously used term "medical negligence" has been adopted. The term "clinical" is sufficiently wide to include all those who provide medical, clinical and other health services, such as radiographers, dentists and nurses. Clinical negligence describes more appropriately the type of negligence claims that arise in all health care-related claims.

JPEG 5 Regulation 3 establishes the clinical negligence franchise panel. It will consist of those solicitors who are already members of the Law Society's medical negligence panel or the Action for Victims of Medical Accidents panel. It is already a requirement that a firm requiring a clinical negligence franchise must have a member of staff who is also a member of one of the two accredited panels that I have mentioned.

Regulation 4 sets out the scope of the regulations in relation to particular types of claim. It also limits the right of a person requiring legally aided representation to select a representative under Section 32(1) of the 1988 Act. It makes it clear that other powers enjoyed by the board to limit representation for civil legal aid pursuant to a contract, which are contained in Section 32(2) of the 1988 Act, are not affected by these regulations.

I should explain that, although the regulations may have a short life because of the repeal of the Legal Aid Act 1988 by the Access to Justice Bill, the arrangements are intended to continue indefinitely. We are keen to establish a high standard of quality among the providers of clinical negligence legal services. We want to begin that task before undertaking the more fundamental reforms envisaged by the Access to Justice Bill, which is now before Parliament. The regulations will continue to apply until the new Act is enacted, when they will no longer be necessary because the provisions of the Access to Justice Bill will enable us to set and monitor the standards of legal service providers, and to secure the services of providers through contracts.

The commission will therefore be able to respond quickly if, for example, the requirements for admission to either panel are reduced below an acceptable level. However, the regulations are necessary under the provisions of the present legislation to enable us to limit representation to quality suppliers only.

I commend the regulations to the Committee and the House.

Mr. Edward Gamier
(Harborough)
: Thank you, Mr. Jones. May I join the Minister in welcoming you to the Committee? I doubt that you will find our subject entertaining, but you will probably find it more interesting than do many Government Back Benchers in this Committee and throughout the House of Commons. As far as I can see, they have not paid much attention to the progress of the Access to Justice Bill. Judging by the Minister's comments in the Chamber yesterday, I doubt whether he has read the Bill at all. Clearly, he has read the regulations before us, but I doubt whether he has fully appreciated the constitutional anomaly that they throw up.

The Minister was right to point out that the regulations are made under section 32 of the Legal Aid Act 1988, which will be repealed by the Access to Justice Bill. At first blush, the Lord Chancellor's Department has produced a characteristically hasty piece of work. The regulations anticipate the repealing of the 1988 Act at a time when the Access to Justice Bill, which is to repeal it, has not yet been fully scrutinised by the House or the other place—it has just finished the third day of its Committee stage in the Lords. That Bill will give the Lord 6 Chancellor 17 new powers, which is the largest acquisition of Executive power by a Law Officer or Minister in charge of the judiciary with which the House has had to deal since the granting of universal suffrage. The Bill steamrollers on through the Lords and, with the compliant majority enjoyed by the Government, will no doubt be similarly pushed through the House.

I am concerned about the timing of the regulations. Similar regulations might be made after the Access to Justice Bill has been enacted, but to sneak the regulations through now, while that Bill is still being discussed in the other place and has yet to be discussed in the House, is, at the least, unwise. For that constitutional reason alone, the Committee should reject the regulations.

At the moment, approximately 10,000 solicitors' firms offer advice and representation under legal aid. Through their policies, the Government will reduce that number to about 3,000 firms. That is a remarkable definition of access to justice, and in other circumstances the proposals would be subject to far fiercer criticism. The regulations give the Lord Chancellor, as a member of the Executive, huge additional powers to control who may appear in the courts and offer legal advice. The Minster seemed to admit that that could easily be achieved through the market. As he said, some solicitors' firms are expert in the field of clinical and medical negligence and attract most of the work because they do it well and successfully. They achieve the best results on behalf of their lay clients. Why therefore do we need now, before the Access to Justice Bill is enacted, a restrictive set of regulations to limit the firms of lawyers that are entitled to take on such work—when, as the Minister has admitted, the system already limits the category of solicitors that undertake such work, by virtue of their expertise? There is a glaring illogicality in the Government's approach to the regulations and to the legislation behind them.

Proper scrutiny of the regulations should be delayed until the Access to Justice Bill has become law—in other words, until the other place has completed its full deliberation of the Bill, the House has discussed it on Second Reading, with all that that implies, and a Standing Committee has given it due and proper consideration. It should not be for this Committee to waive the regulations through. I accept that section 32(7) of the 1988 Act gives the Government power to make regulations restricting the choice of representation and, were the Access to Justice Bill not being considered, many of my objections to the regulations would not now apply. However, because the Government have chosen to dismantle much of what is good in our legal system and have decided radically to alter the way in which legal services are provided to the general public, we should resist the implementation of the regulations at this stage. I therefore urge the Committee to reject them.

We are discussing the terms on which such a solicitor will be granted taxpayers' money to conduct a case. The regulations will not necessarily affect the Law Society—any change is a matter for it.
