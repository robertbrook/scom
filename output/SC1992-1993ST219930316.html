Draft Asian Development Bank (Fifth Replenishment of the Asian Development Fund and Second Regularised Replenishment of the Technical Assistance Special Fund) Order 1993

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs (Mr. Mark Lennox-Boyd)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Asian Development Bank (Fifth Replenishment of the Asian Development Fund and Second Regularised Replenishment of the Technical Assistance Special Fund) Order 1993.

The Chairman
: With the agreement of the Committee, it will be convenient to discuss at the same time the other order before us, namely, the Draft International Finance Corporation (1991 General Capital Increase) Order 1993.

Mr. Lennox-Boyd
: The purpose of the two orders is to provide for continued support to two international financial institutions, which we judge to make extremely effective contributions towards the development of their member countries, and which mutually reinforce the priorities that the Government have set for the British aid programme.

The Government's objectives for the aid programme are set out in the departmental report which was published last month. Our overall aim is to promote sustainable economic and social development and good government, in order to reduce poverty and improve the quality of life for poor people. That requires action in several areas: the adoption of sound economic policies; encouragement of the private sector as the source of wealth creation; investment in economic and social infrastructure; projects and programmes which have a positive impact on the lives of the poorest, and improve the status of women; protection of the environment; and higher standards of public administration.

The Asian development fund—the ADF—is the soft lending arm of the Asian Development bank. It provides loans on highly concessional terms to most of the poorest countries in the region, as well as to small Pacific island countries. The Asian Development bank's borrowers are extremely diverse, ranging from large, populous low income countries in south Asia to micro-states in the Pacific. In general terms, the more advanced countries or those considered sufficiently creditworthy borrow from the bank, while the poorest or less creditworthy countries borrow from the fund.

The fund's resources are replenished periodically by donor countries. Negotiations on the fifth replenishment were concluded at a meeting in London in December 1991 and raised $4·2 billion for commitment over the period 1992–1995. Subject to parliamentary approval, the United 4 Kingdom's contribution will be £86 3 million. That will be paid in four equal instalments in the form of promissory notes.

The major borrowers under the Asian development fund in recent years have been Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, the Philippines, Indonesia and Nepal. During the fifth replenishment period, the fund expects to resume lending to Vietnam and Burma, if circumstances permit, and to start programmes in Mongolia and the central Asian republics. India and China borrow only from the bank; they have never had access to concessional resources through the fund.

We welcome the Asian development fund's work in tackling poverty reduction in the region, and I commend the order to the Committee.

I turn now to the other order. The International Finance Corporation—the IFC—is part of the World bank group. Its aim is to promote private sector development in member countries. It acts as a lender and equity investor in private sector enterprises, originally exclusively in developing countries but also in recent years to countries in the process of transition to market-based economies in eastern Europe and the former Soviet Union. The IFC also plays an advisory role, which is increasing in importance.

In the second half of the 1980s, the IFC began a five-year programme of expansion, supported by a capital increase approved in 1985 which doubled its capital base from $650 million to $1·3 billion. Approvals grew at an average rate of 20 per cent, a year. It diversified its activities, with a greater focus on capital markets development, privatisation and help for small and medium-scale enterprises, especially in Africa. The IFC also successfully embarked on a policy of borrowing from the international capital markets rather than solely from the World bank.

This growth coincided with a general shift in the 1980s in thinking about the nature of the development process. In the 1960s and 1970s, many developing countries sought to protect their economies from outside competition, and discouraged foreign direct investment. There was excessive state control of productive enterprises and regulation of prices, which frequently led to inefficient use of resources as well as increasing the opportunities for mismanagement and corruption. As the failure of these policies became increasingly apparent, recognition of the importance of market forces has grown rapidly. The trend has been reinforced over the past few years by the collapse of command economies. Market-driven growth, and a larger role for the private sector, are now—I am glad to say—seen as essential to wealth creation and the reduction of poverty.

The IFC is well placed to assist in this process. Its activities fall into three broad categories: investment, resource mobilisation, and advice. To provide the resources to meet those challenges, a capital increase of $1 billion has been approved, bringing the total authorised capital to $2·3 billion. That will allow the IFC to plan for growth of 10 per cent, to 11 per cent, a year during the 1990s.

During the discussions on the capital increase, consideration was given to strengthening the role of the World bank group as a whole in supporting private sector developments. It was agreed that there should be closer coordination between the IFC and the World bank; and, in particular, that the IFC should assist in the preparation of private sector assessments covering major borrowers. The United Kingdom has been offered an allocation of 52,615 JPEG 5 shares at a cost of $1,000 a share, making a total commitment of $52·6 million. Subject to parliamentary approval, our subscription will be paid in five equal instalments. We strongly support the work of the IFC and its impressive achievements under the leadership of Sir William Ryrie. I commend the order to the Committee.
