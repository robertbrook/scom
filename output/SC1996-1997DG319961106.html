Draft Child Support Departure Direction and Consequential Amendments Regulations 1996

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. Andrew Mitchell)
: I beg to move,

"That the Committee has considered the draft Child Support Departure Direction and Consequential Amendments Regulations 1996.".

On behalf of the Committee, may I say what a pleasure it is to appear before you this afternoon, Mrs. Winterton.

These regulations pave the way for the full introduction of this important change in the way in which child support liability is calculated. They follow on from the anticipatory regulations, which we debated in May, which have now been successfully piloted.

The decision to test the departures regulations and procedures by running a pilot, followed from the lessons learnt from the start-up of the Child Support Agency. As expected, the pilot proved extremely valuable: in particular, it gave the agency the opportunity to test and refine procedures before it had to introduce the full scheme. The pilot exercise began as planned on 9 April. A pilot evaluation report has now been produced and copies placed in the Library.

I take this opportunity to thank the staff of the pilot offices in Hastings, Gravesend, Basildon, Ilford and Ealing for their hard work on the pilot cases, which were completed within a very demanding time frame to enable lessons to be learnt and changes to be made in time for the full introduction of the system.

As a direct result of problems identified by the pilot, the Child Support Agency has been able to make a number of useful refinements to its procedures, to the computer system and to the letters and forms sent to parents. Those refinements will help the agency to deal more smoothly and efficiently with the full implementation of the departures system.

I looked carefully at the evidence from the pilot to see if any changes were appropriate to the grounds for departure, but the pilot confirmed my view that there is little need for change.

During the pilot, there were applications in all of the departures categories, the most popular being inconsistent lifestyle; apportionment of housing costs; travel to work; debts of the relationship; and contact costs.

4 Members of the Committee will be interested to note that there was an almost even split of applications between parents with care and absent parents and that applications from parents with care were as likely to succeed as those from absent parents.

Fifty-seven per cent. of applications were on more than one ground, with an average close to three grounds for each of the multiple ground applications.

The pilot shows that around 10 to 15 per cent. of all applications are likely to be successful. That level of success is very much in line with other similar appeals systems. Just over 2,000 departure applications have been dealt with during the pilot.

We will, of course, continue to monitor the new scheme very closely. The pilot was not able to test all aspects of the departures scheme and full live running may identify issues that the pilot could not.

I do not propose to go over the regulations in great detail, but it will, I hope, be helpful if I mention the main provisions. The departures system introduces an important new principle into the child support scheme—the use of discretion which will provide the flexibility required to allow the greater recognition of expenses and commitments not covered by the formula. At the same time, it will enable the agency to crack down on absent parents who seek to reduce their child support liabilities by diverting income elsewhere.

There are three broad catergories under which a parent may apply for a departure. The first category, in regulations 13 to 20, covers certain special expenses that the applicant has to meet. These are high travel-to-work costs not fully dealt with in the broads brush allowance for travel-to-work costs; high costs in maintaining contact with children; costs arising from the illness or disability of the applicant or a dependant; debts incurred while the family were together; pre-April 1993 financial commitments from which it is impossible or unreasonable for the parent to withdraw; and the costs of supporting stepchildren, where responsibilities were taken on before April 1993. Except in the case of illness or disability and the costs of supporting stepchildren, a threshold of £15 must be passed before a parent can qualify under the provisions. In the case of a parent applying under more than one of these grounds at the same time, only one £15 threshold will be applied.

The second category, in regulations 21 and 22, is where there has been a property or capital transfer—often referred to as a clean-break arrangement—before 5 April 1993. There are three circumstances in which a parent may wish to make a departure application: first, if the allowance under the broad-brush formula provisions does not adequately reflect the value of the settlement; secondly, if a formula allowance was awarded when the whole of the settlement was for spousal liabilities; and thirdly, if no formula allowance was given. The different evidence requirements and method for valuing the settlement can mean that the departure system will give a different result. If a departure direction is given, the formula allowance will be removed and the value of the settlement deducted from the weekly maintenance liability.

JPEG 5 The third, and final, category is in regulations 23 to 29 and covers certain other specified circumstances. They include first, assets capable of producing an income, or higher income, and examples could be where a parent has large amounts held in cash that could be invested or empty properties that could be rented out to obtain an income; secondly, diversion of income, including cases in which a parent diverts part of his income to be paid to a new wife or partner or in which money is not taken as a salary but is left in a business where the absent parent still has access to it; and, thirdly, lifestyle cases in which a parent's overall lifestyle is significantly out of line with the level of income on which the maintenance assessment is based, including, for example, cases in which a self-employed parent declares only low profits by offsetting income against business expenses or in which the parent has a number of expensive pursuits.

Cases involving diversion of income and inconsistent lifestyle will, because of their complex nature, initially be referred directly to a child support appeal tribunal for decision if there is evidence to suggest that there is a case to answer.

Committee members will be as concerned as I am about absent parents who are able to conceal or divert large parts of their income to reduce their liability and pass on their responsibilities to the taxpayer. The new rules will close those loopholes, ensuring that children receive the maintenance that is rightfully theirs.

I deal now with the transitional arrangements. The regulations do not prescribe the order of take-on of departure cases but cases will be taken on in a number of separate tranches. That will avoid a backlog of cases building up with consequent delays in obtaining decisions.

When we invited parents to take part in the pilot, we made a commitment that pilot cases would be taken on first, subject to a check that their circumstances remained unaltered. The agency will honour that commitment by writing to those parents to seek confirmation that they wish their departure applications to go forward. The agency will then deal with those cases first.

For operational simplicity, the CSA will also take on new cases—cases in which the maintenance assessment takes effect after 2 December this year—as they arise. Information about how to apply for a departure will be sent to parents with notification of their maintenance assessments.

Other cases in which there is an existing assessment will be taken on in phases. Mailshots will be sent out, inviting applications for departures.

The first tranche to be contacted will include cases in which liability is likely to cease in the short term. That will be a limited group of cases in which the youngest child is 15 years old or over. Remaining stock cases will be taken on according to the age of the maintenance assessment on an oldest case first basis since those applicants with the oldest maintenance assessments will have waited longest for relief under the departures system.

6 We aim to invite applications from all stock cases by April 1997 although, if volumes are higher than the pilot has indicated, that period may be extended to September 1997.

Let me make it clear that parents will not lose out because of this phased take-on. Parents with a maintenance assessment current at 2 December 1996 who make an application for a departure by 1 December 1997 will have any departure award backdated to 2 December 1996, providing that all conditions were met at that date.

I turn to the appeals process, one aspect that could not be tested in the pilot. Departure directions determined by the Secretary of State will carry the right of appeal to the child support appeal tribunal, which is independent of the Child Support Agency. In addition, as I have indicated both on diversion of income and on lifestyle, cases that raise novel or particularly contentious issues may be referred directly by the CSA to the independent tribunal service for determination.

Most of the procedures for handling departure appeals are the same as those used for other types of decisions in child support, except that in certain circumstances a tribunal may be constitued by a chairman sitting alone. Those cases include appeals against a decision to refuse an application following a preliminary consideration, and property and capital transfer cases—both complex cases that are referred directly for a decision and appeals against directions given by the agency. It will be open to the chairman, however, to decide that an application or appeal should be put to a full tribunal. The arrangements will enable appeals to be dealt with quickly and efficiently. The changes introduced by the regulations are part of the continuing process of reviewing the child support scheme. We have already introduced several measures to make the system fairer. The new arrangements will give us the flexibility that we need to provide further help for those parents upon whom the formula bears hardest. The changes and the continuing improvements in the administration of the scheme will further the process of public acceptance of the principles behind the Child Support Act 1991. I commend them to the Committee.

Mr. Malcolm Wicks
(Croydon, North-West)
: Child support policy—policy relating to the financial maintenance of children in family breakdown or other circumstances in which children do not live with both natural parents—is difficult territory for Government and for Parliament, as we have discovered in recent years. There are at least two reasons for that. The first is that making such policy is an attempt by Government to deal with complex and often fraught and traumatic human relationships, and to intervene at some of the most difficult times for adults and children. The second relates to the issue of whether public policies should be based on discretion, and the examination of individual cases on their merits—perhaps through judicial discretion in a court-based system—or whether an administrative system JPEG 7 based around a national formula is possible. The Child Support Act is, of course, based on the latter model. It is an administrative system with a national formula. Today we are to debate how to build flexibility, sensitivity and discretion around that system.

This is not the occasion to rehearse all the arguments relating to the issue that I have outlined, but a reminder of the context in which we are judging the regulations may be a useful background for the Committee. The Labour party has urged the Government to reform child support. We have no wish to go back to the old court-based system—mainly because it did not work, and it is not the job of social policy to reinvent the broken wheel. We have sometimes pressed successfully for reforms. The appeals system is the result of pressure by our party. As the Minister said, a White Paper on child support has been produced in recent times, and the Child Support Act 1995 amended child support policy. First, does the Minister agree that by any measure the take-up on the pilot study has been disappointing? I understand from the report that in about 25,000 cases, clients of the Child Support Agency were invited to apply, if they wanted to, for departures. The report states that 23 per cent. requested application forms but only 6 per cent. of the 25,000 made formal applications, and that only 10 or 15 per cent. of applicants are likely to be successful. If my arithmetic is correct, that represents less than 1 per cent. of the group that was invited to consider applying. Am I right in thinking that the group of 25,000 was not selected at random from the CSA caseload, but that it consisted of people who might have a prima facie case for departure? If that is so, a success rate of slightly less than 1 per cent. is surprising. I do not make the mistake of expecting even 50 per cent. of what appear to be prima facie cases for departure to be successful. However, if my arithmetic is roughly correct, the success rate is surprisingly low for a target group of likely cases. Can the Minister shed more light on that small percentage?

The matter is not simply arithmetical. It has occasioned debate in the House about how to ensure that cases of just grievance in which, for example people believe that travel-to-work costs have not been taken into account, or in which the parent with care—usually the mother—believes that her ex-husband is camouflaging income, are properly tackled. It was 8 stressed by hon. Members of all parties that sensitivity and discretion should be allowed in such cases. In view of that, the success rate is poor.

The Minister said that roughly half of the cases were brought by parents with care, normally mothers, and half by absent parents, usually fathers. However, a slight majority—53 per cent.—were brought by parents with care. As I am not the President of the United States, I am not too worried about a clear majority, but the figure is 53 per cent. Many cases were about inconsistent lifestyle—cases in which an absent parent claims that his income is too low to pay more, yet he allegedly drives a Jaguar, goes on foreign holidays and thus seems to hoodwink the CSA about his true income.

A smaller category of cases involved diversion of income—cases in which an absent parent pays his current partner a substantial income as company secretary and thus hoodwinks the system. Who should provide the evidence in such cases?

The evaluation report states that

"around 90 per cent. of applications under inconsistent lifestyle or diversion of income grounds can be eliminated at an early stage as being unsubstantiated by evidence provided."

It goes on to say that

"many PWCs"—or parents with care—"were unable to provide adequate evidence to support their application."

Clearly, an application requires evidence in order to be successful. However, the report suggests that the parent with care should provide the evidence. That may not be fair. A genuine problem could exist—the absent parent's income could be far higher than he admits, or he might be diverting income. Does the Minister expect the mother, who is often in vulnerable circumstances, to produce accounts and an actuary's report? I exaggerate to emphasise the point. If there are reasonable grounds for an investigation, the CSA should undertake it, or the case should go to a tribunal that can call for all the papers and full disclosure. On the basis of the evaluation report, I believe that mothers do not get a fair deal and that their cases are dismissed too lightly. My constituency caseload supports that view.

I asked the National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux about the matter. In a letter, it states:

"This places an unfair burden on the individual parent with care who will not necessarily have the resources or power to uncover deception or to secure genuine evidence from their estranged partner."

It adds that it thinks that a tribunal might be the best means of obtaining such information.
