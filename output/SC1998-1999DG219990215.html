Draft Development Commission (Transfer of Functions and Miscellaneous Provisions Order 1999

4.30 pm

The Minister for the Environment (Mr. Michael Meacher)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Development Commission (Transfer of Functions and Miscellaneous Provisions) Order 1999.

The order is made under sections 35 and 37 of the Regional Development Agencies Act 1998. The purpose of the order is to achieve four things: to create the new Countryside Agency by renaming the Countryside Commission the Countryside Agency; to transfer the Rural Development Commission functions to the Countryside Agency; to provide for appropriate RDC property, rights and liabilities to be transferred to the Countryside Agency; and to enable the Countryside Agency and the Urban Regeneration Agency, known as English Partnerships, to provide certain specialised services in support of the Regional Development Agencies.

The order is part of the process of implementing certain provisions of the Regional Development Agencies Act 1998. In particular, it will put into effect the decision to create the Countryside Agency from the merger of the Countryside Commission with the parts of the Rural Development Commission that are not being transferred to the Regional Development Agencies.

The Government have been pursuing their objectives to delegate decision making to the English regions, as hon. Members will be well aware. Regional Development Agencies, established under the 1998 Act, will advance the development of a more strategic approach to regional issues, including regeneration. In considering the roles and responsibilities of the RDAs, it was decided that rural regeneration should be an integral part to be included within the RDA's remit. It was, therefore, concluded that the rural regeneration work currently performed by the regional teams in the Rural Development Commission should transfer to RDAs on 1 April 1999.

There has been some public concern—I think it is misplaced—that this approach might militate against rural areas. Therefore, to ensure that rural issues are given due weight, RDAs are also being given a specific remit to serve their rural areas, and at least one member of each RDA board outside London has rural experience. RDAs are also being given guidance on rural policy matters. Of course, my Department has consulted rural interests on its content.

4 The decision to transfer rural regeneration work from the RDC to the RDAs gave consideration of the future of other statutory agencies added impetus. As part of the Government's comprehensive spending review, the Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions and the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food conducted a joint review of countryside and rural policy. The review considered a range of institutional options, and stated that there was considerable common ground between the work of the RDC and the Countryside Commission. Bringing the two agencies together will consolidate their work and create a powerful new champion for rural England.

In addition, during and following the review, the Government emphasised the need for a more integrated approach to policy making. Many rural issues can obviously benefit from being considered in the round. The Rural Development Commission and the Countryside Commission have, for many years, been working closely together in several areas, for example, on joint experimental projects to promote green tourism. It was therefore thought that a merger could build on that relationship and establish a more integrated approach. Considering social, economic and environmental needs of the countryside together should help our overarching aim of sustainable development.

As hon. Members know, the merger was announced by the Deputy Prime Minister on 27 March 1998 during the passage of the Regional Development Agencies Bill. The combined experience and expertise of the Countryside Commission and Rural Development Commission in the new agency—under the order, it will be named the Countryside Agency— will provide a national source of research and advice about countryside and rural issues. It will cover social, economic and environmental matters, and it will promote and help others to develop policies and programmes to overcome rural disadvantage. That will be backed up by a continued programme of innovative projects on the ground, through pilot and demonstration schemes. The Countryside Agency will provide advice direct to Government, and will also be a source of advice to the RDAs.

The order enables schemes to be drawn up for the transfer of staff and assets from the RDC to the Countryside Commission and to English Partnerships. In parallel, the provisions of the Regional Development Agencies Act 1998 include making schemes to transfer staff and assets from English Partnerships and the RDC to the RDAs or other public bodies.

The order also enables English Partnerships and the Rural Development Commission, during its final months, to provide services to the RDAs. In particular, those services cover information technology support for regeneration programmes. That will ensure that the expertise and systems of English Partnerships and the RDC are available to the RDAs in the early days to enable them to become established quickly and seamlessly, so that they are not distracted by being involved in operational issues. This JPEG 5 measure is without prejudice to the wishes of the RDAs to consider their long-term strategies and to establish independent systems.

On 1 April 1999, the RDC's regional regeneration activities, with the relevant staff, programmes, rights, assets and liabilities, will transfer to the RDAs. I remind the Committee that the RDC's statutory functions are to keep under review and advise the Secretary of State on all matters relating to the economic and social development of rural areas in England, and to carry out or assist others to carry out measures likely to further such development. Those statutory functions will transfer to the Countryside Agency.

In practice, that covers the RDC's countrywide activity and its valuable research and advisory roles. The RDC's staff, programmes, property, rights and liabilities to match those activities will also transfer. Only a small residue of the RDC, limited to winding up its affairs, will be left, with a small number of commissioners. There will be a few members of staff, headed by an accounting officer, to finalise the accounts and settle any outstanding personnel and establishment work. We intend the final wind-up to take place by the end of September, for which another affirmative resolution order will need to be made under the Regional Development Agencies Act 1998. The staff concerned have elected to stay on for that process, and will then either retire or be made redundant.

The interests of the staff are important and we must bear them closely in mind. The staff transferring to all the new agencies will do so on their existing terms and conditions, with no detriment to any of their employment rights and with continuity of employment. As is usual in such cases, staff will be offered broadly comparable pension provision; in this case, through membership of the principal civil service pension scheme, which has identical provisions to that operated by the RDC.

Staff transfers have been worked out through a series of steps. The first identified the work programmes; the second the posts that go with them; and the third drew up plans to transfer them to several different agencies—either the Countryside Agency, the eight RDAs or English Partnerships—if they were not remaining to wind-up the RDC. That involved regular and detailed discussions with the agencies and the staff themselves. Once that was achieved, a staff preference exercise was held to enable the selection of staff for each agency on a fair and competitive basis. [ Interruption.] I hope that the hon. Member for West Chelmsford (Mr. Burns) is paying attention—

Mr. Burns
: Yes I am.

Mr. Meacher

Trade unions were consulted throughout. Plans to overcome that potential mismatch included seeking volunteers for different work as well as for redundancy. A number of staff have opted to take on new responsibilities within the agencies and others have volunteered to take redundancy, but most of the posts will be filled.

The transfer schemes cover office accommodation and will transfer existing freeholds or leaseholds. Following detailed discussions and negotiation, the Countryside Agency and the RDAs will between them inherit the RDC offices, some of which will be shared and some of which will become surplus to requirements. In time, we expect the agencies to rationalise their accommodation, particularly because some groups of staff will be divided between several locations. But there will be a good geographical spread in the offices of the Countryside Agency and the RDAs.

The new Countryside Agency will have a new chairman and some new members. Other members will be drawn from the existing boards of the RDC and the Countryside Commission to provide continuity and valuable experience. The chairman will be supported by the current chief executive of the Countryside Commission, and decisions on the full board composition will be taken soon.

Finally, I want to say a word on budgets and programmes. As the RDC's activities are being divided between the Countryside Agency and the RDAs, it follows that the equivalent budgets will also be sub-divided. The RDC's budget for 1999–2000 is around £43 million, about £25 million of which is for rural regeneration, which will transfer to the RDAs. The balance of £18 million from the RDC's budget will go to the Countryside Agency, including more than £4 million for the rural transport partnership to support innovative projects in rural areas and to complement JPEG 7 the rural transport development fund, both of which are run by the RDC. The new agency will also inherit £24 million from the Countryside Commission.

The comprehensive spending review highlighted the case for greater expenditure to help overcome rural deprivation; to provide for access and recreation; and to cover landscape protection. In addition to inheriting funds from the RDC and the Countryside Commission—

Mr. Tom Brake
(Carshalton and Wallington)
: The Minister said that the Countryside Commission will inherit £24 million. Is that the total Countryside Commission budget?

Mr. Meacher
: The Countryside Commission budget includes that £24 million. I do not know whether that is its total budget. I shall ensure that the hon. Member for Carshalton and Wallington (Mr. Brake) has an answer before the end of the debate. The budget certainly includes most of the £24 million.

The comprehensive spending review was designed to achieve three additional purposes. Therefore, as well as inheriting funds from the RDC and the Countryside Commission, the Countryside Agency will receive another £6 million to enable it to take forward the three initiatives that I have mentioned. Additional funding will be provided to cover the costs incurred by the agency in the merger. The detailed allocation of funds will be agreed in the agency's first-year operating plan.

This funding will give the agency an effective start— by strengthening its work of promoting and conserving the natural landscape, by supporting rural communities and by promoting access to and enjoyment of the countryside. It will also provide for pilot projects. Part of the additional funds will be used for necessary restructuring costs and to ensure that the RDC is efficiently wound up.

I can now give the hon. Member for Carshalton and Wallington the assurance that he sought: the £24 million is the total Countryside Commission budget, so the whole budget will now go to the Countryside Agency.

On that basis I commend the order to the Committee.
