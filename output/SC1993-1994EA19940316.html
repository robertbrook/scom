Court of Auditors

Report on the Environment

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Mr. Tony Baldry)
: I shall make a short statement about the European Court of Auditors' report, which was prepared in response to a resolution by the European Parliament relating to discharge of the 1988 Community budget. The report represents the outcome of audits of the Commission and six member states: Germany, Greece, Spain, Italy, the Netherlands and the United Kingdom. It covers the objectives and resources of Community environmental measures, the management of appropriations for this area, the relationship between structural instruments such as structural funds and the environment, and the implementation of environmental directives.

As the explanatory memorandum—sent to the Committee in October 1992—makes clear, the resport was published in September 1992, so much of it is now a matter of history. The report was not debated by the Committee earlier because there were other, more pressing, matters that it wished to discuss. Much of the report has already been acted upon.

Most of the criticism in the Court of Auditors' report is directed at the Commission's handling of environmental policy, and it highlights concerns about serious lack of co-ordination, poorly defined objectives and poor project control. The report also expresses concern about the slow progress in the implementation of environmental directives and doubt about whether those, on their own, can induce a significant change in environmental behaviour. There is some specific criticism of member states, but there is practically none of the United Kingdom.

The report raised some important issues about the way the Commission and member states seek to manage the Community's environment. We have long been concerned by the apparent lack of overall co-ordination on environmental matters in the Commission. In many Community-funded projects, it appeared that one part of the Commission did not know what another part was doing. General thinking has been to compartmentalise the environment, without realising that environmental concerns impinge on many other areas of Community policy, sometimes involving direct conflicts which need to be resolved at an early stage of policy formulation.

Since the audits that gave rise to the report, many of those issues have been addressed. Lack of co-ordination is still a major issue sometimes, but I am glad to say that developments in the past two or three years have gone a long way to meet the report's recommenda- 2 tions. There is increasing recognition by the Community as a whole of the need to consider the environmental impact of all policies.

I welcome the new emphasis on the environment that came about in the Maastricht treaty, which stresses the need to promote sustainable development and integrate the needs of environmental protection into other Community policies. The Commission has also made an attempt to get to grips with the problems outlined in the fifth environmental action programme called "Towards Sustainability", the Community strategy document for the environment from 1993 to 2000. That programme promotes the themes of sustainable development and integration with other key policy areas and stresses that we all have our part to play in protecting the environment, whether as national or local government, industry or individuals. Many of the programme's objectives and targets present significant challenges for us and others to meet, but the fifth action programme represents an important step forward. It sets out an overall Community strategy, takes full account of the way in which the environment impinges on other areas and proposes objectives and targets and suggesting responsibilities for action. The programme also recognises that legislation alone may not be enough and that additional measures, such as market-based instruments, may be required.

The Commission has adopted new internal procedures to identify and justify proposals with significant environmental consequences and designated a senior official in each of the directorates-general to oversee progress and implementation. The Commission will report on progress in its annual report and has adopted a code of green housekeeping. We welcome those developments in integration, as it is a theme that we have consistently promoted. For example, during our presidency, we made it a major theme of our informal Environment Council and promoted debate on it at a number of other Councils.

The Court of Auditors' report expressed doubt about EC directives' effectiveness in tackling problems alone, which touches on what evaluation there is of Community environmental policy. A continuous flow of legislation emerges from Brussels, but to date there has been no effective mechanism to review the impact of such legislation on the world. We must encourage the Commission to concentrate attention on that.

The United Kingdom fully supports measures that will enable the Commission and the Community to do a better job of enforcement because we want European Community law to be applied consistently across the Community and all member states to take their obligations seriously. A number of recent developments should help. A network of environmental enforcement agencies has been set up—Her Majesty's inspectorate of pollution in the United Kingdom and its equivalents in other states—to exchange practical experiences and develop common approaches to implementation. The Commission has undertaken to produce a report annually on implementation and enforcement that is to be discussed by the Council of Ministers. We now, at last, have a European environmental agency, which will collect and analyse environmental statistics. That should JPEG 3 help the Commission to take better-informed decisions on environmental policy, which can only be to everyone's benefit.

The report that we are discussing today is something of an historical document, because time has moved on. It is valuable none the less. The Commission and the Community have made considerable progress since its publication, but I am happy to try to answer any of the Committee's questions.
