Sea Fishing (Enforcement of Community Quota Measures) Order 1993

4.30 pm

Mr. Elliot Morley
(Glanford and Scunthorpe)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Sea Fishing (Enforcement of Community Quota Measures) Order 1993 (S.I., 1993, No. 387).

I am grateful for the opportunity to query some aspects of the enforcement order, which makes provision for various penalties to enforce European Council regulation No. 3919/92. The regulation raises many issues concerning total allowable catches and quota measures and their impact on the United Kingdom fishing industry.

The order also confers powers of enforcement on British sea fishery officers under article 6. That is topical in view of what happenedd recently off the Channel Islands when sea fishery officers experienced difficulty in boarding ships at sea and HMS Blazer had an unfortunate experience in Cherbourg harbour. It is coincidental that our debate is taking place against the background of those activities, but I shall refer to them.

We tabled a prayer to enable us to raise some important matters concerning the United Kingdom's fishing industry and we would welcome the Minister's comments on them. First, we want to consider the enforcement measures.

Articles 3 to 5 cover existing penalties and the recovery of fines. The Opposition have no objection to implementation of the Order because we want proper fishery management in United Kingdom waters and proper powers to back that up, but are the regulations working properly and meeting their objectives of ensuring that our fishery resources are properly managed.

The main issue that I want to raise is black fish and the illegal landings that take place in almost every port in the country. Such landings tend to be identified with Scotland, but I do not believe that Scotland is alone in that. We do not know the scale of black fish landings, but they occur in every port. A fisherman told me that there is an unofficial black fish economy with black fish quoted rates and that the orders given for black fish exceed the legal quotas which are laid down by the European Community and covered by the order. That fisherman went so far as to say that because the black fish economy is illegal, prices are more stable than the official price offish in the present problems. How does the Minister intend to tackle the problem of black fish, which has existed for a long time and is suspected to be considerable? I have been told that sometimes more black fish are landed in Scottish ports than legal fish, although it is difficult to discover whether that is correct.

The regulations on quota management apply only to fishermen and not to processors. Will the Minister confirm that it is not illegal for processors to buy illegally-landed fish, and will he consider extending a penalty to processors? 4 It is one thing to deal with the landing of fish by the fishermen—the regulations clearly state how penalties will be enforced in that respect—but my understanding of the provisions is that, if the fisherman gets his fish ashore to a processor, the processor can legally buy them. In such a case there seems to be no deterrent that the Minister can implement. In future, could regulations such as these be amended to make it an offence for the processor knowingly to buy illegally landed fish from outside the quota?

Fishermen are also involved in that matter. Is the level of fines that is laid down a suitable deterrent? I have spoken to fishermen about black fish and they have told me that they are forced into going down the road. They either break the law or go bankrupt. Faced with such options they take illegal action. I am not saying that that is a justification for breaking these quotas, because there is no such justification. Once one starts to go down that road, one undermines the development of a sustainable fishery and becomes involved in a vicious downward spiral in which fish become scarcer and scarcer and the pressure greater and greater.

However, I think that the Minister would accept that there is much disaffection in the United Kingdom fishing industry. I hope that the Minister recognises that fact, which cannot be denied when our ports are being blockaded by United Kingdom fishermen. In such an atmosphere, it is harder to get fishermen to obey laws because they become disaffected with authority. They regard the law and the regulations as authority and, quite wrongly, feel that by ignoring the law they are hitting back at authority.

I repeat that I do not justify such an attitude, but the British fishing industry faces a serious problem.

The issue of quotas and TACs is controversial. I know that when we have debated other regulations we have had similar arguments about the best way to manage the European fisheries. Quotas and TACs are a management tool. The problem is highlighted when there are large swings in quotas. The haddock quota was dramatically increased at the beginning of the year. That is a contributory factor to the present crisis.

There is a case for introducing technical conservation measures as an alternative to quotas, although I accept that they would be difficult to develop and even more difficult to enforce. The current abuse, with illegal landings, does not inspire confidence.

Fishermen must know that they are taking a risk; the penalties for black fish landing are clearly outlined. Fishermen can have their catch and gear confiscated and face considerable fines. However, apparently those penalties not seem to be an adequate deterrent. Will the Minister consider taking the penalties one step further so that fishing licences are suspended? I am reluctant to suggest such a measure because I know that by suspending someone's licence one is suspending that person's way of making their living. However, fishermen suggest that the issue needs to be tackled and that strong deterrents are needed. The strongest deterrent would be licence suspension. Will the Minister consider that possibility?

Article 6 of the provisions relates to the powers of British sea fishery officers. Article 6(2)(a) clearly provides that British sea fishery officers have the power to board vessels. If they suspect that an offence has been committed, they have the power to take that boat and crew to the nearest convenient port. In the case of La Calypso, it appears that JPEG 5 there was a difference of opinion about what was the nearest convenient port. The captain was adamant that it was Cherbourg. He set off for that port, despite what the boarding party said, and with the boarding party on board.

The powers in such an incident are clear. Article 7 makes it an offence to obstruct officers carrying out their duty. Will the skipper of La Calypso will be prosecuted under that article for ignoring the direction, legally given, by the British fishery protection officer who boarded his vessel? That raises the question of what will happen if a skipper will not co-operate when he has been boarded and directed in that way. In all fairness, the Calypso incident is not the first time that a boarding crew has been taken off a boat against its will and I do not suppose that it will be the last. A policy statement from the Minister would be useful to clarify how he views such incidents and how he thinks the crew should react when the captain of the fishery protection vessel sees his boarding party illegally taken to a foreign port by a vessel which will not respond to his radio signals and which will not stop.

In the current dispute, the press hinted that the right hon. Member for Suffolk, Coastal (Mr. Gummer) was suggesting that boarding parties should be armed. It is one thing for the right hon. Member for Suffolk, Coastal to have a little tantrum about Perrier water at press conferences, but it is another to lead a boarding party waving pistols, with a cutlass between his teeth, demanding that action be taken. I know that the Minister takes a more sensible line. I hope that he will calm down the right hon. Member for Suffolk, Coastal when, from time to time, he gets carried away.

This is a serious issue. What will happen if a vessel, particularly a foreign vessel, will not obey the regulations? I recall that, during the cod war, Icelandic fishery protection vessels began using solid shot. I am loth to suggest that as a solution, although it would be effective. If my memory serves me correctly, the idea of using solid shot was to punch a hole in the fishing vessel to slow it down. However, at least one British crewman was killed accidentally as a result of its use.

Co-operation is essential. The order results from an EC measure. It should be taken for granted that every EC member state will abide by the measures agreed by the European Community and will ensure that they are enforced. If one member state is seen not to ensure that its fishermen abide by agreed negotiations and treaties, others will follow suit.

The incoming French Government have not helped this matter by stating that they want to reopen negotiations on the common agricultural policy and on the general agreement on tariffs and trade. That sends the wrong signal to their citizens and in particular, their fishermen. If the French Government give the impression that they can suddenly renegotiate international treaties that they do not like, how do they expect French fishermen to abide by EC rules that they do not like? The incoming French Government may have given misleading assurances and promises to their electorate. It would not be the first time that that has been done. But I know that you, Mr. Hill, would not want me to go down that road so I shall confine my comments to the order.

The crews of HMS Blazer and the Brocklesby acted properly in the circumstances. They carried out their duty professionally, particularly those on board HMS Blazer which had fairly young trainees on board when it was 6 boarded in Cherbourg. They did not allow themselves to be provoked nor did they retaliate inappropriately. Although those crews have been very frustrated by the way in which they were treated, they acted professionally and responsibly. They are entitled to support from the Government and the European Community. I hope that we shall receive assurances that they will be supported.

We support the framework of the order and the powers that are being given to the Minister to enforce agreements within the European Community. The Opposition feel, however, that there are problems, particularly regarding black fish landings in Scotland. We feel that there are weaknesses in the way in which the order will operate. Sanctions should be taken against processors. At present, processors can legally buy and use black fish even though, in many cases, they know that it has been landed illegally. If we do not stop that practice, there is no future for the fisheries in this country or in the European Community.

As a mere soft-handed southerner from England—albeit married to a Scot—I thought it peculiar that fish could be dumped in such a way. It struck me that there should be a better way of controlling not only how fish are landed but how they are caught. I have read the order, and it seems that at last there will be proper restrictions on the landing and the catching of fish. We will not just be catching fish and dumping them, but will be preventing fish from being caught. The point of the exercise is not to prevent fish being landed, but to prevent the oceans from being deprived of fish for future generations. If that is the purpose of the order, I wholeheartedly welcome it and hope that my hon. Friend the Minister will reassure me on that matter.

    Every year, we need several statutory instruments for full enforcement of the annual TACs and quotas. Some refer to technical conservation, for example. The 1993 TACs and quotas were set out in Community regulation No. 3919/92, which was approved by the Fisheries Council on 20 December. Some Community regulations on sea fishing are directly enforceable in United Kingdom ports under section 30(1) of the Fisheries Act 1981. The Act permits us to bring charges against people only for illegal fishing in United Kingdom limits—not for the fish that they might have on board even if they are not fishing, or the fish that they land. The order permits us to charge in relation to offences that occur outside the British fisheries limits. It is necessary to supplement our permitted action under the basic rules.

    The 1981 Act also provides that if we wish to set lower fines for certain offences where the maximum penalty of £50,000 would be too much, we must introduce a statutory instrument to do so. That, again, permits us to levy lower fines. The level of fines is set in accordance with the generality prevailing across the legal system for certain categories of offences. The order, which is made under section 30(2) of the Fisheries Act 1981, covers any fishing boat registered in the United Kingdom, wherever it fishes, or any fishing boat, of whatever origin, fishing within British fishery limits, including the fishery limits off the Channel Islands. It provides for penalties which include a fine and forfeiture of gear and of the catch. The maximum penalties that the order specifies correspond to those for analagous offences under the Sea Fish (Conservation) Act 1967, as amended by the Criminal Justice Act 1991. Offences for illegal landing of, for example, over-quota fish, will be punishable on summary conviction by a fine that does not exceed the statutory maximum, which is usually £5,000. The order provides for the necessary powers of entry for sea fisheries officers. Those powers are provided for other fisheries offences.
