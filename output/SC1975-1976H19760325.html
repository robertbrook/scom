Mr. Powell
: I am sure that I speak for the Committee as a whole when I say what an inexpressible pleasure it is to us, Mrs. Jeger, to see you back in your place in the Chair [Hon. Members: "Hear, hear"]. This implies no disrespect to those who have occupied it temporarily, but there can be no substitute for the calm and gracious presence with which you alone are able to guide our proceedings.

You reminded the Committee, Mrs. Jeger, that we are taking together, although clearly they raise separate points, amendments relating to paragraphs ( c) and ( d) and the concluding words of subsection (3). Perhaps I may briefly recapitulate what I said, so that I may put the precise questions which I hope the right hon. Gentleman or his colleague will be able to clarify.

I take first paragraph ( c). I should like to explain by way of an analogy the difficulty which, prima facie, we feel in respect of this paragraph. Let us suppose that the allegation referred to a misdemeanour or an offence other than an offence under this Bill. In that case, whether the allegation was or was not true, there would be remedy under existing law for whoever was injured by the making of that allegation, or by the truth of that allegation. My submission is that that 340 alternative applies equally to those damaged by allegations, true or false, of unlawful acts under the Bill.

If a person goes about making unfounded allegations that employer X is discriminatory, as that is an unlawful act the employer has his remedy against that person under existing law and ought to seek it in that way.

On the other hand, if it be true that the employer is practising discrimination as defined in the Bill, it is the purpose of the Bill to apply a remedy, and those who seek that remedy will be protected against discrimination by the provisions of paragraphs ( a) and ( b). I hope that I have put sufficiently precisely to the right hon. Gentleman the reasons why it seems to us that paragraph ( c) is, for the purposes of the Bill, taking a whole category of offences as it were, out of the general law and out of the range of remedies provided by the general law. So much for paragraph ( c).

I come to paragraph ( d), and here the expression is so general that we should be obliged if the Minister of State could indicate the other types of act which might be done by persons other than those set out in paragraphs ( a) and ( b), which might be the cause of his being victimised or would justify his having the protection against victimisation afforded by the subsection. So much for paragraph ( d).

I turn to the last words from which I select construing, I hope correctly, the particular words which seem to be to embody a quite extreme provision. I simply start with the words "by reason that" he suspects the person victimised intends to do any of the things set out in ( a), ( b), ( c) and ( d). For instance, one could have an employer alleging discrimination on the ground that he took a decision in the course of hiring, firing or promoting, and so on, by reason of the fact that he suspected X of intending to allege that he, the employer, had committed acts which, without that assertion being openly so made, would amount to a contravention of the Act.

I ask the Committee to contemplate the remoteness and the subjectivity of the circumstances which are here envisaged. There are two layers of subjectivity at least, and there may be three. First, there is the layer of suspecting. We envisage circumstances in which an offence of discrimination, a contravention of the Act, would occur as a result of acts or JPEG 341 omissions based upon a suspicion in the mind of the person who committed them. This might be very difficult to prove. I feel sure that the right hon. Gentleman will not take refuge in the fact that such a point might be difficult to prove in the ultimate stages of appeal for which this part of the Bill provides, because we are seriously considering whether it is necessary to provide safeguards against victimisation in the conditions set out in lines 13 to 15.

But it is not just one layer of subjectivity. One does not merely have to consider that the employer acted as he did because of a suspicion in his mind, a suspicion which he might not have communicated to anyone, a suspicion which might be well founded or ill-founded, malicious or genuine. But it could be a suspicion that the person victimised in the definitions of this clause intended to do something.

We have got into remote regions of speculation where we are going to consider the act of am employer to see whether it reveals that he suspects that someone intends to do what, by definition, has not yet happened. To put the mansard roof upon, that doing may be no more than the making of an allegation.

Therefore, unless I have misconstrued the situation, what we are saying is that it is possible to make a complaint of victimisation and to do so on the ground that X, the employer, suspected that I intended to make an allegation. I really question whether the Committee can think it right to put that kind of provision into Acts of Parliament and either to open—if it is open, and if it is not open in practice let us not cumber up the Bill with it—such a recourse to the citizen or to put citizens at danger of the proceedings of the Bill on that basis.

These amendments address three separate questions to the right hon. Gentleman. I hope he will agree to reconsider the acutely subjective regions into which the subsection is led by the last three lines. I am not necessarily complaining of the totality of those three lines, but I believe that we have gone too far into a cubing of the subjectivity on the basis of which victimisation could be alleged.

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Stanley Orme)
: The Committee 342 will recognise that victimisation is a difficult matter to prove, particularly in industry. I have had industrial experience and a great deal of negotiation on victimisation. A person is often not dismissed for the reason he may have suspected. He may have been dismissed for some other alleged misdemeanour, rather than for being an active trade unionist, or a member of a political party.

We must, therefore, protect the right of a person making a complaint which involves his employer and protects him from victimisation. That is the central core of what we are discussing. In other words, in an ideal society if an employee feels that his employer may have acted contrary to the Bill—which the agency will decide—he should logically be free to make such an accusation. The employer should either defend it, or if he has committed an offence he should admit it and put it right.

We know, however, that industry is about complicated, detailed, human relationships, and Members who have worked in industry know that there are difficulties, such as the recalcitrant employer and employee who may act irrationally and irresponsibly. The Government feel that there must be protection under this Bill for people to speak freely. If the Bill is to work and this key clause is central to it—it will only work in an open sense. It will only work if people can freely speak their minds and if, when they see an injustice, they can refer it for examination by a totally independent, rational body—not by the defendant or the appellant, but by an independent body.

I understand the tenor of what the right hon. Member for Down, South (Mr. Powell) is saying. I am sure that he will agree that defining this legislation in terms that will not be misunderstood is a difficult drafting problem.

Coming to the three amendments, the Government believe that acceptance of any of them would weaken the provisions of Clause 16. We fell that this freedom from victimisation, with the proper safeguards which it will give to the employer, should go forward.

It may be helpful if I make it clear that these provisions do not, in any event, protect against discrimination a person who makes allegations which are false and JPEG 343 not made in good faith. This is made clear by subsection (4).

Amendment No. 98 would remove from Clause 16(3) the protection from victimisation now given to a person who alleges that another has committed an act which is in contravention of the Bill whether or not he has made a complaint to the Fair Employment Agency. Without that safeguard it is possible that a person might not make allegations, however well founded, that an employer, or other, was contravening the Bill for fear that as a result he himself would be discriminated against. The Bill ought not to allow a person who sincerely believes that the law is being broken to be prevented from pursuing the matter. I think that I outlined that point at the beginning of my remarks.

I turn now to paragraph ( c) with which the right hon. Gentleman dealt. This paragraph protects a person from victimisation. It does not affect such remedies as the employer would have taken under the general law—namely, action against defamation of character, slander or other common law charges. Therefore, it does not affect the common law in that sense.

In relation to paragraph ( d) the right hon. Gentleman asked the pertinent question of the interpretation of the words within the clause. A person could be victimised by his employer because he had appealed to the court under Clause 28 against a finding by the agency. A person could also be discriminated against because he had alleged that the victimiser or a person by whom he had been previously employed had followed practices which failed to provide equality of opportunity. Clearly these and similar cases must be protected, and therefore paragraph ( d), as worded, is necessary to cover that particular point.

Amendment No. 100 would remove the protection from victimisation of a person who had not actually done any of the things listed in paragraphs ( a) to ( c) but whom the victimiser knows intends to do, or suspects has done, or intends to do one of them. The right hon. Gentleman asked for an example relating to paragraph ( d). An employer who has contravened the Bill would be able to dismiss an employee whom he knows or suspects intends to give evidence to 344 the agency, the courts, or the appeals board. Thus it would be easier for the employer to get round the provisons of the Bill since he would be freer to obstruct the agency, the courts and the board in the discharge of their functions. Obviously that is not a situation which the Bill should permit. It is right that the Bill should protect well-intentioned persons who can and do provide information or take any action in relation to the Bill which helps to achieve its purpose. In its present form Clause 16 does so.

I have replied in some detail to the amendments moved by the right hon. Gentleman. Clause 16 as it stands is justified to deal with what I recognise is a difficult problem. However, it is one which has to be faced.
