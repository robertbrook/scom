Education (School Information) (England) Regulations 1993

10.30 am

Mr. Win Griffiths
(Bridgend)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Education (School Information) (England) Regulations 1993 (S.I. 1993, No. 1502).

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the Education (School Performance Information) (England) Regulations 1993 (S.I. 1993, No. 1503).

Mr. Griffiths
: We prayed against these statutory instruments not because the changes were bad but because of some aspects of the league tables that result from the regulations. I shall deal first with specific changes.

Statutory Instrument No. 1502 consolidates existing regulations and makes reasonable changes to the information that schools and local education authorities have to publish. We welcome the fact that schools must now publish their own prospectuses, although the changes merely recognise universal practice. At least it is now a specific requirement. It is good also that a school should refer in its prospectus to the ethos and values that guide the school. The additional information that can be obtained from the key stage 1 and 3 results in the form prescribed by the regulations is useful. No one could object to what is said about truancy.

The publication of information about a school is also useful for the community that it serves. It is right that a community should know exactly what a school sets out to do and its achievements. It is good to see that independent schools and further education colleges come within the ambit of Statutory Instrument No. 1502.

Statutory Instrument No. 1503 requires schools to give information to LEAs about pupil performance and achievement, and about truancy, and LEAs to pass it on to the Secretary of State for Education, to be published in league tables for the 1993 school year. That raises important questions, and I hope that the Minister will answer some of them today. For example, he will be aware that he will not receive as much information on key stage 1 tests this year as he did last year. What estimate has the Department for Education made of the number of schools that have carried out the key stage 1 and 3 tests and, of those schools, of the number that will send the results to their LEAs to be forwarded to the Department?

4 There is much anecdotal information to suggest that not all schools have carried out those tests or, if they have, that they do not intend to pass the information to their LEAs for the Department. The Department has written to the chairmen of all school governing bodies, reminding them of their legal responsibilities. That could put them in an invidious position.

First, teachers may decide to undertake the tests but not to pass the results to the LEA, but they would nevertheless like to tell the governing body how the school is performing. In that case, they will have passed information to the governing body that they wanted to go no further. The governing body could therefore be in some difficulty in complying with or respecting the professional wishes of the teachers.

Secondly, if teachers put through the tests but decided to inform parents how their children are doing—a universal practice, regardless of whether the tests are taken—would the Department exert pressure on the governing bodies to obtain the results from the teachers, so that they could be passed on through the system?

Earlier this year, the Secretary of State missed a golden opportunity to take a step back from the rather headlong and precipitous approach to assessment, testing and the publication of tables. He should now accept what has been happening in schools and wait for the Dealing review before proceeding further. It is clear that almost no one in the teaching profession is against the use of assessment and testing, but teachers question the sort of testing and, more particularly, the incredible amount of extra work that is required after the tests are marked to complete the tables.

Next is the matter of truancy. It is important to know what is happening, but how many local education authorities have provided information on truancy rates in their areas? To some extent, the Department's publications on the consultation show surprising disagreements as to how truancy should be defined. Even within one LEA area, schools may apply different definitions of unauthorised absence. Greater clarification is required.

Bearing in mind that Statutory Instrument No. 1503 relates to information for league tables, what is expected of the tables? We do not believe that there is no need for comparative lists of league tables to be produced for schools at key stage 1 or any subsequent stage, unless they are related to far more sophisticated analysis than that of raw data. The publication of all league tables should stop until there is a fair and agreed way of presenting information to the public.

It is clear that raw results are completely inadequate for that purpose. It is widely recognised that different schools have different intakes. Some recognition needs to be made of the performance of pupils in schools where the intake is relatively good as against those where it is not as good. A pupil's achievement should be examined in relation to his or her position when he or she started at the school.

JPEG 5 Excellent research has been undertaken for some years at the Institute of Education at the university of London by Sally Thomas, Desmond Nuttall and Harvey Goldstein. In the first year, six local education authorities—Conservative and Labour—were involved. In the second, nine LEAs were involved.

The results are interesting: On average, pupils in girls' schools perform significantly better than those in mixed schools, but all-boys schools did not show a similar improvement. Pupils in voluntary schools, excluding Church of England schools, performed significantly better than those in county schools.

Also a higher proportion of pupils entitled to free school meals were systematically associated with lower performance, and higher percentages of pupil attendance on one selected day in the 1990–91 school year were systematically associated with higher performance, higher percentages of year 11 pupils staying on in the sixth form also were—perhaps as would expect—systematically associated with higher performance.

The proportion of pupils for whom English is a second language, school size as measured by the number of year 7 forms at entry, and school mobility do not appear to be systematically associated with lower performance.

All those findings have a bearing on the form in which the results of any one school should be published. It is to be regretted that within the statutory instruments, the Government are not prepared to publish, alongside the examination performance indicator, the number of pupils receiving free meals, the number whose mother tongue is not English, and the unit cost of education spending. The Government might have been expected to have been prepared to accept the latter, given their views on expenditure.

All that information is readily available and would usefully put the results from any school in context. It would also have shown exactly how a school was performing in relation to the type of child who entered the school. There is, for example, evidence that some of schools that score highly overall because they have a good intake come out badly when the job they have done for less able pupils is examined—taking account of pupils' performance when they started at the school and when they left.

Instead of having a fixation about comparative league tables, the Government should consider how schools provide for children with different abilities. The league tables do not show that. It would have been welcome if the statutory instruments provided for such matters to be taken into account.

The statutory instruments are an improvement on the previous position, but we remain intractably opposed to the publication of league tables based on raw school data on examination results. Such results cannot tell us anything useful about the way in which 6 the school serves all its pupils. I hope that the Government will take account of such matters and that I shall receive a positive reponse from the Minister.

: I am grateful for the opportunity to speak. I am not a member of the Committee, but I have the right to attend it because the regulations were referred to the Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments, which I have the dubious privilege of chairing. That Committee is responsible for reporting to the House on specified matters—for example, the unusual use of powers, provisions that are ultra vires and outside a Minister's powers, and so on. Its task is especially difficult because this Government are producing more statutory instruments than any other Government in history and it is not surprising that some of them are defective.

The Joint Committee believes that S.I. No. 1503 is defective in several respects and has so reported. Copies of its report are available in the Room and in the Vote Office.

I want to raise two points from that rather complicated report, in which we included, as we are required to do, the memoranda that the Department for Education provided on request.

Regulation 9(1) of S.I. No. 1503 states:

"The governing body of every maintained secondary school and every maintained middle school"

is required to provide information. Regulation 9(2) states that

"The information referred to in paragraph (1) is information".

The details of the information then follows and the paragraph concludes that the information is

"contained in a document provided to the governing body by the Secretary of State."

In statutory instruments, we make legislation that is as powerful as primary legislation—although it is not considered to be so and it tends to slide through the House without much scrutiny. Those who support the Jopling report and want to be home for tea and crumpets must consider the scrutiny of statutory instruments—

The Chairman
: Order. The hon. Gentleman is going wider than is legitimately permitted.

Mr. Cryer
: The scrutiny of statutory instruments should be raised. The Committee is undertaking that scrutiny and that process takes time. By and large, the regulations are affirmative—they are certainly being prayed against. Although on average about 120 prayers are tabled, fewer than one quarter are allocated time. Many statutory instruments may contain similar mistakes that go by the board.

Statutory instruments have the power of law and can bind people with criminal sanctions. However, such measures often go through unheeded.

JPEG 7 Regulation 9(2) explains what should be included in the document provided to the governing body by the Secretary of State and paragraph 9 of the memorandum from the Department of Education explains:

"The purpose and effect of including this wording is to emphasise the main aim of the provisions."

There is no prescriptive limitation on the amount of information that the Secretary of State can demand. Prescriptive limitation can be found in the primary legislation, but that does not provide carte blanche for the Secretary of State to say, "We want information to be published from governing bodies." That right is laid down by legislation that has passed through the House.

Regulation 9(2) refers to

"the results in public examinations and courses leading to vocational qualifications or units achieved by pupils".

Those words do not constitute a prescriptive requirement but emphasise the main aim of the provisions. That is not the purpose of the legislation. It is perfectly legitimate to include that emphasis in an explanatory note—that is what explanatory notes are for—but we are dealing with the content of regulations that contain words that bind people and impose legal obligations on them.

I should be grateful if the Minister would explain whether regulation 9(2) is regarded by the Department as the limited amount of information that can be requested, and whether the document that will be provided to the governing body by the Secretary of State will be based entirely and exclusively on the list in regulation 9(2).

Regulation 11 deals with the publication of local and national performance information relating to pupils aged 16, 17 and 18 by governing bodies. Regulation 11(2) states:

"The information referred to in paragraph (1) is information about the results in public examinations, courses of study for vocational qualifications and in the International Baccalaureate Diploma undertaken by pupils aged 16, 17 and 18 in schools and institutions within the further education sector".

Will the Minister explain how the further education sector crept in? Section 22 of the Education Reform Act 1988, on which the Minister relies for the powers to produce this regulation, makes no mention of further education. In the second memorandum, for which we asked, the Department states:

"The information about further education institutions is prescribed in exercise of section 22 of the Education Reform Act 1988"—

a dubious case for the powers—

"(as extended by section 24)".

Section 24 provides those powers as long as they are extended by order.

8 My information is—we have included this in our report—that no order has been made. Section 24 is not relied on in the first memorandum or in the statutory instrument, which gives rise to the suspicion that the Department was looking for something on which to base the power. It came up with section 24, which has not been exercised and applied. That is a careless way of dealing with a measure that—as I have emphasised to the Committee, and it knows full well—is as powerful as primary legislation that has gone through a convoluted and extended process in the House.

I hope that the Minister can respond suitably by explaining how the inclusion of further education is justified when there appears to be no use of the powers available to him to do so, because that is the basis on which we have reported the order to the House?
