    Mr. Clappison
    : We now come to new clause 39 which deals with the important right of first refusal.

    It may help the Committee if I describe the right of first refusal and how our proposals to curb abuses of that right fit in to the leasehold package.

    The right of first refusal is contained in part I of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1987. Many members of the —Committee will be familiar with its provisions, particularly those who were members of the Committee which considered the 1987 Bill.

    922 Part I of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1987 requires that freeholders wishing to sell the freehold of a block of flats must first offer it to the qualifying tenants, which include both leaseholders and regulated tenants. If the freeholder fails to do that, the tenants have a right to buy the freehold from the purchaser at the price that he paid.

    The Committee may recall that part I of the 1987 Act arose from the work of the Nugee committee, which reported in 1985. Although it constrained the previous rights of landlords to sell freely, the principle was accepted in many quarters. The British Property Federation, for example, had already given guidance to its members suggesting that they should offer the residents first refusal when disposing of blocks of flats.

    The assumption was, no doubt, that because the price was the same, the freeholder would be as happy to sell to the tenants as to a third party. During the Bill's passage through Parliament, no one suggested that landlords might not fulfil their obligations. The right to buy from the purchaser was perhaps thought to be a sufficient safeguard. In that context, penalties for failing to offer the right of first refusal were considered superfluous.

    In practice, many vendors have found that it is much cheaper to put the sale in the hands of agents and auctioneers than to commence negotiations with large numbers of tenants, expecially if a portfolio of properties is for sale. There have been some problems with the opportunity for tenants to exercise the right of first refusal, some of which have come to public attention and are well known.

    Although in some ways the abuse of the right of first refusal is a separate issue, there is a strong link with the problem of residential leasehold abuse by rogue landlords which the other new clauses in the group address. The Committee will be familiar with the problem, having considered it this morning. Those rogue landlords, otherwise known as ground rent feeders, obtain the blocks of flats which they exploit from landlords who have not offered their tenants the right of first refusal. Ensuring that the tenants can exercise their right to buy their ground rent is the first limb of our strategy to protect leaseholders, the other being the measures on service changes to help people who have suffered problems, such as those which we discussed this morning, as a result of the activities of bad landlords.

    I now come to the Government's proposals for dealing with the abuse of the right of first refusal. Subsection (2) of new clause 39 contains the main remedy, inserting new section 5A into the Landlord and Tenant Act 1987, to make it a criminal offence, without reasonable excuse, to make a sale falling within the scope of the Act without first having served the notices required by section 5. That will stop the institutions, portfolio owners, accountants and others who act as receivers for bankrupt companies from ignoring their obligations. They cannot afford to commit criminal offences if they want to retain their reputations. The Committee will also see that officers JPEG 923 of bodies corporate can be prosecuted if the offence has been committed with their consent, connivance or neglect.

    We have set the fine on conviction at level 4—a maximum of £2,500. For the institutions and accountants with reputations to preserve, the fact that failing to serve the notices will be a criminal offence will, no doubt, be a considerable deterrent. Receiving a criminal conviction is no light matter, especially for some of those who might be affected by the liability in question.

    Honest landlords who do not know the law should be protected by competent professional advice. Estate agents, auctioneers, solicitors and licensed conveyancers should check with the vendor that the right of first refusal has been offered before handling the sale of a freehold reversion in a block of flats.

    The Committee may wonder: what if an agent or solicitor knowingly or negligently connives at the commission of the offence? When the offence is discovered and the vendor is prosecuted, those who helped him are at risk of prosecution for aiding and abetting Such a conviction would be no light matter, in particular for those people's professional reputations. One imagines that prudent people would take care to avoid it happening to them.

    We have made provision for prosecutions to be brought by the local housing authority. That mirrors the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 which allows the local housing authority to prosecute service charge offences. It does not, however, prevent the police and the Crown Prosecution Service from bringing a prosecution for the offence in the normal way if it is considered appropriate. The aggrieved parties would also be able to bring a private prosecution.

    Subsection (2) of new clause 39 contains the principal remedy, but subsection (1) is also important. It attempts to address the problem caused by avoiding or failing to give the right of first refusal, by creating a new company through the transfer of a freehold to that company, which is associated with the landlord, and then selling shares in that associated company to a preferred purchaser.

    The exemption for sales to associated companies is necessary to allow groups to carry out corporate restructuring in an orderly way. There is, however, scope for the abuse which have just mentioned, and that must be addressed. It is not easy to think of a way round the problem which will be watertight, but we have, I believe, gone a significant way towards dealing with the most blatant abuses by the measure in subsection (1) of new clause 39. It means that usually the company to which the freehold has been sold must have been associated with the vendor for at least two years before a sale is not considered to be a relevant disposal. We hope that that substantially addresses the problem.

    Finally, subsection (3) of new clause 39 means that the measures that I have described will not be retrospective. It would clearly be a huge burden to attempt to unpick the large number of property transactions to which part I of the 1987 Act applies.

    924 I now move on to new clause 40. As I have just mentioned, tenants have a right to purchase the freehold or head lease from the new landlord if it has been sold over their heads. The problem is that very few tenants are aware of that and by the time they realise that they had rights, the time limit for exercising them has passed and there is nothing they can do. At present, tenants have either two months in which to serve a notice requesting information about the disposal, or three months to serve a purchase notice on the new landlord. Those time limits start from the time they discover that the transfer has taken place.

    Tenants will usually find out about the transfer when they receive a notice from the new landlord served under section 3 of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, which requires him to notify the tenants of the assignment and to give them his name and address. The penalty for failing to do that is a level 4 fine—a maximum of £2,500. Landlords, of course, rarely fail to serve section 3 notices because they need to tell the tenants where to pay the rent and service charges.

    It is important to consider that from the tenant's point of view. One day, a tenant may receive a letter from a company that he has never heard of saying that it has bought the freehold and that the rent should now be paid to the new landlord. Like most people, he knows nothing about the right of first refusal and will therefore not realise that he and his neighbours have a chance to buy the freehold at a fixed price. He will not realise that unless the tenants act within two or three months, their rights will be lost. Ground-rent feeders rely on that when they buy up freeholds at auction. They are then able to oppress the unsuspecting leaseholders with excessive service charges, as we have already discussed in some detail this morning.

    The solution to that problem is contained in new clause 40. When a new landlord has to serve a notice under section 3 of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, he must also serve a notice under new section 3A on all qualifying tenants if the sale was within the scope of the 1987 Act. Most important, the notice will say that the disposal was one to which the 1987 Act applied; that the tenant may have rights to obtain information and to acquire the new landlord's interest; that the time limits for action start once a requisite majority of tenants have received the notice; and what those time limits are. That serves to safeguard the tenants' rights and interest by giving them notice of their rights and interest at the appropriate time for them to consider exercising them. The penalty for failure to serve the notice is a level 4 fine.

    We hope that that procedure will provide that enough tenants will be able to take action once they discover that the freehold of their building has been sold over their heads. It also means that the breach of the primary duty to offer the right of first refusal will not go unnoticed. A notice under new clause 40 can only be served if an offence has been committed under new clause 39. The tenants will know that the guilty party is their previous landlord and can therefore take appropriate action. I commend the new clauses to the Committee.

Many tenants pay to their landlords security deposits, which are held as security against rent arrears, damage to property or the removal of furniture. That is a curious omission because usually when people hold money on behalf of another person there is a requirement to hold it in a separate account.     Let me deal first with valuation, I have some sympathy with my hon. Friend's intentions. We all want to make the enfranchisement procedure as simple and straightforward as possible, in order to encourage leaseholders to exercise those important rights. However, we must also ensure that the procedures are fair to all parties involved, and we need to remember—this is an important point for the Committee—that leasehold enfranchisement is essentially a form of compulsory purchase of the freehold interest by the leaseholders. The price paid must therefore represent a fair market value for that interest, which the current valuation method in the Act seeks to achieve.

    I turn now to the valuation procedures. There are essentially three elements in the price formula set out in schedule 6 to the 1993 Act. The first is an amount that reflects the open market value of the freehold. The second element is a share of the marriage value, which we have heard about. The marriage value reflects the special value generated by the merging of the leasehold and freehold interests, and is not reflected in the value of freeholds traded on the open market and not involving a purchase by the freeholder. The Act states that the freeholder should receive at least 50 per cent. of the marriage value and, as the Committee has heard, most leasehold valuation tribunals have been sharing the marriage value on a 50:50 basis.

    The marriage value concept was well established prior to the 1993 Act, in relation to leasehold enfranchisement, and the purpose is to share the benefit of enfranchisement on a reasonable basis between leaseholder and freeholder. It represents a genuine benefit.

    The third element in the price is any additional amount to cover "injurious affection" to the landlord.

    In the light of this description of the existing formula, I hope that members of the Committee will agree that in the search for simplicity, the alternative methods of valuation contained in the new clause would depart from an essentially fair system. The 962 amendment would not produce a price that was fair to all parties in every case. Although I listened with great interest to the arguments presented by my hon. Friend the Member for North-West Leicestershire, I cannot accept them.

    With reference to my hon. Friend's proposals on costs, I am afraid that I must take issue with him on two counts. First, as an important general principle, leaseholders should pay the reasonable costs of the landlord. That is because the enfranchisement rights contained in 1993 Act amount to compulsory acquisition. It is a long-established convention that in such cases the purchaser should pay any costs incurred by the vendor. In fact, however, the Act provides that the elements of the landlord's costs which the leaseholders must pay are quite restricted. Most importantly, they do not have to pay the landlord's costs involved in appearing before a leasehold valuation tribunal.

    There are protections for leaseholders with respect to costs. The leasehold valuation tribunal can be requested to determine the landlord's reasonable costs in respect of enfranchisement.

    The second issue is the suggestion in the amendment that in cases where there is a dispute that goes to the leasehold valuation tribunal, the tribunal should have the power to award costs, and that where the freeholder is subsequently found to have refused an offer by the tenants that was within 10 per cent. of the adjudicated amount, the award should be against the landlord. That, I believe, would not be desirable. The present safeguards are adequate and the system represents a fair and balanced approach to enfranchisement.
