    When my right hon. Friend the Member for Skipton and Ripon and I were in the Department, we recognised that, within reason, local authorities needed to know where they were as soon as possible. The crux of our amendment is that it attempts to persuade the Government to recognise that, and to ensure that local authorities have some understanding of where they are, so as to cut down surprises and give them the opportunity and sufficient time to take the detail into consideration in their budgets—which they should to some degree have done anyway, although many authorities leave this till the last minute. This would give them time to assess whether to introduce savings, change their method of approach, take some products off their wish list, use a bit of competitive tendering instead of just using the direct labour force alone. They could recognise a combination of quality of service and so on by these principles.

    Part of this derives from the anticipation, preempted by amendment No. 197, that there may be other types of authority—beacon authorities were mentioned—and a question arose about whether local authorities should take a few Blairisms or some political correctness from the back of the White Paper on beacon councils and follow that line as it would make for a more benevolent approach towards a particular local authority. Authorities need to know far enough in advance that they have this in their hands. Hence the amendment, which chooses 31 December as the date by which the Secretary of State must publish the statement of the set of principles.
