That the Committee have considered the draft Double Taxation Relief (Taxes on Estates of Deceased Persons and on Gifts) (United States of America) Order 1978.

The Chairman
: I think that it would be for the convenience of the Committee if with this we were to take the following:

Draft Double Taxation Relief (Taxes on Income) (Canada) Order 1978.

Draft Double Taxation Relief (Taxes on Estates of Deceased Persons and on Gifts) (Republic of South Africa) Order 1978.

Mr. Davies
: I shall deal, first, with the two conventions with the United States and South Africa because they both relate to taxes on estates. They represent a further instalment in the renegotiation of the estate duty agreements which was made necessary by the introduction of capital transfer tax. They both replace conventions made shortly after the war-As well as taking account of subsequent changes to the tax systems in the respective countries, these new conventions improve the reliefs available where property is doubly taxed. In particular, they apply to taxes on life-time gifts as well as to death duties.

Both coventions are based on the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development model death duties convention approved by the OECD Council in 1966. For many years our income tax conventions have been based on an OECD 4 model, but this is the first occasion on which an OECD model has been followed at all closely by this country in a death duties convention. It may be helpful if I first comment on the main provisions of the model.

Under the OECD model, the country in which the deceased is domiciled is, in general, given the sole right to tax the deceased person's estate, wherever it is situated. The country in which the deceased was not domiciled for the purposes of the convention may, however, tax immovable property and unincorporated businesses, or business branches, situated in its territory together with certain ships and aircraft; the country in which the deceased was domiciled giving relief for any resultant double taxation. To prevent these rules from being misused by someone changing his domicile shortly before death, the official commentary on the model provides in such a case for the country in which the deceased was previously domiciled to have a subsidiary right to tax the whole estate for 10 years after the time when he ceased to be domiciled there.

The model, of course, cannot take account of all the policies and laws of OECD member States and some members have reserved their position on particular articles. The Americans, for example, reserved the right to tax their own nationals, and in 1966 the United Kingdom reserved the right to tax shares in United Kingdom companies. The reservation still stands but, as will be apparent from the American convention, we recognise that there are arguments for giving up the right to tax shares if the other provisions of the convention are satisfactory. The OECD model does not cover gift taxes, but the Committee may like to know that a study group, on which the United Kingdom is represented, is looking at the possibility of extending the convention to cover gift taxes.

The main difference between the model convention and the estate duty conventions is that the latter divided up taxing rights by providing detailed situs rules whereas the OECD model allocates taxing rights directly. The model, unlike some of the estate duty conventions, also provides rules which ensure that a person is not treated as domiciled in both States.

JPEG 5 I would like now to comment briefly on the two estate duty conventions before the Committee.

The American convention follows the OECD model in giving sole taxation rights over most categories of property to the country in which the deceased or, in the case of a life-time gift, the transferor was domiciled. As an exception to this general principle, however, the other State may also tax its nationals in accordance with its domestic law, although it will have to give credit for tax charged by the State in which the deceased or transferor was domiciled.

As I explained, the Americans have always taken the line in OECD that they were not prepared to give up completely their right to tax their own nationals, and in these circumstances the Government did not consider that it would have been right for the United Kingdom to give up any charge it may have under domestic law on individuals who are domiciled in the United States under the convention but who are United Kingdom nationals. To have done so would have led to a lack of balance in the convention.

The effect of the convention is that anyone who is domiciled in the United States and who is not a United Kingdom national will be liable to capital transfer tax only in respect of any immovable property or unincorporated business which he owns in the United Kingdom. The Americans are, of course, similarly restricting their charges over individuals who are domiciled in the United Kingdom.

The Committee will note that this limited taxing right of the country where the individual was not domiciled does not extend to company shares in that country. The Americans particularly asked for this, and although it was a point on which the United Kingdom had reserved its position in the OECD, the other provisions of the convention adequately protect the United Kingdom's interests, and we took the view that this concession—which, of course, is reciprocal—could reasonably be made.

The convention provides rules for determining a person's domicile. He is treated as domiciled in the United Kingdom if he is domiciled here under our general law or deemed to be so domiciled under the provisions of section 45 of the Finance Act 1975. He is treated as domiciled in 6 the United States if he is regarded as "resident"—which, in effect, means domiciled—in the United States for estate or gift tax purposes, or if he is a national of the United States, and he had been resident there in the previous three years.

If, as a result of these rules, a person is domiciled in both States, the convention provides a number of "tie-break" rules. In the main, these follow the OECD model, with the important exception that a national of one State will not be treated as domiciled in the other until he has been resident there in seven out of the previous 10 years.

A number of OECD countries have a comprehensive tax charge—that is, they tax a person's world-wide estate—if a person is merely resident in that country. This means that where an employee is sent to work in such a country, the whole of his estate might become liable to death duties in that country. It seems to us reasonable, however, that there should be some minimum period of residence before a person's estate becomes fully liable to death duties. The "seven out of 10 rule" is unlikely to have any significant effect in relation to the United Kingdom and the United States, as normally neither country will treat people who are merely resident as domiciled for tax purposes. The rule will, however, serve to remove any doubt and it will serve as a useful precedent for other negotiations when the need for some such provision may well be greater.

The Committee may like to know that it will be our policy in other negotiations to seek to include a provision on these lines to help the British business community abroad. I hope that these provisions will be welcome to those whose jobs takes them outside the United Kingdom for a number of years.

Such individuals would normally remain domiciled in the United Kingdom, and without a provision on these lines the whole of their estates would be liable to tax not only in the United Kingdom but in some cases additionally in the country in which they work. It is far from being the case that United Kingdom taxes are invariably more onerous. For example, spouse transfers will be exempt here but normally taxable in the other country.

JPEG 7 The Committee will, I think, also welcome the provisions in article 8 of the convention which deal with deductions and, in particular, provide a number of improvements to the reliefs for spouse transfers. The article enables United Kingdom nationals who are not resident in the United States to obtain the benefit of the United States marital deduction, which would not otherwise be available to them. This could be of some benefit to the estate of a United Kingdom national where the United States has a charge under the convention. Under our own law, the only restriction on the exemption for transfers between spouses is where the transferor is domiciled in the United Kingdom and the transferee spouse is not. The exemption is then limited to £25,000.

The scope for improving on this without providing an opportunity for tax avoidance is not great. We have however agreed to make two minor improvements for United States taxpayers where our restricted exemption would otherwise apply, in return for the concession the United States have given United Kingdom nationals.

First, the article provides an exemption of up to 50 per cent, where the transferor was domiciled in or a national of the United States. This rule will be of advantage only where the transferor spouse was, under our domestic law, domiciled in the United Kingdom and the transferee spouse was domiciled abroad. Secondly, the article provides for a 50 per cent, exemption where a person domiciled in the United Kingdom leaves a life interest to his or her American spouse.

Article 8 also gives the executors of a United Kingdom national who was neither domiciled in nor a national of the United States the option of having the deceased person treated for United States tax purposes as domiciled in the United States, if that produces a lower United States tax charge. The Americans have a different rate scale for non-resident aliens, but this is combined with a lower threshold. Normally the non-resident alien scale would be more favourable than the rate scale applicable to residents and nationals, but there are circumstances where this is not so.

I now turn briefly to the South African convention. This follows the OECD 8 model more closely than the American convention. It gives the sole right to tax most items of property to the country in which the deceased, or, in the case of a life-time gift, the donor, was domiciled. The other country has the right to tax immovable property and unincorporated businesses situated in its territory, certain ships and aircraft, and company shares and debentures. The country in which the deceased or donor is not domiciled is given subsidiary rights, along the lines envisaged in the commentary to the OECD model, to tax other property where the deceased or donor has been domiciled in that country within 10 years before the transfer.

The rules for determining a person's domicile are similar to those in the American convention, and the Committee will note that again a minimum period of residence in one of the countries has been provided before a person is treated as domiciled there for the purposes of the convention. This provision is likely to have more importance in the South African context because South Africa imposes a comprehensive tax charge on persons who are ordinarily resident in South Africa.

I have not commented on the provisions of either convention which relate to settled property. These are highly technical and if there are any questions which members of the Committee have about these or other provisions, I shall try at least, to answer them.

In general these two conventions enable the United Kingdom to retain the right to tax settled property which is has under its own law where the settlor was domiciled in the United Kingdom. In other cases the conventions provide for the United Kingdom charge on settled property to be limited to the specific categories of property mentioned in the conventions. These provisions are consistent with the provisions for dealing with non-settled property.

Both conventions take account of the changes brought about by the introduction of capital transfer tax. In a number of aspects they improve the reliefs currently available for the relief of double taxation, and I commend the conventions to the Committee.

Perhaps I could now turn to deal briefly with the Canadian double taxa- JPEG 9 tion convention which, of course, is an order relating in the main to income. This new convention replaces the existing agreement with Canada dating from 1966. The new convention takes account of changes in each country's tax legislation since the 1966 agreement, and the language is updated to bring it into line with the new model convention published in 1977 by the OECD. I do not propose to give a detailed summary of all the provisions of this order, but I should like to mention briefly the important question of the tax treatment of dividends.

The treatment proposed for portfolio dividends flowing from the United Kingdom in this order follows that provided for by the dividends articles in other orders which have recently come before the Committee. A tax credit in respect of dividends paid by a United Kingdom company will be given to Canadian portfolio investors, by which I mean individuals who are resident in Canada and companies resident there which control less than 10 per cent, of the voting power in the United Kingdom company paying the dividend. The tax credit is of the same amount as that given to individuals resident in the United Kingdom.

The dividends paid to Canadian investors of this class will be subject to a withholding tax not exceeding 15 per cent, of the aggregate amount of the dividend and the tax credit paid. However, for Canadian direct investors, that is, companies holding more than 10 per cent, of the voting power in the United Kingdom paying company, no part of the United Kingdom tax credit is payable and no United Kingdom withholding tax is charged. For dividends flowing to United Kingdom investors—direct and portfolio—from Canada, as under the existing convention, the rate of Canadian withholding tax is not to exceed 15 per cent.

It will be seen that the arrangements concerning direct investors differ from those in other recent conventions or protocols in that the Canadian direct investor is not given half of the tax credit to which portfolio investors are entitled. Canada was not prepared to reduce its treaty withholding tax to direct investors resident in the United Kingdom below 15 per cent, and, for that reason, the United Kingdom was unable to offer its half-tax credit concession in those circumstances.

10 I should also like to mention certain other points of difference between the new convention arrangements and the existing convention arrangements. Under the interest article, reciprocal exemption from tax in the State of source of the interest is now offered if the loan or credit is made, guaranteed or insured respectively by the Canadian Export Development Corporation or the United Kingdom Export Credits Guarantee Department. All interest paid to United Kingdom residents in respect of bonds, debentures and similar obligations by the Government of Canada or by political subdivisions or local authorities is to be exempt from Canadian tax. Under the revised capital gains article, gains made from the disposal of rights and licences and associated assets connected with the exploration and extraction of oil and gas will now be taxable by the State in which the oil and gas is situated. This is important in relation to the United Kingdom oil and gas resources and the associated licensing agreements. The convention conforms to the generally accepted international pattern. I consider it to be fair and properly balanced, and I commend it to the Committee.

If there are any questions on any of the conventions, I shall do my best to answer them.

JPEG 19 The hon. and learned Gentleman then asked, in relation to article 8(2) of the United States convention, about marital deduction and the scope for avoidance. The problem from the United Kingdom aspect arises from the fact that if one gives the full spouse exemption to transfers between United Kingdom domiciled transferors and non-United Kingdom domiciled transferees, it is possible for the asset to get completely outside the capital transfer tax net. That exemption is dependent to some extent on the person not becoming absolutely entitled to the asset. Once a person becomes absolutely entitled to the asset, the exemption is withdrawn retrospectively.

The hon. and learned Gentleman asked how one reconciled the United States' right to tax its own nationals with the domicile provision. The basic principle is that domicile gives rise to a tax charge. The United States has always taxed nationals on a world-wide basis for income tax purposes as well. In view of its insistence on keeping that right, we have said that we wish to tax our nationals, too, on the same basis. If the primary taxing right is on domicile, then there is a subsidiary right in relation to the national. Of course, the country that taxes on a national basis has to give credit to the country that taxes on a domicile basis, because the domicile is the primary right of taxation—subject, of course, to immovable property and some other properties.

The term "United States" does not include Puerto Rico, the Virgin Islands and Guam. I do not think that it is customary to include United Kingdom dependencies. The United States wishes to exclude these three dependencies.

I turn to article 17, on pensions. Pensions are normally taxed by the resident country and not the source country. The Canadians have, apparently, very generous pension arrangements whereby they give a considerable tax deduction for contributions made. They feel that because of these arrangements, which are more generous than those in other countries, they should retain some right to tax the pension when it comes out at the other end. We negotiated with them that over and above £5,000, the source country—in this case, Canada—would have the right to tax, but that total tax would be limited to the tax that would be payable if the resident country taxed the pension. That does not mean that any more tax is paid, but the Canadians get a cut of the tax which they would not otherwise receive because of their pension provisions. We thought that to be only fair.

It is not a case of getting at them, but they are in a different position from that of other people in that their property is tangible, movable property which they can take anywhere in the world and this presents difficulties as regards taxation.
