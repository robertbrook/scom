Draft Council Tax Benefit (General)

Amendment Regulations

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. Alistair Burt)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Draft Council Tax Benefit (General) Amendment Regulations 1993.

The Chairman
: With the agreement of the Committee, we shall debate also the draft Council Tax Benefit (Permitted Total) Order 1993.

Mr. Burt
: The regulations which I commend to the Committee, introduce amendments to existing council tax benefit regulations.

Hon. Members will recall that the House carefully and comprehensively debated the main council tax benefit regulations last year. The amending regulations will change the earlier regulations in a small, but mainly beneficial way. A copy of the regulations has been put before the Social Security Advisory Committee and the local authority associations have been formally consulted. I am, as ever, grateful for their comments.

The amending regulations are necessary for three reasons.

First, they will ensure that the council tax benefit regulations are aligned with other income-related benefits. The Government's objective is for common rules to apply across benefits wherever practicable. Many of the amendments are therefore being introduced to reflect changes made to other income-related benefits last year, or those due to come into effect in those benefits from April. Secondly, they will correct minor, often typographical, mistakes.

Thirdly, they are a response to concerns expressed by some local authorities and other interested bodies that a small number of provisions require clarification. Our policy on council tax benefit has never been in doubt; the amendments merely ensure the fulfilment of that policy. Many of the changes are technical so I shall not give the committee a full account of them. As an example, however, we have connected a wrong cross-reference in regulation 10 by changing the letter (a) to (b).

I shall explain in greater detail other changes which I am sure that the Committee will find of interest. We are amending regulations to ensure that payments to foster parents, which are already ignored as an income in council tax benefit, canot be treated as self-employed earnings. That beneficial change ensures that people gain the full benefit of fostering fees. Without the amendment, people undertaking fostering duties might be regarded by local authorities as engaged in self-employment and suffer a reduction in benefit.

4 The regulations increase the amount that can be disregarded from a student's income towards the cost of books from £257 to £267 a year, in line with other income-related benefits.

The Committee will welcome the introduction of a new earnings disregard for carers. From April 1993, all carers who are entitled to the carer premium in any income-related benefits, including council tax benefit, will be entitled to the £15 disregard on their earnings. A similar amendment will be made to all income-related benefits and will help more than 2,000 people who combine work with providing carer. It will give further encouragement to carers who would like to undertake extra work alongside their caring commitment, but who feel unable to do so. The improvement is additional to the carer premium that will be worth £11.95 a week from April, and is payable to carers whether or not they work.

The regulations provide for the removal of the outgoing maintenance disregard of any maintenance payment made by people receiving council tax benefit. At present, a rule operates, in only community charge benefit and housing benefit, whereby the amount of income taken into account for benefit purposes can be reduced by the amount of maintenance paid. That will normally result in the award of higher benefits than would otherwise have been paid. That means that effectively the tax payer is helping to subsidise maintenance payments regardless of the recipient's income. The introduction of the Child Support Agency in April will remove that anomaly. The Agency will gradually assume responsibility for assessing child maintenance according to a standard formula. That will ensure adequate maintenance for the support of children while protecting the needs of the absent parent and any family. Until the Agency takes over, people will be advised by local authorities that they may seek to vary their maintenance payments from April either by application to the courts or by voluntary agreement in the usual way. That step will enable people to make the necessary arrangements, if they wish, before the change takes effect. The amendment will ensure consistent treatment of outgoing maintenance payments in council tax benefit, housing benefit and other income-related benefits, from April.

In line with changes already made to the housing benefit regulations last October, we are amending the way in which the income of a non-dependant and second adult are calculated for council tax benefit purposes. That change ensures a full disregard of payments from the Macfarlane Trusts and the special fund set up to help haemophiliacs and other people who have tragically contracted the HIV virus through NHS infected blood products. Hon. Members will know that those payments are already fully disregarded in respect of benefit recipients and members of their families. The proposal will ensure that non-dependants and second adults are treated similarly. Because of the special nature of the payments, hon. Members will be pleased to hear that we are also protecting the confidentiality of beneficiaries by providing that payments do not have to be declared to the local authority.

Another change, which I am sure will be welcomed, is a full disregard of guardian's allowance of £10.95 a week from April. This will help about 2,000 people across the income-related benefits with the costs of bringing up an orphan. Therefore, people on income-related benefits will JPEG 5 gain the same help from guardian's allowance as those people who have higher incomes and do not have to claim benefits.

As I said before, several changes are being made to guarantee that our policy intention is achieved. For example, the regulations provide that people receiving the higher pensioner premium under community charge benefit will continue to do so on the transition to council tax benefit. We are also ensuring that belated awards of mobility allowance paid as a lump-sum will not affect benefit entitlement for a period of 52 weeks from when they are awarded. I am sure that all of those improvements will be very welcome for people on benefit.

I shall now deal with the Council Tax Benefit (Permitted Total) Order. The main Council Tax Benefit (General) Regulations provide local authorities with a discretionary power to make additional payments of council tax benefit when they consider the circumstances of the claimant to be exceptional. Under the provision, local authorities can increase the amount of benefit paid in particular cases, but not so as to exceed 100 per cent. of the maximum council tax benefit, or 25 per cent. of second adult rebate.

The Secretary of State is required, by primary legislation, to set a ceiling, or a permitted total, on the amount of discretionary payments that an authority may make. The Council Tax Benefit (Permitted Total) Order fulfils this requirement.

The order sets the ceiling for discretionary payments at 0.1 per cent. of the total amount of council tax benefit that the authority pays out in the course of the year. I should point out that that ceiling is in line with the limit that applies currently in housing benefit and community charge benefit and has been agreed with local authority associations, following consultation. It is a welcome addition to local authorities' spending powers, which, for individual authorities, can amount to several thousand pounds. They could be used, for example, to help a person who has spent extra money on having to visit a sick relative.

As I explained earlier, the regulations and the order make minor changes, most of which are entirely beneficial. They will assist thousands of people who will be liable to pay council tax. I commend them to the Committee.

I am unclear about whether such a disparity exists. I shall respond to the hon. Gentleman by letter about that highly technical matter.

The hon. Member for Greenwich made a point about eligible rent. The change is beneficial. Payments made to a third party, which are treated as the claimant's income or capital, and used for day-to-day living expenses, including rent; and regular charitable and voluntary payments, which are intended or used for the same day-to-day expenses, are taken into account, subject to the £10 disregard. In housing benefit and community charge benefit, it was always intended that rent should refer to the rent for which housing benefit is payable; that is the net rent after all deductions have been made for service charges, high rents, water charges and non-dependant deductions. The regulations currently refer to "eligible rent", which means the net rent before any deduction is made for non-dependants. The amendment to the regulations, which is reflected in the housing benefit regulations, will ensure that the rent after deductions for non-dependants is the amount that is taken into account.

I do not believe that the ceiling is unnaturally low. The permitted total has applied in housing benefit circumstances since April 1988, when it replaced a limit of 10 per cent. of total local authority benefit expenditure. The reduction in the permitted total reflected the removal of local schemes provision for disregarding all or some of war disablement or war widows pensions by local authorities. 14 The permitted total should be sufficient to allow local authorities to use it carefully to help many people. The provision has not been widely known. It is possible that people who have found themselves in difficulty were unaware of the discretionary power of local authorities.

In future, local authorities may be prepared to say more about the power because people may find themselves attracted to it, and could receive assistance.

On the hon. Gentleman's point about contracting out, all decisions on benefits have to be made by the authority, not a contractor. At present, the Government encourage authorities that wish to do so to consider contracting out of the administration procedure for housing benefit but not out of the decision-making procedure. In a speech in Weston-super-Mare the other week, I made it clear that the Government believe that the decision-making power remains entirely with the authority.

Adjudication, including adjudication on payments under the permitted total order, will be a matter for the adjudication officer.

Mr. Burt
