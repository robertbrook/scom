Hill Livestock (Compensatory Allowances) (Amendment) Regulations 1996

4.30 pm

Mr. Paul Tyler
(North Cornwall)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Hill Livestock (Compensatory Allowances) (Amendment) Regulations 1996 (S.I. 1996, No. 27).

I welcome sitting under your chairmanship, Mr. Stewart, and hope that during the debate there will be a reflection of the extreme importance of the subject to hill farmers in Scotland. I know that their concerns are close to your heart.

In the view of many Members—from all parties and from all parts of the country—hill farming support is the virility test of the sincerity of Ministers in their White Papers. There have been White Papers for Scotland and for England and I understand that the White Paper for Wales is to be published today or tomorrow. I expect to see in them a reflection of the importance that is attached to this type of support for this type of farming.

Some members of the Committee might not be entirely conversant with the economic problems of hill farming, so I shall sketch their importance. The less favoured areas cover 53 per cent. of the agricultural area of the United Kingdom and contain almost 70 per cent. of its beef cows and more than 60 per cent. of its sheep flock. This is not a minority or insignificant fringe issue.

Within the LFAs, agriculture provides vital social, environmental and economic elements. Farming generates employment, both with the purchase of inputs and the employment of labour. Farmers are essential to the local economy in a way that, perhaps, they are not in lowland and other areas. The environment that they create is crucial in attracting visitors—it is vital to the tourism industry.

Incomes for the sector recovered between 1991 and 1993, but fell sharply in 1994. In 1992 and 1993, the Government used the partial recovery in farm incomes generally as a justification for cutting support for hill livestock farmers. The real value of hill livestock compensatory allowances, which the Government pay on beef cows and breeding sheep, was reduced. When farm incomes fell in 1994, there was no commensurate increase in HLCAs to compensate. It is a classic case of "heads we win, tails you lose".

Overall support for hill and upland farmers was reduced by between 6 per cent. and 9 per cent. on the 1993 level. In 1993–94, more than 40 per cent. of the full-time hill and upland farmers in England had an annual farm income of fewer than £10,000. In Scotland, as you, Mr. Stewart, will know, the 4 equivalent figure was almost 30 per cent. and in Wales more than 44 per cent. They are extraordinarily low figures for an extraordinarily large number of people.

In 1994, LFA farm incomes fell substantially, but the Government still froze the HCLAs to be paid in 1995. A small recovery in income is forecast for the current year, but LFA farm incomes are not expected to recover to 1992 or 1993 levels. In such circumstances, there is widespread support from the National Farmers Union of England and Wales, the National Farmers Union of Scotland and the Hill Farming Initiative for a return to the 1992 level.

At this juncture, it is important to enter a particular plea for those hill farmers who have been badly hit in the past 12 months by a combination of late frosts in 1995; a shortage of fodder, which made it expensive; set-aside already reducing the amount of hay and silage available; animals being unable to lay on the same amount of fat as they would in a better spring or during the summer; and the drought, which has affected the profitability and development of stock. If there is a long, cold spring in 1996 in those areas—some have recently been badly affected by the weather—those problems will continue.

Against that background, Ministers have rightly been stating in their White Papers that they want to decouple support systems. They want to encourage environmentally friendly livestock management in a way that assists vulnerable areas with fragile economies, which desperately need a support system that encourages wider environmental, employment and social objectives.

HCLAs have been precisely targeted, cost-effective and universally popular. They have been an effective form of investment in the landscape of the uplands. Yet they are withering away before our eyes because Ministers have not had sufficient guts to stand up to the Treasury axe. I was struck by the comments made a few days ago by the retiring president of the National Farmers Union for Scotland, John Ross. He said that the major disappointment of his presidency was that, during that period, the real level of HLCAs had dropped. His dismay is echoed by farming leaders throughout England and Wales. Those Members who have met the Hill Farming Initiative will know how important HLCAs are to the economies of all upland areas.

Since our debates in the autumn, the Minister has backtracked in two important respects. First, despite his eloquent attempt to persuade us that the number of eligible ewes in disadvantaged areas had to be reduced, he subsequently backtracked. I pay credit to him for listening to the representations made by people throughout the country.

The Minister for Rural Affairs (Mr. Tim Boswell)
: It would be fair to put on the record that any backtracking reflected the fact that I had indicated to the Committee that rates were being reduced as a precaution and to preserve the position for our consideration. We then considered the matter and came to the conclusion that the hon. Gentleman is about to acknowledge.

Mr. Tyler
: I acknowledge that, but the Minister's argument in support of that reduction was eloquent. I have in a letter a far fuller version than that which he expressed in Committee. I do not wish to embarrass the Minister by paying credit to his extremely well-argued case for the change, but his eloquence gives the assurance that he has just suggested a slight tinge of déjà vue.

Had that change been pushed through, there would have been a potential loss of £7.32 per hectare for those farmers concerned. That would have given the lie to the assurance that the Minister gave us at the time that HLCAs were not being cut.

The second issue that arose in that Committee was where we came in the league table in Europe. I do not have up-to-date figures because the Ministry has been unable to provide them, so I am not aware of our position in relation to the three new admissions to the European Union. In 1994, we paid the second lowest level of HCLA in Europe—47 ecu per livestock unit. That is very low, and many of our competitors give far greater support in other areas.

In real terms, farm incomes in less favoured areas have not returned to the levels of 1992–93, let alone those of the 1988–90 period. Since the HLCA values were considered appropriate in 1992, it is hard to find anybody who will produce a logical explanation for allowing the subsequent cuts to stand. Nobody outside the Ministry accepts that.

In the longer term, there will have to be improvements and an adjustment. The mechanism is by no means perfect. The wildlife trusts and farming unions would like to move HLCAs further away from coupling and to give them an area basis, taking into account real farm incomes. By contrast, it is now generally accepted that the moorland scheme has proved an abject failure. The take-up has been pathetic and even the latest announced changes are unlikely to make a dramatic change. I hope that the Minister will be able to show that he attaches a long-term future to the HLCAs. The time has come to seek to breathe new life into HLCAs so that we can have a new life in the hills.

In every part of the United Kingdom, this debate is being seen as a test case for the sincerity of the rural White Paper. Will the Government change their attitude in taking away support from the less efficient forms of investment and bringing it back to a form of investment that is widely recognised to have huge environmental, social and employment advantages? I hope that the Minister will be more encouraging than he has been on previous occasions.
