Social Security (Adjudication) and Child Support Amendment (No. 2) Regulations 1996

10.30 am

Ms Liz Lynne
(Rochdale)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Social Security (Adjudication) and Child Support Amendment (No. 2) Regulations 1996 (S.I. 1996, No. 2450).

I welcome you, Mr. Thompson, to the Chair.

I am against the regulations for several reasons. From 21 October, five changes have been brought into the tribunal procedure. Before I go into detail, however, about the changes, I wish to talk about the chaos brought about by their implementation. They require an appeal special form, which is not currently available in a number of areas. I understand from the citizens advice bureaux that such forms will not be available until the new year in some parts of the country, and that people should submit their own written applications. That will lead to confusion, delays and poor implementation of the procedure.

Oldham Benefits Agency contacted the local citizens advice bureau. It said that one of its clients had sent a letter of appeal about incapacity benefit, but that that was not enough. The client wanted to point out that he should have more points because of his poor health. The letter has now been referred to an independent tribunal.

The changes have been rushed through. Why the speed? The regulations were laid before the House on 27 September, while Parliament was in recess. It is absolutely scandalous that they have been implemented one month later, before Members of Parliament have had a chance to consider them. That action has undermined the Government's wider consultation on approving the decision-making and appeal process of the social security system, which ended 18 October. There was hardly enough time to assess the Government's own consultation process.

As Parliament rose for the recess, the Government announced their long-term plans for reforming the appeals tribunals and, as I said, the regulations were laid before the House on 27 September. They will abolish the three-person appeal tribunal with a legally qualified chairman. Instead, one person—usually not a lawyer—will sit. Why bother with consultation if the Minister is pushing through the changes?

I turn now specifically to the regulations and to the harm that they will cause. It is less likely that claimants will attend appeal hearings or be represented at such hearings. The Government's statistics claim that it is three times more likely that claimants will win an 4 appeal if they turn up at the tribunal and if they are represented. Figures show that 47 per cent. of unaccompanied claimants are successful, while only 20 per cent. of non-attenders are successful. That figure increases to 67 per cent. in cases of people who both attend appeals and are represented.

Non-attendance is particularly damaging in incapacity benefit hearings. Nearly half of the appeals are successful, but that is mainly because of the simplistic nature of the all-work test for incapacity benefit and because of the report of a Benefits Agency doctor.

The refusal of benefit is often based on superficial evidence, which is why such claimants win on appeal. If a claimant does not turn up for an incapacity benefit hearing, the doctor's report is simply rubber stamped. People will lose out because of the changes. The main people who do so are those who are not highly educated or who are unassertive. Such people are not necessarily aware of the advantages of an oral appeal.

Even those who request an oral appeal now risk being denied justice because of the short period of notice.

Rochdale citizens advice bureau wrote to me, saying that imposing five days' notice would put it under more pressure by reducing the number of people who could be represented. It will be difficult for citizens advice bureaux and other agencies to prepare appeals within five days. The letter states:

"the notice given to my advisers of hearing dates is insufficient now—5 working days, which effectively means that few of our clients will be represented, or even have a written submission prepared for them."

We all know of the workload imposed on citizens advice bureaux; they cannot possibly prepare submissions in five days for so many appeal hearings. The bureaux are already over-stretched trying to prepare appeals, particularly in cases involving incapacity benefit. Imposing such a short period of notice will put them under untold pressure; and, of course, if claimants have no representation, their appeals are less likely to be granted.

A further reason for opposing the regulations is that many appeals for incapacity benefit are the result of mistakes or oversights by the Benefits Agency or the Child Support Agency. The agencies must first concentrate on getting things right. The Government know that reviews are costly. They want to cut social security benefits. Why can they not get it right first time round, rather than having to create a costly appeals procedure? It adds extra costs to income support, incapacity benefit, child support and a range of other benefits. The Government are avoiding the central issue, which is why I prayed against the regulations. They will lead to vulnerable people being denied benefit, which is why they should be opposed.

10.37 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. Roger Evans)
: It is quite clear from what has been said by the hon. Member for JPEG 5 Rochdale (Ms Lynne) that there has been considerable misunderstanding about why the regulations were introduced and what they seek to achieve.

The Government did, indeed, introduce a Green Paper—nowadays, Green Papers come in colours other than green, such is the improvement in colour printing. As the hon. Lady said, the consultation paper, "Improving Decision Making and Appeals in Social Security", is a radical document. If it is to be implemented, it will in due course require primary legislation. However, that is a matter wholly different from what we are discussing this morning.

To avoid further misunderstandings, we made it clear at paragraph 5.8 of the Green Paper that the short-term changes that we are discussing this morning would be proceeded with separately, as they are part and parcel of our good housekeeping—modest, small, practical measures to improve the operation of the independent tribunal service. Those changes are necessary, in a modest way, to stop the system being messed around and becoming less efficient.

The key issue was not mentioned by the hon. Lady; she showed no concern for those who are kept waiting—in the case of appeals to the social security tribunal, waiting almost invariably without benefit. The Government are very concerned that the average delay is currently 26 weeks. That delay has been marginally reduced, but people still have to wait for about six months without benefit before an appeal can be heard. These reforms are directed to reducing inefficiencies; they have no bearing on whether justice is being done, but are designed to ensure that the scheme operates properly.

Ms Lynne
: Everyone in the Committee is aware of the delays suffered by people wishing to make an appeal, but that is not what we are discussing. The Minister must accept that five days is not long enough for people to prepare a case.

Mr. Evans
: If the hon. Lady reads the regulations, she will see that it is actually seven days. [HON. MEMBERS: "Come on."] It may be that some Opposition Members have no experience whatever of how courts and administrative tribunals operate.

In practice, people receive much more than the bare minimum notice. The Benefits Agency tells people in advance the date of the appeal. If that date is inconvenient, it may be renegotiated and refixed. What has become intolerable is that when the parties are content that the case proceed, there is a shackle in the rules that prevents it from going ahead on a particular day. The seven-day period is eminently sensible and practical and, in the context of the practice of courts and tribunals generally, it is relatively generous. It does not cause problems.

Mrs. Maria Fyfe
(Glasgow, Maryhill)
: Is the Minister seriously telling us that someone who may have less than average mental capacity, who may not have English as a first language and who may be ill educated will have to enter into negotiations with an officer of the Department of Social Security, who will be seen as having authority over that person?

6

Mr. Evans
: That is a coloured and exaggerated example, which does not bear any resemblance to what goes on. If people, whatever their circumstances, want their appeal to be heard on a different date, they simply have to ask. Normally, the request is granted. We must not have an unnecessary shackle.

The Government are dealing with something that does not happen in the court system. Tribunals were listing five cases a morning, but people were not turning up or there were objections to the case going ahead even though there was a tribunal vacant. The people who suffered were those in the queue; that is the consideration that seems to have gone astray in the objections of the hon. Member for Rochdale.

Ms. Lynne
: The Minister says that the period is seven days. Is he including weekends? If so, he obviously expects the citizens advice bureaux and other advice-giving agencies to work through the weekend.

Mr. Evans
: I am not suggesting that. It is not unreasonable to say on a Monday that the appeal hearing will be on the following Monday unless a good reason is given why it should not proceed then. I find it remarkable that that should be a matter of controversy.

Let me now deal with the way in which independent tribunals were giving decisions. I visited the independent tribunal service in Cardiff and spoke to a helpful full-time chairman, who explained the process to me. In every case there, the chairman properly took a full manuscript note of the evidence, wrote out his judgment in longhand and dictated it to a typist. About a week later, the document returned; he proofread and signed it. There were exceptional cases when chairmen helpfully wrote out their judgments there and then so that the appellant could get benefit quickly, but the elaborate procedure I described occurred in almost every case, which we estimate added an extra two weeks' delay before the appellants got their decision. Most of those long documents were never read by anyone for any serious purpose—less than 2 per cent. were the subject of appeal to the commissioners. In every straightforward case, the tribunal chairman had much homework to do and much paperwork to generate, for no practical purpose.

I took the view that that was simply intolerable. The ordinary county court judge sitting in the country on the first Monday of a month gives 20 or 30 oral judgments there and then. We have introduced, through the regulations, a series of practical, effective options so that the tribunal can decide which course is most suitable for the particular case before it.

First, like the county court judge, the tribunal will have the option to give an oral judgment there and then, which will contain not only the decision but the reasons for it. Some chairmen will want to do that and others will not. The second option applies where the case is more complicated. The chairman will be able to give a short summary of the decision in writing there and then; there will probably be a short adjournment in his room while the bones of the decision are handed JPEG 7 down in writing so that the appellant knows the result, with an option that the full reasons can be supplied later if the appellant requests them.

The third option is the present procedure, namely, that a full reserved judgment—both the conclusion and the reasons—can be given later.

That is a practical and effective way of providing justice without generating vast amounts of idle paperwork. Oral procedure will of course be recorded, so there will be no need to type it up unless that is requested. I suggest to the hon. Member for Rochdale that that better accords with sensible administration and with the practical way in which other courts and tribunals operate. It is perhaps surprising that this independent tribunal service did not have those powers earlier.

Ms Lynne
: Returning to the five-day period, can the Minister explain why the Council on Tribunals opposed the reduction in the notice period from 10 to five days, and recommended a period of 21 days?

Mr. Evans
: I gave way to the hon. Lady out of indulgence, because I thought that she was listening and had a serious point to make about what I had just told the Committee. I shall return to her other point when I reach the appropriate part of my speech.

So the first change is in the method of giving judgment. It is not a trifling change. It will save a couple of weeks and help the independent tribunal service to deal with matters more expeditiously.

The second change involves oral hearings. Nobody suggests that people who want an oral hearing should not have an absolute right to one. That will remain the position under these rules. People who want an oral hearing should ask for one, and they have an absolute right to one. I hear what the hon. Lady says. People who turn up are likely to do better. There is a 19th century French proverb, "He who is absent loses". Anyone who has experience at the Bar, as I have, will immediately appreciate the fact that people who turn up and are properly represented tend to do rather better than those who do not turn up.

Mr. Alan Howarth
(Stratford-on-Avon)
: Why have the Government ignored the advice given by the Council on Tribunals that it is important to explain to appellants that it is desirable and in their interests to seek an oral hearing in information provided to them?

Mr. Evans
: I am not sure that that is correct. The position on oral hearings is simple: if people want one, under the rules before the Committee they can have one. It is unacceptable to have cases listed for oral hearings to which people do not turn up—to which they never intended to turn up—resulting in the tribunal wasting a morning slot. Tribunals try to organise a sensible list that keeps waiting to a minimum for everybody involved. People may not want an oral hearing for various reasons. They may be ill-advised; they may have been advised that they do 8 not have much of a case and may simply be going through a paper exercise. But if they want an oral hearing, they can have one.

It is not unreasonable to make it incumbent on the appellant to ask for an oral hearing. At the same time, we will protect the tribunal from being mucked about by appellants who never intended to turn up, but simply wanted to block the queue in an unacceptable way.

Mrs. Fyfe
: Does the Minister have with him a form that is handed to appellants to explain that they may appeal either orally or in writing, and to point out that it is to their advantage to turn up for an oral hearing? Do they have such a form handed to them that is written in plain English? If so, can we see it now?

Mr. Evans
: I do not have the final leaflet, which will be issued presently, but the Benefits Agency has been instructed to give full guidance on how the system will operate under the rules.

The Council on Tribunals did not want to do quite so much as the hon. Member for Stratford-on-Avon (Mr. Howarth) suggested. It wanted us to confirm that there was an absolute right to an oral hearing. We have happily confirmed that, because it is the case.

Mr. Alan Howarth
: Is the Minister telling the Committee that the Council on Tribunals did not recommend to the Government that appellants should be advised that it was likely to be to their advantage to have an oral hearing?

Mr. Evans
: I will read the correspondence from the Council on Tribunals again to make sure that I have got the position right. I have the correspondence in my bundle; if I may, I will deal with that later.

There are a number of other matters that were not referred to by the hon. Member for Rochdale, but which are also important. Regulation 7 makes it easier for an appellant to withdraw his appeal, thus partially remedying the situation of appeals proceeding to a full hearing even when the appellant has decided not to appeal—another piece of administrative nonsense that simply clogged the list with cases that would never be properly pursued.

Regulation 6 frees chairmen from basic interlocutory work, such as agreeing to postponement requests, by making it possible for that work to be done by clerks—a measure that directs manpower more constructively. Regulations 3 and 5(3) give tribunal chairmen strengthened powers of direction to permit speedier disposal of cases where there is a lack of co-operation, where unreasonable demands are being placed on the organisation, or where the case lacks merit. Such directions will also make appellants' responsibilities clear to them and include arrangements for expeditious handling of the case. As in any other court or tribunal system, the tribunal will have proper powers to deal quickly with cases where the appellant has not been sensible or has mucked the tribunal around and to give direction where there is a failure of compliance. I stress that it is only when an appellant fails to comply with the direction that anything worse will happen to him.

JPEG 9 The reasons for an appeal are important. It is almost inconceivable that a decision could be appealed against without an appellant stating what that decision is or without giving an indication of the basis of the appeal. If at least basic requests are not made of the appellant, the system gets clogged up with people appealing simply because they do not like the original decision.

The previous leaflet made no mystery about giving potential appellants a certain amount of guidance. We have built on that. We are not talking about a formal legal pleading whereby if one does not get the technicalities right, one's case will be struck out. The purpose of requesting information is to give the tribunal an indication of what is being appealed against and the basis of the sense of injustice. There are cases, for example, where the receipt of benefit is dependent on age and the appellant is over or below that age; if that were the only point of appeal, it would help everyone concerned and speed matters up if that could be identified at an early stage. Such a case could be disposed of quickly without taking its 40 or 45 minutes in the morning list and thereby stopping other people's cases from being dealt with.

The amount of information required is subject to the good sense and judgment of the tribunal. Whatever the type of appellant, we cannot allow every aggrieved person—and people are understandably aggrieved when what they have asked for its denied them—the right to an appeal process without having the basis of their unhappiness identified or without a form of check being introduced. I am sure that the tribunal chairman will operate the powers of direction fairly and sensibly.

Ms Lynne
: Can the Minister explain why appeal forms were not made available throughout the country from 21 October, when the regulations were enforced? When will they be available nationwide?

Mr. Evans
: In about a fortnight. The regulations make no absolute requirement to use the appeal form; that is merely the suggested procedure. If someone prefers to write a letter, including the few pieces of information requested on the appeal form, that would constitute a perfectly valid appeal. Guidance has been given for the Benefits Agency, which will inform potential appellants of the arrangements until the new form is available.

Ms Lynne
: Will the Minister therefore issue further guidance stating that letters are acceptable? In certain areas of the country, people are being told that they cannot appeal because they have not used the proper form; they have sent in a letter. As I have said, in the case that I mentioned the letter has not been accepted, although that decision is now being looked into.

Mr. Evans
: I am grateful to the hon. Lady for expressing that concern. Clearly, that is wrong and it should not happen. Regulation 4(5)—new paragraph (5A)—states that

"Where an appeal is not made on the form approved for the time being, but is made in writing and contains all the particulars required under paragraph (5), the chairman of the tribunal may treat that appeal as duly made."

10 There is no intention to impose unreasonable technicalities. It is sufficient to provide the information in writing. If people are being told wrongly that that is not good enough and if the hon. Lady can give me more information, I shall look into that matter.

Mrs. Fyfe
: Will the Minister give an undertaking that appeals will not be time-barred because of disinformation from local offices?

Mr. Evans
: I cannot answer a hypothetical question. Before the hon. Lady becomes angry, let me explain how the procedure operates. Obviously, if the Benefits Agency or the independent tribunal service or, indeed, any other Government Department causes a loss—whether by wrongful advice or administrative mistake—to a member of the public, there is a well—known scheme for compensation. No doubt that scheme could be applied to the sort of cases that have been mentioned.

There are other small details in the regulations. One of the more bizarre, out-of-date features of the old measures under which we had to send out notices to a person's current address even if we did not know what it was is dealt with by regulation 8(4). The old regulation has been removed, and we have tidied up matters.

I commend these modest, good-housekeeping regulations to the Committee. In the legal world, they are quite boring and commonplace. Generally throughout the legal system, they have been seen as necessary for the better administration of courts and tribunals for many years. Even if they are novel in this aspect of tribunals' jurisdiction, they are well established and common sense.

10.57 am

Mr. Henry McLeish
(Fife, Central)
: It was good tactics for the Minister to speak second, because his case is threadbare. The right hon. Member for Sutton Coldfield (Sir N. Fowler) is nodding already. I shall appeal over the Minister's head—I hope that he will respect that—to his Back-Bench colleagues, as profound issues about the regulations are simply being glossed over.

The Minister uncharacteristically talked about people being messed around and mucked about. He said that the comments of my hon. Friend for Glasgow, Maryhill (Mrs. Fyfe) were coloured and exaggerated. I suspect that he is slightly unsure about some of the problems that he is inheriting as a result of the regulations. Indeed, his last comment to the hon. Member for Rochdale suggested that things are going fundamentally wrong, and he was honest to say that he would look into that. As the Minister has to look into elementary matters now, he will have a major headache as problems, such as those that the hon. Member for Rochdale mentioned, unfold within his Department.

Although it is often difficult to find, there is a spirit in the House for consensus and co-operation on issues that should not be about ideology or party politics. The regulations should be such a subject. The history of the appeals system from the introduction of social JPEG 11 insurance in 1911 is one of tremendous success. That success has been built on under both Conservative and Labour Governments. However, under the regulations, which are not just boring bits of law, that process is in danger of being not only halted but reversed. That is the profound disquiet that the Opposition feel about what is going on. That is why I should like to think that Conservative Members, who are often dragooned into statutory instrument Committees, will listen to the concerns being expressed this morning.

In a nutshell, the measure is a fundamental attack on the protection that has been afforded to millions of people in trying to negotiate what is increasingly an extremely complex social security system. There are two issues at stake: the regulations themselves; and—I shall choose my words carefully—the shambles of the consultation process surrounding them. I urge the Minister to acknowledge that the new appeal machinery will make the system less accessible and less effective. It will be more complicated, more confusing and more bureaucratic.

The issue is about fairness and reasonableness. Groups and organisations have made comments and expressed their anger about the regulations. They agree with us that an insult has been handed out to organisations such as the National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux, the Disability Alliance and a host of others who work at the sharp end in dealing with an increasingly complex system. They go far beyond the remuneration that they receive for their work.

The Government have shown a contempt of Parliament, which I shall discuss in a moment. It is extraordinary, even by the Government's standards, that we find that while Members of Parliament are away, the mice will play—the mice being Ministers in this case. Parliament is being held in contempt. We try to uphold the traditions of the House that have developed over centuries, so we cannot be happy with a Government who ignore not only the opinion of the public, claimants and voluntary organisations, but the traditions of the House.

Of course, at the core of the issue is a comprehensive attack on the rights of individuals and the independence of tribunals. We should be giving a lead by introducing sensible regulations that take rights forward and increase protection. Tribunals must be independent and have the necessary means to carry out their work. Apart from the generality of appeals within the system, of which there are hundreds of thousands every year, we have a profound concern for those in the community who are among the most vulnerable and disadvantaged in terms of being able to process their rights through forms, procedures and appeals. I appeal to Conservative Members to consider the difficulties involved.

Before discussing the detail of the changes, which I feel the Minister glossed over, I want to put on record the process and the nature of the shambles that has unfolded since 23 July when the consultation 12 document, "Improving Decision Making and Appeals in Social Security", was published. People wanted to respond to that document and I gather that many organisations have done so. On 23 September, Order No. 2450, which we are discussing today, was made and four days later, on 27 September, the order was laid. The 18 October saw the end of the consultation for the document published on 23 July and then, with breakneck speed, the regulations were implemented on 21 October. Now, on 7 November, we are having a debate. Any organisation or institution looking in on us must think that that is absurd beyond belief. The consultation was a sham; the whole thing is a mockery and, on 7 November, 17 days after the regulations have been sent out to the offices and implemented throughout the country, here we are in Committee.

I am not well up on the definition of a shambles, but this must be quite close to what that definition might be. It would be helpful if the Minister could apologise. He could do the decent thing on the consultation process and take this nonsense away, talk to his senior advisers, people at the Benefits Agency and those involved in tribunal procedures. He should again on a matter that could be profoundly damaging.

The shambles does not stop there. The Minister referred to advice that was going around the offices. He may contradict me but, accepting that the regulations were introduced on 21 October, I belive that the first document was sent to adjudication officers, managers and appeals officers on 7 October—14 days before the regulations took effect. The first line in that long circular stated, "Sorry, the 23-day rule can't apply because the orders were laid on the 27th". The implication is there but even by the standards of the Department, in terms of its own consultation and negotiation, it had breached the time that would be allowable to do things properly. I expect the Minister to respond to that point.

The document is even more fascinating than that. Obviously it was issued by Quarry House in Leeds, Room 3, S25—just to add to the whole mystery. One paragraph said that typists will need new letters, which should be available in January 1997. I am talking about the period from 21 October to January 1997. The Minister alluded to that—along the lines of, "Well, we'll just carry on using the old forms; a bit of form here, a bit of letter there. As long as the process includes all the arguments and as long as they are valid, fine." Is that a recipe for efficiency, simplicity or help? Of course not.

The document is extraordinary. Even typists, who do an excellent job in the Department, were mentioned. They are crucial to ensuring that people receive the information that they require, but they will not receive the necessary letters to do such work until January. Equally important, the document goes on to mention the new advice and the new form, but it does not even give the precise date of January. It says that they might be available "in the new year". Is that July of next year or January of next year?

The Government through to the benefit offices have not a clue of what is happening in the real world. People, whether in a Conservative, Liberal Democrat JPEG 13 or Labour constituency, are involved in appeals procedures. They are at the sharp end, along with the helping organisations. But what do we find? They have been subjected to a nonsense of a consultation process. Even the benefits offices are not prepared; they do not know what is happening. That can only add insult to injury for those people in the real world whom we represent.

I should like to be charitable and suggest that what has happened is part of a Benefits Agency ploy to enable it to say to the Minister, "Look, we are sick and tired of all this. Please drive the regulations through. It will make life easier." Or is it a ploy of the ministerial team in the Department of Social Security? If no case can be made for the changes, why have they been introduced? Why should we want to disadvantage people under the system for no good reason while steamrollering through the changes in the shortest time possible?

Consultation is one thing, but the changes themselves are a more important consideration. The first significant change concerns the form to launch an appeal, a matter of which I have made much already. It does not represent merely a legal crossing of a "t" or a dotting of an "i"; it is something profound for many people in the system who cannot negotiate. A present, people can write a letter when they want to appeal. That makes life easier because the onus is on the system, not the individual. It is vital that that is retained.

Forms allow the bureaucracy to dictate the basis on which the appeal will be made. That is not the function of the appeal procedure. It should be to allow people to exercise their rights. The independent tribunal procedure should process the information, thus ensuring a level playing field and fairness. It is important that we register our protest on behalf of appellants about the fact that while there are no new forms, there will be more confusion but, when the new form is introduced, people will be disadvantaged.

My second point is that when people appeal, they will have to give a summary of the arguments in favour of that appeal. We are not talking about Rhodes scholars, people with PhDs or people with degrees in higher technology. At the sharp end of all this nonsense are people who have difficulties in numeracy and literacy. Some of the people who come to my surgeries—like those, I am sure, who come to the surgeries of other hon. Members—find it difficult to negotiate life. We are not here to make subjective comments about whether that is good or bad; it is a reality.
