DISCRIMINATION IN EMPLOYMENT

The Minister for Social Security and Disabled People (Mr. William Hague)
: I beg to move amendment No. 28, in page 5, line 42, leave out "unduly prejudice an employer" and insert " be reasonable".

The Chairman
:With this we may take the following amendments:

No. 93, in page 5, leave out lines 44 to 48.

No. 94, in page 6, line 5, leave out "from public funds".

No. 29, in page 6, line 6, at end add—

"(5A) Regulations may make provision, for the purposes of this section—

  1. as to circumstances in which it is reasonable for an employer to make accommodation of a prescribed description;
  2. as to circumstances in which it is not reasonable for an employer to make accommodation of a prescribed description.


"(5B) Regulations made under subsection (5A) may, in particular, make provision by reference to the cost of making the accommodation concerned.".

Mr. Hague
: The amendment essentially addresses the point that we have already discussed under amendment No. 18 and therefore you will be delighted to hear, Mr. Hogg, that I do not wish to labour that point this morning.

As I explained at our last sitting, the Government have no objection to placing on an employer an obligation to make reasonable accommodation for a disabled person— indeed, that is the very obligation that we will place on employers via the Disability Discrimination Bill. We object to the provision on the ground that it will relieve the employer of that obligation only if the accommodation would unduly prejudice the operation of the business. The Government remain of the view that the correct test is one of reasonableness, which is what amendment No. 28 seeks to establish.

With regard to the factors that would decide the issue of reasonableness—obviously, people would need to know what was and what was not reasonable—we envisage making regulations under clause 4(5A), which amendment No. 29 would add. It is right to consider that issue fully in order to inform employers of what is and what is not expected of them.

Clause 4(5B), which amendment No. 29 would also insert, specifies that, for the avoidance of doubt, one of the matters that the regulations would encompass would be the relevance of cost. Furthermore, so as not to squander the knowledge and expertise that are already to hand, we envisage consulting widely on such regulations 172 and taking account of existing material, for example that in the code of good practice, which is published by the employment service, and in the civil service code.

Amendments Nos. 93 and 94 would amend clause 4(5). The former would delete clause 4(5)(b), a provision that would allow employers to take into account when assessing the impact of an accommodation the overall financial resources of the workplace. I raise the issue to elicit from the hon. Member for Derbyshire, North-East an explanation of what that provision is intended to achieve. I should be interested to hear, for example, what weight he thinks should be attached to the workplace budget as opposed to the overall resources of the employer in question, which in my view would be a more realistic and sensible way in which to think about the issue.

Amendment No. 94 would delete the words "from public funds" from clause 4(5)(d)—the provision that would allow for the availability of grants from public funds to be taken into account. Again, the amendment is designed to elicit for members of the Committee the hon. Gentleman's intentions. Why should grants from public funds alone be taken into account, rather than those from all the funds that might be available to the employer?

The overall effect of the amendments would be to remove a number of provisions which are unclear and difficult to interpret or which, as currently drafted, would impose unreasonable burdens on employers. I commend them to the Committee.

Mr. Hague
: I beg to move amendment No. 30, in page 6, line 6, at end add—

"(5C) Nothing in this section imposes any duty on an employer in relation to a disabled person if the employer does not know, and could not reasonably be expected to know that the disabled person concerned—

  1. is, or may be, an applicant for the employment; or
  2. has a disability.".

174 We have touched on this subject before so I shall not detain the Committee much longer on clause 4.

In the Bill as drafted, an employer could be held to have discriminated against a disabled applicant for a job even though he or she did not know, and could not reasonably have been expected to know, that the person was disabled. I am referring in particular to the situation where the employer, being unaware of the applicant's disability, failed to make reasonable accommodation as required by the clause.

It is simply not reasonable for an employer to be expected to make an accommodation for a difficulty arising from a disability unless he or she was, or should have been, aware of that disability, and the likelihood of it affecting the disabled person in that way. The Bill should not require that reasonable accommodations be made simply on the hypothetical basis that there might at some time in the future be a job application from a person with a disability. The obligation to make such accommodations is confined to the situation where it proves necessary to address the particular needs of a particular disabled person.

The Government amendment is designed to ensure that an employer is not penalised in circumstances where he or she is simply unaware of the relevant facts. For example, if a disabled person failed to attend for a job interview and was subsequently rejected, the employer would not be held to have acted unlawfully if there was no reason why he or she should have known at the time that the person was disabled in a way likely to have such an effect. That would be fairer. Although I do not wish to stray too far from the amendment under discussion, I would remind the Committee that under clause 5, the employer's freedom to seek information about the health or medical status of a job applicant would be severely limited. The amendment would improve the Bill's fairness and I look forward to hearing the hon. Gentleman's comments on it.

Mr. Hague
: We have debated clause 4 quite a bit already. It interrelates with clause 2 and with clauses 5 to 11, and is of course the principal employment clause in the Bill, covering the relationship between an employer 176 and his actual or prospective employees. It makes it unlawful for an employer to discriminate against a disabled person in the recruitment of new employees or against disabled existing employees.

To reinforce what I said earlier, I ought perhaps to make it clear, although I hope that it goes without saying, that the Government support the broad intentions underlying clause 4 and have introduced similar provisions in our own Disability Discrimination Bill. There is a need for legislation to be introduced to protect disabled employees and job applicants from being unfairly discriminated against on the grounds of their disability, including in the labour market.

However, there are a number of fundamental differences between the approach tiiat we have taken in our Bill and that of the hon. Member for Derbyshire, North-East. We touched on some of those differences in debating the specific amendments to clause 4, and, indeed, those to earlier clauses. By relying on the concept of "qualified disabled person" the Bill creates unnecessary confusion and complexity. With its provisions on "reasonable accommodation" it further muddies the waters and in some instances threatens to impose unreasonable burdens on business.

That is a function of the Bill's drafting but I do not think that it was the intention at the outset. My hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Smith) helped to highlight that problem a few moments ago. Furthermore, the Bill fails to make proper allowance for the fact that there may be occasions when less favourable treatment is justified—although that notion is implicit in the Bill's concept of "qualified disabled person", in the sense that a disabled person has to be qualified to benefit from the Bill.

The Government tackle that in a different way, not by having the concept of "qualified" but by setting out some situations in which less favourable treatment is justified. That approach is much more straightforward. It allows us to make it clear what those situations are, whereas the use of the term "qualified" leaves it up to a commission or, ultimately, the courts to decide how that term is to be interpreted.

The latter point is addressed in new clause 3. Given that the Government believe that the word "qualified" should be omitted from the Bill, new clause 3 in consequence would establish certain limited circumstances in which, contrary to the general position, less favourable treatment of a disabled person would be justified.

We have debated that proposition before and at some length in connection with previous amendments. We all recognise that irrespective of whether the individual is disabled, there may be circumstances where it is entirely appropriate to treat one individual less favourably than another—for example when that individual is simply less suitable to do the job. The Government accept that where matters under an employer's control place a disabled person at a substantial disadvantage because of the effects of the disability and it is reasonable for the employer to make an adjustment—or, in the words of the Bill, an "accommodation"—that he should do so. However, that is very different from saying that no matter what the effects of a person's disability, the employer will be allowed to take them into account only if they impinge upon the essential functions of the job.

JPEG 177 The Bill makes a mistake in that. Such a provision, if it came into force, would be seen to be unfair to employers and would therefore be less likely to work. Clause 4 as drafted, without qualification by new clause 3, would mean that it would never be possible for an employer to treat a disabled person less favourably on the ground that his or her performance was impeded—even after reasonable accommodation—in carrying out nonessential tasks. As long as a disabled person could do the essential duties, which could be quite narrow, he or she would be entitled to the same status as a non-disabled person who was able to perform the full range of tasks.

That would create much resentment and would not foster an environment in which disabled people were readily accepted in the workplace. That is the rationale for the new clause, which is one of several amendments we have moved on the concept of the qualified disabled person. It is the counterpart of what the Government wanted—to remove "qualified". Given that the hon. Member for Derbyshire, North-East has rejected that removal, he is not likely to accept the new clause. I think that I have correctly anticipated his arguments. However, it is important to make the Government's approach clear.

Could the hon. Gentleman speak on the issue of small firms? That has been a contentious issue in the debates on the Government Bill. How can small firms be expected to judge what is a reasonable accommodation in any one case, especially as most small firms are much less likely than large employers to have experience of employing disabled people. They are also unlikely to have personnel specialists or staff with expertise in disability issues. Will not it be particularly difficult for small employers, generally without expert knowledge, advice or experience, to know which of the range of selection tests is most likely to reflect the skills or aptitudes of a disabled person, as set out in clause 4(2)(h)? Can I also ask the hon. Gentleman about 4(2)(d), which we have not yet debated and which refers to people having an association with a disabled person? Very many people could be said to have such an association; it is very imprecise terminology. How are employers or disabled people expected to know who is and who is not covered by the Bill? It could open the Bill up to abuse because almost anyone who was rejected for a job could claim that it was because he or she had an association with a disabled person.

Those are my continuing anxieties about clause 4. We have highlighted some differences of approach while agreeing on the overall objective. I welcome comments by the hon. Gentleman.
