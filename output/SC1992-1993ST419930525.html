Draft Double Taxation Relief (Taxes on Income) (Ukraine) Order 1993

10.30 am

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Stephen Dorrell)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Double Taxation Relief (Taxes on Income) (Ukraine) Order 1993.

I welcome you to the Chair, Dr. Blackburn.

I commend the hon. Member for Newcastle-upon-Tyne, East (Mr. Brown) for his interest in these double taxation treaties, which are an important part of the world trading system. They facilitate the flow of capital around the world and they integrate the parties to double taxation treaties into the world trading system, which is in the interests not only of this country but of our treaty partners.

The double taxation treaty with the Ukraine marks an important step forward in the process of integrating the various component parts of the former Soviet Union into the world trading system. It is the first double taxation treaty with a former Soviet Union country. The treaty was signed at the time of President Kravchuk's visit to the United Kingdom in February. It marks an important step forward in the history of the former Soviet Union and in the integration of Ukraine into the world trading system.

I should like to draw the Committee's attention to two particular aspects of the treaty. First, it provides that taxing rights in the context of royalty and interest flows rest with the resident state, not with the receiving state. That is at the request of Ukraine because it is keen to attract an inward flow of technology and it does not want to exercise a taxing right on royalty flows associated with that technology back to the owner of the technology in the United Kingdom. We welcome that: it is a departure from the OECD standard treaty, a copy of which I can see in front of the hon. Gentleman.

The second aspect is that the treaty provides that investments made from this country in Ukraine will not be subject to the rules that the Ukrainian authorities apply to its own enterprises, limiting the tax deductibility of salaries and interest on long-term debt. Those rules would inhibit the flow of British capital and British interest in investment in Ukraine. The Ukrainians have agreed that those rules should not apply to investment from this country, which once again shows the seriousness of their intention to integrate their economy into the world trading system.

I welcome that interest and I commend the treaty to the Committee as giving tangible expression to it.

The article states:

"EC governments yesterday were warned not to enter into double taxation agreements with countries outside the EC as some treaties could be potentially discriminatory.


Mrs. Christiane Scrivener, the commissioner responsible for fiscal matters, confirmed that the European Commission was studying bilateral double taxation treaties to establish whether they disadvantaged companies based in other EC countries."

The context of that statement is the double taxation treaty between Germany and the United States of America. We have our own difficulties at the moment with our double taxation treaty with the United States.

Does the treaty meet the fears of Mrs. Scrivener? Have we entered into an agreement that disadvantages our partners in the European Community? What are the mechanisms for resolving such matters, not just on our side—where they are well established and understood—but on the Ukrainian side? Article 28 is always inserted to protect the interests of diplomatic missions overseas. The negotiators of these matters take a special interest in the treatment of civil servants' overseas postings; a special clause protects their position.

In the Ukraine, everyone will have been a public servant before the change of regime from Communism to independence. It follows, therefore, that the article as it relates to pension rights—if they accrue in the Ukraine—must cover everyone, at least in as much as it has historical impact. Can the Financial Secretary say whether pension rights are accrued under the current system in that country or whether the obligations of the former regime are carried over and, if they are, whether there is blanket cover under the order. I realise that the treatment of income will have altered as there is no longer a Communist Government. The United Nations deliberations will have been considered widely by member states. Can the Financial Secretary say how far those deliberations are reflected in the treaty?

The Opposition's main concerns relate not so much to tax treatment of individuals but to that of businesses. Article 11 refers to tax treatment of interest. Article 6 appears to deal with circumstances of joint ventures between the Ukraine and Britain. This is not an OECD standard. Those matters may have been covered by the Financial Secretary in his opening remarks. If not, will he tell us why a non-OECD standard clause is in this part of the order and—relative to paragraph 6 of article 11—say whether the purpose was to provide special treatment for joint ventures between the United Kingdom and the Ukraine? My hon Friends, who are following the debate intently, would welcome an assurance that the order does not open another debt equity debate similar to that in our debates during the passage of Finance Bills in 1991 and 1992. We should also like to know whether the tax treatment of those 6 matters is either the same in both countries or is covered by the treaty in such a way that there is no opportunity for what the Americans call double dipping.

We are anxious, too, about transfer pricing. The United Kingdom ratified the arbitration convention to deal with these matters on 18 December 1992. It was signed by the 12 member states of the European Community; I believe that we were the fourth to ratify the agreement. The Treasury issued a triumphalist press statement at the time, saying that the purpose of the convention was to eliminate double taxation which could arise if a transfer pricing adjustment was made in one contracting state and the other contracting state involved did not make a corresponding downwards adjustment. We understand the principle—God knows, it comes up every year in the Finance Bill—but can the Financial Secretary say how the principle is addressed in the treaty? The opportunities for wholesale tax avoidance in the early development of the Ukrainian economy may be limited. In a rapidly developing economy, mechanisms could be put in place that are a serious drain either on the Ukrainian Exchequer or on our own.

In the 1988 Budget and Finance Bill, we considered the double taxation treatment of capital gains from intra-group share exchanges. The purpose was to prevent gains or losses being charged or allowed more than once. We are anxious about people who are charged more than once, but even more concerned about those whose transactions are allowed more than once. The Government have a position on these matters and I hope that it is reflected in the treaty. I could not quite satisfy myself that the way in which it was constructed would allay our anxieties, expressed in Committee. Article 10 deals with capital gains. The Government recently issued a statement of practice dated June 1988—section 10 of the Capital Gains Tax Act 1979 refers to capital gains tax and the double taxation provisions set out in sections 788 and 806 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988. The statement of practice that was issued must also, presumably, sit alongside this treaty. Can the Minister say exactly how that will work in practice in the specific circumstances of the Ukrainian economy? These general questions are being asked not just by me but by people across the country. Mr. Dorrell
: I congratulate the hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, East on his obviously profound knowledge of, and interest in, bilateral matters between Britain and the Ukraine. He said that the treaty represented a bold step forward for the Ukraine into the free enterprise market economies that the western world has been living in, and benefiting from, for many years. I agree with his description of the treaty as playing an important part in that process. I welcome the bold step taken not just by the Ukraine, but the Labour party in its interest in providing an infrastructure which facilitates the integration of such economies into the trading system. I also welcome its newfound enthusiasm, referred to by the hon. Gentleman as he was concluding, for free trade. I warmly endorse the interest in bilateral trade between Britain and eastern Europe and look forward to that new principle being extended more broadly into the economic policy contributions made from the Opposition Front Bench.

The hon. Gentleman asked me a series of questions which I shall try to answer, but not necessarily in the order that they were asked. He asked about the nature of the Ukrainian tax system from which double tax relief is being sought. I hope that he will forgive me if I do not go through the Ukrainian tax code, but he might like to know the basic outline of the tax on profit of enterprises. It is levied at 30 per cent. on gross profits, although for joint ventures in which foreign participation meets certain minimum requirements the rate is reduced to 15 per cent. and the income tax on citizens of the Ukraine, foreign citizens and stateless persons is applied on the basis that Ukrainian citizens and foreigners living in the Ukraine for more than 183 days in a calendar year are chargeable on their world-wide income. The tax rate for individuals whose salary is paid in karbovantsi is progressive up to a top rate of 50 per cent. applicable to the taxable income exceeding 60,000 karbovantsi a month. That is the basic provision of the corporation and income tax system in the Ukraine.

The hon. Gentleman also asked about the Government's commitment to eliminating tax avoidance, which I agree is an important issue. The principle that underlines the double taxation treaty must be to try to avoid the double taxation of profits and income—capital gains—but, to ensure that, wherever possible in the bilateral relations between the two states, income is at least taxed once. The United Kingdom's treaties are negotiated with the purpose of achieving such results.

Article 23 of the treaty will continue to allow that to happen. However, when domestic provisions in one country combine with the convention to result in avoidance of all taxation—or a very much reduced rate of taxation—article 23 restores the taxation rights of the state of source on that income. I hope that that will put hon. Gentleman's concerns on that subject to rest.

8 The hon. Gentleman was very concerned about article 19, particularly its effect on pensions. He expressed the view that the majority of Ukranians used to be employed by the state. That, of course, used to be true, but it is no longer true. There is a growing private sector, in which employees will be subject to the non-governmental pensions article of the treaty. The Government service article enforces the basic proposition that when a Government pay an employee for Government service, that payment is taxable in the country in which the payment is made. The hon. Gentleman might also be interested to know that article 19 follows closely the OECD model code on those subjects.

I shall deal with the hon. Gentleman's questions about sub-paragraphs (6) and (7) of article 11, which is the interest article of the treaty. He asked about the effect of those two sub-paragraphs. Sub-paragraph (6) prevents interest that is paid by a company resident in one country to a company resident in the other country being treated as a dividend or distribution, except when the recipient company is controlled by residents of the other country. Sub-paragraph (7) states that the provisions of the article will not apply if the debt claim in respect of which interest is paid was created or assigned mainly to obtain the benefit of article 11—the interest article to the treaty.

The hon. Gentleman asked about article 9, which provides that suitable arms' length adjustments may be made in determining the profits of an enterprise carried on by a resident of one country in circumstances in which there is an element of common control with an enterprise carried on by a resident of the other county and when artificial conditions are made between the two enterprises. An article containing such a provision is generally included in our double taxation conventions and accords with OECD principles.

The hon. Gentleman asked about transfer pricing. The arms' length principle is reflected throughout the treaty, but especially so in article 10, which deals with associated enterprises. It provides that suitable arms' length adjustments may be made in determining the profits of enterprises reseident in either Ukraine or the United Kingdom.

The hon. Gentleman also asked about settling differences between either the taxpayer and the tax authorities or between the two tax authorities. As between the taxpayer and the tax authorities—either in this country or in Ukraine—that is a matter for domestic law. The issue that matters in the context of a double taxation treaty is the way in which the two jurisdictions reconcile their differences. That is what is set out in the treaty.

The Ukrainian Ministry of Finance is specified in article 3 as the competent authority. I am advised that it has set up a division within the Department to deal with the provisions of the treaty, which provide that differences between the two countries will be settled by negotiations—that is a familiar concept in double taxation treaties. The OECD is involved in helping states of the former Soviet Union in double taxation matters. I am pleased to say that the Inland Revenue is involved in the training effort that has been offered by the OECD in that context.

The hon. Gentleman asked me whether the treaty is discriminatory against other EC countries. He quoted the concerns of Mrs. Scrivener to ensure that member states of the Community should not sign treaties that are discriminatory. The OECD model is designed to avoid discrimination between the contracting parties to the JPEG 9 treaty—that is the purpose of the double taxation treaty—as between the United Kingdom and other member states of the European Community. It is in the nature of a bilateral treaty between Britain and Ukraine to establish rights and obligations that are unique and would not exist without the treaty. Therefore, to say that the rights and obligations of German or French enterprises vis-à-vis Ukraine are the same as those for United Kingdom enterprises is nonsense. It is the purpose of a treaty to establish a position that did not exist before.

Our purpose is not to discriminate against other European member states, but to establish a nondiscriminatory framework for trade and investment flows between this country and the treaty partner—in this case, Ukraine. That is, as the hon. Gentleman has recognised, the logic that underlies the negotiation of double taxation treaties. I congratulate him on his warm welcome for this treaty, which extends that principle.

Mr. Dorrell
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Double Taxation Relief (Taxes on Income) (India) Order 1993.

The Ukrainian economy is a new economy, which we are trying to integrate into the free world economy. The Indian economy is much more familiar to all members of the Committee. It is a major industrial economy, which the Indian Government have been trying for years to integrate more closely into the world economy than was the tradition established by Nehru after independence. I welcome that development. It is in the interests of the residents of India. It is also in the interests of the rest of the world for India to be a major trading partner and home for foreign investment and for investment flows to be encouraged in both directions. I welcome the move that the treaty represents on the part of the Indian Government to apply more standard OECD-type terms to their bilateral economic relations with the United Kingdom.

The convention introduces three major changes compared with the convention that it replaces and which was signed in 1981. First, the new convention ends the arbitrary discrimination in which income flows generated by pre-1981 investments did not benefit from reduced treaty witholding rates. Secondly, witholding rates on royalties and technical fees are significantly reduced compared with the 1981 position. A slightly complex article provides for that in the treaty, and the rates are down to 20 per cent., 15 per cent. and, in some categories, 10 per cent. compared with the 30 per cent. across the board that was provided for in the 1981 convention. Thirdly, the shipping article now conforms to the OECD model whereas the 1981 provision granted India, for a 10-year period, taxing rights which would, following rules, have belonged to the United Kingdom.

12 For obvious reasons, the shipping article need not detain us long when we are discussing our relationship with Ukraine: it is more important in the context of our relations with India. However, they are three important changes which reflect the wider drive of Indian policy to integrate the Indian economy more closely in the world trading system. I commend the treaty to the Committee.

The hon. Member for Wallasey (Ms. Eagle) raised an issue that is more relevant to trade policy or human rights policy than to double taxation. It is a familiar issue. It is sometimes argued that human rights abuses by a trading partner provide a case for restraint of trade. I do not accept that argument. I do not condone human rights abuses but they should be tackled by local jurisdiction. Trading policy is an extremely blunt instrument for dealing with such a focused anxiety. Trade policy is blunt but the taxation system is even blunter.

We form a view about the regime with which we deal when we engage in double taxation negotiations, but our purpose is to facilitate a fair taxation of profits between the two countries that sign the treaty. It would be a mistake to believe that we can solve through the taxation system, every bilateral issue that arises between the two countries. The hon. Lady raised a legitimate anxiety. However, it cannot be properly tackled in the context of a double taxation treaty.

The hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, East asked about the future of the law of domicile and the law of residence. The law of domicile is not a concept in tax law. The Inland Revenue is not conducting a review of the effect of the law of domicile on the tax system. The Government, under the leadership of the Lord Chancellor, have been reviewing the law of domicile and the law of residence. However, the effects on the tax system of any changes would be a result of a change in the general law concept. It is not a process that is led by the Inland Revenue. The law of domicile and the law of residence have important effects that extend beyond the tax system. It would be wrong for such issues to be led by tax considerations. To my knowledge, there has been no recent change to the law of domicile. To consider relaxations of the law that are linked to donations is to assume that relaxations have occurred. They have not. The hon. Gentleman's proposition is, therefore, hard to substantiate. A review was announced a long time ago. It is proceeding, but one cannot link relaxations and donations.
