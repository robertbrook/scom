I understand that the suggestion was made in a newspaper article. I cannot comment further on that, but the Government's position is that we are unaware of proposals for major changes to that Act.

Perhaps hon. Members will allow me to deal with the order, which is very important, even as a draft order. I know that I speak for all members of the Committee when I say that the welfare of children is of utmost importance and it is proper that the Committee is considering it.

It was to ensure the welfare of the children of Northern Ireland that my noble Friend Lord Arran, who then had responsibility for health and social services, announced the publication on behalf of the Department of Health and Social Services and the Office of Law Reform of a proposal for a draft children order in July last year. In response to that publication, more than 100 individuals and organisations with an interest in child care have forwarded comments, which are being considered. I am confident that the views of members of the Committee will form a valuable contribution to that consulation process.

The order will form the most important and wide-ranging reform of the law relating to children in Northern Ireland this century. It is a substantial document and I shall not take up time this morning by embarking on a detailed exposition of its contents. Those are set out in the explanatory document which accompanied the proposal when it was published and which hon. Members have before them today. I shall confine myself to saying a few words about the scope of the proposed legislation.

The order will bring together most of the public and private law relating to children in Northern Ireland in a single, coherent, statutory framework along the lines of the Children Act 1989, which has been in operation in England and Wales since October 1991. It is based on the fundamental principle that the welfare of the child is paramount.

The drawing together of public and private law can be seen in the new concept of parental responsiblity which the order will introduce. That responsibility will continue when it becomes necessary for a child to be taken into care. We firmly believe that as far as possible children should be brought up and cared for within their own families and that family ties should be preserved even in case in which the state must intervene.

The order supports the concept that children are best looked after by their families. In the case of a child in need, JPEG 7 the order will secure that objective by the provision of services to the child and his or her family. It is not, regrettably, always possible, or indeed in the child's best interests to be brought up in the family environment. Where children are looked after away from home the Children Order will provide a legislative framework to ensure that their welfare is safeguarded and promoted. Whether they are looked after in accommodation provided by a health and social services board, a voluntary organisation, a registered children's home or are the subject of a fostering arrangement, the order will ensure that those involved in looking after children and the conditions in which they are accommodated meet a high standard.

The order is far reaching. It sets out the powers and duties of health and social services boards and the services to be provided, including those for disabled children. It will ensure the protection of children who are at risk. It addresses problems that may arise in relation to the child's education and makes provision to safeguard the welfare of children who are employed. Detailed provisions are included to ensure that those who provide child minding and day care services are subject to registration and inspection procedures.

The Children Order will affect most aspects of the provision of services for children and their families. It will also have a major impact not only on the health and personal services, but also on the courts, the police, the education services and the voluntary child care sector. As with the Children Act in England and Wales, all of this represents a substantial change in the law affecting the care and upbringing of children. Much preliminary work will, of course, be needed to ensure its successful implementation. However, I believe that the proposed Children Order represents a broad consensus on how child care law in Northern Ireland should develop and I am encouraged, as is my noble Friend Lady Denton, by the general welcome that has been given to the publication of the proposal. I am confident that the order will form an integral part of the community care reforms in Northern Ireland by providing the legal foundation for a comprehensive, community-based and needs-led child care service in which the interests of the child are paramount.

Mr O'Brien
: In considering the order, I want to refer to three articles, because I am sure that other hon. Members want to contribute to our debate on the important and lengthy order.

The order will bring Northern Ireland into line with the Children Act 1989, which now applies to other parts of the United Kingdom. Again, we must draw to the Minister's attention the significant difference between Northern Ireland and other areas of the United Kingdom. In my constituency and in other areas of England and Wales, there is local government involvement in the administration of such legislation. We are witnessing—we discussed this matter at length on Monday—the introduction of extra quangos in Northern Ireland. With the introduction of trust boards there will be limited or no local input into the administration of services. We shall, at least, seek an assurance from the Minister that provision JPEG 9 will be made for local people to have a voice and devise initiatives when considering the order.

Following discussions with groups in Northern Ireland, some of the broad changes in the order should be welcome, but several questions need to be addressed and, perhaps, some amendments considered by the Minister and Lady Denton. We read that the child's welfare is paramount, that a panel of guardians will be created to safeguard the interests of the child and that the child can have his or her solicitor and direct access to court. Those are principles and proposals that many organisations involved in child care in Northern Ireland would welcome, with some interpretation. Other references in the order require qualification and explanation. What is the interpretation of "children in need"? We understand that children with a disability are included, but the phrase,

"a reasonable standard of health or development"

which is often used in the order, requires more explanation. It is open to various interpretations. Paragraph (3) of article 50 states:

"Where the question of whether harm suffered by a child is significant turns on the child's health or development, his health or development shall be compared with that which could reasonably be expected of a similar child."

If we then consider article 17(a) and use the same comparison of similar children, does the definition of children in need take account of poverty, culture, ethnic children or the lack of resources in a specific area? Will the comparison between children in similar circumstances refer to the problems that can develop in certain parts of the region?

Article 19(3)(b) allows a board to provide day care for under-fives, although they are not in need. There is no obligation on a board to provide day care for under-fives if the children are not in need. Trusts will have different policies depending on the area, the resources available and the demand for those services.

The application and iterpretation of what represents children in need is a complex issue. The definition of "in need" varies so much that the order will be to the detriment of those shildren who are already socially, culturally or economically disadvantaged. I ask the Minister to address those important issues and to impress upon Lady Denton the need to qualify the interpretations, which people who are involved in caring for children in Northern Ireland want to promote.

Article 17(a) should be amended to include cultural needs and the social and economic disadvantages of children; in other words, it should cover some of the concerns to which I referred. Many people have expressed that opinion, and I agree.

There should also be a duty on the Government, perhaps through the appointment of a children's rights commissioner in Northern Ireland, to monitor the differential impact of services geographically and in relation to gender social class, race or ethnic origin, disability, language or other status. If the Minister takes 10 seriously the request for a children's rights commissioner, that would be a constructive approach towards implementing the order in Northern Ireland.

If it is found that different provisions are made in different trust areas, corrective action should be taken to adjust those discrepancies. Resources should be made available to help children in need in the wider sense, rather than in the narrow interpretation outlined in the order. There should be statutory provisions for an annual report to Parliament, perhaps by the children's rights commissioner, similar to that submitted by the Lord Chancellor under the Children Act. That would provide an annual review of how the order operates in the Province. Such a report could be helped by an independent monitoring group made up of the voluntary organisations which play an active part in the care of children in Northern Ireland.

Will the Minister advise the Committee about training and the trust boards'involvement? Who will be reponsible for training social workers and back-up staff and for ensuring training standards? Will that be the responsibility of independent boards or of a national directorate, or will provision be made by the Department? On accountability, will there be a further dotted line for social workers who report to the Minister? In another place, Lady Denton said that there would be a dotted line from a social worker to her. Will there be a dotted line under the provision, and how will that operate? Many people want clarification of those issues.

What provision will be made to continue the voluntary initiative? Many voluntary organisations provide initiatives. How will that be encouraged under the order? How will the provision of preventive services apply? The hon. Member for Belfast, South (Rev. Martin Smyth) asked whether evidence exists of the experience of the Children Act 1989 in England and Wales. Considerable research evidence shows that the family support part of the Act is not working as intended. Families receive help when it is too late—when they are in crisis—and not at an earlier stage. Red lights flash, but no action is taken because of a lack or resources and training. Many families receive help only when a crisis arises.

Mr. McGrady
: Does the hon. Gentleman agree that in view of the discussion last Monday, and with the introduction of 12 cash-limited trusts to administer the legislation in Northern Ireland, resources will not be adequate to service families when the early warning signs appear? Does he agree that only those in distressed circumstances can be helped because of the circumscribed resources?

Mr. O'Brien
: That is exactly what I was trying to impress on the Minister. People who have applied the legislation in England and Wales have provided the crucial evidence that, owing to the lack of resources and trained people to administer the service, families who need assistance are denied it in the early days and it is provided only when a crisis arises. That is too late. If the legislation is to be successful in Northern Ireland, provision must be made for resources and a means of communication so that families that are having difficulties are helped before a crisis arises.

JPEG 11 Table 3.1 of the 1992 report on the Children Act shows clearly that in the first year of the Act's operation, local authorities targeted services at families for which they already had responsibility, whereas other needy groups—for example, school truants and families whose gas, electricity or water had been disconnected—were very low on the list of priorities. The report shows that further investigation is needed to show how the legislation is working in England and Wales. The services that are suffering from lack of resources must be identified so that the same problems do not arise in Northern Ireland.

In England and Wales the evidence has been provided by local authorities and in Northern Ireland it will be provided by trust boards, which the Minister will set up. One can readily see that the evidence in Northern Ireland will not be forthcoming in the same way as in England and Wales.

That is why we should have an independent monitoring body in Northern Ireland, made up of local people, which would have an input in reporting to Parliament.

What is happening in England and Wales is disturbing when we consider what will happen in Northern Ireland, where proverty is widespread. For example, 95,000 children live in one-parent households, 70 per cent. of which are dependent on state benefits for their income; and 39 per cent. of children live in households with a total income of £121 a week or less. In October 1993, debts owed to the Housing Executive were more than £18 million. There are 13,885 claimants who have their benefit deducted and who find it very difficult to meet the extra costs that are sometimes necessary to provide for the family. More than 11,500 people have other benefits deducted. The order will do nothing to help the real problems affecting children. That is why some of us have asked that there should be an opportunity for members of the Committee to consider the order after Lady Denton has examined the proposals.

The matter of small private children's homes, which operate in England and Wales and perhaps also in Northern Ireland, should be investigated. A private home which has four children or fewer does not come under the inspectorate that covers larger homes. Registration of smaller homes should be considered. If there are three or four children in a private home which is not registered or not inspected, those children could be at risk.

There is no record of small private children's homes in Northern Ireland. Some of my colleagues have raised that matter in the House and emphasised the need for registration and inspection. I hope that the Minister will make inquiries and ensure that the problems emerging in England and Wales will not be allowed to develop in Northern Ireland.

I have put to the Minister one or two significant points which people inside and outside the House will want him to address. I accept that he might not today have the answers to some questions, but I would appreciate it if he gave the 12 Committee the opportunity to receive awnswers in writing.
