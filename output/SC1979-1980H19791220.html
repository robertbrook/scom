156 It seems to me that the House of Commons continues to pass legislation, often phrased in curious language, normally on the advice that precedent demands it—those precedents are usually bad—and that courts and lawyers demand it for the sake of clarity. However, clause 1(3) is one part of the Bill that does not deserve the strictures that I have just passed concerning legislation as a whole. On reading the clause, I cannot see a difficulty in understanding it. I explained in our last sitting that its intended effect is to make sure that the licensing law is seen to be embodied in two pieces of legislation—part III of the 1970 Act and part I of this Bill.

To make sure that they are construed in the same way and interpreted together, this small subsection states:

"This Part and the 1960 Act shall be construed and have effect as if this Part…were contained in Part III of that Act".

It then refers to clause 35, which is the interpretation clause, and points out that it will apply to all the terms interpreted in it in respect both of the 1960 Act and this Bill. I am surprised that that has defeated those eminent lawyers in the House of Commons to whom the right hon. Gentleman has shown the clause.

I turn to amendment No. 44, which illustrates the pitfalls that can occur in amateur drafting—indeed, it has happened to me during my Parliamentary career. I shall try to show what is wrong with the wording chosen by the Opposition to try to improve subsection (3). First, the amendment refers to the commencement date. However, there is no specific commencement date in the Bill and therefore clearly there is nothing to which the amendment can refer in that respect.

Different provisions in Part I of the Bill will come into operation on different dates, according to orders made under clause 57(5). That is the first technical defeat. The words

"as amended by this and previous acts."

are unnecessary and superfluous, because JPEG 157 section 20(2) of the 1978 interpretation Act makes clear that any references to an Act are to be taken to mean that Act as amended.

The main point of the amendment seems to be to leave out the words in brackets in subsection (3)—which so worried the Opposition in our last debate. However, the result of leaving that out would give the impression that an Act of Parliament can somehow amend itself, which it cannot. Any amendments to the 1960 Act must result from the provisions of this Bill.

If any amendments or repeals contained in this Bill were to be regarded as part of the 1960 Act, some rather odd results could occur. That Act would then be regarded as containing repeals or amendments to its own provisions and it could also be taken to include repeals of, for instance, the Minibus Act 1977—yet that Act was passed 17 years later.

It may be a pedantic point and one that would not much trouble anyone interpreting the Act.

In talking about innovation as I did when I opened the debate, it is true that we have in mind small operators. We think that more small operators, perhaps those who at the moment are confined to coach and excursion business, may move into the stage carriage business and provide a more flexible and lower cost service in those rural parts of the country where services are inadequate.

Small operators can have many advantages. Because they are closer to the loyalty they tend to have lower overhead charges than large national or regional operators. It tends to be much easier for a smalll operator, who personally manages his business, to adjust his timetable to the needs of a small local community. We believe that small operators will take advantage of the new criteria for licensing and come forward to provide some variety in services or improve services which are lacking.

The Opposition argument—no doubt we shall return to it in more detail later—is that any new operator will largely be going for profitable services, damaging the network and damaging the cross-subsidisation upon which the present public transport system depends. As we have made clear, we believe that it is a wholly exaggerated argument. But I personally and the Government, accept that there is a place for some cross-subsidisation in passenger transport.

I give an obvious example. It can easily be demonstrated that passengers at busy times of the day are subsidising the fares of passengers at other less heavily patronised times. It appears to us wrong that there should be any presumption that the present pattern of cross-subsidiation has 186 to be retained. What has developed over the years, because of the restrictive system of licensing, fares control and so on, is that long-standing patterns of cross subsidisation are maintained. Sometimes the choice that has been made between passengers in one locality who are paying more for their fares than they otherwise should, in order to keep fares down for passengers in another locality, is not a matter of sensible economic or deliberate choice. The system of licensing has tended to freeze the pattern of cross-subsidisation.

The other part of the argument is the question of whether all new applicants would go for the profitable services. The questions begged by that argument are: what is profitable, what sort of service and to whom is it supplied? As I said on Second Reading, some of the most unprofitable services in the country are some of the busiest and most heavily patronised services in our big cities. Whether a particular service in a surburban or rural area is profitable depends on the size of the vehicle provided, the overheads of the operator, the way in which he arranges his manning and, whether his timetable accurately reflects local travelling needs. A badly organised operator with heavy overheads can make a loss on a service out of which a better organised and more suitable operator could make a profit.

We do not have any change in the present system because the licensing authorities, bound as they are by past practice and legislation, will exclude the new applicant, leaving the unprofitable existing applicant to look to the local authority for subsidy and to have his services protected.

As I said at the start of this debate, those are some of the considerations in our minds in seeking to change the atmosphere, to change the criteria and to encourage innovation.
