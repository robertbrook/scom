Museum of London Bill

10.30 am

Motion made, and Question proposed,

That during the proceedings on the Museum of London Bill the Committee do meet on Tuesday 14 January 1986 and Thursday 16 January 1986 and thereafter on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past ten o'clock.— [Mr. Luce.]

Mr. Norman Buchan
(Paisley, South)
: I should like to say a word about the sittings motion, and the best way to do that is by moving an amendment.

I beg to move, to leave out "and Thursday 16 January 1986". There will have to be a consequent amendment to delete "and Thursdays" but presumably that will follow after we have dealt with the first amendment.

There is no reason why we should proceed at this appalling speed. It is clear that the Committee's intention is not to proceed with the Bill until after the Christmas recess and so there is no reason to proceed with such haste thereafter. We all have plenty to do on Thursday, but, more importantly, a delay will give us time to investigate the workings of the museum and to let the Government change their mind.

The Government have changed their mind in a very short space of time. After all, it was only this year that the Local Government Act 1985 was passed. It set out a very different proposition from that with which we are now confronted in the Bill. Section 43 of that Act wipes out democratic representation through the abolition of the GLC and, consequently, its members. On page 27, section 43(1) says:

"and as from that date the number of members to be appointed by the Prime Minister under paragraph (a) of that subsection shall be increased from six to twelve."

That Act was passed only a few short months ago but now legislation has been introduced that seeks to alter that and in place of the "six to twelve" it is proposed to alter the number from six to nine, in the same way that the Corporation of the City of London should have the number of its members increased from six to nine.

That has come about within a short space of time. It is almost as short as the time we would have to discuss the Bill were we to do it in proper depth, which would take us towards Easter of next year. There is no reason to proceed as quickly as the sittings motion proposes, for by the time we meet on 14 January the Government may well have decided to change the number. They may want to make it seven or 11. They may object to even numbers and decide that odd numbers are preferable. Two lots of nine add up to an even number and the Government may wish to alter that so that the Chairman does not have to use his casting vote.

We all know that in any case it will not be long before the legislation is changed. There must be a general election during the next two years, and there seems little reason to rush through crudely measures 4 that will have to be reversed if we are to maintain democracy for the Museum of London and, in the wider context, for this country. The amendment is intended to give the Government time to rethink their position and to withdraw the Bill or change their mind about it.

On Second Reading we were told why there was to be a change after such a short period. The Minister said:

"As the House will recall, last Session the Government announced their intention to divide the GLC's share equally between the two other partners after abolition."

I could not trace that declaration but no doubt the Minister can tell us when the Government made it. The Government could not carry out their intention. The Minister continued:

"It was not possible to make provision for those new arrangements in the Local Government Act 1985 which abolishes the GLC. This was partly because the Government wanted to make amendments to the Museum of London Act 1965 and to effect other repeals, neither of which could be done under the terms of that Act."

Later he said:

"The position is slightly confused by the fact that the Local Government Act 1985 included interim provisions that would not take effect until after 1 April next year."— [Official Report, 21 November 1985; Vol. 87, c. 469-470.]

I am not surprised at that confusion, because this has been a very confused Government, but I do not see why they could not have included in the Local Government Act 1985 the provisions in the Bill. No doubt the Minister will explain the Government's change of mind.

If the Government are so confused that they need to change their mind, we should give them more time. They could have between now and January—the whole of the Christmas recess—to consider clause 1. We shall not progress very far with clause 1 because six amendments have been grouped together. If the Government listened to Opposition arguments on successive Tuesdays, and I trust that we could clarify the Government's confusion and help them to find a more lasting solution.

However, there are much more fundamental reasons why we want to change the sittings motion. We have a deep objection to the measure. It digs deeper into the foundations of our democratic principles than do the museum's archeological excavations into the foundations of London. The Bill appears to be merely a measure connected with the Museum of London, but it digs deep into our democracy.

The Bill is the first example of the Government's attitude towards elected representatives, elected local authorities, and local government and democracy. I am open to correction, but my understanding is that the Bill is the first legislation to flow from the abolition of the GLC. It was argued that the GLC was expensive and cumbersome and that we should restore local democracy to where it belonged—to the local boroughs of London. It was asked why the London boroughs should be tyranised by the large-scale GLC and why local democracy should not prevail in the local boroughs.

It is now several months since the Local Government Act 1985 was enacted. Yet, after several months of long consideration, and after examining their faults JPEG 5 and confusions, the Government still came back with the same proposition. They said that the purpose of abolishing the GLC was to provide local democracy, but their first act is to wipe out local democracy. It happened to be the Museum of London, but it might have been bigger things. This is the first example. Instead of giving local democracy back to the local boroughs, they have stripped them of power and control, and stripped away any democratic representation in the operation of the museum.

This is the crucial aspect. There is a new Minister who is rapidly finding his feet in the Department. Now that such a cavern of possibilities has opened in front of him, he should be given time to move around more carefully before plunging into the antidemocratic abyss with which he has been saddled.

The Minister should have time to reflect during the recess before tentatively bringing in new proposals in January. Let us provide time to see how democracy can be restored. I stress this point because this is the first example we have seen of the carrying out of the Government's policy since they abolished local democracy throughout great stretches of England and within London.

Another reason for further consideration is that many consequences follow from this measure. The general principle of the Bill is to share the governorship not with local or neighbouring boroughs but with the Corporation of the City of London. That is a very curious body. I hope that hon. Members will have an opportunity to consider some statistics involved in the Corporation which need a lot of research.

Replacing the democracy of the GLC, half the governors will be either elected or nominated by a council—I do not know how democracy in the City of London operates. The Corporation will be elected by 4,600 residents, as against 10,330 non-residents. We found on Second Reading that half the governors of the Museum of London would come from a body which is elected in proportion of more than two to one by non-residents of the area.

You are long in the tooth, Sir Anthony, as I am now becoming—I mean metaphorically. One of the great planks of British democracy has been the principle of one man, one vote. As British democracy matured, it was said that it was intolerable that people who were not even resident but who were involved financially in an area should receive a vote, and that the business vote should be wiped out. But in the City of London, where there is even more money, the business vote remains. Among the electors of the Corporation, 4,600 are residents and the staggering figure of 10,330 are non-residents.

That is intolerable. It is a problem that goes much deeper than mere technicalities. It is our first example, since the abolition of local authorities throughout the country, that the Government do not intend to use this opportunity to strengthen local government, but almost contemptuously to flout democracy by having governorship composed only of members nominated by the Government—the very Government that is destroying democracy—and the other half directly from the Corporation of the City of London.
