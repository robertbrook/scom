Mrs. Fyfe
: I wish to illustrate more fully the Opposition's view on the clause by copying the teachers' idea of using visual aids when the argument is too abstract for the class to handle. For example, when a teacher of primary one wants the class to grasp the idea that three minus two equals one he or she may use three apples, hide two and ask how many are left. I say that not to insult the intelligence of Conservative Members. We can all remember how Alec Douglas-Home admitted frankly and engagingly that he could not think about economics without taking our a box of matches and moving them around.

I invite Conservative Members to imagine a small rural school with a small number of pupils and parents. To make it easy, let us imagine that those pairs of parents are called Lang, Rifkind, Forsyth, Hamilton—the laird, of course—Buchanan-Smith, Stewart and Walker. We could imagine two white settler families called Leigh and Howarth, and let us suppose that there is another couple called Irvine. That makes 10 sets of parents who between them might have 24 children, because 2.4 children per family is the norm. The couple called Irvine could organise a ballot to impose their wishes on the others. Could anything be more unreasonable than allowing the Irvines to impose their views on all the other moderate middle-of-the-road parents? That visual aid may help the Government to realise how unreasonable their intentions are.

Subsection (5) makes a school board responsible for determining whether, at the date of signing a request, a parent had a child at the school. Considering the "Alice in Wonderland" wording of that subsection, Conservative Members had a nerve to complain earlier about the loose wording of one of our amendments.

Subsection (5) invites parents to decide whether a child was at school regardless of the reality. A child is either at the school or not. It is a fact or it is not a fact. How can parents decide whether a child was at the school? That relates to their eligibility to request a ballot. This is truly an "Alice in Wonderland" world.

The crucial question for the Minister to answer is, why 10 per cent.? Why not 20 per cent, as in England? 576 I know it would not be the same 20 per cent, as in England, but we shall discuss that later. I hazard a guess that far more schools have small numbers of pupils in Scotland than in England and Wales. All the islands have small numbers of children in primary schools, and there are isolated communities in the Highlands, in Orkney, the Shetlands and in the Borders. Therefore, the chances are that we have far more small communities with small numbers of pupils on the roll. In many cases there are nowhere near 300 pupils, but only a couple of dozen, or even less. No doubt hon. Members who represent rural areas can give details about that, but I am well aware that many schools have tiny numbers. So why are we getting 10 per cent, instead of the 20 per cent, that has been written into the English legislation?

2 am

Now, at 2 am all the reasonable points have been made. The Minister has conceded that, but he is not even giving us a commitment to take the amendment away to look at it again in the light of either further information or possible consultation about what would constitute a fairer cross-section and base of parental opinion as we start the self-governing procedure. There is a feeling of disappointment that the Minister will not accept these points. That disappointment is not felt so much by us because we are reasonably hardened, but it will come as a shock to many people in Scotland who have supported this simple measure to find that, without qualification, the idea has been thrown out by the Minister. Sadly, there is nothing further for the Opposition to add and we would like to have a vote on the issue.
