4.35 pm

Mr. McFall
: The job of Opposition Members is to make things hard for the Government, not for you, Sir James. Nobody in Scotland wants the measure. We want to impress that on Conservative members who represent English constituencies. Greyhound racing in Scotland has declined over the years. The statutory instrument, if it is adopted, could cause its demise. History shows that two years after the licensing of betting shops was introduced in 1961, afternoon greyhound racing was wiped out. Between 1960 and 1970, attendances at greyhound racing events fell from 15 million to 7 million. It has been suggested that the statutory instrument will cause a rapid fall of 15 per cent, in evening attendances.

There are two greyhound clubs in Scotland, Shawfield and Powderhall. They belong to the National Greyhound Racing Club. Many people gain entertainment and satisfaction from going to meetings at those tracks, not JPEG 5 least those who go to see the form of the two dogs belonging to my hon. Friend the Member for Kilmarnock and Loudoun, which go by the names of Graciously and Rose Gold. Graciously is such a good dog that half the time it will not leave the trap when it is opened. The other half of the time, it looks for my hon. Friend on the terraces, wanders up to him, licks his nose—and forgets about the hare.

Mr. Meale
: I do not want to disrupt my hon. Friend, but he has the wrong dog. Rose Gold is the culprit.

Mr. McFall
: That is even more entertaining.

Mr. Phil Gallle
(Ayr)
: I am not a member of the Committee, Sir James, but I understand that I may attend a sitting and intervene occasionally. The dog referred to by the hon. Member for Dumbarton (Mr. McFall), which never leaves the trap, sounds like a potential Labour Prime Minister.

Mr. McFall
: Unlike the Trident who represents Ayr? I see. I recall that I was very kind and considerate to the hon. Gentleman in the last Committee of which we were both members, and he has started off this afternoon by being insulting. I shall remember that.

Shawfield and Powderhall are subject to registration and legislation. There are veterinary surgeons on the premises, as well as laboratories for dope testing. If Scotland loses those tracks, we shall be left with what are called the flapping tracks. They have less stringent conditions which do not have the same safeguards. That would not be in the best interests of the punters or the dogs, whose quality would decline as a result.

The Minister received 11 submissions from Scotland on the regulations, from the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities, the Association of Chief Police Officers, the Association of Scottish Police Superintendents, the Scottish Police Federation, the Scottish Bookmakers Protection Association, the Scottish Consumer Council, the City of Glasgow licensing board, the East of Scotland Bookmakers Association and the Shawfield and Powderhall Greyhound Association. Not one of those organisations is in favour of the regulations. COSLA, which represents all local authorities, and the police bodies are vehemently against it so why do we have it?

There is no Scottish Conservative Back Bencher on the Committee. We are subjected to this regulation because it is a diktat from the Home Office. Did the Secretary of State set up a committee to look into it? Of course he did not. He probably conferred with his friends in William Hill, in Ladbrokes and in Coral and said, "Right, boys, perhaps you have done me favours in the past. What favours can I do for you?" The reply was probably, "Well, you can get the greyhound races televised so that we can corner the market and get more business. In the process, if small and independent bookmakers go out of business, that does not matter to us. We will tell our employees on the line that their conditions will be changed."

Most of those employees are women who already have commitments. I had a letter from a woman with a handicapped son who suffered from cerebral palsy. She needed to be home at night but William Hill told her that she must accept that her conditions would be changed or she would be on the street. Her choice was to work night 6 after night to earn enough to support her son or to live on income support. That is what has happened as a result of this diktat. I believe that that is wrong and so do some Conservative Members.

I quote from the news release issued by the Conservative Member for Stockton, South (Mr. Devlin). He spoke about the consultation process at the Home Office to which 900 people responded. Few were in favour of the regulation. Among betting office staff, 4,046 out of the 4,167 who responded to the Home Office consultation exercise declared themselves against it. In addition, the Secretary of State recieved three petitions, each containing more than 2,000 signatures, from greyhound racing supporters rejecting the proposals. What does the hon. Member for Stockton, South, a Conservative Back Bencher, think is the reason for that? He said:

"What this whole affair shows is that a shady deal has been struck between the vested interests of the bigger bookmakers and the Government, to the detriment of independent bookmakers, greyhound racing and betting shop staff."

Does not the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland feel ashamed to be making regulations for Scotland that were conceived in England for the benefit of two or three bookmakers, and that will damage the interests of employees in the industry and their families?

I received a signed letter from someone in Scotland but I shall not give his name as he could face the sack if I identified him. He said that he was informed by William Hill of his new contract of employment, which included evening work, and told that if he refused to sign it he would be dismissed forthwith. His choice is unlimited overtime to suit the company's business needs or the dole. That is absolutely shameful.

Scotland's social context should be taken into account. Many urban areas in Scotland are full during the day but deserts in the evenings. What are the implications of evening opening for the employees of bookmakers' shops, who are mainly women and have to take the day's takings to a local bank in an empty shopping area at 9 or 10 o'clock at night? Has the Minister considered their safety? That will be a big issue. When some men realise that betting shops will be open until 10 pm in areas where there are not many people, women could be easy prey to armed robbery.

Dare the Government undertake a consultation exercise? They should ask women for their views. Some people will leave work at 4 or 5 o'clock on a Friday evening and go to the bookies to place a bet. When they find that the bookies are open till 10 pm, they will go next door for a pint, come back at 7 o'clock and place another bet, before they go back to the pub for a pint, get a wee bit more drunk, lose more money, and finally return home, drunk and without money. That process of social disintegration could happen if evening opening is permitted.

If there were sound reasons for evening opening, I would be the first to consider them, but three big bookmakers support evening opening simply to make extra profit. Those bookmakers are probably involved in the food and drinks industry. Social disintegration will occur to increase their profits. That should not be tolerated in Scotland. The interests of the community, the workers and the punters at greyhound tracks should be taken into account. I speak on behalf of all my colleagues. Hook forward to hearing the Minister provide one scrap JPEG 7 of evidence to show why he, in all his wisdom, believes that the regulations will be good for Scotland. Chief police officers and others reject the proposals. Scotland is unanimous in opposing them. Let the Minister explain why he intends to force them on us.

4.47 pm

Mr. McKelvey
: The basis of our objections to the extension of betting shop opening hours has been skilfully outlined by my hon. Friend the Member for Dumbarton.

The make-up of the Committee fills me with dismay. I do not say that from a nationalistic point of view; I do not worry about people's ethnic origin. Some members of the Committee, apart from the Minister, may have an interest in Scottish affairs. They may have Scottish relatives, or their granny may have run away with a Gordon Highlander. Two Tory Members are sitting in Committee and will attempt to speak about the issues. Why are they not members of the Committee? What kind of deal was done to protect the Government if our proceedings follow the die that has been cast?

The make-up of the Committee is not even vaguely democratic. I do not argue that Anglos should not sit on Scottish Committees, because they can learn something about Scotland by doing so. There are Anglos among the Opposition members of the Committee, not because they are Anglos but because they have an interest in the racing industry and know the business. If every Conservative member of the Committee has a strong Scottish connection, or expertise in greyhound or horse racing, or in the bookmaking business, I shall be interested in their comments.

I suspect that a decision has been taken. The Home Secretary, who wishes to be known as El Libré, said recently that he wants to liberate the betting shops. I have news for the right hon. and learned Gentleman—if he wants to liberate Scotland, starting with the extension of betting shop hours, he has started at the wrong end. It would be better to give the Scottish people a multi-purpose referendum so that they can decide how best to govern themselves. The people of Scotland should decide when and for how long betting shops should be open.

The licensing boards in Scotland are quite liberal and perform an important function. When an application is made to open a betting shop, the board to which the application is made first considers the character of those applying and then whether opening such a shop would create any social difficulty. Then it can grant a licence, because it knows that betting shops in Scotland cannot remain open after half-past six.

Now we are to have foisted on us—mainly by those who, if there were a vote, would have no direct connection with Scotland in terms of representing Scottish seats—regulations which we do not want. During the consultation process, the Home Secretary was given information regarding a survey into customer opinion. Customers were questioned in 13 William Hill, 11 Coral and nine Ladbrokes betting offices. William Hill, Coral and Ladbrokes are the three biggest bookmakers. Only three independent bookmakers were included in the poll, two of which represented the Betting Office Licensees Association on the big organisations. In Scotland, all 8 bookmakers who have submitted their views are unanimously opposed to any extension of opening hours. William Hill, Ladbrokes and Coral were so confident that they had the ear of the Home Secretary that they put in no submissions from Scotland. There are no BOLA submissions from Scotland Even BOLA does not appear to want the proposed extension.

There are only two greyhound racing tracks in Scotland regulated by the National Greyhound Racing Club Ltd. They operate on a very narrow profit margin. If the betting shops are allowed to open until 10 o'clock, especially on Fridays and Saturdays, there is no question but that the crowds that greyhound racing currently attracts will be diminished sufficiently to put the two tracks out of business. I shall read some of the submissions that we have had from Shawfield and Powderhall later in our proceedings.

Mr. Meale
: The bookmaker inquiry into betting shop opinion covered 35 or 36 shops throughout the United Kingdom, only two of which were in Scotland. It seems that during the course of a day, the total number of people consulted in Scotland amounted to 32 people in Glasgow and 32 in Edinburgh. That is ridiculous, considering the population of Scotland.

Mr. McKelvey
: That is a good point, and I have tried to illustrate it.

Submissions are still coming in. I have one here, hot off the fax, from two bookmakers in my constituency. They probably depend on me for a large part of their income. I recall with some fondness that when I was hospitalised, the first get well card that I had when I woke up in the morning said, "Get well soon. Hurry back, Missing you. Takings down drastically. Harry and Gladys the bookies". I know bookmakers and they know me. I use their premises frequently and I know why they are opposed to late opening.

If the big three get a real grip on dog racing—especially in England, where they already own five out of the 12 BAGS tracks—and take over or close the dog tracks in Scotland and obtain an additional track or two in England, they will have a monopoly—or, rather, a triopoly. It is not healthy—it is immoral, in fact—for a bookmaker to own a racing track. Bookmakers should not be able to manipulate the odds and own the betting shops. Theirs may be the only ones left, as the late night extension—according to my correspondents—could close shops.

Richard Murray, of my constituency, makes the point that the big bookmakers

"have already monopolised our daily race service by their ownership and loading this service so much that staff on a Saturday are in a position that at particular points on a Saturday where they should be having lunch breaks they have to cope with twelve races going off in a ten minute interval making it very difficult for staff to work contentedly and efficiently."

Small bookmakers have to bring in more staff when they can, which puts their costs up. If they have to bring in more staff for extended periods they will be in greater difficulty.

I want Conservatives to think about what I have said, if only for one reason. It is mainly women who work in betting shops in Scotland. Already William Hill has told them, "There is your contract of employment. You will JPEG 9 be required to work longer shifts, until 10 o'clock on Fridays and Saturdays, and on Sundays"—they are confident that they will have Sunday racing. Staff have been told that they will not receive extra increments for those hours of work, that days off in lieu of those worked can be taken only when racing is cancelled and that such days cannot be accumulated. In other words, staff are told, "There are your working arrangements. You sign the contract. Of course, this is a democracy, and we are giving you the opportunity not to sign. If you do not want to, you are finished on Friday." It is an outrage and a scandal in a civilised country and we should not accept it.

I cannot for the world understand why the Minister, who is well known to Committee Members and whom we would all, I am sure, consider a kind, understanding and humane gentleman, sits there presiding over the Conservative Benches, knowing that no one in Scotland wants the measure. If this were not a Standing Committee on Statutory Instruments, but a court, and if you, Sir James, were presiding not only as the chairman but as a judge, the person on trial would be the Minister. A jury of Scots would hear the charges against him. Opposition Members would be the prosecutors. Two charges would be laid, after all the submissions and evidence had been read.

First, the Minister would be charged with conspiracy, because he is conspiring with his colleagues to foist upon Scotland something that the people do not want. Secondly, he would be charged with complacency because he does not care about the wishes of the people of Scotland, and he will allow the foisting to take place. In those circumstances, after the jury, the people of Scotland, had considered even what has been heard so far, a case would have been made. You, Sir James, would have asked the jury, "Have you considered your verdict?" "We have, Sir," they would say. "Lord James Douglas-Hamilton," you would have to say, "Will you stand and face the jury. Will the jury deliver its verdict on the accusations of conspiracy and complacency?" "Guilty on both counts."

Mr. McKelvey
: I have answered the hon. Gentleman's question: I do it not for any particular or personal gain, but because I am concerned about the jobs that the Shawfield and Powderhall stadiums provide, about all the jobs, held mainly by women, that are threatened at the consortium's betting shops and about difficulties that they may encounter on late night openings. Although that will happen only in the summer, the staff will probably be smaller. Already, there is a growing tendency for bookmakers shops to be held up in Scotland, by people wielding hatchets, knives and, on three occasions recently sawn-off shotguns.

We have a problem in Scotland, which will get worse if we extend hours to give people the opportunity to raid the betting shops. Although the police say that they can contain the situation, they are concerned that the later night openings may create difficulties.

Even those people who accept the late-night opening of the betting shops all believe that they should not stay open until 10 o'clock. The Home Affairs Select Committee said that they should close no later than 9 o'clock, and, if possible, they should be geared down, to an 8 o'clock closing or 9 o'clock closing at the maximum. We have been forced to accept 10 o'clock opening.

Every submission—the Minister will have already scrutinised them—says that we cannot and should not have betting shops open on a Saturday evening. Saturday evening would knock the greyhound stadiums off because that is the biggest and best night for them. It has been said, "Why don't the greyhound people get their act in order?" They have done so: we have the finest greyhound tracks in the world. I suggest that all Committee Members should take a trip to Walthamstow or Wimbledon to experience the service that the public is provided at those magnificent tracks. We want to keep those tracks.

Scotland tried some innovations. The greatest innovation was inviting the public to greyhound racing on JPEG 11 a Saturday night for free. In the first couple of Saturdays, people were piling in—Scots will go anywhere if it is for nothing—but, it started to settle down. Once the innovation was no longer an innovation, the excitement of a free night at the dogs did not appeal to those who were not really interested and attendances went back to being made up of the same group of people who are the backbone of greyhound racing, and the tracks started charging again.

The Government ask, "Why don't they take more money off the Tote?" If the tracks take more money off the Tote to compensate for diminishing attendances, attendances will fall still further because people want a fair deal from the Tote. They do not want bookmakers to take a bigger whack of their money without distributing it We are at the limit of what we can take off the Tote. Customers will accept no further diminution of the prize money that they can win at the greyhounds.

We are trapped, just like my dog who does not come out of his trap, because the Home Secretary has decided that as a decision has been taken for England and Wales, it will be foisted on the people of Scotland irrespective of the submissions that may be made or whether there is any justice in it. I appeal to those members of the Committee who have not already made up their minds to consider the weight of evidence, listen to the Minister's views on that and consider what will be done to an industry that has not asked for the measure. They should remember that the big three bookmakers—Coral, William Hill and Ladbrokes—have not submitted a request for late-night openings in Scotland. As there is no demand for it, why are we wasting our time going through this process in order to satisfy the whim of the Secretary of State, who wishes to be El Libré? I ask members of the Committee to consider those issues carefully. If they do, they will agree with the points that have been made by the Opposition.
