Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 (Codes of Practice No. 5) Order 1998

4.30 pm

The Minister of State, Home Department (Mr. Paul Boateng)
: I beg to move.

That the Committee has considered the draft Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 (Codes of Practice No. 5) Order 1998.

What a pleasure it is to meet this afternoon under your chairmanship, Mr. Benton—a feeling that I am sure is shared by all my hon. Friends—to consider an important matter that relates to the policing of our country.

There is perhaps no more important power available to the police in the exercise of their functions than the power to stop and search an individual in the circumstances prescribed by law. Because of its importance, and because of the aid that it can give to the police—when it is properly used—in detecting and preventing crime, it is right that the Committee should have the opportunity to reflect on the order. That is not only because it is a police power, which, when properly exercised, is of enormous significance and importance, but because where it is exercised in circumstances that give rise to concern, it can have a very damaging effect on the relationship between the police and the community. The police recognise that fact and have taken steps, over the years, to make sure that that power is properly available to them, and is properly used.

Hon. Members rightly want to ensure that the codes and orders that affect that power can be supported. We believe that the time is right to update code A to reflect changes in legislation made by section 8 of the Knives Act 1997, section 25 of the Crime and Disorder Act 1998 and section 13B of the Prevention of Terrorism (Temporary Provisions) Act 1989. It is also desirable to clarify how searches under stop-and-search powers are to be conducted.

The Government propose to commence section 8 of the Knives Act and section 25 of the Crime and Disorder Act at the same time that the revised code A would, subject to the agreement of the Committee, come into force—that is, on 1 March 1999. Section 13B of the Prevention of Terrorism (Temporary Provisions) Act 1989 is already in force.

Section 8 of the Knives Act amended section 60 of the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994, to allow an inspector to authorise the exercise of stop-and-search powers for up to 24 hours if he or she reasonably believes that incidents involving serious violence may take place, or that people are carrying dangerous instruments or offensive weapons in a specified locality in the police area.

4 Section 25 of the Crime and Disorder Act further amended section 60 of the 1994 Act, to provide a power for a constable to require the removal of, and to seize, items used to conceal identity, within the specified locality for which an authority under section 60 of the 1994 Act has been given. The office exercising the power must reasonably believe that the item is being worn or carried wholly or mainly for the purpose of concealing identity.

The Committee will be aware of the practice adopted by certain elements, from time to time, of wearing balaclavas to prevent their identity from being determined—for instance, in the course of demonstrations and sit-ins of the sort that are all too often the precursors to disorder and criminality. The concern on the public's part about the carrying of knives is something that Committee members will be aware of from their constituency mailbags. We are all united in our determination to support the police in using the law to bear down on such a dangerous practice.

Section 13B of the Prevention of Terrorism (Temporary Provision) Act 1989 came into effect on 3 April 1996. It allows an officer of the rank of assistant chief constable, where it appears expedient to do so to prevent acts of terrorism, to authorise his officers in a specific area to stop and search pedestrians and anything carried by them for articles that could be used for terrorist purposes. Sadly, it has from time to time been necessary to use that power, but I hope that in the future it will not be necessary to use it with the same frequency. None the less that power is of importance.

As regards the conduct of searches under stop-and-search powers, code A currently provides that where on reasonable grounds it is necessary to conduct a search that involves more than the removal of an outer coat, jacket or gloves, this shall be done out of public view, for example, in a police van or police station, if there is one nearby. That is to protect the dignity of the individual and all the evidence shows that it reduces the likelihood of intervention either by a crowd that may gather in such circumstances, or by the friends and associates of the person being searched.

Code A does not specify the extent of a search that may be conducted in a police van, nor does it specify how searches carried out at a nearby police station are to be conducted. Consequently, there is some uncertainty about the procedures for conducting such searches, and the relationship with the procedures in code C for searching detainees at police stations on detention. The proposed revision to the end of section 3.5 of code A is intended to clarify these issues.

Section 67 of the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 provides that the Secretary of State may revise the whole or any part of a code of practice, and when he does he must prepare and publish a draft code, consider any representations made to him, amend the draft as appropriate and lay it before Parliament for approval.

A draft revised code A was accordingly published together with a consultation paper in August 1998. The documents were sent to 137 consultees and 15 responses were received. In general, the proposals to update the code JPEG 5 to reflect changes in legislation were welcomed, as was the proposal to clarify how searches under stop-and-search powers are to be conducted.

The way in which the police use their powers is a key test of whether they are delivering their services fairly to all sections of the community. It can have a significant effect on the lives of citizens, on perceptions of the police—not least those of members of the ethnic minority community or by young people—and on the willingness of those communities to co-operate with the police in tackling crime and disorder.

In December, we published a report on the results of the second year of ethnic monitoring of police activity under section 95 of the Criminal Justice Act 1991. Members of the Committee may remember the interest that that, understandably, generated. The report showed, as in the first year, that there were substantial differences between ethnic groups in the use that the police make of their powers to stop and search, arrest or caution suspects. Black people in particular were getting disproportionate attention from the police.

Although there are complexities that make interpretation of the data less than straightforward, the figures give cause for concern. I am pleased to see that the Metropolitan police service is tackling the issue and has developed a plan, currently being piloted in five sites in the Met, to manage the use of stop and search fairly and effectively. That focuses on ensuring that managers control the way in which the power is used and that they challenge unfair practices. More generally, the Association of Chief Police Officers is also taking forward work that will lead to the development of good practice guides for forces on the effective use of section 95 monitoring data.

We are currently awaiting the report of the inquiry into the death of Stephen Lawrence. Getting to grips with the issue of stop and search will be one of the key challenges for the police service as it works to gain the trust and confidence of all sections of the community.

As I said, managing the way in which stop and search is exercised is central to its fair and effective use. That is why we have included a new note for guidance in the draft code, which emphasises the importance of supervising officers considering and addressing evidence that stop-and-search powers are being used in a discriminatory way. Supervising officers are advised to take account of the information on race and the criminal justice system published under section 95 of the Criminal Justice Act 1991.

The police associations have all welcomed the proposed revisions resulting from changes in legislation. Both the Police Federation and the Police Superintendents Association have warmly welcomed the clarification on the conduct of searches under stop-and-search powers, which will have the effect that searches involving exposure of intimate parts of the body may not take place in the back of a police van, and that such searches involving juveniles will require the presence of an appropriate adult.

I find it heartening that operational police officers themselves consider it right that the conduct of searches under stop-and-search powers should be more tightly 6 regulated. That is for the benefit of the police officer as well as of the citizen who is to be searched. I cannot stress sufficiently how vital it is that our police should feel confident in using the stop-and-search power, which is an important tool in the course of their ordinary duties. We must never arrive at a state in which the police feel reluctant to use that power, as it is designed to enable them to act properly in a way that helps them bear down on crime and criminality.

It is important that the revisions are made to the code so that the legislative changes can be fully implemented, and that there is clarification of how searches under stop-and-search powers are to be conducted. I hope that the Committee will approve the draft order, which will allow the revised code to come into effect on 1 March.

One cannot engage in question and answer sessions in those circumstances. The House has a duty to equip me police with the powers, codes and order that enable them to exercise their discretion properly.

If it is possible to engage in a question and answer session police officers will do so. Good police officers do not want to incite a violent response and will take the necessary steps to talk someone into a search. However, in certain circumstances, they must have the freedom to do what this code allows them to do, which is to get on with the search and do the difficult and dangerous job of policing.
