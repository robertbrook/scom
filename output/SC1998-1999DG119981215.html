Draft Financing of Maintained Schools Regulations 1998

10.30 am

The Minister for School Standards (Ms Estelle Morris)
: I beg to move

That the Committee has considered the draft Financing of Maintained Schools Regulations 1998.

I welcome you, Mr. O'Brien, to the Committee. I think it is the first time that I have served under your chairmanship. I am sure that it will be a pleasurable experience and that we will get these regulations agreed.

The regulations were considered on 8 December by the Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments. They cover England and Wales, with variations which have seemed appropriate in the light of differing circumstances and consultation responses. The enabling powers are in sections 46 to 48 of the School Standards and Framework Act 1998. Those sections, as enacted, apply to the new school categories—community, foundation and voluntary—which take effect on 1 September 1999. They also apply to the existing categories—county, voluntary and grant-maintained—between 1 April and 31 August by virtue of the School Standards and Framework Act (Modification) Regulations, which were made on 30 October under section 144 of the Act. In short, the sections apply to all schools, irrespective of their present or future category, and there are provisions for the transition stage between April and September.

The regulations give effect to our proposals for a single funding regime for all schools within the new framework established under the Act. In practice, they replace the divisive dual system of financing which has existed since the introduction of GM status. The detailed proposals were set out at the end of May in the consultation paper "Fair Funding", and similar papers were issued in Wales in June.

There are several key objectives in the regulations. First, and most importantly, they put in place a framework that matches funding with the respective responsibilities of schools and local education authorities. That makes the funding arrangements more transparent; people can be accountable for what they spend and what they do. Within the framework, the regulations give all schools maximum scope for self-management by requiring significantly higher levels of financial delegation than has been the norm in LEA schools. But, they do so without forcing schools to take on responsibilities for which they do not feel ready. Another key objective is to treat community, voluntary and foundation schools equally. Schools will no longer get extra funding because of their 4 categories. The regulations also enable a smooth transition to the new funding framework for GM schools. This is a challenging time for such schools.

Extensive consultation has taken place on these regulations. We received more than 3,000 responses to "Fair Funding" in England and more than 500 to the document published in Wales, which led us to look carefully at our proposals, particularly those on the timing of additional delegation, which was the principle area on which we received responses. Decisions on the main issues were announced on 23 September both in England and Wales. In England, those decisions will mean that about £1 billion more can fall within the total of funds going directly into the classroom—but in a way that allows schools to feel comfortable with their extra responsibilities. Since September, we have undertaken detailed consultation with LEAs and national organisations, which has led us to modify the regulations so that valuable local flexibility is provided without the loss of essential features.

Three crucial matters are governed by the regulations. The first is the balance between funding retained centrally by LEAs and funding distributed by them to schools, the second is the methods used by LEAs to allocate the funding that they distribute to schools, and the third is the content of the schemes that will regulate financial relationships between LEAs and the schools that they maintain, ensuring that governing bodies and head teachers can run their schools without unnecessary intervention from the LEA.

It is vital to strike the right balance between pressing on with self-management and ensuring that schools have access to essential services. After consultation, we revised the timetable for delegation to give LEAs more time to work out suitable local arrangements in consultation with schools. We also reconsidered the range of items for which funding can be held centrally. Affected items include school meals, for which delegation will become optional for primary and special schools; outdoor education, which, following consultation, will still be funded centrally, if local authorities so wish; and library and museum services, for which earmarked funding is to be devolved to primary schools from April 2000. That means that schools currently maintained by LEAs will have substantially greater delegation.

Delegated funds will be available for a wider range of administrative support services from April 2000. English schools will take full responsibility for non-capital building repairs and maintenance from April 1999, and Welsh schools from April 2000. The process for approving education development plans is crucial to the success of "Fair Funding", and will require LEAs to refocus their expenditure on inspection and advisory services and teaching and learning-support activities. Expenditure on supporting services that cannot be justified by reference to approved education development plans must be delegated from April 1999 in England and from April 2000 in Wales.

JPEG 5 Funding for school meals must be delegated to secondary schools by 2000. In England, such funding must be delegated by 1999, provided that there are no contractual complications. From 2000, primary and special schools in England can opt for the delegation of funding for meals. In Wales, the position for such schools is slightly different, because LEAs must decide whether to accede to their requests for delegated funding.

LEAs have contracts for some centrally organised services that do not expire until after the date by which funding must be delegated. We recognise that problem and understand that many schools could go elsewhere for such services, leaving LEAs with a liability that they cannot meet because they have no funds. After considering responses to "Fair Funding", we have decided that delegation of funding must go ahead on time, but that LEAs that are at risk of serious financial difficulties can apply for schools to be bound into contracts for up to three years. Such binding-in provisions must be included in LEAs' school financing schemes, which require the Secretary of State's approval. When such approval is given, LEAs will be able to delegate funds at the actual cost of the contract, so that schools do not lose out.

Mr. Phil Willis
(Harrogate and Knaresborough)
: Does that regulation apply to schedule 2,10(a), (b) and (c), which deal with special educational needs? I support the Minister's proposals for developing inclusive education, especially the important half-way step of partial integration. Will three-year contracts be available to encourage such collaboration? At the moment, only annual contracts are available, so long-term provision cannot be planned.

Ms Morris
: I was not referring to such contracts. Some local authorities have signed contracts for three years or more. We wanted to ensure that they were not left with a financial liability that they can meet only at the taxpayer's and ratepayer's expense. Special educational needs do not fall into that category. Forward planning is important, and the Treasury has given my Department an indication of the funds that will be available over the next three years through the comprehensive spending review.

Our aim is to ensure that we raise the level of delegations to LEA schools. Making the transition from the existing system of financing grant-maintained schools could mean that such schools undergo a period of instability, which is what we want to avoid. Our general principle is that, where funding for a service must eventually be delegated, LEAs must delegate it to GM schools from April 1999. That will ensure continuity in the delegation of funding, to which they have become accustomed. The principle is reflected at various points in schedule 1 for England and regulation 5 for Wales.

Part III and schedule 3 deal with the way in which the individual schools budget is distributed to schools through LEAs' funding formulae. The key principle has been continuity. LEAs already have flexibility to reflect local curcumstances and priorities in their formulae; we do not want them to make major changes 6 if they see no good reason to do so. Therefore, the regulations do not set out a single formula, but offer in schedule 3 a range of factors under which LEAs can meet local requirements. LEAs will retain their discretion as to how to count pupils for funding purposes, but 80 per cent, of primary and secondary funding must be allocated on a pupil-led basis. We received several responses on that issue. It is difficult to make a judgment between 81 per cent, and 79 per cent., and there was no clear consensus to change from the current 80 per cent. On reflection, we felt that the present rule is about right.

The formulae must be fair to all categories of schools, and must be fully considered by all schools in an area. LEAs must consult all schools, including current GM schools, on their allocation formulae. Many are doing so already. The requirement includes consultation on age weightings used to allocate funding as well as all other factors.

We do not want GM schools to experience unmanageable changes in their budgets as a result of the new system. In England, we intend to protect the 1998–99 amount of funding in cash terms received per pupil for 1999–2000. I know that representatives of GM schools have welcomed that. The funding arrangements in Wales are different. They relate to so few schools—about 20—that the funding is based primarily on the local management of schools arrangements of the relevant LEA. The difference between GM and local authority school budgets is thus less marked in Wales. My hon. friend the Under-Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Hain) has decided not to introduce transitional arrangements for GM schools in Wales.

The arrangements for managing the transition of GM schools' budgets continue the process of aligning them with those of other schools, which was begun by the previous Government. They instituted a phased removal of the unfair cash protection of central annual maintenance grants.

Part IV and schedule 5 deal with contents of the schemes for the financing of schools that LEAs will have to draw up. Each scheme will define the financial relationship between an LEA and the schools that it maintains, and will require approval by the Secretary of State. The regulations simply list topics that must be covered. Statutory guidance issued by the Secretary of State under schedule 14 will indicate in more detail how we expect LEAs to cover them. A draft of that guidance has already been issued.

It is important to note two things. First, the outline scheme, and schemes that LEAs will submit, cover ground already covered in greater or lesser detail in LMS schemes. They provide a good opportunity to review LEAs' financial provisions after nine years of LMS. Secondly, the scheme is as binding on LEAs as it is on schools. It will secure crucial rights for schools, such as the right to a bank account and the right to determine spending priorities. Part V of the regulations also sets out requirements for publication of schemes.

JPEG 7 The regulations are crucial to the setting up of a new framework for schools. We must ensure that money allocated to schools is spent on pupils' needs. For the first time, there will be transparency and accountability in the educational spending of local authorities. Much has been done over the past few months to get the system ready. I appreciate the way in which local authorities, the GM sector and schools have responded to the consultation and worked rapidly in the past six months to ensure that the new schools funding system will be ready by the start-up date. I thank all of them for that, and commend the regulations to the Committee.

When the School Standards and Framework Act came into force, the Government issued statements about delegation of budgets down to schools. Several commentators and others had things to say, and the Government portrayed the move as 100 per cent, delegation. With this Government, we know better than to believe the rhetoric because the devil is always in the detail. The detail of these regulations is that there is 100 per cent, delegation of budgets to schools only after the LEA has been allowed to top-slice a significant number of items of expenditure. Schedule 1 identifies no fewer than 89 such items that an LEA may retain within its budget rather than allow them to be delegated to schools. As the regulations show, the reality differs greatly from the Minister's rhetoric. As in so many other cases that I have often quoted as a member of Committees on education, the rhetoric is one thing, the reality another.
