The Minister of State, Treasury (Mr. Anthony Nelson)
: Before venturing into the deep waters, or rather the brave new world of gilt repos, I should like to say a word or two about clause 71 and its purpose. I shall then turn to the more general matters that you have allowed us to discuss, Mr. Winterton, which were commented on by my hon. Friend the Member for Carshalton and Wallington and the hon. Member for Edinburgh, Central.

Clause 71 disapplies the accrued income scheme from transfers under certain agreements for the sale and repurchase of securities. Such agreements are often referred to as repos. The accrued income scheme applies to certain securities issued by companies and Governments; in particular, it applies to United Kingdom securities. Therefore, although the measure applies more widely, it would facilitate the introduction of a gilt repo market.

The effect of the accrued income scheme is that the interest paid on securities is taxed on the persons who held the security during the period in which that interest accrued, rather than on the person who received the interest when it was paid. The interest is allocated between them for tax purposes in proportion to the period each person held the security. Under a repo, the economic benefit of the interest remains with the original holder of the security, to whom it returns. It would be wrong for part of the interest to be allocated for tax purposes to the person to whom the security is sold under a repo. The accrued income scheme works by reference to transfers of securities. At present, both the sale and the repurchase trigger the scheme. Clause 71 disapplies the scheme from both.

The hon. Member for Edinburgh, Central raised several points about the Government's possible proposals for the introduction of a more open gilt repo system. I should therefore like to make a statement about the Government's intentions. As the Committee is aware, the Government have been consulting widely on the establishment of an open gilt repo market. The Chancellor announced in the Budget that the Government favoured, in principle, the establishment of such a market. Following the consultation process undertaken by the Bank of England and the Inland Revenue, the Government have decided to proceed with the establishment of a repo market.

The introduction of the repo market will be accompanied by the introduction of gross payment of interest on gilts to all likely participants in the gilt repo market who so choose, combined with a system of quarterly accounting for those gilt holders subject to United Kingdom tax who choose to receive interest gross. The Government believe, following the consultation process, that that represents the most sensible method of facilitating the introduction of an efficient and liquid 366 market, while simultaneously protecting the financial position of the Exchequer.

We hope to table new clauses to the Finance Bill to effect those tax changes. We envisage that the provisions will be brought into force to allow the gilt repo market to begin on 1 January 1996. That will require the necessary legislation to be in place, the regulations to be worked up and the systems to be determined. I hope that the market will begin on 1 January 1996. We shall publish a press release later today with more details.

Mr. Nelson

Friend the Member for Stamford and Spalding (Mr. Davies) knows, the present gilt market works when the gilt-edged market makers—the so-called GEMMs—operate in the primary market. They operate with the agreement of the Bank of England and they have privileges both in operating and in their access to the gilt market. They can go short of stock and they can obtain stock to fulfil short positions by, for example, lending arrangements. That can happen at present, but it is, as it were, an exclusive GEMM, and the market is made up of an exclusive network of institutions.

Our intention is to establish an open gilt repo market, which will enable more institutions to participate directly by allowing them to go short of gilts, as the GEMMs can do at present. The first restriction is, therefore, who can operate at present.

Secondly, a tax restriction operates. As my hon. Friend may know, a problem arises with a gilt repo when securities are sold, but before they are repurchased interest is payable on the gilt. There has to be a manufactured payment to ensure that the counterparty repays the interest to the holder with whom the economic interest rests. If there were a mismatch between the tax status of the counterparties—if one is a net recipient and the other a gross recipient—the operation of a proper gilt repo market would, in effect, be inhibited.

The point raised by my hon. Friend the Member for Stamford and Spalding was that we could change only that law to establish a repo market. However, that would require a substantial change in the bodies that are allowed to receive gross payments of interest under gilts. That would be a substantial cost, albeit only a cashflow cost. Our intention is to limit the new arrangements—with quarterly accounting and gross payments—to a number of players in the new market who will be able to set up special accounts with the Central gilts office. Essentially, JPEG 367 there have been two restrictions—the designation of the market players and the operation of the tax system.

The provisions are designed to prevent people turning income into capital, for example, by selling equities just before a dividend is due to be paid so that the dividend is received by the buyer. No bond washing has occurred and therefore the bond-washing provisions are inappropriate. The clause removes that anomaly.

Thin capitalisation is the process of financing subsidiaries with greater amounts of debt, in comparison with equity, than would be expected between persons dealing at arm's 382 length. Because interest payments, unlike dividend payments, can be deducted in calculating taxable profits, that can result in a significant reduction in tax paid. The new rules will provide for more consistent treatment of such thinly capitalised companies than has been possible hitherto.

The present approach to thin capitalisation involves a combination of domestic law and our tax treaties. Such treaties differ and, consequently, the law's impact on thinly capitalised companies varies depending on the precise interaction between domestic law and the treaty concerned. In many cases, the law enables the Inland Revenue to treat the part of the interest paid by a thinly capitalised company that exceeds what would be expected between companies dealing at arm's length as if it were a dividend.
