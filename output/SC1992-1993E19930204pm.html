Education Bill
Clause 245
ABOLITION OF REQUIREMENT TO ESTABLISH EDUCATION COMMITTEES

Question proposed, [this day]:That the clause stand part of the Bill.

4.30 pm

Question again proposed.

Mr. Win Griffiths
(Bridgend)
: Before our proceedings finished this morning, I was warming up towards discussing the democratic accountability found in the present system of local government, LEAs and their education committees and sub-committeees. I said how democratic accountability was honourable—indeed, essential—and that it stretched back through this century.

The provision of state education arose because of the total failure of the private sector to provide a good quality education, other than an expensive one for the elite. Therefore, the Church of England and non-conformist Churches provided basic education for the population at large. In the 19th century, Governments took on board that responsibilities—quite literally—with the establishment of board schools.

The board schools of 120-odd years ago had a better system of democratic accountability than the grant-maintained schools of today. With the board schools, it was not only the parents of children who had the right to elect the members of the school board; it was all eligible ratepayers, whether or not they had children in a particular school. That is as it should be.

Under this new, constrained system of democracy, there is a host of taxpayers—payers of income tax, VAT, Excise duty, the new business rate tax and the poll tax, soon to be the council tax—who contribute to the funds that are spent in grant-maintained schools, but have no say as to who will be responsible for running them.

The Bill proposes to get rid of LEA committees, which, throughout the 20th century, have been responsible for ensuring accountability at the local level in the provision of education in their areas.

Clause 245 would omit section 6(2) and part II of schedule 1 to the Education Act 1944. Section 6(2) states:

"The local administration of the statutory system of public education shall be conducted in accordance with the provisions of Parts II … of the said schedule."

The said schedule refers to the first schedule, which states:

"Every local education authority shall, in accordance with arrangements approved by the Minister, establish such education committees as they think it expedient to establish for the efficient discharge of their functions with respect to education."

Such a requirement will be removed from primary legislation. Does the removal of that part of the Education Act 1944 mean that an LEA will no longer have education 1382 committees, because the power to set up the committees has been taken away from it? Alternatively, will an LEA still be able to have education committees? If so, what legislation will cover their governance? Part II of schedule 1 of the 1944 Act sets out several provisions on how the committees should operate. They will be taken out of the schedule by clause 245.

Some of the provisions that will be removed from schedule 1 are instructive. In view of the Government's long-awaited, much postponed, yet-to-be-published proposals on the reform of local government in Wales, one provision is of specific interest to Wales. The Secretary of State said that he intends to publish proposals on the creation of unitary authorities in Wales, the number of which will be in the lower 20s. That means almost trippling the number of education authorities in Wales. It has already been agreed generally that there is no way in which 20-plus education authorities can each provide all the services that are currently provided by the eight authorities. The expertise does not exist.

Paragraph 3 of part II of schedule 1 comes to the aid of the Secretary of State. It states:

"Where it appears to the Minister to be expedient that two or more local education authorities should combine for the purpose of exercising some but not all of their functions with respect to education and that those authorities should establish a joint committee for that purpose, the Minister may after consultation with the authorities by order establish a joint education committee of those authorities and provide for the reference to the committee of such questions relating to those functions as in the opinion of the Minister should be so referred".

Again, that requirement is being removed from primary legislation. I have examined the schedule in the Bill to see if such powers are conferred in another way. They are not. I can only assume that the Government are removing the provisions from the 1944 Act for the express purpose of getting rid of local education authorities. I accept that such a measure does not exist at present, but some requirement could be introduced that would cover Wales if the local government reforms were implemented, although I am still sceptical about them. The Minister cannot go into too much detail now as we are awaiting the publication of the proposals by the Welsh Office, but contained in them could be a suggestion that what we are about to remove from the 1944 Act will be put back into the legislation for Wales.

The Minister of State, Welsh Office (Sir Wyn Roberts)
: What is removed is the statutory requirement to establish an education committee. Furthermore, it is possible to have different commencement orders for Wales under this part of the Bill. We are agreed that discussion on the future of education in Wales and the organisation of the Welsh Joint Education Committee is best left until the local government reorganisation after the publication of the White Paper and the introduction of legislation. That is possible in view of what I said about the different commencement orders. Education authorities can still establish committees under the Local Government Act 1972.

Mr. Griffiths
: I thank the Minister of State for that reply. I shall take up that theme because he seems to be saying that although the clause could be left to the Secretary of State to enact at a later stage when he considers it appropriate, even if he were to do so, a local authority could set up an education committee under the 1972 Act.

Perhaps we are going through a wholly unnecessary process.

Are the Government proposing to publish part II of schedule 1 of the 1944 Act as guidelines for education authorities that set up education committees, or do they intend to introduce a completely new set of guidelines? It would be interesting to know what guidelines will be issued for non-statutory education committees. Perhaps education authorities will be able to set up these committees in whatever way they like.

In paragraph 5 of part II of schedule 1 of the 1944 Act it wisely states:

"Every education committee of a local education authority shall include persons of experience in education and persons acquainted with the educational conditions prevailing in the area for which the committees acts."

That seems to be a wise provision. In this new world of non-statutory education committees, will members of such a committee be required to have anything to do with the world of education in their area, to know about education or to have experience of it?

The new clause would amend the Children Act 1989 so that independent schools with boarders would have to register as children's homes only if, within a two-year period, more than three pupils remained for more than 295 days during both years. That would be irrespective of the number of pupils accommodated for shorter periods—in practice, those resident during term time. Amendment No. 584 is a technical amendment consequent on the new clause.

My hon. Friend the Member for Norwich, North (Mr. Thompson) substantially argued the case. I shall explain briefly why. The present wording of section 23 of the Children Act 1989 requires a school to register with its local social services department as a children's home if it has 50 or fewer boarders. I remind the Committee that schools with more than 50 boarders still have a duty to safeguard and promote the welfare of children which is enforceable under provisions in the education Acts. No one wants to overturn those provisions for smaller sized schools.

The fewer than 50 rule has proved unsatisfactory and anomalous, and in my experience I know that to be true. It has just occurred to me, not having had the benefit of the debates on the Children Act 1989, that it is unusual to JPEG 1425 define something not de minimis but de maximus, which would apply to the fewer than 50 rule. The requirement for children's home regulations was not intended to cover the many large independent schools with small boarding houses. Those are schools rather than homes and are covered by the schools test. The cut-off number of 50 is arbitrary and we have no evidence—if there were, I would consider it—that schools with 20 or 30 boarders require the level of social service department supervision appropriate to establishments that may be a child's main home.

There will be occasions when an independent school acts in loco parentis for more than the normal term time. Some children may be accommodated during holidays and the new clause would affect that. A school that regularly homes children during holidays is closer to being a child's home. We have attempted to find a more suitable definition of the type of school that should be required to register as a home because, for all practical purposes, it is a home. The new clause would provide a reasonable test for that. If pupils usually return to their parents or guardians for 10 weeks or more, no registration would be required. That would make it unnecessary for the majority of the 250 or so schools registered to remain registered as homes. They would be in the same position as those schools with larger numbers of boarders. The amendment and the new clause are sensible and balanced and would correct an anomaly. I urge hon. Members to accept the new clause.
