Income Support (General) Amendment No. 3

Regulations 1993 (S.I. 1993, No. 1679)

10.30 am

Mr. Keith Bradley
(Manchester, Withington)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Income Support (General) Amendment No. 3 Regulations 1993 (S.I. 1993, No. 1679).

It is not my intention to delay the Committee unduly this morning but I think that it is important to raise a couple of matters relating to the regulations. I am pleased that the Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments noted what I had already seen: that the regulations were defectively drafted. I am pleased that that Committee intends to amend them on those grounds. When I considered the regulations, I thought that they were defective in a broader sense of the word but I am prepared to accept that they can be drafted correctly at a future date and that we can proceed today. I am glad that that defect was spotted.

I should like to discuss two matters relating to the regulations: first, the issue surrounding the restrictions on income support on mortgage tax relief; and, secondly, the issue of urgent cases payments and the consequent reduction in entitlement for persons from abroad—I think that they are popularly termed PFAs.

The order in which I have listed my points may not be correct in terms of how they appear in the regulations, but it is how I have arranged them, so I shall deal with them accordingly. On the issue of income support for mortgage tax relief, there has been an enormous amount of publicity surrounding notorious cases where people were being maintained by large income support payments, in what were considered to be inappropriately large residential properties. In many ways, there was a certain merit in the Government reconsidering such cases, because there was public concern about the amount of money being paid out. The Government put out for consultation changes to the regulations to tackle the problem. As we often find when the Government try to change regulations, they tend to take a sledgehammer to crack a nut and bring into the net people who, in other circumstances, would not be considered to be inappropriately receiving income support payments to ensure that they kept a roof over their heads.

When the regulations were put out for consultation, there was considerable concern about how the proposals would actually impact. Organisations such as the National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux and the Child Poverty Action Group expressed particular concern about how the regulations would operate in practice.

The report of the Social Security Advisory Committee did not oppose setting a ceiling in any circumstances, but expressed concern about the proposals and made certain recommendations based on evidence submitted by groups involved in dealing with the consequences of changing the income support regulations. Those groups strongly 4 believed that the new ceiling for income support was far too low. The figure of £150,000 for the current year, which will be reduced to £125,000 next year with continual reviews thereafter, seems unreasonable to many people. Although the impetus of the proposal was to check millionaires living in millionaires' mansions, who receive vast amounts of income support to sustain their lifestyle, it is erroneous to suggest that £150,000 in any way sustains people in such properties. Although the value of the property market in London has fallen considerably, the south-east is still an expensive area in which to live and £150,000 buys a reasonably modest property. Because of the fall in house prices in the south-east, people with a mortgage on a property of £150,000 are often in negative equity. Although we all hope that the Government will change their economic policies, which will strengthen the economy generally and sustain house prices, there is currently little optimism on that front. Organisations, such as the National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux, have stressed that people living in modest properties with negative equity are not the people at whom the proposals were aimed, but they will be brought into the net of restricted support for mortgage interest payments.

As the Child Poverty Action Group has clearly shown, regulations already exist to allow the Government to place restrictions on payments for housing costs. Under schedule 3 of the social security regulations, interest on a loan can be restricted where

"the home is larger than is required by the claimant and her/his family in comparison with suitable alternative accommodation."

There was public outcry over the types of properties that were being supported because they were inappropropriate for the needs of the families living in them, but existing regulations deal with that problem. Secondly, the interest can be restricted where

"the immediate area in which the home is located is more expensive than other areas in which there is suitable alternative accommodation."

That restriction could, however, be brought into effect without any change to the regulations. Thirdly, interest can be restricted where

"the housing costs are higher than those for suitable alternative accommodation (e.g. because of the size of the loan)."

Existing regulations deal with that possibility without the need for wide-ranging changes, which may hit families whom the Government did not intend to hit.

When the Social Security Advisory Committee reported on the changes to the regulations, it made certain recommendations to the Government. First, that

"the proposals should be deferred until the Government gained assurance from the insurance industry that mortgage protection insurance would be made available to existing borrowers"

Secondly, that

"if the assurance could not be given, the new regulations should apply only to loans taken out after they come into force."

Thirdly, that

"the ceiling should not apply during the first 16 weeks of a claim when only 50 per cent. of eligible interest is met by Income Support."

Fourthly, that

"plans to reduce the ceiling to £125,000 from April 1994 should be deferred until the position has been monitored."

The Government's own advisory committee raised five crucial points about the implementation of the regulations. Unfortunately, only one of those recommendations, which concerned the limit on the loans when homes had been adapted for disabled people, was accepted.

JPEG 5 Will the Minister explain why the Government ignored not only the evidence that was submitted to the advisory committee by many expert organisations, but the advisory committee's report, which clearly outlined the major worries that existed about the matter? Why do the Government consider that they must introduce extra measures, when it is clear that existing regulations can deal with the problem? Why do they believe that the ceilings that are being applied reflect accurately the housing costs and the housing market? What will be the impact of the regulations, especially on lone parents and self-employed people given that the recession means that they are finding things a great struggle? They may lose their jobs and they certainly do not want to lose their homes.

I am talking about modest homes in the south-east and the south of England that do not provide excessively over-generous accommodation. Families at least deserve to remain in their homes. There is no merit in people becoming homeless on the back of the regulations if such measures are inappropriately pitched in terms of the capital value of the property that they cover. Will the Minister give us an assurance about the regulations and explain, first, why they are required and, secondly, how they have been drawn up and what targets they are designed to meet?

I wish now to explore the changes to regulation 70 of the Income Support Regulations and the consequent reduced entitlements to income support for persons from abroad. The changes are contained in regulation 2 of the Income Support (General) Amendment No. 3 Regulations. As we know, persons from abroad are entitled only to income support paid on an urgent case rate and in limited circumstances. The new amendements would restrict those circumstances even further. We have heard a great deal of rhetoric from the Government about restricting benefit payments to people from abroad, but we are dealing this morning with specific and narrow circumstances on which the changes will impact. We would welcome an explanation of those changes.

Let us consider two crucial changes. Persons from abroad who seek to vary their leave will not qualify for urgent case payments. Before August, urgent case payments could be made to those seeking leave, provided that the leave was not subject to the requirement that they should have recourse to public funds—in other words, permanent leave. Public funds are defined in the immigration rules as income support, family credit and housing benefit.

Persons from abroad who seek asylum status are now specifically mentioned as being entitled to urgent case payments. They were entitled to that before the amendment was introduced, so the position of that group remains unchanged under the regulations. Will the Minister clarify that?

Who will lose out from the variation of leave? Until the changes in August, foreign spouses were able to claim urgent case payments when applying for permanent leave. They are admitted initially to the United Kingdom for 12 months, subject to the requirement that they do not have recourse to public funds. Income support rules state specifically that foreign spouses are not entitled to income support or urgent case payments during that period. They would normally apply to the Home Office for permanent leave to remain just before the expiry of the 12 months, but we know from our constituency immigration cases and appeals against decisions by the Home Office that it is often 6 many months before a final decision is made on whether someone is allowed permanent leave to remain in the country.

Even in dire financial circumstances, spouses from abroad will be unable to claim urgent case payments for themselves, their partners or their children, although they would have been able to do so under the previous system. The only option will be for a member of the family of the couple who is already settled in this country to make a claim on behalf of the family, but benefit will be granted only for the claimant and not for the whole family. If a couple's children enter with a spouse from abroad, no income support will be paid for the chidldren. They will be forced to live on an income level well below the Government's definition of the poverty line.

The family unity of people who legitimately obtain temporary leave to remain in the country before seeking permanent leave will be means-tested, and while the Government are considering that application there will be no entitlement to benefit. Only a few cases and small sums of money are involved. We are not talking about vast amounts of benefit, so why do the Government believe that it is appropriate to put a restriction on such people?

We must question the Government further on asylum-seekers. Not all who seek refuge in the United Kingdom are willing to apply for asylum status because they know that they may jeopardise their status once they are able to return to their country of origin. They may, however, apply to stay on compassionate grounds and may ultimately be awarded exceptional leave to remain. They have, however, no right to claim urgent case payments while awaiting a decision, by the Home Office, which again may take some time. The Opposition believe that people who are here as genuine refugees and who are ultimately granted extended leave to remain on exceptional or compassionate grounds should be entitled to urgent case payments, which are equivalent to a reduced level of income support. They should be entitled to some financial support while their cases are being considered by the Government.

I hope that the Minister will explain why foreign spouses and asylum-seekers are being excluded from the regulations. I shall not say any more unti I have heard the Minister's reply, when I may want to question him further.

I shall deal with the regulations in the same order as the hon. Gentleman and, therefore, I shall first discuss the matter of mortgage interest. The hon. Gentleman was right to refer to the publicity surrounding some cases and to suggest that there was merit in the Government acting to deal with them. He also said that we have existing restrictions to deal with some of the problems. We have retained those restrictions, but, of course, it was patently obvious from the cases that arose that the restrictions could not always do the job. We provided the opportunity for the restrictions to be deferred for up to a year. It was during JPEG 7 that period that the Government were most open to criticism from the Opposition, as well as from others, who said that the situation was not good enough and that we must do more. In response to the general opinion of the taxpayer—that it was inequitable to provide income support payments to those with substantial mortgages—the Government acted.

It is always a problem as to what extent existing limits should change. The hon. Gentleman asked whether we had moved into more modest territory. It is a matter of degree and of understanding fully what the restrictions do and do not do. There is no suggestion that no support should be given to those who have a mortgage of £150,000, which drops in the following year to £125,000. The regulations will, however, prevent support from being applied over and above that level. I should have thought that the vast majority of taxpayers, who do not live in such properties, would believe if fair that their tax, which is used to provide for other people through income support, should go to support extensive mortgages—£150,000 and £125,000 are not small sums—and that, by that, they would be fulfilling their commitment as citizens. Over and above those amounts, however, the aim of the proposal is that house owners should be prepared to provide for themselves to cover the circumstances in which they might otherwise have had to seek income support.

Mr. Bradley
: What percentage of families with a mortgage in south-east England live in properties worth below or above £150,000, and, from April 1994, how many will live in properties worth below or above £125,000?

Mr. Burt
: The figure is about 1 per cent. I suspect that that is a little imprecise for the hon. Gentleman and I shall take steps to provide a clearer answer. He will appreciate that the figures and the size of properties involved are relevant only to a small group of people.

I want to make the major point clear. We are not talking about a total absence of support, nor are we talking about a circumstance that would lead to homelessness, as described by the hon. Gentleman. The Government are entitled to be concerned, on behalf of the taxpayers, about who should bear particular responsibilities—whether a responsibility is the duty of the individual or the state. As always, we maintain the principle that in circumstances of dire emergency, the state has a role to play—none of us, as taxpayers, would think that wrong. When considering the total social security budget, however, the Government are entitled to ask where the British people believe responsibility should lie.

In the matter of mortgage interest, we believe strongly that home owners, especially those in receipt of higher incomes and those who have bought properties at a higher price, can take steps to cover a situation which would otherwise have led to a request for support from the taxpayer. That principle is implicit in the regulations. We believe firmly that a market will arise to provide house insurance above the levels of support where income support would normally have been applied and thus the individual will be covered. We believe strongly that the individual should bear that responsibility. I have difficulty with the argument from the hon. Member for Withington, that that principle should not apply.

Therefore, our move has been well thought through and is one which we are entitled to rely on in relation to merit.

The provisions of the European convention on social and medical assistance, ECSMA, to which the hon. Gentleman referred, go wider than European countries—they include Turkey. Nationals of those states which have signed that convention are admitted to the United Kingdom under the immigration rules. That usually means that they do so on condition that they do not become a charge on public funds. In some instances, they also have work permits which means that they cannot be treated as available for work, which is a basic condition for receiving income support. That prohibition created difficulties when Turkish Kurds were seeking asylum in the United Kingdom in 1988–89, so the regulation was changed.

Any ECSMA national who is an asylum-seeker is not affected by the change. It was never intended that other ECSMA nationals admitted on the condition that they did not become a charge to public funds should be entitled to income support if they tried to vary those conditions. Thus the change from 2 August is in line with Government thinking and restores the original intention. I hope that that explanation has helped.
