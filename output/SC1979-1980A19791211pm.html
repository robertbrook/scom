COMPANIES BILL [ LORDS]
New clause 19
DATA AND ESTIMATES

"(1) In addition to the accounting records that a company is required to keep under the provisions of the Companies Acts, the directors of every public company shall secure and collate not less than once in each accounting reference period such data about the company's affairs and transactions and those of its subsidiaries and prepare such estimates of the future course of the business as in the opinion of the auditors (or, where such a committee has been appointed, of the audit committee of the board acting with the advice of the auditors) are required to allow a reasonable assessment to be made of the future profitability of the company and of its ability to pay its debts as they fall due.

(2) The data and estimates prepared in pursuance of subsection (1) above shall be provided as soon as reasonably practicable to all the directors of the company, to the company secretary and to the auditors, but shall in no circumstances be disclosed to any other person, unless to an employee of the company designated by the directors or in accordance with statutory requirements.

(3) In forming their opinion as to the scope and extent of the data and estimates to be prepared in pursuance of subsection (I) above, the auditors shall have due regard to the recommendations of the board, the nature of the company's undertakings and the cost of preparation.

(4) In the auditors' report the auditors shall state if in their opinion the requirements of subsection (1) of this section have not been complied with."—[ Sir B. Rhys Williams.]

New clause 38
Qualifications of Company Secretary

"It shall be the duty of the directors of a public company, when appointing the secretary of the company, not to appoint anyone other than a person who in the opinion of the directors has adequate knowledge and experience of the duties and responsibilities of a company secretary and who either

  1. on the appointed day held the office of secretary of the company or of a responsible assistant to that secretary; or
  2. for at least three years of the five years immediately preceding the appointment has held the office of secretary of a public company, or
  3. is a member of the Institute of Chartered Secretaries and Administrators; or
  4. is a member of any of the institutes of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales, or Scotland, or Ireland; or
  5. has been called or admitted in England and Wales or Scotland as a barrister, solicitor or advocate."—[ Mr. Graham Page.]

Mr. Graham Page
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

We had a very happy lunch together, Sir Stephen, and I hope I am still capable of moving the clause. It concerns the qualifications of company secretaries. It seeks to ensure that the secretary of a public company—I stress "public company"; I am not dealing here with private companies—is qualified to do the job of secretary. It is as simple as that. It seeks to do so by imposing a duty upon the directors, when they are making an appointment of a secretary, to see that in their opinion the secretary is properly qualified, and that he has certain qualifications which are listed in the new clause.

In reference to paragraph ( c), one of the qualifications being that he is a member of the Institute of Chartered Secretaries and Administrators, I ought to admit at once that I am an honorary Fellow of that Institute, and that the clause may perhaps seem special pleading on behalf of that institute. After all, it is one of the qualifications for the job to be a member of an institute which teaches persons to be proper secretaries of companies. I am sure that that is necessary. There have been JPEG 693 many abuses of company law and, if I may so put it, company morality or standards over the past few years, some of which have shocked the public and which could have been avoided, perhaps, by having a professional man as secretary who would have properly advised his directors, not only on what the law might be but on the standards of practice in company administration. I do not say that all these abuses would have been avoided had the secretary been a professional man qualified to carry on the job, but I believe that some might have been avoided.

4.45 p.m.

I believe that the best way to ensure to the public that too many of these abuses do not occur is to have the key man in a company—and I call the secretary the key man—not only with a knowledge of administration, company law and company procedure, but who has professional standards on which he can judge his own conduct and the advice which he gives to his directors.

It is true that the company's auditors have to be qualified. They have to have the full qualification of auditors—but strangely enough, they are the only officers of a company who require qualifications, and although they may spot malpractices, they can only spot them after the event. They are not there to prevent malpractices. They are there to identify them when they have happened, if they have happened. They approach the matter from an outsider's point of view.

The secretary is the man at the centre, who looks at the company administration from the insider's point of view, and accordingly advises his directors. Again, directors by law do not need to be qualified. It may be that some of them are very well qualified both in company practice, commercial law and other professional matters. But they have great responsibilities—to the shareholders, to the employees, to the company's customers, to the suppliers and to the community at large.

In carrying out those responsibilities, they ought to be properly advised from the administrative point of view by a qualified company secretary. He should be the guardian of the law and even of the moral conscience of the company. The directors rely, I think typically, on the secretary to ensure that the company is fulfilling its responsibilities under the law, and he holds 694 that unique position at the heart of the business. The secretary is an officer of the company. He is potentially liable under the Companies Acts to criminal proceedings in a wide variety of circumstances.

Where duties are imposed on the officers, frequently the secretary is the officer who is actually responsible for seeing that those duties are carried out. If they are not carried out, he may be criminally responsible. A high proportion of the statutory obligations relate particularly to the administration of companies and to matters for which the secretary is responsible. A survey was made recently of the duties of the secretary, when it was found that in more than 90 specific instances failure by a secretary to perform his job properly could result in criminal penalties. Indeed, as the Committee will know, section 177 of the Companies Act 1948 requires every company to have a secretary. But it stops there.

The Act does not say that a company must have a secretary who is qualified to do the job. He can be a secretary who is wholly unqualified. Any unqualified or inexperienced person may be appointed as secretary, even of a public listed company in which the public is invited to invest. If it is thought that my argument is perhaps aimed more at public listed companies than at public companies in general, I admit that that is so, but perhaps it is invidious to make that distinction between public listed companies and other public companies. Therefore in new clause 38 I have merely expressed it as applying to public companies.

Mr. Page
: I do not have the figures but, as my hon. Friend says, the majority certainly are qualified. It is perhaps the minority at which this clause would be aimed, and a minority which may defeat the public confidence in public companies that there is always a man there who is qualified to advise the directors.

To some extent, if this new clause were accepted, it would make no great difference JPEG 695 to many public companies. Many public companies ensure that they have either a professional man outside secretarial professionalism, such as a lawyer or an accountant, as secretary. Others make sure that they have one who is trained as a secretary and who knows about all the necessary administration and company law.

The secretary represents the company to the outside world. He is the person to whom people make complaints. He is the person to whom people write about the company. He is the person from whom they expect to receive a reply on behalf of the company, and the person who, to outsiders, must represent the shareholders and the employees of the company—not only the directors—the person whom the public would regard as the one to whom they make their inquiries about the company. He is the person responsible for complying with the law and seeing that the company complies with the law.

I glanced over a number of the responsibilities of a secretary. His responsibilities and knowledge of administration and Company law start even before the company is formed. If he happens to be named in the articles of association, immediately he has certain responsibilities towards the public through the registrar of companies. He has responsibilities with regard to the fixing of the registered office, with regard to the letter headings of the company and the seal. He has responsibilities with regard to the commencement of business of the company. Indeed, many of those responsibilities have been increased by the present Bill.

The secretary has responsibilities to see that the company has the statutory books, the accounting records, the books of share certificates, the minute books and the registers—which seem to increase in number each time we pass a new Companies Bill—the register of members, the register of charges, the register of directors' interests, the register of voting interests, the register of debentures and so on. The secretary has to know about all the requirements of those registers and be able to keep them accurately. He has responsibilities relating to the capital of the company and the securities, the increase of capital, and seeing that the proper notices are served and filed with the registrar. He has responsibilities in connection with the auditors and their proper appointment—,if they require to 696 be dismissed, the proper notice that has to be given—and the disclosures which have to be made to the auditors.

The secretary is responsible for the annual returns, for the documents that have to be annexed to those annual returns, for dealing with the subsidiaries' annual returns and so on. He is responsible on the transfer of shares and debentures, though he may have the registrars of the company behind him to deal with the actual details of that. He is responsible for advising directors on bonus issues and so on. He has to ensure that the statutory meeting is held, and is held properly, and notices are given, and he is responsible for the procedure at meetings.

When it comes to the winding up of a company, the secretary's responsibilities are innumerable, and I shall not endeavour to list them.

With that brief outline of what the secretary of a company has to do, how he has to advise his directors and his responsibility in seeing that the company does the right thing, it can be seen that it is essential—not just desirable, but essential—that the company secretary should know his job. He can know his job only if he is qualified, if he has been taught to do it, and has learnt to do it.

The last thing I should wish to do by my new clause is to push out of office any of the very qualified secretaries at present employed and appointed—qualified by their experience. But this is the sort of thing that has arisen every time that over the years we have wished to make some statutory provision on the qualification of professional men. We have always recognised that qualifications are gained by experience as well as by examinations, and on every occasion—doctors, dentists, accountants and so on—whenever statutory provisions have been brought in saying, that the person holding himself out as an accountant, dentist or whatever it may be shall have qualifications, we have said that those who are already practising, who already have qualifications, shall be accepted. I have endeavoured to do that in the clause.

Subsection (2) enables the court, where it makes such declaration, to make such further directions as will give effect to the declaration.

Leitch Brothers Limited in 1932 that, if a company continues to carry on business and incur debts when there is to the knowledge of the directors no reasonable prospect of the creditors being paid, it is in general a proper inference that the company is carrying on business with intent to defraud.

Why should we be talking about this reform at this time? In practice, small firms are frequently stimulated to undertake business with a subsidiary because the small firm in question becomes perhaps unduly influenced by the connection of that subsidiary with a prestigious and substantial parent company. When the truth dawns, when the subsidiary gets into trouble, and the parent company then disclaims any responsibility for what has happened, the small business man may in fact, as often happens, be deeply shocked, embarrassed and prejudiced as a result of that discovery.

While of course it may not be good morality for holding companies to adopt that attitude, the present law permits them to regard it as good business. The current commercial practice seems to be that moral obligations in this respect may be regarded as secondary or, in some cases, hardly of any consequence at all. An article appeared in the Sunday Times last weekend, after we had put down our own new clause. It was argued there that increased diversification may have altered the pattern of commercial judgments in this regard. It asked, why worry about upsetting a subsidiary supplier in the ice-cream business if the rest of the group deals in oil futures?

That sort of argument is cogent. It is built upon experience. It is regrettably common practice for a parent company to adopt the sort of scheme of which I have spoken. It is common practice, too, to create a group image, and if that is considered to be advantageous to the parent company, why should not liability, or responsibility, flow from that towards the creditors of the subsidiary?

There are examples where subsidiaries are faced with something close to this representation but probably not in fact constituting misrepresentation in law, 718 when the parent company is seen to be involved to some extent on the face of it with the subsidiary, and the latter assumes that the parent company will in fact indemnify the subsidiary. Skilful use of this advice can certainly cause others to rely on the existence of the parent company as having strength and resources which will come to the aid of the subsidiary in due course if need arises. Often in this sort of situation the subsidiary itself is without resources, or without resources of any consequence. It is subsidised by the parent company, and is viable only as long as the subsidies are forthcoming.
