Clause 156
DUTY OF DISTRICT HEALTH AUTHORITY OR NATIONAL

HEALTH SERVICE TRUST TO NOTIFY PARENT ETC.

Mr. Win Griffiths
(Bridgend)
: I beg to move amendment No. 788, in page 91, line 41, leave out subsection (3) and insert—

'(3) The health authority or trust shall inform the parent of the named person or any voluntary organisation that is likely to be able to give the parent advice or assistance in connection with any special educational needs that the child may have.'.

The amendment has been discussed at some length with the Special Education Consortium and the Spastics Society. It is relevant to the earlier debate about local authorities, and I hope that a few brief remarks will be sufficient to obtain a clear statement of the Government's view.

The amendment would ensure that a health authority or national health service trust provided the name of a person or voluntary organisation whose services would be available to the parents of a child who is likely to be statemented. The parents would then be able to obtain advice from a voluntary organisation which, because of its involvement with children of special needs, would be well qualified to give it.

When parents are faced by the statementing process they have little idea of what is involved. The practical support offered by the voluntary sector should be used to a greater extent. The efforts of everyone involved would probably be more successful if statutory and voluntary organisations worked together and with the parents. As soon as the parents are put in the picture about what help and advice is available, the statementing process can be undertaken without too many hitches. Unfortunately, hitches often occur in the current statementing process.

Most of the time, but not always, the health authority, through its contacts with the child, is the first to become aware that a third child may need special educational help to overcome his difficulties. The Education Act 1981 contains some general provisions about that process, but practice shows that they are not fully effective. In this short revisit to the 1981 Act, we should take the opportunity to reinforce those provisions to ensure that health authorities give parents information about voluntary organisations, which can help them during the statementing process.

If the local authority is also involved the current arrangements will be improved and that will help to initiate inter-agency co-operation between it and the health authority or trust and useful information sharing. That will ensure that the statementing process, instead of being a nightmare, as it is too often, will be undertaken with co-operation, knowledge and understanding by parents who will benefit from receiving help from voluntary organisations.
