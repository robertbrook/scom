LEGAL ADVICE AND ASSISTANCE (SCOTLAND) (FINANCIAL CONDITIONS) (No. 2) REGULATIONS 1978

10.30 a.m.

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Law Officers' Department (Mr. Arthur Davidson)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the Legal Advice and Assistance (Scotland) (Financial Conditions) (No. 2) Regulations 1978.

The Chairman
: Before we start, I would intimate that I think that it will be for the convenience of the Committee if we take at the same time the following regulations also set down for consideration:
  • The Legal Aid (Scotland) (Financial Conditions) Regulations 1978.
  • The Legal Aid (Financial Conditions) Regulations 1978.
  • The Legal Advice and Assistance (Financial Conditions) (No. 2) Regulations 1978.
    • The Question on all these regulations will be put at 12 o'clock at the latest, if no hon. Member has any objection to that procedure.

    Mr. Davidson
    : These regulations, as the Committee will know, increase the financial limits for legal aid in civil proceedings and for legal advice and assistance in civil and criminal matters. As you indicated, Mr. Doig, I shall introduce all of them together. As a formality, I have moved the motion relating to the first set of Scottish regulations, but the Committee will note that my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland is here, and he will deal with any Scottish matters arising. I am sure that it would not be overwelcomed were I to pretend a knowledge of Scottish law which I clearly do not have.

    Mr. Nicholas Fairbairn
    : Or English.

    Mr. Davidson
    : I should object to the hon. and learned Gentleman assuming that he had any knowledge of English law, so that applies both ways.

    4 As the Committee will know, in recent years the financial limits for legal aid and for legal advice and assistance have been increased annually in line with increases in supplementary benefits. In that way, the limits have kept pace with the rate of inflation. I have presented such regulations in Committee, as has my right hon. and learned Friend the Solicitor-General, and we have claimed no more for the regulations than that worthy but limited objective.

    The regulations before us today continue that practice, but I am glad to be able to say that this year they are simply a holding operation. Last year in the debate on similar regulations, at which, I know, many of my hon. Friends and one or two hon. Members opposite here this morning were also present, I concluded by saying—in a hardly memorable or elegent phrase, but I used it none the less—that I hoped one day it would be possible to announce that I would call dramatic increases in legal aid limits.

    While I cannot yet totally fulfil my hopes, I can look forward today, I think, to fulfilling them, and I hope to give what I might call, in a better phrase, a useful prologue to fulfilling those aspirations. My noble Friends intends, as he indicated in another place, to make further regulations in the early part of next year which will make significant and urgently needed improvements to the financial conditions for legal aid, advice and assistance, with the objective of bringing a far larger proportion of the population within the civil legal aid scheme. I know that that is desired by every member of the Committee. It is common ground that we wish to remedy the deficiency to which hon. Member after hon. Member has referred in every debate that we have had on the regulations.

    I wish now to deal with the regulations before the Committee. The legal aid regulations raise the lower income limit— that is, the limit below which a contribution from the litigant is not payable— from £760 to £815 a year and the upper limit—that is, the limit above which legal aid is not available—from £2,400 to £2,600 a year. Both limits, of course, relate to a person's disposable income. This figure, which has some bearing on my noble Friend's package to which I JPEG 5 shall refer later is arrived at after allowances have been made for dependants and deductions made for necessary expenses, such as income tax, rent, rates and any other reasonable commitments of the applicant.

    The regulations also increase the lower capital limit—that is, the limit for which no contribution is payable from capital—from £340 to £365, and the upper capital limit—that is, the limit above which legal aid is not normally available—from £1,600 to £1,700.

    My noble Friend has also made other legal aid regulations, which are not subject to affirmative resolution and which come into force at the same time as these regulations, increasing the allowances for dependants in the assessment of disposable capital. That will mean that the upper capital limit of £1,700 will be further increased to £2,080 for a married person with two children.

    The legal advice and assistance regulations that we are discussing make corresponding changes for legal advice and assistance, commonly known as the green form scheme. The upper disposable income limit for legal advice and assistance is increased from £48 to £52 a week and the disposable capital limit is increased from £340 to £365.

    My noble Friend has also made two other sets of legal advice and assistance regulations, which again are not subject to affirmative resolution and which come into force at the same time. Those regulations raise the lower disposable income limit for advice and assistance, below which that advice and assistance is free, from £23 to £25 a week. Consequential amendments to the scale of contributions for legal advice and assistance are made and increased allowances for dependants are prescribed for assessing disposable capital. The new capital allowances means that a married person with two children will be able to obtain advice and assisance if his disposable capital is less than £745.

    As I indicated earlier, the regulations are no more than an interim arrangement. They make modest increases, and I claim no more for them than that. My noble Friend felt that the practice of annual uprating should continue even though he is in the process of preparing a package 6 of significant improvements to the financial conditions for legal aid.

    For reasons that I shall briefly explain, it is not possible—much as I should like it to be—to give precise details of my noble Friend's proposal at this stage. However, I can help the Committee, as I have a duty to do, by giving a broad outline.

    The improvements that my noble Friend has in mind will gradually increase to a maximum of about £6 million a year. That is within the public expenditure plans announced by the Chancellor of the Exchequer on 9th November. At this stage, it might be useful if I were to set the proposed improvements that my noble Friend has in mind in the context of the civil legal aid scheme as it is today.

    As my noble Friend said in another place, despite the very low limits that have been imposed on the scheme by years and years of financial stringency, the scheme is, and has been, of immense value to a large number of people who without it would not have been able to enforce or defend their legal rights before the courts. I am sure that all hon. Members who have criticised the low number of people who have benefited will agree with that.

    Despite this financial stringency, the cost of civil legal aid has risen from £26 million in 1975–76 to about £38 million in 1977–78. To get a full picture of the full scope of legal aid one must add to that the cost of criminal legal aid in magistrates' courts, which, incidentally, also falls upon my noble Friend's Vote. This has risen from £14 million in 1975–76 to around £20 million in 1977–78.

    Mr. Malcolm Rifkind
    : Are these figures for the United Kingdom or do they relate only to England and Wales? If they relate only to the latter, can he give the figures for Scotland, too?

    Mr. Davidson
    : They relate to England and Wales. I shall certainly get the other figures for the hon. Gentleman.

    Mr. Edward Lyons
    : My hon. Friend has now given us the figures of expenditure for civil legal aid in England and Wales and for criminal legal aid in England and Wales in the magistrates' courts. Has he any figures for the cost of legal aid in the Crown courts?

    JPEG 7

    Mr. Davidson
    : My hon. and learned Friend anticipates my very next statement, which is that the cost of legal aid in the Crown court—which is a matter for my right hon. Friend the Home Secretary —is now about £23 million.

    Last year the number of claims submitted by solicitors for advice and assistance given by them under the green form scheme was aproximately 323,000. Over 147,000 legal aid certificates were issued in 1977–78 to cover actual legal proceedings and more than 20,000 of those certificates were for non-matrimonial proceedings in the High Court.

    These figures indicate that the scheme still permits a large number of people to pursue their remedies or defend their rights in the courts at a cost that they can at least afford.

    Mr. Edward Lyons
    : Are these figures net after recovery of some legal aid contributions from persons assisted or are they gross figures before such recovery?

    Mr. Davidson
    : I am beginning to wish I had not given the figures. They are net after deductions.

    I have given those figures and indicated that there are still a large number of people who benefit from legal aid, but the package of improvements which my noble Friend intends to introduce will substantially increase the coverage of the scheme and benefit many more people.

    My noble Friend is determined that the package should ensure that such resources as are available should go first to those in greatest need—namely, those on low earnings, families with low incomes and pensioners. I know that everyone will agree that the present low limits cause considerable hardship in those areas, and my noble Friend is determined to do all he can to end or ease that hardship as soon as possible.

    At the same time as improvements are made in the financial limits of the scheme, my noble Friend intends to take the opportunity to make a determined effort to reduce the present complexity of the financial conditions for obtaining legal aid. They are complex and, like the social security regulations, are a real jungle. He is determined also to simplify the present complicated and expensive system of legal aid assessment. This will 8 result, one hopes, in making even further resources available.

    As I say, it is not possible, much as I should have liked to do so, to give details of those proposals now, but I have been able to tell the Committee what my noble Friend has in mind as to his strategy for the future. The aim is to benefit those in greatest need to the greatest possible extent from the amounts which have become available, but this aim can be met only if the package can be worked out properly. That is why at this stage I do not think that it would benefit anybody if I were to try to be more precise than I have been. To work out the package it is necessary to adapt the existing scheme and those parts which can be adjusted to meet that need.

    Perhaps I could hint at some of the considerations which my noble Friend must and does bear in mind. The prime regulators are the upper and lower limits both as to income and capital and the amount which is taken by way of contribution once the disposable income or capital of the applicant exceeds the lower limit. In making adjustments one must not only take into account current earnings but also the pattern of what are loosely now called welfare benefits, and also—which is more important in this sphere—the highly complex system of disregards and discretions used in the assessment of disposable income and capital.

    As I am sure the Committee will know, the assessment of a person's resources is made in accordance with the Legal Aid (Assessment of Resources) Regulations 1960. Work is still proceeding on seeing whether it is possible to simplify those complex matters, and my noble Friend hopes to be able to give details of the scheme by early next year.

    The significant improvements which result from the package of changes will, I hope, be introduced in the early part of next year, and its basis will be to ensure that there is a substantial increase in the lower limit, namely, the limit below which legal aid is available without contribution. It is this move which will assist those in greatest need. It is also this which enables us to make a start on the simplification of the legal aid assessment procedure. There will be accompanying appropriate increases in the allowances JPEG 9 for dependants to ensure that such improvements as are made will also benefit those with families.

    I have mentioned that one of the regulators is the amount which is taken from an applicant by way of contribution. At the moment the amount of the contribution asked of assisted persons is fixed by statute and cannot be changed by regulation. Accordingly, my noble Friend intends to include in the legal aid Bill which, as hon. Members will know, was referred to in the Queen's Speech, a regulation-making power enabling him to adjust the amount of contribution to be called for. This is a crucial and important part of the proposal. Clearly, if the contribution payable by those only just above the limit at which legal aid is free is made smaller, this affects the general figures for financial eligibility. The Bill is therefore an integral part of my noble Friend's proposals, and he is hoping to introduce it in the near future.

    If the lower limits are increased it is, of course, necessary to make corresponding increases in the upper eligibility limit, namely, the limit above which people are not able to obtain legal aid. My noble Friend's intention is to bring back into the scheme by an increase in this limit a large number of households who have ceased to be eligible for legal aid. It has been indicated in successive Committees that at the lower level of income one can obtain legal aid but over a certain level, even if one's income is fairly modest, one is denied the benefit. That is a deficiency which my noble Friend hopes to eradicate.

    This matter has been dealt with not only in our Committees but also in the Report of the Legal Aid Advisory Committee. Mention is made of it in paragraph 15 of their 26th annual report. The report concluded that the percentage of the population eligible for legal aid had dropped significantly. Hon. Members have made that point many times. My noble Friend's proposals should help to restore much of the lost ground.

    If the income limits are increased, it will be necessary to increase both the lower and upper capital limits. My noble Friend intends to do that as well. Clearly there would have to be corresponding increases in the limits for advice and assistance under the green form scheme.

    10 I should make it clear that this package of proposals is without prejudice to any consideration by the Government of whatever recommendations may be made by the Royal Commission on Legal Services. As the Committee knows, the Royal Commission is dealing with a number of very important matters affecting legal services, including the proper balance between the legal aid scheme and publicly funded legal services, such as those rendered by law centres and citizens' advice bureaux, as well as with the difficult question of representation before tribunals. It would not be right for the Government to make additional commitments in these areas until the Royal Commission has reported.

    There is, however, one area where my noble Friend is resolved to give help, as he said in another place. I refer to help for law centres, which are an essential part of legal services, working in areas of greatest need. They are the very areas which my noble Friend the Lord Chancellor hopes to assist through his package. The package will, therefore, include some modest reinforcements for them pending the outcome of the Royal Commission's deliberations. I hope that what I have said indicates the determination of my noble Friend to put this package to the best possible use.

We welcome to these proceedings certain new faces and many already familiar. It is refreshing that this is now a holding operation.     It is a commentary on the state of our legal governmental administration—this is JPEG 13 not a party point but a general point which applies to lawyers and lawyer-politicians of whichever party happens to be in power—that over the years, particularly on this aspect, we have not received our fair share of the cake. In governmental terms, legal administration is divided three ways—namely, among the Home Office, the Lord Chancellor's Department and the Minister's own Department.     The aim must be—accepting the financial constraints on any Government—to return as quickly as we can to where we started in 1950. The figures are striking.     I have dealt so far with civil legal aid, but there is also the matter of criminal legal aid. As the Lord Chancellor said, there is a complexity of matters that can arise in such tribunals. I think that they should be covered by the scheme. However, if somebody does not want legal aid and is adequately represented by an experienced trade union official, or some- 14 one else, so much the better. No one needs to apply for legal aid.

    I am concerned about the complex case, and particularly with the person who might not be covered by his trade union in such proceedings. We know the various issues that can arise. The person concerned may, in effect, be going against his trade union and may have to "go it alone". Those are important matters which should be considered. There is a formidable case for an extension of legal aid.

    I know that all this involves money, but at least a sum of £6 million is coming into the kitty. I welcome that package.
