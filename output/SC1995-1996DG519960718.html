10.52 am

The Minister of State, Home Office (Miss Ann Widdecombe)
: I thought it would be helpful to the Committee to explain the purpose and effect of the changes in the immigration rules. I shall endeavour to answer the detailed matters raised during the debate at the end, if I am successful in catching your eye, Sir James.

Hon. Members with an interest in immigration matters will know that the cornerstone of United Kingdom law is the Immigration Act 1971, which came into force on 1 January 1973, and the immigration rules made under it. Those rules are rules of practice laid down for the guidance of those entrusted with the administration of the Act. They have been refined and developed over the years to reflect a variety of changes in circumstance and policy. The changes before us today reflect a number of such developments.

The proposal at paragraph 1 of the rules change derives from the scrutiny on enforcement of immigration laws, which found that many people who were either here unlawfully or who had been admitted on the clear understanding that they would not have recourse to public funds were gaining access to publicly funded benefits and services.

On 18 July 1995, the Home Secretary announced a programme of work agreed by the Government to implement recommendations made by the scrutiny. Those were designed to ensure that people who were here unlawfully or as visitors did not receive benefits to which they were not entitled.

One important and effective way of achieving that is to make it a condition that those given limited leave to enter or remain do not have recourse to public funds. The list of public funds has been extended to include, as the hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, North said, those non-contributory benefits that are now linked to immigration status. The list will be further amended as other amounts and services are similarly linked.

Paragraph 2 of the rules change makes application forms compulsory—

Mr. Alton
: Further to the intervention of the hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, North, will the Minister give us more detail about the number of people that those entitlement changes will affect? The hon. Gentleman asked about the number for the past 12 months, but how many people will be affected and how much will the measure save during the current period?

10

Miss Widdecombe
: If I can do so, I shall give the hon. Gentleman further information on that point later in the debate. If I cannot do so during the debate and if the information is available, I shall convey it to the hon. Gentleman in writing and send a copy to the rest of the Committee in due course.

Paragraph 2 of the rules change makes application forms compulsory for certain immigration applications. Compulsory forms will mean a better service to applicants at a lower cost to the taxpayer.

Under the old system, a clear request for an extension of stay constituted an application, but immigration applicants were often unsure about the information that they had to provide for their applications to be properly considered. Thus, we often received letters, such as those to which the hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, North referred, which in effect said little more than, "I want to stay longer."

Following such a letter, we would have to write to request further information. Often such requests would be for basic information such as full name and nationality, or even the purpose of the application. Self-evidently, that was inefficient and a source of delay. In the first quarter of 1997, we took 97 days on average to resolve non-asylum applications under that system.

Last year, we announced our intention to move towards the introduction of compulsory application forms to try to solve the problem. There can be few other areas of public life where the use of an application form is not already a perfectly natural part of the process. So, last June, we commenced a 12-month trial of voluntary application forms, during which applicants showed themselves willing and able to use the forms. We received many more than 6,000 completed forms, and applicants generally completed them with great care. The trial showed that applications made on those forms could be resolved far quicker. On average, it took us only 45 days to resolve applications made on the voluntary forms.

The need for us to make further inquiries was greatly reduced by requesting the basic information that we needed on the form. Inquiries were made in only 26 per cent. of application form cases, often because documents were missing or the form was not fully completed, but we needed to make further inquiries in about 62 per cent. of non-application form cases.

The pilot scheme conclusively showed that the use of application forms benefited applicants, the administration of the immigration system and the taxpayer. However, all applicants must use the scheme to realise its full benefits, which means that it must be compulsory. Although the trial was extremely successful, fewer than 5 per cent. of applicants decided to use the forms in the voluntary pilot scheme.

JPEG 11 During the voluntary trial, we engaged in consultation with a wide range of outside bodies, including the main immigration representatives, all United Kingdom universities and the Plain English Campaign. The forms were amended to take account of the comments received, many of which were favourable. For example, the Commission for Racial Equality said:

"The introduction of forms is clearly welcome."

The commission felt that the forms were user-friendly and as straightforward as was possible in the circumstances. The British Medical Association said:

"These are a very useful and important development in the service you provide."

In contrast, the Immigration Law Practitioners Association did not welcome the forms and sought leave to mount a judicial review soon after they became compulsory—not during the consultation period. Although we consulted ILPA, its detailed concerns—unlike those of other bodies—were not made known to us until that late stage. In the light of points made, however, we suspended the forms and undertook to revise them in two respects. First—the issue raised by the hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, North—the original forms required all relevant documents to be enclosed. We shall amend that requirement so that if there is a good reason why documents are not available—for example, a passport may have been stolen—the applicant can explain why and the application will still be valid.

Secondly, ILPA felt that on one or two questions the forms went beyond the strict requirements of the rules. I assure the hon. Gentleman that we are carefully checking that no more information is requested than the rules demand. He asked me to give an undertaking that we would operate on the basis that the forms would reflect what was demanded by the rules, and we shall do so. Once those revisions are complete, we shall reintroduce them with a view to restarting the scheme, probably in September.

In the short period before they were suspended, the forms proved to be extremely effective. In the public inquiry offices, applications were presented with all the requisite documentation and a fully completed form. Applicants did not seem to have difficulty with the new system. Applications were resolved more quickly and caller waiting times were reduced. The introduction of the forms will strengthen immigration control. Those who do not have a right to be in the United Kingdom will have their applications refused far more quickly.

I accept—and I make it transparent—that there will be a few applicants who leave matters to the last minute and then submit an invalid application. Those people risk losing any possible appeal rights if we refuse the subsequent valid application. We have 12 sought to minimise that risk by creating a document reception centre. Its sole function will be to decide the validity of purported applications—not the merits, but the validity. Any invalid applications will be returned quickly to the applicants with information that will help them to make a valid application.

If, despite all that, an application is received after leave has expired, it will still be considered in the usual way and many such applications will be granted. It should be noted that in 1995 the after entry and appeals directorate refused only about 8 per cent. of the applications that it received.

Although some may lose appeal rights, that is far outweighed by the benefits to the great majority from the introduction of compulsory forms. As it was a matter of debate on the unrelated Bill, I should point out that compulsory forms will have an effect on unscrupulous immigration advisers. I do not pretend that the forms will be a complete answer to that problem, but they will help.

Such forms are easy to use and state what is required. If applicants need help to fill them in, the forms point out that they can contact our telephone inquiry bureau. Applicants are required to sign the form, which means that we know that the application has been made with their knowledge and consent. Many thousands of applicants under the old system chose not to employ a representative. Forms make the system simpler and easier for all applicants and should reduce the need for them to seek help. Compulsory forms will also help solicitors who are not experienced in immigration law, who were mentioned by the hon. Member for Blackburn (Mr. Straw) during the unrelated Asylum and Immigration Bill debate.

Overall, the introduction of compulsory application forms will result in a better service to applicants and better value to the taxpayer. We will tighten control and there will be less scope for abuse of applicants by unscrupulous and exploitative immigration advisers. Clearly, it is right that we should go down the road that is proposed.

I turn briefly to the other changes. The change at paragraph 3 provides that applications for further leave to remain from sponsored students should normally be refused if their sponsor does not consent. That serves to protect the interests of overseas Governments and international organisations which sponsor students in the UK on the understanding that they will return to their own country at the end of the course.

The final major change before us is the replacement of the appendix indicating the nationals or citizens of countries who require a visa to come to the UK. The expanded list reflects changes brought about by the introduction of the European Union common visa list. Article 100c of the treaty on European Union required the Council to adopt a list of third countries whose nationals require visas to cross the external borders of the member states. The regulation was published in the JPEG 13 Official Journal of the European Community in October last year and we took the opportunity of this rules change to bring that into effect on 4 April 1996.

The initial proposal from the Commission contained a list of 126 countries. While I admit that there are still a number of countries from whom we would not otherwise have sought to require visas, the United Kingdom was successful in reducing the figure to 101. The result is that 14 countries needed to be added to the UK's current visa requirements. Two other countries on the common visa list—Tanzania and Gambia—have already been made subject to our visa requirements in advance of these changes.

In addition, we have made some small, technical amendments. For example, there is a technical change deleting Liechtenstein from the list of countries whose nationals may qualify as au pairs because Liechtenstein signed the European economic area agreement in May last year, enabling its nationals to benefit from free movement and employment rights. Another minor amendment concerns EC association agreements with central and south-east European countries so that there are now four more countries—Bulgaria, the Czech Republic, Romania and Slovakia—whose nationals benefit from those agreements.

They are sensible and practical amendments which, for all the reasons I have mentioned, enhance the effectiveness of the Government's immigration policy. They tidy up loose ends resulting from policy and practice changes and I commend them to the Committee.
