Clause 16.—(REGISTRAR'S POWER TO REQUIRE INFORMATION CONCERNING BIRTH TO BE GIVEN.)

10.30 a.m.

Question proposed [23rd June], That the Clause stand part of the Bill.

Question again proposed.

The Joint Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mrs. Judith Hart)
: When we rose yesterday I was just finishing our discussion on the Question "That the Clause stand part of the Bill". There were two very small things that I wanted to say. One was in answer to the hon. Member for Glasgow, Maryhill (Mr. Hannan), who asked me if telephone communications between registrars' offices would not cover the problem. It is made clear in Clause 14 that an informant must attend personally at the registrar's office where the registration is to be made, and Clause 13 makes it clear that births can be registered in one of two districts only, either where the birth takes place or where the parent lives. Telephone information alone cannot constitute the information the registrar needs. It has to be personal attendance, and one could not cover this by telephone information. If, however, the informant lived some distance away, the registrar would try to find an informant who would not have to travel such a distance. It would be only in the case of absolute necessity that this kind of move would be involved.

The hon. Member for Edinburgh, West (Mr. Stodart) had asked how a decree granted under Clause 16(3) could be enforced against an informant who was resident in England. Circumstances might well arise where a married couple had a child in Scotland and removed to England before the child was registered. The practical answer to this is that it is intended to instruct registrars that they should not normally serve notice on 52 people no longer resident in Scotland. If the father or mother had removed, they would be treated as being unable to give the right particulars and the registrar would pursue one or other of the requirements under Clause 14. It would be unusual for the registrar not to find a qualified informant in Scotland. It would be unlikely in 99 out of 100 cases that the registrar would have to ask an informant to come a long distance. This then is my answer to the points raised on Clause 16.

Mr. N. R. Wylie

Lady has just dealt, I do not see how a court would pronounce a decree on a summary application if the informant was in England, because the court would not have jurisdiction to do that. I have some doubts how it will. The Clause gives power to the registrar to require certain qualified informants to apply personally and to give the information which he has not already been given under the terms of Clause 14. How is the registrar to know that the birth has taken place if he has not been informed by the mother or father of the child or by some other informants? As far as the other informants are concerned, under Clause 14(1, b) they are only obliged to give information in the case of the death or inability of either the father or mother. Mrs. Hart
: There is nothing specifically new about this provision in the Bill. It has worked very well for 100 years. The Registrar General gets his information from a medical officer of health or a hospital and this is how he is able to try to track down cases where the birth has not been registered. This has been in practice for 100 years and I do not think there is any need to worry that in the course of the next 10 years or so this procedure will suddenly collapse. It is not a new provision.

If I may look back, the Register of Corrected Entries was created by the 1854 Act for the purpose of recording correct particulars in cases where the original entries, whether for births, deaths or marriages, JPEG 55 contained errors. It was what it said it was, a register of corrected entries. In practice, through the years however, there has been certain informal developments and the register has come to be used not only as a means of recording corrections but also for the recording of other events subsequent to registration such as decrees of divorce, decrees of paternity and bigamy convictions, and all sorts of other things.

Therefore, the title "Register of Corrected Entries" is no longer a correct title because it will record something rather different from and additional to corrected entries. Unless we could find a way of keeping the initials "R.C.E." we should have two sets of initials in use in these marginal annotations and throughout the whole work of the registrars, and this would be a real difficulty. So we have tried very hard—I have racked my brains and so has everybody else—to find another word beginning with "E" that would allow us to keep "R.C.E." and yet would be an accurate description of what this would be. The Committee has to decide whether it will bear the slight indignity of having "etc." in the Bill or whether it will deprive the Registrar General and the registrars of their happy association with the intials "R.C.E." Having said that, I must confess that I find myself unwilling to make the registrars and the Registrar General so unhappy as they would be if we took away their "E." Therefore, I find myself prepared to tolerate the use of the "Etc." because it means we have 56 an accurate description and because they can keep their "R.C.E." That is my explanation of why we have this word "Etc." and I hope the Committee will find it possible to accept it.
