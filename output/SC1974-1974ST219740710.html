Dr. Gilbert
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft instrument.

This order amends the 1972 order with a similar title. Its operative Article is 3( c), whose effect will be to permit industrial establishments in Winsford at present receiving regional employment premium to continue to do so for a further year. Without the order payments would otherwise cease from 8th September this year.

The Government are introducing the order because they take the view that, since we announced in the Budget Statement our decision to maintain the present arrangements for paying REP generally, while we review its future, it would be only equitable to do so for firms in the Winsford area. The order will extend the payments for one year. That will at least cover the period during which we are considering what future arrangements we want to make. I hope that my remarks will be adequate for the Committee's purposes. If there are any questions, I shall be happy to try to answer them.

DRAFT CALF SUBSIDIES (UNITED KINGDOM (VARIATION) (No. 2) SCHEME 1974

10.35 a.m.

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. E. S. Bishop)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft instrument.

It may be thought that there is little to say about this because of the variation proposed in the scheme, but I think that the Committee should have a brief explanation in view of the fact that there was another variation scheme a short time ago.

This variation scheme proposes to apply the £10 increase in subsidy rates which was recently approved for calves born in Great Britain to all calves born in the United Kingdom. In other words, this variation scheme provides for calves born in Northern Ireland to become eligible for the £10 increase.

The reason we are having to take two bites at this particular cherry is, I think, well known to Members of the Committee. Briefly, the original intention was to exclude calves born in Northern Ireland from the increase because, when the first variation scheme was drafted, a beef marketing subsidy, broadly equivalent in value to the £10 increase in the calf subsidy rates for calves born in Great Britain, was in operation in Northern Ireland; its purpose was to prevent distortion of trade across the Border as a result of the difference in the guide price in the Irish Republic and the United Kingdom.

The situation has since changed. My right hon. Friend the Minister announced in the House on 19th June that, following agreement reached in the EEC Council of Ministers on 17th-18th June, the guide price in the United Kingdom and the Irish Republic was to be the same from 1st July: that in consequence the special subsidy in Northern Ireland would end on that date, and that, subject to parliamentary approval, the higher rate of calf subsidy would become payable in Northern Ireland. The proposed variation scheme gives effect to the last of these decisions.

I should like to say a few words about the way this second variation scheme is 6 drafted, since some concern was expressed about the drafting of the first variation scheme. I think that the position is easier to understand if one bears in mind that the principlal scheme relates to the United Kingdom as a whole and that it originally provided for uniform rates to apply to calves throughout the country. As the principal scheme applies to the United Kingdom, a scheme which varies it must necessarily do the same and be made by the same Ministers even if the variation affects only part of the country. In fact, the first variation scheme replaced two paragraphs of the principal scheme by paragraphs providing for higher rates in Great Britain and for lower rates in Northern Ireland. The intention in the second variation scheme is to remove the difference in rates as between Great Britain, on the one hand, and Northern Ireland, on the other, and legislate for a uniform rate throughout the country.

I hope that this will sufficiently explain why the wording of the second variation scheme does not explicitly refer to an increase in subsidy rates in Northern Ireland. I can, of course, give an assurance that if the scheme is approved it will put Northern Ireland calves on exactly the same footing as calves in the rest of the United Kingdom.

We had a full debate on the earlier variation scheme ranging over the beef industry as a whole; and the Minister has since informed the House, in the course of the Supply debate on agriculture on 26th June of his intention to press immediately for direct interim action to be taken, in recognition of the need to assure beef producers that their returns will not drop below about £18 a live cwt. for clean quality cattle. I cannot add to this at present, but against this background I confidently commend the draft scheme to the Committee.

Mr. Bishop
: I think one can give that assurance. We shall watch the position carefully. When discussing the beginning of a scheme of this sort, clearly one has to watch the situation carefully to see what the trend is and what other action will be necessary to deal with it. I have made the point that, as a result of this measure, the total support for a beef producer will rise to £100 million per year—the equivalent of £2·50 per live cwt. This is a step in the right direction to deal with the problems which have been raised this morning.

With regard to the Northern Ireland position reference was made to Part II of the scheme which, of course, comes into stage B—the carcase aspect. Calves born anywhere in the United Kingdom on or after the 30th October 1973 and certified live—that is stage A—will, until further notice, be paid a subsidy of £18.50 if male, or £16·50 if female.

When carcases are presented for certification at stage B, the effect of the variation scheme will be that carcases certified anywhere in the United Kingdom on or after the 16th December next will, until further notice, be entitled to the same increased rates of subsidy. The cost of applying the increased rates in Northern Ireland will be about £5 million per year.

I think I have dealt with all the questions raised, possibly not all satisfactorily, but I assure the Committee that the matter will be kept very much under review and my right hon. Friend will not hesitate to take other action necessary to retain confidence.

Mr. West
: I am sure that the Minister will remember the concern expressed with regard to the calf slaughterings and that the high figure for the month of June quoted by my hon. Friend gives great cause for alarm. As those of us with practical experience of the workings of the farmers throughout the country know, generally speaking, milk is at a low price 20 during mid-summer and that is the time when farmers tend to concentrate more on the rearing of calves, particularly on the dairy farms, whereas, when milk is at a high price in the winter, the farmers ease off and tend to sell more calves.

If this rate of slaughtering goes on throughout the expensive period for rearing calves, I am afraid that the slaughtering rates will be much higher unless something is done, and this is why I pinpoint this matter.

With regard to Northern Ireland, I said earlier that the Minister's announcement of £1·76 per cwt. for cattle in Northern Ireland gave the impression that this scheme would last to January 1975.

The announcement was made in regard to cattle throughout the United Kingdom, and the Minister has said that this will not be implemented until he has the certification machinery established. Naturally, if I know the farmers—I know my inclinations, too—they are tending to hold up the stock for marketing until this new scheme comes into operation after the certification machinery has been set up. There will be many farmers throughout the United Kingdom who will hold up the sale of their cattle until the better market price comes into operation.

This will present again a tremendous glut on the market, a further depression in price, and a great cost to the Treasury. The Minister has said in the House, and to me personally, that he must talk further with members of the European Economic Commission about this new scheme. I would hazard a guess that the Minister would not have been prepared to put his head on the chopper, as it were, in announcing this scheme in the House if he had not a fairly good assurance that the scheme would be accepted by the Commissioners in the EEC.

I hope that no time will be lost in setting up this machinery, in order that the scheme can be implemented at the earliest opportunity.

Another plea I make on behalf of my fellow farmers in Northern Ireland is that, as we have the certification machinery in being, ready to put into operation, and now that we have lost the £1·76—and the farmers are disappointed, having bought cattle on the understanding that they would get the benefit of it when marketing the cattle—I believe that a JPEG 21 strong case exists for the early implementation of the scheme. There will be no delay in Northern Ireland in establishing the certification machinery, which, as I have said, is already in being.

I ask the Minister to impress upon his right hon. Friend the need—not only for the benefit of the farmers of Northern Ireland—to reduce the burden of expenditure that will undoubtedly fall upon the Treasury whenever the market is glutted, on the introduction of this scheme through cattle being held up for that period of marketing.

Mr. Bishop
