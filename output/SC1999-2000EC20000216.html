Telecommunications Legislation and Radio Spectrum Policy

[Relevant Documents: European Union Documents Nos. 14320/98, 12838/99, 12839/99 and 12840/99.]

10.30 am

The Minister for Small Business and E-Commerce (Ms Patricia Hewitt)
: I welcome the opportunity to debate the future regulation of Europe's communications infrastructure and services. I remind the Committee how extraordinarily fast that whole area is moving. The markets already create about 15 per cent. of European Union gross domestic product and they are generating one in every four new jobs. The communications market and the information society will therefore have to drive Europe's single market and to reduce regional and social economic differences. It is thus essential that we get the framework right to deliver cheap and fast telecommunications.

The telecommunications markets in Europe, with the exception of Portugal and Greece, were liberalised from 1 January 1998. The purpose of the package of measures that has been proposed by the Commission is to simplify, strengthen and update the regulation of the sector so as to ensure, first, that liberalisation is effectively implemented and secondly, that regulation is effective, consistent and light handed.

The Commission has reviewed the package of measures to liberalise the market and offered its blueprint for a new regulatory framework. The two-month period for public consultation closed yesterday, but our Government have arranged that the formal United Kingdom response will not be delivered to the Commission until the Committee has had the opportunity to discuss the issues.

The new regulatory framework has to take account of competition and other changes since the markets were liberalised in 1998. That is why the review communication of last November drew on and was accompanied by several other documents, and why we have such a full pack for the debate this morning. We have the Fifth annual report on the Implementation of the the Telecommunications liberalisation package, the results of the consultation held on next steps in radio spectrum policy, the results of the consultation held on the convergence Green Paper and a report on the development of the market for digital television in the European Union.

I will start with the fifth implementation report. Besides the review documentation itself, the Committee has particularly identified that report for debate. It looks at how member states have transposed the liberalisation directives into national law and how the rules have been applied in practice. The result is a comprehensive 2 assessment of a number of important regulatory themes: the structure and function of the national regulatory authorities—NRAs—licensing and interconnection, universal service, tariffs, numbering, radio frequency administration, rights of way and the state of competition in local access, which are all matters about which hon. Members are concerned.

The report finds that since full liberalisation at the start of 1998, competition in national telecommunications markets has been more vigorous than many had expected. The evidence is to be seen in significant price reductions across a wide range of services, an increasingly large choice of new services and a number of new market entrants. To give a little flavour of the impact of that competition, a 10-minute local call costs on average 13 per cent. less than it did in 1997 and long distance calls have fallen on average by 30 per cent. Therefore, we see real benefits to consumers as a result of liberalisation.

The Commission also identifies weaknesses that can be barriers, particularly for small businesses within the single market, and which need to be addressed in future regulatory framework. In particular, there is a low level of harmonisation between the licensing and interconnection regimes in different member states, divergence in how EC rules are implemented at national level, differences between the powers, resources and practices of the NRAs, poor enforcement of cost accounting rules, which can frustrate competition via interconnection and leased lines, lack of competition in local access, not only in the United Kingdom but right across member states—we are addressing that ourselves—and customers lack the leverage to complain effectively because tariff information is unclear.

That is the background and rationale for the Commission's regulatory framework review document, which is the next item on the agenda. The current regime has evolved over many years. It now contains more than 25 separate legal measures. It simply does not fit a new world in which markets are moving fast and broadcasting and telecommunications are converging.

The Commission advances five principles for the new regulatory framework. In future, regulations should be kept to a minimum; be based on clearly-defined policy objectives; strike the right balance between flexibility and legal certainty; evolve towards technological neutrality; and, whether they are agreed globally, regionally or nationally, they should be enforced as closely as possible in line with the activities that are being regulated.

Following those principles, the Commission proposes a structure that will include some sector-specific directives, non-binding measures through guidelines and self-regulatory codes that would be consistent with the directives but allow a much faster response to market changes, and gradually greater reliance upon the general competition rules of the treaty.

As far as the sector-specific legislation is concerned, the Commission proposes that five instruments will replace 20 current ones: the framework directive identifying specific policy objectives and four specific JPEG 3 directives eliminating provisions that are no longer necessary and setting limits for NRAs' discretion. The new framework would cover licensing, access to infrastructure—in other words, the local loop—and interconnection, universal service and privacy and data protection.

Accompanying that would be the new "soft law" measures—the guidelines and codes of practice. That makes very good sense. In such a fast-moving market we need co-regulation wherever possible, with Governments working in close partnership with business and consumers. Indeed, I have just come from launching Trust UK, which is an example of such a co-regulatory approach.

The next item to which we need to turn our attention deals with radio spectrum matters. It may help if I explain that the proposal for a European Parliament and Council decision on spectrum policy, which would form the basis for further harmonisation at Community level, is potentially wide-ranging. We fully share the objectives of rapid and effective action at EU level to secure spectrum harmonisation where major Community interests are at stake. However, we have told the Commission that we will examine carefully the specific proposals that it is likely to produce in the next few months. We welcome the fact that it has acknowledged the importance of the role of the CEPT—European Council for Post and Telecommunications)—, which is the 42 European-nation body, in international spectrum management and its desire to build upon rather than duplicate existing mechanisms. However, we want to ensure that any delegated authority for this purpose given to the Comission under the comitology procedures does not bypass the machinery of the European Parliament and Council of Ministers in areas that should properly be subject to political control.

At the Telecoms Council on 30 November 1999 I gave this regulatory blueprint a warm welcome, as did all my colleagues from other member states. We endorsed the five principles to which I have referred. The Telecoms Council will meet next on 2 May. At that point, we shall not have any new legislative instruments before us, but we shall need to react quickly to the report that the Commission will release in mid-April on its consultations about the future regime. This debate therefore provides a valuable and timely opportunity to sharpen the UK contribution to that process. We have exercised a leading and influential role in the development of EU thinking on convergent markets and it is essential that we keep up that work.

(Rutland and Melton)
: I thank the Minister for her opening remarks. By and large, we Opposition Members agree with the tenor of her comments—the wish to see continuing liberalisation and 4 increased competitiveness. Oftel has made it clear that the reason for that timetable of July next year is that we have to go through a process whereby BT and the competing operators trial the process of co-location within the local district exchanges. Oftel has also made it clear that if as a result of the pilots that are taking place this year it becomes possible to bring forward the July 2001 deadline, that will be done.

In relation to the rest of the European Union, the European Commission is itself proposing in a directive that local loop unbundling should be mandated across all member states. It has already been agreed in several member states and implemented in others and is the subject of consultation in many. We are all proceeding apace on the issue of local loop unbundling.

The hon. Gentleman asked about the backbone across the Atlantic and possible impending mergers. Any mergers would be a matter for either national or European competition authorities and it would certainly not be appropriate for me to comment on such matters.

Ms Hewitt
: I certainly have not had any complaints about unfair competition in relation to transatlantic access. Enormous investments are being made in fibre optic links across the Atlantic, and those are increasingly being rolled out, not simply within the United Kingdom but across the European Union and indeed stretching into the Asia-Pacific region. Those fibre optic internet protocol backbones will enable the transmission of extraordinary volumes of data, far higher than anything that we are seeing at the moment or have seen in recent years. If there are concerns about anti-competitive behaviour or issues to do with interconnection regimes, those should be taken to the appropriate regulatory authorities where they will be properly dealt with.

Dr. Stephen Ladyman
(South Thanet)
: Further to that answer, many of the world's largest telecommunications companies are now European companies and will require free access to spectrum in markets outside Europe. Is the free access to spectrum for non-European companies, which these plans will give in Europe, reflected in other markets that Europeans may want to get into? If not, will we implement the plans in such a way as to strengthen our negotiating position for access into those other world markets?

Ms Hewitt
: My hon. Friend raises an extremely interesting and pertinent point. We, the European Union and the Commission take the view that there should be non-discriminatory access to radio spectrum. We believe that that will enable us to create a highly competitive market at the leading edge of technological development, which will bring very large benefits to European consumers and business. Because in Europe we have a proper system of spectrum management and we have harmonised spectrum allocation, we are way ahead—probably by two years or more—of the United States in mobile telephony. The United States failed to deal with the issues of interoperability and spectrum allocation and thus has not been able to take full advantage of mobile telephony because one cannot use the same system across a variety of different states.

Ironically, we found at the last Telecoms Council that there had been some correspondence between the Commission and the American Government on access to the European spectrum. The suggestion was being made by the American authorities that the way in which spectrum was being allocated for third generation mobile in Europe might discriminate against American operators seeking to enter the European market.

As member states, we all strongly backed the Commission's response to the American authorities, but it is scarcely appropriate for them to complain about non-existent barriers to entry into the European market, when we are still trying to obtain proper access for European operators to American markets. We will continue, as will the Commission, to prosecute that issue with considerable vigour.

Mr. Tim Loughton
(East Worthing and Shoreham)
: I first declare an interest in an internet company.

In view of the Minister's comments about telecommunications competition in Europe being more vigorous than expected, does she agree that in future we should perhaps rely more on competition law and less on sectoral regulation when dealing with telecoms companies?

Ms Hewitt
: The relationship between general competition law and sector-specific telecommunications regulation is an interesting issue that we must grapple with at international and European level.

The United Kingdom Government's view is that we should move towards greater reliance on general competition law and that, as telecommunications markets are opened up and become more competitive, there is less need for sector-specific regulation. When the Director General of Telecommunications published his JPEG 7 strategy last month, he made it clear that he intends to lighten the regulatory touch as markets become more competitive, but in some circumstances a sector-specific approach may still be required—for instance, in relation to universal service or to issues such as leased lines, local loop unbundling and roaming, which are utterly specific to the telecommunications sector, where a great deal of sectoral and technical knowledge is needed and where, as we said in relation to the local loop, competition does not yet exist, so that, particularly on interconnection, the regulator must ensure that competition is opened up.

As that greater competition is achieved, as it has been in the mobile telephone market, spectrum allocation issues need to be dealt with only as long as there is spectrum scarcity and spectrum must be allocated to allow efficient operation and give proper economic value. One does not need a sector regulator to deal with interconnection pricing issues in the way that one still does for the local loop.

As our national sector regulator, Oftel will make full use of the competition powers in the Competition Act 1998 when they come into force next month. It is not a question of having either competition law or the sector regulator: the two can go together, but driving in the direction of greater reliance on general competition law.

Ms Hewitt
: I am grateful to my hon. Friend for raising that important issue, because there is concern about possible threats to health from low-level radiation. The National Radiological Protection Board, our national body that deals with these issues, has confirmed that there is no scientific evidence to support the proposition that, except in the narrow band around the mast, there is any threat to health from low-level radiation. However, we have already established the Stewart committee to conduct an independent review of all the scientific evidence so that the Government can ensure that our policy is based on the best available scientific evidence. It is for the telecommunications operators and the local authorities to ensure that radio masts are properly sited and access to the immediate vicinity properly barred. A regime is in place that deals adequately with the health issues, but the Stewart review will ensure that we have the best possible scientific evidence, which we shall want to discuss with our European partners in the context of the new telecommunications framework.

Miss Anne McIntosh
(Vale of York)
: May I press the Minister a little further on the competition aspect? I appreciate that the Competition Act 1998 has not yet come into effect and that we are simply applying the European competition framework, but the industry feels that the original 1998 liberalisation framework has not worked and is not boosting competition sufficiently and that monopolies still exist in all but name. The companies that had a public monopoly continue to be the big guys across the European market.

Is the Minister satisfied that the documents allow for sufficient competition and for regulatory intervention that would be proportionate to the prospects of competitive entry for smaller players and new entrants to the market?

Ms Hewitt
: Yes, I believe that the proposals will provide an adequate framework, but I stress that the United Kingdom has a highly competitive telecommunications market. Clearly, there is not competition within the local loop, although there is competition from the cable operators and, increasingly, from mobile telephone operators. That is why national and international call charges have fallen very fast.

The mobile telephony market is also highly competitive. Because the Radiocommunications Agency has been able, with great technical ingenuity, to determine that five licences will be available within the third generation spectrum, we have been able to ensure through its design that there will be at least one new entrant into the third generation mobile market, a move that is being watched with great interest by our European partners. Certainly the former monopoly operator is by no means the dominant player in the mobile telephone market or the market for international call charges: it has significant market power, but it is not in the same position as it is in relation to the local loop.

The picture will vary from one member state to another, depending in part on the speed of liberalisation in those countries, but the Commission is clearly determined to drive through implementation of the liberalisation directive, and its review process is very powerful. Moreover, it is putting in place a framework that will ensure that the national regulatory authorities have the powers that they need and work increasingly closely together with a consistent use of consistent powers. That, in turn, will help to give operators, who will increasingly be pan-European, the certainty that they need across the emerging single telecommunications market.

Mr. Ian Bruce
(South Dorset)
: I shall be making a speech later and I am not sure whether this is the appropriate time to declare an interest, but I should point out that I advise the Telecommunication Managers Association, and have shares in Cable and Wireless Communications and BT. I want to ask the Minister about jurisdictional issues arising from regulation between Europe and the United Kingdom. The hon. Lady said that everyone in the UK agrees that regulation will be reduced, but the Utilities Bill, which is being considered in Committee, includes JPEG 9 27 pages of new primary legislation that will introduce regulation-making powers for various social issues. Will the Minister comment on that, and in particular on the jurisdictional relationship between Europe and the UK?

Ms Hewitt
: I hope that the hon. Gentleman will not allow anti-European paranoia to run away with the debate. There is no question of the European Commission trying to get its hands on the fruits of the spectrum auction.

I do not think that the jurisdictional issue that the hon. Gentleman raises poses a real problem. There is an EU framework for competition law and telecoms regulation of which we must take account. In many cases, we have to translate international law or action into sector-specific directives. Legislation that covers matters covered by European law is compatible with European law. For example, the Electronic Communications Bill, which has recently completed its legislative passage through this House, will among other things implement the European Union directive on electronic signatures. We would have enacted the Bill in any case, but it also brings into force that directive.

As I said, the European Commission makes it clear that, wherever a policy is set—and it will be set at different levels according to what the policy is—the implementation of regulation should be as close to the operators as possible. That is why it has rightly rejected calls for a European telecoms regulator. Such a regulator is not on the agenda.

On spectrum management, decisions on which part of spectrum to allocate to which purpose are taken at a wider than EU level. They are taken within CEPT but, through the Commission, EU member states effectively speak with a single voice, ensuring a satisfactory result. However, allocation of that spectrum is a matter for national authorities. In respect of proposals for the new telecoms framework and the way in which spectrum should be made available, the Commission has changed its position markedly in the past two years, and I welcome that. As the hon. Gentleman said, we are auctioning third generation spectrum. Two years ago, the Commission was hostile to the idea of auctions and believed that administrative allocation—or so-called beauty contests—was the proper way forward.

We believe that in the context of third generation mobile, which involves a limited amount of very valuable spectrum, an auction provides the best way of securing the most economically efficient outcome, because it allows the market players, rather than the Government, to decide the value of the spectrum. Not only does the European framework allow us to use 10 auctions where we decide that it is the right thing to do, but, interestingly, other Governments are increasingly doing the same. For example, the Dutch Government have already announced their intention to use an auction for spectrum allocation, and the German Government are likely to use an auction for their third generation mobile allocation, which will follow shortly after ours. Other member states are looking to our experience and expertise in this matter.

Finally, we welcome the Commission's suggestion, in its proposed new framework, that it should in future permit spectrum trading. That is not currently permitted under European law, but we believe that in certain circumstances it can offer a simple, efficient, market-based mechanism for the further allocation of scarce spectrum. We want to be able to use it here, and welcome the fact that the European Commission is proposing a change to the European regime that will allow us to do so.

Mr. Loughton
: With regard to the fruits of auction, as the Minister put it, the major beneficiary will be the Treasury. Will she comment on the revenue that the Treasury raises from the allocation of band widths and third generation licences for mobile telephone companies, for which I gather that the rates were increased? Does she believe that the technology tax of a digital television licence fee has any competitiveness implications in a European context?

Ms Hewitt
: The third generation spectrum auction involves no risk to our competitiveness; quite the contrary. It enables the 13 competing operators that have indicated their intention to bid for the spectrum to decide for themselves the value that they place on each of the licences that we are auctioning. The focus of spectrum allocation is not to maximise revenues to the Treasury, but to ensure the most economically efficient allocation of an extremely scarce resource.

The idea that it will be too expensive and prevent new operators entering the market represents a misunderstanding of the nature of the market. An operator that cannot afford to pay for the spectrum is unlikely to be able to afford the infrastructure investment—in masts and so on—that will be required to use the third generation spectrum. The cost of the spectrum itself is a small part of the investment that mobile operators will make in exploiting the extraordinary potential of third generation spectrum. Anyone who suggests that the Government should set an administrative price in preference to allowing the market to decide, or that we should simply give the spectrum away, clearly does not understand that the auction is the best way to allow market operators and entrants to value the spectrum for themselves and to ensure that the operators that secure the licences have the resources to make the necessary investment to exploit their value. The route that we are taking will enhance competition and competitiveness.

The digital licence is being considered by the Government in the light of the Davies report, and no decision has yet been made.

The context for defence has changed significantly since the end of the cold war, but the military need for spectrums has not reduced markedly. Modern warfare places a high premium on mobility and on the instantaneous and highly efficient communication of large volumes of data. High band width mobile communications are therefore becoming increasingly important.

Much military training that is carried out by United Kingdom forces in Europe is now conducted at home, which places even greater pressure on the band width that is allocated to the MOD. The MOD keeps its spectrum requirements under review, and I am glad to say that the Radiocommunications Agency and the MOD agreed sharing arrangements under which we can make military spectrums available to civil services, on a shared or an exclusive basis, subject to overriding military use in an emergency. That is a sensible and practical way forward.

Unlike many other countries, we charge the MOD for the spectrum that it occupies. We already have in place an efficient pricing incentive which should encourage the MOD to make its use of spectrum as efficient as it can and to make shared use arrangements when it is possible to do so.

On the cost of unmetered calls, the hon. Member for South Dorset referred to ISDN. That is a classic example of what happens when one backs one kind of technology 20 that does not work out especially well. BT is making a substantial investment in ADSL and in the local loop. The first 400 exchanges will come on-line next month and an aggressive roll-out programme will follow.

The retail costs of ADSL have not yet been announced by BT, but we need to consider the cost of leased lines. At a recent Select Committee hearing, the Director General of Telecommunications said that in retrospect he felt that Oftel had not pursued the matter sufficiently aggressively. It is now making rapid progress in relation to benchmarking and the cost of leased lines.
