Draft Jobseeker's Allowance (Amendment) Regulations 1996

4.30 pm

The Minister of State, Department for Education and Employment (Mr. Eric Forth)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Draft Jobseeker's Allowance (Amendment) Regulations 1996.

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the draft Jobseeker's Allowance and Income Support (General) (Amendment) Regulations 1996 and the draft Social Security (Adjudication) Amendment Regulations 1996.

Mr. Forth
: May I, at the outset, say how sorry I am—I am sure that other members of the Committee join me—that the hon. Member for Makerfield (Mr. McCartney) is unable to be with us today. I understand that he is indisposed, and I send him our best wishes for a speedy recovery.

The Committee will recall that the main set of Jobseeker's allowance regulations was debated and approved by the House in January 1996. In that debate and during the passage through the House of the Jobseekers Act 1995, hon. Members were able fully to consider the many facets of the jobseeker's allowance and what would make it different from, and superior to, the help currently given to unemployed people. With the implementation of JSA in October this year, we shall be making jobs—and the pursuit and winning of jobs—the focus of all our efforts on behalf of unemployed people.

When the principal set of regulations was laid before the House, we made it clear that the Government might need to come back to the House with further regulations, including amendments to the main JSA regulations, before JSA was implemented. We constantly review the structure of all social security benefits, to ensure that they remain as consistent and equitable as possible—that they reflect the Government's policy intentions and provide the taxpayer with the best value for money. As part of that process, several changes have been made to income support since the JSA regulations were introduced.

An essential feature of the design of income-based JSA is that it should be aligned with income support when appropriate. Those changes in income support now need to be reflected in JSA, to ensure that the provisions will also operate in JSA when it implemented in October.

4 We also recognised in January that some corrections might need to be made to what an extensive and complex body of regulations. I remember some fascinating exchanges with the hon. Member for Makerfield over the definition of N and our promise to amend it at the earliest opportunity. Further detailed scrutiny of the regulations has thrown up a number of similar points of detail that we aim to set right in these amending regulations. Furthermore, the work of the central adjudication services in preparing the guidance of the chief adjudication officer on JSA has brought to light some ambiguities in the regulations. We take this opportunity to clarify the main regulations, to ensure that there is no confusion over interpretation.

Most of the amendments made by these regulations are minor and I will not seek to describe in detail all the regulations.

Mr. John Patten
(Oxford, West and Abingdon)

The Chairman

Mr. Forth
: So not to the Committee's patience, it will be convenient to highlight a few of the significant elements of the regulations.

During our recent debate on the Government's project work pilot regulations, the hon. Member for Manchester, Withington (Mr. Bradley) asked why we had not provided in the JSA regulations for risks to health and safety to be cited as "good cause" for leaving an employment programme or training scheme. I hope that he will be pleased to see regulation 6 of the first set of amending regulations, which reflects what my hon. Friend the Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security confirmed during that debate.

Regulation 10 introduces new provisions in the jobseeker's allowance regulations covering persons from abroad and asylum seekers. Those provisions mirror changes in income support introduced after the principal JSA regulations had been agreed. The Government believe that, as a general rule, the taxpayer should not be called upon to provide a benefit income for people from abroad. The regulation will ensure that only those people who claim asylum on their arrival in the United Kingdom and who have permission to work will be supported by the public purse while they look for work. The Committee may recall that the equivalent income support regulations have already been the subject of a full debate in the House and in another place. The provisions do no more than bring JSA into line with income support.

Regulation 11, is required because of changes in income support. The rules governing the treatment of capital belonging to claimants in residential care, nursing homes of certain other types of residential accommodation were recently changed so that those JPEG 5 claimants will be allowed £16,000 of capital before they lose their entitlement to benefit, and up to £10,000 will be wholly disregarded when calculating the benefit to which they are entitled. The number of people in JSA who fall into that category may be small, but we are anxious to ensure that they benefit from the same improvements as those on income support.

The Jobseeker's Allowance and Income Support (General) (Amendment) Regulations that were laid on 30 April are concerned mainly with clarification and with keeping JSA aligned with income support. Regulations 3, 4 and 6 are all concerned with ensuring that people with disabilities can, at the same time, benefit from the provisions for part-time students, volunteers and those on temporary lay-off and restrictions on their availability due to their mental or physical condition. The problems in ensuring that those different regulations interacted properly were identified by the Disability Alliance, and I am grateful to the alliance for alerting my officials and enabling us to set matters right. The majority of the other amendments similarly correct ambiguities.

Regulation 33 is an amendment not to JSA regulations but to income support regulations. It will ensure that claimants who care for people with disabilities are not faced with the choice of giving up their caring responsibilities or losing benefit. At present, a carer's entitlement to income support is linked to the actual payment of attendance allowance or the higher or middle rate of disability living allowance care component by the person for whom he cares. From 7 October, carers will also be entitled to IS when the person for whom they care has claimed attendance allowance or DLA but the claim has not been decided, or when the claim has been decided and AA or DLA will be paid from a future date.

The Social Security (Adjudication) Amendment Regulations are, in the main, amendments consequential on the introduction of JSA. In most cases, the amendments amount to little more than the replacement of references to unemployment benefit and income support with references to JSA. However, we require two new adjudication provisions for JSA. Regulation 56A enables an adjudication officer to decide a JSA claim in the jobseeker's favour and for JSA to be paid while the officer is still considering the separate question of a sanction. That makes good commitments that were made during the passage through the House of the Jobseekers Bill that JSA would continue to be paid in full in advance of any sanction decision. Regulation 56B provides that, when a claimant switches to JSA from income support, he will not have to serve the three waiting days that are customary to new claims to JSA.

For the most part, the regulations do not introduce new provisions. They are necessary to ensure that the provisions of income support and JSA are kept broadly 6 in line where appropriate, and that there is no confusion about the interpretation of certain provisions when JSA is introduced. I commend the regulations to the Committee.

Training is currently in process and it is expected that all necessary training will have been completed before 7 October when JSA comes into operation.

I was asked specific questions about the new short-time rules, when it was alleged that workers will be forced to leave their employers without redundancy payments. We fully accept that people should be able to claim JSA if they are laid off or put on short time because of temporary industrial problems. For the first 13 weeks, such workers will be treated as available, if they are prepared to resume full-time work with their existing employer or to take casual work while the problems remain. Those problems must be temporary and time-limited. It is not the function of the benefit system indefinitely to subsidise employers. After 13 weeks, people who have been laid off will be able to continue to claim JSA but, like any other claimant, they will have to be prepared to take up a full-time job if one becomes available, even if that means leaving their existing employer. There will of course be no change to the special procedure under which people can apply for redundancy payment if they have been laid off our put on short time for more than four weeks. There is no reason for them to miss out on redundancy payment entitlement.

On the matter of the letter to my hon. Friend the Minister of State Department for Education and Employment on the subject of project work, I do not have the answer to that question to hand, and either I or my hon. Friend the Minister will write to the hon. Gentleman.

I was asked about persons under 18 and contributory JSA. That was a purely drafting point raised by the central adjudicating service, which was concerned that there might be somebody in that class who would otherwise suffer—that is why the order was amended. Few, if any, people are likely to come within that class.

I was asked concerning asylum seekers how much has been saved to date, the costs to local authorities and how much has been refunded to local authorities. It is too early to give the Committee a helpful answer, 16 but I shall write to the hon. Gentleman with such information as is available. The important point is that the numbers are already showing signs of decreasing. We estimated savings of £129 million in the first year—taking into account transitional protection. We cannot readily estimate the deterrent effect of the Government's proposals.
