Adoption (Intercountry Aspects) Bill

11 am

The Chairman
: I should like to say at the outset that adequate notice should be given of amendments. As a general rule, I do not intend to call starred amendments, including any starred amendments that we may come to during an afternoon sitting of the Committee.

Clause 1
REGULATIONS GIVING EFFECT TO CONVENTION

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Mark Oaten
(Winchester)
: May I say what a pleasure it is to serve again under your chairmanship, Mr. Malins. The previous occasion was on the Welfare Reform and Pensions Bill, which was complex, long and detailed, and you steered us through two months of proceedings during which I learned a lot about the procedures of the House. I think that I can guarantee that the proceedings on the Bill before us will not be as controversial, or last as long, as those on the previous Bill.

The Adoption (Intercountry Aspects) Bill is important and recognises that, in recent years, there has been an increase in the number of couples deciding to adopt children from foreign countries. The Bill will put in place protections and controls that recognise that trend.

The Hague convention, which is central to the Bill, was supported by the Government five years ago, but is yet to be ratified. The Bill will put that right. It allows for good practice to be put in place, recognising that local authorities have a duty to deal with intercountry adoption. It will tidy up some of the questions on entitlement to British nationality for children who are adopted in that way. It will make it clear to those who wish to bypass the due process of adopting a child in a foreign country that it is unacceptable to do so, and that bringing children into this country without going through the proper process will result in legal action against them.

I want to put on the record my gratitude to hon. Members of all parties, especially those who have come to the Committee today. I was encouraged by the lengthy and positive discussion that we had on Second Reading. I know that the Under-Secretary of State for Health, the hon. Member for Barrow and Furness (Mr. Hutton) is on the Floor of the House, but I want to put on the record my gratitude to him for the support that he and the Department of Health have provided in preparing the Bill.

4 Clause 1 is a major and important part of the Bill. It will enable the United Kingdom to give effect to the 1993 Hague convention, which was signed in January 1994. It will also provide the framework for setting minimum standards for processing intercountry adoption applications that work in the best interests of children who are adopted by people living overseas. The convention is drafted flexibly to allow countries that have signed up to its articles to be able to absorb them into their own substantive law. That is what clause 1 and other clauses will achieve. We signed and gave our support to the convention many years ago. It is long overdue for ratification and I am pleased that clause 1 will enable us finally to join the convention and ratify it.

Mr. Andrew Hunter
(Basingstoke)
: I warmly endorse the words of welcome extended to you, Mr. Malins, by the hon. Member for Winchester (Mr. Oaten). I have a few brief questions on clause 1.

Like most hon. Members, I approach the problem with constituency experience. Two issues concern me. The first is the length of time that it can take for local authorities to complete the process of granting adoption rights to parents. The second issue, which came to my attention recently as part of a constitutency case, involves the recognition of intercountry adoption. A United Kingdom citizen who adopts a child from another country may not have the legitimacy of that adoption recognised by the UK authorities. What will happen when that child seeks British citizenship? Does the Bill deal with those concerns?

Mr. Oaten
: I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman for raising those points. He asked about time scales and whether the Bill will help to speed up local authorities. The intention is to remind local authorities that they have a statutory function to deal with intercountry adoption, which is dealt with in other clauses. I hope that regulations and good practice will encourage authorities to act. The hon. Gentleman will be aware that Hampshire is setting good practice by speeding up the process. We should be able to turn to other authorities and say that if Hampshire can complete the process in this time scale, then it can be done in other parts of the country.

An important effect of the Bill is to implement the Hague convention, which will speed up the time scales and the process of a child's adoption between signatory countries. A reduction in the number of requirements and court cases will also slim down the time involved. As for the ability to recognise adoptions, the Bill incorporates the duty that is in the convention to acknowledge an adoption that has been set up by another convention country.

Mr. Tom Clarke
(Coatbridge and Chryston)
: I, too, congratulate the hon. Member for Winchester for getting his Bill this far.

I have one or two points to make which will help me to set clause 1 in the context of the Bill. My role in international development made me aware of some of the problems. Parents in underdeveloped or war-torn countries understandably want to give their child the JPEG 5 advantage of growing up in a relatively wealthy country but, a year or two later, they may want the child back. City of Glasgow council, which is 10 miles from my constituency, has to its great credit been making arrangements for children of Kosovar refugees. The Bill may have an impact on them.

My last point concerns clarification of clause 1. Given the plans of the hon. Member for Winchester for the implementation of the Bill and the fact that local authorities will have a pivotal role, have the Government given him an idea of their view on resources?

Mr. Oaten
: The right hon. Member for Coatbridge and Chryston (Mr. Clarke) makes an important topical point given the sad events in Kosovo. The instinct of the British public when they see the images on television is to rush out and adopt a child. As the right hon. Gentleman said, there are problems with that. Relatives and parents who are presumed dead during a war may merely have been dispersed. It would be wrong if an adoption were rushed through and it was subsequently discovered that the parents were alive in another part of the world. Such cases may happen during wartime. That is why there is a convention that, during conflicts, permanent adoptions do not take place until at least two years after the conflict occurred. One hopes that that period will be sufficient to ensure that no relatives are still alive. It would be improper to break that convention.

Sadly, the Prime Minister has made it clear on many occasions that the refugees from Kosovo who are currently coming to this country should not be found homes for adoption because they want to go back to Kosovo. He expressed that view strongly yesterday when he visited the area.

The right hon. Member for Coatbridge and Chryston asked whether the Government had made resources available for good quality and good practice. He will know that private Member's Bills cannot incur costs, and that such considerations do not apply to the Bill. However, the critical issue is not cost but education and awareness in social services departments and local authorities. Some authorities perform the task very well, and they have provided a first-class service with very few additional costs. The Bill's provisions will help other authorities to provide a similar service without necessarily involving additional expenditure.

Clause 2
CENTRAL AUTHORITIES AND ACCREDITED BODIES

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Hunter
: Will the hon. Member for Winchester comment on paragraphs (a) and (b) in subsection (1), which state: 6

"The functions under the Convention of the Central Authority are to be discharged … in relation to Wales by the National Assembly".

Paragraph (a) states that those functions will be carried out

"separately in relation to England and Scotland by the Secretary of State".

One might have expected a devolved power to be granted to Scotland. What is the rationale for doing so in relation to Wales but not in relation to Scotland?

Mr. Oaten
: As the hon. Gentleman says the clause deals with the establishment of central authorities and their powers. Perhaps I should explain the general position before I answer his question in detail.

The central authorities will be responsible for implementing the Hague convention, and, in England, Scotland and Wales, the Department of Health will continue to be responsible for inter-country adoption. The functions to be discharged by the central authorities will differ little from current practice. It makes good practical and economic sense for the respective Department of Health offices to discharge the central authorities' functions.

When the Hague convention recommended that the central authorities should be set up, the intention was that a Government body should be involved. Later clauses deal with the process by which that will be done in Scotland and by the National Assembly for Wales. As we shall discuss later, there is a difficulty in relation to Scotland. Moreover, although initial meetings have occurred, the Assembly's first historic meeting is not due to take place until 1 July. The timing of the relevant legislation means that it is not yet possible to deal with the situation in Scotland in the Bill. I shall discuss that matter further when we come to later clauses.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 2 ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Clause 3 ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Clause 4
EFFECT OF CONVENTION ADOPTIONS IN ENGLAND AND WALES

Mr. Oaten
: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 3, line 5, leave out 'a child adopted under'.

The Chairman
: With this, it will be convenient to take amendments Nos 2, 3 and 4.

Mr. Oaten
: The amendment and those grouped with it will have a minor effect. It clarifies the intention of proposed new subsection 3A to section 39 of the Adoption Act 1976, which will enable an application to be made to the High Court by any person adopted under the convention. The amendment would remove from line 25 the obtrusive and irrelevent word, "above". As drafted, the proposed new section could be interpreted to permit only children under 18 to apply to the High Court. Clearly, such a restriction was not intended and it is more likely that such applications will be made by the adoptive adult.

JPEG 7 11.15 am

The amendment's reference to the adopted child is consistent with the meaning of "adopted child" in section 39 of the 1976 Act. Section 39 provides that, where the adopters are a married couple, an adopted child should be treated in law as if he or she had been born as a child of the marriage. Clearly, that status is conferred not for the duration of childhood, but for life.

Amendment agreed to.

Amendments made: No. 2 in page 3, line 14, after 'the', insert "adopted".

No. 3, in page 3, line 19, after first 'the', insert "adopted".

No. 4, in page 3, line 25, leave out "above".—[ Mr. Oaten.]

Question proposed, That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Oaten
: I want to say a few words about clause 4, which is complex and requires explanation. United Kingdom adoption law recognises only one type of adoption, known as full adoption, which creates a new and irrevocable legal relationship between the child and the adoptive parents, and severs all legal ties between the child and the birth parents. However, in some other countries, certain forms of adoption, known as simple adoptions, do not sever those ties. Article 26 of the Hague convention recognises full adoptions and simple adoptions, but does not prejudice the application of any legal provision in force in the contracting state if it is more favourable to the child.

Clause 7
ACQUISITION OF BRITISH CITIZENSHIP BY CONVENTION ADOPTIONS

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Hunter
: According to my understanding of the explanatory notes, in the case of a convention adoption, provided that the child's adoptive parents are habitual residents in the United Kingdom, the clause will enable the child automatically to acquire British nationality even if neither of the adoptive parents is a British citizen. Is that right?

Mr. Oaten
: The clause is complicated. It will require at least one of the adoptive parents to be a British citizen at the time that the adoption order is made. Therefore, it would not be possible for British nationality to be granted to a convention adoption child unless at least one of the parents had British citizenship.

Mr. Edward Leigh
(Gainsborough)
: As I understand it, clause 7 provides that children who are adopted and acquire British citizenship automatically lose the citizenship of the country from which they came. What is the precedent for that? Is there any argument for children having dual nationality? Is it right for children to be deprived of their original cultural heritage? Is it right that children who find new parents should automatically lose all links with the country from which they came? I am worried about that and would like some reassurance from the hon. Gentleman.

Mr. Oaten
: The hon. Gentleman raises an important point. I have spoken to a number of adopting couples about this and it is essential that the origins of the child are recognised and celebrated. I have met couples who have gone on family holidays to the country of origin, thereby making it a feature of family life. The intention behind the clause is not to wipe out any nationality or suggest that the adopted child should become English and forget his or her origins. Indeed, dual nationality would be an option if that were the choice of the individuals concerned.

The clause gives adopted children from foreign countries similar rights to those enjoyed by children adopted in this country. The intention is to create a level playing field. Couples who have adopted a child from a foreign country can already apply to the courts for citizenship for the child, but the clause makes that an automatic right, without recourse to the courts, as part of the convention.

Clause 9
ADOPTION SERVICE TO INCLUDE INTERCOUNTRY ADOPTIONS ETC

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Oaten
: This is an important clause. I mentioned earlier the hope that local authorities would take a positive attitude towards intercountry adoption. Under the clause, local authorities should make little or no distinction between domestic and intercountry JPEG 9 adoption in their treatment of adoption applications. Local authorities will be expected to treat intercountry adoption applications with the same thoroughness and within the same broad time scales as they do domestic adoptions. They will also be expected to apply the principle that a person who is not suitable to adopt a child living in the United Kingdom cannot be recommended as suitable to adopt a child living overseas, which is an important point. There is no short cut—parents who have been turned down for adoption in this country cannot assume that they can go abroad to adopt a child, as if that child were a third-class citizen. I hope that the clause will ensure that local authority practices improve throughout the country.

Mr. Oaten

Clause 10
APPROVAL OF ADOPTION SOCIETIES TO PROVIDE INTERCOUNTRY ADOPTION SERVICES

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Rowe
: I hope that this is an appropriate point to raise this awkward issue. The point has been made to me on more than one occasion by people who are interested in the subject, that the mechanisms for controlling adoption in this country pass through such a fine bottleneck that only one or two people set the policy for the entire system.

Many years ago, I was a civil servant in the Scottish Office. It dealt with a relatively small number of mentally ill criminals in state institutions. The branch that was supposed to deal with those prisoners contained what was, in those days, called a higher executive officer. He was the executive of the appeal mechanism. All state prisoners who felt aggrieved JPEG 13 wrote to the Secretary of State, and the higher executive officer processed the letter. Because the Scottish Office was so small, however, he also drafted the Secretary of State's reply. Clearly, that was an improper way of dealing with such matters and when it eventually came to light, the two functions were properly separated.

Given that a very small unit within the Department of Health runs the adoption service in this country—it sets the standards, creates regulations, makes the eventual approvals, and so on—there is anxiety about whether aggrieved adopters, for example, can feel confident that if they disagree with the Department of Health or one of its approved agencies, they will be treated even-handedly.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Health (Mr. John Hutton)
: It might help the hon. Gentleman if I point out that the Government's White Paper on modernising social services made it clear that when we have the opportunity, we intend to introduce measures to improve the regulation and inspection of voluntary adoption agencies. We will look for the earliest opportunity to convert the intentions that we outlined in the White Paper into a practical set of legal requirements applying to the operation of voluntary adoption agencies. I hope that I have reassured the hon. Gentleman that we take on board what he said about ensuring standards and quality practice in adoption agencies and we intend to legislate to reinforce that through more effective regulatory and inspection arrangements.

Mr. Rowe

That was what I was anxious to point out.

Mr. Oaten
: I wonder whether I might help the hon. Gentleman. He makes a good point about where in the process there is a chance for an appeal—for want of a better word—against a decision.

At points during the work on those reports, discussion—I would not like to use the word "appeal"—can take place between those carrying out the report and the individuals who have applied to be approved to adopt. Thus, if the couple have concerns about the process, it is right that they should air them at that point, rather than being turned down and then trying to appeal to the Secretary of State. The Bill clearly establishes that the Department of Health—that is, a Government body—remain the central authority that oversees that process. I am 14 certainly not implying that the Bill should take the final decision-making process away from Government.

Mr. Tom Clarke

Clause 13
CONSTRUCTION OF CERTAIN REFERENCES IN 1976 AND 1978 ACTS

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Oaten
: The clause deals in some detail with how a decision can be taken on whether a child is suitable for adoption at present. In my reply to the hon. Member for Faversham and Mid-Kent, I spoke about the need for home study reports. The clause prohibits privately commissioned home study reports, which is extremely important in trying to do away with the bad practice of adoption. At present, local authorities or approved agencies have the important responsibility of carrying out home study reports. It is right and proper that the responsibility should remain there. What gives cause for concern is the fact that JPEG 15 individuals could purchase a home study report from another organisation and wave around the world a report that purports to say that they are suitable to adopt a child. There are several reasons why that would be wrong and the clause rightly prohibits such reports.

There is a concern that paying for a report must put its independence in question. A couple paying £5,000 or £6,000 for a report on whether they are suitable to adopt a child would be somewhat aggrieved having paid that amount to discover that the report turned them down. That probably makes it clear that such a practice cannot be right.

It is also important that a social worker and someone with the relevant skills and attributes should be involved in putting the report together. That is not always the case when a report is privately commissioned.

Finally and very importantly, there will be occasions when police and private reports on individuals have to be obtained. Local authorities and appointed agencies will now have that power, but those producing a private study report will not. Issues of access to information are involved. The kind of information that should be considered to decide whether couples are suitable would not be provided to those who carry out private study reports.

This is an important clause which will, I hope, do away with the bad practice of privately commissioned reports.

Clause 14
RESTRICTION ON BRINGING CHILDREN INTO THE UNITED KINGDOM FOR ADOPTION

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Oaten
: I do not wish to over-emphasise the problems that the clause would address. There have been some reported cases in the media and there are sad examples of individuals who have privately commissioned home reports or tried to bypass the proper process for bringing children into the country. The clause makes it a criminal offence for a person resident in the British Isles to bring into the United Kingdom for the purpose of adoption a child who is resident outside these islands unless he or she has gone through the proper process.

Those requirements may apply either prior to the child's arrival, to deal with evidence showing that a person or couple is preparing to break the law, or within a period to be prescribed after a child's arrival. 16 For example, it could be an offence for an adopter to fail to notify his or her local authority of the child's arrival within the specified period.

Current legislation dealing with such offences provides that proceedings can be brought only within six months from the date on which the offence was committed. That could mean six months from when a child was brought in late at night at Margate. The clause ensures that proceedings for an offence may be brought within six months from the date on which sufficient evidence is available to demonstrate that the illegal adoption occurred. That means that an adopter cannot bring in a child to Margate, hide that child for six months, and then breathe a sigh of relief. The criminal offence will kick into place from when the action is discovered to have occurred.

I would not want the Committee to get the impression that hundreds of such cases arise every year, but the clause is important, and it is right and proper to introduce provisions to tidy up the law in respect of such cases.

Mr. Leigh
: I understand the reason for the clause, and I do not oppose it. I am, however, a tiny bit worried about it, as it deals with people who are often very desperate. The provisions potentially allow the imprisonment of a mother for committing an offence as specified under the clause. What she has done may be wrong, but does the Minister genuinely envisage such people going to prison?

Another point occurs to me in relation to the hon. Gentleman's remarks. The clause deals with circumstances that are often tragic and which often involve childless couples. I repeat that what such people do is wrong, but at least they know that there is a cut-off point. As the Bill provides no six-month cut-off point, couples might seek to hide a child forever. Is the clause in the child's interest in that respect?

Mr. Oaten
: The hon. Gentleman expresses important concerns. I must again emphasise that the provisions relate to isolated cases. I understand his point about couples being desperate, but we discussed earlier the importance of putting the child first. It is these very clauses that are needed if we are to speak the language of putting the child first. It is difficult to deal with a desperate mother with such legislation, but it cannot be right to put a child through the trauma involved.

Perhaps a person covered by the clause will have met a child in the airport in Amsterdam. He or she could then have driven the child for many hours through the continent, and travelled back by ferry, bringing the child into Britain at Margate. However desperate a person is to adopt, that process cannot be right, and it cannot be proper to allow a child to be put through such stress. Some resort to criminal action must be available in those circumstances.

Of course, the courts will be sensitive and sympathetic, but some individuals will be involved in such activities in an organisation. They will not necessarily be desperate mothers, and may be gaining JPEG 17 money from the process. Hon. Members from all parties would want the firm hand of the law—although I hate the phrase—to have force in those cases.

Mr. Hutton
: In addition to those remarks, I should like only to draw the attention of the hon. Member for Gainsborough to a specific aspect of the clause. We are trying to tidy up the law relating to the six-month period, which is necessary for reasons made clear by the hon. Member for Winchester. I should point out that proceedings cannot be brought more than three years after the offence in question was committed, which is a necessary and sensible measure. The fundamental point that I should like to make to the hon. Member for Gainsborough is that the provision is an essential part of the new foundations on which we want to build better practice in intercountry adoptions. We cannot have people bringing children into the United Kingdom without the proper procedures established in the Hague convention, which the Bill will incorporate into British law. I understand the hon. Gentleman's concerns about civil liberties, but the provision is important and necessary. It will support the better practice that we want to achieve by introducing the Bill.
