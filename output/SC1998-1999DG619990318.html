That the Committee has considered the Education (Relevant Areas for Consultation on Admission Arrangements) Regulations 1999 (S.I. 1999, No. 124).

Mr. Foster
: I welcome you to the Chair, Mr. Benton. From informal discussions with members of the Committee, I understand that while we could have a seven and a half hour debate on the five sets of regulations, the majority of hon. Members wish to proceed more rapidly so that we can attend education questions in the House in an hour and a half—although I am sure that you would welcome the chance to remain in the Chair for seven and a half hours, Mr. Benton.

The regulations will come into force on 1 April 1999 and will apply to the admissions to primary and secondary schools from September 2000. They give effect to the Government's code of practice on school admissions. Statutory instruments Nos. 124 and 126 relate to the Greenwich judgment, which appears in paragraph 5.4 on page 9 of the code. No. 124 also relates to the appointment and role of adjudicators, which is covered in paragraphs 4.9 to 4.12 of the code, while No. 258 refers to partial selection and aptitude—a mysterious concept that has not yet been well defined—which are covered on pages 10 and 11 of the code.

Supported by my hon. Friend the Member for Harrogate and Knaresborough (Mr. Willis), who will no doubt add to and improve on my remarks, I will explain why we do not support the regulations or the parts of the code of practice to which they give effect. The regulations give rise to some measures that we fundamentally oppose in principle and to others that demonstrate the Government's confusion, which means that they could create significant problems in our schools.

4 All members of the Committee stage of the School Standards and Framework Act 1998 agreed that the school admission arrangements should be, in the words of my hon. Friend the Member for Harrogate and Knaresborough, simple, clear, fair and open. Similar words are now contained in the code of practice. I hope to demonstrate that the regulations do not meet those basic criteria. The admission arrangements are not simple, clear, fair or—because of their complexity—open.

While on the question of openness, will the Minister confirm—indeed, I hope that she will deny it—that what Baroness Blackstone said in another place was correct? On 11 March in column 440 of the Official Report, she said that the code of practice on appeals, which is a vital part of admission policy, is to be consulted upon only during the Easter break.

When in opposition, the Government were highly critical of the Conservative Government's consulting on education matters during the holidays. Yet I understand that consultation is to take place during the Easter holiday, that the final version of the appeals procedure will come before Parliament in June and that it will be implemented in September. That is cutting it very fine. The orders will come into force on 1 April, yet we are told that the other side of the coin, the appeals procedure, is badly delayed.

I note with interest that in press notice 63/99, issued on 5 February, the Minister said that for the first time, parents would be able to appeal to fully independent appeals panels if their preferences had not been met. However, although I agree with what the hon. Lady wants, no consultation has been held on the appeals procedure. It seems rather odd that she should announce what will happen before the consultation has taken place. She certainly appears to have jumped the gun.

I have some specific points to make about the regulations. I start with statutory instruments Nos. 124 and 126, which contain the effects of the Greenwich judgment. Members of the Committee will remember that that judgment ruled that applications made by parents who live outside the LEA area for their children to attend an authority's schools must the considered equally with applications made by those who live within the area.

That has caused a great deal of anger and angst among the many parents who found that their children could not be placed at their own LEA's schools because children from outside the area had taken their places. That problem is particularly marked in schools that are selective or partially selective, because the level of competition is higher. It effectively enlarges the school's catchment area, and makes it more difficult for local parents to get their children into a local school.

We argued during the passage of the School Standards and Framework Bill that it would be more appropriate to give the LEA responsibility for admission arrangements for all the state schools within its area. We disagree with the Government's intention to support the Greenwich judgment. The regulations allow that ruling to continue without giving us the chance to change it.

I shall give a couple of examples. The Tiffin boys' school in Kingston-upon-Thames had an entrance year of 69 pupils from within the borough and 80 pupils from outside, and the Tiffin girls' school took 55 girls from the JPEG 5 borough and 70 from outside. It is almost unbelievable that some selective schools in the Sutton LEA area have pupils who travel daily from Brighton. I ask the Committee to think of the distances involved, because it will be relevant later.

We oppose the effects of the Greenwich judgment. We want legislation to overturn that judgment; unfortunately, the regulations do not do so. It will now be possible, subject to consultation, for admission authorities to draw any boundary they like, with one exception. They will not be allowed to draw the boundary of the LEA itself. That is bizarre, especially in the light of paragraphs (4) and (5) of regulation 5 of the Education (Determination of Admission Arrangements) Regulations. Given that the Government intend to continue to support the Greenwich judgment, it is understandable that the regulations require schools whose governing bodies will be their own admission authority to consult their own and neighbouring LEAs. However, paragraphs (4) and (5) place distance limits on the requirements, which, as the Minister knows, are defined in terms of what is called in paragraph (5) the "radial area".

The limits that the Government have imposed are such that, given the distances involved in cases such as that which I described of Sutton schools and Brighton, it is quite possible that such schools will not have to consult people in the area from which they have traditionally drawn pupils, and continue to do so, as is the case in Sutton. The Minister shakes her head; I look forward to her explanation. I am more than happy to be told by the Minister that I have misread paragraphs (4) and (5) of regulation 5, but that is my interpretation and that of others who have examined those regulations.

The Education (Relevant Areas for Consultation on Admission Arrangements) Regulations give effect to the appointment of adjudicators. It is slightly odd that the chief adjudicator and several other adjudicators have already been appointed. The hon. Member for Maidenhead (Mrs. May) said last night that a former Member of the House, Mr. Robin Squire, who used to serve frequently on Committees such as this, has been appointed. We are opposed to adjudicators, but if they must be appointed, I welcome Robin Squire's appointment, and am sure that he will do an excellent job. However, what job will he do? Adjudicators have been appointed despite the fact that the regulations that describe their job have not yet been approved by Parliament. How can the Government have appointed people when we have not yet agreed what they should do? That is the purpose of the regulations.

Will the Minister explain why religious matters, which are important, relating to admissions should be dealt with by the Secretary of State, but equally important issues of race and gender should not? Incidentally, matters of religion lead to another oddity that the Minister should explain. She will recall that in opposition the present Secretary of State for Education and Employment said, "Watch my lips—no selection by interview or examination". The worry is that allowing such interviews to continue produces the danger that all sorts of other areas might be strayed into—for example, the social standing of the parents, the ability of child and so on. I do not understand why Ministers think it necessary to go back on what they said before the election. After all, the Government say that the purpose of the interview is to obtain information about religious commitment, but evidence about that can be obtained without the need for an interview to take place. Information on baptism, confirmation, church attendance and so on can all be obtained by other means— for example, in writing from the local vicar or priest.

I hope that the Minister will explain why that provision is necessary, especially given that during the consultation on the regulations the Church authorities made it clear that they had no particular desire for such a provision to be in the regulations. I have recently asked the Church of England Board of Education for its view on that matter. One of its officials told me:

"Throughout the passage of the Bill, we did not see a particular need for interviews, and felt it could go. We would have been content to see it disappear."

JPEG 7 So why do the regulations contain that provision?

I turn now to the Education (Aptitude for Particular Subjects) Regulations (S.I. 1999, No. 258). The Committee will be delighted to know that I do not intend to rehearse in detail the Liberal Democrat views on selection. I hope that those who were present will agree that those views were well aired during the consideration in Committee of the School Standards and Framework Act 1998. In summary, we agree with what the Secretary of State for Education and Employment said before the previous general election. I have quoted him saying that there would be no selection. The School Standards and Framework Act 1998 and the statutory instruments will allow selection not only to continue but to expand under the guise of selection by aptitude. That will create a raft of problems.

Another part of the admission procedure gives priority to the siblings of children who are currently at the school. Children from outside the area, who may not necessarily have particularly high ability, attend the school because they are the siblings of children who already attend the school because of its selection procedures. We gave examples showing how the sibling rule means that more and more children come from outside the local area, resulting in fewer and fewer opportunities for local children.

We Liberal Democrats object to the statutory instruments in principle. I know that the Minister for School Standards frequently spoke against selection before the previous general election, but if she has now changed her mind and done a U-turn and is determined to allow selection by aptitude to take place, why has she come up with such a bizarre list as that contained in statutory instrument No. 258? Why is there selection for those subjects but not for science, especially when we are hoping to have specialist schools for science? Why has the Minister omitted science from the list when she wants to have schools that specialise in that subject? That makes no sense, and it is another example of the Government not thinking the matter through.
