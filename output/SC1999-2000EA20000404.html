Banana Imports

10.38 am

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Ms Joyce Quin)
: We have had an unusual start to the meeting, which has given me more time to reflect on what I want to say to the Committee on a subject to which I think we all attach great importance. I spoke on a different subject in the Committee last week—the flax and hemp regime in the European Union—when we managed to achieve what I was told was a rare consensus. I hope that we can have consensus on today's subject, on which there has traditionally been wide agreement on both sides of the House, especially as we approach the negotiations to find a solution to this difficult issue.

The House has shown considerable interest in the future of the European Union banana regime. Indeed, it was debated in this Committee two years ago, when there was common cause in recognising the potentially serious implications for the Commonwealth Caribbean countries. Also, an Adjournment debate on the Caribbean banana industry took place in Westminster Hall last December, introduced by the hon. Member for Hertford and Stortford (Mr. Wells). Some members of the Committee who were present during that debate spoke warmly in support of Caribbean banana producers.

As I said then, the Government are consious of the United Kingdom's historical and political commitments to our banana suppliers in the Caribbean. I assure the Committee that it remains our firm goal to achieve a solution to this long-running dispute that takes those commitments fully into account. As was highlighted in the December debate, it is a complex issue, for which it is hard to find simple solutions and the slow pace of reform reflects its sensitivity.

As hon. Members may recollect, when the European Union reformed its regime in 1998 it was responding to an adverse ruling by the World Trade Organisation. The EU tried to deal with the elements of the regime judged incompatible with international trading rules, but unfortunately the revised regime was also found to fall foul of WTO rules. The United States responded by introducing retaliatory trade sanctions, some of which are still in place. They amount to $191 million a year, as approved by the WTO. It is a matter of great concern to the Government that such measures hit UK and other EU exporters.

The WTO's arbitration panel has just ruled that Ecuador can introduce sanctions to the value of $201 million. That has yet to be approved by the WTO's dispute settlement body. We hope that Ecuador will not exercise its rights, but will concentrate, with others, on putting in place a new regime.

2 In addition to the United States and Ecuador, there are many parties to the dispute, each with their own views on the way in which the EU banana regime should be changed. The only practical way forward, therefore, is to reach agreement with all the parties.

The Government's objective is to achieve a revised EU banana regime that will not be challenged at the WTO and that reflects EU commitments to the most vulnerable suppliers in the Caribbean. We also want US sanctions to be removed. However, achieving agreement to a new regime is complex.

The Commonwealth Caribbean countries supply less than 8 per cent. of the EU banana market, almost all to the UK. They have benefited from guaranteed access to a restricted market, in which prices have been high. Successive WTO rulings do not say that we cannot give preferential treatment to our traditional Caribbean suppliers; it is the way in which we do so that has caused difficulties.

As was said in the December debate, many Caribbean farmers find themselves in a vulnerable position. For reasons of climate and topography, bananas cost more to produce in the Caribbean then elsewhere. That is allied to a high dependency on bananas in the Windward Islands and in specific areas of Jamaica and Belize. The banana industry provides not only employment for a large proportion of the work force but valuable export earnings. A collapse in the industry would cause serious social and economic problems, particularly in rural areas.

The UK is committed to helping Caribbean countries to restructure and reduce their dependency on bananas. Virtually all the aid available for banana industry restructuring and diversification comes from the EU, but the UK plays an active role in shaping and monitoring that assistance through its participation in the donor group. However, prospects for diversification are limited, certainly in the short term. The opportunities that exist depend to a certain extent on the banana industry's continuing viability. That is why we must ensure a continuing market for Caribbean bananas in the UK, and ensure that the industry restructures in such a way as to increase competitiveness and efficiency.

There is enormous good will in this country towards the Caribbean, and we firmly believe that, with improved efficiency and effective marketing, its banana industry has a future. However, good will and astute marketing are not enough. Preferential trade arrangements for the Caribbean, and for other countries, play an important role.

The EU is seeking renewal of the African, Caribbean and Pacific waiver in the World Trade Organisation, which covers preferential treatment for ACP states compared with other third countries. It includes a new banana protocol that recognises the overwhelming importance to the ACP banana suppliers of their exports to the European Community market. The new protocol is a sign of the EU's continuing commitment in this area.

The European Commission faces a difficult task in finding a way forward. Besides protecting our JPEG 3 traditional ACP suppliers and satisfying WTO requirements, any solution must take account of EU banana farmers and budgetary concerns. Those four elements are difficult to reconcile. The Commission's current proposal offers some hope of ending the dispute, provided that it is adjusted to take account of vulnerable Caribbean suppliers.

The Commission has reluctantly concluded that the only option that will be secure from further challenge in the long term is a tariff-only regime. Under such a regime, ACP countries would benefit from a tariff preference relative to imports from Latin America. The level of the tariff for imports from non-ACP third countries will be the subject of negotiations under article XXVIII of the general agreement on tariffs and trade. Clearly, the higher the tariff that can be negotiated, the less we will have to look to other measures to help the Caribbean.

To allow ACP and EU producers to adapt to this future regime, the Commission's proposal includes a transition period, about which there was considerable discussion in the December debate, which it envisages will last until 1 January 2006. Until then quotas will continue to operate.

The basic model of a transitional quota regime leading to a tariff-only regime commands support inside and outside the EU. Caribbean Governments have said that they can accept that approach, as have the United States and Latin American countries. Most EU member states can accept it too. It therefore offers the best hope of resolving the dispute, but differences remain on key details.

Like the Caribbean countries, we in the UK would prefer a longer transition. The date of 2006 represents a compromise between the countries that want a tariff-only regime immediately and those that want quotas for a much longer period. We wanted a long transition and have been in contact with the Caribbean countries about that.

There is no point in agreeing a long transition if vulnerable suppliers go to the wall immediately afterwards. We should not lose sight of the importance of agreeing the terms for the final regime and of allowing the Caribbean to make detailed preparations. We must also pay heed to the important issue of licence allocation during transition. We have serious concerns about the licensing foreseen in the European Commission's proposal and we have made those concerns clear to the Commission and to other member states. Our concerns relate primarily to the management of quota C. Vulnerable Caribbean suppliers need to be confident of regular substantive access to our markets during the transition period and a striking price auction would deliver no such certainty. A system of historic licensing would be much better. The United Kingdom is not alone in that view. The Commission, other member states, Latin American countries and even the United States—in fact, virtually all parties to the dispute—now express a preference for historic licensing.

Following discussions with the United States and Latin American countries, the Caribbean countries 4 have made a proposal along the lines of the Commission's model, but wiht licences in quota C being allocated on the basis of a reference period—from 1995 to 1997—for ACP operators. That would give Caribbean operators fewer licenses than they currently enjoy, but sufficient to be confident of an operationally effective level of access during the transitional period. There is no reason why that should not be feasible. A reference period of that kind is gaining increasing acceptance among the parties to the dispute.

It may be helpful if I refer to the views of the European Parliament's Committee on Agriculture and Rural Development, referred to in the amendment tabled by the hon. Member for Hertford and Stortford. However, that committee's opinion is not, at this stage, the European Parliament's opinion. The European Parliament will consider the issue, including the recommendations of the Committee on Agriculture and Rural Development in a plenary session next week. The committee's report includes some welcome elements, such as deleting the proposed auction system for ACP bananas in quota C, to which I referred earlier. However, it would also remove the transitional nature of tariff quotas and create a permanent quota system.

The difficulty is in finding a way forward on which all parties agree—a point made strongly by the hon. Member for Hertford and Stortford in December. I would prefer not to be locked into supporting the opinion of a European Parliament committee that has not yet been discussed at a plenary and does not run along the same lines as the Caribbean countries' proposal, which has some support from the United States and other parties to the dispute. Therefore, although I support the view that lies behind the amendment, I feel that it would not be helpful at this stage of the negotiations. As I explained, the view of the Committee referred to in the amendment is not the opinion of the European Parliament, which will debate the issue next week.

I assure hon. Members that we are in close touch with the Caribbean countries. Understandably, they are anxious about the way forward and have had discussions with the United States and others. Clearly, discussions between the European Commission and the United States are at a delicate stage, but they are at least talking about and giving some support to the Caribbean countries' suggestions. For that reason, we want to be seen to facilitate the process of negotiation rather than to close it off.

As we know, the main sticking point in negotiations has been finding an acceptable reference period for historic licensing in quotas A and B. The Caribbean proposal is for a pre-1993 reference period—in other words, a period before the European Union regime existed—which is naturally supported by the United States and most Latin American suppliers. Even Ecuador, whose exports to the European Union have increased in recent years and which would therefore prefer a late reference period, has said that it could accept the Caribbean model if some changes were made to the licensing arrangements—for instance, if a slice of quotas A and B were allocated on a post-1993 basis. However, the task of finding a compromise remains difficult.

JPEG 5 An exclusively pre-1993 reference period poses problems for the Commission, which fears that it would be challenged in the European Court of Justice by traders who started operations after 1993. Recent Commission negotiations with the United States and others have therefore focused on ways of changing the Caribbean proposal along the lines of Ecuador's suggestions to a hybrid pre-1993 and post-1993 reference period, which might be acceptable. There are signs of progress. On 20 March, the Agriculture Council encouraged the Commission to continue its discussions with third countries in an effort to reach agreement.

As I said, we must not forget that US sanctions totalling $191 million continue against EU companies until the dispute is resolved. Ecuador has also effectively just been granted the right to launch trade retaliation totalling $201 million. Therefore, there is a pressing need to find a solution, not only for British companies but for the banana trade and Caribben farmers.

The makings of a deal are on the table. We await the outcome of the Commission's further negotiations with third countries and the deliberations of the European Parliament, which is expected to deliver its opinion at its April plenary.

During last December's Adjournment debate, the hon. Member for Hertford and Stortford urged me to

persist, pursue, harry and hassle to get an agreement.—[ Official Report, Westminster Hall, 15 December 1999; Vol. 341, c. 75WH.]

I assure him and other hon. Members that that is precisely what we have been doing and will continue to do. We are determined to reach a solution that helps vulnerable Caribbean banana producers. That is the issue at stake. There are many other issues, some of which are technical, but because of that overwhelming interest, it is essential to press forward to a solution.
