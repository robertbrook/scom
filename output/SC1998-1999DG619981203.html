Value Added Tax (Input Tax) (Amendment) Order 1998

10.30 am

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Dawn Primarolo)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Value Added Tax (Input Tax) (Amendment) Order 1998 (S.I. 1998, No. 2767).

The order deals specifically with an avoidance issue that has occurred with the arrangements for value added tax on the leasing of cars.

The Government are committed to fairness in taxation. As part of that commitment, we are determined to deal with all tax avoidance swiftly and surely. The right hon. Member for Fylde (Mr. Jack) will confirm that the previous Government took a similar view and dealt swiftly with direct challenges to the tax system.

When tax is avoided through the use of artificial schemes, fairness is undermined, making the burden disproportionate. Some businesses are forced to pay higher taxes because those that do not want to pay the full rate use creative tax devices. The order prevents the use of one of those creative tax devices, which the Government find unacceptable.

The order is an anti-avoidance measure. We have acted quickly to tackle a blatant avoidance scheme that has been marketed, unfortunately, by some of the leading accountants. VAT revenue of about £20 million is at risk.

The order closes a loophole in the VAT rules that apply to private motoring in leased company cars. It is a complex matter, involving VAT and input and output tax. I shall do my best to assist the Committee by explaining the problem.

In August 1995, the previous Government changed the rules on taxing such private motoring. The change was a business facilitation measure intended to benefit leasing businesses and their customers in the finance and insurance sectors. The change replaced one method of taxing private use with another. At the time of the change, special transitional rules prevented double taxation. Some businesses—not all—with large company car fleets have abused a loophole in the transitional rules. The order deals with only those companies that have used such schemes. All other businesses—the vast majority that has used the rules legitimately—will be unaffected.

The avoidance scheme involves interposing an artificial leasing company between a real leasing company and its business customer, and making artificial pre-payments to the artificial leasing company. Unfortunately, such 4 arrangements are not unfamiliar in different aspects of taxation for various reasons, and this Government and the previous Government have always acted swiftly against them.

Under the old rules, VAT on the purchase of leased cars could not be recovered, but all the VAT incurred on the leasing charges was recoverable. Under the new rules, VAT on the purchase of leased cars is recoverable, but only half the VAT on leasing charges can be recovered. Using the avoidance scheme, all the VAT on the purchase and on the leasing charges is recovered, so completely avoiding taxation on the private use of company cars.

To put it simply, two schemes relating to VAT and leasing are available. Those schemes—the choice of which depends on the sector, and which we will call option A and option B—provide for the recovery of VAT at different points. The avoidance scheme built a bridge between option A and option B, thereby providing the benefits of both options. I am sure that that was never the intention of the previous Government, as such a scheme provides no taxation at all.

Private motoring in business cars is taxed by limiting the amount of VAT that businesses can recover on the purchase or lease of the car. That simplified procedure avoids the need for businesses to keep detailed records of business and private mileage, and was agreed on the establishment of the regulations.

Before 1 August 1995, VAT incurred on the purchase of a car by a leasing company could not be recovered. VAT on the subsequent charges made by the leasing company to its customer was recoverable by the customer in the normal way. From 1 August 1995, VAT on the purchase of the car could be recovered by the leasing company, but only half of the VAT incurred on the leasing charges could be recovered by the customer where the car was available for private use. VAT could still be recovered in full on the leasing charges where—in rare cases—no private use was available.

Transitional measures were put in place that excluded pre-existing leases from the new arrangements, and attempted to counteract avoidance schemes that might seek to circumvent the new arrangements by artificially bringing forward the dates of leases. The order addresses the loophole in those transitional measures.

Leading accountants marketed a packaged avoidance scheme that took advantage of the loophole by inserting an artificial associated leasing company between the real leasing schemes and the business customer arrangements. A business using that scheme could still lease cars benefiting from the full VAT recovery on the leasing charge and the full VAT recovery on purchase. Such cars would be obtained VAT-free, which is contrary to Parliament's clear intention—in amendments introduced by the previous Government—in relation to cars that are available for private use.

Mr. Michael Jack
(Fylde)
: Will the Minister give way?

Dawn Primarolo
: I shall be happy to answer any questions from hon. Members after I explain what happened in the courts.

JPEG 5 Customs contested the scheme—the placing of an artificial leasing company—in the VAT tribunal and the courts. They won in the High Court but lost the argument in the Court of Appeal. Article 2(b) of the order limits the recovery by the associated artificial leasing company to half of the VAT incurred on leasing charges if it has sub-let the cars concerned before 1 August 1995. The change only applies to leasing charges invoiced or paid on or after 13 November 1998.

The change protects against the use of the avoidance scheme in future. Very little tax has been lost so far, but we must ensure that the scheme is not used more widely. Therefore, following the Court of Appeal loss, we felt that we should take quick and sure action.

To sum up, the amendment corrects a small technical loophole that was, unfortunately, in the provisions in 1995. I am not casting any blame; with the best will in the world it is difficult for Ministers and officials when drafting legislation to ensure that the most creative tax planners do not find a way in which to abuse it. Under the 1995 provisions, there was a tiny opportunity to do that, which was exploited and led to a loss of revenue and which would have led to a considerable future loss in revenue if we had not taken action new. The order is specific and targeted. It will enable the previous Government's intentions to be realised.

Concerning the order, my understanding of the way in which the scheme works—I apologise for examining the matter again but I want to be sure that I have got it clear in my mind—is that a lessee who entered an agreement with a leasing company prior to 1 August 1995 could reclaim 100 per cent. of the VAT on the leasing charge. If the leasing company obtained the vehicles after 1 August, however, it could also reclaim 100 per cent. of the VAT. In simple terms, it was an arbitrage scheme that exploited the changeover date.

Presumably, the lessee had to enter into an agreement while the previous regime was in force—that is, before 1 August 1995. The likelihood is, however, that the primary period for the lease would be no more than three years; indeed, it would probably be shorter because those involved would be anxious to take advantage of the scheme before it could be changed. It is, therefore, likely that higher payments would be made for a shorter period. In any case, it is unlikely that any agreement is still in its primary period today. As a result, any leases that were taken out using the scheme are almost bound to be in the secondary period on a peppercorn rent. However, the Customs and Excise press release and the Financial Secretary both state that the measure is not retrospective and applies only to future lease payments. To some extent, therefore, it is shutting the stable door after the horse was bolted.

I find it a little difficult to see how Customs and Excise have come up with the figure of £20 million for the amount of revenue that the measure protects. Given that it is not clear how new schemes could have been taken out and that payments under the old schemes have in large part been made, the revenue at stake would be only a fraction of that amount. My understanding of precisely how the scheme works may be incorrect, but that is how it appears to me. I would be grateful if the Financial Secretary would clarify the point.

JPEG 7 I want to make one or two general observations about the arrangements that govern the payment of VAT on leased vehicles. When the matter was originally debated in July 1995, the Financial Secretary said that the 50 per cent. block on VAT on leasing charges and the 100 per cent. block to input VAT on outright purchase were a rough and ready alternative to directly imposing VAT on the private use of business cars. She rightly said that that approach created a distortion in the market for the provision of cars. If a business leases a car that is not used exclusively for business purposes, it can reclaim 50 per cent. of the VAT. If it purchases a car that is not used exclusively for business purposes, however, it can reclaim no VAT. That is not wholly satisfactory; indeed, the artificiality of that device originally gave rise to the avoidance scheme that prompted the measure that we are debating. Why has the hon. Lady rejected the alternative that she suggested when in opposition? Moving to a system under which VAT is charged on the private use of business vehicles would allow businesses to reclaim a proportion of VAT that was equivalent to the proportion of time during which the vehicle was used for business purposes. The system of clawback, which is used by Customs and Excise for other business assets, could then be applied.

It has been suggested that such a case-by-case assessment might be bureaucratic and administratively expensive for small businesses. The Financial Secretary referred to the need to keep an account of the mileage that was recorded in each specific circumstance. I understand, however, that such a system operates in Germany, where it appears to work. I do not see why it could not be made to work here. It would seem fairer, and would remove the distortion identified by the Financial Secretary. Will she address that point? [ Interruption.]

Hon. Members whisper, sotto voce, "harmonisation". The Financial Secretary will be aware that a few months ago the European Commission made proposals to harmonise rules relating to the recovery of VAT incurred in other member states, and to introduce common rules for input tax blocking for business expenses. The Commission proposes that member states should have the option to apply the normal rules of deduction or a simplified system for motor cars. I have described the usual rules, which apply in Germany. The simplified system proposed by the Commission would require member states to set another limit on input tax recovery, which must not be lower than 50 per cent. If the proportion of business use of the car in question were below that limit, the usual rules would apply. If that proportion were 50 per cent. or more, the ceiling would apply, and 50 per cent. of VAT incurred on expenditure on the car would be deductible input tax.

The Commission' s proposal appears to be a halfway house between the system that applies in the UK and that which applies in Germany. I do not advocate to the Committee that we should sign up to harmonisation proposals from the Commission. The proposal is on the table, however, and it will be debated during coming months. Will the Financial Secretary comment on it, and does she feel that it can be squared with the order?
