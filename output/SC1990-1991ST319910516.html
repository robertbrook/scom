10.31 am

Mr. William O'Brien
(Normanton)
: The published documents show that some of the regulations in these statutory instruments came into force on 1 March and the remainder on 1 April, before Parliament had the opportunity to discuss them. That is disappointing.

I shall deal with English and Welsh local authorities and my hon. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Cathcart (Mr. Maxton) will deal with Scottish authorities. The issue is sensitive and affects thousands of councilors throughout the country. Those people are not wealthy ladies and gentlemen of independent means. They have volunteered to serve their communities and make real sacrifices—of time, money, family commitment and career prospects—to ensure that democracy continues in their localities. That is the background.

It is typical of the delay, postponements and lack of consultation that have attended the Government's handling of the regulations that Parliament has the chance to debate the changes only after they have come into force. That is shameful and an indictment of the Government. We are discussing a measure that affects the livelihoods of councillors throughout the country after its implementation.

Before Easter, the Government were at pains to stress that their main aim was to strike a proper balance between central Government and local government. The Secretary of State said on the Floor of the House of Commons that he wanted to ask the people what form of local government they wanted. He implied, but did not state, that centralisation had gone too far and that it would be inappropriate to impose solutions on local communities. Now the Government are imposing yet another central solution to a perceived abuse by telling local government how much councillors are worth and how they should be paid.

The Government's acceptance that attendance allowance should be retained, if only as an option, has been welcomed. Most authorities have decided to pay the allowance because, even within the new scheme's constraints, payment per duty recognises that not all 4 councillors can devote the same amount of time to their civic responsibilities. The Government are not content, however, to set a maximum figure for members' allowances. They prescribe percentages of the overall quantum for different sorts of payment, require payment of special responsibility allowances, define those who can receive such payments and tie attendance allowance to formal meetings, overlooking the various other duties that a councillor must perform.

The Local Government and Housing Act 1989 is firmly, but selectively, rooted in the recommendations of the Widdicombe committee, which were replicated in the White Paper. It appears, however, that members' allowances will also be included in the Secretary of State's review of local government. Will the Minister confirm that that is the case? If so, will there be genuine and meaningful consultation on the formulation of the new scheme? Will the promised commission to consider members' allowances receive evidence on this issue? Who will be invited to give evidence? Will any notice be taken of the views expressed by the various organisations, including the Association of Councillors? Will the Minister give us an assurance that there will be no faulting on any Government proposals?

It is fashionable for the Government and for individual members of the Government to admit that mistakes have been made. Are the Government admitting that the 1989 scheme fails to recognise the valuable, varied and demanding role of elected councillors? Are the Government ready at long last to take a more objective look at improving the system?

The Widdicombe report, Command 9797, stated in paragraph 6.106 that, once fixed, the levels of allowances should be regularly and objectively reviewed. We support it on that issue, and note that the members of the committee added:

"We cannot condone the manner in which attendance allowance has been allowed arbitrarily to fall in real value since its introduction. The original maximum daily allowance was£10. Had it kept in line with average earnings it would have been about£40 per day in 1985–86. In fact it was then£16.

Yet the new scheme continues to leave any uprating in the hands of the Government.

Across political party lines, the Association of District Councils, the Association of County Councils and the Association of Metropolitan Authorities are agreed that there should be an objective mechanism for the annual uplift of allowances. It should not be left to the Secretary of State'to decide whether there should be an increase.

The Widdicombe report acknowledged that the committee's proposals did not deal with the problems of pensions and benefits. Paragraph 6.111 states:

We have not proposed changes because the issues relate more generally to taxation and social security, and extend beyond our remit. We hope, however, that they will be pursued by others."

Work on this pressing problem has been in abeyance since 1988. The Department of the Environment has now written to local authority associations, proposing a July meeting to consider a revised code of guidance. This we welcome, but there should be a full review, with recommendations that address key areas, not merely restate the position. Again, the Government's request is issued across party lines in local government. I ask the Minister to consider the proposed July meeting.

The Government have promised that the scheme will be reviewed at the end of the first year of operation. Local authorities are worried that the review will be confined to JPEG 5 patterns of spending and will not take account of fundamental principles and problems. The review mechanism need not wait a further year because section 18(4)(c) of the 1989 Act allows regulations to be made which.

"contain such incidental provision and such supplemental, consequential and transitional provisions in connection with other provisions of the regulations as the Secretary of State considers appropriate."

It is clear that the Government will not scrap the new scheme before it comes in, but they have the power to improve the scheme by regulation and they should seize the opportunity to do so.

The Department of the Environment's commitment to the review at about the end of the first year that is given in its recently published circular is welcome, but it does not answer our question about the terms of reference of the review? Again, I ask the Minister to consider those terms of reference.

Members' allowances cannot be considered separately from leave arrangements. The Government have adopted the Widdicombe committee's proposal to impose a ceiling on the paid leave of a council employee who may be allowed to undertake council duties, but they have apparently done nothing about the committee's other recommendation on paid leave, namely that private sector employers should take a positive and flexible attitude to granting paid and unpaid leave to employees carrying out council duties. Individual employees must still negotiate "reasonable" time off under section 29 of the Employment Protection (Consolidation) Act 1978. Codes of practice for local government abound yet there is no similar statutory guidance for the public sector. Will the Minister also look at that?

The Widdicombe Committee recommended the abolition of attendance allowance and financial loss allowance and the Government conceded the option for local authorities to pay attendance allowance if they wished. However, the Government have painted themselves into a corner in that allowing elected members to claim financial loss allowance would require primary legislation. At the same time, magistrate members of police committees who are not yet restricted to the deliberative or consultative role recommended by the Widdecombe committee may still claim financial loss allowance. A magistrate sitting on a police committee can claim financial loss allowance but a locally elected councillor sitting on the same committee is denied that privilege. Perhaps the Minister could explain why that is allowed to happen.

The Government regard it as administratively ineffective to allow councillors to claim the allowance but it is perfectly in order for magistrates to claim even though they are not accountable to the local community and, in many instances, receive their payment from, the same clerk in the local authority. The numbers of those who are claiming are relatively small, but continuing to handle such claims is no more complicated than administering provisions for the new scheme. We see no reason why the claim of financial loss allowance should not operate within the claims by local councillors.

The joint authorities are creations of the Government, and arise from the abolition of the Greater London council and the metropolitan districts, but the new scheme does not treat them fairly. The passenger transport authorities, in particular, remind the Government that they are not simply former metropolitan county council committees given 6 separate status but carrying out the same essential functions. They have a significant role in their communities. Passenger transport authorities have had to face dramatic changes through bus deregulation, and instability has arisen from the constant change in the bus network.

Then there are the joint police authorities. As the Minister knows, West Yorkshire joint police authority has said that the new system of payment of allowances is unworkable and unfair and that it should be scrapped and replaced with a more liberal scheme applied by the local authorities involved with the joint police authorities.

As I have said, we have fully met our undertaking.

Within that quantum, we have established allocations per councillor by local authority type and size for the non-metropolitan district councils in England and Wales. I emphasise that in determining the respective levels of allowance, the Government do not intend to imply that members serving, for example, on district councils are any less hard-working or committed than their counterparts in metropolitan areas or on county councils. But we had to take account of the differing work loads and functions of each type of authority. We also accepted the argument of certain district councils that the size of their areas and populations implied greater demands on their councillors' time than the average.

In the case of joint authorities, we had to make a judgment about their single-service responsibilities in relation to the varied tasks of the metropolitan districts and London boroughs. On the basis of evidence collected by the AMA we increased from£500 to£800 the allocation per councillor for those authorities and updated that amount to take account of movements in costs subsequently. The present figure is£870.

Clearly, some high-spending authorities will have had to make adjustments to accommodate expenditure on allowances within the ceilings imposed under the new system. We have provided transitional arrangements in the regulations to allow for that. For many authorities, those arrangements are, of course, unnecessary because they were spending well within the maximum permissible when the new system was introduced.

I move on to the framework which the regulations prescribe for the payment of allowances. I have mentioned already that we worked closely with the local authority associations in drawing up our proposals for a new system of allowances. We listened carefully to the views of local government. In particular, we moved significantly from the original proposals in our White Paper for a simple, flat-rate allowance, accepting that local authorities should continue to be able to pay an element of attendance allowance to their members if they wanted to.

We welcomed the proposals put to us by the three main English and Welsh local authority associations. They proposed that the potential for abusing the attendance-allowance based system which existed at the time could be limited by restricting the range of approved duties or by 14 placing a limit on the total payable by each council. Those two key features were built into the regulations that we are considering today.

We firmly believe that the new system represents a distinct improvement on the one it replaces. With some modifications, the new arrangements carry forward the Widdicombe committee's proposals.

For the first time, the new system places a ceiling on how much a local authority may pay by way of allowances. Within their overall ceiling local authorities now have discretion, subject to certain limits specified in the regulations, to decide how to allocate their budgets between the three types of allowance—that is basic, special responsibility and attendance allowances. Payment of attendance allowance is optional. Fine loss allowance is no longer payable to councillors in the main local authorities. The new system therefore places accountability for spending decisions on allowances where it belongs—with the council.

Where an authority chooses to do so, it may also pay attendance allowances for duties specified in its scheme, which must come within the definition of specified duties in the regulations. As I have said, Councillors may no longer claim financial loss allowance. The Widdicombe committee found that only about 1 per cent. of councillors were claiming the allowance—or in other words about 250 councillors. Nonetheless, the decision was of great concern to many in the local authority world. I believe the decision to have been the right one.

My hon. Friend the Member for Eastwood (Mr. Stewart), the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland, will respond later in the light of comments from Scottish members of the Committee.

We welcome this opportunity to debate the regulations. I have already said that we are looking at councillors' remuneration in the context of the local government review. But that does not mean that we have closed our minds to looking at the need to change aspects of the current system, if there is a compelling case for doing so. We have already undertaken, therefore, to review next year the operation of the system covered by the regulations.

The regulations flow from the Widdicombe committee's proposals and the detailed research on which those proposals were based. They are the result of extensive discussion in the House and outside with the local authority associations. They make more than reasonable provision for overall expenditure on allowances when judged against recent actual levels of spending.

I commend the regulations to the Committee.
