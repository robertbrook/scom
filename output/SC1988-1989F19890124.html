"(3) Any savings of mobility allowance paid under that section which the recipient intends to use in connexion with mobility shall be disregarded in calculating the amount of any income-related benefit payable under Part II of the 1986 Act.".

The Committee will see that the amendment is reasonable. Under the supplementary benefit regulations, if a disabled person were saving his mobility allowance to buy a car—which is an eminently sensible thing to do—or for other purposes that would increase his mobility, that money was disregarded and did not count as part of his capital. Many of the factors previously disregarded under the supplementary benefit regulations were reproduced in the income support regulations, but this for some inexplicable reason was not. The factors that continue to be disregarded are intimately analagous with that which we are proposing now, for example, personal possessions,

"a car, furniture and fittings in your house, and family belongings are generally ignored in the calculation of capital."

The exception is where an item has been bought specifically to bring the capital amount below the level of the limit. Therefore, if a car itself is disregarded, the savings for it, which can be seen as deferred payments for it, should also be disregarded.

The other heading is "earmarked capital". If the capital for sale of a house is ear-marked for house purchase in future, it is ignored for up to six months, or longer in certain circumstances. Our amendment deals with a similar circumstance. People save their money to reduce their dependence on the state and thus increase their mobility. With the present price of cars, their capital could soon reach £6,000 and their entitlement to income support would be eliminated completely. This comes under thrift, one of the litany of virtues of which the Government approve. I shall be uncharacteristically brief in introducing the amendment because I have firm confidence that it will be accepted by the Minister with enthusiasm and gratitude.
