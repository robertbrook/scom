Transmissible Spongiform Encephalopathies: Controls on Cattle, Sheep and Goats

Relevant document: [European Community Document No. 12559/96, on the exclusion from human food and animal feed of specified risk material as regards transmissible spongiform encephalopathies.]

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Jeff Rooker)
: I am glad of the opportunity to discuss with the Committee the controls on specified material from cattle, sheep and goats. They were adopted by the Commission on 30 July as Commission decision 97/534/EC, which comes into force on 1 January 1998.

A similar proposal was rejected by the Agriculture Council in December 1996. Since then, following inspections to check the implementation of Community measures on bovine spongiform encephalopathy in member states—other than the United Kingdom and Portugal—the Commission concluded that no member state could be considered free from the potential risk of transmissible spongiform encephalopathies. We drew that report to the attention of our experts on the Spongiform Encephalopathy Advisory Committee, who advised that existing United Kingdom controls on specified material from cattle should be extended to imports. SEAC also recommended that United Kingdom controls on sheep and goat material should include the spinal cord and the spleen, in addition to the current controls on heads.

My right hon. Friend the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food announced in the summer that unless the resurrected Commission proposal was adopted, we in the United Kingdom would introduce unilateral controls on imports in line with SEAC's advice. That was not an attempt to introduce by the back door a ban on imported beef, but to ensure for the British public that the same controls were applied to imported beef wherever it came from as were applied to home-produced beef.

Let me now turn to the decision. It defines specified risk material—or SRM—and prohibits its use for any purpose. It also prohibits the use of the vertebral column of cattle, sheep and goats for the production of mechanically recovered meat. When removed from the carcase, the SRM is to be stained and disposed of by controlled methods.

The decision also prohibits the import of SRM into the Community and requires imports of products of animal origin intended for food, animal feed or 2 medical, pharmaceutical or cosmetic products to be accompanied by a declaration that they do not contain, and are not derived from, specified risk material. At present, there are no derogations for any third country from those requirements.

We received notice this morning by fax that the Commission has tabled an amendment to the decision on the cosmetics and pharmaceutical aspect for discussion in its Standing Veterinary Committee. The matter needs careful study and I shall submit a memorandum about the amendment to the Select Committee on European Legislation when it has been examined fully. As the situation is changing as I am talking, it is difficult for me to be accountable at present to the Committee for such a decision.

I wish to refer to the practical effect of the decision for this country. As a result of the order, there will be no change to the controls on cattle. All our checks are in place and are being policed rigorously and, since May, we have taken many steps to improve the situation. We are committed specifically to maintaining our existing controls over animals slaughtered in the United Kingdom when they go further than the controls in the European Union. We have used the precautionary principle and have gone further than is required, which we are allowed to do.

The situation for sheep and goats is different. The decision requires us to extend our existing controls on the heads of sheep and goats to include the spleen of all sheep and goats, and the tonsils and spinal cord of sheep and goats aged over 12 months—commonly known as mutton—or those that have a permanent incisor tooth erupted through the gum. That, of course, is in line with what the SEAC experts said we must do. That good, independent, scientific advice was made known in the summer and we have followed it.

Concern has been expressed about the difficulties of removing spinal cord from sheep without damaging the meat or slowing down the slaughter line. As a former production engineer in manufacturing industry, I am well aware of the problems of slowing down production lines, which is usually done to improve the quality of the product. However, we acknowledge that it is not easy to remove the spinal cord from sheep, which is why we asked Bristol university and the Meat and Livestock Commission to undertake trials on methods of removing spinal cord. SEAC took those results into account when advising the Government, and copies of that research were made available to the industry. We recently completed consultation on implementing those controls in Great Britain. Needless to say, when introducing legislation, we shall take fully into account what has been said, including what hon. Members say today.

The Meat Hygiene, Service's enforcement of the existing SRM controls in licensed slaughterhouses costs the taxpayer about £18 million a year. When the extra controls on sheep and goats are introduced, the cost will rise by about £26 million a year. The Government have concluded that the cost can no longer be borne by the taxpayer. We therefore propose that from the start of the next financial year, the cost should be recouped from the industry, as is currently JPEG 3 done in respect of Meat Hygiene Service inspections. To that end, we shall soon be consulting on draft legislation. It will be genuine consultation, taking note of the industry's concerns.

The United Kingdom's main worry in adopting the Commission's decision has been about its effect on the cosmetic and pharmaceutical industries. We might have had an interesting discussion about that, but we cannot because it is yet another moving target in Brussels. However, I understand the difficulties, which is one of the reasons why it is being looked at again. Not only the UK is affected.

It came to public attention just before the summer recess that the previous Government had failed to legislate for the beef ban as required. That left us withoug the legal power to take action against the spivs and smugglers who give the industry a bad name; we could not even check lorries at ports. That was rectified on 1 August. The recent EC action against the UK was not over standards in our slaughterhouses, but over failure to enforce the ban. I make that clear because of previous mischievous press reporting.
