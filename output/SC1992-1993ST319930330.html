10.30 am

Mr. Henry McLeish
(Fife, Central)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Council Tax (Transitional Reduction Scheme) (Scotland) Regulations 1993 (S.I., 1993, No. 277).

I shall be brief.

Mr. John Maxton
(Glasgow, Cathcart
)
: Why?

Mr. McLeish
: My hon. Friends might want me to speak at length, but I shall have to disappoint them.

On Tursday, the council tax will be implemented in Scotland, After the debacle of the poll tax—I hope that even the Minister agrees with that view—we have the council tax. Measured against the poll tax, anything would have been an improvement on that system of raising local government finance. However, concerns have been expressed in Scotland about the tax and the provisions that we are discussing.

The Government are cushioning high-value properties in Scotland by developing the banding system. The highest value property will pay no more than three times the basic rate. The high-value properties had their component capped by the Government to ensure that the outcry expected by the Conservatives before the general election did not occur. That is the reality of the banding system.

Mr. Maxton
: Many people in Scotland do not yet know what band their houses are in.

Mr. McLeish
: That is true. I must confess that even my regional council in Fife has given no information about what band our properties will be in. The Minister looks askance at that remark. There are few regions where householders have been advised of their valuation band. Clearly, with only two days before implementation of the council tax, that is a worry. I suspect that local authorities, not the Government, will be inundated with appeals against valuations.

The capping of high-value property caused a great deal of disbelief. The tax was not based entirely on the ability to pay but was caught up in the Government's political expediency before the election.

There is also disquiet about the transitional scheme. Few properties, if any, in the A, B and C categories will benefit. The Minister might want to comment on that. Householders in the upper-value properties will benefit from the £25 million that has been set aside by the Government for the transitional scheme. I should welcome the Government's comments because, if we are talking about a system of local government finance in which the ability to pay is uppermost, the scheme smacks of a transitional component which will benefit the well-off at the expense of those at the bottom of bands A, B and C.

4 At a time of crisis in local government caused by the Government's half-baked scheme for reform and the more absurd notion of privatising water, this should be a time for spending money wisely. Although £25 million may not be a significant amount, it could be better used to invest in jobs, and, more important, in services. There is a delicate balance between central Government and local government, and there should have been much wider discussions about this scheme. That is the view of the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities, which feels that, at a time of stringency, a priority should have been given to any available cash. That does not seem to be reflected in these regulations.

I know that it is a bit early for the Minister, but he is always up to challenges. I can see him smiling already at the possibility of explaining how the amounts that are payable in transitional relief will be calculated. I hope that his brief will extend to a layman's interpretation of pages 3 and 4 of the regulations. The Minister is nodding. I hope that that means that we shall have an explanation.

Regulations are always complex. However, I would like the Minister to explain simply how they will affect council tax payers. How will the amount be calculated? Will the Minister confirm my fear that the regulations will help people at the higher-value end of the property market in Scotland?

Perhaps the Minister will want to reflect on the growing concern about the inconsistent way in which properties are valued and banded. That inconsistency has surfaced in the press in the past few days. Perhaps the Minister will comment briefly on the guidance that has been given from central Government to local government about how to achieve a consistent pattern of valuations throughout Scotland. From what I gather, inconsistencies arise not within particular local authority areas but in particular streets. My hon. Friend the Member for Clydebank and Milngavie (Mr. Worthington) has asked a question about streets in his constituency, where properties seem to have been valued inconsistently. That inconsistency has created not only local anomalies but local anger about how the tax has been implemented.

It is useful to use the Committee as a forum to voice these concerns. Had the Government taken more time in considering the replacement for the poll tax, some of the inconsistencies, anomalies and injustices that will arise from the council tax could have been avoided.

The rates were scrapped because of Conservatives' concern about revaluation. The poll tax was scrapped because of the monumental mess that was inflicted by the then Prime Minister and, at extraordinary cost, VAT was hiked up to get us out of that mess. We now have a council tax which, instead of being a well-thought-out programme of how best to shape local government finance in the 1990s, was born of political expediency.

I hope that the Minister will be able to allay some of our fears and, in particular, to deal with the issue of money being spent to featherbed those of the upper end of the valuation bands who, in our judgment, do not need extra financial assistance.

Mr. Maxton

Mr. Stewart
: I am grateful to the hon. Member, who has anticipated an important point that I was to make later. We see no problem in making the scheme automatic. The basic principles were circulated to local authorities several months ago.

The scheme provides for relief in cases where a household's council tax bill exceeds its final aggregate community charge bill by more than a threshold amount. That is clearly set out.

The hon. Member for Fife, Central asked how the system will work. That is set out in detail in the regulations. We decided that it would be reasonable for those in the higher bands who face higher tax bills to pay a higher initial amount during the transition to the full bill. In that sense, the scheme is progressive. For that reason, the threshold amounts rise in even steps from £91 a year, or £1.75 a week, in band A up to £182 a year, or £3.50 a week in band H.

Mr. Maxton
: Does the transitional relief scheme operate on the gross amount due on a property or on the discounted amount due for a single person household?

Mr. Stewart
: On the discounted amount.

The level of the scheme tax figure is the lowest of three options. In addition, there are amending regulations, which are a response to particular points that have been made during consultation. I must emphasise that the principles of the scheme, as I said in answer to the hon. Member for Cathcart, were communicated to local authorities as long ago as last July. Operational details have not changed since. Therefore, in response to the hon. Gentleman's reasonable question, I can say that the Government are confident that authorities should be able to include transitional relief calculations in initial council tax bills. The first bills, which shall be sent out next month, should contain correct calculations made on the basis of the scheme.

Mr. Stewart
