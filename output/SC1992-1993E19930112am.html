Clause 15
GRANT-MAINTAINED SCHOOLS

Amendment proposed [15 December 1992]: No. 235, in page 8, line 42, at the end to insert the words

'and in determining the amounts of such grants it shall be the duty of the funding authority to exercise its powers with a view to ensuring minimum standards of provision for sufficient books and equipment, the maintenance of school buildings and sufficient resources to limit class sizes to a level prescribed by orders from time to time made by the Secretary of State.'— [Mr. Steinberg.]

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Schools (Mr. Eric Forth)
: I welcome you, Mr. Martin and all the Committee members back after what I am sure we all agree was a well-deserved break. I look forward to stimulating and productive discussion of the Bill under the terms of the sensible timetable on which we have just agreed.

Before we broke up for Christmas, we were considering an amendment, which was discussed at some length, which would allow clause 15 to be used to determine the amount of books and equipment for schools and the appropriate level of maintenance for school buildings and class sizes. However well intentioned that amendment was—Opposition Members explained it at length and with passion—the Committee is neither the appropriate place in which nor the vehicle with which to introduce such a concept.

The hon. Member for Bridgend (Mr. Griffiths) implied that there was not necessarily a relationship in education between resources and results or between class sizes and results. That issue is now beginning to be discussed in a sensible and sensitive way. As I recall, he also agreed that local education authorities themselves differ greatly in their circumstances. That is the key to the problem with the amendment. The amendment, rather arbitrarily, implies that the funding agency should get involved in the detail of the allocation of resources and in judgments about books, equipment, building and class sizes that are rightly the province either of the local education authorities—as it is at the moment—or of the schools themselves in the growing grant-maintained sector.

Anyone who has a passing knowledge of the way in which Government and financing work knows that there is an overall allocation of resources to a particular sector and within that, an allocation or distribution of resources must be made. I advise strongly against people believing that they could drive such matters from the bottom up. It is impossible to say to 24,000 very different schools in very different circumstances, "Here is a desirable level of resource; here is what is necessary for the maintenance of books, equipment and so on. Build that up from the bottom and arrive at a figure at the top." The schools would then say, "Well, let's have it then."

Many Opposition Members have an intimate and expert knowledge of the way in which LEAs work and know perfectly well that an authority cannot be run on the basis of saying to all the schools that if they provide a list of what they need they will get it. Life is not like that at LEA level or at national level. The implication of the amendment is that life is like that, but it is not and, I suspect, never will be, despite the wishes of many members JPEG 701 of the Committee. The amendment, well-intentioned though it is, should be rejected, perhaps with some regret, so that we can get on with the business before us.
