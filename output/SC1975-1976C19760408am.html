AGRICULTURE (MISCELLANEOUS PROVISIONS) BILL
New Clause 10
APPLICATIONS FOR TENANCY OF THE HOLDING

"(1) Any eligible person may within the relevant period (as defined in the preceding section) apply to the Tribunal for a direction entitling him to a tenancy of the holding.

(2)Where only one application is made under this section the Tribunal, if satisfied that the applicant is an eligible person, shall determine whether he is in their opinion a suitable person to become the tenant of the holding.

(3) Where two or more applications are made under this section, then, subject to sub-section (4) below, subsection (2) above shall apply to each of the applicants as if he were the only applicant.

(4) If the applicants under this section include a person validly designated by the deceased in his will as the person he wished to succeed him as tenant of the holding, the Tribunal shall first make a determination under subsection (2) above as regards that person, and shall do so as regards the other applicant or each of the other applicants only if the Tribunal determine that the person so designated is not in their opinion a suitable person to become the tenant of the holding.

(5) If under the preceding provisions of this section only one applicant is determined by the Tribunal to be in their opinion a suitable person to become the tenant of the holding, the Tribunal shall, subject to section (Opportunity for landlord to obtain Tribunal's consent to operation of notice to quit) of this Act, give a direction entitling him to a tenancy of the holding.

(6) If under the preceding provisions of this section each of two or more applicants is determined by the Tribunal to be in their opinion a suitable person to become the tenant of the holding, the Tribunal shall, subject to subsection (9) below, determine which of those applicants is in their opinion the more suitable person to become the tenant of the holding and shall, subject to section (Opportunity for landlord to obtain Tribunal's consent to operation of notice to quit) of this Act, give a direction entitling that applicant to a tenancy of the holding.

(7) Before making a determination under subsection (2) above in the case of any applicant the Tribunal shall afford the landlord an opportunity of stating his views on the suitability of that applicant.

(8) In making a determination under subsection (2) above in the case of a particular applicant, or a determination under subsection (6) above as between two or more applicants, the Tribunal shall have regard to all relevant matters, including—

  1. the extent to which the applicant or each of those applicants has been trained in, or has had practical experience of, agriculture;
  2. the age, physical health and financial standing of the applicant or each of those applicants; and
  3. the views (if any) stated by the landlord on the suitability of the applicant or any of those applicants.

(9) Where subsection (6) above would apply about from this subsection, the Tribunal may, with the consent of the landlord, give instead a direction specifying any two, any three or any four of the applicants within that subsection, and entitling the specified applicants to a joint tenancy of the holding.

(10) In this section " will" includes codicil, and for the purposes of this section a person shall be taken to be validly designated by the deceased in his will as the person he wishes to succeed him as tenant of the holding if, but only if, a will of the deceased which is the subject of a grant of probate or administration either—

  1. contains an effective specific bequest to that person of the deceased's tenancy of the holding; or
  2. does not contain an effective specific bequest of that tenancy, but does contain a statement specifically mentioning the holding or the deceased's tenancy thereof and exclusively designating that person (in whatever words, and whether by name or description) as the person whom the deceased wishes to succeed him as tenant of the holding.

(11) For the purposes of subsection (10) above a statement which is framed so as to designate as mentioned in paragraph ( b) of that subsection different persons in different circumstances shall be taken to satisfy that paragraph if, in the events which have happened, the statement exclusively designates a particular person.

(12) direction under this section given in favour of a person by reason of his being a person validly designated by the deceased as mentioned in subsection (4) above shall be valid even if the probate or administration by virtue of which he was such a person at the giving of the direction is subsequently revoked or varied.

(13) For the purposes of this Part of this Act an application under subsection (1) above which is withdrawn or abandoned shall be treated as if it had never been made ".— [ Mr. Strang.]

    For a considerable period, there has been an extremely intense feeling throughout the agricultural industry at the unfair and unrealistic constraints placed upon it by the restrictive trade practices legislation and by the failure of successive Governments, both Conservative and Socialist to alleviate the situation or even to recognise the true extent of the problem.

    United Kingdom restrictive trade practices legislation is far more formidable than anything that exists with our European partners. It contributes significantly to a perpetuation of the very weak bargaining position of small producers or businesses vis-a-visbuying power concentrated in fewer and fewer hands.

    United Kingdom legislation is very comprehensive and covers any recommendation or agreement on price, production, supply, description, persons to whomsupplied, and so on. It presumes that everything is restrictive unless it is covered by one of a few specific defences, none of which is relevant to agriculture or appropriate to smaller businesses. All agreements or recommendations have to be registered. Penalties for a failure to do so are very severe indeed. Virtually all registered agreements have to be defended before the court or, if they are not, 1180 abandoned. The cost of defence is prohibitive and the chances of success are minimal, virtually non-existent.

    The only exemptions to date are certain co-operatives, and even these have to have a sophisticated constitution, frequently unrealistic and contrary to their commercial and taxation requirements.

    There are important areas of the main legislation that are inappropriate not only to agriculture but also to trade organisations and associations of smaller businesses. This is recognised in European legislation. Quite rightly, the farming industry says " if we are to be part of Europe, let us appreciate and have a full understanding of European legislation".

    Much collective action amongst smaller businesses is neither harmful nor against the national interest, nor of major economic significance. t is frequently advocated by Government. It can help rationalisation of production and facilitate the achievement by small businesses of economies of scale. TheEEC, therefore, exempts from certain of the Community's general rules against restrictive practices companies that have a turnover of less than 150 million units of account or cover less than 10 per cent. of the market.

Community regulationsgive a very wide measure of exemption to agricultural associations because they recognise their weak bargaining position vis-a-vis the large buyers, processors and freezers, and also because they recognise that collective action by groups of farmers is neither against the national interest nor of great economic significance.

Companies and mergers minimally below these limits escape the legislation because they are clearly regarded asbeing of insufficient economic significance. The NFU has never argued that agriculture should be free from all constraint.
