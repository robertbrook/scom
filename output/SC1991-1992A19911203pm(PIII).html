Clause 53
AUTHORITIES SUBJECT TO DESIGNATION

Mr. Gould
: I beg to move amendment No. 302, in page 36, line 42, at end insert—

"() In this Chapter any powers given to the Secretary of State to designate an authority shall not apply if that authority has complied with section 32 above or in the case of a major precepting authority section 43 above.".

The amendment would allow a Secretary of State to designate or cap an authority only when it had failed to comply with the statutory provisions for determining its budget or precept. It would leave the capping procedure in place but prevent the Secretary of State from using it except when an authority had failed to act lawfully.

I do not conceal the fact that the amendment is designed to wreck capping. That will be no surprise to Ministers, as we are opposed to capping in principle. The amendment would, in most cases, end capping powers. We believe that the case against capping is overwhelming, and our arguments, which we have employed on many occasions, will by now be familiar. However, it is not only the Labour party which has expressed root-and-branch opposition to capping; several remarkable people have, from time to time, said that they could not tolerate capping. One of the earliest such opinions was expressed in 1984 when the capping power was first proposed. In a letter to the Conservative party newsletter, "Newsline", Rosalie Mondiot, who was then leader of South Oxfordshire district council, stated:

"What we are concerned about is that this ill thought out bill will… constitutionally damage the recognised right of local authorities to levy their own rates and to be accountable in a local democratically elected manner."

That is the authentic voice of someone who understands and is dedicated to local government and the principle of local democracy. I believe that South Oxfordshire district council sat in Henley—at that time I was living fairly nearby. The fact that the letter came from a Conservative leader of a Conservative local authority adds weight to her spirited defence of local government democracy.

Mrs Mondiot was not alone in her opposition. The Association of District Councils provided a briefing for the Second Reading debate on the Rates Bill 784 in 1984. That association is Tory controlled, yet it stated:

"The Association is deeply convinced that a vigorous democratic and independent local government is an indispensible part of our national system of government. The previous Conservative government recognised this… It declared that 'local government was an essential part of the whole democratic framework of government' and that, above all else, a genuine local democracy implies that decisions should be taken—and seen to be taken—as locally as possible."

That view was echoed by John Lovill who was then chairman of the Association of County Councils, again a local authority association dominated by the Conservatives. He is reported as saying:

"The Bill would give (the Secretary of State)… powers well beyond those which he has now and which will impinge on the autonomy of local authorities to take decisions about the level and quality of services delivered locally to their community."

That was powerful testimony from Tory figures in local government when the capping power was introduced.

We can bring that picture up to date. Mr. Roy Thomason was until recently the Tory leader of the Association of District Councils. That association is still, by the skin of its teeth, Conservative dominated. In 1991, he said of the Local Government Finance and Valuation Act 1991, which extended capping to every council in the land:

"I am shocked that the Government should so attack many of its own supporters in local government, particularly district councils which have raided their balances to keep charge levels down this year and maintained services. It is also shocking that the Environment Secretary should completely ignore all the assurances previous ministers gave when the capping provisions were introduced that low spending authorities would be excluded from the controls."

The announcement of the provisionsal 1992 capping criteria a week or two ago prompted a joint press release from the five national local authority associations condemning universal capping. The position of local government is therefore very clear.

The record is replete with statements from all sorts of people, including the Secretary of State, to the effect that the principle of capping is anathema to the concept of true local government democracy. I do not intend to deploy the whole argument against capping, but I shall refer to another set of objections to the basis on which the criteria for capping are selected. They are, to some degree, selected on the basis of standard spending assessments. There is a wide range of evidence that SSAs are deeply flawed. If the basis is flawed, so is the capping that is based upon it.

The Government consultation paper stated:

"The SSA system is based on extensive research and provides about as fair a way of measuring needs as can be devised."

That was immediately contradicted by Mr. Ken Morris, the county treasurer of Northumberland county council, who said:

"This bland statement is as crass as it is indefensible."

That is pretty harsh language; Mr. Morris clearly felt very strongly about the issue. Later, I may give an JPEG 787 example of one factor that might have led him to that strong expression of opinion.

The Secretary of State's immediate predecessor, the right hon. Member for Bath (Mr. Patten), who is now chairman of the Tory party, agreed in early 1990, I think, that SSAs were flawed and needed revision. He promised that he would do something about it, but nothing has been done.

Let us consider some respects in which SSAs have proved to be an uncertain basis on which to measure anything, let alone capping power. In 1989, the AMA criticised the way that the highway maintenance SSA was calculated. In relation to road lengths, it said that the data on relative maintenance costs had been built up on the basis of a 1980 survey by the Association of County Councils of a handful of sparsely populated counties. The AMA said that the data were out of date and completely unrepresentative.

The AMA said that the data on traffic flows were unreliable and that the Department of Transport and the Department of the Environment kept producing different and incompatible sets of figures and were unable to explain the discrepancies. AMA representatives were unable to recreate or reconcile figures given to them for their own authorities. The AMA said that the data were unstable. The Department of Transport produced evidence of changes of up to 6 per cent per annum for authorities.

In 1990–91, according to the SSAs for highway maintenance produced by the Department of the Environment, outer London was spending £15 million, or 13 per cent more than its SSAs, and inner London was spending £36 million, or 60 per cent more than its SSAs. Despite all the evidence of deteriorating roads in the capital, the Government believed that there should be a substantial cut in road maintenance in London. In 1992–93, SSAs for highway maintenance have been increased by only 1.1 per cent.

In the category "Other Services", it is striking that the SSA for flood defence was distributed mistakenly in 1990–91. Past expenditure was dominated by Thames Water precepts on boroughs which were proportional to rateable value. In 1990–91, however, the precepts were proportional to adults, but the SSA was still allocated on the basis of rateable value. The result was a substantial gain—surprise, surprise—for Westminster and to a lesser extent Camden, while other authorities ended up with inadequate SSAs and higher poll tax bills in their areas as a consequence.

In the vexed aspect of capital spending, the main element of SSA is calculated by adding together debt repayment and interest charges for the debt outstanding, including debt that is likely to arise during the financial year. A figure is taken subtracted to reflect the interest that is earned on capital receipts. That sounds reasonably logical. However, as Rita Hale, an expert on these matters, explained during her time at CIPFA:

"The main weakness in the SSA calculation is the starting point—the figure for the assumed debt outstanding at 31 March 1990. That figure is derived from adjusted GRE figures for 1989/90, but those GREs did not cover the debt charges for all local authority services and only covered the cost of financing 788 capital expenditure incurred after 1 April 1981 for that limited range of services. So the notional debt figure is merely a construct; it bears no consistent relationship to the actual figures for individual authorities."

As a consequence, the debt repayment figures used in the calculation of SSAs are often substantially different from the actual debt repayments that an authority must make. Matters get worse, however. The Association of London Authorities pointed out:

"The assumptions that are built into the SSA calculation about the interest authorities will earn on capital receipts lead to the DoE assuming authorities will receive income they have no hope of raising, because they do not have the implicit capital balances in hand."

That may explain the harsh language of Mr. Ken Morris, the county treasurer of Northumberland county council. As a consequence of those factors, the SSA for Northumberland for 1990–91 assessed the county's interest receipts on balances at £4 million. In reality, however, interest receipts were only £2 million, so Northumberland poll tax payers lost £2 million in lost grant because of the formula.
