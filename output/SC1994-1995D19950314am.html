It is my pleasure to resist new clause 3. The hon. Member for Bristol, South (Ms Primarolo) advanced her arguments at the last sitting and I have the record of that to refresh my memory. She started by criticising the fundamental expenditure review that the Government have undertaken in respect of Customs and Excise. I assure her that it was not a cost-cutting exercise; it was a recognition that the Government owe a duty to the public to scrutinise public bodies and to subject them to disciplines in the absence of the commercial and competitive disciplines that normally apply in the private sector.

The review bore out our belief that Customs and Excise can maintain—indeed, improve—its output, even with fewer staff. A staff reduction programme will take place over the next five years, although certain departments within Customs and Excise will have their staff increased.

The review also found that a disproportionate amount of audit resource was being devoted to very small VAT-registered businesses and recommended replacing the commitment to a visit within three years of registration for new businesses with an alternative, risk-oriented programme, underpinned by improved education, 700 information and support services for newly registered businesses. Audit visits will continue to be made to traders that are identified as non-compliant, thus protecting revenue.

New clause 6
RATE OF POOL BETTING DUTY

"(1) In section 7(1) of the Betting and Gaming Duties Act 1981 (amount of pool betting duty) for '37.50 per cent.' there shall be substituted '32.50 per cent.'.

(2) This section shall apply in relation to bets made at any time by reference to an event taking place on or after the date on which this Act is passed.".—[ Lady O. Maitland.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Lady Olga Maitland
(Sutton and Cheam)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

This new clause presses the case that pool betting duty of 37.5 per cent. should be reduced to 32.5 per cent. The new clause is not contentious and I hope that it will have the support of both sides of the Committee. It is a probing clause and I do not intend to press it to a Division. I am grateful to have had the opportunity to explain it privately to my hon. Friend the Paymaster General and I now want to explain to the Committee why it is essential to have an even playing field with equal taxation for the national lottery and the pools promoters.

The football pools companies are suffering a crisis, having been hit by the extraordinary success of the national lottery. The Committee may have seen the report in the Daily Express last Friday under the headline:

"Vernons is a £100m loser on Lottery."

It quoted Vernons reference to

"unfair competition from the National Lottery."

704 Last Sunday The Sunday Times printed an article with a halcyon picture of couple sitting on a paradise beach. The caption stated:

"Sun shines on the winners: but the outlook for the £900m pools industry is anything but bright".

The headline stated:

"Pools' days may be numbered as lottery has a ball".

I shall not set out all the details from The Sunday Times article, but it stated that City analysts estimate that there is a danger that one or even two pools promoters could go under as a result of the pressure from the national lottery.

It might be helpful if I set out exactly why matters have reached such a crisis. We have to bear in mind the spectacular success of the national lottery—way beyond our wildest dreams. Lottery sales for this year alone are estimated at almost £4 billion, which is almost two and a half times more than the original sales estimate.

Mr. Tim Smith
(Beaconsfield)

Lady Olga Maitland

Those additional sales bring additional tax revenue for the Government—£266 million more than projected for 1995. The amendment to reduce the betting duty on the pools would cost the Government £40.3 million at most, so the lottery produces more than enough revenue to cover the gap. To answer my hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Smith), the lottery will generate £1.1 billion for charity in 1995, giving £220 million to each of the five good causes.

I ask the Committee to bear with me while I give the lottery statistics from November 1994, when it started, to 31 January 1995. Lottery sales totalled £620 million, tax revenue £70 million and Camelot's profit £30 million. Scratch cards are to be introduced later this month—a move tipped to generate an extra £1 billion in revenue. A mid-week draw is also to be introduced and about 15,000 more terminals will be set up to sell tickets. All of that will put tremendous pressure on the pools.

The severe impact on the pools makes me think of what was said when I sat on the Committee on the National Lottery Etc. Bill. We were assured that the lottery would not harm the pools. The Government claimed in their official statement:

"It is certainly not our intention to harm the pools and we strongly believe that the pools and lottery can co-exist."

The then Minister of State at the Home Office, my right hon. Friend the Member for Fareham (Sir P. Lloyd) acknowledged in the Chamber the then conflicting evidence on the likely effect of a national lottery on the pools industry, which he said

"illustrates the need for a careful examination of the issues. We should not just brush aside their concerns… We need to study the evidence carefully and to give those directly affected the chance to put not only their views, but also the evidence on which they base their views."—[ Official Report, 17 January 1992; Vol. 201, c. 1266–7.]

There is plenty of evidence that, due to the uneven playing field, pools companies have been hit very hard, as they can confirm.

JPEG 705 In 1993, before the national lottery had been established, football pools donated millions of pounds to charity, as well as helping football teams raise money for soccer. The pools contributed £65.4 million to the Foundation for Sport and the Arts. A further £35.6 million went to the Football Trust to assist all levels of football. It should be emphasised that the Football Trust, in particular, goes out of its way to help the game at grassroot level, which does not normally excite big donations. Royalty payments of £9.3 million went to the football authorities and £326.7 million went to the Exchequer from pool betting duty. Due to the spectacular success of the national lottery, however, those amounts are seriously at risk. We could end up with a situation where one good cause has simply obliterated a smaller, existing one with a proven track record.

There is no evidence that the national lottery will fund all the losses that it has caused charities that have depended so much on the pools. It is evidence of the downturn that since the national lottery began to function in early November, the effect on the pools companies has been drastic. Zetters, the smallest pools company with 3 per cent. of the market, has reported that turnover is down by 10 per cent. It has been forced to initiate a massive restructuring and an intensive cost-cutting programme in order to stay afloat. Today I spoke to its chairman, Paul Zetter, who admitted that in the past couple of months figures are slightly up, but said that he felt that that increase fudged the issue because, had it not been for the national lottery, his profits would have been infinitely greater.

Vernons, the second largest pools company, has experienced a 15 per cent. fall in revenue and, as a result, has had to make 95 workers redundant—a reduction of one sixth of its work force. In addition, it has had to slash its charitable donations. While it used to give an additional 5p of every 105p stake to the Foundation for Sport and the Arts, now it can give only the 2.5 per cent. tax element that it is required to give, all because of competition from the national lottery.

In practice, the effects of the national lottery have led to an end to large donations from the foundation for projects such as building sports pavillions, leaving only small donations to keep the little charities going, such as the sponsoring of a youth concert, which does not require too much money.

In 1994, before the national lottery began operating, Vernons contribution was worth £8.2 million. Littlewoods, by far the largest pools company with 77 per cent. of the market, has also suffered a 10 per cent. loss in the 14 weeks since the lottery began. It has cut 560 jobs among its 4,000 workers. An insider at the company claimed that the lottery could easily

"finish the pools if drastic steps are not taken".

As a net result, everyone suffers. The increased numbers out of work means a drain on national resources through social security payments. Those people are not entitled to payments from the national lottery. In addition, the good causes that were supported by the pools lose out seriously. Less money is available to help charities.

706 The Football Trust is receiving much less money to continue its work in upgrading fields. The National Council for Voluntary Organisations has estimated that charities will receive about £36 million less this year due to the lottery. The Foundation for Sport and the Arts is down £8.2 million due to lost contributions from Vernons alone, to say nothing of what it will lose from other companies. Normally, the foundation receives 2.5 per cent. of all revenues—a total of £65 million in 1993. All that money has gone.

An eerie precedent is set by Ireland. When it began its state lottery in 1987, more than 80 per cent. of its charities reported diminished income. The pools industry has clearly suffered greatly in the short time since the lottery was invoked. One reason among many why the pools companies have been doing so poorly is that there are serious inequities in the laws that regulate the two industries. I shall not take up the Committee's time by dealing with aspects that are irrelevant to the Finance Bill; instead, I shall concentrate on the importance of examining taxation inequities.

The level of taxation is the crux of the new clause and reason for the unfairness between the two industries. The discrepancies in taxation and contributions to good causes are significant. The pools pay betting duty at 37.5 per cent. whereas the lottery is taxed at 12 per cent. Pools are also forced to donate 8 per cent. of their earnings to charity, while the lottery gives 28 per cent. to good causes. The national lottery's total outtake is 40 per cent., 5 per cent. less than that of the pools, which is 45.5 per cent. The new clause would rectify the inequality by lowering the taxation on pools to 32.5 per cent. That would make the outtake between the two competing interests the same. Alternatively, the figure could be raised to 37.5 per cent., provided that that is paid by both parties.

We should consider why the pools would benefit from a level playing field and fairness, which the Committee will agree is important. Because the lottery has been so successful, it has produced an extra revenue that would cover six times over a 5 per cent. reduction in betting duty for the pools. Although the lottery gives more money to good causes, that money is spread over a large area, which reduces its effect. The pools companies' donation to charity is deeply entrenched in football. When the companies suffer, the sport does too—something for which the lottery cannot make up.

The argument that the lottery is primarily philanthropic but that the pools are run for commercial reasons—profit—does not hold water. Camelot makes a higher percentage of profit than the pools companies do, which may make it seem more profit-oriented. The pools and the lottery are both run for good causes—28 per cent. of the lottery's earnings is given to good causes and more than 5 per cent. becomes profit: 8 per cent. of the pools companies' earnings is given to good causes and less than 3 per cent. is profit. The lottery and the pools both donate more money to good causes than they keep in profit. However, Camelot earns more in profits—in percentage of sales and in pounds—than the entire pools industry. It is doubtful whether Camelot is any less commercial than the pools companies.

The pools cannot give any more money than they already do to good causes. The lottery can give a higher percentage to charity because of the 25.5 per cent. JPEG 707 difference in betting duty from the pools. In 1990 and 1991, when the betting duty on pools was reduced by 2.5 per cent., the extra money was rerouted not to profit, but to charitable organisations such as the Foundation for Sport and the Arts and the Football Trust.

Although the pools cannot give as much money to charities as the lottery can the pools money is not as widely dispersed as that from the lottery. The organisations to which the pools can give—primarily football—are greatly dependent on that money. Those organisations have already suffered a large drop in pools funding because of competition from the lottery. In 1995, the Football Trust will receive half the amount of contributions that it received in 1994. That will severely affect the grassroots level of the game and the progress in providing ground safety and refurbishments to ageing fields.

Vernons has withdrawn its clients contribution to the Foundation for Sport and the Arts and, unless betting duty is reduced, Littlewoods will be forced to do the same, probably in August. Plans to restructure the Football Trust to cover ground safety for other sports—cricket, rugby and non-league football—have been halted, as the pools companies, suffering huge losses, can no longer afford to consider the matter unless there is a significant reduction in betting duty.

More would be gained by reducing the burden of taxation on the pools to the same level as that on the lottery. There would be not only a level playing field but, if the lottery and pools are permitted to compete on even terms, a chance that the pools would flourish. The pools have never been—and are unlikely to be—a threat to the lottery. All the pools companies want is fairness in an activity in which they have a genuine competence.

It is in no one's interest to have a flagging pools industry. The drain on national resources includes paying social security for lost jobs, as well as the loss of money for good causes, which might not be compensated for by the lottery. Of course, the lottery might consider making up the lost benefit by donating the difference to the Foundation for Sport and the Arts so that causes do not lose out. But that is probably not a realistic prospect.

It would be helpful if my hon. Friend the Paymaster General would say that he is keeping the pools problem under review. It would also be helpful if he were to compensate in the forthcoming Budget for the difficulties faced by pools companies. I remind him that both the then Secretary of State for National Heritage, my right hon. Friend the Member for City of London and Westminster, South (Mr. Brooke) and the then Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State, my hon. Friend the Member for Salisbury (Mr. Key), said in the Committee on the National Lottery etc. Act 1993 that it was not their intention to harm the pools. They believed that the pools and the lottery could co-exist. It is in all our interests to ensure that that is so.

New clause 13 is of wider significance than may be appreciated by hon. Members on both sides of the Committee. Although it relates principally to inheritance tax and the failure to index some exemptions, it might have a wider impact. The new clause might prevent discrimination against the institution of marriage and some ethnic minorities, in particular those from the Indian subcontinent. The problem is not one of inheritance tax in general, which is dealt with every year when we index the principal threshold, but of the indexation of specialised thresholds that were set out under the original legislation, many of which have not been indexed for many years—one has not been indexed since 1975.

Many people might think that the exemption limit for transfers to non-domicile spouses applies to people who do not live in the United Kingdom. That is not the case. It would be wrong for us to confuse non-residents with non-domiciles. It is possible for a British citizen to be nonresident in the United Kingdom, but domiciled in the United Kingdom for inheritance tax purposes. It is also possible for someone to be resident here, but not domiciled here. That can apply where, for example, a British citizen has married someone of overseas origin who is resident, but may not be domiciled, in the United Kingdom. In some of our immigrant communities, someone who married an individual who originally came from outside the United Kingdom may have married someone who is not domiciled here for inheritance tax purposes.

That is especially relevant in the case of the annual exemption for transfers to non-domiciled spouses. Under general tax law, transfer between spouses is an exempt transaction—husbands and wives can give sums of money to each other without affecting their tax situation. However, that does not apply in the case of inheritance tax. It is possible for a couple both to be resident in the United Kingdom, but for one spouse to be non-domiciled here because he or she originated in another country and may have retained connections with that other country or expressed a wish to be buried there. A transfer between such a couple would not be exempt for inheritance tax purposes.

Let us suppose that a husband gives a half share in the family home to his wife and that his wife, although resident here, is non-domiciled in the United Kingdom. If the husband dies, that transfer will not be free from inheritance tax—even though it is the family home, in which the spouse and children continue to live. A tax charge may be imposed that forces them to sell the family home.

Many people thought that the provision applied only to people who live overseas, but it does not. Domicile and residence are not the same thing. It is easy to understand how this measure can impact either on those who work overseas and marry foreign-born spouses, or on those who are immigrants to this country.
