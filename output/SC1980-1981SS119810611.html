: The Committee will be relieved to hear that I was just at the end of my remarks on this matter, but perhaps I might recapitulate briefly to restate the position to myself if to no one else.,

I think that consideration in the interval since we last met has rather strengthened my conviction that there is no need for this general power of waiver. Having read what was said by the Minister and by some of his ingenious supporters, I cannot say that I am swept away by the force of the examples that have been advanced. I do not want to go into the niceties of the specific scenario that was sketched, but I do not believe that the dangers inherent in the general waiver—the possibilities of abuse—are justified by the example of a disillusioned and distrustful potential parent-in-law determined to ensure that the potential son-in-law shall have no claim in the house.

Strangely enough, the one that I found most convincing was that produced by the hon. Member for South Angus (Mr. Fraser) when he advanced the possibility of a waiver being signed in order to make good or make convincing a security to raise money, perhaps to save a family business. But I should have thought that, in that event, one would not need to do it by this general waiver power. I should imagine that it would be possible, if security documents were being drawn up, for the wife to consent to waive her interest for the purpose of that security and that presumably would lapse when the security was repaid. That would meet the point made by the hon. Member for South Angus.

On the other hand, there is the possibility, as we discussed, of the spouse signing under duress, whatever assurances may be obtained at the time, or, in a haze of good will, making decisions which would prejudice herself and her children without realising their full significance.

My view is that we do not need this waiver, given the fact that, when it comes to the break-up 46 of a marriage or the disposal of property, under clause 7 there is adequate machinery for waiving consent to any dealing in the property. It seems to me that, if good will still exists, that can be used. If good will at that stage does not exist, then there is a social case for saying that the spouse's position should not have been undermined and alienated at a much earlier date.

I shall listen with interest to what the Minister has to say on this matter. I ought to mention that I do recognise that, to some extent, following amendments would remove some of our doubts.
