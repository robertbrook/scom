It is the lifeblood of the pit, and we cannot continue to operate a mine without adequate ventilation unless we run a considerable risk. Ventilation is important not only to maintain a standard of comfort in the pit, but also from the point of view of the health of the men working in the pits, so that if the ventilation is not adequate, they run two risks. One is that they may suffer physically as a result of inadequate ventilation, and the second is that they run considerable risk of losing their lives.

I have taken the trouble to look at the records of the explosions which have taken place in the area of Lancashire in which I live, and I find that from 1852 to 1910—before the passing of the 1911 Coal Mines Act—there were 24 serious explosions, and that 95 per cent. of those explosions, according to the evidence revealed at the subsequent inquiries, were due to inadequate ventilation. When we arrive at a state of affairs like that, with 24 explosions in that period of time, can the Government or the Committee wonder why we are so persistent in our demand that this Clause should be taken back?

There is another vital point that has to be considered. In years gone by, when our pits were not manned to the extent which they are today, we had pits which, if they employed 300 men, were considered large pits, but today, in some modern pits, we may have as many as 1,500 or even 2,000 men and boys working underground at one time. That is a matter that should be taken into consideration by the Government.

What was the number of lives lost in the series of explosions to which I have referred? The lowest number was six, and the highest, which concerned an explosion in December, 1910, when, at the Pretoria pit in Lancashire, 344 men and boys were hurled into eternity. Why? Because somebody had failed, and failed 1254 miserably, to see that there was adequate ventilation in that pit.

No further words of mine are required in stressing the importance of this matter, and I am not arguing the case from my own point of view, but because I have taken the trouble to search the evidence submitted by people who know something about the conditions in that pit prior to that terrible explosion. Since those days, we have tried repeatedly to impress upon the Ministry, upon the inspectors and the mine managers the importance of adequate ventilation.
