Double Taxation Relief (Taxes on Income) (Guyana) Order 1992

10.30 am

Mr. Nicholas Brown
(Newcastle upon Tyne, East)
: On a point of order, Dr. Blackburn. May I welcome you to the Chair of your first Committee and congratulate you on achieving that office? I am sure that the Committee will make speedy progress under your astute tutellage.

10.31 am

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Stephen Dorrell)
: The hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, East (Mr. Brown) has taken the words out of my mouth. So anxious is he to preserve his position in this morning's debate that he wanted to be the first to record the Committee's congratulations to you, Dr. Blackburn.

I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Double Taxation Relief (Taxes on Income) (Guyana) Order 1992.

The background to the double taxation arrangements between Britain and Guyana is as follows. Arrangements were concluded in 1947, but they lapsed in 1971 when Guyana became independent. Following independence, there was spasmodic interest in the re-establishment of the double taxation regime between the two countries, interest which was seriously taken up in 1988.

According to the existing arrangements for negotiating double taxation, the Inland Revenue undertakes discussions directly with the other country and drafts a treaty to be submitted to Ministers for approval. Once approved, it is tabled in the House and comes into effect by delegated authority under the provisions of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988 when the Privy Council makes an order that it should take effect. The Act also requires a prayer in the House before such an order is brought into effect. That is why we are here this morning.

The Inland Revenue has produced such a double taxation treaty. It was signed in Georgetown on 31 August 1992. It has been submitted for approval, which has been granted. It was tabled in the House on 6 November and we must now debate the prayer that the treaty be brought into effect. If it is given effect, it will join a body of more than 100 double taxation treaties between this and other countries.

The hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, East will have studied the terms of the treaty and discovered that it defines the terms necessary to understand the double taxation arrangements. It itemises different types of income, including capital gains, and allocates the taxing rights for different types of income between the two contracting parties. It provides arrangements to avoid double taxation of classes of income to which taxation rights are shared between the two contracting parties. The treaty is based on model arrangements published by the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and 4 Development. It will greatly assist trade and investment between Britain and Guyana. One might even say that it is a very important measure for those who are involved in trade and investment with Guyana. I commend the order to the Committee.

10.35 am

Mr. Nicholas Brown
: The Financial Secretary was right to stress the importance of the treaty and, in doing so, he has set out the method by which double taxation treaties, of which there are more than 100, are dealt with by the House. The Committee must scrutinise the treaty and report back to the House, so this is the forum in which such matters are scrutinised in detail.

I understand that the Government wish to make greater use of Committees such as this to examine financial legislation: not only double taxation treaties, but other matters that have hitherto been dealt with in Committee on the Finance Bill. Opposition Members are somewhat anxious about that.

The treaty with Guyana replaces the previous treaty, which was negotiated in 1947 and terminated by Guyana in 1971. If I may borrow a phrase from the Financial Secretary, the agreement is an evolution of Guyana's policy on double taxation treaties with us.

The Financial Secretary said that the double taxation convention with Guyana is comprehensive. It covers a range of topics, including capital gains tax and corporation tax, but it is not as comprehensive as the Financial Secretary states. Of course the treaty covers capital gains tax and corporation tax—it would have a gaping hole if it did not—but it also covers a range of personal incomes, business profits, dividends and interest, directors' and other fees and so on, and it includes an article on the elimination of double taxation.

I cannot, however, find in the treaty any reference to article 22 of the OECD model convention, which covers capital. In a comprehensive treaty such as this, I would have expected to find something closely parallel to the model convention, but I cannot. The Committee is probably familiar with the OECD model convention on double taxation treaties, and I do not doubt that Conservative Members can recite all its articles by rote. For those who cannot, it may be helpful if I outline the contents of article 22 on the taxation of capital:

"1. Capital represented by immovable property referred to in Article 6, owned by a resident of a Contracting State and situtated in the other Contracting State, may be taxed in that other State.


2. Capital represented by movable property forming part of the business property of a permanent establishment which an enterprise of a Contracting State has in the other Contracting State or by movable property pertaining to a fixed base available to a resident of a Contracting State in the other Contracting State for the purpose of performing independent personal services, may be taxed in that other State."

Paragraph 3 refers specifically to

"ships and aircraft operated in international traffic and by boats engaged in inland waterways transport, and by movable property pertaining to the operation of such ships"

Shipping is covered by a separate article, although it might have been covered by incorporation of the OECD model article 22 on the taxation of capital. The fact that the treaty does not include the model article verbatim does not mean that at least some of the matters are not covered by it. Indeed, I suspect that some of them are. Other parts of the treaty appear to deal with some of those points although JPEG 5 they do so in a way different from that recommended by the OECD. Why have the Government agreed to a treaty phrased differently from the OECD model?

The article in the treaty which deals specifically with shipping and air transport is not worded in precisely the same language as that recommended by the OECD. There are a number of possible reasons for that. The key difference is that article 9 of the order states that

"Profits derived by a resident of a Contracting State from the operation of ships or aircraft in international traffic shall be taxable only in that State."

It goes on with a definition that seems more restrictive than the OECD's recommended one.

Gold dredging is one of the pillars of the Guyanan economy. The ownership of the dredgers, which may be owned by international companies rather than those registered purely in Guyana, may have been a consideration in the drafting of some craft trade with Venezuela up the Pomeroon river; perhaps the ownership of those vessels is covered by the treaty and is a matter for special consideration. I am sure that the Financial Secretary will have something to say about that and about the sort of cargo which those craft carry.

Treaties of this kind attempt to prevent fraud and the evasion of customs or other dues, and to deal with illicit trade. No doubt the Financial Secretary will assure us that the legitimate interests of the Guyana and British Exchequers will be fully protected by article 9 of the treaty even though it departs from the wording recommended in article 22 of the OECD model convention.

I am surprised that the Financial Secretary is looking perplexed. I should have expected him to have at his fingertips enough information about trading patterns on the Pomeroon river and about the main Venezuelan exports to Guyana to allow him to intervene spontaneously.

Article 12 deals with the taxation of interest—no small matter. Article 12(7) is a non-standard clause. I am sure that there are special reasons why the Government decided to word article 12(7) to (11) as they have. Their objective is not exceptional and the Opposition do not object to it. We should however, like the Government to tell us why they have tried to achieve those objectives by the arrangements set out in the treaty. The arrangements are not standard. They might refer to something specific in relation to trading relationships between Britain and Guyana or, perhaps the Government of Guyana asked for some special requirement to be met. I would understand if that were the case, but some explanation should be offered to the Committee.

As I am sure the Financial Secretary will tell us in a moment, the purpose of article 12(7) to (11) is to avoid double taxation. Indeed, that theme runs through the treaty. As such, it is not objectionable. It deals with the treatment of distribution. Yet the issue is similar to that covered by clauses 51 and 52 of this year's Finance Bill.

However, the Government's approach in the treaty is the opposite to that adopted in the Finance Bill. I would be the first to acknowledge that there is more than one approach to a problem, but I should like to hear the Government's justification for taking an approach, which, as I am sure the Financial Secretary will concede, is inconsistent with their approach to the same issue in the Finance Bill. Perhaps that approach will block a potential loophole, but it is the opposite to deciding that interest cannot be treated as a 6 distribution and the implication of article 12 is that it can. That may have something to do with the structure of businesses in Guyana. I look forward to hearing the Financial Secretary's explanation.

Article 12(11) of the order may be specific to Guyana and act as a qualification of the other paragraphs. Alternatively, the Financial Secretary may argue that article 12(9) covers that point and is intended to act as a qualifying section. If that is the explanation, I understand it, although I again draw to the attention of the Committee—particularly to those members of the Committee who sat through the Finance Bill—the inconsistency of the Government's approach. The Opposition do not oppose the objective of article 12(7) to (11) but seek clarification of the Government's aims. Without some provision of this sort, the arrangements might create opportunities for what charitable Government members call tax planning and what the Opposition refer to as tax avoidance or tax evasion. It is not unreasonable for the Government to make arrangements to cover such matters. It is necessary that they justify those arrangements and I invite the Financial Secretary to do so.

Apart from gold dredging and trade with Venezuela, to which I referred earlier, agricultural trade is Guyana's mainstay. Sugar and rice, important export commodities, will be governed by the arrangements set out in the treaty. I am not aware of the structure of the businesses that deal in those main exports, but if they are international, rather than national, companies and if some of them are British-owned, they will benefit substantially from a treaty to deal with the taxation of whatever profits they make.

I am sure that such companies have every bit as much to gain from the GATT round as they do from the double taxation treaty, and I can understand why the Guyanan Government seem to have adopted—I was going to say an evolution of their previous policy of not agreeing to the free market—a policy of agreeing to the free market. That evolution will be to the advantage of those who trade internationally with Guyana and those who own institutions which trade with Guyana.

I appreciate that some of the points that I have made are matters of detail. I would not want it to be thought that we are carping or not being constructive about the treaty. We wish the treaty well and support the principles that underpin it, but because the Committee represents the only forum in the House for scrutinising such matters, I think it important that we be fully satisfied that the treaty does what it is supposed to before we return it to the House.

I draw the Financial Secretary's attention to article 9. Is he satisfied that the arrangements set out there to deal with air transport and shipping do not proved a potential loss to the British Exchequer in the area of flagging out? I refer to the possibility of the ownership of airline fleets or, perhaps more plausibly, shipping fleets, being onshore in Guyana with no fully reciprocal obligations to Great Britain? I presume that that happens with double taxation treaties that Guyana has made with other powers—most likely the United States.

The OECD model convention is quite specific on those points, and I am worried that the treaty adopts a different approach more specific to the special needs of Guyana.

Having explored these issues with the Committee, and having clearly excited much interest among Conservative Members. I shall let the Financial Secretary respond. I shall return to the issues later, if that proves necessary.

10.53 am

Mr. Dorrell
: The hon. Gentleman raised three substantial issues arising from the treaty. The first concerns article 22 of the OECD model and the question of why there is no provision in the treaty to match that article, which governs double incidence of capital taxes. The answer is simple: there is no prospect of the double incidence of capital taxes in any country bilateral with the United Kingdom—for the not-very-difficult reason that the United Kingdom does not impose capital taxes.

Mr. Brown
: But does Guyana?

Mr. Dorrell
: I am not certain, but there is no prospect of double taxation arising bilaterally between the two countries, because this country does not impose capital taxes. The treaty is concerned not with the Guyanan tax regime, but with avoiding double taxation. Because we do not have a capital taxes system, there cannot be double taxation, whatever the regime is in Guyana. I have just been assured that Guyana does not tax capital either.

Secondly, the hon. Gentleman asked about the definition in the treaty of the taxing rights arising from shipping and aircraft. We have long preferred to base taxing rights for shipping and aircraft on the residence of a company, not on the OECD formation, which relies on effective management.

Part of the answer to the hon. Gentleman's question about the difference between this treaty and the OECD model was given by his own further question about flagging out. It is clearly more difficult to relocate the residence of a company than the effective management of a company. That is part of the reason why, for taxing rights, we prefer residence to effective management. The treaty, therefore, reflects our preferred formulation.

Mr. Peter Mandelson
(Hartlepool)
: The Minister says that residence is more important and that the Government prefer residence to effective management. Will he explain to the Committee what he means by residence?

Mr. Dorrell
: The hon. Gentleman may know that, in English law, the question of residence of a company is one that has detained lawyers for a much longer time than the Committee has at its disposal. I do not propose, therefore, to engage in a detailed definition, but if the hon. Gentleman would like to see the definition for the purposes of this treaty he should look at article 4 of the treaty.

The third issue raised by the hon. Gentleman concerned the article covering interest and the arrangement of circumstances in which an interest payment from one country to another is or is not treated as a distribution. The article covering interest provides, as a derogation from the domestic law of both countries, that interest paid by one company to another should not be regarded as a distribution, except when one company is a SO per cent subsidiary of another. The concerns—we discussed them in this year's Finance Bill—about the potential use of interest payments as a means of tax avoidance apply most when one company is a 50 per cent subsidiary of another. That is why domestic law is retained in such cases.

We and the Guyanese have sufficient confidence in our respective tax regimes to say that, where there is not a 50 per cent subsidiary relationship, there should be a derogation from the usual terms of domestic law, which 8 provide that an interest payment outside this country can be taxed as a distribution. I hope that I have answered directly the three points that the hon. Gentleman properly raised concerning this important treaty.

The Financial Secretary referred me to article 4, which is improtant to our understanding of the order and of how the arrangements will operate in practice. That is the crux of the matter, as can be seen from the explanatory note.

"The Convention provides for business profits not arising through a permanent establishment to be taxed only in the country of the taxpayer's residence."

That is the crux of the order; it is a key to understanding how the arrangements will work in practice.

Article 4 deals with who is a resident. It provides:

"For the purposes of this Convention, the term "resident of a Contracting State" means any person"—

this is the important part—

"who, under the laws of that State, is liable to tax therein by reason of his domicile, residence"—

whatever domicile may be. I have already shown that it depends on the season or month of the year.

The order does not make clear the difference between domicile and residence. I defy any member of the Committee to try, but perhaps the Financial Secretary can tell us the difference. Article 4 continues: "place of management"—another ambiguous phrase—

"or any other criterion of a similar nature."

The sky is the limit. Wherever a person chooses to make his bed and lay his bones, under the widest interpretation of the order, might constitute a criterion of "a similar nature".

Article 4 continues:

"The terms 'resident of the United Kingdom' and 'resident of Guyana' shall be construed accordingly."

Article 4(2) states that where an individual is a resident of both contracting states, his status shall be determined in accordance with a whole set of rules. Not only is it difficult to draw watertight conclusions about where a person's residence, or domicile is in the co-operative republic of Guyana—that is complicated enough—but there is the additional complication of people who are residents of both contracting states. Where is such a person assessed for taxation for the purposes of the order?

The rule upon which the order elaborates tries to help, but I am not sure that it does. Article 4(2)(a) states:

"he shall be deemed to be a resident of the Contracting State in which he has a permanent home available to him".

What is "a permanent home"? What does "permanent" mean? It means fixed, unmoving; something that a person owns; and, of course, one can own two fixed dwellings in two different places.

It is not very helpful to define one's residence as the place where one has a permanent home. Many Members of Parliament have two permanent homes. I certainly have two such homes, and I think that other members of the Committee have, too. I have a permanent home, which I would regard as my principal home, where I keep my goods, chatels, books and prized possessions, in my constituency of Hartlepool, where I live. I also have a JPEG 13 permanent home that I use during the week, when I attend to my parliamentary duties. I own both homes. I know that only too well as I pay huge mortgages on each of them. So I know the definition of permanence only too well. I pay through the nose for the permanence of my two homes.

I defy anyone, using that analogy, to tell me which of those two homes is the permanent one. I do not think anyone could, because both homes are permanent.

My point relates to the differences between here and there. The order aims to help us to solve the dilemma of where an individual has two permanent homes when it is not possible to determine the principal as opposed to the permanent home. Had the order used slightly different language and tried to define a principal as opposed to a permanent home, it would be easier for us to understand the order's proposals, and how they will work in practice. The order does not offer such a definition. It merely refers to two permanent homes. The order does, however, try to take us a step further, aiming to develop the definition of a resident. Article 4(2)(a) states that the person will:

"be deemed to be a resident of the Contracting State with which his personae and economic relations are closer (centre of vital interests)".

With the best will in the world, I think that anyone would find it difficult to draw up criteria to provide a clear and understandable framework that would enable someone who is deeply immersed in and entirely familiar with his economic and business circumstances to determine where personal and economic relations are closer—someone who is not familiar with the economic circumstances or business arrangements of the individual would have even more trouble. What happens if an individual's personal relations point one way and his economic relations another?

All hon. Members can envisage circumstances in which an individual has two permanent homes in two different countries. His family, his friends, his wife—or her husband, if she is female—his country of origin, where he enjoys spending his leisure time and where his or her personal relations point may not necessarily be in the same country as his economic activity, relations and business orientation.

14 I shall take the analogy which I have previously offered to the Committee a step further: my personal ties are where I spend the bulk of my personal time and have my friends and that is in my home in Hartlepool—my constituency. That will come as no surprise to members of the Committee. Arguably, my economic relations and ties are not in Hartlepool because that is not strictly where I earn my living. I earn my living where I am employed and I am not employed in Hartlepool in the strictest sense, although I am employed on behalf of Hartlepool as the people have elected me to represent that constituency. I am employed formally at my place of work in London as opposed to my place of domicile. Therefore, article 4(2)(a) does not further the understanding of how the order might work or provide the best possible and most infallible definition of residency.

If members of the Committee were to ask me where my centre of vital interests is, I would be hard put to say. In a sense, the people who have elected me where I live are most vital to me. On the other hand, the role that I am able to perform in my economic place, is vital.

Article 4(2)(b) helps further:

"if the Contracting State in which he has his centre of vital interests cannot be determined"—

and I think that I showed quite persuasively how difficult it would be, although not impossible, to determine where a person's centre of vital interests is—

"or if he has no permanent home available to him in either Contracting State, he shall be deemed"—

and this is crucial—

"to be a resident to the Contracting State in which he has an habitual abode".

We have already referred to the difficulty of defining or drawing up strictly understandable criteria for describing an habitual abode. I have also said how people are frequently forced to make their habitual abode in different places at different times of the year as economic opportunity arises. Of all the definitions of residence contained in article 4, this is the most loose and flabby.

I do not think that the term "an habitual abode" is susceptible to accurate or consistent definition. It is open to ambiguity. People could discuss its meaning for hours and construct different versions of what it might mean to different people in different countries in different circumstances. Far from helping us to conclude what a residence is, the term is positively unhelpful.

If it were possible—and I assume that it is not—to redraft the order, I would suggest that that term should be the first priority for reconsideration because it would create more fuss and bother than help and elucidation.

The order, having plunged us into this muddle, tries to lift us out of the mess of ambiguity and put us back on terra firma by stating in article 4(2)(c) that

"if he has an habitual abode in both Contracting States …"—

if the person has two or more habitual abodes, whatever they are, in each of the Contracting States—

"he shall be deemed to be a resident of the Contracting State of which he is a national".

With one passport per person, it should be unambiguous and straightforward to conclude of which state that person is a national.

If the place of residence is not clear from where a person's home is, from where a person's economic relations take place, or from where a person's habitual abode may be, it could be determined by describing or eliciting a person's nationality.

JPEG 15 A problem would arise if the person were a national of two contracting states, that is, a person with dual nationality. A friend of mine is a national of three countries—Britain, France and the United States of America. He has a profliferation of passports and I would be hard to state his nationality. Similar circumstances could occur when interpreting the order.

The more I consider the order, the more it makes me wonder who drafted it. Article 4(2)(d) states that

"if he is a national of both Contracting States or of neither of them,"—

heaven knows where he or she may be a national—

"the competent authorities of the Contracting States shall settle the question by mutual agreement."

That provision prompts two questions: who reaches the mutual agreement, and what happens if that mutual agreement is not reached?

I know who would agree in this country; we have a fairly clear set of arrangements by which a fairly clear group of people determine such matters. They are the commissioners of Inland Revenue and their staff, and they are a competent, well-resourced, experienced bunch of people. Some people may use other words to describe them, especially if they are on the harsh receiving end of their decisions. I am strictly neutral. Who reaches the conclusion? Where does mutuality begin and end? It ends not in this country where the Inland Revenue is but in the Co-operative Republic of Guyana.

That brings me to the point made by my hon. Friend the Member for Edinburgh, Central (Mr. Darling). If mutual agreement is to be reached on such matters, reliable people must be in post who have proper experience and knowledge to make judgments with some continuity and consistency. They must have a certain dependency.

It is not part of my argument, to which the Financial Secretary will be eager to respond, to cast aspersions on the arrangements in Guyana. After all, the republic has other problems, including reaching a stage when it can hold a general election on the basis of a mutually agreed electoral register, let alone anything else. We can only begin to imagine its difficulties in putting people in place with the criteria, judgment and consistency to deal with individual cases.

Will the Financial Secretary say who, where and in what post is the person in Guyana who has the necessary experience and professional judgment to reach the mutual agreement referred to in article 4(2)(d)? If all else fails, and the worst occurs when it is not possible through the terms of order to identify such a person, let alone equip that person with the necessary criteria with which to determine such matters, the terms of residence can be discarded. The definition of a place of effective management and where that might be situated, as described in article 4(3) would then come into play. A person wending his way through the different definitions—almost as though he was taking part in a obstacle course—of the rules for residence and different qualifications would reach the point of complete despair. Due to his inability to define properly the place of residence, he would fall back on a place of effective management.

Will the Financial Secretary offer his qualification of those terms in relation to residence and place of effective management, which is the term referred to in paragraph (3)? I am sure that the hon. Gentleman will be able to do that because he has cosiderable knowledge of the 16 background of those matters. He was helpful to me during the proceedings on the Finance Bill earlier this year. None the less, it is incumbent on him to offer the Committee that guidance, because the matter rests on that definition. If it fails, the order will collapse. The matter is as crucial as that.

"Nationals of a Contracting State shall not be subjected in the other Contracting State to any taxation or any requirement connected therewith, which is other or more burdensome than the taxation and connected requirements to which nationals of that other State in the same circumstances are or may be subjected."

That is the essence of article 25, and it may seem reasonable and to accord with the philosophy of double taxation not only of this treaty but of the 100 or so other treaties that the United Kingdom has concluded with our international partners, until one reads about Guyana's economic background and some of the treaties that it is trying to conclude with its partners, not in Europe but in Latin America.

I have grave doubts whether the United Kingdom and its business interests will receive non-discriminatory treatment as a result of some of the treaty provisions that Guyana is about to enter into. An example involves the plans of the Caribbean community Governments—the Caricom Governments—to impose new tariff restrictions as a result of the creation of a common market in that part of the world in approximately two years.

The Committee will know that a full debate is taking place in the Caribbean about the need for Governments in that part of the world to unite and work together, perhaps on the model of the European Community. According to an article in the Financial Times of 30 April this year, Caricom has been arguing that the common external tariff is needed to protect its members' economies while it creates that common market which would give it the strength to compete in the global marketplace. Many of us will sympathise with the problems faced by developing JPEG 17 economies such as Guyana's, and perhaps even with their need to engage in protectionist measures to ensure that they get a fair deal in the international marketplace.
