The Minister of State, Foreign and Commonwealth Office (Sir Nicholas Bonsor)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft European Communities (Definition of Treaties) (Partnership and Co-operation Agreement between the European Communities and their Member States and the Republic of Belarus) Order 1996.

We are to debate four orders. Provided that no member of the Committee has an objection, I propose to generalise. Most, if not all, of what I am about to say will apply to all the orders; I do not think that the Committee would wish me to repeat what I am about to say four times. I hope that the hon. Mr. Dalyell

Sir Nicholas Bonsor
: The orders relate to partnership and co-operation agreements—or PCAs, as I shall call them from now on—which the European Communities and their member states signed in June 1994 with Moldova and in 1995 with Belarus—that is the agreement that we are now discussing—Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan. They specify the agreements as Community treaties under section 1(3) of the European Communities Act 1972.

The agreements are mixed agreements. In other words, some of their provisions fall within Community competence and others fall within the competence of member states. That means that, before they can come into force, the agreements must first be ratified by all 15 member states and by Belarus, Moldova, Kyrgyzstan and Kazakhstan. The European Community must also ratify each agreement separately.

4 The draft orders must be approved by both Houses so that Community obligations under the agreements may be implemented by the United Kingdom. The orders were approved by the House of Lords on 16 April. If both Houses choose to approve the orders, they will then be submitted to the Privy Council.

I should like to give the Committee some background to the negotiation of the agreements to highlight their main points and to place them in the context of our broader relations with Belarus and the other nations. In doing so, I hope to demonstrate how the agreements make a substantial and practical contribution to the process of reform in those countries. They are good for the four countries and good for our objective of spreading prosperity and security across Europe and into our neighbours in central Asia.

Past relations between the European Community and the Soviet Union were governed by a modest trade and co-operation agreement signed in 1989, which provided a basic framework for managing trade relations and the limited co-operation that existed at the time.

Following the break-up of the Soviet Union, the European Community, under the stewardship of the United Kingdom presidency in 1992, decided to intensify its relations with each of the newly emerged independent states. The situation called for a new kind of relationship, based on partnership and pratical co-operation, to help those countries through the difficult process of fundamental political and economic reform.

The partnership and co-operation agreements, which are individually tailored to suit the particular requirements of each state, are designed to provide a practical and workable framework for that co-operation. Eventually all the 12 countries of eastern Europe and central Asia are expected to conclude PCAs with the EU. The first such agreements were signed with Ukraine and Russia in June 1994. In 1995, we were one of the first member states to ratify those two agreements. In addition to the four agreements that we are debating today, the EU signed PCAs with Georgia, Armenia and Azerbaijan on 22 April 1996.

Ratification of the PCAs can take years, so it is likely to be some time before the agreements with Belarus, Moldova, Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan come into force. To bridge that gap, the European Community has negotiated interim agreements with each country. Those agreements will bring into early effect the trade provisions of the wider PCAs, and thus help to facilitate trade and promote prosperity.

What the agreements mean in practice is as follows. They aim to intensify political and economic links between the EU and each of the four countries. That will be achieved through regular political contacts, increased trading opportunities and wide-ranging economic co-operation. I shall highlight a few of their most important features.

First, the agreements envisage regular political dialogue by setting up a framework for regular consultations. That means that there will be a forum for high-level political contacts where anything of concern to the parties can be freely discussed. The agreements also set up co-operation councils in which Ministers will meet once a year to review the agreements and to explore ways of building on them.

JPEG 5 Secondly, the agreements are an important step in building closer trading links. They help to bring the countries concerned closer to the world market economy. In particular, they provide for the mutual abolition of quotas on most goods, for most-favoured nation treatment on tariffs, and for the abolition of discriminatory internal taxes. The agreements are also the first step in a long process of drawing the countries of eastern Europe and central Asia into the international trading system by introducing them to trade rules and disciplines.

Thirdly, the PCAs provide for continued economic co-operation under the Technical Assistance for the Commonwealth of Independent States—TACISprogramme to help entrench real and lasting reform. Fourthly, the agreements help to set up a regulatory framework for trade and investment that, in the long term, will provide a more stable climate in which traders and investors can operate. In particular, they set out terms on which companies can set up in the territory of the other party; and they aim to bring practice into line with the EU' s in important areas such as the protection of intellectual property.

The PCAs are explicitly based on respect for democratic principles and human rights. If one of the countries fails to fulfil its obligations under the agreement, the EU may take appropriate measures, including suspension of the PCA.

Economically, the former Soviet Union countries had very different starting points for their development, and that gap has remained. We have to distinguish between the co-called European former Soviet Union countries—Russia, Ukraine, Belarus and Moldova—on the one hand, and transcaucasian and central Asian countries on the other. That fact is reflected in the agreements; the European group, for instance, has wider agreements that will develop closer political and economic ties. They contain an evolutionary clause opening up the prospect of negotiations in 1998 leading eventually to free trade areas.

During our presidency in the first half of 1998, the UK is likely to launch the review on possible free trade areas with Belarus and Moldova, as well as with Russia and Ukraine. The outcome of the review may not be the same for each of the four countries. A free trade area is, on present trends, a distant prospect, but establishing such a long-term objective provides all those countries with an incentive to undertake the kind of economic reform that we want them to pursue.

Helping Belarus, Moldova, Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan to develop proper, functioning market economies is the best way to support them. The agreements are in their interests—and in ours. They offer a practical example of the European Union serving the purpose that we want it to serve: underpinning European security; encouraging political and economic reform; promoting more open trade; and helping our businesses to explore new markets. Those are the key United Kingdom objectives, and the EU is helping to achieve them.

I should now like to say a few words about the wider political context in which we are addressing the agreements. We are dealing with countries that are trying to come to terms with their newly acquired 6 national and regional identities and with their place in the international order. The context is one of encouraging democratic regimes in eastern Europe and central Asia that will offer us new partners and guard against a return to former dividing lines. So the agreements are a first important step in the process of creating a framework of relations by drawing the four countries into the international economic system and reducing their relative political isolation from the west.

Our objective is to provide a vehicle to draw the countries in question closer to the west and help them to consolidate their political and economic reform. The agreements can be used to influence their behaviour and encourage closer co-operation with the EU. They provide an alternative: it is for the countries concerned to choose between the EU and closer economic integration within the Commonwealth of Independent States, which must, as we have frequently emphasised, be voluntary. It is neither in our interests, nor in those of the countries concerned, for the CIS to be their only option for economic co-operation and development. Indeed, I believe that that would be profoundly dangerous.

Despite slow progress with political and economic reform in Belarus and Moldova, reflected in failure so far to reach a new agreement with the International Monetary Fund, we consider that the best way to influence development in Belarus is to open up avenues of co-operation. The PCAs will help the process along. I shall deal with the other agreements as we come to them.

I believe that it is clearly in our interests that the process of reform in all these countries should succeed. The success of that process does not lie entirely in our hands. Through the agreements, however, the European Union will be making a significant contribution to helping that process along. I therefore ask the Committee to demonstrate its support for the long-term objective by giving its approval to the principle behind the PCAs—in particular, in this instance, the one with Belarus.
