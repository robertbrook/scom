Draft Education (Student Support) Regulations 1999

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Education and Employment (Mr. George Mudie)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Education (Student Support) Regulations 1999.

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the draft Education (Student Loans) (Scotland) Regulations 1999.

Mr. Mudie
: It is with some trepidation that I rise to address the Committee. My predecessor from the Welsh valleys, my hon. Friend the Member for Pontypridd (Dr. Howells), who is now Minister for Competition and Consumer Affairs, introduced similar regulations on 28 July last year, and he was removed from the Department for Education and Employment on the same day. I do not know whether there will be a vote on my performance at the end of our proceedings, but I shall be diligent.

The two sets of regulations will give effect to our proposals for student support for those who will enter higher education in 1999–2000, and for those who have entered, or will enter, in 1998–99. Parallel regulations for Northern Ireland will be made shortly, and they will cover the same ground.

The significant difference between the two sets of regulations is that those for England and Wales cover both grants and loans, whereas those for Scotland cover only loans. Existing regulations made under the same legislation cover the payment of grants in Scotland, and they will remain in force. The two sets of regulations are designed to have the same effect for loans.

The draft Education (Student Support) Regulations were tabled on 4 February and the draft Education (Student Loans) (Scotland) Regulations were tabled on 8 February. They have been considered by the Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments.

There was a lively and interesting debate in Committee last July because the regulations then under consideration dealt with a new policy. I say with some hope that today is not the time to go over that ground again. Over the past six months we have consulted wildly— [Laughter]—wildly, as well as widely—on the technical aspects of implementing the policy set out in the regulations now before us.

The regulations relate to the academic year 1999–2000. They will apply to students who enter higher education during that year and to those who 4 enter during the 1998–99 transitional year. They will not apply to students who enter higher education before 1998–99, or to those who entered in 1998–99 but for whom exceptional arrangements were made. Those students will continue to be subject to the previous arrangements, and we will shortly introduce regulations on mandatory awards and student loans that will govern those arrangements for the coming year.

The Government are keeping their promise to students and their families. We are increasing the resources available to all students for their living costs by 2.5 per cent., in line with forecast inflation. We are honouring our pledge that students' families will not be expected to contribute any more in real terms towards living costs than under the old mandatory awards regime.

The increases apply equally to existing students who continue under the old mandatory awards arrangements and to new students who in 1999–2000 will be eligible for 100 per cent. loans. The same rules for parental contribution will apply to both groups of students.

Students to whom the regulations apply will be eligible to receive a loan towards their living costs and a grant towards the cost of tuition fees. The maximum full-year loan for a student living in London will be £4,480 and the maximum contribution towards tuition fees will be £1,025. Those whose circumstances are exceptional—for example, disabled students and those who have dependants—will continue to be eligible for supplementary allowances in the form of grants.

The regulations will provide for the payment of loans and supplementary grants for living costs, and for grants towards tuition fees. Provision for the repayment of loans will be dealt with in a further set of regulations to be made later this year.

The regulations deal with eligibility, the amount of loan available, grants towards tuition fees and supplementary grants towards living costs. They deal with the application process that students should follow, the financial assessment of the level of support to which students will be entitled, and arrangements for paying support to students in instalments.

A key feature of the regulations for England and Wales is that they bring together for the first time provisions for the payment of all types of higher education student support. We have simplified and aligned the different criteria that applied in the past to student loans and to mandatory awards. The result is a set of arrangements that will be clear and transparent for students and for those who administer the arrangements—local authorities, the Student Loans Company and higher education institutions.

Local authorities will be the first port of call for all students wishing to apply for support. Local education authorities will assess each student's application to determine the amount of support to which the student is entitled. They will notify the student of the result and, at the same time, notify the Student Loans Company, which will make payments to students. Higher education institutions will be responsible for confirming the students' attendance and for handing over the first payment. Our aim is that all students will JPEG 5 receive payment by the first day of term, and thereafter in termly instalments. Students must play their part in the arrangements by meeting the deadlines that we have set for making applications.

Mr. Phil Willis
(Harrogate and Knaresborough)
: The issue may take up less time if the Minister responds to a question straight away. Although the regulations clearly state that there will be penalties for students who fail to submit the information within the set time, there is no obligation on the Student Loans Company or the LEAs to perform. Although the idea seems to be that the Student Loans Company will process cases within one month, the regulations will not oblige it to do so. Is that an omission, or will the Government set out clear statutory requirements for the Student Loans Company and the LEAs?

Mr. Mudie
: The hon. Gentleman mentions penalties for students. No one is anxious to impose such penalties. However, students may impose penalties on themselves if they do not meet the deadlines. The situation will be much easier to assess, to administer and to pay for if the deadlines are met. We will then be able to assess the performance of local authorities, the Student Loans Company and the other relevant institutions. However, if the students do not meet the deadlines, there will be problems.

There has been some excitement surrounding the Student Loans Company during the past year, but— without wishing to be accused of complacency—we think that the record is not bad, with 97 per cent. of cases being dealt with on time. Only one application in 5,000 has had a problem. The regulations will make it easier for all the participants. We are willing to let the new arrangements run, and we hope that with streamlining and clear deadlines, if everyone plays their part, we shall not have problems.

The different legislation and administration arrangements in Scotland mean that the various adjustments to eligibility can be achieved within the terms of the existing grant regulations that—like the regulations for Scotland that we are considering— were made under the Education (Scotland) Act 1980. Similarly, the centralised administrative arrangements that already exist in Scotland will continue.

We have consulted on the policy and administrative procedures reflected in the regulations with key representative organisations, such as the Committee of Vice-Chancellors and Principals, the Local Government Association and the National Union of Students—the latter being a particular favourite of the Opposition. Those organisations have helped us to ensure that the technical aspects of the regulations are right, and we are most grateful for that.

There are some new features, to which I draw the Committee's attention. Our aim is to widen access to higher education, and the regulations contain policy measures to help to achieve that.

My hon. Friend the Member for Harrogate and Knaresborough (Mr. Willis) will be pleased to hear that we have extended loans to students aged under 55 at the start of their course, who intend to enter employment after completing it. That fulfils the pledge 6 that the Secretary of State made last June— [Interruption.] It is nothing to do with my hon. Friend's age.

Mr. Oliver Letwin
(West Dorset)

Mr. Mudie
: In certain circumstances, even the hon. Member for West Dorset (Mr. Letwin) could become my hon. Friend. That is an example of the spirit of inclusion; perhaps it offends the hon. Gentleman.

Mr. Stephen Day
(Cheadle)
: If some Opposition Members are the Minister's hon. Friends, will he advise us whether they should be sitting on the Government Benches?

Mr. Mudie
: We have never objected to hon. Members crossing the Floor. They can do that any time that they wish. We might feel some trepidation about the prospect of certain hon. Members crossing the Floor, but they have not done so yet.

The extension of loans to those under 55 fulfils the pledge that the Secretary of State made last June. It is a welcome extension, and will be of real benefit to those wishing to retrain or to develop new skills at that stage in their lives.

We are also introducing a new allowance of up to £100 per week for students who enter higher education after having been in care, and who pay housing costs in the long vacation. Such students have often found it difficult to find a place to live in the long summer vacations. It is usually impossible for them to stay with their parents or to look to them for help and support. We are determined not to let those few students down as they seek to improve their lives and job prospects through higher education.

We announced last month that from autumn 2000, part-time higher education students on a low income will for the first time be entitled to loans of at least £500 to help with the costs of their courses. We will allocate £30 million to support the new loans from 2000 to 2002. The loans for new and existing part-time students on a low income will help with their course expenses, such as books and equipment.

In addition, £5 million has been allocated for the 1999–2000 academic year to enable fees to be waived for those starting undergraduate courses while in receipt of benefits. Similar sums will be made available for subsequent years. The money will be available to existing as well as new students engaged in part-time undergraduate study towards a first degree, an HNC, an HND or other undergraduate credit-bearing higher education provision funded by the Higher Education Funding Council for England. In that case, "part-time" means at least 50 per cent. of a full-time course.

I would like to make clear to the Committee our position on an issue in the regulations that I know has caused concern. Last week, the National Bureau for Students with Disabilities, which is known as Skill, made representations to us about the effect of the regulations on disabled students over 50. Although we have extended loans to certain students between 50 JPEG 7 and 55, the effect of the regulations is that students who do not qualify for support towards living costs will not in future qualify for disabled students' allowances. I assure the Committee that the Government have heard those concerns sympathetically. We are clear that we wish disabled students to continue to receive support towards costs arising from their disability. We are considering how best to achieve that, and we will explore the options with Skill and announce our plans soon.

With those few remarks, I commend the regulations to the Committee.
