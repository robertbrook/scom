Clause 42
REMAND IN CUSTODY IN ABSENCE OF ACCUSED

Mr. Robert Kilroy-Silk
(Ormskirk)
: I beg to move amendment No. 51, in page 44, line 31, leave out subsection (2) and insert—

" (2) Section 2 of the Imprisonment (Temporary Provisions) Act 1980 is hereby repealed.".

This amendment would repeal section 2 of the Imprisonment (Temporary Provisions) Act 1980 which enables defendants to be remanded in their absence without their consent. First, I support clause 42 which seems to be a sensible way of reducing the unnecessary demands that are currently being made on prison officers' time by the necessity for them 448 to do escort duties, and it does away with those unnecessary demands on their time without restricting the right of any defendant to insist on a personal appearance at a remand hearing if he so chooses. I shall be supporting clause 42.

In contrast, however, section 2 of the Imprisonment (Temporary Provisions) Act provides for defendants to be remanded in their absence without the safeguard of consent by the defendant which is rightfully contained in clause 42. The 1980 Act also contains no maximum time limit between court appearances. Under its provisions, a defendant could be remanded in custody without a personal appearance at a remand hearing for an indefinite period and against his will, nor does the Act require such a defendant to be legally represented.

It is true that during the prison officers' dispute the Home Secretary asked the courts to ensure that legal representation was available for defendants. He made that plea after pressure had been put upon him during the passage of the Bill through Parliament. It appears that legal representation was offered to defendants in most cases during the course of that dispute and the operation of the Imprisonment (Temporary Provisions) Act.

According to a sample survey carried out by the Home Office in one week in December, about 7 per cent, of defendants remanded in custody in their absence in that period were unrepresented. Clearly, although it is a small number, even 7 unrepresented defendants out of 100 who are remanded in custody in their absence is seven too many, and I am sure that the Minister would accept that.

The right to personal appearance in court to present one's case for bail is obviously very important in terms of principle, and it is certainly crucial in practical terms. Apart from the fact that remand prisoners are often held in the most appalling conditions in the local prisons, a defendant who is remanded in custody may lose his earnings, his job, have difficulty with his accommodation all as a result of being refused bail. His family may suffer financially as well as emotionally from his imprisonment. He is placed at a severe disadvantage in preparing his case for trial in terms of tracing witnesses, collecting evidence in mitigation and pressing his solicitor to give sufficient attention to his case. For those and other reasons a personal appearance should only be waived by a defendant who, after considering all the facts and taking legal advice, consents to forego his fundamental right to a personal appearance in court.

In the light, therefore, of what I regard as the sensible provisions in clause 42, which I support, I cannot see any reason for the retention in legislation of section 2 of the Imprisonment (Temporary Provisions) Act, which I regarded as offensive and indefensible at the time and which I find no more attractive at the moment. Given the principles and the practice which will be laid down in clause 42, there seems to me to be no reason at all for the retention of this section of the Act on the statute book. I hope, therefore, that the Minister will feel able to give a clear assurance that if he is not able JPEG 449 now to accept this amendment he will return to it at another stage in our proceedings.

Mr. Arthur Davidson
: I strongly oppose clause 42 and schedule 8, of which it is part. The requirement for a prisoner waiting trial to be present in court every eight days before he is further remanded has been an important safeguard against injustice and unfair imprisonment. It has protected the prisoner held on remand in custody over the years, and it continues to protect him well.

There are several reasons why it has proved to be an effective safeguard. The necessity for the accused to make a regular appearance in court every eight days is important for several reasons. First it enables the accused to discuss his case with his solicitor, or with his counsel. That is clearly of great significance and help to the accused, if his case is to be properly prepared, as it ought to be.

If a bail application is to be successfully made, it is important that the accused should be in court to see his solicitor, to give him proper instructions, and posibly to acquaint his legal adviser, whether it be solicitor or counsel, about any fresh circumstances that may have arisen during the eight days previously. It also does something else. By his constant, regular appearances in court, it puts pressure on the prosecution to prepare the case properly and speedily and not to indulge in lethargy and undue delay. I am not saying deliberately, but it frequently does happen, to the disadvantage of the prisoner and the accused. It also puts pressure on the defence to prepare its case quickly, to put its arguments in court more quickly than it might have done. That is plainly to the advantage of the accused. Incidentally, if he is eventually acquitted, or given a non-custodial sentence, it eases the strain on the prison system, particularly in the case of prisons where remand prisoners are held.

It also enables the accused to see for himself what is actually happening to his case—and why should he not know what is actually happening? His future is at stake. It is his case that is to be discussed. It is a decision about whether he is to be remanded, or whether he is to be given bail. It is important that he should not be excluded from those arguments, that at least he should see for himself how far his case has progressed, and if it has not progressed, at least be able to ask his solicitor why it has not progressed. That is an added reason why he ought to be in court regularly.

It should be evident to the Committee—I am sure I am not telling hon. Members anything of which they are not aware—that the defendant remanded in custody is at an obvious disadvantage over the preparation of his case. He cannot contact witnesses himself. It is difficult—in some cases impossible—for him to telephone his solicitor, and so on. It is, then, vital to him that he should have that opportunity of seeing his solicitor every eight days, to consult with him and to help prepare his case.

In fact, solicitors rarely go to prisons to see their clients. I am not saying that they do not go, but they do not go frequently. That is the view of the Law Society, which is strongly opposed to the provisions of clause 42 and schedule 8, and wants to see the present eight-day remand system retained, as does the Legal Action Group. The Howard 452 League also has great doubts as to the provisions which the Government propose to enact by clause 42.

This important safeguard which I have described, that has served our justice system very well over the years, and has prevented countless people from being held in custody when it is unnecessary, is now under threat. It is under threat not for any logical reason—not because the system is not working—but because the production of prisoners at eight-day intervals, as the Home Secretary says, places an unnecessary burden on the police and the prison service.

I understand that that is so, and I understand that about 7 per cent, of the prison manpower is used in escorting prisoners to and from court for remand applications. That is a regrettable fact, but it is not in my submission a reason for doing away with the eight-day system. Half of those who are remanded in custody are eventually found not guilty or are given non-custodial sentences. This means that half of those people who are remanded in custody should not be so remanded, since they are eventually found not guilty or are given non-custodial sentences.

I accept that the Government have gone some way to limit their original proposals and that the prisoner is now not to be remanded in his absence unless he consents to that remand. That is all very well, but unfortunately there will be many prisoners who will be disadvantaged by the results of that proposal.

The Law Society has found that in many cases a major factor in the decision as to whether or not the prisoner refuses or consents to the relaxation of the eight-day remand will be the attitude of the court. There will be courts which will expect defendants to agree to remands in their absence, while others may remain genuinely neutral or may even discourage defendants from agreeing to have their case remanded in their absence.

The Law Society also found that the attitude of individual solicitors is likely to vary considerably. Some solicitors, perhaps not for the best reasons, will be happy that remands should take place in their clients' absence. Others, for perhaps better reasons, will take the view that their clients should always be present on a remand hearing. There will be a difference in attitude between those accused who are subject to the provision.

There will be defendants who are knowledgeable and experienced, those who are more literate and articulate, perhaps defendants who are more used to court procedure—because they may have experienced it before—who will insist on their right to be brought to court for every remand hearing. There will be others who are less articulate, less literate, less used to court procedure, less used to dealing with solicitors, less aware of the consequences of what might happen if they are not represented, who will more passively accept the advice offered by their solicitors or by the court and who will be more easily dissuaded, particularly if the court takes a strong view about the need for the accused not to be in court. Such accused might JPEG 453 well, therefore, forego their right to be represented, be remanded in their absence and might well suffer as a result. The tragic effect is likely to be that the defendants who are in most need of the protection of a regular appearance before the courts will decline to consent and, on the other side of the fence, those who do not require a regular appearance, because there is very little likelihood of them actually being granted bail, will give their consent.

These are that the person to be remanded must be present in the court, that he must be aged 17 or over, and that he must be legally represented. Those are three important safeguards. These give a magistrates' court the power to remand a person in custody in his absence, subject to certain conditions being fulfilled. The next stage is that the court is required, after making the required explanations, to ask the person before it whether he consents to the hearing and determination of applications for remands in his absence. Now we come to the substantive requirements.

The court is required—where it proposes further to remand a person who has previously been remanded in custody without that person being brought before a court—to be satisfied that certain conditions are fulfilled. It is also possible, however, that a person who had first declined to give his consent at his last remand appearance, might subsequently change his mind while in prison. It is envisaged that in such circumstances, the person would write direct to the court indicating that he has consented to the next hearing taking place in his absence, or he might request his legal representative to inform the court, either before or at the hearing.

The second condition is that the person has not been remanded in his absence on more than two occasions immediately preceding the hearing in question, because the maximum is 28 days. The effect of this condition is to ensure that a person who is being consistently remanded in custody over a period of time is produced in court at least every fourth hearing; that at the time of giving his consent the person was aged 17 or over; and, lastly, the court must be satisfied that since agreeing to the remand hearings taking place in his absence that person has not withdrawn his consent.
