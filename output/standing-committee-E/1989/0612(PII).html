Clause 62
TRANSFER OF UNITED KINGDOM BRANCH OR AGENCY

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Chris Smith
: The overall effect of clause 62 is to allow an overseas company that carried on a trade in the United Kingdom through a branch or agency to incorporate that branch or agency into a fully-fledged United Kingdom company, equally liable to United Kingdom tax, on a tax-free basis. The act of incorporation would not incur additional tax simply by virtue of taking place.

The clause seems to us to be relatively unexceptionable but there is one point on which we should like guidance: An overseas company might wish to incorporate its branch or subsidiary here to sell a United Kingdom overseas branch as a United Kingdom company. There would be no tax charge on the sale of the shares in the United Kingdom subsidiary, so allowing tax-free incorporation. This seems to us to allow United Kingdom tax to be deferred or avoided in certain cases.

It is posssible that section 278 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1970 would come into operation to cover such case, but the position is not clear, and we are not sure whether that section comes into play and would, in effect, crystallise the deferred capital capital gains charge. If the relief under clause 62 is claimed, and soon after that the company is sold to a third party, will the gains rolled over under clause 62 be crystallised when that company is sold? The provisions are not clear. Does section 278 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1970 apply, and will the crystallisation take place?

We would welcome assurance from the Government on that question, but we are happy to accept the broad principle of the clause.

Mr. Ryder
: I thank the hon. Member for Islington, South and Finsbury (Mr. Smith) for giving me notice of his question. I understand that section 278 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1970, to which he referred, imposes charges on certain deferred gains when a company leaves a group, as the hon. Gentleman described. The answer to his question is no, the 208 section will not apply to gains deferred under the clause. There is no reason for it to do so, as the clause will not itself create extra scope for avoidance; of that the Inland Revenue is certain.

Mr. Smith
: I hear what the Economic Secretary says, but the potential for avoidance remains if, using the relief from taxation provided by clause 62, a branch is transmuted into a company to facilitate an onward sale, so relieving the selling company from tax. I am grateful for the Economic Secretary's clarification of the non-applicability of section 278 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1970, but his answer leaves me somewhat apprehensive. Had that section applied, crystallising provisions would have been in place. As we now know that that is not so, we must watch what happens as a result of clause 62 very carefully indeed. We are worried that there is the potential scope for a bit of sharp practice by companies deliberately using the provisions to transmute a branch into a company. That possibility is something that we are keen to avoid.

The Government appear to believe their own assurance that there is no problem here. We shall watch very carefully to ascertain whether that is true. We suspect that it may not be.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 62 ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Clause 63
RELIEF FOR INTEREST

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Beith
: Clause 63, which deals with relief for interest, reveals one of the Chancellor's little victories over the Prime Minister. It shows how the Government do not want to see a steady increase in the threshold for mortgage interest relief. They regard such relief as a limited and perhaps declining part of the housing subsidy system.

Mortgage interest tax relief is a housing subsidy, with a stated objective of helping to promote home ownership. There is a strong case for making it part of a wider system of support that would assist householders who rent as well as those who buy, yet no such social argument can be made for continuing to pay mortgage interest relief at the higher rate of tax. We have sought, in successive Budgets and Finance Bill debates, to make the Government recognise that there is no logic in giving greater help to the richest people, who pay the highest rate of tax, to buy their houses than is given to those on lower incomes. The logical thing is to confine the relief to the standard rate of tax.

It will be interesting to hear, in the context of the decision to include this clause in the Bill, what is the Government's current thinking on mortgage interest relief. If they had limited it to the standard rate of tax last year, they would have saved £440 million. That is a handy sum, for which I am sure the Government could have found good uses. Moreover, they would have ended a biased form of subsidy whose assistance appears to be tilted towards those who are least in need JPEG 209 of it. The decision not to index the threshold to the standard rate of tax shows that the Government are to some extent worried about the system's operation, although I still cannot understand why their anxiety does not extend to the relief at the high rate of tax.

If the Government had increased the threshold this year, it would not have helped home buyers. It would have made things more difficult for first-time buyers and had strong repercussions in a housing market that has had enough repercussions to last it a long time. I agree with the Government's decision to keep the level as it is.

Let us examine the fine details of the amendment. The clause will do away with a specific restriction on the impact of section 50 of the Finance Act 1988, which JPEG 219 enabled BES to cover investment in companies that let residential accommodation on assured tenancies. In addition to imposing certain limitations on the value of those premises, it dealt also with a property that was done up or what happens if the locality in which it was situated became more desirable. Mr. McCartney

Mr. Boateng

Presumably, with such a development in mind, it was considered necesssary to make a proviso about the increased desirability of property.

Under the clause, that proviso will not be taken into account. If, as a result of an area becoming more desirable, the limits on the value of a property are exceeded, that will not cause the specific investment to cease to qualify for BES. Such a provision is an undesirable attempt to beathe new life into an undesirable and unhelpful proposal. We would scrap it; it should not be misued in that way.

It is necessary to consider how BES might be used for its genuine and original purpose, which is to encourage new investment and the taking of risks in legitimate enterprises which benefit the nation. That is a proper use of schemes such as the business expansion scheme, but it is not right to use BES to speculate in the residential property market or to provide a convenient tax shelter for the better off. That is an abuse of the system. We have consistently opposed that and will, in due course, remove it.

The BES is now manifestly failing to fulfil its purpose and I wish that the Government would take seriously the fact that it is falling far short of that purpose.
