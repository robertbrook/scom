Clause 1
SOLICITING FOR THE PURPOSE OF PROSTITUTION

Mr. Stuart Randall
(Kingston upon Hull, West)
: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 7, at beginning insert—

"(a) In subsection (1) of section 1 (offence of soliciting a woman or different women for the purpose of prostitution from a motor vehicle, persistently or so as to cause annoyance or nuisance), for the words "man" and "woman" in the first place where each occurs, there shall be substituted the word "person".".

The Chairman
: With this we may take amendment No. 2, in page 1, line 7, leave out from "1" to "the" in line 9.

Mr. Randall
: The amendments would introduce flexibility into the legislation. We support the thrust of the clause, but want it to state that a "person" commits an offence when soliciting a "person" for "the purpose of prostitution". I hope that hon. Members have copies of the Sexual Offences Act 1985, otherwise the argument will be tricky to follow. I notice that the hon. Member for Walthamstow (Mr. Summerson) does not have a copy. That Act states:

"A man commits an offence if he solicits a woman (or different women) for the purpose of prostitution."

We are convinced that the Committee will support this serious, unisex amendment.

We must consider the changing recognition of the age of consent for homosexual and lesbian behaviour. Although the Minister will refute this, there are strong pressures to lower the age of consent in line with the age of consent for heterosexuals. I have reservations about that, but pressure groups argue that homosexuals and lesbians can have the same love relationships that heterosexuals have. Although it will take time, liberalisation is inevitable.

Libertarians and others believe that in a free society people should have the opportunity to choose their relationships. Current sterotypes are changing. I am a greate believer in marriage; I dislike the recent trend of the break-up of family life. I am not in favour of loose relationships or people living together before marriage. I am rather old-fashioned.

(Streatham)

Members for Norwood (Mr. Fraser) and for Vauxhall (Miss Hoey). Many of the problems tackled by the Bill are encountered in Lambeth.

I entirely understand the point of the amendments and I sympathise with some of what was said by the hon. Member for Kingston upon Hull, West. At first glance, it seems right to enlarge the scope of the Bill to include those men who from a car solicit youths walking along the pavement or prostitutes who cruise in their cars and solicit men. To my knowledge, that has not happened in Streatham, but it might. Legislation should exist to apprehend and bring those people to trial. However, the changes are unnecessary because those matters are covered by existing legislation.

In the case of a woman soliciting from a car, section 1 of the Street Offences Act 1959 makes it an offence to

"loiter or solicit in a street or public place for the purpose of prostitution."

A kerb-crawling prostitute would, therefore, be liable to be stopped, questioned and prosecuted. A prostitute does not have to solicit persistently on foot or in a car to be prosecuted. That point is well covered.

Section 32 of the Sexual Offences Act 1956 deals with soliciting by males for homosexual purposes. A male soliciting in a car or on foot faces a maximum of two years' imprisonment on indictment, or on summary conviction up to six months' imprisonment and/or a maximum fine of £2,000. Those offences, rightly, already appear on the statue book. Homosexual soliciting attacts a substantial penalty.

The Bill proposes to increase the maximum penalty for kerb crawling from £400 to £1,000. The average penalty is about £100. With a bit of luck, if the maximum is raised, the average will rise to £300 or JPEG 7 £400. The Bill proposes that the maximum penalty for kerb crawling should be £1,000, but the maximum penalty for homosexual kerb crawling is already a custodial sentence and a £2,000 fine. Therefore, an insuperable difficulty arises unless another amendment is tabled to change the maximum penalty for kerb crawling, which would probably mean returning to the Criminal Law Revision Committee for its views and opinion.

The amendment is unnecessary because the relevant legislation already exists. It would also make nonsense of the maximum penalty of £1,000 in the Bill, in the light of the maximum penalty of £2,000 and custodial sentence mat already exist. I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman for raising this point because it has given us the opportunity to remind potential lady kerb crawlers or homosexual kerb crawlers that far greater penalties, than we suggest in the Bill, already exist.

I hope that the hon. Gentleman will seek leave to withdraw his amendment.

Mr. Randall

Mr. Fraser
: The offence of kerb crawling in the 1985 Act can be tried only summarily. People accused of kerb crawling can be tried only by magistrates and cannot no matter how great the potential damage to their reputation or living, put their case before a jury as they would with an offence of dishonesty. It is important to bear that in mind.

I shall illustrate my argument with two cases—one real and the other hypothetical. The first involves a judge who was one of the first people to be convicted under the Sexual Offences Act 1985. He went before the magistrates and was convicted of persistently soliciting prostitutes in the west end. He was probably a good judge. He went to appeal and his case was reheard in the Crown court. However, rehearing appeals in magistrates courts does not involve a jury. Appeals are usually heard by a Crown court judge sitting with two justices. The judge's reputation would have been irreparably damaged by a conviction for 8 persistent importuning of prostitutes. He was found, on appeal, to be not guilty of the offence. I hope that that example illustrates the high risks to a person's reputation when he faces such charges as soliciting prostitutes.

We should also not forget that even when appeals are granted, they are not always successful. We know that in the case of the Guildford Four. Their case went to the Court of Appeal but was unsuccessful. There may be a risk of encountering a judge like the one described by W.S. Gilbert in "Trial by Jury":

"Though all my law is fudge,


Yet I'll never, never budge,


But I'll live and die a judge."

It is a big problem if a person charged with an offence encounters such a judge when appealing against conviction. That is unknown, even in very serious cases.

I turn now to my hypothetical example. Let us consider the Road Traffic Regulation Act 1984, which states that in cases of speeding the opinion of one police officer or witness alone is insufficient for conviction. There must be corroboration from a second police officer or witness, or a reading from a measured speedometer. Because the Bill removes the word "persistently" or the need to prove any nuisance or annoyance, it risks making the weight of evidence necessary too light. Conviction should not take place without corroboration unless there is persistence or nuisance and annoyance. The amendment leaves existing law entirely undisturbed. It would require corroboration only because of the risk to a person's reputation.

My hypothetical case involves the hon. Member for Streatham (Sir W. Shelton) facing difficulties in the next general election. He finds that proposals for the south circular road and the poll tax make his electoral chances slim. He pleads with Conservative central office for help and receives the support and assistance of his hon. Friends the Members for Birmingham, Selly Oak (Mr. Beaumont-Dark), for Twickenham (Mr. Jessel) and for Luton, South (Mr. Bright). [Laughter.] Roads in south London have been partly closed by Lambeth council. Hampton court maze is a doddle compared with some of our back streets.

Let us imagine that the hon. Member for Luton, South finds himself cruising round the back streets searching for the roads where he is supposed to canvass electoral support. He is looking for Ockley Road, but cannot find it. He looks for somebody to ask directions, but can see only an attractive lady on the corner of the street who is beckoning towards him.
