Mr. Cook: It might be convenient for members of the Committee, and relieve their feelings after the awkward period of bi-partisanship before dinner if we descended into a period of grubby party-political divisions. I hope that I shall fulfil that cathartic role in the next 10 minutes.

Amendment No. 758 seeks to delete subsections (4) and (5) which extend the thrust of the privatisation arrangements of the clause to non-residential care. So far we have debated only how the use of private agencies and the voluntary sector applies to residential care. These subsections apply to domiciliary care.

As someone who once was a councillor on a social services authority, I come to this debate with a strong sense of pride in what I and many other Labour councillors and councils have been able to do for the people whom we represented by providing proper and decent standards of domiciliary care for those in need. Indeed, as a member of the Opposition, I take unashamed delight in the fact that every one of the 20 councils at the top of the league table for the provision of home helps is in majority Labour control. I do not feign any sense of either surprise or regret that my party is so well represented. The Minister for Health must be familiar with those figures, because her consitituency is in the county that, in the whole of England, has the lowest provision of home helps for elderly people. Doubtless the people of Surrey have intimate family ties and extended links of family kinship. Nevertheless, it is difficult to credit that Surrey can get by with half the home help provision of Croydon let alone one fifth that of Wigan.

Col. 832 That brings me, unfortunately to the White Paper on community care to which the Minister was party, possibly informed by her experiences in Surrey.

Mrs. Virginia Bottomley: My experience also includes Peckham and Brixton.

Mr. Cook: I regret that the White Paper is not adequately informed by the Minister's experiences in Peckham and Brixton. I recognise Surrey in the White Paper, but not Peckham or Brixton. One reason is that one of the most startlingly new and unexpected elements of the White Paper is the proposal to involve the private sector in domiciliary care. The original drafts of the White Paper, which were helpfully provided to the Sunday papers 10 days before publication, suggested stronger language than we found in the printed version. Nevertheless the authorised version is sufficiently alarming. On page 23 it states:

"The Government expects social services authorities to explore ways of further stimulating private and voluntary provision for domiciliary, day and respite care wherever possible … Social services authorities will be expected to make clear in their community care plans what steps they will be taking to make increased use of nonstatutory service providers, or, where such providers are not currently available, how they propose to stimulate such activity. In particular, they should consider how they will encourage diversification in the non-residential care sector."

Where did the idea of privatising non-residential care come from? It did not come from the Griffiths report, on which the White Paper is allegedly based. There is no hint in the Griffiths report of involving the private or the non-profit making voluntary sectors in a wider range of non-residential care. Nor did the idea of such involvement come from the voluntary sector, on which Ministers have relied heavily when interpreting those paragraphs. Statements from the voluntary sector since the publication of the White Paper have expressed pained surprise and anxiety. A briefing from the Spastics Society, for instance, states:

"We cannot and will not be able to take on all those aspects of local authority services relating to people with cerebral palsy. We are deeply concerned about any assumptions that the public provision of care for disabled people can be in the main shifted to the private and not for profit sector without devastating consequences for disabled people."

The director of the National Council for Voluntary Organisations, speaking on behalf of a wide range of organisations which, apparently, are expected to step in the breach and take over from local authorities, said:

"Pluralism in care does not—and cannot—mean wholesale voluntarisation of mainstream services".

The director further stressed the need to safeguard the unique, valuable role of the voluntary sector in providing,

"advocacy, self-help, community development and innovation",

all of which are inevitably in conflict with substituting for local authorities in mainstream social service provision.

The idea of privatising non-residential care did not spring from public demand, either. I am aware from earlier debates that Conservative Members do not care to be reminded of public opinion about these issues. However, a poll carried out by, I think. Care revealed Image Col. 833 that 83 per cent were opposed to the privatisation of the social service functions of local authorities.

The proposal for stimulating the private, not-for-profit voluntary sector certainly did not come from the local authorities. The obligations imposed on them by the Government have given them wide experience of working with private contractors, and the idea of handing domiciliary care to private contractors has left local authorities in a state of cataleptic paralysis. On clear, consistent, fact which has emerged from that experience is that private contractors mean high staff turnover. Some private contractors have gone through all of their staff in one year—some have gone through even more. The prize is held by a contractor who, in one year, went through his entire staff four times over. Such rapid staff turnover is not adventitious but is an inevitable result of the staff conditions that private contractors rely on. Not only do they rely on casual, part-time staff; they seek them out, so that they can avoid paying national insurance contributions. They do not offer their staff accrued holiday pay, private superannuation deals, or any other conditions or terms that would encourage staff to stay for more than the minimum period. They do not have intensive training because, as they expect high staff turnover, it would be a lost investment. The application of that labour culture to domiciliary care alarms me. One reason why home helps are valued by the people whom they serve is their familiarity. People want home helps whom they know and equally they want home helps who know them and who understand their background, emotional difficulties and family circumstances.

Mrs. Mahon: Indeed.

Mr. Cook: My hon. Friend the Member for Halifax (Mrs. Mahon) anticipates my argument. People need home helps whom they can trust. We are talking about helpers who enter their homes on a regular basis. They can see any valuables, or any antiques that there may be in that home. They have the keys to homes. Frequently, they are asked to collect pensions from post offices, against the explicit instructions of local authorities. Flexibility is important as a home help's work may vary from day to day and client to client. I have seen specifications given to private contractors by local and health authorities where every minute is measured and every task is billed and pinned down. An intimate personal social service such as the home help service cannot be reduced to narrow and detailed specifications, just as family obligations cannot be reduced to work studies.

Another reason why the private contractural approach does not fit the home help service is that all experience of domiciliary care shows that it works best when it is managed on a small scale, according to the needs of individual clients and in consultation with the local social services team. There is no evidence that the private sector is interested in that intensive small-scale management. Therefore, the Government would find it easier to privatise nuclear power generation than the home help service.
