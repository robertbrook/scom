Mr. Cook
: On Tuesday, we debated the creation of self-governing trust; all the amendments are about the protection of the rights of staff during that process. I have noticed, on leafing through various papers that 440 have been prepared by health authorities contemplating self-governing trusts, that staff appear to have been replaced by "human resources". Whether we call them staff or human resources, we are dealing with an important issue. The staff make the hospitals work, and they will have to continue to provide patient care whatever arrangements we make in Committee.

It is tragic that we begin by debating the rights of staff at the point of transfer, but we established last Tuesday that the staffs right to be consulted about the creation of self-governing trusts was to be set aside by the Government. Self-governing trusts will be created without staff being consulted, and even in the face of opposition — in the ballot to which I referred on Tuesday, 42 were in favour and 1,384 were against. Staff will be transferred to self-governing trusts under clause 5, irrespective of their wishes or their views on their patients' interests. That reasonably leads us to clause 6, which deals with the rights of protection of staff.

Clause 6 provides protection for staff only at the point of transfer, when they will work under the terms and conditions that applied the day before. It does not provide any protection for staff from the second day of their transfer. That is worrying staff in Health Service hospitals. Their worry is fuelled by what they have seen happen over the past six years, when the services and functions of hospitals have been handed over to private contractors. I avoid the word "privatisation" because I know that the Under Secretary of State for Scotland will object by saying that transferring functions and services to private contractors is not privatisation. However, that process has a dramatic effect on the pay and conditions of the staff who are handed over.

Private contractors have invariably been responsible for, lower pay, fewer superannuation rights, less holiday time and less time at work. The aim of private contractors in personnel management—they would call it human resource management—is to make full-time staff part time so that the employers can evade paying national insurance contributions, while the staff are left without national insurance rights. The profits of private contractors have, in the main, been achieved at the expense of the staff. By employing badly paid, part-time, casual staff without proper training, private contractors have generated an immense staff turnover. There is a recorded case of a private contractor having achieved staff turnover of 400 per cent. within one year. Such rapid turnover disrupts attempts to create good working teams onwards in the interests of patient care and means that staff have no opportunity to gain experience or develop knowledge of the work expected of them.

Staff anxiety provides the background of my argument. But there is a more pertinent reason why staff should be nervous at the prospect of self-governing trusts. Documents which occasionally leach out of the fifth floors of RHA offices show that in the regions, and no doubt in the districts also, people are already thinking about how self-governing trusts might use their freedom after independence from the NHS. A document entitled, "Let's put Patients Before Profits", has been produced, for internal use only, by the Trent RHA. Had it claimed JPEG 441 to put staff before profits, it would have been entirely wrong. In that respect, at least, it has been honestly named. But it might more appropriately have been named, "Let's Put Profits Before Staff'. Throughout the document, staff management is referred to as "human resource management".

I shall share with the Committee four sections of the document, to show the new attitudes of the Trent RHA towards its human resources. Page 9 offers advice to managers contemplating the creation of a self-governing trust. Management and Staff are regarded not as part of a joint venture but as being in opposition. Human resources are to be exploited and manipulated by tactics, as the following quote will show:

"It may be an appropriate tactic from the first day of the establishment of the Self-Governing Trust not to confer recognition on any organisation in order that the Self-Governing Trust can pick the appropriate time and opportunity to begin to enter into discussions for recognition of collective bargaining purposes."

The document mentions picking an appropriate time, not to recognise collective bargaining purposes but to begin to enter into discussions on the recognition of trade unions. The logical corollary of that statement is that there will be no accepted means of collective bargaining. On page 10, the document continues:

"The Self Governing Trust will have a choice. It may choose not to enter into collective bargaining arrangements. It may choose to continue to adopt the Whitley Pay Strategy or alternatively it may seek to impose pay deals."

I ask my hon. Friends to note those final words. The document continues:

"The Trust will also need to have a clear view of its pay strategy and whether it chooses to move totally to individual remuneration packages and away from collective agreements."

It contains no assurances on trade union recognition or on collective bargaining agreements, even with organisations such as the Association of Professional Ambulance Personnel or any staff union that the self-governing trust may create. There is not even a minimal assurance that there will be a collective organisation of any sort, to say nothing of a recognised and registered trade union.

The document hints at what the changes might imply for staff pay.

For instance, a paragraph on page 13, headed

"Abandoning Whitley in respect of a particular Staff Group"

makes a distinction between staff for whom there is competition in the labour market and staff for whom there is not. The doom-laden words written about those for whom there is not competition are:

"Where the issue is one of lack of competitiveness, buying out the staff group may not be a viable option as this would merely increase costs when the defined problem was that costs were already too high."

I invite my hon. Friends to peer through those elliptical phrases.

When the management of the Trent RHA states that the defined problem is that costs are already too high, it is effectively saying that the staff are being paid too much. As I go around hospitals not many staff press me on that problem; the converse is the defined problem for them.

442 The solution to the problem, as defined by Trent RHA, is:

"The options here appear to be ... to 'red circle' the existing work force".

I am not sure whether the term red-circling has as yet entered the medical dictionary. I am not familiar with the process, but I can infer what it means from

"and offer different rates to new starters".

In other words, the RHA proposes a two-tier system of wage settlements in its hospitals. There will be those who were taken on under existing pay and conditions, as clause 6 does not give self-governing trusts the opportunity to dishonour existing pay and condition agreements. Those staff will find that their pay and conditions, which clause 6 purports to protect, are further protected by red-circling. Therefore staff within the ambit of clause 6 will not be able to improve on their pay and conditions, or even achieve annual rises.

Looking at the present labour market in the NHS, I believe that the new fractured labour market will have two consequences. First, the trend will be that wages and conditions at the bottom of that labour market will be depressed, as unskilled personnel find themselves exploited. I have dwelt on that point and it is unmistakeably in the minds of managers.

There may be a simultaneous trend at the top end of the labour market, when doctors and nurses with high qualifications for the first time find that there is JPEG 445 competition for their labour. One advantage to the public purse of the present NHS is that it is a monopoly employer and can therefore exploit that strength when it negotiates with people who have skills to sell. Doctors in Britain are not well-paid compared with doctors in the rest of Europe, which partly reflects the way in which the present bargaining arrangement strengthens the hand of the public purse against the professsion.

The second issue of public concern is what the cost of the new arrangements will be. Conservative Ministers are much given to criticising the present system of settling pay in the Health Service as cumbersome and remote, and there are undeniably problems with that remoteness, but the system is centralised and comparatively cheap. If all major hospitals have their own department of human resource management, it will add to their considerable additional expenditure and, perhaps more significantly, create a major diversion of management time into personnel management, staff negotiation and settling of conditions, activities which at present do not distract them from the proper provision of patient care to their local community.
