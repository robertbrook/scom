Mr. Michael
: The amendments are important because they seek to protect the doctor's right to prescribe in the patient's best interest. The traditional confidence of patients in doctors is in danger of being undermined unless the amendments are accepted. That confidence is the basis of our Health Service. The Opposition find it incredible that the Government are prepared to put it at risk.

The crisis of the moment involves the ambulance service, which is vital in time of emergency or sudden illness. The Government's reluctance to negotiate is straining the loyalty of ambulance workers unbelievably.

There have been many other examples of the Government's unwillingness to listen to those who deal with our health day in and day out. The worst has been the unseemly wrangle between the Secretary of State for Health—I regret that he is not taking part in today's debate, as his personal opinions are of such importance —and doctors who have sought to safeguard the 612 doctor-patient relationship. The differences in the statements about meetings on that dispute cast serious doubts on the Government's intentions. The amendments provide the Secretary of State and the other Ministers with a chance to come clean.

In many statements, the Secretary of State has claimed to have met doctor's demands, but the BMA has made it clear that serious doubts remain. Even if the doctors were satisfied by the Minister's undertakings, that would not be enough; my plea is on behalf not of doctors, but of patients. If the Minister for Health is serious in her wish to reassure doctors and the public, she will accept the amendments. If she does not accept them, she will cast further serious doubt on the Government's intentions.

Indicative drug budgets will impose a financial restraint upon drug prescriptions. The Minister has claimed that they will not. The Secretary of State has claimed that the Government's management proposals will not infringe a GP's right to prescribe all the medicines that individual patients require, but he has refused to clarify that unequivocally for those who are suspicious of his intentions.

A doctor might still have the right to prescribe the drugs that a patient requires, but he will have to balance the needs of various patients in order to stay within his budget. What does the right of which Ministers have spoken really mean? There will be clear limits on budgets at family practitioner level. How will they be kept to without limiting individual doctors? There will be clear limits at area health authority level. How will they be kept to without limiting individual doctors?

There have not been satisfactory answers to these questions. In Wales, there is no RHA to remonstrate with the Secretary of State for Wales if problems arise—not that remonstrating with the right hon. Gentleman always produces results. It produces courtesy, but not always results. The Secretary of State for Wales is therefore judge, jury and treasurer in his own court. That is not satisfactory. There has been no response to the anxieties expressed by doctors in Wales.

The Government are restating a right for GPs but hedging that around with administrative and budgetary restrictions, thus putting doctors in a double bind. Ministers seem to think that they can persuade the BMA to accept vague promises about how the Secretary of State for Health will implement financial management arrangements that have not yet been published. There is doubt about that, but even if they did, there would still be no basis for confidence among members of the public, patients and doctors who are suspicious of his intentions. If the Secretary of State means what he says, he and the Minister for Health can prove it by accepting three of the amendments before the Committee.

Amendment No. 13 would ensure that there were no financial or other sanctions against a practice that exceeded its drug budget. Amendment No. 15 would ensure that a doctor did not lose pay for exceeding his or her indicative drug budget. Amendment No. 14 would ensure that a doctor did not have to apply to a family practitioner committee, as it is now called, before exceeding the budget.

Within the cash-limited, money-dominated ethos that the Government are creating in the Health Service there will be plenty of encouragement to save money when that can be done without hurting patient care. Most doctors are enthusiastic about cutting waste in medical practice, when JPEG 613 that does not damage patient care. Patients and the public understand the need for financial responsibility. All doctors support positive developments such as medical audit undertaken by their peers. That means judgment on prescribing being made by doctors, not by administrators and accountants. We cannot accept the Government's diktat on a topic on which they have produced no real evidence. Prescribing in the United Kingdom is low compared with that in many other countries. Our doctors are not profligate.

The Government have not explored the alternative of management by reason and consensus. Indeed, one sometimes wonders whether they understand the words. The Minister has a simple choice: the hon. Lady can accept the amendments and be believed, or reject them and confirm the worst fears of doctors, patients, consumer groups, the media and the Opposition that the Government are intent on pursuing a piece of damaging ideological nonsense without regard for evidence or common sense.

I had hoped that the Secretary of State would be here to amplify what he said on 10 October:

"The firm budgets for drugs at RHA and FPC level will not operate as cash limits. The management arrangements proposed by the Government will not infringe in any way the right of a general practitioner to prescribe all those medicines which individual patients might need. These points will of course be carried through into our legislative proposals to Parliament."

In order to do that, the Minister must accept our amendments. She will be breaking a promise made by the Secretary of State if she does not.

There have been accusations that the BMA misunderstood the Government on the drugs budget. The Government say that they have no intention of depriving patients of the drugs that they need. But the wording in working paper 4, section 9 is clear:

"Legislation will be needed in order to require FPCs and RHAs to keep to their drug budgets".

That can only mean a cash limit. Working paper 4 states that an FPC might encourage the local development of repeat prescribing control systems and formularies. It refers to the need to monitor individual practices, and to doctors making use of the prescribing analysis and cost system data. The six FPCs have piloted the proposals only during the 1989–90 financial year. Time has not been given to learn the lessons from those six pilot schemes. That is simply not a good enough way to legislate.

What will happen at regional level if the total drugs budget exceeds the allocation? We have been told that there will be a firm limit. Will the money have to be provided at the expense of other parts of the service? If a central contingency fund is to be available, what will it be and how will it work? In what circumstances does the Secretary of State envisage that the consent of the FPC will be required? The FPC will be given power, but we have not been told what restraints there will be on it. Administrators and accountants will be placed under obligations by the Government. How will their inclinations be limited so as to deliver the promise—I emphasise that it is a promise—that doctors will not be subject to financial constraints when they prescribe the drugs and medicines required by their patients?

In November, the Secretary of State said that family doctors who took the budgets with which to buy hospital 614 care would be given money for drugs within them and expected to keep to their overall budget. In other words, doctors will be free to prescribe within the practice budget, but it will be a peculiar freedom, because they will be able to prescribe only at the cost of other elements in their budget for patient care. Their right, therefore, will have no meaning or validity. Once more, I emphasise that the Secretary of State's promise in his letter of October cannot be kept without the amendments.

Other GPs will be given indicative drug budgets for medicines and, as the Secretary of State said, they

"will be expected to take this budget as a bench mark of what they ought to be prescribing".

However, the Labour party has shown that practices can be subject to many pressures. Within one practice there are variations in the requirements of patients, and there are different social requirements. There is no background of years of experience with pilot studies to suggest that the system can work or to show where safeguards should be built in. The Minister does not have access to experience from which to show that safeguards will be established in the new management systems.

Public and patient confidence is important. A 1989 poll showed that nearly 25 per cent. of the sample believed that the plan to give doctors a drug budget would lead to doctors failing to prescribe expensive drugs, even—and I stress this—when they were necessary. Half the poll sample thought that the drugs budget would make doctors "more careful". In what way can GPs be more careful? Not by being more careful about what they prescribe for a patient, but in the way in which they control their finances.

Care about the way in which prescriptions are issued should be encouraged through every mechanism and in co-operation with every part of the Health Service, including doctors. Doctors should not use the other sense of "prescription", which is the sense used in the Bill. I warn the Government of the public's worry, which is reflected in the poll and which has grown rather than diminished as a result of what I called the unseemly wrangle between the Secretary of State and the doctors. The unseemliness was on the part of the Secretary of State, not the doctors. They were simply trying to protect the freedom that the Secretary of State promised in his letter of October, but which is not safeguarded in the legislation. The view of the consumer was expressed in a Which report in answering the question:

"What happens if my GP takes a practice budget and runs out of money before the end of the year?"

The answers given in the report were:

"GPs have four options. They can overspend, and the money will be deducted from the next year's allocation; they can try to negotiate extra money; they can start their own waiting lists; or they can refuse to pay for your treatment."
