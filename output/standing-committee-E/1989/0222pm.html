If the Government gave local authorities that power—it would not limit their role in other important matters—the proposal may be well worth considering.

It is important for the Minister to tell us the Government's views. In many instances, disabled people must consider two major issues. The first is the operation of the social fund, about which we heard so much in yesterday's ruling. Many people with disabilities may try to gain access to the social fund and be refused grants or loans because of cash limits. Although they may qualify, the money may have run out at the local office. That is unreasonable. It is reasonable to ask whether, in such circumstances, the local authority would be free under new clause 3 to assist and recognise the disabled person's needs and to make money available. I do not wish to open a can of worms, but if the money is to be ring-fenced no doubt the Minister will appreciate that life is made more difficult.

The second issue relates to benefits and the independent living fund, which is being eroded. It seems that the limited cash made available for the fund is being redirected via local authorities. We cannot be JPEG 1229 sure that it will reach those for whom it was intended—the people with disabilities and perhaps their carers. What are the Government's views on those two issues?

The hon. Member for Mid-Kent and I, and perhaps other Committee members, have been given case studies. However, I believe that die overwhelming role for local audiorities, properly resourced, is that of provider. Perhaps the case studies are known to the Minister. Organisations such as the British Association of Social Workers and, indeed, the Audit Commission have said that, in exceptional cases, the enabling role might be the best way to provide succour and recognition to people in special circumstances with disabilities. Given that the argument goes back a long way and will probably not be resolved today or in the remaining sittings, I shall not complain too wildly if the Minister says that it is not possible to accept new clause 3. I do not necessarily support the new clause. However, it gives us a splendid opportunity to open the door on to Government thinking so diat hon. Members can reflect on the Minister's response and on the contents of the new clause.

Sir George Young
(Ealing, Acton)
: I think you now know, Dame Janet, what a rugby referee feels like when Llanelli are playing Newport.

New Clause 9 deals with complaints procedures. I am conscious that my hon. Friends will start complaining if the debate goes on too long. In earlier debates we touched on the appeals and complaints procedure. In cc. 1826 and 1059 my hon. Friend the Minister of State outlined her initial resistance to building into the Bill either an appeals procedure or a complaints procedure.

The White Paper says about complaints procedures:

"Where these do not already exist, authorities should establish procedures for receiving comments and complaints from users of services. Procedures should be publicised. They will be an essential safeguard for users and will also act as an important monitoring and management instrument for social services authorities and service providers alike."

Basically, the argument is that if they are an essential safeguard for users, they should be written into the bill. Is it right to omit them and leave the matter to the discretion of the local authorities?

If one looks at other services provided by local authorities, one finds an appeal machinery built in. If someone is refused housing benefit, where it is a discretionary decision, there is an appeal procedure. I speak as a local Member, who has encouraged people to go through the appeal procedure; it ends up with councillors. That is a safeguard against someone getting the wrong decision from a relatively junior official. Likewise, in the education service, if one's child is allocated a school with which one is not happy, there is an appeal procedure, which again goes to councillors.

The services that we are talking about are every bit as important as decisions on housing benefit, but at present there is no right to complain or appeal. Someone who applies for a service may simply be turned down and there is nothing he can do about it.

I have read the advice from Bill Utting to directors of social services. I understand the kind of procedure that it is hoped that local authorities will implement, but I wonder why we should not write it into the Bill and whether we are not leaving a little too much to goodwill. As the Bill is drafted, local authorities have no right to receive the necessary money to pay for community care; no one has a right to an assessment nor to services; no one has a right to appeal. At some 1264 point one needs to consider the Bill and start to put obligations on people to provide the services that we want.

I regard the appeal process as something that will build up confidence in the system rather than being, to use my hon. Friend the Minister's words, a complex, cumbersome, bureaucratic process. People have rights and we are trying to give them those rights and services, but present they have no redress. I assume that the local government ombudsman will be entitled to investigate complaints of maladministration, so they will have that, but that takes a long time, as we have all discovered.

I hope that my hon. Friend the Minister of State will reflect on her initial decision not to write into the Bill any complaints or appeals procedure, simply leaving it to the common sense of local authorities. The rights at stake are important and we must ensure that people have a right to insist on the services that we are trying to give them.
