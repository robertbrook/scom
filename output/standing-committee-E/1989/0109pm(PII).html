Mr. Alun Michael
(Cardiff, South and Penarth)
: Perhaps I may start a more peaceful session than our ealier one, in which such courtesies somehow got lost, by wishing you, Dame Janet, a happy new decade. I am sure that that will not be the length of this Committee stage, although to do justice to the Bill and to the people of the United Kingdom we should perhaps keep it in Committee that long.

I follow my hon. Friend the Member for Monklands, West (Mr. Clarke) by stating that I am a member of the General and Municipal Workers Union although not one of its sponsored MPs. That involvement often brings the advantage of meeting people who work in the machine rooms of the Health Service and, indeed, the machine rooms of social services care. That is not a declaration of interest but a declaration of my qualification to speak for Wales.

I also follow my hon. Friend's passionate practicality in proposing an amendment which is both simple and practical. The Committee would help the country and the House if it accepted that the overlap of functions is extremely important. My hon. Friend the Member for Wakefield (Mr. Hinchliffe) referred to that and to his experience at a professional level. My experience has been as chairman of juvenile magistrates and as a community worker. In both those capacities I can testify to the same need for a close working together of health and social services at a policy level as well as a practitioner level. That is the point I want to underline. It is important that policy level decisions are taken on the basis of knowledge and understanding problems and not simply as a result of a paper or academic level understanding of the problems.

The overlap of functions is very important. The experience that many Opposition Members have had, which leads us to this conclusion, surely should be reflected by the experience of hon. Members on both sides of the House in dealing with constituency problems. There are many occasions when a constituent's problems give rise to a need for two letters rather than" one—one to the local authority and one to the health authority to raise two halves of an issue which, for the individual who needs help, is one single problem. Often, the overlap involving the Member of Parliament in helping communication and smoothing the edges of responsibilities between health and social 86 services illustrates, in the direct experience of many of us, the need for this very close liaison to be built into the new institutions of the Health Service.

I was concerned at some of the comments from Conservative Members about the nature of the top policy group that is to be created. The description given in a speech before the Committee was suspended for a brief period suggested that it would be a group of people who will be expected to understand problems simply by reading and shuffling papers in some sort of remote pinnacle of decision making. That is a false idea of good management. It is not what is needed. What is needed is a broad representation within the health authority's decision-making structure, whether at both district and regional level, to include knowledge of the community as well as clinical and caring interests. If members of the committee do not have such qualities we shall have a group of amateurs shuffling papers and abrogating the responsibility to ensure that the best possible service is provided.

It worries those who run the Health Service that it is possible that too few people at senior level in the Health Service—[ Interruption.] I know that that does not interest the vociferous hon. Member for Pembroke (Mr. Bennett). He should listen as he might learn something of benefit to his community.

: It is clear that the hon. Gentleman has not read the amendment. I suggest that he does so before intervening again.

The narrow drawing of definitions becomes a problem only if there are too few places on a health authority. That is what worries health authority managers. They know that members of the authorities will be busy people with qualifications that are remote from the running of the Health Service. We have experience of such nominations from the Conservative party. The people appointed may not have the time to devote JPEG 87 to gaining the necessary knowledge of the Health Service. This Government's recipe is management by neglect at the top of the Health Service.

The hon. Member for Mid-Kent (Mr. Rowe) earlier seemed to be developing the odd Conservative business theory that matters can be dealt with remotely, without practical knowledge of the problems. That is not the way in which local business works best, nor the way in which dynamic business men work, so why on earth should it work in the National Health Service? It is an important service—Conservative Members may understand it in this sense—and an important local business. Business should be dealt with in a hands-on way by people who understand it. We propose that consultation should be built in to ensure that people on the decision-making councils have practical experience and knowledge.

The prevalence of disability in all categories is 21 per cent. higher in Wales than in England. Even when adjusted to take account of the differing age profiles of the population, it is still 18.5 per cent. higher. The worst comparision is for the most severely disabled people: 5 per cent. for Wales and only 2 per cent. for Great Britain as a whole. It is important to underline the importance of disability in terms of the Health Service.

There is frustration in dealing with disability or other categories of special need at ground level when decisions are made at the remote board, paper-shuffling level. It results in the worst possible service for patients because decisions are made without experience and understanding. In recent years, in contrast, creative work with disabled people has started with a carrot of additional money and has brought together people in the housing authority, social services authority and health authority in each area. Such day-to-day liaison between professionals, decision-makers and members of health authorities has been extremely important and creative. It has been constructive, responsible, efficient and caring, but that liaison and understanding should take place at member level, because members and officers at various levels need to work together as a team, within the health authority, within the social services department and across the boundaries of their decision-making. It is a simple and basic way to approach decision-making.

In dealing with problems affecting elderly people, it is not possible to separate Health Service concerns from social services concerns. One cannot say, "We shall make the health decisions, and leave it to functionaries at a lower level to make the social services and the health services fit together." The decisions need to be planned and delivered in co-operation.

Preventive services are increasingly important for elderly people. In the Bill and the White Paper—so reluctantly produced in response to the Griffiths report—the thrust is towards greater operation of community services. We are told of the importance of linking community services with GP services. We have the community services of the health authority on the one hand, and the home helps and other community services provided by the social services department on the other hand. We have adaptations for the disabled, as an illness or disability becomes progressive. All these are not matters of theory; they are matters 88 of day-to-day practicality, and it is important that they be managed properly for the benefit of the patient or the client of the social services department. These are practical, not theoretical, issues, and they affect the quality of life that flows from the public services that people need.

I fear that the rush to judgment—it is a slow rush— on community care, which led to the White Paper being produced at the last minute and being tacked on to this Bill to avoid the necessity of having a separate Bill, has not allowed sufficient time to appreciate the problems. The amendment, so ably moved by my hon. Friend the Member for Monklands, West, seeks to put that right and to build in consultation at the right time in the composition of the health authority, thereby undoing some of the damage that the Bill is in danger of doing. Increasing emphasis and responsibility for community care are placed on the local authority in the White Paper. That demands more liaison, not less. It demands more thought at the policy-making level, not less. The overlap must be at the policy level, and must involve proper accountable development of policy and practice in the Health Service, taking account of the implications that Health Service decisions will have for care in the community.

I wish to pay tribute to the individual commitment and creativity of councillors who have been members of health authorities in my area, and to the staff of both the social services and the health authority who have provided competent caring services in recent years, despite the immense problems that have been caused, in effect, by the Government. The creativity of the overlap between the health authority, local government and the voluntary sector has been considerable, and the amendment would encourage that to continue. It is therefore extremely important to accept the amendment and ensure the continuation of that creativity.

It is usually the social services that monitor and report on such factors; they are frequently the first port of call for people in difficulties.

The Government claim that the purpose of the Bill is to make the Health Service more efficient. But surely there are several areas in which social services JPEG 89 actively help to promote efficiency and could help the Health Services to be even more efficient. One such area is the drift of medicine towards the preventive end of the spectrum. That move is important to the future efficiency of the Health Service and better health for patients. Social services are often the agencies which can best show whether preventive medicine, or the striving towards it, is making an impact on people's health.

In Wales, there have been laudable campaigns-some of which have been fronted by the hon. Member for Cardiff, Central (Mr. Grist), the Under-Secretary of State for Wales—such as those to reduce the rate of respiratory diseases and the high incidence of heart disease. Often the people who suffer from such diseases are missed until it is far too late. Frequently their condition is detected not by general practitioners, hospitals or consultants but by health visitors or paramedics, so to speak—those in the community whose work overlaps both areas.

In the transition from one regime to another, it is vital for the population to see that the new edifice or administration contains the sort of expertise discussed today. If we are to have a more efficient Health Service, including the elements of preventive medicine, it is essential for social services to play, and be seen to play, a role on the new committees.

Amendment No. 47 has an important aspect. The Opposition believe that the clear liaison between health authorities and social services departments should be maintained. The link that currently exists between the voluntary sector social services and health authorities should also be maintained. Sir Roy Griffiths's report which laid the groundwork for the community care part of this Bill examined the problems of collaboration between social services departments and health authorities. Sir Roy said that there were real problems with collaboration and joint ventures and he highlighted the breakdown which he saw occurring in the relationship unless the Bill addressed the issue of who is ultimately to be responsible for the assessment of people in community care. He recommended that local authorities should be responsible for assessing the needs of elderly people and those with a mental illness before they were either discharged home to their families or put into residential care.

The Government recognise in their own Bill the importance of social services departments and the valuable work that they do; that has been missed by all the contributions from Conservative Members.

The Government give social services departments a central role in community care in their Bill but do not seem to accept that they ought to be consulted on the most important parts of the Bill, such as that dealing with the membership of health authorities. That is why Sir Roy Griffiths addressed the issue. He recognised the importance of collaboration and joint planning. He also knew that the breakdown, when it occurred, damanged patients' confidence in the Health Service. New structures are needed to restore that confidence.

The relationship between health authorities and social services departments must be cemented and increased by the Bill. Social services departments are now planning for their new role in community care. Because of their new community care responsibilities social services departments are examining a range of issues. They are considering the number of elderly people in the community and the projected increase into the next century. That is important both for them and the Health Service. Because of the increase in the number of elderly people there will inevitably and sadly be an increase in the incidence of dementia and mental illness in the elderly. Initially they will have to be dealt with by the Health Service in the hospitals. Then they will have to be discharged, either into residential or nursing homes, or into the community. It is therefore essential that the two departments collaborate.

Social services departments are also identifying the number of disabled people and the care that they will need. They are seeing whether there are sufficient JPEG 93 carers in the community to look after the increasing number of elderly, mentally ill and disabled people; whether they need long-term hospital care; whether there are sufficient community hospitals for the recuperation of people who have been treated in acute hospitals; whether there are day-care facilities; whether there are residential places for respite care. There is a host of services for which social services departments have new and increased responsibilities as a result of the Government's failure. It would be the height of reason for the Government to accept the amendment. Contrary to what the hon. Member for Pembroke said, the amendment does not involve the establishment of a new bureaucracy, damaging quality or paying people for doing nothing. It requires consultation—talking to people.

All the Opposition members of the Committee talk to social services departments in our own constituencies about their new responsibilities. The consultations relate not to the responsibilities of social services departments in isolation but to how they fit in with those of the health authorities—which are also invited to the discussions. Common ground is seen. Our consultations include representatives of the voluntary sector, which will have increased responsibility because of the community care provisions. Voluntary organisations are worried about their new responsibilities because they have not been fully told what is entailed. We must ensure the greatest collaboration among the bodies which share the increased responsibility.

I know that the social services department in my constituency welcomes the challenge that is contained in the community care provisions of the Bill provided that adequate resources are allocated and that proper training for the staff involved is given. The Government should enshrine in the Bill the right of those bodies to be consulted. The Government should accept the amendment and the enhanced responsibility of social services departments.
