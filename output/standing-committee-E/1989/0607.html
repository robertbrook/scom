clause Clause 48
title CONVERTIBLE SECURITIES

Mr. Smith: The clause simply inserts schedule 8 into the Bill. Schedule 8 and clauses 48 and 51 cover securities and the labyrinthine tax system which deals with them.

It is worth noting the comments of the Institute of Fiscal Studies, which expressed the matter extremely well in its observation about tax. It mentions last year's Col. 142 changes and those proposed for this year in the whole securities tax regime. The IFS states that, as a result of the changes last year and this year,

"there are ordinary debts and securities: sterling and foreign currency securities: qualifying and non-qualifying corporate bonds: convertible and non-convertible securities: securities issued at a deep discount and those not so issued: deep gain securities and non-deep gain securities, each of which may be subject to some different form of tax regime. And if the proposals put forward in the latest consultative paper"—

which are largely the proposals that we are discussing—

"are adopted we shall have qualifying and non-qualifying convertible securities and qualifying and non-qualifying indexed securities."

All of those different forms of security attract different taxation rules. It is surely nonsensical to have so complicated a system. The Institute adds:

"The challenge that faces us is to sec if there is not an easier way to achieve a rational regime for taxing securities, without the layer upon layer of technical complication and obscurity."

I very much endorse that conclusion. The provisions of clause 48, schedule 8 and the other clauses that we are about to discuss will only deepen the complexity of the problem. I hope that, in the tranquility of the next few months, the Government will consider rationalising the taxation regime for securities so that it is simpler and more comprehensible to ordinary people.

It is important that we understand the effect of the clauses under consideration, and that requires a little background. Until the early 1980s, the tax treatment of bonds issued at a discount or redeemed at a premium was not clear. The general opinion was that such bonds were not usually attractive, because the issuer might not get a deduction for the discount or premium and because the return for the investor would usually be taxed. Such bonds became more attractive when indexation allowances meant that capital profits would often be assessed as a nil or small taxable capital gain. The Government decided in 1984 to allow issuers a deduction on a yield basis year by year for any discount or premium, but still to tax investors only on a receipts basis where a security was issued at a deep discount—defined as 15 percent., or over 0.5 percent. a year—and provided that certain strict conditions were met. By 1989, those bonds had proliferated, partly because they were sometimes linked to conversion into equities. The investor accepted a low interest rate, thus producing cheap finance for the issuer and either a premium on redemption or shares instead of any principal repayment.

In 1989, it was thought right that the return on bonds issued at a discount or redeemed at a premium should generally be taxed as income, not as capital gain, even when the bonds were not deep discount securities. We supported that decision, believing it right to switch the taxation regime from capital to income taxing the profit as capital gain would usually have produced little, if any, tax due to the indexation allowances and the non-liability of overseas residents to capital gains tax.

Therefore, in 1989, the deep gain legislation was introduced. We supported that: it was broadly an anti-avoidance measure and attacked certain securities Image Col. 143 which the issuers did not want to be deep discount securities and for which the existing regime was too generous. Even at the time, it was realised that the deep gain rules had to be drawn widely to catch all kinds of securities where the uplift—whether on a sale by a holder or on redemption—should be taxed as income rather than gain and that as a consequence, many exceptions would be needed to exclude innocent cases. The net was being drawn wide to catch the non-innocent but, in the process, exceptions had to be included to allow the innocent to survive. As a result, the Finance Act 1989 excluded from the net any deep discount security, all shares, qualifying indexed securities as defined, certain types of gilt and all convertible securities as defined—which broadly meant a quoted convertible issued before 8 June 1989. The significance of that date is that it was when the Finance Bill Committee debated the matter. The hon. Member for Esher (Mr. Taylor) will remember it particularly well because he was active in our debates.

However, that left subsequently issued convertibles caught by the deep gain legislation if they were issued with an option to redeem at a premium exercisable by the investor at the appropriate time. It was felt that the premium in such cases had to be regarded partly as income, because it was a guaranteed profit, but that part of the return might be akin to gains on the underlying equities which should be taxed as capital. Consultations were promised by the Minister during our debates last year. Those consultations took place and the basic principle that the Government adumbrated in the consultation document that they issued was that any guaranteed income element should be charged as income whereas the balance should be taxed as capital gain. That is a principle with which we concur because it is a sensible regime and broadly follows the regime set out for deep discount securities.

As we embark upon this part of the Bill, I thought it appropriate to set out the background as clearly as is possible with such complex legislation. We broadly support the thrust of schedule 8 but we have a number of detailed questions which will be more appropriately discussed under the amendments to it. As to the general principle, we accept the division between income and capital which the Government seek to enshrine in the measures on convertible securities but we wish that they would sit down sensibly, over a sustained period, and come up with a more rational and easily understandable tax regime for securities—not to let any sharp practice through the tax net but to ensure that people are clearer about where the tax treatment of securities lies. At the moment, it is undoubtedly far too complex.

Clause 51 deals with the deep discount securities regime. Normally, to make it work there must be a fixed redemption date and price. Only then can the fixed yield to maturity on the bond be calculated and, along with it, the interest element for each year which the issuer may deduct in his tax computations. Accordingly, where there arc two or more possible redemption dates and prices, it is usually impossible to Image Col. 161 say what the yield to maturity is and to what deduction the issuer is entitled each year.

At present, it is probably the case that bonds of that type qualify as deep discount securities, provided that the discount is deep whatever redemption date is chosen. The clause appears to be an attempt by the Inland Revenue to get rid of the problem by saying that such bonds cannot qualify as deep discount securities at all. Deep discount securities can perhaps best be regarded as a legalised form of tax deferral. In principle, we wish to limit their availability.

We support the general thrust of clause 51, but in one instance the evil which the Inland Revenue seems to be aiming at does not exist. Amendment No. 90 deals with when the yield to maturity is the same, whatever redemption date is chosen. Therefore, the issuer's tax position would always be known. We accept that, when there are two possible redemption dates and the yield is uncertain, the provisions of clause 51 should apply, but they are unnecessary when there are two possible dates and the yield is known.
