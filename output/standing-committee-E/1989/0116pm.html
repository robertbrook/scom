The Minister for Health (Mrs. Virginia Bottomley): The debate has been dominated by hon. Members from both sides of the Committee recognising the importance of the family practitioner services. Indeed, those services are one of the greatest strengths of the National Health Service. Recently, the Government have sought to enhance their role in the White Papers entitled 'Promoting Better Health' and 'Working for Patients'. Those White Papers make it clear that we want to develop the responsibilities of the family practitioner services and to encourage them to plan, assess and manage the extremely costly services for which they are responsible. The amendments specify the qualities that we want to see in members of family health service authorities. The issue has been well debated. We want individuals of distinction and experience who have skills that can improve and maintain effective, high-quality primary health care services.

The hon. Member for Ross, Cromarty and Skye (Mr. Kennedy) suggested that the proposals were a vote of no confidence in the family practitioner committees. Nothing could be further from the truth. We want to build, develop and refine all that we have learnt, and ensure that family health service authorities are well-equipped to provide their important services.

Many hon. Members have suggested that members of an authority should live or work in the area. Local people will have a strong interest in the provision of local services. Many members of the authorities will live or work within the area. The issue that is being debated, however, is whether that should be a criterion for their appointment. Local knowledge is extremely important but we do not want to be unduly prescriptive and require that members of the authority live or work in the area when they may have worked there all their lives and moved away recently. They may have been involved in a voluntary organisation in the vicinity, but be excluded by the requirement that they live and work there.

I hope that hon. Members will feel reassured that we appreciate and understand that local knowledge is an important factor, but we do not want unduly to tie the hands of those making the appointments. We want high-quality, effective, skilled people who will ensure that the billions of pounds that are spent on the family practitioners service are spent effectively in the interests of patients.

The hon. Member for Ross, Cromarty and Skye spoke about the significance of continuity. In "Working for Patients" we made it clear that we would ensure that the new lay membership would preserve a measure of continuity with the family practitioner committees, as currently constituted. I hope that that will reassure those who are concerned about what could otherwise mean substantial changes for groups who are already taking on a number of important new responsibilities.

Mr. Maxton: This is the first Scottish debate on the structure of the Health Service in Scotland. For the benefit of English and Welsh Members perhaps I should say a brief word about the differences between the two systems to enable hon. It is worth pointing out that whereas the English part of the Bill stands on its own, The Scottish part amends previous legislation and, in particular, The National Health Service (Scotland) Act 1978. I am grateful to the Minister for assuring me that copies of the 1978 Act will be available this evening.
