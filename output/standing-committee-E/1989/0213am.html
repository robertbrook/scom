National Health Service and Community Care Bill

Clause 50
RESIDENTIAL ACCOMMODATION WITH NURSING

10.30 am

Mr. Tom Clarke
(Monklands, West)
: I beg to move amendment No. 742, in page 48, line 8, at end insert—

'(2) The Nursing Homes Registration (Scotland) Act 1938 shall be amended as follows—

  1. for the words "Health Board" and "Board" where they appear throughout there shall be substituted the words "Local Authority" and "Authority", respectively.
  2. in section 5(1) (inspect of nursing homes), for the words from the beginning to "authorised by the Board" there shall be substituted the words "any duly authorised officer of the local authority.".'.

The amendment is similar to some of the amendments relating to England that we discussed until late on Thursday. The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland who will reply, will be aware of the similarities, but it is recognised that in practice the systems for registration and other social service matters differ in England, Scotland and Wales. In the debate on Thursday, the Under-Secretary of State for Wales said:

"Basically, we agree that the local authorities and health authorities would have to work closely together, not only on this matter but generally in the provision of services to people in the locality. Certainly in Wales we look to the local authorities to take the lead to set joint care plans, which is slightly different from the arrangements in England." — [Official Report, Standing Committee E, 8 February 1990; c. 853.]

That is a fair point.

Many people in Scotland believe that in this, as in other community care matters, the Welsh model sets a good example to the rest of the United Kingdom. The ingenuity of the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland and his stout determination to defend Scotland, will lead him to produce arguments different from the persuasive ones employed by the Under-Secretary of State for Wales on Thursday.

The amendment would require nursing homes to be registered by the new local authority inspection units, as residential care homes will be. As local authorities will be responsible for funding both types of home, it is important that an efficient and workable system be established— [Interruption.]

The Chairman
: Order. I can scarcely hear the hon. Member's speech because of the chattering that is going on.

Mr. Clarke
: We have been told that the White Paper, which lay behind the Government's drafting of this clause, is about introducing a considered and rational approach to the management of community care services, and to 860 overseeing such services. We have been told that it is also about identifying accountability. But if split responsibility for registration is retained, it will be more difficult to have a sustained and rational approach, and to pin down accountability. Furthermore, if responsibility remains split between health boards and local authorities, wasteful dual registration systems will have to be retained, and extra liaison between registration staff in the two authorities will be necessary. From experience, hon. Members on both sides of the Committee will know that some unscrupulous owners will be tempted to play one authority against the other by making the arrangement unacceptable. That is not a fantasy. It has happened under the existing arrangements.

I thought that the Committee intended to improve services that were not working as well as we would like. If the existing arrangements for nursing home registrations are retained, under the Government's proposed financial arrangements local authority staff will have to visit nursing homes to check on standards for placements purchased through the local authority, using funds from the Department of Social Security.

It is nonsense to go to such lengths to unify and simplify the financial payment system for nursing homes, by bringing it within the responsibility of the local authority alongside the financial system for residential care homes, and yet to leave the registration system split. If there is no move to unify the registration system we shall be left with an awkward arrangement whereby the new arms-length and more objective registration system for residential care homes will not apply to nursing homes; and the old and less independent registration system will continue. That is surely unacceptable and inefficient, especially as we are all convinced of the need for arms-length inspections.

Moreover, it will be much more difficult to apply to nursing homes any new standards that might be introduced by the Government or by subsequent Governments following the Bill's enactment. One of the Government's reasons for not changing arrangements might be worries expressed by the medical professions. But, there should be no cause for such worry, as I would still expect local authority registration units formally to seek health board approval for the nursing component before finalising registration.

If the amendments before the Committee prove inadequate, and if the Government accept our principles, we should return to the problems of registration on Report. Our proposed system would be no different from the system under which local authorities seek the opinions of fire masters and building control and planning departments of district councils to ensure that proposed residential homes meet the applicable regulations.

The Opposition are advancing strong arguments. It might be said that we represent the opinions only of those involved in social work. We do represent their opinions, but support for our view goes far beyond that and embraces people who are involved in the Health Service and in nursing care provision. Of course, as we would expect, our opinions are supported by Age Concern (Scotland), as well as by many organisations involved in the Scottish working group on care in the community: for instance, the Association of Directors of Social Work, and the Church of Scotland (Committee on Social Responsibility). We are entitled to claim the support of the Psychiatric Nurses Association (Scotland), the Scottish Council on Disability, the Scottish Society for the JPEG 861 Mentally Handicapped, Alzheimer's Scotland, and, significantly, the Association of Scottish Local Health Councils. The group's paper, which is available to hon. Members, expresses our view. Among the organisations involved in that paper are Community Service Volunteers —Scotland and the Scottish Association for Mental Health. A significant body of opinion tells us that we should seize this opportunity to abolish dual registration. In the paper, "Care in the Community—Scottish Working Group", the group states:

"In order to ensure consistency, the local authority power to register residential accommodation should be extended to include nursing homes given that they will, after April 1991, be responsible for meeting the care costs of their residents."

The amendment would mean that like residential care homes, nursing homes would be registered by the new local authority inspection units as the Scottish working group advocates. It is important to ensure consistency in the quality of service provision. Given the pressures on local authority time, it is unlikely that contractual conditions with nursing home providers would be enforced strictly enough to check standards. Many professionals believe that the system of dual registration allows homes to provide a poor service. It is unacceptable that some establishments have been refused registration as residential care homes by the local authority, yet succeeded in being registered as nursing homes. In theory nursing homes should be required to provide a higher standard of service as they deal with vulnerable people.

As well as having worrying implications for the quality of care, the distinction made between nursing homes and residential care homes is largely historical and of limited relevance today. The Bill gives us the opportunity to correct what is wrong. The amendment would avoid confusion and contradictions in applying high standards and would avoid wasteful duplication of effort. We are told that that is the Government's objective. Certainly it was the main thrust of some of the Griffiths report's recommendations. If our amendment is accepted, we shall have made considerable progress on that.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Michael Forsyth)
: The hon. Member for Monklands, West (Mr. Clarke) has made his case well, but there is a distinction between residential care homes and nursing homes. They have things in common, of course. Nursing care is provided by health professionals and both need to meet health care standards. It seems appropriate therefore that the bodies that have expertise in that area, the health boards, should be responsible for the registration of homes— [Interruption.]

The Chairman

Gentleman suggested. I therefore ask him to withdraw his amendment.
