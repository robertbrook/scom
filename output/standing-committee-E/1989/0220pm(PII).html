Clause 45
POWER OF SECRETARY OF STATE TO GIVE DIRECTIONS

Mr. Tom Clarke
: I beg to move amendment No. 623, in page 44, line 45, leave out "either individually or".

I apologise because I seem to have developed a cold today, perhaps as an allergy to television lighting, but I see that that problem has been resolved. It is odd that with Scottish provisions we seem to take three clauses to say what the English manage to say in one. Nevertheless, I shall be as brief as possible, consistent with placing on record Scottish worries and questions in a probing sense, especially through this amendment. No doubt the Minister will respond in kind.

Earlier today, Ministers were a little worried about the local government response to their views. I assure the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland that he need have no worries about this amendment because it was drafted by COSLA. COSLA's worries, which are similar to those expressed this morning, are that if the clause remains unamended it will give more and more powers to the Secretary of State. When and how would the Secretary of State for Scotland use those powers? Would he police individual local authorities? We envisage that to be the impact of such powers. Is he to be the court of appeal? Does the clause, in effect, ask the Secretary of State to issue individual directions as opposed to general guidelines?

That is consistent with the argument that we put forward last week. No Opposition Member would object—quite the reverse—to the Secretary of State giving general guidelines. Indeed, it would be a sign of his interest, something that we have tried to encourage. My position is consistent and I say this to save the Minister from reciting my speech from last week. There is a big difference between inviting the Secretary of State to be involved in a spirit of co-operation in genuine joint planning and giving him the power to direct. Until the Minister replies, we are not sure what those directions will be.

JPEG 1159 The notes on clauses for clause 45 state:

"This clause gives the Secretary of State a power to issue directions to local authorities as to the way in which they should exercise any of their social work functions."

They also state:

"This is an entirely new provision"—

it is important to note that—

"which will extend the Secetary of State's powers to control the nature and direction of local authority provision of social work services."

That is a wide-ranging provision. There is a danger, and COSLA and Scottish local authorities would share this view, that the provision could be used, as the document implies, for any social work powers under the social work legislation, including powers relating to children on probation.

The White Paper was hastily introduced and, if there was consultation, it was done in a week. We have had the Children Act 1989 which, for the most part, does not apply to Scotland. Nobody expected that, if we were to consider powers involving child care and other important matters, such provisions would be introduced in a Bill such as this. Does the Minister have such things in mind? If so, with what other aspects of social work do the Government intend to deal?

Such matters seem well outwith the scope of community care as envisaged by people in Scotland. Why is the Bill drafted in this way? Why did the Government seemingly change their mind and decide that the Bill should go outwith community care? If they were dealing exclusively with community care, as most people expected, the powers would relate to assessment, the regulations and monitoring, the very issues which led us to complain last week. The Minister will recall that—if he has forgotten, he has the Hansard report in front of him—the Government refused to take those issues on board. They declaimed to use their powers in a spirit of consultation, joint planning and co-operation.

This morning, we heard the Minister's colleague, the Under-Secretary of State for Health, talk about privatisation, even if it was to deny it. If people in English local authorities are worried about privatisation by the back door, the Minister will not be surprised if those in Scottish local authorities are even more worried.

The Government seem to have used the Bill in a cavalier manner to interfere in any social work service that appeals to them. Through this probing amendment, I invite the Minister to tell us why that is so.

Clause 56
REPEAL OF REMAINING PROVISIONS OF HEALTH SERVICES ACT 1976

Mr. Nicholas Bennett
: I beg to move amendment No. 799, in page 51, leave out lines 23 to 30. Unlike the hon. Member for Monklands, West (Mr. Clarke), who spoke for nearly 45 minutes on one of his amendments, I shall be brief and attempt to move the amendment in about 30 seconds. There has been too much time wasting in the past.

The clause removes the Secretary of State's powers over independent hospitals of the future. The amendment would ensure that the Secretary of State's present powers over hospitals—[ Interruption.] My hon. Friends are delaying my 30 seconds.

The Chairman
: Order. I should be grateful if hon. Members were silent so that I could listen to the hon. Member for Pembroke (Mr. Bennett).

Mr. Bennett
: Clause 56 takes away the powers of the Secretary of State over new independent hospitals, but unfortunately retains his power over existing authorisations given for them. The Secretary of State should not be allowed to retain such powers over existing JPEG 1181 hospital. Amendment No. 799 removes lines 23 to 30 and puts existing hospitals on a par with new hospitals. That is a logical extension to the clause.

Mr. Maxton
: I found the hon. Gentleman's remarks offensive. The arguments have been stated before, but there is great deal of interest in the Bill in Scotland and we have dealt with the Scottish clauses in a limited time. We have deliberately tried not to keep the hon. Gentleman from his bed, where he perhaps should be, although he may not wish to be. His remarks about my hon. Friends' speeches are offensive. We have put our arguments concisely and if Conservative Members had done the same, we would have dealt with the clause more quickly. I wish to ask the Under-Secretary of State for Health one question—does clause 56 apply to Scotland?

10.45 pm

Mr. Freeman
: Yes, it does. As my hon. friend the Member for Pembroke (Mr. Bennett) is an intelligent man and an assiduous member of the Committee, I am sure that, on reflection, he will not wish to advocate the cause of retrospective legislation. No member of the Committee, not even the hon. Member for Glasgow, Cathcart (Mr. Maxton), would espouse such a cause. Only one permission has been granted under the legislation that we seek to annul. As far as my hon. friend the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland and I are aware, no representations have been made from the hospital, which is content with the agreements. I hope that my hon. Friend the Member for Pembroke will not persist with the amendment.

Mr. Nicholas Bennett
: My hon. Friend describes this as retrospective legislation, but it is not. In retrospec- 1182 tive legislation, the law is changed to the disadvantage of people. I wish to change the law so that hospitals that are now disadvantaged by being in a different position from new hospitals will be dealt with on the same basis in future. I do not understand why my hon. Friend wants to retain controls over exisiting independent hospitals when he does not propose to have controls over new independent hospitals. Existing hospitals will be at a disadvantage if the amendment is not accepted.

Mr. Freeman
: I am sorry to bore the Committee again, but perhaps I can reassure my hon. Friend. The consequence of the amendment would be to annul the agreement reached between the Government and one hospital. No representations have been made by that single hospital to relieve it of of its obligations on training, size and capacity. I appreciate my hon. Friend's ardour and interest in the general principle, but the amendment is unnecessary.

Mr. Nicholas Bennett
: Why does a hospital that had no choice but to enter into that agreement have to continue to be disadvantaged by restrictions? My hon. Friend wishes to retain on one hospital restrictions to which new hospitals will not be subject. However, I shall not press the amendment to a vote. I beg to seek leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 56 ordered to stand part of the Bill.
