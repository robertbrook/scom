Mr. Clarke
: I am sure that the Committee is delighted now that we have reached consideration of community care. I think that we all agree that it is a profoundly important aspect of the Bill.

Amendment No. 580 and new clause 13 deal with an important aspect of community care which has undergone much discussion in the reports of Griffiths and Wagner. The thrust of the amendment and new clause is to accept the shift towards a more mixed economy provision of community care. The Government and Opposition agree that that shift is necessary. We should seize the opportunity provided by the Bill to ensure that the right care is provided, as we are dealing with the care of the elderly. Therefore, our proposals for the inspectorate's roles and for the method of inspection, are important.

It is important also that arrangements for inspections and lines of accountability are established evenly in each 760 sector. We make no distinction in the standards that should be set in both the public and private sectors. We accept that there have been grievous errors in both sectors and we shall not attempt to whitewash those this morning. It is important to ensure that the inspectorate is correctly established; that is the aim of our amendments and new clause.

It is important that the inspectorate's role, the method of inspection and its accountability should be to the advantage of the consumer. Our proposals for the board include consumer participation, as well as the participation of those who are responsible for registering and providing the services that would come under inspection.

Under our proposals the system of regional boards would provide a mechanism for the broad representation of relevant interests, including those of health and housing bodies, and voluntary organisations. It would include users' interests as well as those of local authority social services. It is self-evident that it would embrace almost everybody involved in service provision, and would ensure accountability, inspection and the continued discussion of best practice. Best practice even in terms of inspection, may exist in parts of the country, but we want to extend it throughout the United Kingdom. There are regional variations in service provisions, local authority input, consumer needs and even levels of deprivation. Apart from ensuring national standards, regional boards, because they would operate at a regional level, could more easily become involved in monitoring, which I am sure all Committee members accept is essential if the system is to work. The Association of Metropolitan Authorities wants —this seems to me to be reasonable—local government representation to play a major role on the regional registration boards because of the key functions envisaged for local authority social service departments. I am not sure what the Government's thinking is on that, but I remind the Minister that even Sir Roy Griffiths, who considered an enormous amount of evidence on the matter including the report of the Audit Commission for England and Wales, took the view that the lead role in community care should be provided by local authorities.

In addition, progress reports from the regional boards would act as an umbrella. Local authorities agree with me that service provision should be of the highest standard and proper monitoring should not only cover the provision of service and the needs and rights of the user but take account of the importance of value for money.
