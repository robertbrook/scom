The clause proposes several changes. The first—to which amendments Nos. 60, 61 and 63 relate—is what might be called the ratchet effect. Perhaps I may explain how that works. I worked out some examples because that sometimes helps in such complicated matters. Let us suppose that a bank has a debt of £100 and, up until now, has provided £50, thus showing £50 as the asset in the balance sheet. Suppose that this year it decides to provide a further £20 to write off £20, which means it will have written off 70 per cent of the debt. Under these proposals, it will not be able to have that £20 for tax relief; it will get only 5 per cent of the original debt—in other words, £5. Therefore, if it writes off £20 next year, it will get only £5 and £5 in the following year, £5 in year three and £5 in year four. It will take four years to obtain the tax relief because that is how it is restricted.

That is also true of an actual loss, to which one of the other amendments relates. If the actual loss is more than the provision and the debt is £100 and £50 has been provided to date; if the debt is sold on the secondary market for £30, the actual loss is £20. But the bank will not be allowed to have that £20 for tax relief; it will have only £5.

What happens if the opposite applies? Let us suppose that the bank has a debt of £100 and, up until 1990, has provided £50 but then decides that the chances of recovering the money have improved. It might be argued that that is unlikely. But if that is so and it decides to reduce the provision by £20, the whole of that relief that was previously granted is clawed back in one go. There is no matching here. If the provision is increased, one can get only 5 per cent. If the provision is clawed back, the whole lot is lost in one go.

The purpose of the amendments is to make the ratchet effect work both ways so that it works in favour of the Inland Revenue and, in the unlikely event that the provision is reduced, in favour of the taxpayer. I hope that my hon. Friend agrees that this is an equitable principle on which to base these provisions.

Question proposed. That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Chris Smith: Clause 67 is deceptively short. It extends the qualifying period for tax relief for contributions to approved local enterprise agencies for three years, from April 1992 to April 1995. That is a logical step, given that clause 68 provides tax relief for business contributions to training and enterprise councils. To continue the tax relief on business contributions to local enterprise agencies is in tune with that philosophy. Local enterprise agencies are advice shops, established to provide information on starting small businesses. As such, they are entirely worthy and valuable bodies. They will provide all sorts of advice. They have tended to concentrate on property advice rather than on other forms of advice, but that is a mild criticism. Local enterprise agencies have done much that is to be welcomed.

The general rule for deciding whether any tax concession is welcome must rest on whether the benefit to be gained is worth the cost to the Exchequer.

We have to promote small business. Many people have splendid ideas about the complexities of setting up a business, and they need assistance. We must continue to offer incentives to ensure that local enterprise agencies can be established and carry forward their work for the next three years. We do not therefore object to the proposal.

What do the Government envisage that the measure will cost? I imagine that it is negligible, but we should like the Minister to confirm that. As a basic principle, we do not oppose the extension of relief for those bodies for three years.

Mr. Lilley: I am glad that the hon. Member for Islington, South and Finsbury (Mr. Smith) welcomes the clause. The cost will obviously depend on the generosity of the companies making contributions. Independent sources suggest that about 5,000 companies Image Col. 271 support local enterprise agencies and that about £15 million is given by the private sector each year. That will obviously result in a corresponding reduction in tax revenue, depending on the tax rates of the companies concerned.

Mr. Smith: The Government are providing incentives to business to help local small business to be established. Mr. Lilley: Local authorities are already exempt from corporation tax. 1 shall not detain the Committee further by elaborating on such a simple clause.
