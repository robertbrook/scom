The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Health (Mr. Roger Freeman): On behalf of the Committee, I wish Harry a happy birthday.

Joint finance is the mechanism whereby £120 million of health resources is devoted to schemes involving a joint agreement among RHAs, DHAs and voluntary organisations. Its introduction in 1976 reflected the need to care for certain groups in the community. Plans for such agreements can be implemented only through joint consultative committees. Many hon. Members will know of such agreements in their constituencies.

The schemes have been valuable. In 1987–88, 42 per cent. of their resources were spent on the care of the elderly, 43 per cent. on the care of the handicapped, and unfortunately only 5 per cent. on the care of the mentally ill and disabled. Those figures are a commentary on the relative importance placed on those services by local authorities. They are one reason why we shall debate the specific grant for the community care of the mentally ill.

About 60 per cent. of the resources are spent for revenue purposes and 40 per cent. for capital purposes. There is a taper in joint finance schemes. Understandably, local authorities are sometimes reluctant to take responsibility for pump-priming schemes that have been initiated with health authority and local authority money, because of the revenue consequences. The Government have reached no conclusions about the future of joint finance but we shall review the options in the spring and summer. We shall inform Parliament of any proposals to change joint finance at the earliest opportunity.

The options range from recycling all those resources through local authorities, either with specific grants or non-specific grants, ring-fenced or not, perhaps through regional health authorities to local authorities—as we Image Col. 721 propose for the mentally ill—or through district health authorities or the Department. We are considering the options carefully. I assure the Committee that, as paragraph 6.14 of the White Paper on community care, "Caring for People" states, we have a commitment to community care and the resources will not be withdrawn and used for other purposes. Given that we accept the bulk of Griffiths's recommendations, we are looking for the most effective way of carrying forward joint finance. I repeat my commitment that when we have reached a conclusion, which I hope will be in the summer, my right hon. and learned Friend the Secretary of State will come to the House and properly report his conclusions and recommendations for the future. There will then be an opportunity to debate the continuance of a valuable and important scheme.

As my right hon. and learned Friend the Secretary of State knows, I have always been a tremendous fan of the work that the Audit Commission does. Two years ago, I tabled a question to his predecessor asking whether the Audit Commission should be invited to look at the economy, efficiency and effectiveness of the Health Service to see that it managed and deployed its resources in the best possible manner. It has taken two years to reach that point. Many of us have campaigned for the Audit Commission to do value-for-money studies in the Health Service to ensure that best practice prevails, and that we encourage practices that give best value for money in the promotion of patient care.

I am dismayed at one provision in schedule 4, and it can best be described as follows. Section 27 of the Local Government Finance Act 1982 empowered the Audit Commission to review the value-for-money effect of ministerial guidance or directions and statutory provisions. However, schedule 4 provides for the external audit of the NHS to be taken over by the Audit Commission and paragraph 19 will effectively stop the provisions of section 27 of the 1982 Act applying to ministerial guidance, directions and statutory provisions in the Health Service. That would be a mistake.

Perhaps I should develop my case further. I am grateful to the Library for furnishing me with information about events that took place long before I was privileged to come to this place. In 1982 the Government did not commence the Bill on the basis of giving away section 27. Indeed, the intention then was exactly the same as today: that it should not include ministerial decisions and directions within the remit of the Audit Commission.

At the time, there was great pressure in Committee from both sides because this is not a party-political issue; it is about keeping the executive accountable and making sure that the value-for-money studies performed by the Audit Commission do some good. At that time, my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Defence, who was then a Minister in the Department of the Environment, conceded that it would be right to amend the Bill, and an amendment was moved in the Lords. When it returned to the Floor of the House of Commons, my right hon. Friend said that he regarded the amendment as an important step forward in terms of the accountability of Ministers to the House, and he thought that Ministers in key spending Departments should accept that the disciplines of the Audit Commission were a legitimate part of the scrutiny of the executive.

Above all, at that time, two particular arguments held sway: first, that from time to time Ministers make mistakes. That is unusual in this Government but it has happened in the past. There is merit in accepting the idea and allowing the Audit Commission to take it into account. Secondly, and perhaps more importantly, it protects Ministers to allow this process to take place. At the moment, a local authority could easily hide behind the effects of this section and argue that although there may be a strong case for a value-for-money study in a particular area, as it affects the decision made by the Minister, which is not allowed to be examined by the Audit Commission, it cannot be proceeded with. I believe that that would be a mistake. The commission may well think that something is bad value for money but if it stems from a ministerial decision it cannot proceed.
