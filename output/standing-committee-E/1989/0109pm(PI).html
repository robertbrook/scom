REGIONAL AND DISTRICT HEALTH AUTHORITIES

Ms. Harman
: Before I speak to the amendments, I wish to point out that I am sponsored by the Transport and General Workers Union, members of which work 56 in the Health Service.

The purpose of the amendments is to challenge the time when the clause will be brought into effect. We have attempted to do something more fundamental than amending the Bill's commencement; our aim is to prevent the provisions from coming into effect until Parliament has an opportunity separately to examine the question of timing, and a resolution about whether the clause should come into effect.

We felt that is was necessary to deal with the matter in two stages—to consider the substance of the clause, and to give the House an opportunity separately to consider whether the clause should be brought into effect—for four reasons. First, the Government have no mandate to bring in the Bill and the clause as part of it. Secondly, the Government have undertaken no consulation on the Bill or the clause. Thirdly, we know that there is overwhelming opposition to the Bill and clause. Fourthly, the speed with which the Government plan to implement the Bill will, in our view, make this clause, along with the others, unworkable.

I shall comment, first, on the fact that the Government have no mandate. This morning, the hon. Member for Gainsborough and Horhcastle (Mr. Leigh) said that this was a most important Bill—the most important Bill, I think he said—yet it found no place in the Government's manifesto at the last election. I challenge Conservative Members to show us any election address which stated that they wanted this clause, or even that they wanted the Bill. It is a Bill for which there is no public support, and the Government have no mandate for this part of it.

Secondly, the Government have undertaken no consultation. There were no consulations on the review that first floated the idea that local authority members of district health authorities should be knocked off those authorities.

There were no consultations on the review that led to the Bill. There was no Green Paper that would have allowed members of the public and members of the medical profession to discuss the clause. No views were sought from the medical profession. Nor, despite the rhetoric to which we are continually subjected about consumerism, were views sought from patients' organisations.

The Government should have consulted the medical profession and those who work in the National Health Service. It is arrogant and wrong to dismiss their views. That has enabled the Government to say that everybody who is against the Bill is against change. The problem is that they have not listened to those who work in or use the Health Service and who want change but profoundly disagree with the changes that the Bill, especially this clause, will implement.

Above all the Government should have consulted the organisations that represent patients. The amendments would give the Government the opportunity to consult the organisations that they so manifestly failed to consult before they came to the House to seek to put the clause into effect. There are many experienced and articulate organisations whose views should have been taken into account. The organisations which the Government should have consulted before seeking to JPEG 57 bring the Bill before the House inclde: the British Diabetic Association; the British Epilepsy Association; the British Kidney Patient Association; the Haemophilia Society; the Migraine Trust; the Muscular Dystrophy Group of Great Britain; the Parkinson's Disease Society of the United Kingdom Ltd; the Royal National Institute for the Blind; and the Royal National Institute for the Deaf. That short list of organisations shows the weight of experience and opinion that would be available if we had a Government who were prepared to listen to the views of outside organisations.

There needs to be separate consideration of the timing of the clause because of the massive opposition to it. Doctors, nurses, midwives, the BMA, the Royal Medical Colleges and the Joint Consultants Committee had what they described as an "unprecedented" joint press conference at which Sir Anthony Grabham said:

"The people who have been treating patients for the past 40 years have been denied an opportunity to express their concerns to our Prime Minister. I think that is significant."

Opposition comes not just from those who work in the National Health Service, though theirs is profound and widespread. Above all, it comes from the public. I shall quote some opinion polls that demonstrate that. A street ballot was conduted in Bristol by the Manufacturing, Science and Finance Union. I hope that the Government will not scoff at the fact that I am quoting a street ballot. They themselves—significantly —have tried to avoid taking any test of public opinion. I hope that they will not scorn those who in good faith have tried to. The results of the poll on the Bill—the clause was one of the issues canvassed—were: 7,623 against: 98 for; and 29 spoilt ballot papers.
