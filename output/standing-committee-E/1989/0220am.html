National Health Service and Community Care Bill

Clause 44
POWERS OF THE SECRETARY OF STATE AS RESPECTS SOCIAL SERVICES FUNCTIONS OF LOCAL AUTHORITIES

10.30 am

Mr. Tom Clarke
(Monklands, West)
: I beg to move amendment No. 771, in page 43, line 40, leave out from beginning to line 7 on page 44.

The amendments that we have tabled to clause 44 are designed to probe the Government's thinking. I hope that the Under-Secretary of State for Health will consider that when he makes his response. The clause is frightening in many ways, because at first sight it gives the Secretary of State many powers, which hitherto no one had envisaged, not over only community care, with which the clause deals, but over a much wider range of social services. We want to use the amendments to discover precisely how the Secretary of State for Health intends to use those major powers.

In the notes on clauses, the Government say:

"this clause enables the Secretary of State to issue directions to local authorities covering all their social service functions".

Therefore, we do not exaggerate when we say that, if that happens, it will represent a major development in the relationship between the Secretary of State and his successors and local authorities and those involved with community care in a wider sense.

Will the Under-Secretary tell us the precise directions that the Secretary of State has in mind? Will he respond to the view that the clause seems far too wide, and gives far too many powers to the Secretary of State? That opinion is widely held, not only by my hon. Friends, but by many voluntary and professional organisations and local authority associations. Those associations hope that the grey areas of community care, where the demarcation lines are not clear between them and the Secretary of State, will be clarified today.

The Opposition do not dispute the right of the Secretary of State to offer guidance. Indeed, in last week's debate on Scotland our criticism of the Secretary of States was that he was not giving guidance to Scotland. WScte should have liked the Secretary of State for Scotland to be more involved. Our reason for tabling the amendment is that the Secretary of State's powers will be too wide ranging. It is not an exaggeration to say that they will be sweeping new powers over many aspects of social services, including those for children, which many hon. Members feel have been adequately 1082 dealt with by the Children Act 1989. Enormous thought went into that Act. To be fair, I should say that the then Minister for Health, the hon. and learned Member for Putney (Mr. Mellor) extensively involved both sides of the Committee, as well as voluntary and professional bodies.

The Minister must from time to time think about how local authorities will interpret the Bill. It is odd that the Bill which will give sweeping powers to the Secretary of State to deal with children, assessments and so on, will apply in addition to the Children Act.

Mr. Clarke
: My hon. Friend has made an excellent point. He is in a strong position to do so because he was a member of the Standing Committee that considered the Children Bill, and he has great knowledge of and interest in such matters.

The clause will give the Secretary of State various powers, including the power to direct local authorities in matters relating to children. That leads to the question of how those powers have found their way into the Bill. Why were such powers not discussed when previous legislation, including the Children Bill, was debated.

Many are worried that the Secretary of State will assume these powers almost by the back door. Mr. Ken Judge, the director of the King's Fund, was quoted in Social Work Today on 30 November 1989:

"This particular Bill goes further down the road in promoting vague enabling powers which concentrate power in the hands of the Secretary of State without much chance of Parliamentary review. These potentially Draconian powers are there to give a clear indication to local authorities not to thwart the basic thrust of Government policy."

If Mr. Judge is right, Opposition Members should be very worried.

We know that co-operation is important in community care, and the Minister's contributions in Committee have shown that he shares that view. But the Government are departing from that by giving the present Secretary of State and his successors powers that have been described as draconian by a man who, judged by his utterances, is reasonable in his views. It is important, therefore, to ask the questions implied in the amendments.

Mr. Clarke
: My hon. friend is right. He speaks with enormous experience because he has been a distinguished social worker. The Committee has benefited from his experience.

If the clause is to be agreed unamended, we have to consider its practical implications. They are as my hon. Friend the Member for Wakefield (Mr. Hinchliffe) has just described. The Secretary of State will have the power to issue directions about child care, assessment, and other matters. That is more power than the Committee would wish to give to any Secretary of State. The Minister and the Government should therefore clarify why they need to take such power upon themselves. They seem to be attempting to gain them under the guise of community care.

Many people feel that the Bill is the most important of the decade; indeed, it will take us into the next decade. Therefore, the Government should explain their view of the role of all the parties that will be involved in community care, including that of local authorities, voluntary organisations, carers, the users and consumers. The Government should also explain the important role of the physically disabled, for some people feel that we have not given sufficient priority to them. That accountability is essential, and we have tabled amendments to the clause because we feel that it is missing. I hope that the Minister will see the importance of that, and will respond to it.

The clause will surprise many people if it stands unamended. The main thrust of this part of the Bill arose from the Griffiths report. We have not identified anything in the report that would lead the Government, the Committee or the House to give the Secretary of State such draconian powers, as Mr. Judge described them. If there is nothing in Griffiths, it would be interesting to hear from the Under-Secretary what gave rise to the idea. There was little about it in the White Paper although the Under-Secretary might argue that the notes on clauses, which are our main guide to the Government's thinking, imply that the power will be used sparingly.

The substance of general directions could relate to how we should expect local authorities to prepare progrmames of care in the community by social services deprtments. Those are two clear examples of general directions.

That principle is enshrined in legislation and there is no way in which a Secretary of State in the present Administration could jeopadise it. Similarly, a Secretary of State in another Government, who might approach the issue in a different way, could not prejudice the provision of independent sector accommodation.
