Mrs. Mahon
: I, too, shall say a few words on family planning and about negotiating and buying contracts. That could have a disastrous effect on the family planning service. At present, the DHA provides family planning services that give women a choice. I am worried that the choice available to women whether to go to a clinic or to their GPs for contraceptive advice could be restricted under the contract arrangements, although the Government stated that women should have that choice.

Local family planning clinics are important to women, especially those who already have small children, because they are convenient.

Some local family planning clinics are held in expensive properties in sought-after locations. It is possible that managers with an eye to profit before service could consider the buildings as a means of making extra money and refer women to their GPs. Women will thus no longer have the option of going to a clinic, and some are reluctant to go to their GPs. Many women, especially the young, are worried that GPs may not provide the same confidential and comprehensive service as the clinics. Women who attend the clinics are offered eight different methods of contraception and specialist counselling.

An NHS in which the patients follow the money could have a detrimental effect on a good service.

Mr. Freeman
: The amendment proposed by the hon. Member for Peckham (Ms. Harman) would vitiate the proposed contracting system. In reply to the hon. Lady and the hon. Member for Halifax (Mrs. Mahon), we hope that DHAs will consult local GPs about their existing referral patterns and their wishes in respect of future referrals, including those to family planning clinics.

We expect that in the first year, 1991–92, the contracts will broadly reflect the existing referral pattern. Many DHAs and GPs do not know their existing referral patterns.

The hon. Member for Peckham mentioned noncontractual, emergency referrals. Under clause 4, there is an automatic procedure for emergency referrals outside the contracting system. The receiving hospital will send the bill for treatment to the appropriate DHA.

8.45 pm

Members referred, sets out the 262 procedure for such referrals. We want a sensible, quick consultation between the GP and the DHA—not the district general manager — on those ad hoc, one-off, nonemergency referrals. We want common sense to be used in granting permission for such referrals. It is not possible to specify how large the contingency fund should be in each DHA because that depends upon how well it can fulfil the GPs' contractual requirements.

The hon. Member for Peckham argued that some hospitals are not accepting patients because they are under funded. I agree with my hon. Friends the Members for Stamford and Spalding (Mr. Davies) and for Maidstone (Miss Widdecombe) that some hospitals are not accepting referrals because their budgets for particular specialties have run out; for example, the consultants may have worked so effectively and efficiently that they cannot undertake any more hip replacements for the rest of the financial year. That is the efficiency trap to which my hon. Friends referred. Our proposals for contracting and allowing money to flow from one district to another will help to resolve that problem.

We propose a fairer system of allocating funds to DHAs based on weighted capitation. Contracts will be placed in advance of that allocation, and it will take some years to achieve. When the districts have a fair, automatic allocation of money, it will be up to them to place contracts and to decide on the location of services for patients. Money will flow across district boundaries to the receiving hospitals. That will enable part of the problem of the efficiency trap to be solved. I hope that the hon. Member for Peckham will not press her amendment to a Division.

I wish to speak, first, to amendment No. 150. It seeks to ensure that the great advances made in the appropriate care of children in hospital are not thrown away in the new competitive environment created by the Bill.

Much progress has been made recently on the most effective and appropriate treatment of children who are hospitalised — not least, on how best to protect their psychological wellbeing. The trauma of a child's separation from its parents at a time of illness and anxiety is real, can cause considerable psychological damage, and can impair recovery. Consequently, it is now accepted practice to allow parents to stay with their children in hospital because it ensures the minimum disruption to their wellbeing and decreases their feeling of vulnerability. We believe that those welcome advances in treatment methods should be guaranteed, despite the switch to the profit motive, to say nothing of the competitive ethos which lies at the heart of the Bill and which is such anathema to Labour Members and the public.

Amendments Nos. 152 and 153 are slightly different. Essentially, they deal with very important staffing issues. They seek to maintain and improve upon the current employment standards in the National Health Service, thereby guaranteeing the quality of employment.

There are both moral and practical reasons why the amendments should be accepted. The National Health Service is the largest employer in western Europe, with slightly more than 1 million employees. It is a very labour intensive public service, and spends three quarters of its budget on its pay bill. It has often been said, but it bears repetition, that the National Health Service's most precious asset is the people who work in it. The amendments seek to protect that asset.

The experience of competitive tendering for cleaning, catering and domestic services offers some salutary lessons about the effects of the contracting process. The Government are fond of claiming that such privatisation saves millions of pounds, but the reality is a grim tale of cuts in wages and hours, and of loss of holiday entitlement and sick pay. Savings have been made at the expense of dedicated and committed National Health Service employees. The previous employment of one of the Ministers involved in the Bill consisted of driving health authorities into entering into such contracts wherever possible. The hon. Member for Stirling (Mr. Forsyth) knew that his work involved the exploitation of labour and the lowering of employment conditions.

We cannot continue to sanction such treatment of National Health Service staff, wherever they work. Not only is it wrong, but the looming demographic crisis means that it is also foolish and irresponsible.

The Health Service is entering a period when it will have to compete with other employers for the diminishing pool of young workers if it is to guarantee appropriate staffing levels. That means developing fair employment practices, guaranteeing equal rights for women and ethnic minorities, and so on. Women are crucial to the future of the NHS and they currently make up 80 per cent. of the entire work force. In some areas the NHS relies on ethnic minority communities for staffing and all too often such workers are found in the lowest grades with little hope of career progression. Without the equal opportunity strategies outlined in amendments Nos. 151 and 153, the widening alternative employment prospects for women and ethnic minorities in other areas of the economy are likely to lead to a more severe staffing crisis than at present.
