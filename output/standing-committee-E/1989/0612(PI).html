Clause 52
ALLOWANCE FOR ABANDONMENT EXPENDITURE RELATED TO OFFSHORE MACHINERY OR PLANT

Amendment proposed [7 June]: No. 138, in page 40, line 46, at end insert—

"(1A) Where any person carrying on a ring fence trade, in consequence of a demand pursuant to an agreement binding that person and one or more others in relation to the exploration and operation of an oil field, pays an amount to another person (whether or not the operator of the field) in circumstances such that it is unlikely that any appreciable part of the payment will later be returned to him, that payment shall, for the purposes of this section, be regarded as abandonment expenditure incurred at the time of the payment by the person first above named, where it is paid as a contribution towards the future costs of closing down of, or any part of, an oil field by way of the demolition of machinery or plant which has been brought into use for the purposes of that trade and which is or forms part of an offshore installation or a submarine pipe-line; and where this subsection applies, subsection (5) below shall apply with the necessary modifications.


(1B) Subsection (1A) above shall not apply—

  1. where the person in question has an interest in the oil field of 15 per cent or more, or
  2. where the main reason, or one of the main reasons, for the making of the demand or the payment was the obtaining of a tax advantage for that person.".—[Mr. Boateng.]

Mr. Paul Boateng

: At the end of our deliberations last week the Minister said that fiscal factors should not cause artificially early abandonment of oil fields, particularly those on the margin that are run by small operators. That assurance is welcome, but it sits oddly with the current impact of the Bill if it is not amended.

The Opposition have taken the precaution of following through a worked example of the impact of the Bill and the impact of our amendment on such a marginal oil field and have found that there is likely to be an artificially early abandonment of such oil fields, which the Minister seeks to avoid, if our amendment is not accepted.

I take as an example a small, declining oil field whose petroleum revenue tax is covered by the oil allowance, so no PRT will be paid. Its profits are such that it would not be liable for corporation tax in any of the three years before abandonment. If there were only a small, marginal commercial profit, but nothing else, before abandonment and the abandonment costs were £100 million the consequence of the Bill would be that in the first year—going backwards—the loss would be about £25,000. In the second year profit of £5 million, would be covered by the losses in the first year. In the third year the profit of about £20 million could also be covered by the first-year losses immediately before abandonment. Let us further suppose that four years before abandonment the profit was £35 million, and 170 five years before the oil field was abandoned it was £50 million. Under the old system no PRT would be paid, because of the impact of the lack of any PRT relief and no corporation tax would be payable after the loss in the third year was carried back. The system set out in the Bill would have the same result. However, if amendment No. 138 were accepted, the company could not benefit by closing the oil field a year early, despite the sacrifice in commercial profits and the loss of jobs, and of oil, that that would entail. The implications would be that one would have a tax repayment with interest of 35 per cent of £5 million, £20 million and £35 million. The company would then be able to reclaim £21 million. If the company closed down a year and a half early, it could reclaim full relief of 35 per cent on £100 million plus. That is a positive tax incentive to be anti-social because the nation would lose jobs and oil as a result of an early close-down. It would be uncommercial because the company could have made a profit during the last year or year and a half. The problem would be eliminated if the three-year claw-back were extended, but the Minister ruled out that option. Our amendment would also remove the commercial advantage that more can be claimed by relief. I hope that the Minister will consider that example and say whether the calculations are wrong. I think that they are correct. Our amendment must be accepted if we are to achieve fiscal neutrality which prevents early abandonment. Will the Minister consider that matter again?

We were disappointed by the Minister's response to the concept of a trust fund. He said, and we accepted, that payments to a trust fund had the same commerical effect as actual abandonment expenditure, but that is only true if the tax effect is the same. Were tax relief to be given on payment into a trust fund, which is one alternative or the amendment accepted, the tax effects would be different. In those circumstances, payments into a trust fund would be more advantageous if the three-year clawback limit remained. If the carry-back period is increased the two alternaties would offer tax relief and would be similar in overall effect. But it is not true, as implied by the Financial Secretary, that our amendment would have little effect, because under the current three-year carry-back rule, it would provide effective tax relief in many instances where it is not available now. That would be of special advantage to the small operator, and the nation would benefit from enhanced oil production and the retention of jobs. We would welcome, if not a change of heart, at least a response to those points from the Minister.

Clause 53
CARRYING BACK OF LOSSES REFERABLE TO ALLOWANCE FOR ABANDONMENT EXPENDITURE

Mr. Lilley
: I beg to move amendment No. 121, in page 42, line 37, leave out "subsection (2)" and insert "subsections (2) and (3)".

The Chairman
: With this we may take Government amendments Nos. 122 and 123.

Mr. Lilley
: Clause 53 provides for extended carryback of losses attributable to the new abandonment allowance. The purpose of the amendments is to allow abandonment losses to be set against any profits of the company, rather than just against trading income from the ring fence trade of North sea exploration and production.

It may help the Committee if I now explain the effect of clause 53, as amended. The clause, together with its amendments, introduces a three-year carryback of losses caused by abandonment expenditure to improve companies' ability to get effective relief quickly.

There will be no oil production revenue from the field at the time of final abandonment. The substantial costs incurred in abandoning offshore facilities may JPEG 177 mean large corporation tax losses for some companies. Under present rules those losses may be relieved against any profits of the company only of the current or previous year. In some cases, and particularly where the company had no other profitable activity at the future time of abandonment, this could be insufficient effectively to relieve the costs. To assist companies in that position, the clause extends the period for which abandonment losses may be carried back. In future such losses, and only such losses, may be carried back for a period of up to three years.

Under the clause, before the amendments, losses attributable to the new abandonment allowance could be carried back beyond the normal one year, up to a maximum of three years only against trading income from the ring fence trade. The purpose of the amendments is to allow abandonment losses to go back against any profits of the company.

The change is being made in response to representations that the restriction to ring fence trading profits of the offset of abandonment losses under the carryback extension was unnecessarily restrictive. The oil industry gives the example of a company with only one field or only one remaining field. That may well choose or be forced by its fellow-participators to fund abandonment costs by setting money aside out of current production. The effect of the clause before the amendment is that abandonment losses could be set against oil production income in the years before abandonment only, and not against investment income or gains from the funds set aside. The industry suggests that that could encourage premature field abandonment, in order to take loss relief against earlier, higher, production profits.

I hope that the amendments meet the concerns of the industry and of hon Members, particularly the hon Member for Makerfield (Mr. McCartney). In particular, I hope that it will benefit some small companies in the North sea.
