Mr. Cook
: Most of the amendments—certainly those standing in my name and in the names of my hon. Friends —have the same objectives. I assure the Under-Secretary that they have been tabled in a spirit of helpfulness, and with the anxious hope of improving the Bill. We hope for a similarly helpful and positive response.

The amendments address twin problems. Clause 3 sets out the primary duties and functions of district health authorities, and is the only clause in the Bill to do so. I invite Committee members, all of whom have read the clause in detail, to reflect on what it tells us about those duties and functions, and to put themselves in the position of a visitor from Mars who, with no knowledge of the National Health Service, is confronted with clause 3. Such a person would discover that the functions and duties of DHAs are to place contracts, because, according to clause 3, that, and nothing else, is their sole responsibility. It mentions no obligations to ensure standards or types of medical care, but merely an obligation to place contracts on behalf of the resident population. Those on the Treasury Bench think that they need stipulate only that health authorities should place contracts. That is a striking testimony to their confidence in commercial contracts as a way of securing public service.

The first problem, therefore, relates to the minimum health provision expected of DHAs. The second problem addressed by the amendments is the absence in the Bill of any reference to the core services, which were debated extensively during discussions on the White Paper. Committee members will remember that core services were presented in the White Paper as the basis on which existing specialties and provisions of hospital treatment would be protected. Given the importance that Ministers attached to core services every time they were interrogated in the House and in public, it is striking that there is nothing in either the clauses or schedules that insists that core services be provided.

JPEG 227 The amendments seek to solve both problems, by defining the functions of the DHAs and thus simultaneously imposing on them the need to preserve core services.

We have had in the House a number of exchanges and debates on the ambulance dispute. If we do not oblige district health authorities to provide local maternity services, such deliveries are likely to become more common.

I expect that there will be some cross-party agreement on this. The other addition that we seek to make, in amendment No. 205, is the paediatric service. It is important that paediatric services be provided locally. It is in the nature of the paediatric service that it caters for young children and it is natural for young children in hospital to be distressed and anxious. They are in unfamiliar surroundings. It is important to them and their parents that the parents, especially the mother, have access to them. Young children in hospital tend to have mothers who have young children at home as well. The difficulty for young mothers visiting children in hospital is compounded by the fact that during the daytime the husband is likely to have the car to travel to work. My hon. Friend the Member for Peckham (Ms. Harman) will forgive me, I am sure, for parading a stereotype, but unfortunately it is typical of many young families.

I speak with some feeling on the subject as we have had a long campaign in my constituency to bring in paediatric services from Edinburgh. One of my constituents gave birth to twins, one of whom had to be taken into intensive JPEG 229 care in Edinburgh and the other of whom was discharged. She found herself for three months with one infant at home and the other in a hospital 20 miles away by road and one and three quarter hours away by public transport.

As I go round the country and meet district general managers and discuss closures of units with them, I find that they can almost always give me, to the last 10th of a mile, the distance between the unit that is closed and the unit that replaces it. I also invariably find that when I ask them how many changes of bus are involved in that journey, they cannot tell me. Decisions about closure and the placing of contracts are made by people who frequently use cars. Their decisions affect the way in which health is delivered to people who have no easy access to private transport. That is why it is important that paediatric services also be provided locally.

The Minister may not wish to resist this contention, because when my hon. Friend the Member for Peckham effectively exposed the importance of paediatrics being a core service, the then Minister for Health the hon. and learned Member for Putney (Mr. Mellor), appeared on television that night to say how grateful he was to my hon. Friend for giving him the opportunity to say that paediatrics would be a core service. We look forward to hearing that assurance not simply on television but in Committee this morning.

I stress that it is important that all these core services be listed in the legislation as obligations on district health authorities. The truth is that the invention of the concept of core services by Ministers at the Department of Health is in itself an admission that some specialties will be at risk from the new commercial environment in which the health authorities have to operate. The idea of core services was introduced only as a means of protecting some specialties from being shared as a result of cost pressures.

The difficulty with the Minister's approach to the problem is that the Government refuse to list those core services by saying that it is up to the local health authority to decide what is needed. Therefore, by definition there cannot be a central core of specialties that are locally defined. That is like the Secretary of State for Education and Science inventing the core curriculum and then saying that the components of the core curriculum should be left to local education authorities. If the idea of core services is to be meaningful, it must be centrally recorded somewhere and laid down. The logical place to do that is in the Bill.

There is a formidable range of professional medical bodies which take the same view as that which we are advocating in the amendments. They insist that it is not sufficient to leave core services for local determination; it is essential that core services should be stipulated centrally. I shall refer briefly to the impressive number of professional medical organisations which support the contention for their own specialities. For instance, the Royal College of Midwives says that it is essential that maternity services should be a core service; the Royal College of Physicians stresses the need for a full range of care services for chronic medical illness in children and adults; the Royal College of Radiologists stresses the need for core servies in radiodiagnosis; the Royal College of Psychiatrists stresses that core services must include all aspects of psychiatric services; the conference of colleges stressed that a full range of core services must be developed to include obstetrics, paediatrics and the full integrated care of the chronic sick and disabled. Finally, the British 230 Geriatric Society, which combined consultants with people also concerned with geriatrics outside the medical profession, expressed extreme concern that only some geriatric and psychiatric hospital services will be included in the list.

Given that powerful expression of medical opinion, the sources of which were never consulted when the White Paper was written, it is important for the Committee, at the very least, to write into the Bill the core services named by Ministers, and subsequently accepted by Ministers.

My hon. Friends will understand that as a result of the debates on clause 1, when we discussed membership of the health authorities, and the refusal of the Minister to accept our amendments to clause 1, we are faced with health authorities which are not accountable to local people and which do not represent anyone. Indeed, the Under-Secretary placed particular stress on the importance of them not representing anyone. Therefore, they will have no obligation to consult local authorities about who should be on them or what they should do. They will exist merely to take a corporate view of what specialties they think they should provide to a population by which they were not elected, to which they are not accountable and which they must not represent. In those circumstances, surely it is not sufficient to leave solely to their discretion which core services should be provided for their districts, and in their hospitals.

I shall touch on a technical point which I suspect may form part of the Under-Secretary's response. Each of our amendments stress that a core service should be provided within a district. I anticipate that the Under-Secretary may respond by saying that it will be sufficient for a district health authority to provide for a core service by contract with a nearby hospital. If we can rephrase this to embrace the concept of being approximate, adjacent, or near to, I shall be willing to consider that, but I will look askance at any DHA which claims that it can get by with no maternity services anywhere within its boundary.

It is important to establish the principle that core services preferably be provided in the district or, at the very least, nearby, and that it will not be sufficient—so far as I am aware, it would be technically competent according to the Bill—for the district health authority to place a contract for the provision of a core service with a hospital that is neither within its district nor adjacent to the boundaries of its district.

I turn to the issue of principle. Hon. Members who have read debates of 1945–48 on the origins of the National Health Service will be aware that they did not turn merely on the importance of a public service rather than a private one; they turned equally on the amazingly uneven provision of hospital care in England, Wales and Scotland at the time, and on the great importance of developing a genuinely national Health Service that would provide hospital services for each community.

The concept of a district general hospital providing comprehensive care for most operations that can be undertaken without complications is one of the most important attractions and achievements of the National Health Service. It would be left at risk in a commercial environment, which leaves it open to DHAs to say that it is no longer their function to ensure provision of a local district general hospital and that their function is simply to place contracts on behalf of their resident population, not necessarily within their district.

It would mean a loss of partnership between the hospital and the district primary care services. It would represent also a loss of community pride in the service of the local hospital. Most of all, it would mean a loss of choice to patients. It is all very well for Ministers to say that patients should be free to exercise their choice of whether to be served by their local hospital or to go to one some distance away which may be able to treat them more quickly, but that choice can be exercised only if the local hospital provides the specialty. If we are to protect patient choice, we must protect specialties.

I think that all those potential losses are worth avoiding. If the Minister is determined to take the Health Service down the road to commercial contracts and a business-like commercial basis for health care provision, we do not have the vote to prevent him from imposing that on the British people. We have the right to ask him here at least to accept these minimal protections to ensure that specialties are not shed in pursuit of commercial profit.

Amendment No. 222 addresses one of the conflicts inherent in the Government's approach. On the one hand is the consumerist approach of making services more responsive to patients.
