National Health Service and Community Care Bill

clause New Clause 29
title NHS CONTRACTS (COMPENSATION)


'It shall be a condition of National Health Service contracts provided for in section 4 of this Act that any patient who suffers medical damage as a result of a medical accident shall be entitled to compensation without need to prove negligence.'.—[Ms. Harman.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

4.30 pm

Ms. Harriet Harman (Peckham): I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

Every year, the NHS treats millions of patients, most of whom are satisfied with their treatment and care. But mistakes can and do happen. No organisation or group of professionals is immune from making mistakes. When things go wrong in health care, the consequences can be very serious. Mistakes can mean discomfort, disability— sometimes profound disability—and death. When things go wrong, compensation should be paid, and paid promptly. But that does not happen in the present system, which is a cruel lottery. Most people receive no compensation, and those who do often have to endure court battles and years of delay.

The problem was highlighted recently by the case of haemophiliacs who had contracted the HIV virus through contaminated blood products. They were made an exception to the current unjust system. The Government recognised that they were an exception not because their problem was exceptional—thousands of people every year suffer the injustice of the compensation system—but because over 1,000 of them suffered from the same condition: the NHS had inflicted the same damage on all of them. They formed a group and started a public campaign which led to their being made an exception. We pressed the Government to make an exception and warmly applauded the fact that compensation was awarded. But the injustice suffered by thousands of other people remains. The fact that haemophiliacs were made an exception points to the further injustice suffered by people who are not haemophiliacs who contracted the HIV virus through contaminated blood products.

A woman in the west midlands contracted the HIV virus from a blood transfusion after she had a baby. She did not get any compensation. A constituent—not a haemophiliac—of my hon. Friend the Member for Livingston (Mr. Cook) contracted the HIV virus through contaminated blood products in the NHS and was not awarded compensation under the scheme.

Despite the fact that an exception was made for haemophiliacs, the injustice remains for thousands of other people. The only difference is that they do not Col. 1304 necessarily share the same condition, neither have they all been victims of the same medical accident. That does not necessarily mean that their needs are less. There is a strong case for reform of the compensation system and the Government should accept the thrust of the new clause.

The case for reform is based on a number of arguments. There is the hurdle of having to prove negligence before getting compensation. That excludes many people who might have a case from getting compensation. It is difficult to prove that a doctor has been negligent. Doctors themselves cannot be sure that the unforeseen outcome or accident to their patient is the result of negligence or just something that happens from time to time. Doctors recognise the unfairness of the system. They often want their patients to be compensated but having to prove negligence has two consequences. First, it means that doctors always defend cases because their professional reputation hangs on the negligence claim. Secondly, the insurance companies make doctors fight claims even if they are not keen to do so. Having to prove negligence is too high a hurdle to jump.

Access to the courts is too difficult. Legal aid eligibility limits have been systematically eroded, so fewer people are entitled to legal aid. Many people who might be able to prove negligence and who might have a good case, even under the current system, do not get to court because they do not qualify for legal aid; nor do they have enough money to finance a court action.

There is a risk for people who have the resources to finance a court action but who are not eligible for legal aid. Just when their need for resources is at its greatest, because of the disability or problem caused by the medical accident, they are asked to risk thousands, often hundreds of thousands, of pounds in court fees to prove their claim for negligence before any payment is made. I remind the Committee that people who do not qualify for legal aid have to pay their own legal costs—in my opinion, the fees charged by solicitors and barristers for litigation is scandalous—and run the risk of paying the full legal costs of the health authority and the doctor. The very time when their need for resources is at its greatest is the time when they have to take a huge financial gamble.

I recall a case in which the parents were considering suing in respect of a child who had suffered brain damage as a result of a medical accident. They said, "Do we spend our money on legal fees and risk a second mortgage on our house, or even losing our house, to pay for the court case to get our son his rights? Or do we use the money for the extra services that he now needs?" It is a terrible gamble.

The third argument in our case for reform concerns court delays. Even if people are eligible for legal aid, or if they are prepared to risk hundreds of thousands of pounds to go to court, they do not necessarily get the money when they need it; they may get it many years later. The delays in court procedures are scandalous. Delays of 10 or 11 years are not unusual before people are paid the compensation to which they are entitled.

I shall give a couple of examples. In one case, a girl of 10, whose brain was damaged in a mistake by hospital doctors, was awarded damages of £1 million. Donna— that was her name—was severely brain-damaged at the age of two, after doctors wrongly diagnosed diabetes and administered insulin. She was awarded damages after eight years, and during that time the parents experienced the anxiety of taking litigation and had to provide for her needs without adequate resources. It was reported that Image Col. 1305

"a young girl was awarded £475,000 damages in the High Court yesterday for a hospital error which left her severely handicapped. Tanya Amos, aged 11, was brain damaged after suffering oxygen deprivation during her birth."

It took her 11 years to get the compensation to which she was entitled. Even if one can get access to the courts and prove negligence, the delays are unacceptable and an integral part of the compensation system.

The compensation system is unjust and the cost is spiralling. As the courts are justifiably making high awards to victims of medical accidents, insurance costs are increasing. The figures that I have received in response to parliamentary questions show that the average cost of a hospital doctor's insurance was £135 in 1982. By 1989, it had increased to £1,350. We are getting the worst of all possible worlds. Health authorities, and thus the Government, have to pay enormous insurance bills, yet few victims get compensation, and then only after years of struggle through the courts.

There is a consensus among the professions, the Patients Association and others, on the need for change. The King's Fund Institute report entitled "Medical Negligence", written by Dr. Chris Ham and published in 1988 states:

"First, the procedure is lengthy and expensive. Second, claimants often find it difficult to obtain the services of a solicitor with relevant expertise. Third, the legal process is adversarial and causes those involved to close ranks. Consequently, patients and relatives are often not given adequate explanation or apology when things go wrong, and doctors may be distressed by the apparent hostility of patients".

The Patients Association and the British Medical Association believe that the time has come to change the compensation system. David Bolt, who chaired the no-fault compensation working party of the British Medical Association, said:

"The medical profession hates the present arrangements for pursuing medical negligence claims."

He is not satisfied with the situation, even for the patients who succeeded with their claims.

He went on:

"we feel very sad about the tiny minority of patients who have a real prospect of obtaining compensation through the courts … they have a long and very bitter struggle before them, and have to proceed on the basis that the individual who looked after them was careless in providing their care".

The Government's arguments against change come down to the assertion that it would cost too much. They can sustain that argument only if they know how much the current system costs and how much a new system would cost. It is clear from my parliamentary questions to successive Ministers for Health that the Government do not know the cost of the current system. The cost can be established only if one adds up all the in-court settlements, all the out-of-court settlements, the cost of the in-house RHA and DHA legal advice, the legal aid bills, the costs of people who paid their own solicitors and barristers, the cost in court time and the cost of the insurance bill. That is the cost of the current unfair system. The Government have first to establish the cost of the current system and then the cost of the new system. The cost of a no-fault compensation system would be as high as the tariffs that are laid down by the Government for paying out for various disabilities. The Government could have an expensive system if they paid huge awards to everybody, or Col. 1306 they could have a cheap system that paid out low awards. It does not wash to say that a new system would be too expensive, if there is nothing to compare it with.

The current system should be replaced with a no-fault compensation scheme in which the victim of a medical accident would have to show damage and would be given compensation according to a tariff. Redress through the courts would have to remain as an ultimate safeguard, but for practical purposes it would be used only in rare cases that no-fault compensation schemes could not deal with.

We should also improve professional disciplinary procedures. It is not clear whether the threat of a negligence action has any noticeable disciplinary effect on doctors. It is not clear from any studies whether a doctor's clinical decision is affected by his having to look over his shoulder and wonder whether he might end up in court. Some studies show that the fear of a negligence action affects the way in which a doctor practises, while other studies show that it does not necessarily do so. It is not easy to say that the current negligence-based adversarial system acts as a disciplinary safeguard. However, to give the benefit of the doubt, we should insist that a no-fault compensation system should have improved disciplinary procedures. It sometimes seems to us that doctors are found guilty of misconduct, or of any lapse of professional discipline, only if fraud or sex are involved. It is very rare for the GMC to discipline a doctor for failing to carry out his professional duties.

We have tabled the new clause to get the Government to answer questions about cost. We shall not listen to the argument that it is too expensive unless they tell us how much the current system costs, and make a sophisticated analysis of the cost of an alternative system.

We also want the Government to accept that there is a problem. We know that they have conceded that, because they have made an exception for haemophiliacs. The Government have responded to a public campaign on behalf of haemophiliacs who have contracted HIV through contaminated blood products. We ask the Government to respond also to all those people who are suffering just as greatly but were not covered by that campaign.

I hope that the Minister offers some flexibility in his response to the debate.
