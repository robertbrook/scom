The Chairman
: Before I call the Minister to move the sittings motion, I have some announcements to make.

Hon. Members who served on last year's Committee will remember the difficulties that we experienced in the heat when we moved to a small and crowded room. I took extensive soundings during the past few weeks, and the general consensus was that it would be better to conduct our proceedings in this Room. That is not a totally satisfactory answer, but any alternative would be worse. There are extra desks—

Hon. Members
: Where?

The Chairman
: There are extra desks from last year. I thank the staff who organised the Room. Despite last year's announcement, the traditional green boxes are available. I hope that hon. Members will take one and use them for their Committee papers. This may be the last year that they are provided.

I want to say a word about Divisions. When a Division is called in this Committee, it appears on the annunciator. That does not happen in other Committees. I shall allow a maximum of four minutes for the Division after the announcement has appeared on the annunciator. If all hon. Members are present before four minutes have passed, we shall take the Division. As for the dinner break, I shall generally suspend the sitting at about 7 o'clock, or at some convenient time thereafter, for 90 minutes. If there is a Division in the Chamber, 15 minutes will be added to the break for each one, If Divisions are called while the Committee is sitting, I shall suspend proceedings for 15 minutes.

Copies of the Ways and Means resolutions, on which the Bill is founded, are on the Table.

Adequate notice of amendments must be given. I shall only select manuscripts or starred amendments in exceptional circumstances. Amendments Nos. 8 and 9 do not appear on today's selection list for that reason. Green pads are available to all hon. Members and in the Public Bill Office for the tabling of amendments. The printers have asked me to request hon. Members to submit their amendments of those forms. That is not obligatory, but it will help the printers and may prevent the errors that sometimes appear on Order Papers.

As a general rule, I shall chair the Committee on Tuesdays and Mr. Coleman will do so on Thursdays. Mr. Coleman is not available this evening and, therefore, I shall take the Chair for the first hour and Mr. Leadbitter will chair the rest of today's sitting. I am grateful to him for doing so. Dr. John Marek
(Wrexham)
: I hope that we can have a short debate on clause 1 and that the Economic Secretary will be able to enlighten us.

There is a problem with the Scottish whisky industry. Exports of whisky are high and, therefore the industry is important in view of Britain's present catastrophic balance of payments. It would be right to spend time on this clause and ensure that the whisky industry will not suffer as a result of the proposed changes. It would not be right to mention the Scottish whisky industry without regard to the other effects that it may have on people such as the health effects. I am ever mindful of the problem that drinking alcohol can become an addictive habit and the Committee would not wish to encourage that, but I wish to dwell on the 10 per cent. increase on spirits, the increase on beer and wine was only 7 per cent. What was the rationale behind adding a further 3 per cent. on spirits? I do not say that that increase is wrong nor do I criticise the Government for imposing it. However, I should like to know why the change was made.

I wish to refer to two other small problems. First, Britain is in the anomalous position of being the only Common Market country to discriminate against home-produce spirits in favour of imported wines and other beverages, such as sherries. The reason is that, unlike most other drinks, whisky is not taxed according to alcohol content. As a result, the unit of alcohol is more expensive than some imports, which consequently have an advantage. An efficient industry seeking to export most of its products ought to receive fair play from the Government. I should like to know if the Economic Secretary has any plans to change the method of taxing spirits so that whisky is not at a disadvantage compared with imported alcoholic drinks.

Secondly, at present the Government do not allow a statutory maturation allowance for stocks of maturing whisky which are distilled within the legal three-year minimum. Such an allowance would help the industry. It might be that it would be too great a help, but other countries look after their equivalent industries a great deal more than we look after ours. The whisky industry has been in a bouyant mood and has experienced no special problems, but that may change. If so, I hope that the Economic Secretary will say whether the Government intend to ensure that our industry enjoys equal treatment with other countries in respect of a maturation allowance, without which the industry would suffer if it went into the doldrums.

Member for Blaby (Mr. Lawson). He was then a shadow Opposition spokesman. After all those years no progress has been made on what seems to be an obvious move. Over the years Chancellors and Treasury Ministers have allowed excise duties to fall behind the rate of inflation, so that spirit duties have been substantially reduced. Since 1985 duties on beer and wine have been reduced in real terms every year except 1988, so at some time, some Chancellor is bound to raise them. We can guess why that has been done this year; it is because the Government do not want to raise duties next year. Duties are being uprated now so that the decks are cleared for no increases in a possible election year. I shall be surprised if the Chancellor upratres any duties next year.

8 However, the policy makes no sense, because it has the effect of reducing the real price of alcohol over substantial periods. I understand that it is part of the Government's policy to make the price of alcohol a factor in deterring excess consumption. Evidence shows that if the price of alcohol falls, consumption increases. Therefore, it makes no sense if the real price falls year after year.

Incidentally, that is also an argument against harmonisation towards some levels that prevail in some other Community countries. I have argued in previous years that harmonisation of excise duties is not necessary in achieving a single market. The rate of tax, or duty, is applied uniformly to all products, regardless of their country of origin, at the point of sale. As the amount of cross-border traffic is bound to be less in island countries than in other parts of Europe, it is a relatively small problem for us and our neighbours if our rates are out of line with theirs. I, and others, have had that argument with Lord Cockfield over the years and I place it on the record now. I believe strongly in the creation of the single European market but I do not believe that harmonisation is essential to it.

I hope that, through discussion with other members of the Community, we can arrive at a common view about the role that excise duty should play in policy on alcohol. It is an important issue. The Government have had for some time an inter-ministerial group that studies alcohol problems, yet it seems to have had no influence on the Chancellor last year and a delayed influence this year.
