Mr. Alun Michael(Cardiff, South and Penarth):On a point of order, Dame Janet. I seek your advice. In the list of members of the Committee, my name is written as Welsh surnames were centuries ago: my Christian name is given as my surname. I should be grateful if that could be corrected.

I seek your advice, Dame Janet, on a more serious matter relating to Wales and the position of the Secretary of State for Wales in the Bill. There has been confusion before: you will recall a previous Bill under your chairmanship, Dame Janet, when I raised a similar point. In the Bill, the Health Service in Wales is subsumed within English procedures and there is likely to be confusion about the position and functions of the Secretary of State for Wales. I seek your guidance because the Health Service is precious to the people of Wales and confusion will cause difficulty and lead to objections. Its position in the Bill is not clear. I raise the matter now because the point cannot be made effectively during discussion of the Bill.

The Chairman:Strictly, that is not a point of order for the Chairman. I suggest that when it is appropriate - during the consideration of clauses or amendments - the Gentleman raises the matter at the first convenient moment. It can then be disposed of for the rest of the Bill. Beyond that, as Chairman, I cannot help.

Mr. Robin Cook(Livingston): I begin by saying how delighted I am that we are to spend the next two months together, Dame Janet.

The Chairman:Not alone.

Mr. Cook:I shall be chaperoned. I shall be pleased to rise to speak when you call me, Dame Janet, whichever way round you put my name. I hope that it will assist in future contretemps when I say that I am relieved that we are to be guided through our proceedings by your wisdom and tact.

The motion is one that would not have been put to the first sitting of a Committee when you and I first came into the House, Dame Janet. When I arrived in the House 15 years ago, it was usual for the sittings motion to prescribe morning sittings only. In extremis, after the passage of time, there was Col. 4 a second sittings motion prescribing an afternoon sitting. During this Government's period of office, that custom and practice has changed, although they began by following it. The interval between the two motions shortened, and in the past two or three years there has been a mere two-week formality before the Government reach for the second sittings motion. We have now arrived at the logical extension of that process: at the Committee's first sitting, we are confronted with a sittings motion which rolls into one the two sittings motions and obliges us to have afternoon sittings from the first week after the Christmas recess.

There are other changes, with which my hon. Friends will be familiar. I was a Member of Parliament at the time of the Labour Government, and you will recall, Dame Janet, because it was an experience that you shared, that it was regarded in those days as a mark of failure for a Minister and a Government to have to impose a guillotine to get a Bill through the House. These days, any pretext will do to reach for a guillotine; quite the reverse. Nowadays, Ministers use any pretext to impose a guillotine, and far from regarding it as a mark of failure, they regard it on the contrary as an irritation to be forced to permit scrutiny of a Bill through to its end.

Mr. Cook:I am guided by your advice, Dame Janet. I will make only a passing reference to it - indeed, by now I should have passed off it - but may I continue to make my point? If Ministers have it in mind at some future date to present us with a timetable motion, they will receive no assistance from Opposition members of the committee in making the case for that guillotine. I want to make it clear that we shall subject the Bill to rigorous scrutiny, but we shall do so without wasting time and without filibuster, and I can tell the Minister of State that she will get the motion within about an hour. We are anxious to be able to debate the Bill to its end without guillotine, and in particular, to examine the important clauses on community care which are tucked at the back of the Bill.

It may assist the Minister and her hon. Friends to know the strategy that we propose to adopt during the Bill's proceedings. We regard the Bill as a measure of fundamental importance, as, I am sure, you do, Dame Janet. It represents the most profound change in the National Health Service since 1948. It will transform a social service that is there to treat need into a business enterprise that will be chasing trade. It is put forward by a Government whose only political Image Col. 5 value is the value for money, and who are so bound by ideology that the only way that they see of securing value for money is by introducing competition. Conservative Members are incapable of understanding why so many people in the Health Service reject their proposals, but it is because they understand that a combination of co-operation and teamwork is a better way of serving the patient than competition against one another.

We attach great importance to the Bill. We shall scrutinise it rigorously clause by clause, but I want to signal at this preliminary stage that our strategy will focus on six key issues by which we shall test Ministers' willingness to listen to complaints from outside the House and inside the Committee. First, we shall press that no hospital shall be permitted to enter into a self-governing trust and leave the local NHS authority without a ballot of local people. We shall propose, first, a ballot of the patients it serves - the Government tell us, after all, that patients must come first.

There is enormous interest in the Bill on the part of those outside, including the organisations that work in, and represent the people who work in, the Health Service. Ministers are required to divest themselves of their pecuniary interests, especially shareholding interests, when they are appointed. I accuse no one of impropriety.
