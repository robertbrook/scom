New clause 53
SUPPLY OF LABOUR OR SERVICES

xxx.—REGISTRATION OF CONTRACTS FOR SERVICES

(1) Any person who supplies labour or services for reward to another MAY IN ACCORDANCE with the provisions herein described at subsections (2) (3) and (4) register a memorandum of Contract for Services Agreement.

(2) A Memorandum of Contract for Services Agreement shall be brought about in the following manner. A form SE obtainable from the Department of Employment shall be duly completed and signed by the parties to be charged therewith. Upon signature and on or before the date shown upon the memorandum the supplier of service or labour shall cause to be sent, either by recorded Delivery or Registered Post, authorised copies of the said memorandum to the Inspector of Taxes exercising jurisdiction over the income of the supplier that will arise as a result of the provision of such labour or services, and also authorised copies of the said memorandum to that office of the Department of Social Security which administers Class 2 and 4 National Insurance contributions in respect of the supplier of such labour or services.

(3) A Memorandum of Contract for Services Agreement, shall state the name and address of both the supplier of labour or services and the person or party to whom they are supplied. The Memorandum shall state a brief description of the labour or services to be granted therefore. The issue of one Memorandum shall not preclude the issue of several other simultaneous or subsequent memoranda.

(4) Any material change, variation, amendment or extension to the registered contract for services that may arise during its period of performance shall be endorsed on the Memorandum of Contract for Services Agreement, initialled and dated by the parties to be charged therewith, and on or before the date of such amendment copies of the same shall be sent to the parties above-mentioned in subsection (2) hereof.

(5) No person shall be subject to tax under Schedule E of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1970 (or any Act repealing the said Act), nor shall any person be liable to make National Insurance contributions (either as a primary or secondary contributor under Class 1 National Insurance contributions, in accordance with the provisions of the Social Security Act 1973, nor shall any person be entitled to be treated as an "employee" nor as working under a "contract of service" for any purpose whatsoever, where the registration of a Memorandum of Contract for Services Agreement has been duly undertaken in accordance with the provisions of this Act, except in the following circumstances.

(6) Notwithstanding the appropriate registration of a memorandum (or any amendment thereto) in accordance with the provisions of this Act, an Inspector of Taxes, or the Secretary of State for Social Security, may (provided subsection (7) applies) cause an inquiry to be instituted by either the General Commissioners for the Inland Revenue, or a duly appointed Inspector acting on behalf of the Secretary of State for Social Security, as the case may be, for the purpose of determining whether the parties ought to be treated as bound by a contract of employment, or whether the supplier of labour or services ought to be otherwise taxable under Schedule E of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1970, or whether the parties ought otherwise be liable to make (primary and secondary) contributions under Class 1 in accordance with the provisions of the Social Security Act 1973.

(7) No enquiry shall be instituted under subsection (6) above, or at all, unless the parties have been given written notice of the intention to institute such an inquiry, within SUE calendar months of the date of registration of a memorandum of Contract for Services Agreement (or any variation thereto).

(8) In any such inquiry that arises by virtue of subsections (6) or (7) above, the existence of a duly registered memorandum (or any variation etc, thereto) shall be conclusive evidence that the parties are bound by a contract for services, unless or until the inquiring tribunal is reasonably satisfied that there is admissible evidence of at least five or more of the following factors: 538 JPEG 539

  1. that there is a mutual and continuing contractual obligation on the parties to provide work and the provision of labour and services, that arises outside of and not by virtue of the written terms of the memorandum of contract for services.
  2. that the supplier of labour or services is entitled to holiday or sick pay or maternity leave.
  3. that the supplier of labour or services is entitled to be reimbursed his authorised "out of pocket" expenses by the hirer of such labour or services.
  4. that the supplier of labour or services is not in business on his own account and works wholly under the direction and control of the hirer of labour or services.
  5. that the supplier of labour or services provides the name to only one hirer of the same in the course of any year in which his income tax falls to be assessed or his national insurance contributions are required to be regulated.
  6. that the contract makes provision for termination by notice
  7. that the supplier of labour or services invested only such labour and services in the hirer's undertaking, and did not otherwise stand to gain or lose from the undertaking organised by the hirer of such labour or services.
  8. that the supplier of labour or services is entitled to benefit from any staff consultative procedures organised by the hirer or labour or services,
  9. that the contract makers provision for a pension to be granted to the supplier of labour or services.
  10. that the supplier of labour or services receives clothing or tools under the contract.
  11. that the supplier of labour or services is contractually restrained from working for others during the currency of the contract.

(9) Nothing in this Act shall require a tribunal to determine that a contract of employment exists, even where satisfied that five or more grounds exist amongst those specified in subsection (8) above, but if so satisfied, a tribunal shall determine the proper contractual status of the parties according to the principles of law which would have applied but for the existence of this Act.

(10) Any finding of a tribunal instituted under this Act shall be subject to appeal or judicial review, on a matter of law to the Court of Appeal or the High Court as the case may be, and thence to the House of Lords, with leave either of that House or the Court of Appeal. An appeal shall likewise lie on a mixed question of fact and law in any case where the appellate court considers that an inferior tribunal has given an inappropriate amount of weight to any criteria which the tribunal has used in determining that contractual status of the parties.

(11) Any finding by any tribunal which becomes a final determination of the contractual status of the parties shall not have retrospective effect to the date of such finding unless the court of tribunal orders otherwise on the grounds that the parties, or either of them have acted in bad faith, and then only in respect of that party so acting.'.—[Mr. Watts.]

My new clause seeks to resolve a serious dilemma for the Government. One of the major successes of Government policy over the past 11 years has been the substantial growth of self-employment. It is one of the Government's major triumphs. Curiously, however, the Inland Revenue seems to be pulling in the opposite direction.

We debated actors' problems of reclassification from schedule D to E earlier. Sadly, actors are only one profession among many that have been recategorised. Without amending the law, a long line of professions will be similarly recategorised.

The Inland Revenue's instinct is to treat everyone as an employee to be taxed under schedule E. There may be an element of thinking that the grass is greener on the other side, notwithstanding the disadvantages of self-employed status in terms of lack of security and of entitlement to unemployment benefit. However, we saw in the case of actors that that is not necessarily the case.

New clause 53 is lengthy. Hon. Members on both sides of the Committee will be glad to hear that I do not intend to read through it. It is a lengthy means of achieving a 540 fairly simple and necessary end. It provides the means of establishing self-employed status. A supplier of labour or services for reward may register a contract for sevices agreement with the Inland Revenue and the Department of Social Security. Such registration would entitle the supplier to be treated as self-employed, be taxed under schedule D, and pay class 2 and class 4 national insurance contributions.

If it were merely a matter of self-determination, such a scheme would be open to abuse. A major part of the new clause therefore makes provision within six months of registration for an inspector of taxes to cause an inquiry to be conducted by the general commissioners, and for the Secretary of State for Social Security to appoint an inspector to consider the appropriate tax schedules and classes of national insurance contributions to be paid.

On the one hand, there is a fairly simple mechanism for the supplier of services or labour to make his claim to be self-employed. On the other hand, the Inland Revenue and the Department of Social Security have the right to challenge claims that they consider ill founded.

Subsection (8) seeks to provide an objective test of self-employed status on the basis of 11 typical characteristics of employment. It requires the general commissioners to find that at least five of those characteristics have been satisfied before they can conclude that the person has the appropriate status.

JPEG 541 The new clause would provide the means for a self-employed person to establish his status without being subject to a capricious decision by a tax inspector who is taxed under schedule E. It would also establish an objective basis for the general commissioners to determine, on an application by the Inland Revenue, whether the claim for self-employed status is valid. Such a procedure would be beneficial to the further growth of self-employment which has already made such a major contribution to the revitalisation of the British economy. It can continue to make a major contribution to the further development of the economy if the obstacles to self-employment are removed by measures such as this.

This new clause deals with the important matter of low-cost rural housing. In the Finance Act 1989, section 24A was introduced into the Inheritance Tax Act 1984, extending the exemption to charities already given in that Act to registered housing associations to enable them to provide low-cost housing in rural areas.

This has an important social objective. As a result of changes made last year, landowners are not liable to tax if they give the freehold to housing associations. However, uncertainties exist if they provide housing associations with a long lease.

Hon. Members ask why the landowners would want to do that, and why they do not give up the entire interest in the land. The answer is that often it will be convenient to preserve the character of the use of the land and to have a long lease—say, 999 years. To all intents and purposes that is no different from a freehold but has the advantage of being able to incorporate more easily, or to facilitate the incorporation of restricted covenants, which will ensure that the land is held for the use for which it is designated.

There are uncertainties over the matter, and these need to be resolved if we are to succeed in developing a series of low-cost projects on cheap land. Mr. Wallace
:I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

Although the clause is long and apparently complicated, I shall try to go through it as briefly as possible in the spirit of this evening's proceedings. It is common ground in the Committee that the concept of employee share ownership should be encouraged. In the interest of brevity, I shall not go back to the establishment of employee profit sharing schemes through the Lib-Lab pact, but it is to the Government's credit that they have sought to build on that initiative.

In last year's Finance Act, efforts were made to establish statutory employee share ownership trusts. They differed from previous trusts because the courts determined whether they qualified for corporation tax relief. When the Government promoted statutory employee share ownership plans last year, they established a set of criteria. If donations by companies to trusts met those criteria companies were automatically eligible for tax relief. A recent article is the Law Society's Gazette said that that measure converted a possiblity of tax relief into a certainty. The measure was introduced with the best intentions, but unfortunately they have not been fulfilled. Since the measure was put on to the statute book, no statutory ESOP has been created.

The new clause would remove some of the restrictive conditions attached last year to the creation of statutory 548 ESOPs and is a challenge to the Minister that he should be prepared to accept. The new clause would make it easier for companies to create ESOPs. Subsection (1) would delete the words—

"resident in the United Kingdom".

The purpose is to ensure that employees of companies of Nissan UK, Ford Motor Company and IBM United Kingdom are entitled to share in the benefits of ESOPs, which is not possible now because of the wording of the Finance Act 1989.

The new clause will cover a large number of employees, as it is wrong in principle to deny them the benefits that other employees in the United Kingdom are able to enjoy. If the Financial Secretary is not prepared to accept the new clause, he should provide us with some rationale as to why these employees should be denied the benefit of participation in employee share ownership plans.

Paragraph (2) deals with two possible circumstances relating to the trustees of an employee share ownership trust. The first is set out in new subparagraph (2B) of schedule 5 to the Finance Act 1989, which provides for a corporate trust that would be the subsidiary of the company that funds the ESOP.
