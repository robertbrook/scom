About 90 per cent.—and I quoted the precise percentage—were prepared to travel for quicker and better surgery; there were two separate questions. I should be happy to provide the hon. Gentleman with the reference because when he discusses the subject in the Public Accounts Committee he may find the statistics helpful. I shall write to him.

I shall move on to deal with the main thrust of the comments made by the hon. Member for Livingston on amendment No. 189.

The hon. Gentleman is entitled to use his own 226 definition of core services, but the truth is that there is a misunderstanding, not between the hon. Gentleman and myself—he has spent much time researching the issues—but within the Committee as to what the Government mean by the term "core services." I am happy to set the record straight. The hon. Gentleman knows that I believe in being frank to the point sometimes of embarrassing myself but I do not believe that the original way in which we presented our proposals a year ago was as clear as we would have wished. "Core services" relate only to NHS trusts. In the White Paper, the word "core" has no meaning outside such trusts. The hon. Member for Livingston used the word broadly. We mean the services that must be provided by NHS trusts, because local access is needed. Therefore, it is a restriction on the ability of NHS trust hospitals to change, vary or reduce the services offered.

I agree with my hon. Friend the Member for Ealing, Acton (Sir G. Young) that there is anxiety about the withdrawal of services, in both trust and directly-funded NHS hospitals as a result of the contract system, which would put patients at risk.

I shall amplify the six key principals which where set out in the White Paper and which relate to the argument of the hon. Member for Livingston on the provision of local services.

First, I agree with my hon. Friend the Member for Harlow (Mr. Hayes) that the National Health Service Act 1977 places upon the Secretary of State the obligation to provide a comprehensive range of health services. The Secretary of State has delegated that to DHAs using regulations under section 13 of that Act. We shall lay similar regulations under the negative resolution procedure after the Bill's enactment, setting out further duties on DHAs to provide a comprehensive range of services. It was a Labour Government who introduced the 1977 Act, and included the ability to make regulations.

Secondly, under our proposals the DHAs will be obliged to serve all those who live within their catchment area. The hon. Member for Ynys Môn (Mr Jones) rightly said that the 1977 Act referred to services rather than the residents within a district. We intend to change that obligation so that it relates to all residents.

Thirdly, some services undeniably need local access for residents in the authorities' catchment areas. But we do not define local access as access within a DHA's boundary, as the hon. Member for Halifax (Mrs. Mahon) rightly said. In some places such as in London's spatially small districts local access can be discharged by contiguous authorities.

In Islington and Bloomsbury, patients would have to travel only a few miles. Opposition Members rightly said that the maternity service needs to be provided locally for obvious reasons. However some services, such as heart and liver transplants, do not require local access as provision clearly may be a considerable distance away.

Fourthly, accident and emergency services must be available to all. including foreigners. I shall say later how we intend to enshrine that obligation in regulations.

JPEG 227 Fifthly, NHS trusts that are also hospitals may have to be limited in their ability to eliminate or reduce the provision of certain services if guaranteed local access is required. We shall discuss the matter more fully on later clauses, but the Bill, and regulations following it, will give the Secretary of State ultimate power to determine the minimum range of core services that must be provided.

Sixthly, decisions about services that require local access and the initial definition of core services in and for a district health authority must be made by the DHA. They should not be a matter for the Secretary of State or for primary legislation, because circumstances vary across the country.

Mr. Freeman
: I have several more comments to make. I shall understand if the Member for Livingston wishes to press the amendment to a Division.

The hon. Member for Wakefield (Mr. Hinchliffe) and the hon. Member for Livingston are confused about the responsibilities of DHAs. A DHA has a responsibility to ensure that health care needs of its resident population are assessed and met through the contracting mechanism by directly-managed hospitals, NHS trusts, and perhaps hospitals outside the district boundary. There is a difference between that responsibility, which must be discharged, and the provision of services. We shall help those involved to discharge their responsibility through regulations that will be laid in due course under the 1977 legislation. It would be wrong to be too prescriptive and to define the responsibilities in terms of services as the Member for Livingston would wish. That is the clear difference between us.

I shall refer briefly to the duties of the DHAs. They have a responsibility to plan for the comprehensive health care of their residents. We have set in train development work, centrally funded by the Department of Health, to enable 11 DHAs to begin the process of learning how they should discharge that new JPEG 229 function from April 1991. The districts are Newcastle, Hull, North Derbyshire, Cambridge, North Hertfordshire, Parkside, Lewisham and North Southwark, Maidstone, Wandsworth, West Dorset and the Wirral. They represent a broad geographic and demographic range of districts. They are beginning the process of learning how to discharge the new responsibility. We hope that the lessons learnt over the coming months will be available to all DHAs.

Several hon. Gentlemen raised the accident and emergency issue. I shall explain how the access of non-residents to accident, emergency and inpatient services will be costed. For accident and emergency services we shall require the DHA to provide an accident and emergency service for all comers, whether they are resident in this country or abroad. That service will be funded as a whole by the local DHA.

If accident and emergency patients have to be admitted to a ward, the Secretary of State will prescribe the rates of charge by the inpatient wards for the services rendered to the district in which the patient is usually resident. That will be laid in regulations or in an administrative circular. If the patient is resident in this country, standard costs will be charged to the district and paid automatically. If the patient is resident abroad, the Scretary of State for Health will discharge that financial obligation direct to the hospital providing the service.

The regulations laid under the 1977 legislation will state that accident and emergency services are to be provided to all comers. The regulations will also change the basis of obligation from services within a district boundary to an obligation on the health authority to care for the health needs of its residents. That is a sharp change in function which requires a change in the regulations.

In relation to core services, under schedule 2, the Secretary of State will be able to direct that they must remain within a trust.

The GP will know where the contract for that service has been placed and if the patient's address is within the hospital's area, that is where he will go. The decision about where treatment will be given will not be taken by the GP or the DHA managers acting in the patient's interests. Having diagnosed the problem, the doctor will look at the VDU to find the patient's address and will then know where the patient has to go, on the basis of the DHA having negotiated a contract.

The Government did not dream up the system all by themselves. It operates in America and is called a health maintenance organisation. A group of people live in an area that is equivalent to a DHA area and have a family doctor in the organisation. When the doctor decides that a patient needs to be referred on for treatment, the only place he can send him is wherever the health maintenance organisation has negotiated a contract. Doctors who work in health maintenance organisations say that the managers of the organisation do not listen when they tell them where they think treatment is good. The managers send patients to wherever they can get a good financial deal.
