Mrs. Beckett
:This is the first two sets of probing amendments that seek to restrict the scope of the clause, which aims to abolish stamp duty on the transfer of securities. It is argued that stamp duty should be abolished because the transfer of securities in London would be rendered potentially uncompetitive compared with the regime in other countries and on other exchanges. I say "potentially uncompetitive" because many suggested changes to regimes and exchanges in other countries have not yet occurred.

The same argument was used when the Finance Act 1988 abolished capital duty. We were told that that was necessary to preserve the competitiveness of London as an exchange, because our competitor countries would be seeking to abolish such duties. But from examining a recent summary, I believe that the majority of our competitor countries retain some form of capital duty. So although we are told that stamp duty or the equivalent tax will be abolished elsewhere, that has not yet happened.

388 Many of the rest of the G7 countries still have the equivalent of stamp duty or other duties on share transactions. They have some form of direct tax on the transfer, purchase and sale of securities. Such duties are varied and levied at different rates. There is a case for suggesting that on the whole, where comparable duties arise elsewhere, they are often charged at lower rates. That does not alter the fact that those duties exist and seem likely to continue. The only place where the possibility of abolition is known to have arisen is Germany. However, I have been unable to confirm that abolition is likely to happen in the near future anywhere else.

In addition to whether other countries are abolishing stamp duty, there is the question of whether retaining it would render London uncompetitive. A report earlier this month suggested that, according to pension fund indicators from Phillips and Drew Fund Management Ltd, London is the cheapest stock market in the world. Our costs are lower than those in other major financial centres and in some respects, the difference is quite substantial. The Government will probably argue that London will have an even greater price advantage if stamp duty is abolished, as proposed. Nevertheless, the resulting reduction of costs may be partly offset by other changes. The question remains whether such is necessary to ensure London's competitiveness.

If it is argued that international competitiveness requires the abolition of stamp duty, there may be the potential for ensuring that we do not lose our international competitiveness, without also losing all the revenue that presently accrues from stamp duty, by introducing a provision similar to the amendments in this group which would restrict abolition to quoted shares. I recognise that that would be less simple than wholesale abolition. Can the Minister identify the different revenue consequences of that type of abolition an opposed to wholesale abolition?

I am sure that you, Mr. Cormack, will readily understand how reluctant I am, as any shadow Chief Secretary would be, to see £800 million or £900 million of Governent revenue drift in towards the horizon. We are anxious to retain some of that revenue for future use.

Mr. Lilley
:The hon. Lady has put her finger on a difficult distinction between SDRT and stamp duty. I am reasonably confident that the treatment in the clause is correct, but it is arguable that we could have dealt with it somewhat in the manner suggested in the amendment. However, I understand that only a few people would be affected if we move in one way, whereas if we line up the two treatments in the other direction significant sums would be lost. To accept the amendment would cost up to £100 million.

I shall try to explain this arcane matter. Under clause 92(7) a transfer pursuant to a contract, whether conditional or not, made before abolition day is chargeable to stamp duty. Paragraph (b) of amendment No. 251 would change the provision so that a transfer pursuant to a conditional contract made before abolition day would be exempt from stamp duty if the condition is satisfied after that day. The basis for this proposed change is that it would bring the stamp duty rule in line with the stamp duty reserve tax rule in clause 94(2)(a).

The difference between the stamp duty rule in clause 92 and the SDRT rule in clause 94 reflects a basic difference between the two taxes. SDRT is a tax on agreements to transfer chargeable securities. It follows that no charge should arise in respect of a conditional agreement until the condition is satisfied: section 87(3)(a) of the Finance Act 1986 provides for that. Therefore, where the condition is satisfied after abolition day it would be wrong for the tax to apply to the agreement.

The stamp duty position is different. Stamp duty is a duty on documents rather than an agreement and the documents here are transfers of shares on sale. In determining for stamp duty which transfers on sale are to fall on either side of the abolition line, we decided to follow the precedent of 1986, when the duty was halved. The effect of clause 92 is that a transfer will fall within the stamp duty charge if the date of the contract for sale is before abolition day. In this context there is no need to consider whether such a contract is conditional or not as the amendment proposes. If it is conditional and the condition is never satisfied, no transfer will result and no duty will be payable. If it is satisfied, there is no case for distinguishing the transfer from one completing an unconditional agreement.
