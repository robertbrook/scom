Mr. Cook: The amendments seek to solve a problem that weekly afflicts tens of thousands of elderly people who are resident in private old folk's homes. It equally afflicts and perplexes hundreds of owners of private old folks' homes.

In the bipartisan spirit that from time to time, and against our better judgment, broke out earlier, I noticed that one or two Conversatives Member taxed Opposition Members with always being critical of the owners of private residential homes. I assure the Committee that 99 per cent. of what I shall say will be echoed and supported by every private owner of a residential care home.

There is a growing gap between the level of income support which the Government are willing to pay to the residents of old folks' homes and the amount that the private owners are obliged to charge if they are to succeed in keeping a roof over their own heads as well as over those of their residents. That is a human problem now and it has myriad implications for the new system for which the clause paves the way.

The background to the issue is easily sketched in. It stems from 1980 when the Conservative Administration changed the rules on income support so that elderly residents could claim income support to pay the bill for accommodation in private residential care. The result of the change—I presume that it was the intended result—was an explosion in the number of Image Col. 803 elderly people seeking residential care on income support and in the growth of private homes on the back of the new market. The single most dramatic change in spending on care in the community under this Administration has been the growth in spending on income support to people entering private residential care. It rose from £10 million in 1980 to almost £1 billion last year.

The 1 per cent of my speech that the owners of private homes will not support is that I believe that that money might have been more effectively and appreciatively spent by expanding public provision rather than on subsidising the expansion of private homes. That would have avoided one of the unfortunate by-blows of that expansion. Large numbers of elderly people now in private residential care have passed the means test for income support without necessarily fulfilling social or medical criteria for requiring residential care. The numbers are sometimes exaggerated, but, even if it is 10 per cent of the total, it still amounts to perhaps 10,000 elderly people who are being supported in residential care by public funds. They might be more appropriately supported by public funds in their own homes.

I seek bipartisan support and to be as non-contentious as possible, difficult though the learning curve may be. Ideological reasons may have prompted Conservative Members to embark on the expansion of residential care solely by paying social security to private owners. I accept that it was their right to do so, as they had the power to get it through Parliament. However, I cannot accept—neither, I suspect, can private owners and Conservative Members—that, having facilitated the expansion of private residential care on income support, the Government reneged on the bills that started to roll in as a result of their policy.

IN 1985, the Government changed the rules again. Instead of meeting the full bill for the cost of private residential care, the Department of Social Security imposed a flat limit, payable according to type of residential care. Since 1985, those flat rate payments have been uprated at a rate that is ludicrously behind the rate at which charges in homes have increased. In fairness to the owners, they had to increase their charges if they were to make ends meet.

I am indebted to my hon. Friend the Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, Central (Mr. Cousins) for an imaginative parliamentary question that extracted the same figures on a year-by-year basis, reduced to real, not funny money, taking inflation out of the calculation. From the parliamentary answer, we learnt that the real value of the average payment in private residential homes through income support fell in the year after the change. Even in 1989, five years later, the real increase in the average payment on income support to people in private residential care was only £4 a week higher than in 1984. The result is that there is overwhelming, comprehensive and consistent evidence of a dramatic gap between the average charge for private residential care and the average payment in income support. The hon. Member for Gainsborough and Horncastle (Mr. Leigh) likes evidence to be produced in speeches to the Committee. He demanded more evidence when last I spoke. I assure him that he will Col. 804 have evidence by the bucket full. As plainly he is moved by evidence, I hope, with confidence, to anticipate his vote at the end of the debate.

Last July, Age Concern called a conference which was attended by 37 voluntary and private home owners and providers of care. Each reported that they had difficulty living within income support levels. The British Federation of Care Home Proprietors, a body that represents only the private enterprise sector, reported that the average gap between income support payments and their charges was £40 a week—the Royal United Kingdom Benevolent Association—RUKBA stated that the average gap was £24. The Occupational Benevolent Funds Alliance reported that the average gap in its homes was £44 per week.

Meanwhile, the charities which attended that conference reported an upsurge in desperate pleas from elderly people caught in a position of being faced with bills that they could not pay. Counsel and Care for the Elderly reported that in the preceding 12 months 3,000 new inquiries came from elderly people faced with bills that they could not pay and with no home to which to return.

It may be contended that Age Concern has an axe to grind, that it may employ wonderful warm, caring people, "but that it does not have a hard-nosed business sense or a nose for figures. Lest that happen, let me turn to a recent report by Laing and Buisson, an entirely commercial company that advises people on investment prospects, especially in health care.
