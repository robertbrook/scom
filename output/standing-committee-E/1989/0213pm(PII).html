Sir George Young (Ealing, Acton): Before we adjourned, unanimity was breaking out. We had two excellent speeches, one from each side of the Committee, in support of the wide-ranging group of amendments on clause 41 which, by and large, urged local authorities to consult in the preparation of their plans. I wish to speak specifically about the ones on housing.

In a nutshell, the amendments write into the Bill the expectations that are quite clear in the White Paper and in statements by Ministers. My hon. Friend the Member for Mid-Kent (Mr. Rowe) quoted from paragraph 5.7 on page 42 of the White Paper. There is an even earlier reference on page 6, which says that

"local authorities will be expected to produce and publish clear plans for the development of community care services, consistent with the plans of health authorities and other interested agencies."

They can only do that if they have first consulted the health authorities and other interested agencies.

At the weekend I was reading a speech that my hon. Friend the Minister for Health made at a conference organised by INSIGHT and Community Care. She referred to the framework that will need to be in place by April 1991 and said:

"It should identify the needs of local population; set strategic objectives and priorities for meeting them and set targets for collaboration with other agencies … Local authorities will also be expected to show 'how they have involved service users and carers in their needs' and plans will need to broadly fit in with those of the local health authority and Family Practitioner Committee."

The amendments convert the exhortations of my hon. Friend the Minister into a specific obligation in the clause. In clause 41 as drafted the local authorities have to consult with no one at all when they draft their plans for community care services. That clearly is at variance with the whole philsophy of the legislation, which is to make the service more consumer-oriented and to involve those who provide the care in the drafting of the plans. The amendments therefore impose an obligation to consult, but some of them also provide the forum in which such consultation might take place, with a specific proposal for a committee set up by the local authority with a specified composition.

Some two weeks ago I spent an evening attending a meeting of the Ealing community care forum, which is Col. 928 exactly the sort of organisation envisaged by the amendments. The meeting was attended by representatives from such organisations as Age Concern, Help the Aged, Mind, the Carers National Association, the local housing associations and community health council—exactly the sort of people who need to be involved in the assessment of need and the drafting of plans. The sorts of people who would attend that meeting and who should attend any meetings on community care know more than almost anyone else the shortcomings of the existing system—its inflexibility and its extravagance at times, to which my hon. Friend the Member for Mid-Kent has referred. They have an invaluable fund of knowledge about the present scene.

Such people also have a clear perception of what should happen in April 1990. They have a clear insight into the needs of the clients whom they serve and they are broadly in sympathy with the philosophy behind the Bill, which is to have consumer-oriented packages of care rather than obliging people to take what is currently available.

They also have an interest because many of them are service providers. They would like to provide the sors of services that are envisaged themselves, to bid for the contracts and, they hope, to win them. It would be very short-sighted if local authorities did not consult the sorts of organisations in the sort of forum that I have outlined if they are to make a success of the policy. There is a valuable resource in there, and a Conservative administration has alrady done much to bring voluntary organisations into the planning machinery. The joint consultative committees and the partnership committees set up in the inner cities have representatives of voluntary organisations on them, and it would be very odd if we let the Bill go through without ensuring that the voluntary organisations were part of the decision-making progress.

I touch briefly on the role of housing associations. There are four paragraphs in the White Paper on housing, and one could say that not enough emphasis was placed on the role of housing. Without bricks and mortar there is no community care. Paragraph 3.5.4 says:

"Social Services authorities will need to work closely with housing authorities, housing associations and other providers of housing of all types in developing plans for a full and flexible range of housing."

That is why two of the amendments place a specific obligation to consult housing authorities and housing associations.

Housing associations will be crucial, not only in the provision of housing generally, but also in meeting some of the needs envisaged in the White Paper. Already many housing associations such as Stonham, Leonard Cheshire and Carr-Gomm in London are meeting the special needs that are targeted under the legislation, and they should be part of the decision-making process so that they can explain to the social service authorities what they can provide and what they need. Many housing associations are increasingly meeting the needs of the elderly, people with disabilities, people leaving long-stay hospitals, people with AIDS or HIV, people at risk of domestic violence and young people leaving care, and it is essential that we Image Col. 929 involve those organisastions in the decision-making process.

I am relatively relaxed about the ways in which we meet the objectives, but I am convinced that we must in some way ensure that social service departments go through that consultative exercise before they commit themselves to the plans.
