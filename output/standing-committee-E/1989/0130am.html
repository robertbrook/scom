National Health Service and Community Care Bill

Clause 13
RECOGNITION OF FUND-HOLDING PRACTICES OF DOCTORS

10.30 am

Ms. Harriet Harman
(Peckham)
:I beg to move amendment No. 39, in page 12, line 29, at end add

'but no such fund-holding practice will be cash limited and have to limit the services purchased by the practice because of lack of funds'.

The Chairman
: With this, we may take amendment No. 505, in page 12, line 29, at end insert—

'1(A). No fund-holding practice will be denied funds where in the clinical judgement of a medical practitioner who is a member of such a fund-holding practice a patient on his list requires medical treatment.'.

Ms. Harman
: Amendment No. 39 would ensure that fund-holding practices were not cash-limited. Fund-holding practices were originally to have been called budget-holding practices. But the word "budget" would have given the game away by implying a defined limit which must not be exceeded. The Government recognised that the measure ws deeply unpopular, but instead of changing the substance of the Bill, they referred to Roget's Thesaurus, and now we are debating fund-holding practices. Clearly, the Government think that people will feel that a fund will provide more money than a budget. We want to test whether the change in name from budget-holding to fund-holding practices is also a change of substance that will affect patients and doctors.

The White Paper and the Bill imply that one sum of money only will be allotted for buying goods and services. The Government will say that the new system is voluntary, that only practices that choose to do so will become fund-holding practices, and that they can continue to operate as they do at present if they think that there will be insufficient money in the fund. Why have we been told that there have been so many volunteers, when we know that most GPs are bitterly opposed to the idea of a cash-limited practice budget? An opinion poll in The Daily Telegraph on 18 May 1989 showed that

"family doctors are … unhappy with Government proposals for them to hold their own budget. Only two per cent. welcome the plan and nine per cent. accept it in principle with a few reservations. The vast majority—76 per cent.—reject it altogether, while 12 per cent. have major reservations." Some general practices will be driven into becoming fund-holding practices because, if they do not, they will lose their clinical freedom to refer patients to the hospital that they know will be best for them. Under threat of losing that vital clinical freedom, GPs will be forced to accept the lesser of two evils, and choose fund-holding practices. In an oral answer on 21 February 1989, the Secretary of State for Health said:

518

"General practitioners who do not elect to have practice budgets will still have freedom of choice on referrals although they will have to work more closely with their district health authorities than hitherto in deciding on referral patterns from their areas."—[ Official Report, 21 February 1989; Vol. 147, c. 820.]

What on earth does that mean? It is a load of old guff. I hope, Dame Janet, that when I am a Minister you will remind me not to talk garbage.

This sort of argument brings politicians into public contempt. The Minister does not have the courage to face up to the fact that GPs will lose their right to refer. He says that they will still have freedom of choice, but that they will have to work more closely with their DHAs. That means that DHAs will choose. It is a pity that the Bill has not been more honestly represented. We discovered earlier in our proceedings when the Government rejected our amendment, that GPs and patients would not have the choice.

Recruits will be driven into fund-holding practices by the loss of their right to refer. There will be a fundamental change in the family doctor service and the doctor-patient relationship. Why is that necessary? The Government have put forward not one shred of evidence to show that the family doctor service is expensive or not cost-effective. So why do we need to cash-limit it? If the Government intend to reject our proposal that there should be no cash limits, they had better give us evidence of why cash limits are necessary.

In the immortal words of the Royal College of Nursing, the cash limit will put a price tag on every patient's head. It will contaminate the clinical judgment of family practitioners with commercial judgment. At best it will have a chilling effect. Every day of their working lives doctors will recognise that they are making not only clinical decisions but financial decisions. That will reduce their readiness to refer patients for tests and treatment. When GPs become budget holders, they will have not only a clinical responsibility to each patient, but a financial responsibility to other patients, who will need their services later in the year. They will think not just of the needs of the patient before them but of the needs of their other patients.

GPs will also think of their responsibility to their partners: "If I spend too much money on this patient, I might not have enough money for my next patient. If I spend too much money on this patient, my partner might not have enough money for a more difficult case." The desire not to overspend will create in the heart of the system an incentive to under-treat.

Many decisions that family doctors make are borderline. It is a matter of judgment whether to send a patient for a test or to leave things for a while, or whether to send a patient for an operation. We do not want finely tuned clinical decisions to be contaminated by a tipping of the balance towards commercial and financial considerations.
