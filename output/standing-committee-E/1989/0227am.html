'Where a local authority requests a Health Board or housing agency to co-operate to assist them in the discharge of their functions in respect of the planning, assessment and provisions of community care services, the body to whom the request is made shall co-operate in rendering such assistance in the discharge of the functions to which the request relates as is reasonable in the circumstances. Mr. Maxton
: I am not surprised, Dame Janet, that the three new clauses have been grouped together, because their wording is almost identical. They would amend three Acts to enable local authorities to co-operate with other bodies, thereby ensuring the proper working of community care systems.

As this may be one of the last occasions on which the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland speaks in this Committee, I hope that he will deal with the principles underlying the new clause, and not merely nit-pick at its wording. He derives great amusement from finding fault with the wording of amendments or new clauses, yet he never manages to address their principles. I accept that he has made some concessions on housing authorities, but he has not gone as far as the Opposition would wish.

1270 All hon. Members recognise the need for community care, but the Opposition have doubts about the fact that the Bill will transfer the burden of community care provision to local authorities without providing a legislative framework to ensure that other bodies, which must be involved in providing community care, co-operate with local authorities in assessments, planning and delivery of services. The Bill leaves everything to the local authorities, which will be directed by the Secretary of State. No compulsion will be placed on any other organisation.

In some areas of Scotland—though not in many—such an informal approach may work. The Western Isles, for example, have a single-tier authority, which has housing and social work functions. Some members of the council, such as the convener, are also members of the health board. In such a small community co-operation is possible, because departments meet regularly in the same building and discussions can take place when people meet in the street, if nowhere else. But in Glasgow, where the Greater Glasgow health board, Strathclyde regional council and Glasgow district council all operate, the situation is different. I met the Glasgow health board last Friday, and the problem arose during informal conversation with the general manager.

Strathclyde regional council, which covers a population of 2-5 million, will be the providing authority. It is the largest single local authority in the United Kingdom, and covers a wide range district authorities of varying size and political complexion, though few support the Government. The district authorities within Strathclyde are the housing authorities. Among those housing authorities is the Glasgow district council, which is the largest landlord in Europe covering a population of nearly 1 million, almost 60 per cent, of whom live in council housing. The Greater Glasgow health board covers a slightly larger area than the Glasgow district council, with a population of almost 1 million. Therefore, we are discussing large authorities: Strathclyde deals with a population of 2-5 million, and the other two bodies within Glasgow deal with 1 million each.

The necessary arrangements for co-operation are impossible without a legislative framework and legislative pressure upon authorities to provide it. That is an opinion shared by many who deal with community care.

In an informal conversation, the general manager of the Greater Glasgow health board—who is much closer politically to the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland than he is to me, and who I greatly criticise for ruthlessly pushing through privatisation in the Greater Glasgow health board and for some of his other actions—expressed worries to me about the lack of a legislative framework in which the board could co-operate with the social work department of Strathclyde region. He believes that, if the Bill is to work, such a framework is required.

Strathclyde regional council controls Lennox Castle hospital, which comes within the Greater Glasgow health board area. The general manager is greatly worried about releasing patients—some of whom have been in hospital for 15 or 20 years, should never have been there and would now not go there, but who are institutionalised—without knowing what the methods will be, with whom he will have to work, or how he will ensure proper assessment and the provision of proper back-up services by the local authorities. He is right to express those anxieties because JPEG 1271 informal co-Operation, which will inevitably exist in the smaller authorities, will not occur in the larger ones. Large bureaucracies do not always co-operate with one another.

Within the health boards there is grave anxiety that the co-operation will not take place unless it is forced by legislation. They should be forced to review their structures to discover how they can obtain the best deal for their patients. Although local authorities will become the providers, many people are now in the care of health boards. Unless health boards have a clearly defined structure for releasing their patients into community care, they will not be happy with the Bill.

I should be less than honest if I did not say that the co-operation between Glasgow district council and Strathclyde regional council is not always what it should be. There are personality clashes—oddly enough, not at the political level, as the hon. Member for Tayside, North (Mr. Walker) may think—between officials, especially at the top. If community care is to work, co-operation between regional social work departments and local and district housing departments must be finely tuned.

Housing authorities will come under pressure from local tenants not to provide housing for some groups of people. Everyone sympathises with the principle of community care, but when it is practised, NIMBY soon comes into force. That means, "Not in my backyard," in case anyone is wondering. So housing authorities must be under a statutory obligation to provide housing and to co-operate; otherwise, they may give in to local pressure and not provide necessary facilities.

The new clauses seek to give a legislative framework to such co-operation. They may not be perfectly worded, but a principle is being stated. If the new clauses are not acceptable, we hope that the Government will accept the principle and draft for Report or for the debates in the other place new clauses that will provide the legislative framework that we seek.

I hope that the Under-Secretary responds positively, because hon. Members on both sides are worried about the lack of legislative framework for the other bodies which must be part of community care, if it is to work; otherwise there is the risk that people will be released from hospitals and other institutions and end up on the streets because of a failure of co-operation between organisations. We have seen that in England; we do not want it in Scotland. The new clauses seek to prevent it.
