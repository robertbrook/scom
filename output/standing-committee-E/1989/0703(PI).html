Entertainers

314A Where—

  1. a person undertakes an engagement (whether in the United Kingdom or elsewhere) as an actor, singer, musician, dancer or other entertainer, and

The facts were different and the courts decided that the taxpayer should be taxed under schedule D.

Parliament set out general definitions of schedules D and E, so decisions have been made by the courts. The Inland Revenue, however, must take an initial view before such matters can be tested in the courts. There is contention about the history of that subject.

The Revenue did nothing immediately after 1972, because it expected the taxpayer to appeal. The taxpayer did not appeal, and even then nothing happened for a long time. Recently, the Inland Revenue tried to prevent abuse of the schedule D rules by bringing people who were employed, but not self-employed, into the schedule E net. In the outlined case the Revenue used its powers legitimately under the Taxes Management Act 1970, but it went too far.

Yesterday, my hon. Friend the Financial Secretary answered questions about the authority for the Inland Revenue' distinction between performers and artists taxed under schedule E and those taxed under schedule D. My hon. Friend said that the change was based on decisions made by the courts and that there would be a transitional period so that long-standing schedule D tax arrangements would continue for the rest of the taxpayers' careers. That distinction was made, with the approval of Treasury Ministers, under the powers for the general care and management of income tax in the Taxes Management Act 1970. Parliament empowered the Revenue to make such distinctions, but if the Revenue goes over the top, Parliament should put matters right before the courts consider the case. If the Inland Revenue's decision was challenged by the courts, the proceedings would take time—lawyers rarely determine matters quickly and the case might go to appeal.

Many injustices now exist. The Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales recently issued a guidance note that was agreed with the Inland revenue. The most unfair aspect of te new arrangements for young actors who cannot show three years of schedule D tax returns is the treatment of their agent's commission. If an actor earns £100 and has to pay £10 or £12.50 to his or her agent, the net amount should be taxed, not the £100. It seems extremely unfair to tax the commission When all actors had schedule D tax status, they could claim their expenses for travel, subsistence and auditions. The change in the arrangements has resulted in unfairnesses, but the most blatant unfairness is the taxing of the agent's commission. Something should be done about that.

The starting point should be recognition of the unique nature of the acting and freelance journalist professions, to which new clause 22 draws attention. A sensible tax system should assess the work of a profession to ascertain which legitimate expenses should be considered and should then adjust the tax regime accordingly to get the proper and appropriate tax treatment for the job. The problem should be tackled by starting with the the nature of the work and then devising a tax system to deal with it, rather than starting with a global tax regime and fitting every profession into it. But that is what the Inland Revenue has done. By changing the status from schedule D to schedule E, with no ameliorating provisions in the nature of schedule E status, the Inland Revenue has created major problems for several professions, especially acting and journalism.

The Financial Secretary will probably argue, as he did in the Adjournment debate on 27 February 1990, that if he gives way on one profession he will be on a slippery slope and will have to consider giving way to other professions. There are already exceptions to the schedule E expenses rule—some people are treated as schedule D taxpayers even though, in normal circumstances, they would qualify as employees. North sea divers are taxed under schedule D in accordance with section 314 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988. The exceptions include the concession under which postmasters' salaries are, in practice, taxed JPEG 491 under schedule D and consession A37, whereby partners' directors fees are taxed as schedule D payslip income. The Revenue has already made an exception on those cases and the hon. Member for Beaconsfield and the Opposition argue that, because of the nature of the work undertaken by entertainers and journalists, the Revenue should be flexible about their treatment, too.

There is already a legal provision that has the effect of taking entertainers out of schedule E and into schedule D. Section 134 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988 was originally enacted in 1975. It deems agency workers to be employees, but section 134(5)(a) states that the schedule E rule

"shall not apply—


(a) if the services in question are services as an actor, singer, musician or other entertainer or as a fashion, photographic or artist's model".

That provision in existing tax law was implemented three years after the Hitchen v. Fall case, which the Inland Revenue is quoting as the basis for the subsequent change. Therefore, three years after the case, the Revenue sought to put the principle into a legislative format. The hon. Member for Beaconsfield and I want to drag it back to a schedule D type assessment for expenses incurred by artists, actors and journalists.

The principal difference between schedules D and E relates to the expenses that can be claimed out of pre-tax rather than post-tax income. The Revenue wishes to implement a system that will throw up several difficulties and anomalies. What will happen to touring allowances? Let us take the example of a touring company which is based primarily at one location but goes on tour around the country half the time. Under schedule E rules, the Revenue will say that touring allowances made available to the actors in that touring company for the time they are touring will not be taxable—they will be deductible against their income tax.

However, we should also take the example of a touring company that tours all the time and has no permanent base—for example the Cambridge Theatre Company. The two companies may be performing the same plays in the same locations with the same costs and touring allowances, but the touring allowances for actors in a company that tours permanently will not be allowable against tax because of the schedule E rules. Therefore, great anomalies will arise as a result of that change in relation to touring.

The hon. Member for Beaconsfield has already identified the problem of agents' fees, and I endorse all that he said. In response to representations made by Equity and by the Theatres National Committee, the Revenue has suggested that agents' fees could be paid direct by the management of the company that employs the actor. That would destroy utterly the proper relationship between the actor and his agent, a relationship that is crucial if the actor is to receive work from other employers in due course. The Revenue's proposed solution is not a sensible way of tackling the problem.

The proposed change will cause difficulties for members of the profession, for several reasons. Our new clause would tackle the problem from a different per- 492 spective to that of new clause 12. Instead of saying that everyone shall be on schedule D, the new clause states that the Revenue can have its way. However, even though those people will then be on schedule E, the schedule D definition of expenses should apply. The definition of expenses which are allowable against tax includes not just those which are wholly and exclusively incurrred in the pursuit of a profession but those which are wholly, exclusively and necessarily incurred. The tougher test applies under the schedule E rules. A slightly more flexible test is appropriate for people in the acting and freelance journalism professions.

That is a possible way out of the Government's dilemma. Replying in the Adjournment debate on 27 February, the Financial Secretary said:

"I hope that the discussions between Equity and the Revenue will find some way of softening the treatment of what at present are expenses by recasting them in a way that will comply with the law and will mean that they are not expenses." —[Official Report, 27 February 1990; Vol. 168, c.254]

The new clause offers the Revenue exactly that potential get-out. The Opposition will support the hon. Member for Beaconsfield because his is a sensible way of tackling the problem.

If the Government do not find that solution acceptable, we offer an alternative. The problem will not go away. Actors and journalists need to be able to handle expenses sensibly, and they deserve somewhat better from the Committee and from the Government than the changes proposed by the Inland Revenue.

Enough of intellectual competition, Mr. Cormack, new clause 49 deals with a much more prosaic matter—benefits in kind. The beneifts in kind rules were introduced in 1976. Until 1989 it was generally accepted that when the benefit was of such a kind that the employer would have incurred most of the expenses anyway, the rules meant that only the small extra costs incurred on top of those inevitable costs were taxable as a benefit.

The main aspects of employment to which that applied were education and transport. If a transport company would have operated its scheduled services anyway, it was accepted that allowing its employees or their families to use spare space was not a taxable benefit. If a school allowed its masters' sons or daughters cut-rate education at a fee-paying school, the taxable benefit was limited to the extra cost of books, food, repairs and other such expenses provided again that only capacity that would otherwise have been unused was used.

The situation altered after Pepper v. Hart in 1989, when it was decided that section 156(2) of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988 went further than practice until then. The judge decided that all costs should be taken into account in calculating the taxable benefit, irrespective of whether they would have been incurred anyway. The case involved nine schoolmas- 512 ters at Malvern college, which, I hope, will draw some sympathy from Conservative Members who are no longer paying attention.

The ramifications of judge's decision go wide and tend to affect lower-paid employees who are covered by the benefits in kind legislation. I understand that tube drivers have free underground passes, which they can use at weekends and to travel home at night. Following the Pepper v. Hart case, if a tube driver were one of, on average, 10 passenegers on his journey home, he could, in theory, be taxable on 10 per cent of the apportioned costs of operating that train on that journey. It would be bizarre if that happened. The new clause would ensure that it will not. The administrative problems of enforcing that judgement would be immense.

We can follow the example to its ridiculous conclusion. If the tube makes an operating loss despite charging 70p for the shortest journey, the potential taxable benefits are enormous. The entire losses could be divided among the people deemed to be beneficiaries. The position is bad enough for those who get concessionary travel on the tube, but those who work for British Airways who occupy spare seats on flights would be even worse off. Our new clauses would ensure that the Inland Revenue do not pursue the judgment in the case of Pepper v. Hart to its logical and—for some people—pretty expensive conclusion.
