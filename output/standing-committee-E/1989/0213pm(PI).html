National Health Service and Community Care Bill

Clause 39
CHARGES FOR ACCOMMODATION PROVIDED BY LOCAL AUTHORITIES

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 250, in page 40, line 35, at end insert—

'(3A) Persons who are provided by local authorities with accommodation under Part III of that Act shall, where they meet the financial criteria prescribed by the Secretary of State, be eligible for Income Support and Housing Benefit in the same manner as if accommodated in a Residential Care Home registered under Part II of the Registered Homes Act.'.— [Mr. Robin Cook.]

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman

'except that persons who are provided with such accommodation shall, where they meet the financial criteria prescribed by the Secretary of State, be eligible for Income Support and Housing Benefit in the same manner as if accommodated in a Residential Care Home registered under Part II of the Registered Homes Act 1984.'.

Mr. Alun Michael
(Cardiff, South and Penarth)
: The amendments are about three things which it is difficult to conceive of the Minister speaking or voting against: equality of treatment, fairness and reasonableness. There is little more to them than those simple principles. But I have been puzzled at how Ministers have been wriggling when asked about the different ways in which different people will be treated under this legislation. I asked the Secretary of State for Wales in a written question a few days ago to tell me

"whether he will make it his policy that Government contributions to support people living in homes for the elderly in Wales shall be calculated on the same basis, irrespective of whether the home is run by a local authority, a voluntary or charitable body, or on a private commercial basis."

That would follow one simple principle, which we debated and which the Government tried to persuade us was enshrined in earlier parts of the Bill: that the money follows the person or that the money follows the patient. The response from the Under-Secretary was:

"Our policies for the future funding of care in the community are fully set out in the White Paper 'Caring for People: Community Care in the Next Decade and Beyond' Cm 849." —[Official Report, 9 February 1990; Vol. 166 c.812]

That was hardly a revealing or generous response to a simple question.

The same question put to the Secretary of State for Health elicited a rather different answer. I had put the question to the Secretary of State for Social Security and I am still waiting for him to explain why he transferred it to the Secretary of State for Health. A principle is 894 apparently being undermined here: that the individual is the recipient of benefit. A distortion is being introduced, which is reflected by the fact that two Secretaries of State are passing the buck between them. The question that I asked was:

"whether he will make it his policy to ensure that elderly people resident in homes for the elderly are provided equivalent support from public funds irrespective of whether the homes are publicly or privately run."

— The Under-Secretary of State for Health was more open than the Under-Secretary of State for Wales. After referring to the White Paper, he wrote:


"Under our community care proposals local authorities will have to contribute more to the support of people in homes under their own management than to that of people in private homes but the personal financial position of residents will be same." —[Official Report, 31 January 1990; Vol. 166 c.231.]

In other words, because local authority care is often cheaper and more efficient than the private sector, the latter will be subsidised by the Government at the expense of the local authority. That seems to contradict the Government's idea of the money following the individual.

I presumed that the proposal had been reasoned logically. I thought that if I asked for the reasons, I would be given them. So on 12 February I asked the Secretary of State for Health:

"what are the reasons why Government contributions to support people living in homes for the elderly are not to be calculated on the same basis, irrespective of whether the home is publicly or privately run."

I hope that in reply to this debate the Minister for Health will give us the answer, because to a basic question which relates to legislation that has supposedly been thought through by the Government before being brought before the House, the Minister answered:

"I shall let the hon. Member have a reply as soon as possible."

The timing was generous, bearing in mind the way in which questions are answered in the House. There was plenty of time. The Minister's answers mean that she does not know and that there is no reasonable explanation for the Government's tipping of the playing field, which is what the amendments seek to target. The unworthy thought crosses my mind that the Minister really meant, "I do not wish to answer until the Committee has moved past this point in the Bill because I realise how outrageously unfair my answer will seem." Perhaps the Minister will give a firm answer today. Either way, her response was appalling, unfair and unreasonable.

The Government are fining local authorities for doing what they and she want local authorities to do. The Government want them to provide this sort of residential accommodation. The White Paper stated that and it has been stated in Committee. Why, then, as my hon. Friend the Member for Livingston (Mr. Cook) said undermine the position of local authorities and impose on them the option of closing homes to save money and balance the unfairness? Local authorities will merely be following the financial logic that the Government have tried to persuade us is the only logic to follow.

There is a basic inconsistency in the Government urging local authorities to make the provision and work in partnership with the private and voluntary sectors while, at the same time, tilting the balance outrageously against the public sector and local authorities.

The Minister reiterated that the Government will expect local authorities to retain the ability to act as direct providers. It was suggested that this was of direct JPEG 895 importance to services for people with high dependency or especially challenging behaviour whose care it is essential to safeguard. If the Minister is willing to confirm that the Government mean that, she should make the financial provision fit the requirement and not depend on rhetoric. The financial logic had to fit the Government's plans.

A deliberate shift of services into the community is required if people are to be assisted.

My hon. Friend the Member for Livingston said that, often, services for the elderly are focused on homes for the elderly. My own authority, South Glamorgan county council has acted in this way. It decided that a range of services such as home help provision was needed for the elderly, and that many such services are needed by the elderly who still live in their own homes. The council has based the resources in the community in the one place where high quality services are already provided—in homes for the elderly—instead of in remote county headquarters. It makes sense and has logic. The approach that the Government have adopted is dangerously likely to undermine the sort of commonsense strategy for care in the community, which is what the Government claim their Bill promotes. That unfairness also places an additional burden on poll tax payers. Elderly people share the burden of poll tax so, at some time, they will share the burden that is being placed on local authorities and shrugged off by the Government, a burden that, up to now, has been a national responsibility. It is grossly unfair.

In his original report, Sir Roy Griffiths emphasised the need for improved consumer choice. The Bill as drafted will reduce choice for old people by pressuring local authorities to use private residential homes that are more expensive and on average less efficient than those in the public sector.

During a debate last week, both sides of the Committee referred to occasional failings in both the public and the private sectors. My hon. Friends said that they did not like failings whatever sector they were in, and that all such failings should be tackled. However, we ought to point to the benefits as well. I have had experience in my constituency, and in neighbouring ones, of excellent standards of care in public sector homes for the elderly. I pay tribute to their dedicated staff and their positive atmosphere, in which elderly people are encouraged to take a full part in the life of their communities for as long as they can. They are encouraged to continue developing in their old age, and not to see their old age as something negative, just to be survived.

This is important in a community-based philosophy, as is the idea that homes for the elderly should be drawn into the life of the community around them. That can work in a positive way, and does so best in the local authority setting where such contact is encouraged by professional and voluntary workers, and by local voluntary organisations. I appeal to the Minister to give a—preferably brief —answer, and to admit that the logic of the Opposition's case is so modest and sensible that she will accept the amendments and so restore some common sense to the Bill.

Gentleman if he wishes to justify his statement, but I see that he does not want to intervene at present.

Clause 39 undermines any claim that the Government are trying to help local authorities improve their directly provided care. The opposite is true. The clause means the end of local authority residential accommodation, and that will have a direct effect on the lives of the many people who rely on it. Over the years, vast numbers of people have come to appreciate the fact that the Labour Government passed the National Assistance Act 1948, which brought about that form of provision.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Livingston and others have said, the local authority home, unlike the private sector institution, is genuinely part and parcel of the community. Homes that have been set up by local authorities have been built where people live—not 20 or 30 miles away in the middle of the countryside, in a Victorian mansion no longer needed by a rich family. Homes that are provided in the community not only take people into care when needed, but they offer a community facility.

The Government tell us that the Bill will provide more choice, but I look at what it will mean in my constituency. It will mean distinctly less choice for vast numbers of elderly people who choose not to go into the private sector. Because of the lack of local provision, many of my constituents have to go outside my constituency, outside my metropolitan district. They cannot find accommodation when they need it—usually on discharge from hospital —in the Wakefield district. In a number of cases, people have been placed in private homes, particularly in Kirklees, sometimes in Bradford, and after they have been there for six weeks or longer, they have difficulty, not in persuading Wakefield council to offer them directly provided accommodation, but in persuading the local authority to accept the financial responsibility for placing the people back in their own areas.

This is a matter that worries me greatly. Many of the people who have lived all their lives in Wakefield have to spend their remaining months and years miles away from their families and the community in which they want to spend the rest of their lives. What they are offered is not community care; it is anything but community care, and the Government seem quite content with the system that they have pioneered.

I am proud of some of the homes that my local authority have provided—Waterson house, Queen Elizabeth house, Flanshaw lodge, Honley house, Castle Grove, Walton house and a number of others. They have given first-class care, and they are based in the community —not miles away, but where people live and where families and friends can visit. I am not talking about places that are 20 or 30 miles away, where people are forgotten and left to die. It worries me that those homes will no longer exist once the Bill is enacted. I am worried about the clear financial discrimination against the Wakefield district council if it attempts to continue to maintain them. That is the problem that the amendment seeks to tackle.

My hon. Friend the Member for Livingston mentioned a number of the options that are open to local authorities. One is to close or reduce the number of directly provided homes. But how can local authorities fulfil their obligations to accommodate dependent elderly or handicapped people when they no longer have the direct ability to do so, and when the private sector will not accept such people? Another option is to sell off to other 898 providers, as happened in Bradford. Those of us who live in west Yorkshire do not have far to go to see examples of the extremism of the Conservative party; we see the human consequences in Bradford, where elderly ladies, lifelong Tory voters, have gone to court because the homes in which they lived are being sold over their heads. The same will happen elsewhere, not just in extremist Bradford, when reasonable and sane local authorities are forced to take similar action.

I have mentioned two options. There is one other. Clause 51 allows some differences in the financial arrangements for certain people in local authority accommodation, but some local authorities may wish to cream off people who are assessed as needing residential accommodation and who have resources. They will place them in directly provided local authority homes, and put the people who qualify for income support and housing benefit into the private sector. A person will be assessed as needing residential accommodation. Then there will be a second assessment, based on the person's finances, and because of the discrimination that exists with directly provided local authority accommodation, the local authority will see the person who has £30,000 or £40,000, or a house, as a full-cost payer under the arrangements for local authority residential accommodation. The person will be means-tested, pay the full cost, and go into the directly provided local authority home, while the person who is not wealthy and has no house to dispose of will go into the private sector because she qualifies for income support and housing benefit.

I can see that happening. Local authorities that want to keep their own local authority homes will take that option, because they know that many people choose to go into local authority homes in their own areas. The local authorities that want to continue to provide those homes will have to suck into them people who have money, who will be assessed at full cost and charged the full cost of the care that they receive.

I invite the Minister to refute the assertion that the clause will make that example possible. It will happen in some areas. It worries me that a community asset will be accorded only to people who have resources. Those who have not will go the way of the private sector.

I am not out to knock the private sector. I have been to small private sector homes which offer good standards of care. I spent part of a day during the recess at one with 20 residents in Horbury, at the request of the owner, who knew that I would make some critical comments about private care. I was impressed with the standard of care there. But in my opinion that the private sector is not suited to provide residential care on its own, as the Government envisage, was reinforced because the owner said that she was of retirement age and was therefore contemplating selling the home. When I had complimented her on the homeliness of the home she said, "Yes, but when I sell it it will be picked up by a larger organisation which will immediately double, treble or even quadruple the number of beds." That is happening in the private sector.

There is a home in the Wakefield district with 100 beds. When I started as a social worker we discussed trying to reduce home sizes to 35 beds. We are returning to massive institutions because that is the way to make money—the way to make a killing out of the need for care of vulnerable individuals.
