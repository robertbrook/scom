The Under-Secretary of State for Health said that subsection (4) was highly technical, he was correct. Fortunately, the Under-Secretary is here to respond. Although the matter is technical, and the calculations are complicated, the effect of capital charges will be brutally simple and dramatic. What will happen, and what is in progress, is that every hospital site and building will be valued and a quasi-rent will have to be paid for the first time since 1948. It is worth recalling that three quarters of our hospital buildings were in existence and had been paid for in 1948 by local communities which will now find themselves caught in an annual payments system for the use of buildings and sites.

There will be a comprehensive and wide-ranging register of assets which, in addition to buildings and land, will cover every piece of equipment worth more that £1,000. That could be the largest inventory in European history.
