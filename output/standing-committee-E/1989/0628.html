Mr. Lilley: New clause 17 and the new schedule seek to abolish composite rate tax from 6 April 1991. From that date, building societies, banks and some other deposit takers would pay interest to depositers in the United Kingdom under deduction of income tax at the basic rate. That would be the end of the matter for basic rate taxpayers—deduction at source would satisfy their tax liability. Higher rate taxpayers would be required to pay further liability by an assessment or an adjustment of their coding or other allowances. The tax offices will raise the necessary rate charges.

People who are not liable for tax would benefit from the reform. Under enabling powers in the schedule, regulations could be made later this year that would establish a procedure enabling individuals who are, or expect to be, non-liable for tax to self certify. Interest on their savings would be paid gross, without deductions for tax. Those who did not self certify, but were non-liable, could submit a repayment claim to the Inland Revenue. They would receive a refund of the basic rate tax, deducted from their interest.

The Government estimate that 14 million people would gain under the proposed legislation. About 13 Col. 434 million people are not liable to tax but receive interest that has suffered from composite rate tax and a further 1 million would be liable on only part of the interest that they receive. The 14 million includes 5 million married women, who are non taxpayers under independent taxation, 4 million pensioners, 2.5 million other adults and 2.5 million children.

Self certification arrangements for people who are not liable to tax are a key part of the reform. Since the Budget announcement, the Revenue has engaged in intensive consultation with building societies and banks. It aims to ensure that a fully operational system is established well before the first interest payments on or after 6 April 1991, to which the new rules would apply.

The design of the self-certification form has been discussed fully and will be test marketed. After making regulations to set out the procedures, the Revenue will begin a wide publicity campaign. The Government seek the maximum possible take-up by non-taxpayers. Those who do not self certify and receive their interst under tax deduction would be refunded if they were not liable for tax. The arrangements are complicated, but necessary to abolish the composite rate tax. Its abolition has been widely welcomed by hon. Members. I shall be pleased to respond to any queries. We were unable to include the clauses which arise from consultation with banks and building societies in the original Bill, and I apologise for that.

Mr. Lilley: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The purpose of the new clause is to extend the exemption from tax introduced in 1988 to provide that directors and employees given priority rights in a public offer of shares are not liable to income tax on the benefit that can arise provided certain conditions are met.

The new clause deals with the circumstances in which directors and employees are entitled, by reason of their office or employment, to share allocations in two or more companies whose shares are offered for sale at the same time. When two or more such companies jointly own another company they may decide to reward and encourage the employees of that subsidiary by offering them priority rights of shares in the owning companies. In such circumstances the employees might be offered fewer priority shares in any one company than are offered to the directors of that company.

The "similar terms' rule in the existing legislation does not cater for that possibility. In requires all employees entitled to a priority allocation of shares in a particular company to be entitled to those shares on similar terms. It does not permit priority rights to shares in other companies to be taken into account in establishing whether the 'similar terms' requirement is met.

The proposed change will therefore alter the "similar terms" rule to allow such mixed priority rights to be taken into account. As a result, the exemption will be available when some persons are entitled to a smaller Image Col. 443 allocation of shares in each of two or more companies that are offered to the public at the same time, but the exemption will still be subject to the "similar terms" rule and that will be regarded as being satisfied only when the aggregate value of the priority allocation of shares offered to such an employee is the same, or as nearly the same as is reasonably practicable, as the value of the entitlement of a comparable employee of any of the offered companies.

We should provide for the general circumstances that would include the specific position of companies such as the National Grid Company and Electricity Col. 444 Associated Services Ltd., which are jointly owned by the distribution companies and able to give the same terms in substance to employees of those subsidiaries that are available to the distribution companies, without discriminating against them. The original drafting of the proposed legislation did not envisage those circustances.

Mr. Lilley: May I outline the background to our proposed arrangements for taxing mileage allowances at slightly longer length than is normally my wont, Mr. Coleman? I shall endeavour to do so reasonably crisply.

Under the statutory rules, mileage allowances paid by employers to employees for using their own cars for work are taxable to the extent that they exceed those motoring costs which the employee necessarily incurs travelling in the performance of the duties of the employment. The operation of those rules case by case involves a great deal of detailed record keeping work for employers who have to return details to the Revenue of the allowances paid to employees in respect of motoring costs. To get tax relief, employees themselves have to keep detailed records of their costs and the amount of mileage driven—both privately and on business—so that they can make a claim for tax relief after the end of each year.

For many years, and to cut down on the amount of effort needed by all involved, the Revenue has operated voluntary administrative arrangments—the fixed profit car scheme—to charge tax on any profit element in the mileage allowances that some employees receive. About 500,000 employees are covered by the fixed profit car scheme—FPCS—arrangements at present.

The FPCS arrangements establish the taxable amount in the mileage allowance paid to an employee by reference to tax-free rates which the Revenue sets each year. Those rates are intended as a proxy for the average tax allowable costs of running cars in a range of engine sizes. For each employer who operates the FPCS arrangements, the Revenue tabulates each year, by reference to bands of business mileage, fixed profits which take account of the amount by which the employer's allowance exceeds the appropriate FPCS tax-free rate. Establishing the profit on which tax is payable in that way makes the arrangements very attractive to employers, employees and the Revenue alike because it cuts down on the detailed information which has to be maintained and processed.

Unfortunaely, it has now emerged that the FPCS arrangements have over the years become too generous. Under them, many people now receive much more tax relief than the law permits. There are three reasons for that.

First, the FPCS tax-free rates include relief for depreciation to which most employees are not entitled under the present statutory capital allowance rules. The arrangements were first set up almost 20 years ago for certain limited groups of taxpayers who were statutorily entitled to relief for depreciation. Since then, however, many employees covered by the arrangements now have the option of company cars. Such an option would mean that there was no longer the need "necessarily"—the word used in the statute—to provide a car as the law requires. Further, the administrat- Col. 476 ive attractions have resulted in the FPCS being extended to a wide range of employees outside its original scope.

Secondly, the FPCS rates give too much relief for standing charges such as car tax, insurance and sevicing because the costs involved are not properly spread when business mileage is high. As the FPCS arrangements stand, it is possible in some cases for relief to be given for more than the total standing costs incurred. That is clearly not a sensible or defensible result.

Thirdly, the rates of relief themselves have become too high compared with average motoring costs.

If the Revenue were to change its FPCS arrangements to bring them more into line with the present underlying statutory rules, a considerable number of people who currently use their cars for work would have to pay more tax. Some—particularly high business mileage drivers using large cars—would have to pay substantially more. We do not believe that that would be right. Instead, we propose a number of changes in the taxation of mileage allowances.

First, we propose to relax the capital allowance rules so that all employees who can claim tax relief on their motoring costs when using their own cars for work will also be able to claim capital allowances. That means that the FPCS tax-free rates can, legitimately, continue to include an element of relief for depreciation. This is what new clause 63 does. Because relief for interest on loans taken out to buy a car for work use is linked to eligibility for capital allowances, the proposed relaxation in the capital allowance rules will mean that loans taken out to buy a car will attract interest relief to the extent that it is used for business. The change will allow business motorists who use their own cars to have a fairer statutory basis for tax relief, which in future will cover all motoring costs. It does not make much sense to give relief for one kind of motoring expense but not another, as happens under the present statute.

Secondly, the Revenue will make changes to the FPCS arrangements to ensure that they properly reflect the underlying statute. At present, the top FPCS rate of relief applies to cars of 1,500 cc. and over. That rate will be split in two to cover cars of 1,500 to 2,000 cc, and cars of more than 2,000 cc. That will allow the higher costs of running larger cars to be recognised properly. All existing rates will be frozen until average motoring costs catch them up.
