Mr. Lilley
: This batch of amendments has provoked quite a lengthy debate, which is surprising in view of the Committee's virtual unanimity on the substance of the clause and on what the Government amendments seek to achieve.

We want to introduce new relief, in clause 69, for expenditure on restoring a waste disposal site, which would be available only to waste disposal operators who have obtained the necessary disposal or mangement licence and planning permission, for their disposal operations on a particular site. Relief only extends to payments made to comply with conditions in the licence or in the planning permission.

The purpose of the amendments is to ensure that relief will be available, for expenditure which meets those tests, after a disposal licence has ceased to be in force. Under present law, a licence may be surrendered at any time. Such licences may not necessarily be in force for the period afer the completion of the deposit of waste on the site. Some restoration expenditure may therefore be incurred after the licence has ceased to be in force. We do not want to deny relief for that sort of expenditure, and the amendments accordingly remove the requirement that relief would only be due for a payment made at a time when a licence was in force.

This is only a transitional point because the new waste management licences introduced by the Environmental Protection Bill will no longer permit surrender on demand. So there will be no possibility of a licence being surrendered before all required restoration work has been done.

The relief does not create any opportunity for tax avoidance. On the contrary, by limiting relief to expenditures in conformity with the licence, its scope is minimised.

Mr. Cousins
: I find myself in agreement with my hon. Friend the Member for Carlisle (Mr. Martlew). In his constituency and on Tyneside we have direct experience of many of the circumstances on which many of the operations referred to in the clause will hinge. We both realise—many other Committee Members may not—that possession of rights to a hole in the ground is one of the most valuable commodities on the property market. That is especially true near urban areas where holes in the ground are not in great supply and where waste disposal presents grave problems.

344 Waste disposal planning arrangements in this country work on a county basis. It can be difficult to shift waste disposal across county boundaries. That gives rise to great anxiety. Increasingly, waste disposal authorities that possess a large number of quarrying areas are reluctant to issue planning permission and licences to allow waste to be imported to their county areas. Waste disposal authorities increasingly, regard waste disposal and managment as a problem that must be dealt with on a county basis. In counties such as Tyne and Wear and Cleveland, that resutls in holes in the ground becoming extremely valuable, and the greater the pressure on waste disposal authorites to find disposal sites in county areas, the more valuable the sites become.
