legislation Unnumbered European Community Document concerning Beverage Containers

10.30 am

THE UNDER-SECRETARY OF STATE FOR TRADE AND INDUSTRY (Mr. David Trippier): I beg to move,

"That the Committee takes note of the proposal for a Council directive on containers of liquids for human consumption contained in the Department of Trade and Industry's explanatory memorandum dated 30 March 1984; and supports the Government's intention to improve the environment and conserve resources without imposing unnecessary legislative burdens on industry.

I begin by saying how much we welcome the fact that you are chairing our proceedings, Miss Boothroyd. Whilst politeness demands that hon. Members express such sentiments to all Chairmen of Standing Committees, this gives me particular pleasure because it is widely recognised that you are respected by all right hon. and hon. Members in this House and I am sure that I speak for all members of the Committee when I say that I do not doubt that you will chair the meeting with your customary diligence and skill.

I open the debate with an explanation of the delay in bringing the matter to the House following the original request from the Scrutiny Committee in its thirteenth report dated 3 March 1982. As the Department's explanatory memorandum of 30 March 1984 indicates, the problem was one of trying to fire at a moving target. The original version of the proposal was widely regarded as being unworkable so we waited and, in due course, last year, the revision process began. We thought we had something definite in our sights last November when the Commission Col. 2 issued an amended proposal, based on a simplified version considered by the European Parliament, but no, the Presidency decided to ask the Council's environment working group to continue work on its own revised text.

I regret there is no official version of this latter text to put before the Committee today because the Commission has not produced one. We have to make do with a Department of Trade and Industry informal text, which lacks the usual preamble containing items in square brackets where agreement between member states is still outstanding, and which, as hon. Members have probably already discovered, bears all the hallmarks of a document written by a committee.

At least the purpose of the proposal, as expressed in article 1, is clear—namely to provide for a series of measures relating to the production, marketing, use and disposal of containers of liquids for human consumption in order to improve the environment and to save energy and raw materials. It is also about the only article of the directive on which all sides have been agreed from the start.

The United Kingdom line throughout the lengthy discussions in Brussels has been to support the underlying purpose of the proposal, which is to improve the environment and to save energy and raw materials, but to question the need for a directive in this area. We believe that a recommendation would be the appropriate legal form to adopt. Unfortunately, at the last count, this view was shared only by one other member state, but another is undecided and a third has major reservations. We also have the backing of the European Parliament, which gave its opinion last July in favour of a recommendation. I must not disguise the fact that we face an uphill struggle to get this line accepted at the Council of Ministers where my colleague, the Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, leads for the UK. The views of Members of this House, both on the proposal as a whole and on its contents, will assist the Government in deciding their final position.

Our reasons for preferring a recommendation are threefold: first, the lack of convincing evidence for binding Community legislation in this area; secondly, because a recommendation would fit in better with the need to adopt measures appropriate to local circumstances in each member state; and thirdly, because there is concern among trade and industry Image Col. 3 bodies that a directive would create fresh barriers to trade, or distortions of trade, and discourage innovation in packaging, and there is concern among consumers about their freedom of choice in the shops. If I now turn to the text of the directive, I think the justification for our position will become clearer.

The problems start in articles 3 and 4. The original version required member states to fix, for each type of packaging material, annual targets for a reduction in the quantity of containers in household waste and for an increase in the amount of refilled or recycled containers. That would have been an impossible task, especially as the targets were ones to be achieved and not merely aimed at. Instead, under the present version, member states would be free to draw up their own programmes to reduce the quantity of containers in household waste, and to encourage refilling and recycling where market conditions allow. Voluntary agreements are permitted under article 4, and I am confident that a satisfactory UK programme could be based, if necessary, on existing voluntary arrangements such as the bottle bank and can recycling schemes, without recourse to legislation.

There is concern in trade and industry circles, however, about how other member states would react. While the wording of the articles appears to be more or less even-handed between refillable and recyclable containers, they fear that some countries will use the obligatory nature of a directive as an excuse to bring in legislation against non-refillables. There is a reminder in article 4 about the provisions of the treaty relating to the free movement of goods. A case involving Denmark is currently before the European Court of Justice, however, and the results of that will provide more specific guidance for the future.

The consequences of restrictive national legislation could be twofold: loss of export opportunities for UK manufacturers on the one hand, and the threat of unwanted foreign overcapacity flooding the UK market on the other. The British Steel Corporation, which has a major and growing business in the supply of steel strip for non-returnable beer and soft drinks cans both to the UK and to other European countries, has drawn attention to the adverse effects such restriction would have both on the market for tinplate and consequently on employment in south Wales. A recommendation, even if it contained exactly the same wording as the proposed directive, would not have the same binding force as a directive, because it is not obligatory, and the temptation to bring in discriminatory legislation would be that much smaller.

As regards Article 5, which concerns the marking of refillable containers, hon. Members will be pleased to note that we successfully excluded milk and cream bottles for doorstep delivery from this requirement.

Article 6 is a much weakened version of the original article on standardisation. However, we still have reservations about it on the grounds that standardisation, although an important aspect in the development of the Common Market, is a complex issue which deserves separate and detailed consideration, and we would prefer to see the whole article deleted. On this we are at one with UK trade Col. 4 and industry bodies; on the other hand, we do not share their fears that the article could lead to the disappearance of imperial measures. That seems to be an exaggerated view because, at present, apart from still wine, there is no discrimination in the EEC between imperial and metric sizes.

Annex 1 contains a list of liquids to be covered by the directive. Hon. Members will note that it includes liquids other than beverages. We believe that items 2 —vegetable oils—and 11—vinegar—should be deleted from the list on technical grounds as their containers are extremely difficult to clean for refilling. This point has been acknowledged in Brussels and we hope that it will be suitably recorded.

Annex 2, the reporting guidelines, is considered to be unrealistic as, apart from the first indent, the information is not generally available and would involve a very costly exercise to collect. This probably applies to all member states. Fortunately, the request for such information is qualified by the words "as far as possible", but the whole annex would probably best be deleted.

I hope that I have said enough to show Members why we regard this proposal for a directive to be unnecessary legislation which could have undesirable side effects and why a recommendation would be more appropriate.

Finally, the timing of this debate was made in the expectation that the proposal on beverage containers would be on the agenda of the Council meeting to be held on 28 June. This will not now be the case. At the earliest, it will appear in the late autumn. I hope that the delay will enable other member states to give further consideration to the implications of the proposal. I hope too that the problems of firing at a moving target, which I mentioned at the start, will not be compounded by the target itself disappearing from sight.

Mr. Bryan Gould (Dagenham): I should like to associate the Opposition side of the Committee with the welcome advanced by the Minister to you, Miss Boothroyd. We are extremely glad to see you in the Chair and confident that our proceedings are in very good hands.

I thank the Minister for his explanation of this elusive directive. We have no copy of the text, and we are having to make do with the DTI's explanatory memorandum. However, the Minister has taken us through the provisions of this controversial measure in a fair, and properly sceptical, way.

I shall have to leave the Committee almost as soon as I sit down, as I have a pressing engagement elsewhere. I have explained the position to the Minister, but I hope that it does not mean that he will listen to me with any less care and attention or fail to deal with the points that I hope to raise. I believe that they will also be raised by my right hon. or hon. Friends.

The draft directive is an astonishing example of the EEC Commission's tenacity. The measure has been around for some years now. It has been kicked around in various forms and seen by various institutions and forums, almost all of which have looked upon it with Image Col. 5 less than great enthusiasm. Nevertheless, it has been revived yet again. It is before us in its original legislative form as a draft directive rather than as the draft recommendation which the European Assembly thought more appropriate. It has admittedly been somewhat watered down from its original form, but nevertheless all the potential for damage to British industry and the operation of British trade remains.

It is extraordinary that the Commission should have been so insensitive to the views expressed over some years by various trade organisations, our Select Committee on European legislation and the European Assembly.

The directive is an example not only of tenacity, but of the Commission's almost fatal temptation to extend its jurisdiction to intervention in areas where it is difficult to see that any useful purpose is being served. One pictures Commission officials sitting round wondering what they can do next. This is clearly one of the issues that they swept up from the bottom of the pile, saying, "Let us now turn our attention to this. It may not be important, and we may not have anything sensible to say, but perhaps the damage it does will be relatively limited." That does not seem a proper basis on which to advance proposals for legislation.

The supposed purpose of the measure is to deal with two problems. The first is the environmental problem created by containers of beverages; secondly, it seeks to reduce energy consumption in the production of disposable containers.

The answer on the litter issue is conclusive. The Waste Management Advisory Council in an authoritative report in 1981 made it clear that refuse of this type accounted for only 3 per cent. of household waste and metal containers only 1 per cent. It regarded a measure to control this type of container at the point of production, as it were, as at best marginal in any effort to control litter. It said, rightly—I am sure that it will command support from all members of the Committee—that if we are properly to address the real problem of litter it should be done by concentrating on the litter and those who create it rather than on production.

In general terms, we should have sympathy with energy saving. There is no doubt that those engaged in the business are, for good commercial reasons, intent on reducing energy consumption in the production of the containers. I was sent a letter by Coca Cola, one of the largest bottlers and retailers of beverages. Coca Cola told me that at its Milton Keynes bottling and container plant, in just over a year, it has reduced its energy consumption by 25 per cent. Ordinary commercial considerations are constantly pushing in that direction. It seems unlikely that an indirect means of trying to get at the problem will be effective. It is worth pointing out that if the concept of refillable and recyclable containers is genuinely to save energy by comparison with metal containers, the average number of return trips will have to rise to 17 before significant energy savings are made. The present average is about four return trips. It is hard to see that, even if the directive were given full legislative effect, properly monitored and enforced, it would get to grips with the two problems which the Commission believes that it is addressing.

Col. 6 The response of those who would be directly affected is almost uniformly negative. I, and I am sure many other members of the Committee, have received representations from a variety of bodies, almost all of them hostile. I mentioned Coca Cola and I read a letter from the Scotch Whisky Association, both of which say that the measures are not only unnecessary but would impose an unacceptable burden upon them and, in some cases, be inappropriate. The notion of refillable containers for Scotch whisky is a little difficult to grapple with. The Metal Packaging Manufacturers' Association, has written to me, too, to say that 8,000 jobs depend on the industry and that they fear—I shall return to this point later—that the measures may lead to an inhibition of trade. It is extremely anxious on that account. Behind the metal packaging industry stands the steel industry, especially in South Wales. There is a complex of industrial plant there which depends on this industry. My right hon. and learned Friend the Member for Aberavon (Mr. Morris) will be speaking to that point in a moment.

The industrial interests which are affected are very much opposed to the proposals. The retail industry, through the medium of the Retail Consortium points out that refillable containers would impose considerable burdens on its members in terms of special storage and collections, all, it believes, unnecessary and adding substantially to costs. Consumer organisations—one might have thought that they would look kindly on the measures as they have a consumer protection flavour—think that they are unnecessary, would reduce consumer choice and would, in the end, be unenforceable. I am sure that the Minister is aware that of the 47 bodies who have been asked for representations, just two, one of the environmental organisations and one other, said that there may be something to be said for the proposals.

We are left with the question of why the EEC is persisting with the measures. I do not wish to subscribe too fully to the conspiracy theory of politics, which is always suspect, but I wonder if a clue might not be given by the treatment of the edible oil issue. It is astonishing, for the reasons which the Minister has given, that edible oils were incorporated in this measure in the first place. The notion of refillable containers for beverages does not sit easily with the use of cooking oil in one's kitchen, but it is astonishing that olive oil was excluded. That is blatant discrimination.

I know that we do not produce olive oil but many of our EEC colleagues do. A measure which placed the burden of restrictions on those producing vegetable oils, but excluded olive oil, would have been seen as extremely unfair and discriminatory. Given that that was overt—it was on the face of the measure— I cannot help but wonder whether a similar approach may not lie behind the measure as a whole.

I understand that the tinplate manufacturers and the Metal Packaging Manufacturers Association are extremely worried about the measure. It has been promoted as a step towards environmental protection, saving of energy and possibly towards harmonisation in the long term, but its real effect, if not purpose, may be to provide pretexts for protectionist measures Image Col. 7 or inhibition of trade. The trade is valuable to our industry and we are discussing valuable markets. A measure that gives our EEC colleagues a reason to legislate and say, like the Danes, that they cannot accept British products because they do not comply with this or that EEC-backed legislation, should be resisted.

We are left with a final question and it is on this point only that I see disagreement with the Minister. If no one has a good word to say for the measure and most of the substantive comment draws attention to its potential for damage and harm, why are we entertaining it? The Minister said that a recommendation would be more acceptable than a directive, but why do we need even a recommendation? It may be the acorn which is intended eventually to grow into the oak tree. We have seen that happen before with EEC legislation. Given the hostility that has been shown to the measure by almost all affected interests in this country, the Minister should say to the environment council that the emperor has no clothes. The measure is nonsense; there is no reason for it so why have it?

Legislation should be foisted on people for very good reasons only. Unless there is a good reason, we should not have the measure. That is a perfectly acceptable principle. Committee members may vary about where or whether legislation is required, but in this instance, we are unanimous that legislation is not required.

We should take advantage of that unanimity. The Minister should go to Brussels with the full backing of the Committee, the House of Commons and all industrial interests affected and say that we will not agree to the measure. I imagine that the Minister is limited because it is not a matter affecting a vital national interest and there may not be a veto power. I should like to hear his comments about that. But he should not go too easily for the lesser of two evils if he feels that there is any realistic option of removing the evil altogether.

Mr. Gareth Wardell(Gower) : It is a pleasure to attend a Committee of which, like my right hon. and learned Friend the Member for Aberavon (Mr. Morris), I am not a member. Most of the arguments have been made already but I shall reiterate them to ensure that the Minister is clear about where I stand. Of the 3.4 billion cans used as beverage containers in the United Kingdom, more than 55 per cent. have steel bodies. As beverage containers form 50 per cent. of United Kingdom tinplate production, all of which takes place in south Wales, the impact on the industry of any curtailment in the production of this commodity would he very large.

The drink cans market is one of the few British Steel Corporation growth sectors in the currently depressed steel market. It earns valuable export orders to Europe and BSC has already invested in technological improvements to its product, providing Col. 12 more efficient use of raw materials and reducing costs. Alcoa, the major producer of aluminium tops, is based, as I said, at Waunarlwydd in south Wales. My right hon. Friend the Member for Swansea, West (Mr. Williams) would have been with us this morning had he not been with the Director-General of Fair Trading on another matter.

I gather that the EEC proposal seeks to reduce the volume of containers in household waste, and to promote the recycling of containers, thereby to conserve both raw materials and energy. However, as my hon. Friend the Member for Dagenham (Mr. Gould) has said, the thrust of the proposals is not justified by the conclusions of the extensive study of this subject by the Waste Management Advisory Council in its "Study of Returnable and Non-returnable Containers". The report was published in 1981, and in its introduction it emphasises how, when it was commissioned in 1977, energy and conservation issues were in the forefront of the council's deliberations. No doubt that reflected the then recent uncertainty over oil and energy prices, in particular, and the conservation of world resources in general.

The report states that the absence of beverage containers from household waste would produce no measurable savings in waste collection costs and that beverage containers, especially cans, are comparatively cheap to dispose of compared to other waste. The Metal Packaging Manufacturers Association has informed me that metal beverage containers comprise less than 1 per cent. of household waste, and that cans, especially those in tin, with their magnetic properties, are the easiest to deal with in waste disposal and recycling. The MPMA also informs me that refillable bottles would have to increase their trippage to 17 to save on the waste disposal costs of cans.

The proposed draft directive appears to be unwisely geared to the promotion of refillable containers to the detriment of recycling programmes and, at the same time, threatens some sectors of the United Kingdom economy.

BSC Tinplate at Velindre, in my constituency, together with the other tinplate plants at Trostre and Ebbw Vale, are extremely concerned that a deposit or tax adjustment might be used to encourage the EEC's Image Col. 13 proposed aim of returnable containers. Without a doubt, such measures would lead to job losses in the industry and at Alcoa. Deposit systems would lead to an increase in aluminium bodies, which are not made at Waunarlwydd, at the expense of home-produced steel, all of that aluminium body being imported, with obvious detrimental consequences to the balance of payments. There would also be knock-on effects in those industries which serve the large employers of labour in south Wales. The Waste Management Advisory Council report suggests that 1,700 jobs could be lost in BSC in South Wales. That figure may now be a higher proportion of the whole given the scaling down of the industry which has taken place since the advisory council made its estimates in 1977. Given the potential job losses in the service sector and among small firms it is only realistic to suppose that measures which discriminate against cans would involve large scale job losses.

The three counties of Glamorgan have the highest rates of long-term unemployment in Wales and among the highest in the United Kingdom. We cannot support any measure, however well intentioned on environmental grounds, which could exacerbate our unemployment problem.

Worry about the example of Denmark prompts my concern that the additional burden on local authorities responsible for waste disposal will be intolerable. Wer are all aware of the present financial constraints on local authorities and it is unrealistic to expect them to invest in new waste technology and collection and disposal methods at this time.

An EC recommendation aslong the lines proposed might serve as a useful indication of the direction that the EEC intends to take in waste management and conservation of resources, but, desirable as those aims are, in the short-term the measures would result in an unacceptable level of job losses in South Wales and would impose an impossible burden on local authorities. At a time of financial stringency, we have to be careful to ensure that financial resources are spent on clearly defined priority areas, and the disposal and recycling of beverage containers is not of crucial importance. An EC directive is, therefore, inappropriate and unrealistic.

There seems to be unanimity in the Committee, which is a rare but welcome phenomenon in the House, and I hope that it will recommend a veto of this draft instrument. I hope that the measure will be opposed whenever possible.
