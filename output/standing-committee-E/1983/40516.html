[SIR ANTHONY MEYER in the Chair]

EUROPEAN COMMUNITY DOCUMENTS NOS. 9706/82, 8024/83, /8296/83, 4987/83, 7804/83, 8925/83, 9865/83, 8110/83, 8051/83 and 7809/83 CONCERNING ENERGY ISSUES IN THE EUROPEAN COMMUNITY

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Department of Energy (Mr. Alick Buchanan-Smith)
: I beg to move,

That this Committee takes note of European Community Documents Nos. 9706/82, 8024/83, 8296/83, 4987/83, 7804/83, 8925/83, 9865/83, 8110/83, 8051/83 and 7809/83; and fully supports appropriate cost-effective measures that will help to minimise the Community's dependence on imported oil.

I begin by apologising to the Committee for the mountain of documents before us, but I hope that the Committee will appreciate that this is the first debate that we have had on these matters for a long time and that the documents cover a considerable period. The Scrutiny Committee examined them and rightly decided that they were appropriate for debate. The time is also appropriate for debate because they have been under discussion in the Council of Energy Ministers a number of times, and, on some of the issues, the discussion which has been general so far may be beginning to sharpen up. As I represent the United Kingdom at the Council of Energy Ministers, I should appreciate the views of the Committee about the discussions that I have had at the council.

The motion before us covers a fundamental aspect of the Community's approach to energy, which is to minimise dependence on imported oil. That has been a priority since the first oil shock in 1973 and is enshrined in the Community's energy objectives up to 1990.

The documents before us are comprehensive in their coverage of energy issues, with the exception, perhaps, of nuclear power. But that issue will be subject to a separate debate if the recommendations of the Scrutiny Committee are implemented.

I refer to the main document, 7809/83, entitled "Energy and Energy Research in the Community: a five year programme of action and its financing". It sets out the overall scene and summarises the achievements of the Community since agreeing the 1990 objectives in June 1980. It also describes radical changes in the world oil market since then and urges the Community not to fall into the trap of allowing a softening market to throw Governments, industries and energy consumers off a prudent course.

The Government, and all member states, had little difficulty in endorsing that premise in principle. Even now, less than a year since the Commission produced its paper, changing events prove the fallacy of expecting certainty in energy supplies.

The achievement and maintenance of energy self- 4 sufficiency must continue to be a very high priority, and the paper quite rightly warns that dangers lie in a temptation not to sustain effort when there is no short-term stimulus to do so. The relative glut of oil that we have now must not lead to a failure to take sensible decisions about new investment. Our security of supply up to the year 2000 and beyond depends upon a variety of sources of energy. We must focus on developing the alternatives, such as wind, tidal, biomass, and so on. But in the foreseeable future the major contribution to energy demand will continue to be met from traditional sources: the economic production of coal, oil and natural gas and the generation of electricity—with due regard to safety and environmental factors—from coal and nuclear-fired stations. In addition to investment in production, we must continue to encourage energy consumers to invest in energy saving and the more rational use of energy. It is important that the energy industries continue to give the right pricing indications to consumers if we are to have the rational use of energy.

Nevertheless, as the paper acknowledges, energy is only one factor of production which member states and the Community as a whole must take into account. Although we need to ensure that the situation in respect of energy is as stable as possible, we cannot accept that at any price. We must set overall objectives and then decide where our priorities lie within those objectives.

Paper 7809/83 offers four guidelines which the Government endorse. First, it requires Community programmes to take into account programmes and actions within individual member states and avoid duplication and waste of effort—for example, in research, development and demonstration. Secondly, it requires the Community to provide a framework for the economic supply and consumption of energy. Thirdly, it requires the Community to have a continuing interest in energy expenditure. Fourthly, it requires the Community to avoid rigidity in its programmes and policies.

The paper goes on to describe a programme of action including a number of initiatives which are already under way in the Community such as work on solid fuels, energy demonstration projects, non-nuclear research and development projects—about which we have documents before us today—and areas which are not yet the object of Community action, such as the integration of electricity networks, exploration for hydrocarbons, and greater flexibility of the gas supply system.

If that programme is pursued for five years, the Commission assesses that it will cost some 2 billion écu at 1983 prices. That is a substantial sum and, if agreed, would be a radical departure in terms of Community expenditure.

In discussing the proposal the Council agreed that it had been useful to explore energy throughout the Community in this broad-brush fashion and to consider all the possibilities for action. The Government accept the need to avoid waste and duplication—a point that was originally raised at Question Time in the House—and to ensure that we adopt a cost-effective approach in the face of public expenditure constraints in all member states. Therefore, it is important to select priorities, especially those areas which are ripe for action.

The two areas in which the United Kingdom has sought to make progress are energy demonstration, JPEG 5 which is covered by document 8110/83, and solid fuels, which is covered by documents 4987/83, 7804/83, 8925/83 and 9865/83. The Foreign Affairs Council agreed in March that expenditure of 265 million écu in 1983–86 should be allowed in support of the third Community energy demonstration project programme. Although that total sum has been agreed, the exact allocation of that funding between the various areas of work that might benefit—for example, coal gasification and liquefaction, energy saving, biomass, wind and solar power—remains to be finalised. Questions of cost-effectiveness will obviously affect the outcome of any discussions on that.

Mr. Ted Rowlands
(Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney)
: Will the Minister again tell the Committee the agreed figures for the demonstration project?

Mr. Buchanan-Smith
: The amount agreed at the Foreign Affairs Council was 265 million écu but, as I said, the allocation of that figure is still open for discussion.

The Commission's document covers a very thorough appraisal of the state of solid fuels; of coal, which is of primary interest to the United Kingdom, peat, or turf as it is called in the Republic of Ireland, and lignite, which is of special interest to Greece. Every aspect must be considered: investment in the modernisation and restructuring of economic production in coal use, environmental safeguards and research and development into coal technology. The Commission has argued for expenditure on such items of more than 1,800 million écu over a five-year period.

The Government believe that Community support for investment in the economic production of solid fuels in the United Kingdom should be a high priority for concerted Community action. We believe that it will be a cost-effective investment in an indigenous Community energy resource which contributes to the energy self-sufficiency of the Community to the end of this century and beyond.

We shall continue to press hard in the Energy Council for agreement to the Commission's proposals which we think would make a useful foundation stone upon which to build up further Community energy initiatives. I am disappointed that the agreement has so far not been reached, and I have to tell the Committee that we are not even close to agreement about the initiative on solid fuels. The matter is one of priority, and I shall continue to press it very strongly in meetings of the Energy Council.

Mr. Rowlands
: My apologies for interrupting, but I hope that it will benefit the Committee. The Minister said that the Government threw their full weight behind the Commission's proposal. Is that the draft regulation in document 8925/83 which contains the list of features of the proposed regulation? The Committee would like to know if that is what the Government are backing.

Mr. Buchanan-Smith
: We have not yet reached the stage of deciding on the details of the solid fuels initiative, and the individual items within it. We want to make sure that the solid fuels proposals are considered and not rejected out of hand, as some countries 6 might wish. I hope that we make progress in the Council of Ministers in getting down to the details of the initiative and I should welcome any views that members of the Committee might have, because no decision has been reached. That is the advantage of our debate, and I shall certainly take into account the views of hon. Members on individual aspects of the matter in drawing up lists of priorities within any solid fuels initiative.

The Council has been keen that the Community's energy expenditure should be undertaken with full regard to cost-effectiveness. We are not prepared to approve any measure simply for the sake of having it. That would be foolish and short sighted. I have stressed already the role that the Government and the Community see for energy saving as part of the Community's efforts to achieve self-sufficiency. However, it is important that the general desirability of that objective should not lead us to invest unwisely in any specific aspect of the programme.

Documents 9706/82, 8024/83 and 8296/83 deal with the Commission's proposal for the rational use of energy. I believe that the Committee is aware, from its knowledge of the Community's work on energy, that the Community and the Government place a high priority on energy saving. None the less, the Council so far has been reluctant to support this proposal, and it might help if I explained why.

The problem is that it was proposed to make a sum of 150 million écu available over five years for interest rate subvention of commercial loans to invest in conventional energy-saving equipment. It was not clear, taking into account all the financial aspects, that the proposal would be any more attractive to consumers than normal commercial loans already available. All in all, member states felt that to approve this idea would not further the Community's energy-saving objectives, nor would it use the Community's financial resources cost-effectively. It seems likely that at the next Energy Council meeting the Commission may ask the Council to record that agreement could be reached on this draft regulation.

The final document to which I wish to refer, which is currently before the Council, is the programme of expenditure on non-nuclear research and development. Document 8051/83 describes a proposal for expenditure of up to 379 million écu on research into the development of renewable sources and the rational use of energy. Those are important items in striving for energy self-sufficiency. However, when the Research Council looks at the proposal later this year, I hope that it will temper its desire to make progress with due concern for realistic and cost-effective expenditure.

I apologise to the Committee for subjecting it to such a mass of documentation and for speaking at speed on those items. I sought only to summarise the main issues involved. None the less, I hope that I have managed to concentrate on the main issues and focus on those areas which I, as the Minister representing the Government at the Council of Ministers, think are important. I welcome the Committee's views on the priorities that I spelt out and other matters. I know that hon. Members will have gone through the documents in considerable detail and with care. I appreciate that there are a number of important points that I have not managed to cover this morning. I shall listen with JPEG 7 care to matters raised by hon. Members which I have not mentioned and bear such comments carefully in mind when they are considered in the Council of Ministers. I have tried to clarify some of the issues before us and I thank the Committee for its patience in listening to me. I commend the documents to hon. Members and ask them to support the motion.

10.49 am

Mr. Rowlands
: I do not like to admit a sense of inferiority in the presence of a Minister, but I shall have to confess to it because we know that the right hon. Gentleman has had long experience of handling EEC documents. He paddled his way through a common fisheries policy and ploughed his way through a common agricultural policy in his previous role; now he has turned his attention to a common energy policy. I hope that British coal does not go the way that the herring went and that we can concentrate on the issues raised because some very important issues are buried in these documents.

I have split the documents into two. There is a group which deals with the tortuous courses that have been followed by the Commission and various energy councils to try to find a set of practical proposals to promote and develop the coal industry within the EEC since 1974, and, secondly, there is a closely allied set of documents which deals with research, development and demonstration projects. Because we can lose wood for trees—or trees for wood—among the documents, I wish to draw out and underline the context of the ideas and arguments and some of the principles that have been raised.

The context of the documents is one that is rightly suggested in the motion. There is a justifiably passionate desire, after the oil price increases in 1973-74 and the problems that arose in a Europe short of oil, to minimise the Community's dependence on imported oil. I regret that the motion does not also include a major argument that arose, which was a generally accepted principle. We should not only try to minimise the Community's dependence on imported oil but should positively promote and develop indigenous resources within the EEC. The centrepiece of that alternative strategy was the development of coal production and its use. I do not believe that I would stretch the consensus in the Committee by adding that provision.

Many of the documents reflect an attempt to grope towards a series of practical solutions and proposals to achieve that aim. After 1974 we became over-dependent on imported oil and rediscovered the value of coal. We had forgotten how precious that indigenous fuel was. Successive Governments ran the industry down because it was felt that coal had no real value when oil was being bought for cents a barrel. Suddenly, overnight, assumptions of energy policy were changed and we rediscovered the value and importance of coal, which is one of the largest indigenous resources in the Community.

Since 1974, there have been Summit meetings in Paris, Tokyo and Venice, and a variety of informal meetings in Copenhagen that are referred to in the documents. The principle and the context of those meetings related to the way in which our coal industry could be promoted and developed as a major alter- 8 native source of imported energy resources. Therefore, it would be worthwhile to consider what has happened since 1974. Given the rhetoric at Summit meetings on those issues, what has happened?

Some key decisions were made in 1974. Imported energy was supposed to be cut within the EEC from 63 per cent to 40 per cent. Oil was to be cut from 61 per cent to 40 per cent; solid fuels were to be cut from 23 per cent to 17 per cent, but increasing in total overall amounts. Natural gas was to be increased from 11 per cent to 23 per cent; and nuclear was to rise from 1.5 per cent to 16 per cent in the strategy that was laid down in the mid 1970s.

There have been reviews of those objectives—there was one in 1978 and another in 1979. I hate to add to the Committee's burden, but it is sad that we have not included another important document, which was published in June 1983. I call it Com 83/305 (Final) but I do not know what other numbers are given to it. It reviews the progress of those objectives during the four years 1979–83.

The document demonstrates that there has been tremendous achievement in the cut in dependence on imported oil, mainly because consumption has fallen dramatically. The recession has been the most effective cause of conservation of resources and cuts in imported oil. One of the more significant changes is that one member of the Community became a major oil exporter during that period—the United Kingdom. Around 26 to 30 million tonnes of United Kingdom oil now goes to the EC.

The figures showing that the EC and Europe have become less dependent on imported oil have resulted from two factors. The first is that consumption has fallen dramatically as a result partly of the recession and partly of conservation. The second factor is the amount of oil that the United Kingdom has exported to the EC since 1979. As the document illustrates, if one takes out the United Kingdom contribution of oil, it is still frightening how dependent the EC is on oil. The average is 46 per cent dependence, which is much higher than some of the objectives. Although the figures have dropped satisfactorily overall and we seem to be on target, in many cases—especially that of the Italians, who are 67 per cent dependent on imported oil —we must temper our sense of achievement by checking and cautiously assessing the figures.

Many other interesting factors demonstrate whether we have come close to those targets, including what happened to coal in 1974–84. It is commonly agreed that during that 10-year period coal was to be the centrepiece of an alternative strategy. But what happened to production, consumption and importation of coal in Europe?

I tried to dig out the figures on that and I shall be grateful if the Minister will confirm whether my figures are right. They are a salutary reminder of systems, organisations, and Governments, which set objectives. We can then see whether those objectives have been met during a 10-year period. Coal was to be the centrepiece of that alternative strategy for removing our dependence on imported energy sources but the figures do not give any comfort. First, let us take EC production in 1974. The figures are slightly misleading because there was a strike in the United Kingdom at the tail-end of 1974. Let us take 1974–75. Coal production in the EC was JPEG 9 245 to 255 million tonnes. In 1983 it was 229 million tonnes.

One of the strategies that we were supposed to adopt was the development of our indigenous coal industry as an alternative major fuel source. However, we failed because there was a significant fall in production of coal during the 10-year period 1974–1984.

Secondly, consumption has fallen as a result of the recession. Coal has been the chief victim of the recession and of Government policies generally. An estimated figure for consumption within the EC is around 280 million tonnes compared with a figure in 1973–74 of 296 million, nearly 300 million tonnes. Therefore we are producing less and consuming less of our major indigenous fuel as a result of a variety of factors.

Despite what was said in Paris in December 1974 and in Venice and Tokyo about the role of coal—the documents are an intrinsic part of the practical attempts to carry forward the issue—in that 10-year period, almost the opposite has happened in respect of the concepts, notions, thoughts and ideas of Governments and Ministers. In EC terms, there has been a large and significant increase in imported coal. Coal was supposed to be the alternative indigenous fuel to counter imported energy resources. Indeed, there is a touch of irony because indigenous fuel has become a major source of imports into the EC. The figure has risen from 48 million tonnes to well over 70 million tonnes in the early 1980s, and now stands at about 60 million tonnes.

Whatever conclusions are drawn—I shall make some shortly—about the thinking on how to identify and handle the problems thrown up a decade ago as a result of the oil price hype and the oil revolution which occurred in 1973–74, one major objective certainly does not appear to be on course—namely, the dominant role to be played by coal in EC terms. Consumption has fallen and production has decreased dramatically. Throughout the EC there is talk of major cuts in coal capacity. It is curious that 10 years after the EC started on the policy of encouraging that major source of fuel this country is less dependent on oil imports but is becoming increasingly reliant on coal imports to meet demand.

That point is underlined in a report from another place on EC strategy and objectives, which came out this week. It is interesting, although some of the conclusions are bizarre. However, the facts and statistics are informative. There is an incredible statistic for the year 2000—I do not really believe in such predictions, and I do not suppose that anyone else here does either —which suggests that by then the EC will require 250 million tonnes of imported coal. It should be remembered that at present the world trading market in coal is only about 200 million tonnes. Rightly, the report casts some scepticism about the possibility of such an amount being delivered and the desirability of the EC being dependent on it. The context of these documents and the issues raised by them in relation to the coal industry must be seen in the light of what I have tried to describe.

It might useful for me to declare where I stand on the matter: I do not believe in a common energy policy for the EC. I do not think that is obtainable or necessarily desirable. It is not possible to nudge, push or cajole nine or 10 countries with completely different existing and historical patterns of economic and energy develop- 10 ment into a common energy view. There is a disparate range of requirements—that is why so little has come our of all the discussions and documents on the issues—and little in common, in energy terms, between, say, Italy and the United Kingdom. The Italians need to import most of their energy resources. No doubt their greatest wish is to do so as cheaply as possible, and from whatever source. But it is not necessarily in the interest of other countries with large coal industries to promote a totally free market in imported coal for the EC.

The United Kingdom is energy rich with millions of tonnes of oil equivalent and last year had an energy surplus, with about 60 million tonnes of oil equivalent. That is vastly different from the problems facing the Italians, the Irish or, in certain respects, the French. Any notion that we should try to achieve a common energy EC policy will founder. The danger, if we try for that, is that we shall end up with another CAP.

I represent a coalmining community and we have looked enviously at the CAP. Why not have mountains of coal financed from sources other than ourselves to maintain production? Why not have a common coal policy financed in the same way that we finance massive agricultural surpluses, with large protectionist barriers to make sure that some nations such as ourselves cannot buy cheaper food from certain markets because of the necessity to buy European? If such a policy were offered, I would be tempted in a traditional, old-fashioned way to take what was on offer and carry on producing.

I assume that it is not the Government's aim to create a common energy or coal policy on the lines of the CAP. I do not believe that there is either the will or the community of interest to create a common energy policy. However, we can try to find, in practical and simple ways, some common objectives and then see whether we can produce practical measures to achieve them.

Some of the objectives in the documents under discussion are perfectly unobjectionable and non-controversial, and it is surprising how little progress has been made. The concept of the demonstration projects —of trying to get some form of co-ordination in research and development programmes—among the Governments of the Nine is eminently sensible and practical. It is tragic that little progress has been made even on that rather minimalist idea or position. Although I was interested that in March the Foreign Affairs Council endorsed a budget for demonstration projects, I have a horrible feeling that this time next year we shall still be arguing over which project.

Britain obviously has a major interest and the Minister wants liquefaction and gasification, which are matters of considerable importance to us. Research has been quite highly developed and any backing and support that can be given through a common research and development policy as described in some of the documents would be invaluable.

The Minister appeared to be pouring cold water on hopes of any real progress in the rational use of energy. He picked out only one suggestion from the documents. There are questions of boiler conversion and combined heat and power. I shall be grateful if the Minister will tell us in his reply how far we have progressed towards achieving something, agreeing something and spending JPEG 11 something in practical terms arising from the ideas contained in the documents.

The sum total of his speech, despite the invaluable information that it contained—and this is not a criticism—is that almost nothing has reached the point of agreement arising from this range of documents. In particular, there has been no agreement on the central set of documents in regard to the working party's proposal for solid fuel.

The Commission's proposals are based upon what appears to be a series of reasonable suggestions—that Community aid should be given for investment in modernisation and the development of new solid fuel production; that there should be a transfer of funds from the EC budget to the ECSC budget to provide moneys for rationalisation of the coal industry; that there should be Community aid to encourage a reduction in the current high levels of producers' coal stocks and that there should be revised arrangements for providing aid for Community coking coal.

The Minister was rather coy in not giving more details. I believe that the Committee can bear—certainly, I can —much more detailed discussion of where the Government stand on these issues and how far the Community has proceeded on them. One reason why I intervened was that the Community proceeded as far as to produce a draft regulation. I shall not plough through it; as always, a nice explanatory memorandum has been provided by the Department of Energy and it gives the skeleton of the draft regulation. For the benefit of the Committee, the document to which I refer is dated 3 October 1983 and it relates to EC document No. 8925/83.

I can see the initial attraction to the United Kingdom of seeking to go down this road and trying to get firm agreement on the proposals. We have the largest coal industry in the EC and a large investment programme. However, we shall need an even larger one for the future development of our major indigenous resource of coal. If we can defray a percentage—for example, 25 per cent of the cost of that investment against EC funds—we shall be assisted in the budgetary argument, which I believe is referred to in the documents. We would get some money from other sources to finance investment in the coal industry. None of us should be opposed to that.

We have to be very careful—I wish to discover where the Government stand on this issue—about the conditions laid down in the draft regulation for making applications for such money. I believe that the Committee would find it worth while to study the explanatory memorandum dated 3 October 1983, which is why I wished to draw attention to it. It says that investment aid of the sort proposed

"will be conditional on the coal undertaking having obtained Commission approval for a modernisation and restructuring programme covering 1984 to 1988 for their operations".

That suggests that the Government and the National Coal Board would have to submit a major restructuring programme to the Commission before we could qualify for the money. Will the Government acknowledge that they would be prepared to do that to obtain the 25 per cent investment, because we would then go down the Davignon road that we travelled in regard to the steel industry when we made such commitments? I believe 12 that there is extensive cross-party feeling that the sort of structural changes that we then undertook were not matched by others.

Do we want to pursue, adopt and endorse, or become imprisoned in a Davignon-style policy for restructuring the coal industry, whereby the Commission starts to set specific national targets for production and capacity in the way that was done in the case of steel? My concern was aroused when I discovered that the letter accompanying the documents was signed by none other than Davignon. When Davignon signs documents, one must pay attention because we have experience of Vice-President's Davignon's influence on such matters. I refer to document COM(83) 447 dated 17 August 1983. It contains such major statements as:

"the structural mismatch of the Community coal supply position with respect to demand, a mismatch which is explained largely by the fact that Community coal is not competitive with imported coal. This makes it necessary to act on a broad front to modify the level of production".

—in the EEC.

In other words, we would be going down a path similar to that which we took on steel. The Government may wish to support that. They may believe that that is the way to proceed because it is the fundamental condition of our being given any of the investment aid mentioned in the draft regulations, one of the fundamental documents being considered by the Committee.

That is not the only condition. Let the Committee read on to the next condition imposed for attracting investment aid. The memorandum dated 3 October 1983 states:

"for hard coal, only projects expected to satisfy a defined productivity criterion will be eligible for aid".

Whose productivity criterion? What is it? Who laid it down? The Commission? Nation states? Who will establish this criterion and these conditions?

The memorandum also states that in relation to reduction of coal stocks

"aid will be conditional on the undertaking having first received aid for investment".

The restructuring programme, in turn, must be approved by the Commission. The document raises serious issues of national sovereignty and control over our decisions about a major industry, as happened when the restructuring of steel took place in the EEC. They are major issues which transcend the odd bit of aid and practical assistance to develop the purchase and sale of a few million tonnes of coal. Something much more fundamental is at stake here.

I was privileged to visit Germany a few months ago because I wished to try to understand some of these issues. I went to the Ministry of the Economy—Germany does not have a Ministry of Energy. The head of one of its sections, whom the Minister may know because he has been around a long time, is Mr. Engelmann. He has a magnificent and historic view of the coal industry in Germany. He made it clear that the Germans are unwilling to abrogate any form of control or sovereignty of decisions about the restructuring of the German coal industry. Elaborate discussions took place; I was there in the middle of them. I gained the impression that any obstacle to this type of document would come from the Germans if not from us. I shall be grateful if the Minister, who has much experience of attending these Energy Councils, can confirm that my JPEG 13 impressions are correct. I felt that under no circumstances would the Germans allow the EEC to crawl over the German coal industry. They regard it as of such national strategic importance, historically, politically and socially, in all areas and with all the problems, that they have no intention of allowing effective control over the restructuring of the industry to be taken away or partially transferred from them.

I do not think that I exaggerate. That is implied by the condition laid down in the draft regulations to enable us to collect 25 per cent grant of one kind or another from the EEC to invest in our coal industry.

The documents are designed to try to promote the use of coal. The context that seemed to be missing from the Minister's speech was, what sort of coal industry do we want? He will have another chance to reply. This is an continuing discussions, and I accept that this is merely one stage in a long process. But the Committee needs to consider that question.

I thought that the consensus in the 1970s in Paris, Tokyo and Venice was that Europe should have a large-capacity indigenous coal industry to develop alternative uses for coal not only for power stations but right across the board. Some of the documents reflect that. Yet the worrying sign is that EEC capacity will fall sharply.

I shall be grateful if the Minister—we amateurs tend to be more literate than numerate and experience difficulty in getting and understanding figures—will confirm the figures that I have on the EEC coal capacity as being roughly right and that the slight projection forward I shall make is within reason and accurate.
