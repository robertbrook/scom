[MRS. MARION ROE in the Chair]

World Trade Organisation Millennium Round

4.30 pm

The Minister of State for Trade (Mr. Richard Caborn)
: Thank you, Mrs. Roe, for taking this matter seriously and allowing us to have this debate. I should explain that I am not used to the procedure in this Committee.

Although most people are well informed about next week's round of negotiations in Seattle, it will be useful to update the Committee so that it is au fait with what is going on. The Government believe that we have a tremendous opportunity to move towards trade liberalisation, open markets and the removal of trade barriers. That is the object of the exercise. We want to expand globally the technological age to create more jobs and opportunities, in particular in the developing world. It is important to support Mike Moore, Director General of the World Trade Organisation, who has emphasised that the organisation should be open and rules based, with a distinct procedure that is accepted and followed by all its 134 members.

It is clear from newspaper articles and representations that a number of non-governmental organisations are critical of the WTO's role and the move towards trade liberalisation. They are worried about the effects that that will have on labour and the environment. I give an absolute assurance that our approach to the WTO is the same as our approach to other organisations in the international arena. In our negotiations with the International Monetary Fund, led by the Chancellor, Gordon Brown, and strongly supported by the Secretary of State for International Development, Clare Short, we aim to lift and restructure the debt burden that is placed on developing countries so that they can become a more effective part of the family of trading nations. Our approach to the European Union-South Africa trade agreement is also important because it shows our commitment to the liberalisation of trade. Managing that liberalisation is an important development in the globalisation of trade. We also have a significant role in the EU to move forward the comprehensive agenda that we hope will evolve in Seattle. We want to ensure that we free up trade, and that capacity building and facilitation of developing countries is taken seriously so that they are equal partners.

Although a major part of our work has taken place within the EU, some of us were in South Africa last week with the Commonwealth to build the bridges that are necessary to address the concerns and fears that developing countries have as they move into the next round of the WTO negotiations in Seattle. Our connections in the Commonwealth have helped us to allay those fears and to show that we are genuine partners in our efforts to liberalise world trade through the WTO, which we believe could benefit 4 developing countries. We also have connections with the United States which has found it difficult to accept a comprehensive round of negotiations in Seattle. Nevertheless, we shall make strong representations. On every international front—the United Kingdom's relationship with the United States, with the Commonwealth and with the EU—we played a positive and significant role in drawing up a comprehensive agenda to be followed at Seattle that will address the concerns and fears of all the countries to which I referred.

There must be a political will, not only to set a comprehensive agenda but to deliver that agenda within three years. That target is achievable. We do not want such lengthy procrastination as followed the Uruguay round. We want to ensure that decisions are made—as they should be—within three years and we want to bring certainty to trading patterns around the world. Such a political will should exist in Seattle, and it should continue so that the timetable may be concluded in three years.

For the World Trade Organisation—probably one of the newer organisations in the international arena—to be taken seriously, it must address the trade issues within a reasonable period. If it does that, it may be given the credibility to stand alongside the World bank, the International Monetary Fund and the other international institutions that facilitate orderly conduct in the global marketplace.

We are not keen on what has happened today in Geneva regarding bureaucrats. There will be much for politicians to sign at Seattle—probably more than there would have been had there been greater agreement at Geneva. However, we can proceed effectively next week in Seattle.

We must be sure of the process as well as the substance. Some rules must be strengthened and modernised and the organisation must be strongly based on rules. It must also have a dispute procedure that is respected by all of its 134 members. With that, it can stand alongside those other international institutions.

We are pleased to note the strong possibility of China becoming a full member of the WTO. We hope that the deal between the United States and China on market access will pave the way for a sound deal between the EU and China. We can now proceed with that deal, and we hope that we can finish it rapidly so that we may welcome China to the WTO. That is important and, as everyone knows, the United Kingdom has supported China's entry on the right terms. Significant progress has been made and we hope that China will become the 135th member of the WTO.

Several hon. Members rose—

The Chairman
: Order. We have until 5.30 pm for questions to the Minister. I remind members of the Committee that questions should be brief and asked one at a time. There is likely to be ample opportunity for all hon. Members to ask several questions.

Mrs. Angela Browning
(Tiverton and Honiton)
: May I first apologise to you, Mrs. Roe, and to the JPEG 5 Committee for my not having been in Committee when the Minister opened his remarks? I was delayed in another meeting and came here as quickly as I could.

What is the Minister's evaluation of the unfinished business from the Uruguay round, especially the commitment to liberalisation in the textile and clothing industry? Is that unfinished business a priority for the new talks that are about to begin?

Mr. Caborn
: I do not want to pre-empt what will happen in Seattle—we have said that we want a comprehensive round. You are right, Mrs. Browning: there is unfinished business regarding textiles and other matters. We do not want the unpicking of agreements already put to bed under the Uruguay round. There is more work to be done on the operation of decisions made at Uruguay and on the procedures for implementing them.

We shall ensure that those areas are dealt with in the Seattle round. There are concerns and fears, especially in relation to the developing world, that must be addressed. Some of the decisions that were taken in the light of the Uruguay round, which have not been implemented in the way that they should have been, must be revisited. I stress that the implementation of those decisions—to which, I believe, the reference to unfinished business relates—is different from the actual decisions that were made at Uruguay, which we believe should now be carried out.

Ms Joan Walley
(Stoke-on-Trent, North)
: I thank the Minister for his statement to the Committee. What detailed proposals does he have to assess the unfinished business in respect of concerns about environmental issues? How can he be sure that he can measure how we achieve sustainability as a result of the round that is just about to start in Seattle?

Mr. Caborn
: We must be clear about what sort of animal the WTO is. It is a world trade organisation. Its purpose is not to resolve all the ills of the world. It must, however, have a relationship with other organisations, whether it be the IMF, the World bank, or the United Nations Environment Programme, which deals globally with the whole question of the environment. The development of multilateral environmental agreements is very important, and, where necessary, those agreements should relate to the rules of the WTO. The discussions must include consideration of those rules, as well as their relationship to multilateral environmental agreements. At the moment, there are about 200 multilateral environmental agreements, of which at least 20 incorporate trade-related issues. It is in all our interests—whether at Kyoto or Montreal—that those agreements are linked to the WTO and that there is an understanding, but neither takes precedence. It is all about the sustainability of our environment, which we put at the top of our agenda.

Several codes for assessing the environment have been internationally agreed, and we expect those codes to be used. It is in everyone's interests to ensure that the environment and our resources are treated in the most sustainable way. That is the intention, and it would be far better if there were a proper relationship 6 between those who have responsibility for the environment and the WTO. We must ensure that the two are compatible.

Mr. Michael Jack
(Fylde)
: Are there any aspects of the work of the WTO in the next three years on which Britain will stand on its own and not take the EU position? In other words, what areas can we, the United Kingdom, negotiate, and what decisions are we bound by, as members of the European Union?

Mr. Caborn
: I will stand corrected on this, but my understanding is that we are now in a position of agreement with the EU. We exerted considerable influence on the final outcome in the EU in several areas, including the environment and labour issues. Also, Agenda 2000, which is based on the Uruguay round, deals with agriculture, and we have moved a considerable way towards ensuring that agriculture is on the agenda for Seattle in a positive way. There is no major area on which we depart from the European position, as it stands. I can hear Mr. Jack mumbling in the background, and if he wants to ask me a supplementary question, I am sure that you will allow him to do so, Mrs. Roe.

Dr. Stephen Ladyman
(South Thanet)
: There are many social practices that we, in this country, would consider unacceptable, such as child labour, but linking reform in such areas to trade often looks to a third world country a little like protectionism. How do the Government intend to balance the desire for reform with the desire not to cause any detrimental effect to third world industry?

Mr. Caborn
: That is important and is one of the areas of concern that has emerged in relation to the ILO core labour standards and the WTO. I return to the point that I made earlier to my hon. Friend the Member for Stoke-on-Trent, North (Ms Walley). The WTO is not a vehicle for the resolution of all the ills of the world. It is a trading organisation that is its job. However, it considers other matters, including labour. After the Singapore round, developing countries are extremely concerned about the possibility of labour going into the WTO remit, and, as part of a strong rules-based organisation, leading, as you rightly say, to the use of labour standards as a technical barrier to trade.

I have mentioned Britain's international links, and our relationship with the EU and the Commonwealth gives us a unique position to sound out feeling in the developing world. As my colleague Clare Short stated the other day, that is not to say that we condone child labour, but neither can the matter be resolved by being stuck into the WTO remit. We believe that child labour and other such practices should be tackled by trying to ensure that countries develop and obtain sustainable economies that enable them to trade themselves out of their present circumstances.

In the bigger picture, the IMF has removed $100 billion of debt from the least developed countries, and has undertaken the rescheduling of debt and, in some cases, total debt forgiveness. The liberation of money used to service such debts should enable developing countries to invest in their real economies. The Government have stated that the least developed JPEG 7 countries should have tariff-free access to the developed world market within the three-year lifetime of the agreement, and we have argued that view in the EU. We believe that such restructuring, involving scaling down debts, debt forgiveness and tariff-free access to markets for the least developed countries, is the best way of addressing problems such as child labour. I think that 48 of the least developed countries will benefit from such measures.

A forum should be established to enable us to move towards better understanding. We are suggesting to the EU the establishment of a joint WTO/ILO Standing Working Forum, and we hope that the proposal is on the Seattle agenda. Such an arrangement would facilitate bridge building outside the WTO and address problems with which such countries are geniunely concerned.

The Chairman
: I remind the Minister that he should not use the word "you". He should address his remarks through the Chairman, and should not call hon. Members by name. He must refer to them instead through their constituencies and as hon. Members.

Dr. Vincent Cable
(Twickenham)
: I apologise for arriving a little late. My question follows that of the right hon. Member for Fylde (Mr. Jack). Will the Minister spell out more concretely what concessions the UK made in reaching a common Community position on agriculture? There is a clear difference in tone between the Community position and the recent, forcefully liberal statement made by the Department of Trade and Industry.

Mr. Caborn
: To the best of my knowledge, there were no concessions. We are trying to ensure that the WTO can take on agriculture. We have a clear commitment on Uruguay and have set out our long-term aims. We have stated that we will be negotiating on the basis of Article 20 of the WTO Agriculture Agreement, which states:

"negotiation will be part of an ongoing process of fundamental reform, with the long-term objective of substantial, progressive support and protection."

The EU is serious about participating in this process and conclusions adopted at the Agricultural Council on 27 September recognise that. It is also clear that we have to take account of non-trade concerns such as the environmental and social roles of agriculture, but we believe that this must be done through targeted measures which do not distort trade. We have a clear position that is consistent with the decisions taken in the EU and with what the WTO laid out in the Uruguay round on the reform of agriculture. Clearly, there will be difficulties in the post-Seattle round of discussions, especially when we get the comprehensive agenda, but they need to be tackled and our position is consistent. There is no difference at this stage and both Labour and Conservative parties in the United Kingdom have always been in favour of the reform of the Common Agricultural Policy and the continuing removal of price support.

Miss Geraldine Smith
(Morecambe and Lunesdale)
: I have received several representations from 8 constituents, Church organisations in my constituency and various bodies concerned that negotiations may have an adverse effect on poorer countries. I would like an assurance that that will not be the case and that Britain will make representations, within Europe and wherever possible, to ensure that that does not happen. Can the Minister also explain some of the positive benefits to the developing countries that these negotiations may have?

Mr. Caborn
: Yes, absolutely. To some extent, we are talking about the credibility of the World Trade Organisation and how we handle these negotiations. The United Kingdom has been in the forefront in ensuring that the EU position is very clear: although we believe that it is in the best interests of developing countries to be part of the trading nations, and want to bring them in, that change needs management. That is why we spoke earlier about what we have done at the IMF. We have also made proposals for capacity building in developing countries, to ensure that they have the skills to negotiate in Geneva and can apply them in their own countries and in the global marketplace. We have put about £10 million into a fund to enable the developing countries to play their full part in the international negotiations. The WTO must revisit its responsibilities for capacity building and development to make it easier and less bureaucratic for the developing countries. That would be in everyone's interest.

We are putting in place a series of measures to facilitate developing countries in capacity building and proper engagement. In respect of their wider economic circumstances, when debt relief is needed we will be doing that too.

In 1997, there was a high-level WTO meeting on relief for other countries, which put trade-related technical assistance in an integrated framework, and that is coming through. Some refinements are needed: such assistance should be targeted much more on the needs of individual countries, which it is not at present. If it were in place, it would facilitate the ability of developing countries to manage change more effectively.

Miss Anne McIntosh
(Vale of York)
: I welcome the Minister's statement, especially in respect of opening up markets and liberalising trade globally. Does he agree that there is a danger of France, and, to a lesser extent, the United States, having a hidden agenda? In the case of France, it is already visible. France will hold presidential elections at about the time the millennium round is starting. It will take over the presidency of the EU in the second half of next year and seems to be set on a collision course with the Government's view, as set out by the Minister, about what they hope to achieve from the millennium round. In my view, the Government should be able to reduce subsidies across the board, especially in the western hemisphere countries.

The United States has a similar hidden agenda. Both France and the United States are displaying protectionist and regressive tendencies in what is meant to be a trade-liberalisation round. What safeguards can the Government give us now and during the progress of the millennium round that the JPEG 9 United Kingdom will not cave in to this hidden agenda but see through the smokescreens, push trade liberalisation and put an end to any form of protectionism by France or the United States?

Mr. Caborn
: The hon. Lady has every right to express those concerns. In principle, we are trying to mitigate those worst circumstances by calling for a comprehensive round. If we achieve a comprehensive round, we can discuss all matters during the next two to three years. Presidential elections will occur during that period, but I understand and acknowledge the political significance of what has been said. The reassurance that I can give is that negotiation on behalf of the EU takes place via the Commission. The General Affairs Council will meet, but it will advise the Commission, which will have competence for negotiation in the WTO. We now have a common position for Seattle, which is to go for a comprehensive round: Agenda 2000. As I said, the competence to negotiate now lies with the Commission. It would be difficult for any country significantly to break away from the agreed position, although that is not to say that there will not be a lot of hard bargaining on details post-Seattle.

It is true that the United States of America has been trying to limit the Seattle agenda. We hope that, given the strong representations that the United Kingdom has made, the United States will broaden its horizons and agree that a comprehensive round would be significant not only for the USA but for the developing world. In that sense, it is early days—we are in the run-up to Seattle and will see what happens there. However, we will ensure that the United Kingdom's position—that we should have a comprehensive round—will be put as forcefully as possible, together with the safeguards.

Dr. Nick Palmer
(Broxtowe)
: I apologise, Mrs. Roe, for having arrived late; I was waiting to be called in response to the Northern Ireland statement.

Does my right hon. Friend the Minister recognise the concern that Britain appears to be a brake on the European Union's willingness to raise the issue of animal welfare in the WTO? In September, the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food stated in a consultation document that the United Kingdom was considering that as one issue that might be raised, and the European Union has in fact decided to do so. Will he confirm that we support the need to examine ways to ensure that European Union decisions on animal welfare are not undermined by the process and production methods limitations of the World Trade Organisation?

Mr. Caborn
: On the contrary, we have pushed the issue of animal welfare. One difficulty is that, unlike in relation to the International Labour Organisation or the environment, there is no internationally agreed standard. The EU has been pushing for that issue to be on the agenda for the next WTO round, with a view to changing the rules to take account of farm animal welfare policy. In June, the Agriculture Council and the Commission made a joint declaration calling for the international acknowledgement of animal welfare rules to be a key point in the EU negotiating brief for the WTO. That declaration was repeated in the 10 Council's conclusions, agreed on 27 September. So the United Kingdom strongly supports the further improvement of farm animal welfare standards in the EU, and has backed calls to include animal welfare standards in the new round of trade negotiations. That will be the EU position at Seattle, but it would be wrong to say that we are in a strong position, because the EU is in a clear minority—as I think that my hon. Friend knows. However, we will push that position consistently, and we have unanimity in the EU.

One fall-back measure that we could consider is the whole question of labelling. Such areas can be explored in side negotiations, but we want animal welfare to be included in the comprehensive agenda. I believe that that is possible and that animal welfare is clearly part of the WTO's remit because article 20 covers human, plant and animal health.

Mrs. Browning
: Following that question, will the Minister expand his comments beyond animal welfare—important though that is—to other matters that will affect trade in food products, including, for example, residues of veterinary medicines and substances such as growth promoters?

The Minister will know of the United States' desire to see USA beef available for sale in the European Union. I understand that the British Government supported the USA in its wish to export its hormone-treated beef here, but as a result of the EU's resistance to that, the USA has, in reprisal, raised tariffs against certain sectors. Ironically, one of the sectors that is particularly affected in the United Kingdom is the decorative packaging industry, which now faces undue tariff barriers from the USA as a result of the negotiating position between the USA and the EU. What representations has the Minister made to the United States and to colleagues in the EU to ensure that UK companies and industries are not subject to those unnecessary tariffs, which have resulted from the tension that exists between the USA and the EU in respect of hormone-treated beef?

Mr. Caborn
: To the best of my knowledge, the UK has stood against the ban on the products to which the hon. Lady referred. That is because WTO rules on food safety allow us to impose trade restrictions on the grounds of protecting human, animal and plant health, provided that that is done on a sound scientific basis, and we have said consistently that in regard to US hormone-treated beef, that scientific basis is not there. That is why we were in a minority of one on the three occasions when the issue came before the Council.

The issue has been on-going for 10 years or more, so it has spanned both Conservative and Labour Administrations. However, the UK's position has been consistent. We believe that the WTO has a responsibility with regard to restrictions imposed for reasons of human, animal and plant health, providing that there is a scientific basis. However, we also believe that there was no such scientific basis for hormone-treated beef, which is why we voted against the EU position and why we were exempted by both the US and the Canadians from their restrictions.

JPEG 11 The UK's position is clear: we support the WTO's position. We believe that there needs to be support for animal, human and plant health—that is right—but that such provisions cannot be applied willy-nilly or used as a technical barrier to trade; the scientific base must be there. We believe that in the case of hormone-treated beef, such a base was not proven, and that remains our position.

Mrs. Browning
: I fully understand the position that the Minister has explained, but I should be grateful if he would address the rest of my question. Why, despite the fact that the UK Government supported the USA's position on this issue, are UK companies—particularly those in the decorative packaging industry now subject to USA reprisals against the EU in the form of unnecessary tariff levels? What representations has the Minister made to the USA and the EU on behalf of the UK decorative packaging industry?

Mr. Caborn
: I have made strong representations. Two different areas are involved, which are being mixed up: beef hormones, which I have discussed, and bananas, which is the subject of the retaliatory action that has been taken by the US that affects the packaging industry. We have been trying to find a solution to that problem, and I hope that the matter can be brought quickly to a conclusion. Last Monday, the matter arose when I was at the General Affairs Council in Brussels, and I have met a number of those involved in the banana packaging industry. We have put pressure on the European Union to find a solution that is acceptable to the US and does not necessarily involve returning to the procedures of the world trade organisation.

Dr. Ladyman
: The Minister mentioned our shared experience with the Commonwealth. In previous rounds, we have at least kept a weather eye on the interests of the Commonwealth and even, on some occasions, tried directly to extend those interests. Given that on this occasion we will be speaking through the European Union, how will the Commonwealth be represented?

Mr. Caborn
: The desires and objectives of the Commonwealth coincide with those of the European Union. The Commonwealth is a unique organisation in that it represents a range of countries from the least developed to the most developed. Its other unique attribute is that it is the only international organisation that has countries in each of the trading blocs—Asia-Pacific Economic Co-operation, the Southern Africa Development Co-ordination Conference and the North American Free Trade Agreement, for example. One can go round the globe and find only one bloc—Mercosur in Latin America—that does not involve a Commonwealth country. That is because it is a party to the Cairns group, and the Commonwealth is thus involved indirectly. When I was in Johannesburg last week, it was interesting to talk to my colleagues from New Zealand and Australia, who are members of the Cairns group. They took a rather bullish attitude—to put it mildly—to the agricultural situation. We were able to open a dialogue with them and with Ministers such as my very 12 good friend Alec Erwin, South Africa's Minister of Trade and Industry. The challenge has been to facilitate a dialogue with countries that are represented in nearly every trading bloc around the world. The dialogue that we have set up will enable us to address the comprehensive agenda and influence the issues of the developing world, as well as those of the developed world.

Dr. Cable
: What steps has the Minister taken to quantify the overall benefits to the British economy of the new round of trade liberalisation? As there are so many pressure groups, both self-interested and idealistic, campaigning against liberalisation, it would be helpful to set out those benefits. Have the Government undertaken that exercise?

Mr. Caborn
: I do not believe that we have undertaken a specific exercise, although we monitor various areas. I understand that the EU is considering the potential benefits of a comprehensive round for the global economy, as well as the economies of the EU. We are still at the pre-negotiation stage of setting a comprehensive agenda for Seattle next week. I cannot give the hon. Gentleman precise details now, but I shall write to him about the matter.

Mr. Keith Darvill
(Upminster)
: Does my right hon. Friend agree that secrecy is one of the main complaints against the WTO? Would it be a good idea to de-restrict many of the documents that flow from the WTO, other than under exceptional circumstances?

Mr. Caborn
: My hon. Friend is absolutely right. The WTO's rules must be modernised in a number of areas, and transparency will be crucial to establishing the credibility that it will need to gain the confidence of the 134 member countries. Although the dispute procedure has, by and large, done a good job, it could also be modernised and improved. The procedure must provide certainty and credibility for all members, and it must be even-handed. It is important that the smallest of the 134 countries does not feel overawed by the biggest.

Mr. Jack
: The Minister announced at the beginning of his remarks that the round was likely to take three years, and, a moment ago, counselled the Committee that an agenda had not yet been agreed. Has he consulted the Leader of the House to ensure that there will be opportunities, in Government time, for Parliament to be informed about the progress of such discussions and to debate them at each stage?

Mr. Caborn
: The round might take two rather than three years, so it would be presumptuous of the Government to plan for three years. I assure the right hon. Gentleman that my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Trade and Industry has already been in contact with the Leader of the House to ensure that we report from Seattle at the earliest opportunity so that we can be questioned. After Seattle, we should have a reasonable idea of what the agenda will be and what timetable will be pursued.

I assure the right hon. Gentleman that not only Parliament but civil society will be kept informed. This deal is not merely for business per se; it is for the consumer, whether in the United Kingdom, Europe or beyond. We want to provide transparency and JPEG 13 openness, and be available for cross-examination. I have been in my current job for three months, and I have already had four meetings with the NGOs, on my own or with my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State, in the run-up to Seattle. I have also had meetings with Church groups and other bodies.

Dr. Ladyman
: It is a good principle that consumers should be entitled to information about how the things that they buy are produced. Such information would include the environmental impact of the method of production, and the social background to production. What plans does the Minister have to ensure that agreements are reached on eco-labelling and the other labelling initiatives, which will provide consumers with such information?

Mr. Caborn
: Clearly, the consumer should be informed about what he or she buys, and labelling would contribute to that. Labelling also enables the consumer to keep a discerning eye on animal welfare or the environment. We hope that it will be on the agenda for discussion, as it is an important part of informing the consumer. One of the criticisms that has been lodged with the WTO, probably with justification, is that it has been seen to be more on the side of the producer than the consumer. It is important that the WTO should integrate such concerns into its practices.

Dr. Evan Harris
(Oxford, West and Abingdon)
: In the Minister's opening statement, he mentioned the concerns of NGOs. He will know, through correspondence, that the Oxford Oxfam Group and the Oxford branch of the World Development Movement have asked me to bring their concerns to his attention. One of those concerns is that the WTO is a rich man's club—it would be men—which makes rules that exclude, or do not give sufficient consideration to, developing countries. The multilateral agreement on investment is given as an example of that. How will the approach of the EU and of the United Kingdom differ from the failed negotiations that took place on the multilateral agreement on investment, with regard to investment issues in the WTO?

Mr. Caborn
