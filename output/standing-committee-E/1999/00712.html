The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Miss Melanie Johnson)
: I am pleased to discuss the European Community preliminary draft budget, or PDB, for 2001, the Commission's proposals to revise the Berlin 4 financial perspective and for future spending on external relations, and the regulation creating the rapid reaction facility. Committee members have already had the opportunity to read the Government's views of the documents in four explanatory memorandums: on the PDB, the Commission's proposal to revise the financial perspective, the Commission's plans for the future of external aid—or category 4—spending, and the rapid reaction facility. I shall keep my introductory remarks brief. A second explanatory memorandum was issued recently on the full preliminary draft budget, which I hope that Committee members have read.

First, I shall focus on the Commission's preliminary draft budget for 2001. It covers the second year of the financial perspectives, which were agreed at the 1999 Berlin European Council. The EU budget for the last year, 2000, was the first to be agreed under the new Berlin agreement, and successfully demonstrated that budget discipline is possible in EU spending, and that we can deliver a budget that respects the financial perspective and the new inter-institutional agreement on budgetary discipline. As last year, it is important that the EU budget for 2001 sends a clear message that the EU can set firm spending plans and stick to them.

EU budgetary discipline is important, because setting firm limits on expenditure acts to improve value for money from spending, and hence assists in reducing financial mismanagement and fraud. Expenditure in the EU budget represents a significant strain on national budgets for many EU member states, and we must therefore be just as keen to seek efficiency and effectiveness from EU spending as we are in our national budgets.

As the United Kingdom is a net contributor to the EU budget, it is in the UK's interests to restrict growth in the budget as much as possible while working to achieve a more efficient use of existing resources. That desire for rigour in the EU budget is shared by a majority of member states.

Turning to the general picture for 2001, the European Commission has proposed an increase of 3.9 per cent. in commitments and an increase of 5 per cent. in payments for the year 2001. The large increase is primarily a result of the reforms to agriculture that were agreed at Berlin. Spending on mandatory payments under the common agricultural policy rises by about 7.5 per cent. in the 2001 PDB, compared with the adopted budget for 2000. That mainly reflects the implementation of Agenda 2000 reforms agreed last year, including an increase of almost 25 per cent. in provision for rural development measures. That reflects progress in the Commission's approval of member states' plans, and the fact that specific rural development programmes are now coming fully on line.

Notwithstanding the specific situation with regard to agriculture, the UK Government are concerned that the proposed increases in levels of commitments and payments in the EU budget for 2001 are too high. The Government will look for savings across the budget— JPEG 5 in line with our domestic policy priorities—in both commitments and payments in the course of the discussions on the 2001 EU budget.

In terms of our other priorities for the 2001 budget, the Government are also keen to ensure that sufficient funds are allocated to programmes that will take forward the initiatives on economic reform in Europe agreed by Council at the Lisbon summit.

As with the EU 2000 budget, a key issue in the negotiation of the 2001 budget is the issue of providing funding for reconstruction in the western Balkans. The Commission has issued a proposal to revise the Berlin financial perspective expenditure limits in order to fund additional aid to Kosovo and the wider western Balkans. The Government strongly believe that it is not necessary to revise Berlin, and that the EU can and should find sufficient resources from within the existing external aid financial perspective limits to provide aid where it is clearly needed. We must, therefore, set priorities for EU aid that allow us to contribute to the recovery of the region. An economically and politically stable Balkan region is in all our interests.

The Government note that the Commission has said elsewhere that money in category 4, the external relations section of the EU budget, has been inefficiently spent in the past. Financial assistance to the Balkans from the EU budget must be funded through re-prioritising spending on EU aid programmes within the Berlin ceiling.

Furthermore, EU spending plans for the western Balkans must be set in the context of all sources of finance to the region, including bilateral commitments and funds from the international financial institutions as well as the EU budget contribution. Aid must also take account of the absorption capacity of the region.

The Government will work closely with other member states to set priorities for EU aid and thus allow sufficient resources for the Balkans, while respecting the Berlin ceilings. As part of that work, the UK Government will also seek to increase the overall proportion of EU aid that goes to the poorest countries over the financial perspective period.

On the broader future of the EU aid budget, beyond 2001, the Government are especially concerned that these funds must be spent as efficiently as possible, and on the most deserving cases. We therefore welcome the proposals for reform of EU aid spending that have come from Commissioner Chris Patten. We look forward to seeing more detail on the proposed reforms, and to working with the Commission to ensure that the EU aid budget—including the European development fund, which represents 10 per cent. of all world aid—is spent wisely.

On the proposal for a regulation on the rapid reaction facility, the Government support the principle of easier and more effective deployment of Community funds in questions of emergency or crisis. We therefore support the inclusion of the new budget line in the EU budget for 2001. The new line will, of course, need to be accommodated within the Berlin ceiling for category 4.

6 Finally, as I said at the beginning of my remarks, we continue to seek improved efficiency and effectiveness from all EU expenditure. To that end, we remain determined to tackle fraud at every level in the European Community. The Government therefore welcome the doubling of funding in the EU budget for budget lines that reinforce anti-fraud activities in member states in parts of the EU budget where administration is primarily undertaken by member states. The Government also welcome the funding proposed by the Commission for other anti-fraud activities—although on the proposed increase in staff for OLAF, the European anti-fraud office, the Government believe that what is important is the quality, not necessarily the number, of staff.

I commend the resolutions to the Committee.
