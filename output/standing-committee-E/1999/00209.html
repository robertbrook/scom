legislation Common Strategy on Ukraine

[Relevant Documents: European Union Document No. 13523/2/99, the Common Strategy on Ukraine, and the unnumbered Explanatory Memorandum dated 31 January 2000 from the Foreign and Commonwealth Office on the Presidency's work plan on the implementation of the Common Strategy of the European Union on Ukraine]

The Minister of State, Foreign and Commonwealth Office (Mr. Keith Vaz): May I first welcome you to the Chair, Mr. Malins?

I want to apologise for the fact that the final version of the common strategy did not appear in the pack of papers that was sent to members of the Committee last week. Members were sent the correct version today.

I should like to say something about Ukraine and the Government's policy towards it.

The Chairman: Order. I remind the Committee that there can be no interventions during the opening statement. There will be plenty of opportunity for them later.

Obviously, in the generosity of our hearts we would want to forgive the Minister and the Foreign Office for inadvertently supplying papers as late as 24 hours before the Committee was due to sit, but could you advise me, Mr. Malins, on whether it would be proper to proceed with our sitting, given that we have had only 24 hours to scrutinise those papers? Lady will not be alone in having begun to read them only at 9 o'clock sharp this morning. In my view, it is proper for this debate to proceed on the basis that the papers arrived Col. 2 just sufficiently beforehand. However, the Minister will have noted the hon. Lady's comments.

Mr. Vaz: I have indeed noted them and I repeat my apology. We will endeavour to ensure that it does not happen again.

Ukraine's achievements since regaining its independence are considerable and the United Kingdom applauds them. In less than nine years, Ukraine has consolidated its role as an independent sovereign state with a functioning democracy. Ethnic and linguistic divisions within the country have been well managed and present no serious threat to the nation's stability.

Ukraine has pursued a remarkably balanced foreign policy—especially so, given her history. She has established significant strategic relationships with NATO, the European Union and the United States and maintains a close and stable relationship with Russia. Ukraine has sought to play a constructive role in seeking solutions to regional conflicts and has played a responsible role within the United Nations, successfully achieving a place on the Security Council for two years from 1 January this year, providing the President in 1997–98 and contributing troops to peacekeeping operations, including in the Balkans. Ukraine has relinquished its nuclear weapons status—a historic decision for which Ukraine deserves our gratitude and thanks.

However, the achievements of recent years will not in themselves bring prosperity and economic growth to Ukraine. The latter can be achieved only by thorough administrative and economic reform, which will promote domestic entrepreneurship and attract foreign direct investment.

The progress of reform depends on the ability of the current pro-reform Government of President Kuchma and Prime Minister Yushchenko to put into practice policies of the type that they have publicly advocated. At the moment, Ukraine finds itself at a critical stage in its economic development. The International Monetary Fund's $2.6 billion extended finance facility for Ukraine is currently suspended. At the same time, Ukraine is at a sensitive stage in the renegotiation of its crippling foreign debt burden. At this critical moment, a parliamentary crisis and a proposed referendum on constitutional change threaten to undermine the cause of reform by tying up Government and Parliament in political knots.

The United Kingdom's objective is to be a recognised and valued partner of Ukraine in a common effort to integrate Ukraine into the community of nations sharing western values. A close bilateral relationship enables us to contribute to the general western objective of supporting Ukraine's transition into a free-market democracy which respects the human rights of its citizens. It positions us well to support current and future United Kingdom interests, including economic ones.

In our contact with the Ukrainian authorities, we aim to promote and support economic reform as a way to encourage economic growth and keep Ukraine internally stable. We encourage political reform, and respect for and belief in democracy.

Image Col. 3 In foreign policy we aim to encourage Ukraine to share our views on a range of international issues. That includes working with Ukraine to make a success of its United Nations Security Council membership. In pursuing this dialogue, the Foreign Secretary had three substantive meetings with Foreign Minister Tarasyuk last year and intends to visit Kiev soon. I also aim to go to Ukraine later in the year, if possible.

Even before the adoption of the common strategy, Ukraine was already established as one of the European Union's key partners. Ukraine is important because of its size, its geographical proximity to the European Union—it borders on four applicant countries: Poland, Slovakia, Hungary and Romania—and its bearing on European stability and security. It is clearly in the European Union's interests to promote democracy and a sound economy in Ukraine, to engage on issues such as the environment, energy and nuclear safety, and to strengthen co-operation on trade, justice and home affairs.

The partnership and co-operation agreement, now in force for almost two years, is the basis of that relationship. Its wide range testifies to the breadth of our partnership. It covers nearly all fields of co-operation, from trade and economic reform to political dialogue and technical assistance.

The European Union's commitment to helping Ukraine is not just a question of fine words. The Community alone has given more than 600 million euros of assistance through the TACIS programme since 1991, not to mention the bilateral arrangements of individual EU countries. The United Kingdom's technical assistance programme, the know-how fund, provides £9 million of assistance a year. It is the third largest national programme after those of the United States and Germany. TACIS has supported a wide range of projects, from the development of tourism to the modernisation of agriculture.

From the outset, the United Kingdom perceived the common strategy as a valuable opportunity to define more clearly our objectives for the European Union-Ukraine relationship. We wanted to look to the future and set out a vision of a closer relationship between the EU and Ukraine, and define practical actions to help us realise that vision. One of the common strategy's strengths is the fact that it clearly expresses the European Union's objectives for the EU-Ukraine relationship in the short and medium term. We are satisfied that those objectives closely resemble and complement our bilateral objectives.

I am grateful to the European Scrutiny Committee for its reports on the common strategy and the presidency's work plan. Some preliminary remarks on the specific aspects about which the Committee expressed interest may be helpful. The European Scrutiny Committee suggests that there is a contrast between the

"optimistic tone of the Common Strategy and the events reported by the Council of Europe delegation"

that monitored the 1999 presidential elections. I do not share that view.

Col. 4 The first of the European Union's strategic goals, as specified in paragraph 5 of the common strategy, is

"to contribute to the emergence of a stable, open and pluralistic democracy".

Such language clearly recognises that Ukraine has not yet attained a level of democracy appropriate for a member of the Organisation for Security and Co-operation in Europe and the Council of Europe. The EU was quick to point out that the conduct of the 1999 presidential election campaign fell short of OSCE standards. Although the EU also agreed with the OSCE that voting and the counting of votes were conducted properly, and that the legitimacy of the election result was not in doubt, the common strategy is not intended to gloss over the campaign's shortcomings, which include bias in the state media and inappropriate pressure on voters by state employees. Indeed, paragraph 12 urges Ukraine to heed the criticisms of the OSCE monitoring mission following the presidential elections.

Paragraph 12 also urges Ukraine to fulfil the commitments to reform made when joining the Council of Europe in 1995. At this stage, the Government categorically do not believe that the death penalty in Ukraine has been secretly reinstated or that political prisoners have been executed. We believe that the moratorium on executions has been respected since its introduction in 1997. Furthermore, abolition of the death penalty—one of the main obligations that Ukraine owed the Council of Europe—has effectively been achieved by dint of the constitutional court's ruling in December that the death penalty was unconstitutional.

I have said that Ukraine has a working democracy. I did not mean to imply that it had a perfect democracy, and I am satisfied that the common strategy makes that clear.

The European Scrutiny Committee report suggests that the presidency's work plan may be over-ambitious. It is certainly wide ranging, which I strongly welcome, but whether it proves to be over-ambitious will be discovered only when we take stock of progress at the end of the Portuguese presidency in June.

The European Scrutiny Committee's reports make several other interesting points with which I am sure that we shall be able to deal in the debate.

Mr. Vaz: It will be spent in co-operation with the Ukraine Government. In adopting a common strategy, it is important that we work through the institutions in Ukraine. The technical assistance provided by TACIS will enable our programme's aims to be achieved. One cannot examine one programme in isolation. The Image Col. 5 technical support that we give through TACIS and the way in which money is spent should be in line with our overall strategy of preparing Ukraine for its place in Europe. It is an extremely important country and needs to be encouraged to see itself as European through the partnership and co-operation agreement and the common strategy. We hope that TACIS, the know-how fund and bilateral agreements between countries will be part of that overall theme.

Jackie Ballard (Taunton): The Minister said that he certainly believed that there had been no executions since 1997. Will he comment more generally on the observance of human rights conventions in Ukraine?

Mr. Vaz: We very much hope that the principles of human rights conventions are being observed. We have no information other than that which we have received and about which I have told Committee members. We strongly believe that the death penalty has not been carried out since 1997. The court's decision to declare it unconstitutional is extremely important.

Given the problems thrown up during the monitoring of the election campaign, Ukraine is under no misapprehension about our desire to ensure that human rights are observed and citizens' rights respected. However, if there are any problems involving human rights, we intend to raise them at the earliest opportunity. The issue must be examined and cannot be put to one side. Since 1991, Ukraine has done well in establishing a pluralistic approach to democracy. However, when faults occur, we must raise them. Our economic interests should not be secondary to the well-being and welfare of the citizens of Ukraine.

Mr. Kelvin Hopkins (Luton, North): The failure thus far to dismantle and decommission the Chernobyl nuclear power station is a serious cause for concern arising from the documents. Will my hon. Friend comment on that and the apparent plans to develop further nuclear power plants in Ukraine instead of using alternative approaches to future energy supplies?

Mr. Vaz: My hon. Friend is right to say that that is an important issue, He will know that decommissioning Chernobyl and ensuring nuclear safety will cost a huge amount. Indeed, it is estimated that that will cost $758 million. Of couse, the Group of Seven has provided support, but Ukraine itself must ensure that the matter is dealt with.

It is estimated that complete decommissioning will take 50 to 70 years, which is a considerable period. A donor conference—a pledging conference—is due to be held in May. We hope that further attempts will be made to help raise funds. However, the matter must be examined because of the history and emotional nature of the word "Chernobyl". However, the Ukrainians are under no misapprehension about the fact that we need to encourage them to decommission and provide better nuclear safety.

Mrs. Gillan: Is the Minister satisfied with the proposal to give a large sum to Ukraine under the TACIS programme? Is he sure that it will be well spent? "It is obvious that no one really knows what to do next. Politics here is very spontaneous."

Mr. Vaz: I have not had an opportunity to read the Financial Times this morning, so I cannot comment on it. Like the hon. Lady, I was busily reading the final version of the papers, which my office failed to deliver. [Interruption.] I thank my hon. Friend the Member for Luton, North (Mr. Hopkins) for letting me have a copy.

Clearly, interesting things are happening in the Ukrainian Parliament. However, we are satisfied that Ukraine remains a stable democracy, notwithstanding the physical contact between its Members of Parliament. I assure the hon. Lady that we do of course want to ensure that any money spent by the European Union or this country on any other country is monitored very carefully. Not a penny of the money will be spent unless we know that doing so will be of value. If the hon. Lady has any evidence to suggest otherwise, we should be keen to look at it. We will not simply throw money at problems and hope that they will go away. Of course the way in which funds are spent must be monitored properly. The rules governing such matters will be adhered to strictly.

Jane Griffiths (Reading, East): The fact that Ukraine was chosen for the second common strategy implies that the EU places an extremely high priority on Ukraine and its development, yet it is much further from EU membership than other former Soviet republics, especially the Baltic states. Will the Minister comment on that?

Mr. Vaz: My hon. Friend is right, and I am glad that Ukraine has been chosen for the second strategy. She is also right that it will be a considerable time before Ukraine regards itself as being ready to be a candidate for EU membership. It is the second largest country in Europe and has a population of 49 million people, so it is extremely important strategically. As my hon. Friend knows, at Helsinki, we in the EU gave the go-ahead for further applicants to be considered for membership of the EU. Four of the countries in the first and second waves border Ukraine, so we have to deal with the matter; it not simply go away. We must ensure that we have a common strategy to deal with the difficulties that Ukraine faces.

I do not envisage Ukraine being ready for candidacy status for a considerable time. Much work must be done before it is anywhere near reaching that stage, but that does not mean that we should not work with Ukraine through the common strategy, the partnership and co-operation agreements and its developing relationship with NATO to ensure that it remains stable. The problems of eastern Europe cannot be shoved away; we must be aware of them and deal with them.

Mr. John Wilkinson (Ruislip-Northwood): What a pleasure it is to see such unanimity of purpose. We are united in our desire to see progress on all fronts in Ukraine and we look forward to an improvement in our already good relations with that country. The importance of Ukraine as a partner nation to the European Union and as a neighbour is appreciated and wholly to be welcomed.

The country was invaded by Hitler's armies in 1941 and it suffered the aftermath of the second world war. The struggle of the Ukrainian People's Army in the Carpathians continued until the mid-1950s. Independence was a dearly held aspiration and it seemed difficult of realisation, but it has occurred and it must now be sustained by the free, democratic countries of western Europe. I regard the common strategy as evidence of Europe's determination to sustain it.

Ukraine has immense potential as a partner with western Europe. Ukraine has been dependent for so long on its relationship with its bigger neighbour to the north; that relationship is crucial, but most of us recognise the importance of a strong, independent stable Ukraine, not least as a counterweight to Russia in such unpredictable times. I regard the common strategy and the attitude of Col. 12 the Foreign Office to be laudable and I wholeheartedly support it.
