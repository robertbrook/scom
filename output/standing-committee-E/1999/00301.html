Mr. Foulkes: Hon. Members will recall that, during the Adjournment debate in Westminster Hall last month, I explained the steps that the Government had taken to help to improve the effectiveness of European Community development assistance. I also accepted that there was still a long way to go, which is why I welcome the opportunity for further discussion of the documents referred to the Committee by the Scrutiny Committee.

The biggest challenge facing the world in the new millennium is the fact that one in four of our fellow human beings lives in extreme poverty; many are starving to death. The European Commission has a vital role to play in helping to eliminate that poverty. The EC is the second-largest multilateral donor, spending 5 billion euros a year. Through the Department for International Development, the United Kingdom contributes about 18 per cent. of that amount—that represents 30 per cent. of the DFID budget. Our principal focus is on EC humanitarian aid, but I will, of course, be happy to take questions on issues arising from any of the documents. I may have some difficulty with those in French, but I shall do my best.

The documents on EC humanitarian aid cover the period from 1992 to 1999 and mainly focus on the activities of ECHO—the European Community Humanitarian Office—which was set up in 1991. ECHO'S budget has grown from 335 million euros in 1992 to 800 million in 1999. Together with the donations from member states, that accounts for about half of worldwide humanitarian aid—a really staggering total. Image Col. 5 The aid is intended to benefit people in poorer countries who have suffered as a result of conflict or natural disasters such as we see, tragically, in Mozambique today.

The Court of Auditors' report focuses on ECHO'S activities from 1992 to 1995, which was a period of rapid growth, frenetic activity and substantial aid flows. However, there was also a lack of strategic thinking; there was no assessment of impact and very little external control. ECHO was, more or less, allowed to do its own thing.

The humanitarian aid regulation of 1996 marked a watershed in relations between ECHO and member states. The regulation was, inevitably, a compromise; while meeting the understandable need to give ECHO the flexibility to respond quickly to crises, it also had to give member states more opportunity to influence those responses. The systems established by the regulation are not perfect. However, since the adoption of the regulation, ECHO has provided more, better quality information on its plans, and a culture of consultation and co-operation has evolved. For example, under the United Kingdom's EU presidency in 1998, the first joint field missions for ECHO and the member states were organised. Thankfully, that practice has continued.

The recent evaluation of humanitarian operations confirms the improvement in ECHO's performance in recent years. However, it is also clear that there is still room for improvement. The issues needing attention include, first, ECHO'S mandate and its role in the so-called grey zone between relief and development; secondly, the coherence and co-ordination between ECHO, the rest of the Commission and the member states; thirdly, ECHO'S emergency response capacity and the question whether it should be able to mount its own aid operations; fourthly, relations with the United Nations and non-governmental organisations; and finally the promotion of disaster preparedness, an important matter in the light of what is happening at the moment.

The Commission has responded positively to the evaluation. Some action is already in hand on, for example, staff training and personnel development. External consultations with member states and other interested parties are under way to learn from their experience. The Government are contributing constructively to that process, and we believe that ECHO is, by and large, doing a good job and genuinely wants to improve its performance.

The Government continue to seek ways to improve the EC's performance. It is vital that we use our resources properly and, where improvements are needed, work together in a spirit of co-operation and common sense to put matters right. In December 1999, the British Government published an institutional strategy paper entitled "Working in Partnership with the European Community", which included 18 proposals to improve the effectiveness and increase the poverty focus of EC development assistance.

I am pleased to say that our pro-Europe, pro-reform policy—which will find favour with at least one Opposition party—is paying dividends. Significant Col. 6 progress has been made. We lobbied for rationalisation of the Commission's structure. Last year, the Commission was reorganised, and the number of Commissioners with development responsibilities has been reduced from five to two. We welcome that simplification, and we also welcome Neil Kinnock's proposal for broad reform of Commission financial issues and staff management.

We have helped to negotiate a much-improved successor to the Lomé convention and the TACIS regulation, which we debated in May. We have also lobbied for a coherent statement of EC development policy, and we expect at last to receive the Commission's first draft later this month. We are working with member states to improve EC co-operation and co-ordination, and a large number of development experts from Britain have been seconded to the Commission. The integration by the Commission of the hitherto separate evaluation units into a single evaluation service has helped to improve accountability in the Commission and the design of future programmes.

The Commission is also drawing up an action plan to improve effectiveness, which will include simplified procedures, increased accountability, the production of an annual report and, above all, greater delegation of decision making to a national level, which member states have requested for some time.

We welcome those improvements, but we are aware that there is still much to be done to improve the poverty focus and the effectiveness of EC assistance delivery. The Government will continue to work with the Commission, member states and members of the European Parliament to keep up the pressure for reform.

Col. 24 I want to deal here with the question raised by the hon. Member for Ruislip-Northwood. He is a thoughtful Member of the House and has been with us for a long time. Friend the Member for Luton, North also raised, related to multilateral assistance versus bilateral assistance. We believe that there are potential advantages and, increasingly, actual advantages to be gained from multilateral assistance.

First, in the case of the European Union, multilateral assistance taps into funds from other European countries that may not have as good a track record as bilateral donors as we do. We can influence the way in which that global fund is spent. Secondly, the European Union leads on trade matters, as my hon. Friend the Member for Luton, North said. As the link between trade and development assistance is so vital, multilateral assistance would mean greater co-operation and coherence between trade and development. Thirdly, multilateral assistance increases the reach of our bilateral funds in countries where we are not able to have bilateral programmes. I know that the hon. Member for Ruislip-Northwood is particularly interested in Latin America. I am often asked about programmes in Latin American countries where we do not have a programme, such as Cuba. I point to the European Union programmes there, which we are part funding. That reach is not available with our bilateral programmes. In principle, if we can do more multilaterally, not just through the European Union but through the United Nations and its agencies, the World bank and the regional development banks, we can contribute effectively not only to poverty eradication and development assistance but to greater cooperation between countries.

A good example of positive engagement in Europe is the Lomé convention, which my hon. Friend the Member for Luton, North mentioned. It has been successfully negotiated, with much improved provision for development and trade co-operation for the ACP Image Col. 25 countries. I shall give my hon. Friend some examples. First, there is to be duty-free access to the European Union for all the least developed and poorest countries in the world. Secondly, free trade areas will be agreed with the ACP countries that wish it. There will be a long transition period of up to 23 years, with the European Union lowering our tariffs first. Thirdly, we shall tackle the technical barriers to market access of the kind mentioned by my hon. Friend. In those three areas, there have been improvements in respect of Lome. Again, they were achieved through multilateral discussions between the 15 European countries and the 71 ACP countries. Of course, matters are not perfect, but there has been a huge step forward. In terms of a constructive working relationship with Europe, the Lome convention has proved effective.
