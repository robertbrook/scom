Mr. Meacher: I am grateful to the Committee for the opportunity to debate an important subject. I take account of your ordinance, Mr. Sayeed, on the point of order of the hon. Member for Stone, but I am sure that today's debate represents an opening shot in what will be extensive discussion of a highly technical and detailed issue. The Government do not intend to deny hon. Members a full opportunity to make their views known on the difficult area of environmental liability. I was asked to come to the Committee and I have responded. Of course, I hear what the hon. Gentleman says about the request that the matter be taken on the Floor of the House, but we are at the scrutiny stage and there will be a later stage. The question of genetically modified organism liability will undoubtedly return for debate on the Floor of the House, as we want it to.

I am grateful for the Committee's report on the document and on our explanatory memorandum. I am also grateful for its highly pertinent questions, on which we have provided a supplementary memorandum dated 5 June, on which it has also reported. As the Committee said, the White Paper is a significant document that raises a host of issues, both political and legal. It stems from a decision made three years ago by the previous Commission, which in turn followed various earlier proposals for EC action in this area that did not come to fruition. The fact that it did not succeed is perhaps a measure of the difficulty of the subject. The main aim of any environmental liability regime is to make the causer of environmental damage pay for remedying that damage, in line with the polluter pays principle, which, as I understand it, is universally supported. In turn, any such regime should encourage greater prevention and precaution, in line with that important principle. The White Paper explores ideas for a regime across the European Union by setting out various options and outlining elements and features that such a regime might include.

The White Paper concludes that the most appropriate action for the Community is a framework directive, which would provide for strict liability for environmental damage that takes the form of contaminated sites, damage to biodiversity and traditional damage—that is, harm to persons or property—when such damage is caused by particular EC-regulated dangerous activities. It would also provide fault-based liability for damage to biodiversity that is caused by non-dangerous activities. The White Paper proposes to make the operator of the relevant activity the liable party.

Image Col. 3 The White Paper argues that retrospection should be avoided so that only damage that occurs after the entry into force of the EU regime would be caught. Member states should be under a duty to secure remediation of the damage in cases that involve a liable polluter. If the state fails to act or does not act properly, public interest groups should have the right to judicial review and perhaps to sue the party that is responsible for the damage. The White Paper also suggests that public interest groups should have a power to obtain an injunction from the courts in order to prevent damage from occurring, a power to take preventive steps in urgent cases and an entitlement to recover their costs from the party that is responsible for the damage. It discusses alleviation of the plaintiffs burden of proof and possible defences.

As we noted in our explanatory memorandum, the United Kingdom has a variety of regimes, measures and proposals in its domestic law on these matters, in which the concept of environmental liability is already widely applied. For example, we have just introduced in England a new statutory regime for dealing with contaminated land, which places liability for its remediation firmly on the polluter in the first instance. Last year, we improved the regime for dealing with water and groundwater pollution and introduced a new system of works notices to get such damage put right. The Countryside and Rights of Way Bill, which is currently passing through the House, includes provision to better secure the remediation of damage to sites of special scientific interest by those responsible. In addition to those public law measures, there is also private law, under which people whose interests are directly affected by a case of damage may be able to pursue a claim in the courts for compensation under various torts. Public interest groups can establish standing before the courts in relation to judicial review, and the Government are considering the question of representative actions.

We have systems already, and we can understand the desire to explore arrangements at the EU level, where we know that there are certain gaps. One of the main issues is exactly how EC proposals relate to what we already have, what the costs and benefits of changes would be, and what objectives we want to achieve. I would be the first to recognise that that is not an easy area in which to arrive at quick answers. In fact, the White Paper raises more questions than it answers. We are currently assessing the reactions to our consultations with various interested parties, and preparing a response to the European Commission.

From the initial response, our views will develop as we further explore the proposals and their implications, and as the Commission itself refines and develops its thinking from the fairly broadly expressed ideas in its White Paper. A full regulatory impact assessment will be needed. A draft directive would be the subject of intensive negotiation and debate, both domestically—I am sure that it will be debated on the Floor of the House—and throughout the European Union, which could take a number of years.

Col. 4 That is the background to the proceedings. The Committee's views and questions, and this morning's debate, will be fully taken on board in our further deliberations.

Does he agree that decisions made on the basis of the precautionary principle are often interpreted as trade disputes-for example, in the case of Canadian and American beef? Does he agree that the precautionary principle is often seen as an excuse for trade protectionism?

The interpretations include the "do not worry about the scientific evidence and get on and do it" approach, which is applied in the case of mobile phones and cellular telephone base stations in terms of radio frequencies. Different interpretations exist in Switzerland, Italy, the United States and here. Does the Minister agree that we need a hard and fast discussion on the meaning of the precautionary principle?

Mr. Meacher: I agree strongly with my hon. Friend. I was closely involved in the negotiations on the biosafety protocol in Montreal in January, which, for the first time, established the precautionary principle as a founding principle in terms of international trade. However, having achieved agreement, the next problem is application. My view, which I have suggested to my opposite number in the United States, is that we need an international protocol about the manner in which the precautionary principle is exercised. My American opposite number strongly disagreed with the idea and, in the course of the negotiations, asked me whether it was a matter of religion or emotion. The appropriate response was no.

Image Col. 7 The protocol on the manner in which the precautionary principle is exercised should establish a clear set of criteria that must be met before the precautionary principle can be exercised. That would cover assurances that all relevant evidence has been taken into account and that any new evidential gap will be closed, and that the application is consistent, coherent and non-discriminatory. A detailed protocol is the appropriate way to proceed, otherwise, the exercise of the precautionary principle becomes a trade dispute.

We can have free and open trade—which I personally support—that is consistent with the protection of the environment. Given that international good will is not a certainty, that interface needs to be clearly defined. That will certainly have to take account of the application of precautionary principles under the protocol.

Mr. Meacher: The hon. Gentleman mentioned the Lugano convention, which, as is explained in the supplementary memorandum, is the Council of Europe's convention on civil liability for damage resulting from activities dangerous to the environment. The previous Government did not sign it, because they considered it tp be of no obvious benefit to United Kingdom law. Even if it would have been of benefit, they considered it so loosely drafted as not to merit signing. The present Government have not signed either, and do not propose to do so.

Col. 8 The hon. Gentleman, whose views on Europe are well known, takes the view that any intrusion into the existing United Kingdom legal or administrative position is likely to be damaging or unhelpful.

Mr. Cash: The Minister said that it would be.

Mr. Meacher: I do not automatically take that view. Many issues of real concern arise, but the Government are strongly of the view that an environmental liability regime, applicable throughout Europe, is desirable, provided, of course, that the details are acceptable.

Mr. Cash: Including the burden of proof.

Mr. Meacher: Of course. That is an important condition.

The hon. Gentleman particularly mentioned the alleviation of the burden of proof for the plaintiff. As the supplementary memorandum states, the problem of the plaintiff having to prove facts that are peculiarly in the mind or knowledge of the defendant is already recognised in the English doctrine of res ipsa loquitur—the matter speaks for itself. The doctrine applies where it is so improbable that the act in question would have happened without the negligence of the defendant that it is reasonable that the burden of proof should switch to the defendant. We also make reference in our memorandum to a case with which I am sure the Committee is familiar, of Alphacell v. Woodward in 1972. In that case, it was recognised that the onus placed on the prosecution to prove that pollution was caused intentionally or negligently by the defendant may sometimes be difficult, and that a lot of pollution may go unpunished. It is therefore reasonable to reconsider the matter. It would be wrong to worry that it is being done at a European level. My concern is that our major considerations should properly be taken into account. I obviously cannot guarantee now that they will, but we shall fight hard for it and I see no reason why it should not be the case. If so, there would be a net gain.

That is the result of non-retrospectivity, and one can understand the reason for that. Mr. Meacher: That is, again, an important area, about which there is uncertainty.
