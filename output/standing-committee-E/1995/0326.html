Mr. Barron
: As the Bill stands, it contains a promise by the Secretary of State to bale out profilgate or incompetent trusts or health authorities. I do not see that the Committee is being asked to do anything less than let that pass. The Secretary of State is guaranteeing to pick up the tab for trusts and authorities that he closes down or which collapse before he can intervene. We are lucky, at least until the Bill has gone through Parliament, inasmuch as a promise is not an open-ended commitment. However, I suspect that if the Secretary of State has his way, he would be prepared to offer the earth in his attempt to restart the stalled private finance initiative in the health service.

Parliament is lucky inasmuch as unauthorised borrowing by NHS bodies is limited elsewhere in law to £5 billion. Although that is a strange kind of luck, it allows us as legislators to say that at least outside organisations must stop spending when they have run up £5 billion worth of debt at the taxpayers' expense. In view of what we have said in our debates, that would also happen without our agreement and, in particular, without Parliament's agreement. Therefore, we are discussing a major commitment and the amendment would ensure that we have the ability to control such a commitment to public expenditure.

Unamended, the Bill effectively gold plates the PFI inside the NHS. It guarantees returns on contracts for the provision of private finance as a replacement for the public-fund capital projects. That guarantee will be made by the Secretary of State under clause 1 and backed by the force of law, however inappropriate, mishandled or against the public interest and the better provision of health care the contracts in question might be. Trusts can run up debts—liabilities—and they should be checked by Parliament or even by the Department—but from what we can gather, there will be no checks. That cannot be right.

There still is, in name at least, a national health service and it should be accountable to Parliament for its services. As it stands, the Bill diminishes parliamentary accountability. Unless changes are made—the amendment seeks to make them—Parliament will have approved legislation that might cost up to £5 billion and over which the Government have no effective control.

The amendment seeks to restore that which should never have been given away. We propose a mechanism by which Parliament has a chance to speak before it signs the cheques. The mechanism would allow individual Members of Parliament to have a say, and they will necessarily have greater and more in-depth knowledge of the problems and the potentials of local health service bodies. That knowledge might be far greater than that of distant Ministers and NHS executives who claim stewardship while denying responsibility. The amendment also provides a JPEG 77 mechanism by which proper and effective scrutiny of the spending and borrowing plans of health service bodies is brought back into Parliament.

One feature of the so-called NHS reforms has been the dwindling of direct accountability and its replacement by an appointed quango. The amendment seeks to limit the rise of at least one segment of that expanding army of the non-accountable.

The Secretary of State is planning to assume an absolute responsibility for the liabilities of health service bodies if they merge, are absorbed, are dissolved by him, are abolished by him, if they go bankrupt or otherwise come to an end. I give such an exhaustive list because the phrase "ceases to exist" remains obscure. The Secretary of State will assume that his responsibilities for the liabilities can be lessened by transferring some or all of them to the health service bodies that succeed or replace those that have, for whatever reason, terminated.

Those liabilities may reach £5 billion. Under the existing terms of the Bill, those liabilities will not have been approved by the House. They will not be approved by Members of Parliament in any way even though we, and the taxpayers who elect us, may be ultimately responsible for picking up the tab. We should be in no doubt that we will be responsible for picking up the tab, either directly through the Secretary of State or indirectly through reduced budgets to other health service bodies. We plan to rectify that situation. Subsection (3) of the amendment would mean that all such liabilities incurred by national health service bodies would first have the approval of the relevant Secretary of State and of the Chancellor of the Exchequer.

The amendment would provide a simple and precautionary measure that would help to prevent prolifigate and unhelpful spending by health service bodies. It would also help to detect and curtail unfair deals with private contractors and to bring about a degree of strategic investment in new or improved health service provision. More importantly, the amendment would give Parliament a say in the spending and borrowing that it may ultimately end up financing. Such a say is sadly lacking in the Bill.

Furthermore, the right of Parliament to have its say would not be limited in the first instance to Ministers. Members of Parliament would also be able to look at proposed financial initiatives by health service bodies and would be able to object to such plans should they so wish. Those provisions are contained in subsection (3)(b) and (4), which provide that a minute of liability be laid before the House of Commons 14 days before any approvals are given by either the Chancellor or the Secretary of State. Hon. Members may object to that minute if they have cause to do so. Their objections may be considered—and, if thought wrong, waived—by the House before any approval decisions can be made by the Secretary of State and the Chancellor. The proposed subsection (5) makes that clear.

78 We are not proposing a brand-new procedure—it already exists in Parliament. Currently hon. Members may object, through the exact same procedures as the amendment proposes, to Treasury minutes that deal with certain categories of liabilities. Only a few weeks ago, the Secretary of State for Health made provisions relating to the reallocation of debt from the seven trusts that he has already dissolved. In the jargon, that is called the reallocation of originating capital debt. To complete the procedures, the Treasury had to agree to the reallocation and to a remission of certain outstanding debts. Our amendment proposes the same necessity for Treasury consent.

In addition, the Treasury was required to lay before the House a minute dealing with the Secretary of State's proposals, including exact monetary amounts. That minute was subject to the exact same procedures for objection that we detail in the amendment. Our proposal is nothing new to Parliament. It is an extension of existing procedures to the incurring of debts before they happen rather than limiting those procedures to the redistribution of debts after they have been run up without agreement.

Our amendment would provide a straightforward and transparent way to deal with important and costly proposed liabilities. It would give the House a role and allow health service bodies to seek approval for their plans in a much more structured and accountable environment. The amendment would realise the Bill's intention of securing the confidence of a potential private finance partner. The Bill will not achieve that aim. It may raise the spirits of private contractors briefly, but potential PFI bidders and banks will consider the longer-term scenario, that we discussed in our last sitting. Banks will realise that the Bill proposes an unfair contract, which gives health service bodies too much scope for action, while restricting Government manoeuvrability. Therefore, it may not pass the detailed scrutiny of our legal brethren.

The Department of Health has been panicked into introducing the measure. It has known for some time that the PFI in the national health service was in trouble. The problem was identified in the autumn of 1994, when Newchurch and Co. claimed that there were anxieties about the Secretary of State's power to dissolve trusts and transfer liabilities. However, the Department of Health had other matters to worry about. It stuck its head in the sand and hoped that the problem would go away—an action for which the Government are famous. But the problem did not disappear and the Bill was introduced. The Secretary of State has been forced to push through an emergency measure. Departmental incompetence is the sole cause of the emergency. The sole effect of the Bill will be to deny the basis of PFI in the health service.

If the Bill is passed unamended, it may cause chaos in public finances for years. Future Parliaments will take a dim view of the decision to allow almost unhindered access to public funds. It is worrying that the Secretary of State even contemplated the measure.

That explanation was not given on Second Reading but it should be provided today. What methods are open to hon. Members to question local health service bodies' spending and borrowing? The majority of hon. Members keep in contact with their local health services. I used to have at least three meetings a year with the health authority. It is all very well to extend the measures for tackling the transfer of liabilities in the NHS, but the extension should be accompanied by proper checks and balances in the new system that should include parliamentary scrutiny and accountability, for which the Bill does not provide. Different trusts, which are independent from the NHS Executive, can run up bills for billions of pounds. We will have to accept the responsibility without having any real check, as individuals or Members of Parliament, on how those debts will be run up and whether they are in the interests of the public.

Mr. Hugh Bayley
(York)
: The more that I consider the Bill, the more I am convinced that the Government are making a fundamental error. The Government support the private finance initiative, as does the Labour party, because they want to transfer risk from the public sector borrowing requirement to the private sector, yet in the Bill, the Government seek to underwrite some of the losses that the public sector might incur if it enters into a project and takes risks.

In last Thursday's sitting, the Minister correctly made the point that private sector capital is always more expensive than public sector capital. From the taxpayers' point of view, the only justification for using the PFI would be that the Government would transfer risk from the PSBR to the private sector. However, the Bill will let the private sector have its cake and eat it. It says, "We want you to use your more expensive capital for NHS development, but we don't expect you to take the full risk that you would take if you were making a private sector investment in a private sector business."

The Labour party supports the principle of the PFI and, as I said last week, it would be extraordinary if we did not, as it was the Labour party which pressed for years, before the Conservative party joined in, to change the Ryrie rules on public investment to allow leasing schemes and partnerships between the public and private sectors.

There are three key differences between the Government's approach to a PFI and the approach that I favour. First, unlike the Government, I do not see the PFI as a covert way of slashing capital spending. Secondly, I do not believe that the PFI is equally applicable to all public services. I made the point last week that the Government need to set clear priorities. The PFI is spluttering along and has not really come to life because the Government have not defined what they see as the key priorities for investment, to give the private sector a steer to where it would be most appropriate to form deals with sources of private finance. Thirdly, the Labour party sees the PFI as a basis for building partnerships between the public and private sectors. It is not a means of substituting private investment for public investment.

There is a sound basis for the caution that my hon. Friend the Member for Rother Valley (Mr. Barron) sounded about how the PFI is applied to the NHS. We JPEG 81 do not argue that it should not be applied. In earlier debates we highlighted several areas in which PFI investment would be entirely welcome and would enhance the NHS, but the range of opinion urging caution goes much wider than the Labour party. Will Hutton, economics editor of The Guardian, wrote recently.

"that the PFI cannot leave the character and ethics of the NHS intact."

A survey by the Association of Community Health Councils for England and Wales found that 63 per cent. of NHS chief executives—almost two thirds—believe that using the PFI for clinical practice would fundamentally alter the ethos of the NHS. I can emphasise that point by quoting a writer whom I consider one of the wisest of the 20th century, George Bernard Shaw. In his preface to the play "The Doctor's Dilemma", Shaw wrote:

"That any sane nation, having observed that you could provide for the supply of bread by giving bakers a pecuniary interest in baking for you, should go on to give a surgeon a pecuniary interest in cutting off your leg, is enough to make one despair of political humanity. But that is precisely what we have done. And the more appalling the mutilation, the more the mutilator is paid."

That comment, written before the first world war, could apply now to the PFI. I do not suggest that a private sector partner in an NHS investment will be paid for amputations by the inch, but the Government's principal indicators to measure NHS efficiency are to do with response times and throughput. Time and again, we are told by Ministers that the case for NHS reforms rests on throughput in the NHS—the number of finished consultant episodes—is higher than ever before. Each year, the number increases. The Government are measuring NHS efficiency against quantity of care rather than quality.

We should measure NHS performance against not the number of procedures undertaken but the effect of health interventions on the health of people. The Government are not doing that. We know the proportion of ambulances that reach heart attack patients or get them to hospital within a specified time, but we do not know what proportion of ambulances get patients to hospital alive. Surely that is the most important measure. We should not look only at the number of people who arrive at hospital alive, but the number who emerge from hospital to live a full and healthy life.

The United States has a privately financed health care system and its health interventions are hugely higher than in the United Kingdom. It spends twice as much of a larger per capita gross national product on health than this country, but Americans do not live longer than we do—in fact, they have a lower average life expectancy. More treatment does not necessarily improve health, yet clinically based PFI contracts will largely be measured on quantity.

The dividing line between those PFI projects for the NHS that I would support and those that I would not is that between clinical and clinically related services, 82 and support services. At face value, that has the Secretary of State's support. In his speech on PFI to the Royal College of Physicians on 21 November, he said:

"It is no part of the government's policy to transfer the delivery of NHS clinical services into the private sector. The PFI is about the provision of the supporting facilities."

I agree with every word of that, but—it is a big but—two paragraphs later in the same speech, the Secretary of State said:

"Some Trusts have explored local arrangements for private sector provision for some clinical and clinical support services. That is a matter for local determination."

The Secretary of State cannot have it both ways. He is either in favour of applying the PFI to support services in the NHS or of it being applied more widely. If the Government said that their policy was the former, and amended the Bill to make it clear where the PFI would be used in the NHS, I would support that—But the Government have not made it clear, which is why the amendment is so important. It would give Parliament the option to approve taking liability for some schemes but not for others. Later amendments, also draw that distinction and would mean that the PFI could be applied to the NHS, but now willy-nilly to any scheme. We must be careful that by applying the PFI to the NHS, we do not destroy its public health care ethos.

As I said at our previous sitting, I understand that to date 44 PFI projects for and within the NHS have been approved, of which 11 are financed for clinical or clinically related services. Thus, one quarter of the projects are in what I regard as the danger zone. It is possible to go ahead with PFI projects in the NHS without entering that zone. Ministers, supported by members on both sides of the Committee, are trying to get the PFI up and running and to make it work. Surely, then, they should concentrate on projects outside the danger zone. The cash split is not quite three to one—£60 million of £165 million to date commited to PFI projects in the NHS has been spent on clinically related contracts. That is a generous estimate. I suspect that the actual figure is slightly lower, but a number were hybrid schemes, including some clinical and some clinical support services, and my figures did not differentiate between them. Perhaps one third of the money has gone on clinical schemes. There is plenty of scope to make the PFI work without entering the danger zone.
