Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

Mr. Elfyn Llwyd (Meirionnydd Nant Conwy): I add my voice in support of the amendment. It is right and proper that we should cater for different cultures and languages. My first language is Welsh, so I have a special personal interest. I just want to flag up the point that it is important that the amendment be considered and added to the Bill.

The Chairman: I call Mr. Jonathan Evans—who is also Welsh.

The Parliamentary Secretary, Lord Chancellor's Department (Mr. Jonathan Evans): But, sadly, not a Welsh speaker.

It is the intention of the Lord Chancellor's Department to ensure that the factors to which the hon. Member for Hornsey and Wood Green (Mrs. Roche) has drawn attention are taken into account in the provision of information. However, as drafted, the amendment is too wide to be capable of sensible implementation. Even if it were not, by making further additions to clause 8(6), which specifies certain areas that may be covered by regulation, the inference may be drawn that that list is exhaustive and, accordingly, excludes other considerations.

It has always been the intention that regulations governing information meetings would be developed as a result of findings from the pilot exercise, which still seems to be the most sensible way of operating. As such matters are new to divorce legislation, it would seem wise to test what is the most appropriate and effective way in which to disseminate information before committing ourselves to detailed provisions in regulation.

The current stipulations under clause 8(6) are intended to give a general indication of the sort of matters with which regulations may deal, and I do not believe that greater specified detail would be particularly helpful at this stage. Nothing in clause 8 would preclude regulations from being made along the lines suggested by the hon. Member for Hornsey and Col. 166 Wood Green. Indeed, the Welsh Language Act 1993, which I had the pleasure of discussing in Standing Committee with the hon. Member for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy (Mr. Llwyd), already requires information at information meetings to be in Welsh, without the addition to which the hon. Gentleman referred. In other words, he and I agree on the outcome, but whether we need to bring about a change in the provisions to achieve it is questionable.

Another aspect that I find decisive is the remark made by the hon. Member for Hornsey and Wood Green when she moved the amendment. She said that we must take into account cultural and religious requirements. I am certain that that practice will be exercised in the Lord Chancellor's Department. Some members of the Committee may have missed this, but she said that more than 140 languages are spoken in her borough of Haringey. She seemed to be suggesting that we should be arranging for the information to be translated into those 140 languages. I am not sure whether that was a practical proposition, but if that is how the amendment should be interpreted, it is another reason why I cannot recommend it to the Committee.

Ms Corston: I must say at the outset, to avoid doubt, that the amendments are about child support. However, I shall do my utmost to restrict m y remarks to issues that are relevant to this Bill, to the way the Child Support Agency interfaces with the current matrimonial legislation, and to the court framework that was in place before the Child Support Act 1995. I give that reassurance because I am aware that a debate that strays into the subject of child support could last not just for a couple of sittings, but for the two years to which the Minister referred.

We all know the consequences of the agency created by the Child Support Act from our postbags and television. It has already been amended twice by primary legislation, which is unprecedented. The Child Support Agency has been the biggest social policy fiasco for a generation. At the end of September 1995, there were 161,309 cases of child support payment arrears, totalling £738 million. It is likely that £1 billion of child support is owing through the agency. An all- Col. 190 party group recently put that estimate to the Minister and he did not dissent.

That level of arrears reflects the fact that many people regard the legislation as unacceptable. The same happened with the poll tax-the legislation was passed by the House but it came up against such public opposition that it became virtually unenforceable. The Act was based on a document called "Putting Children First". The title of the document and its general principle-that parents should be responsible for their children—probably reassured many hon. Members that the Act would reflect that. However, in practice the Act has focused exclusively on the financial obligations of the so-called absent parent and has ignored other issues relating to parental responsibility and the discharge of parental obligations which most of us would consider normal and obvious.

The child support legislation was underpinned by the conventional wisdom that the court system which it was designed to buttress had failed. I doubt that conventional wisdom. Before the Child Support Agency was established, child maintenance for divorcing couples was dealt with by county courts under the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973. Unmarried people had to resolve maintenance through the magistrates courts—a system which did not work well—but the county court jurisdiction did not fail. The problem was that no organisation or agency had responsibility for enforcement and collection of the sums agreed during the ancillary relief procedure in the county courts.

The clean-break mechanism grew up over time in divorce legislation. That break was never supposed to be between parents and children. There has been a good deal of misunderstanding on that point—some of it uninformed, some deliberate—suggesting that clean breaks involved fathers walking away from their children. That was never the intention. The clean break was between the divorcing man and woman. The aim was that, when a marriage ended, the courts should find a solution which ensured that there were no continuing mutual obligations between the parties to the marriage, while making proper arrangements for children.

The clean-break system developed to the extent that men were often persuaded to walk away from equity in the former matrimonial home—the sums were sometimes as high as £50,000—to ensure that the mother and children were secure in their accommodation. Family homelessness was clearly to be avoided and the importance of providing a roof over a child's head was recognised. It was acknowledged that women were less economically secure than men and were generally unable to raise capital for a mortgage. Women often get divorced at an age when they are not mortgageable, so that development meant that many men had to start all over again on the housing ladder.

Many factors were taken into account by district judges and county courts when assessing child maintenance, with consideration being given to sums forgone in other respects—clean-break settlements being one of those. Those factors were laid out in section 25 of the 1973 Act. They included the parties' income; their earning capacity; whether the woman was Image Col. 191 of an age at which she could train to get a better job or to get a job at all; any property owned by the parties; their financial needs and obligations; their ages; the duration of the marriage; whether either was disabled; and the contribution that each had made to the marriage. That last factor recognised the fact that many women who had not worked for some time and had given their time to running the home, supporting the husband in his career, and bringing up the children.

The first and paramount consideration in section 25 was whether there was any child of the family who was under 18. That was always the court's paramount consideration when deciding what arrangements should be ordered to be made between divorcing parties.
