Mr. Barry Legg
(Milton Keynes, South-West)
: When we adjourned for lunch, I was talking about some of the problems that charities face inadvertently not meeting the requirements set out in section 505E of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988. They either fail to meet the primary purposes test in paragraph (e)(i) or the beneficiaries' test in paragraph (e)(ii). I provided a couple of examples before lunch of charities' problems and their activities that are subject to tax.

The Inland Revenue and the Charity Commission have foreseen the problems and have proposed that charities set up trading subsidiaries. For many small companies, however, that process consumes time and effort and necessitates professional advisers. If a trading subsidiary is set up, its tax relief is then obtained under section 338 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988. To achieve such relief the subsidiary has to pay a 476 deed of covenant back to the charity. Again, that process often causes practical difficulties for charities, which have to guess the amount of profit in the subsidiary before the year ends so that they can make a deed of covenant payment to cover that profit and eliminate the possibility of paying tax. Thus many charities have to go in for guesswork and will usually overestimate the size of the deed of covenant. Further dealings with the Inland Revenue are required to sort out the overdeduction of tax when the deed of covenant has been set at a higher rate than the taxable profit.

Charities have to be clear about the differences that sometimes arise between accounting periods and periods for taxation. An example has been brought to my attention of a charity doing as the Inland Revenue suggested and setting up a trading subsidiary which subsequently made up its accounts for 18 months—meaning an 18-month accounting period. That meant a tax accounting period for the first 12 months followed by another tax accounting period for six months.

Being laymen, not tax experts, people running the charity did not understand the situation and made only one payment under a deed of covenant at the end of the 18—month period. That is not uncommon. A mistake was made as their deed of payment covenant did not cover the first 12 months' trading so the whole of the profit for that period was subject to tax. That is another difficulty that charities can encounter when they try to make arrangements under current legislation.

My two new clauses would circumvent those problems. The first provides for charities to have up to £25,000 worth of trading income exempt from tax. Under those circumstances, charities would not have to set up trading companies or to worry about ancillary activities that may lead to tax liabilities. There would be a de minimis limit of £25,000 which would help many charities.

Secondly, new clause 18, which relates to section 338 of the Taxes Act 1988, provides a period of grace for charities with trading subsidiaries that opt for deeds of covenant. Instead of having to estimate the amount of the deed of covenant that they must pay in an accounting period to cover profits, charities would have an extra 12 months in which to determine the amount and could use it precisely to cover the profits in their trading subsidiaries. The new clauses would ensure that charities did not have corporation tax liabilities on their trading activities. That is what everyone wants.

Several charities have made representations to me on the important issues that the new clauses raises. Those include the National Council for Voluntary Organisations, which represents more than 670 charities, the United Kingdom branch of the World Wide Fund for Nature and Help the Aged. My hon. Friend the Paymaster General will know that the regulation task force has discussed the issue. I hope that he can suggest solutions to the problems that I have outlined.

The Paymaster General (Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory)
: New clause 17 proposes that a charity's taxable trading income should be exempt from tax if it does not exceed £25,000 in an accounting period. As JPEG 477 my hon. Friend said, charities already enjoy generous tax treatment on most of their income.

My hon. Friend mentioned that a charity's trading income is exempt if it is related to its primary purpose, and there are many examples of that. If a school, hospital, theatre or opera house is a charity, as many are, the trading activity of receiving fees for its services or performances renders its trading activity as a whole exempt from tax. Trading activities are, furthermore, exempt if the charity mainly employs its beneficiaries. There are many familiar examples of that, such as workshops for the blind. Trading profits in that case are exempt. It is important to make it clear at the outset that investment and other forms of income are not the only ones to be exempt—the trading income that I have mentioned is exempt up to any limit.

My hon. Friend gave examples of unfairness and borderline cases, including that of a sailing school for disadvantaged people. If that is a trading activity and is conducted on a fee-paying basis it will be exempt because it is the charity's primary purpose. He mentioned the fact that spare places in the boats might be filled by members of the public. Trading profits from that source would be taxable, although provision exists to make them exempt if they are de minimis or constitute a small part—perhaps 10 per cent.—of the overall trading activity. That is helpfully set out in an Inland Revenue booklet called "Trading by Charities", which I commend to my hon. Friend.

My hon. Friend mentioned the example of a village hall with a bar or refreshment facilities. That would fall to be taxed. In that case, the bar is not so much incidental to the trade of the village hall as the very trade that is carried on, which would be taxable. The charity commissioners are vigilant in ensuring that charities, however small, do not provide social drinking facilities and claim that that is, in itself, a charitable purpose. That is obviously not charitable, but it also provides unfair competition to those who run nearby pubs or restaurants.

We must always try to balance our aim of assisting charities—which enjoy substantial tax-exempt privileges—with the need to support the interests of businesses and fair competition. I am sure that my hon. Friend will know of the campaign that is run by the Federation of Small Businesses, which has complained about the extent of charities' tax advantages. He may well have read its booklet, entitled "Trading in a Jungle of Unfair Competition", in which the federation draws attention to the fact that, for instance, charity shops are substantially relieved from paying business rates. There is a mandatory 80 per cent. relief, which is frequently topped up to 100 per cent. by local authorities. Throughout the country, the total rate relief is probably in the order of £400 million. Although I cannot accept all the federation's complaints—indeed, some of them were claimed by the charities to be inaccurate—nevertheless there is something in its argument. We must not so upset the rules of competition that we put small businesses in high streets and elsewhere at substantial disadvantage.

Mr. Legg

The charity commissioners and the Inland Revenue suggest setting up a trading company as the vehicle by which to get round the problem of non-primary trading activities. More sophisticated charities are taking that route, while very small charities are penalised for having ancillary activities that are inadvertently subject to corporation tax.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory
: It is always difficult to define what a small business is, but certainly plenty of small businesses in my constituency make less than £25,000 profit a year. We owe it to both sides to keep the present situation stable. I explained that charities' trading activities are exempt if they are carried out as a primary purpose of a charity or by those who are mainly beneficiaries of a charity. My hon. Friend also drew attention to the other means whereby charities can set up trading subsidiaries and then remit the profits under a deed of covenant to the parent charity, thus avoiding tax.

New clause 18, which was also tabled by my hon. Friend, goes further and would make things easier for the charitable sector. He correctly described the way in which companies can covenant their profits to parent charities under rules that are not unique to the charitable sector, but which apply to all companies.

Under the present arrangements, it may be risky for them to donate all their profits; frequently they decide to leave at least some of the profits in the subsidiary company to cover future eventualities. If they want to donate all their profits, the precise figure may not be known in a specific year of account.

Towards the end of an accounting year, the amount of the profits should be known, at least with a high degree of approximation. A deed of covenant can then be drawn up to donate those profits to the parent company. It is common practice to err on the generous and optimistic side when deciding what the final taxable profits will be. They can be adjusted downwards retrospectively if that estimate proves to be too high. That works reasonably well in practice. The new clause proposes that companies should be able to defer making those donations up to 12 months after the end of their accounting period. I wonder whether many charities will want to do that in practice, because they would have to forego the money for an additional 12 months. Surely most of them will want to receive the money as soon as possible.

Mr. Legg
: My new clause certainly provides flexibility, but in practice a trading subsidiary of a charity, once it has drawn up its accounts shortly after the year end and knows precisely what its profit was in JPEG 479 that period, will made the deed of covenant payment, which will equal the profit for the preceding trading period.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory
: Yes, and it may take a considerable time—up to another 12 months—before that tax computation is made on the audited accounts. Therefore, we are contemplating a considerable delay before the subsidiary company can be certain that it is donating the maximum amount to the parent company. I suggest that in practice many charities will want to make the earlier donations. They can do so by the mechanism that I suggested: they can slightly overestimate the profit and then, if necessary, adjust downwards.

My hon. Friend made a valid point: I concede that if they get the estimate wrong it is not possible for them to donate above the sum specified in the deed of covenant. My hon. Friend's new clause would allow charities to top up the profitable element by way of donation afterwards, in addition to the early payment arrangements. That rather modest advance must be set against the fact that the new clause specifies a new category and would create a new complication in tax law, whereas we should try to apply tax law right across the company spectrum. Neither does this special arrangement fit well with self-assessment.

Self-assessment has already been criticised for not delivering some of the deregulatory benefits that we claim. We must be vigilant and try to keep self-assessment as simple as possible. One of the simplifications is that companies, trading bodies and individuals will be able to deal with all their income, expenditure, reliefs, exemptions and profit in one year. My hon. Friend's proposal is that subsidiaries of charities should be able to carry back from a future year into the earlier year of account. That affects the pristine simplicity of self-assessment as described by my hon. Friend the Financial Secretary and it would be an unwelcome regulatory development.

Mr. Legg
: That is what the National Council for Voluntary Organisation wants, and it represents 600 charitable bodies in the United Kingdom. So there is strong feeling in favour of the proposal.

My hon. Friend said that charities would want to use the facility and, from their representations to me, I know that they do. They could still pay the deed of covenant in the earlier period if they so wished. The proposal would give the charities some flexibility and make their lives simpler. Instead of going back to the Inland Revenue and making complicated arrangements for the repayment of tax that has been over-deducted, charities could make a deed of covenant when they were certain about the amount of profit available. That is a simplification.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory
: I have already conceded that my hon. Friend's suggestion is not a bad one. It is not absurd; nor would it be disastrous for the tax structure or for enforcement. However, for a modest increase in convenience for some charities, we are being invited to complicate the tax statutes further. Self-assessment will allow simplification and deregulation: in that context, the proposal represents an unwelcome advance.

480 Although I understand that some charities believe that they would benefit, the Committee must be the guardian of simplicity and deregulation wherever possible. The statutes are already complicated enough, so for that reason I suggest that on balance my hon. Friend's new clause is unwelcome, although I understand the motives that underlie it.
