A typical saver is confronted by a variegated picture. National savings certificates are tax-free when the money goes in and when it goes out. Banks and building societies give a higher pre-tax return but there is then a tax charge of 20 per cent. on interest. Life assurance policies attract favourable treatment when the funds accumulate. Occupational pensions—the major savings vehicle for many of our citizens—attract favourable tax relief on the funds that are invested and accumulated but are subject to pension tax not at the 20 per cent. rate but at the taxpayers' marginal rate, in the same way as earned income. Personal pensions operate in a similar way to occupational pensions but their rules and reliefs differ in detail.

There is another regime for equities, unit trusts and Government fixed-interest stock. TESSAs and PEPs appear simple, since there is no tax impact on the money going in or coming out, but they have more rules. Money must be held in TESSAs for five years without withdrawing interest or capital and there are limits on the annual amount that can be invested in PEPs. There is, therefore, confusion in the comparison of savings vehicles, which has been created primarily by the tax system, and not by the differing needs of savers and borrowers.
