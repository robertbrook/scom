clause Clause 13
title ELECTRICALLY PROPELLED VEHICLES

Amendment moved [this day]: No. 31, in page 6, line 44, leave out from "duty)" to end of line 46 and insert—

",after paragraph 2(1)(a) there shall be inserted—


'(aa) £10 if the motorcycle is an electrically propelled vehicle,'.".—[Ms Primarolo.]

4.30 pm

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

Ms Dawn Primarolo (Bristol, South): Before lunch I moved this amendment, which seeks to reduce the vehicle excise duty on electrically propelled motor cycles to £10, which is less than the £15 proposed by the Government. I reminded the Committee that duty on petrol motor cycles is also £15. By introducing differential fuel duties on unleaded petrol, the Government have shown that they are keen to encourage environmentally friendly activity in consumption. The use of electrically propelled motor cycles is considerably better for the environment.

The Paymaster General (Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory): The Government cannot support the amendment for a number of reasons. At £15, the proposed vehicle excise duty for these electrical vehicles is generous: it is the lowest rate applied to motor cycles. It can hardly be said that expenditure of £15 a year is a serious deterrent to anyone considering that mode of transport as against another.

Our overall aim is to simplify the tax system. The Opposition, in another mode, has called for that. They have tabled an amendment or new clause to urge us to simplify the system. This amendment would take us in the opposite direct: it would create a new complication.

Furthermore, it is not clear that electrically powered vehicles are as environmentally benign as the hon. Lady supposes. It is true that at the point of use electrical vehicles are almost non-polluting, but that is simply because the pollution is shifted to the source of power—the power station. In this country power is generated chiefly by the burning of fossil fuel. It is obvious that electrical vehicles derive their energy not from magic or from the Opposition, but by the burning of coal or gas elsewhere.

Although the use of electrical vehicles results in an overall CO2 reduction, the emissions from power stations, such as oxide of nitrogen, particulate matter from dust and soot, and sulphur, are more polluting. Col. 72 Unfortunately, it is well known that British coal contains a comparatively high proportion of sulphur.

We must look at the overall picture. It is not true that all electrical vehicles have an obvious environmental advantage. Having said that, they have a certain appeal, which is why they are advantageously taxed: they not only attract a low rate of vehicles excise duty—£35 for electrical cars and the proposed £15 for bicycles and tricycles—but are exempted from excise duty. Although we are increasing road fuel rates by 5 per cent. in real terms, that does not apply to electrical power. So electrical vehicles have a privileged status in the tax system. We do not want to add to that by taking an odd £5 off the proposed VED rate.

Ms Primarolo: I have heard it all now. We should be concentrating on power stations, not vehicles that contribute to poor air quality and pollution. I assume that the Paymaster General will later introduce proposals to tax power stations according to the environmental damage that they cause. We wait with bated breath to hear his comments about nuclear energy.

The Paymaster General got into difficulties last year over vehicle excise duty on the highland islands and the Isles of Scilly. We told him then that he had been given a daft brief, and we should do so again. The ability of electric vehicles to improve urban air quality is beyond dispute. The amendment does not propose that all vehicles should be taxed at the level for electrically propelled vehicles. I doubt that there will be a massive surge across the grid as people plug in their electrically propelled motor cycles. We simply want clearly to set out the direction of future policy.

The Paymaster General used entirely different arguments when he justified the measly 15 per cent. reduction in duty on gas fuels, which was intended to assist the gas fuel industry's development—he said that we needed to move cautiously. The amendment makes it clear that a vehicle will be treated more favourably if it is more environmentally friendly than one that runs on petrol. The Minister has not responded to that or explained why duties should be the same. The Opposition therefore press their amendment.

clause Clause 14
title STEAM POWERED VEHICLES ETC

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I have a brief comment. The clause provides that all steam powered vehicles be included in the special concessionary tax class, paying an annual duty of £35. That will be welcomed by all owners of such vehicles. This is a convenient moment at which to tell the Committee that the Government intend to go further and to exempt all non-commercial vehicles from vehicle excise duty if they are more than 25 years old.

In the Budget, my right hon. and learned Friend the Chancellor announced that that discretion would apply to cars and motor cycles, which was warmly received. It has become apparent during subsequent discussions with interested parties that it makes sense to extend the discretion to all other non-commercial vehicles, such as lorries. They may not be very many in number but to try to draw a distinction between exempt private cars, light and heavy vehicles, certain veteran and classic cars and bits of military equipment would be extremely complex administratively.

To give the Committee an example, under the current proposals, an ex-military personnel carrier owned by an enthusiast with seats for six people would be exempt, but if an otherwise identical vehicle had space to carry ammunition, it would be classified as a goods vehicle and be liable to pay £150 a year. If the vehicle had a gun fixed to its top, it would count as a "burden" and would again result in a £150 payment. Not only are those anomalies unfair to enthusiasts, but it is extremely difficult for the postmistress in the village concerned to try to sort out whether the vehicle standing outside falls within the exempt class.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: It is extremely complex, which is why we want to simplify. Steam powered Image Col. 75 vehicles fall into different categories. Steam powered agricultural vehicles such as traction engines were classified in the special concessionary group and paid only £35 prior to the proposals. Steamrollers, however, were classified in the special vehicles group and liable to £150 VED if they weighed over 3.5 tonnes or, if they fell in the private and light goods category, the VED was £140 a year. So there was a range, which we aim to simplify. Now, under the clause, if vehicles are over 25 years old they will pay nothing; and if they are steam driven they will pay £35 a year providing they are less than 25 years old.

Mr. Bayley: I am grateful to the Minister giving way to save each of us getting up and making separate speeches.

The proposal would mean that newer and new—should new vehicles be constructed—steam driven road rollers, for example, will have a lower rate of tax than diesel driven steam rollers. That will create an incentive for manufacturers to consider changing from diesel to steam if the technology can be found to do that. There may well be an environmental consequence if the fuel that they burn is more polluting than diesel. Have the Government considered that anomaly and the possibility that it may encourage the use of a less clean fuel?

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I think that I can assure the Committee that no manufacturer will start building and selling steam driven road rollers to win a comparatively trivial annual advantage in vehicle excise duty. Even if people could be persuaded to buy them, the cost involved in maintaining and running them would overwhelm any duty advantage. I think that I can assure the hon. Gentleman about that.

Mr. Gordon Prentice: I congratulate the Minister on that concession. Until the Minister made his announcement, he would have had to pay an increased rate for his old fire engine. What happened in the past? My constituent, who follows those matters closely, told me that up until a few years ago all serviceable fire appliances were exempt from road tax. Is that the case?

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I shall have to take advice about what the duty rate was in the past. I am glad that our relieving measure has been welcomed by the hon. Gentleman.

The amendment seeks to change the arrangements for the exemption of classic cars. The clause provides that cars, vans and motor cycles more than 25 years old are exempt from vehicle excise duty. There are apparently 150,000 company-licensed vehicles over 25 years old and there is doubtless a significant number of roadworthy unlicensed vehicles of that age.

Why was a 25 year cut-off point chosen? We think that that is too low for the definition of a classic car. The design of many 1960s vehicles is such that they are acceptable as an everyday means of transport; many are in reasonable condition and still used. We need to examine the issue closely. I tried to find commentary on why 25 years was chosen and what proportion of the 150,000 proposed vehicles would be excluded if the age were set at 35 years. If abolishing car tax on such vehicles encourages people to keep highly polluting vehicles on the road, it would defeat the aim of the exercise. The Government could have chosen to list the cars that they Col. 78 felt came into that category rather than using a blanket age.
