Mr. Matthew Carrington
(Fulham)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

After the excitements of new clause 15, I can reassure the Committee that new clause 16 is rather technical and somewhat boring. Consequently, I do not anticipate that it will take us most of the rest of the afternoon to debate it.

The purpose of the new clause is to address an anomaly in the gilts repro market. As matters stand at present, banks that enter into repos with a maturity in excess of one year do not have to withhold tax on the payment of the deemed interest on the repo. Securities houses entering into exactly the same transactions have to withhold tax when they make the final settlement under the repo. Tax paid will be recoverable but there is a delay in payment, which makes the securities houses uncompetitive in comparison with banks. That problem affects only repos in excess of one year. Below a year, securities houses and banks are treated in the same way.

The securities houses to which I refer are some of the large American institutions such as Salomon Brothers JPEG 833 and Goldman Sachs and one or two Japanese houses such as Nomura Securities. All are caught under the present arrangements. The new clause tackles the problem by giving the Treasury the power, through regulation, to prescribe that certain institutions—the security houses—be treated in the same way as banks with respect to repo activities over one year.

The new clause is important. If the Treasury is not given this power, the security houses will be forced to carry out their activities offshore with consequences both to the competitiveness of the City of London and, more importantly, to employment prospects for people living in London since activities will be centred outside the UK. The main purpose, however, is to ensure that the gilt repo market is successful and contains the maximum amount of liquidity.

The seriousness of the problem has been debated. Gilt repos are a new market and it has been doubted whether repos with maturities of a year and above will constitute a significant portion of the market. Some indication of the likely development of these markets can be gleaned through consideration of equivalent markets in the United States. There, some 10 per cent. of repo books extend to a year or beyond. In the United Kingdom today, around 5 per cent. of the repo books are heading towards a year in maturity, but it is likely that the UK market will develop in the same way as the American market. A significant proportion of the market will probably be caught by the present failure to apply equitable treatment between securities houses and banks.

I understand that my hon. Friend the Economic Secretary may be reluctant to accept the new clause. It is a probing new clause, intended to find out the Treasury's intentions. The important fact is that the Treasury does not currently have the power by regulation to alter the treatment of securities houses in this market without primary legislation. Unless action is taken quickly to put the problem right, I urge my hon. Friend seriously to consider whether discussions could take place with the industry with a view to tabling an appropriate amendment on Report; otherwise, we shall have to wait until next year's Finance Bill, which could run into the problem of us having a general election.

I hope that my hon. Friend feels that she can do something positive to overcome the problem. It is in everyone's interest—not just the interest of the Treasury—to ensure that the gilt repo market is as liquid as possible and has as many players as possible. It would be undesirable to exclude from the market the securities houses, which are major players in that financial activity.

New clause 20 seeks to allow enterprise investment scheme companies to bare-boat charter and to own ships. The EIS replaced the business expansion scheme, which had been utilised by a number of shipping ventures and had brought about a modest level of investment in ships, all of which were registered and managed in the United Kingdom. As with the BES, under the EIS the company has to be the beneficial owner of all its ships. Unfortunately, the EIS is not attractive to shipping ventures, because the relatively high level of risk is disproportionate to the relief: 20 per cent. as opposed to 40 per cent. under the old BES.

Shipowner risk usually falls into two categories: first, the trading risk, which is the ability to secure charters at a rate sufficient to generate a trading profit after taking JPEG 839 account of operating and financing costs, and, secondly, the capital risk, which is the possibility that the capital value of a ship may fall faster that its depreciated book value—from my experience of volatile shipping markets, that is a common occurrence.

Under the old BES scheme, 40 per cent. tax relief was sufficiently high for investors to be prepared to accept both those elements of risk. If my hon. Friend accepted the new clause to bare-boat chartered ships, that would remove the capital risk and increase the potential interest of investors, who would still be limited to a 20 per cent. relief.

The new development is that since 1993 it has been possible to enter ships on the British register that are bare-boat chartered to British resident operators. The operator of those ships is not the beneficial owner, but he acts as if he were the owner while paying a fixed charter hire. Conditions would be attached to such ships in EIS companies. As with own ships, they should be registered in the United Kingdom and be subject to the rules on management. In addition, the bare-boat charter should be for a minimum period. The new clause provides a period of five years, which is the right balance.

Considerable risk from such ventures would remain due to the existing one-year charter-out restriction and the chances of a mismatch. The shipping market is volatile, as I said. But the advantage would be to allow companies to establish shipping operations without the need to be highly geared with loan financing. That would stimulate United Kingdom economic activity and employment, which is the aim of the EIS.

New clause 21 would reintroduce 100 per cent. capital allowances for new investment in shipping. That would be an important corollary to rollover relief as it would help those sectors that do not benefit from such relief. I refer, of course, to those companies that operate scheduled services, such as containers, ferries and cruise ships.
