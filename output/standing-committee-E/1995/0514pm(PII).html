Mr. Jonathan Evans
: I was saying earlier that it has been suggested in debate that clause 26 may lead to a two-tier system, with the legally aided party being forced into mediation while the better-off spouse would have full access to legal representation. I do not believe that that will prove to be the case. The Government believe that in many cases, legally aided or otherwise, mediation may have a role to play as a more appropriate way of resolving particular types of family dispute than arm's-length negotiations between lawyers or through court procedures.

The Bill makes that belief clear in several ways.

Private individuals are of course free to spend their money as they see fit, and the Government do not want to prevent them from doing so, but the benefits of mediation are such that I believe that suitable couples will use mediation in appropriate cases, even when they are paying privately. Mediation is not therefore a cheap and cheerful alternative to legal advice. Research undertaken by the Joseph Rowntree Foundation certainly shows that there have been many good outcomes of mediation, and those are not limited to cases that depend on some form of legal assistance. Legal advice will be available in support of mediation for those who need it, and will be state funded for those who are eligible.

When couples mediate, they negotiate with the benefit of a neutral mediator—in other words, they represent themselves in the negotiations. I think that I have made it clear how inappropriate it would be lawyers to be drawn into that process. People should not need a lawyer to represent them in the negotiations, but they may need legal advice and assistance in support of mediation. That will be available when needed. That is an important distinction when, for example, a couple wishes to negotiate matters such as residence and contract arrangements through mediation but to be separately represented in arm's-length negotiations in respect of matters such as property, which may be inappropriate for mediation.

Subject to eligibility, there is nothing in the provisions to prevent mediation services being given in relation to matters concerning the child, with legal advice and support where needed, and legal aid being given for representation in relation to property matters. Those distinctions are frequently drawn in mediation.

270 When public funds are at stake the Government clearly have a legitimate interest in attempting to secure the best service at the greatest value for money, without—I emphasise without—jeopardising a client's right to fair resolution of the dispute. That is the philosophy behind the rebuttable presumption created by clause 26.

I should further make it clear that legal advice and assistance will be available not only in support of mediation but at its conclusion, to ensure that clients are able to seek independent legal advice on the terms of any agreement that is reached through mediation before signing it. That affords clients protection against agreeing to settlements that may not be in the their best interests, although I hope that once mediation has become established as a method of solving disputes and any public misunderstanding of mediation has been dispelled, it will become clear that lawyers rarely see the need to unpick an understanding that has been reached between the parties. That should not be interpreted as any sort of injunction against there being legal advice at that stage of the process. Legal advice and assistance will be available to couples who need assistance with the divorce process and advice about their legal responsibilities, as is the case now under the green form scheme.

I want to respond to the points made by my hon. Friend the Member for Billericay about the actual costs of legal aid in the current structure of divorce settlements. In 1991–92, the total sum of money expended on legal aid in matrimonial matters—civil matrimonial legal aid for representation—was a little more than £150 million. At that time, green form assistance and advice given in support of the divorce process accounted for about £42 million. In the past four years the cost for green form advice and assistance has risen to some £46 million, from £42 million, but for representation in civil matrimonial legal aid cases it has risen from £150 million to £296 million. A considerable investment in legal aid is being expended through the courts.

Mr. Jonathan Evans
: I will respond to the points that were made by my hon. Friend the Member for Stockton, South to reassure the Committee on the clause.

The clause deals with the power of the court to make occupation orders and non-molestation orders on an ex parte basis. It is of great importance that ex parte orders should be available in cases of actual or threatened violence. Such circumstances tend to occur more frequently in family disputes than in other types of litigation because the parties are often still in close contact and in a state of high emotional tension. We all understand that point.

Even in such cases, however, the court should grant an ex parte order only in exceptional circumstances, where the interests of justice or the protection of the applicant or a child clearly demand immediate intervention.

The clause specifies the criteria that the court must consider when hearing an application for an ex parte order. They cover situations in which there is a risk of significant harm to the applicant or relevant child attributable to the conduct of the respondent—that amendment was put into the Bill as a result of observations that were made in the other place—unless an order is made immediately.

Where the applicant is terrified of the respondent, some protection may be necessary to enable the applicant—as my hon. Friend the Member for Stockton, South said, that is frequently the wife—to pursue a remedy. That is also the case when respondents are deliberately evading service. Sadly, evading service is often used by respondents in domestic violence cases as a tactic to wear down the applicant's resolution. The balance of justice and of convenience may be in favour of making an ex parte order with a short return date.

The Bill specifically provides that where an ex parte order is made the respondent must be given the oppor- JPEG 305 tunity to make representations about the order as soon as is just and convenient at a hearing of which all parties will have been notified. The clause also ensures that where an occupation order under clause 33 or clause 35 is made ex parte, an non-entitled applicant will not be prevented from obtaining an initial order.
