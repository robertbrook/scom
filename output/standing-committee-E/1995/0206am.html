Clause 21
EC SECOND VAT SIMPLIFICATION DIRECTIVE

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Paymaster General (Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory)
: It may be for the convenience of the Committee if I say a little not just about clause 21, but about clauses 22 to 25 as well. All those clauses implement the second VAT simplification directive. Therefore, clause 21 is the first of a series of measures designed to simplify the operation of the VAT system, especially for businesses trading with other member states in the European Community. The United Kingdom played a leading role in negotiating the package, which represents a further step towards simplifying the VAT system—part of the Government's drive to deregulate wherever possible.

The European Community VAT system was last substantially revised for the completion of the single market on 1 January 1993. By and large, that has worked successfully and businesses have benefited from the abolition of all fiscal frontiers. It is still necessary to press on where possible and to introduce simplification measures where feasible. The vehicles for such changes are the simplification directives which amend the core VAT directive—known as the sixth VAT directive—negotiated in 1977. The second simplification directive, to which clause 21 refers, was agreed in the spring of last year. Member states agreed to implement the changes from 1 January 1996.

The United Kingdom's approach to the changes is always to try to obtain the best possible deal for our businessess in the context of the single market. For instance, we have secured an important new provision in the shape of fiscal warehousing arrangements, which will offer businesses the opportunity to trade VAT-free in certain commodities such as metals, foodstuffs and chemicals. They will therefore enjoy a cash-flow advantage while the goods remain in the warehousing regime.. The availability of fiscal 90 warehousing arrangements will aslo help to keep London in the first rank of commodity trading centres.

We have also negotiated other changes to the VAT system which should make matters easier for businesses. Those changes include standardisation of the rules for calculating VAT due on imports—we shall cover that in discussions on clause 23—and simplification of the VAT rule for process work which is carried out on goods. That is dealt with under clause 25. The measures may sound very technical—indeed, many of them are—but, nevertheless, they are of true value.

Perhaps the most important feature of how we have implemented the changes has been our close work with trade interests and representative bodies in order to agree the finer working details of the arrangements. We have therefore undertaken extensive consultation as well as exposing draft legislation to those interested bodies. The dialogue between customs and the business community continues.

>VALUE ADDED TAX: FISCAL AND OTHER WAREHOUSING

Ms Dawn Primarolo
(Bristol, South)
: I beg to move amendment No. 73, in page 158, line 46, at end insert—

"(10) Regulations may provide for section 18C(2) and section I 8C(3) not to apply in cases where the Commissioners are satisfied that the services are related to goods removed from a warehousing or fiscal warehousing regime and placed in a warehousing or fiscal warehousing regime in the United Kingdom or another Member State, and such other conditions, if any, as may be specified in the regulations or the Commissioners may impose are fulfilled.",

We are in for a complicated morning's debate on VAT proposals. As the Paymaster General explained, clause 22 and schedule 2 deal with fiscal and other warehousing. They make facility for fiscal warehouses in the United Kingdom with effect from a day that is to be appointed—1 June 1996, according to customs. Schedule 2 contains the legislative changes to the Value Added Tax Act 1994.

The background to the proposals is that chain transactions in commodities have been a problem in the single market. Up to 31 December 1992 trading in commodities escaped VAT while the commodities were kept in customs warehousing regimes. From 1 January 1993 to 31 December 1995, however, that could not technically be done for goods that were considered to be in free circulation in the European Community. That meant that traders who bought and sold goods could be legally obliged to register where the goods were situated. In practice, most states did not pursue the registration requirements—hardly a satisfactory solution.

The second VAT simplification directive, which did not simplify much—it did quite the reverse— JPEG 91 introduces fiscal warehousing to allow trading in a country to be sheltered from VAT on terms that are similar to those for customs warehousing. Not all European Community countries, however, have customs warehousing regimes.

The new United Kingdom law comes into effect on 1 June 1996. The law and the directive specify a restricted range of goods to which fiscal warehousing will apply. Those include most traded metals apart from gold, as well as basic foodstuffs, wool, certain bulk chemicals and mineral and vegetable oils. Those are defined according to the tariff codes that are used for Intrastat purposes. The Value Added Tax Act 1994 will be amended by statutory instrument and will treat supplies of unallocated or unascertained goods in fiscal warehouses as supplies of goods.

As in other warehousing regimes, customs will authorise and control warehouse keepers and will be able to take many factors into account and impose conditions. A fiscal warehouse is defined as a place. Despite the reference to a singular premise, however, customs will allow a warehouse keeper to specify several premises as his warehouse. Warehouse keepers are liable for VAT on goods that are missing or found to be deficient before removal from the regime.

No VAT need be paid on the purchase or acquisition of goods that are to be placed in the fiscal warehouse, provided that the purchaser or the acquirer prepares a certificate to say that the goods will go into the regime. No VAT is due on supplies of the goods while in the regime. When the goods leave the regime, however, the person removing them must account for the VAT on the value of the last supply or acquisition into or under the warehousing regime.

If, however, the remover is registered for United Kingdom VAT, he accounts for the VAT on his next VAT return. An unregistered person is still liable to pay VAT on removal and must pay VAT at the time of removal, as with VAT on goods coming out of customs or excise warehouses. The removals do not count towards VAT registration limits.

Certain services related to goods in any type of warehouse will be zero-rated from 1 June 1996. These include the warehouse keeper's storage charges, permitted work on goods in customs, and any physical work on goods in a fiscal warehousing regime, unless prohibited by regulation. The recipient of the service must give a certificate, except for supplies by the warehouse keeper of warehousing the goods, and penalties equal to the VAT otherwise' due can be imposed for incorrect certificates. The person removing the goods from the warehouse must account for VAT on services supplied since the last supply of goods within the warehouse.

Up to 31 December 1995, processing of goods in warehousing regimes was zero-rated as it was treated as supply of goods. The amendment seeks to clarify the difficulties with the new regime. Regulations will be made in the United Kingdom to allow VAT-free 92 transfer of goods from a United Kingdom fiscal warehousing regime to another such regime, whether in the United Kingdom or in another EC country. However, VAT will still be due on services rendered after the last supply within the United Kingdom warehouse. That may cause difficulty for non-United Kingdom removers of goods. It is likely that a concession will be sought in this area.

The interaction of the fiscal warehouse regime with zero-rating under the terminal markets order is currently under discussion. There are various problems. Not all member states have warehousing regimes that allow VAT-free supplies in the warehouse, so they may not all have fiscal warehousing regimes. The restriction of commodities limits the usefulness of the regime. The provisions are not retrospective and thus leave transactions between 1 January 1993 and the date of implementation in a legal limbo. I would like the Paymaster General to explain how that will be dealt with, though it is hoped that no action will be taken.

The amendment would require regulations to be made to allow VAT-free transfer of goods from a United Kingdom fiscal warehousing regime to another such regime in the United Kingdom or another EC country. However, VAT will be due on related services rendered after the last supply of goods in the first fiscal warehousing regime. Such VAT may cause difficulties for non-United Kingdom removers of goods. The amendment is intended to prevent such VAT charges arising on the supply of related services when goods are transferred between fiscal warehousing regimes.

I would be grateful if the Paymaster General could explain whether other European states will be introducing a similar relief, if they have not already done so, and over what time period it will be implemented.

: I set out in my introductory remarks the benefits of the introduction of a warehousing regime throughout the Community. The directive will apply in all the countries of the European Union. It is up to other countries to pass the directive's provisions into law in the same way that we are doing.

I hope that the hon. Lady will accept the assurance that this is a welcome and simplifying measure for businesses in this country and overseas. It also maintains our unique structure of zero rates.

The inherent flexibility of this approach, which is familiar in the context of income tax and capital gains tax, will enable Customs to differentiate between deliberate avoidance and innocent transactions, which a more rigid and automatic response does not readily allow. For this reason, we have tried to define the scope of the powers as clearly as possible without reducing their effectiveness. The powers can be used only where there has been a supply within a group. A company must either enter or leave a group, or enter into a transaction, as I explained to my hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield. This is what is called the relevant event. The term transaction is of wide application. It covers not only the transfer of assets of a business as a going concern, but, for example, assignments of individual contractual rights or liabilities.

The powers are further circumscribed. The relevant event must contribute to circumstances that result in input tax deduction being disproportionately greater than the corresponding output VAT. Essentially, the tax benefits of an avoidance scheme must be present. Secondly, even if that is the case, the powers cannot be used if the relevant event producing the tax advantage was undertaken for genuine business reasons unconnected with VAT avoidance.
