SPIRITS: RATE OF DUTY

Mr. Malcolm Bruce
: I beg to move amendment No. 4, in page 1, line 16, leave out "£19.78" ', and insert "£18.96" '.

I am sure that you, Mr. Hogg, will recognise that the amendment's thrust would be extremely welcome north of the border. It would, however, benefit the entire United Kingdom economy. Although it may seem at first sight that cutting the duty on spirits by approximately double the amount that was proposed in the Budget would lead to a loss of revenue, the evidence suggests the contrary. Indeed, following the introduction of a 26p rise in spirits duty last year, the Chancellor so depressed sales of home-produced spirits—especially whisky—that the revenue yield fell. The reports from Customs and Excise indicate that that will be the likely outcome over a full year.

When the Chancellor of the Exchequer introduced the 26p rise—which took place, you will remember, Mr. Hogg, after the House defeated the Chancellor's proposal to put 17.5 per cent. VAT on fuel—I described it as the polities of pique. There is evidence to suggest that it was introduced in a rush and quite contrary to what the Chancellor himself had said in his Budget speech only a few days earlier, when he specifically, identified the importance of the whisky industry and said that for that reason he had no plans to introduce any extra duty. And yet, a week later, after the humiliation of a defeat on the Floor of the House, he concluded that he had no option but to put 26p on a bottle.

I pointed out then that the consequence of that was counterproductive. I believe that the logical follow-through is to go further than simply reversing the cut. A cut of 27p as against the previous year's increase of 26p is not a dramatic difference. It enables the Chancellor to say that it is the biggest single cut in duty ever introduced on spirits—which is, of course, true and it is welcome. In reality, however, it is, over two years, only a cut of a penny and not of 27p.

Although I will deal with the Scottish dimension, I do not believe that this is exclusively a matter of special pleading for the Scotch whisky industry and jobs in Scotland. That is one important effect, but probably not the most important in terms of the United Kingdom economy.

The whisky export market is currently in a healthy state, but the home market has been more than a little depressed due to a combination of high duty, the impact of cross-border shopping and a tendency for people to switch to wine consumption, especially when there is such a wide differential between spirits and their alternatives, particularly wine, which enjoys a lower rate of tax. I accept that that is an on-going cultural switch which is taking place naturally and I am not trying to interfere with people's right to make such choices. I want to ensure merely that they are made on a fair and equal basis.

The whisky industry in particular has been complaining for several years that it is at a JPEG 7 disadvantage in the home market when compared with imported competitors. The added cost of producing whisky comes from the statutory requirement for it to be matured for a minimum of three years before it can be classified as Scotch whisky. Of course, many good whiskies are matured for much longer than that. The abolition, several years ago, of stock relief also added to the cost of whisky production and consequently squeezed the margins. That has been further aggravated by a further widening of the gap in duty.

The amendment is proof of our recognition of the whisky industry as a home industry that faces an unfair disadvantage against its major competitors. You, Mr. Hogg, may know this, but it is not widely known that one third of all branded spirits that are traded in the world is made in the United Kingdom. Scotch whisky is the most important of those, but we must recognise that we also produce a large amount of gin and other spirits, which makes a significant contribution to the market.

Export sales of the major British commodities over 10 or 20 years show that Scotch whisky is consistently our biggest single export. It may be that in any given year, other products outsell whisky abroad but year on year, it is always in the top three and, taken over an extended period, whereas the sales of other commodities fluctuate, whisky continues to be a prime export, averaging between £2.5 billion and £3 billion of export sales. It is well known that a healthy home market is necessary if a good export market is to be sustained—even if the home market accounts for only 10 to 12 per cent. of total sales. Executives in the whisky industry have told me, as I am sure they have told Members on all sides of the House, that it has been extremely difficult to negotiate for a fair deal with those export markets where Scotch whisky is unduly discriminated against in terms of duty and import tariffs, when the standard retort from many Governments is "Why should we give you a better deal than your own home Government? If you cannot persuade them of the importance of your industry, why should we treat you differently?"

Mr Bruce
: The hon. Gentleman will be aware that I made it clear to members of the Treasury and Civil Service Select Committee as it was then called, that I was in favour of reducing duty. Its report made it clear that the case on whisky and spirits was unproven—there was evidence to suggest, as Customs and Excise staff told the Committee, that the increase last year had led to a fall in revenue. The Committee reserved its position on spirits but acknowledged that there was evidence that the differential on spirits was having an effect. I was happy to endorse that report. The evidence we heard was useful and I was anxious to ensure that the Committee produced a report that we could all support; that does not prejudice my or my party's right to argue the case for the amendment.

It is true that my predecessor, my right hon. Friend the Member for Berwick upon Tweed (Mr. Beith), did not propose specific cuts in duty—I do not know whether that is because he does not drink. I have been Treasury spokesman for my party for almost two years and we have discussed the issue over that period; the industry has also marshalled its arguments effectively and I am not promoting a personal view It is a party view that has been endorsed by the party's policy committee. The argument that such a policy would be beneficial to the British economy is increasingly understood.

I return to the effects on Scotland. The majority of the industry's jobs are based in the more populous areas of the central belt, where bottling and blending occurs. If the hon. Member for Kilmarnock and Loudoun (Mr. McKelvey) were here, he would support me vigorously, given that he comes from the home of Johnnie Walker and constantly reminds us of its contribution to the British economy, the jobs it creates and the difficulties the industry faces because of that discriminatory tax. In my own area of north-east Scotland mainly small distilleries are based in rural areas. In the past two or three years, 200 people directly employed in the whisky industry have lost their jobs because of the difficulties that the industry has had—those difficulties are not entirely due to tax, although that has contributed to the problems. That may not sound like a great many jobs, but they are relatively highly paid, skilled jobs in rural areas—sometimes very remote rural areas—where alternative employment is non-existent. That is a serious loss to a rural economy.

I have suffered the closure of a distillery in my constituency. Its future is uncertain, but it is not operating at the moment. The sad fact is that the distillery was sold by its previous owners, Morrison brothers, to Suntory of Japan. At the time of the sale I was led to believe that that would be beneficial: it was well-known that Suntory is a relatively acceptable Japanese whisky because it is blended with a substantial amount of bulk Scotch malt to give it smoothness. It seems logical to me that Suntory would want a quality distillery, such as Glengarioch in my constituency, to produce that smoothness. Sadly, for one reason or other, it decided that it did not, so the distillery faces closure. Other distilleries have been JPEG 9 mothballed, and in other areas staffing and production levels have been reduced.

I accept that there is a cyclical factor in the whisky industry, but I assert—my assertion is reinforced by the industry itself—that this discriminatory tax contributes to the decline in employment.

My concluding remarks relate to the United Kingdom economy. Having established that over the years spirits, particularly whisky, have consistently been a major United Kingdom export, it is extraordinary that we continue to impose such high discriminatory taxes on the home market. I do not believe that the French would allow such savage discrimination against their home produced wine industry in relation to imported spirits—the reverse is true. It is illogical to penalise the home product in relation to its major competitor. We do not make significant quantities of wine in this country. I have nothing against English wine: I drink it from time to time and some of it is extremely good, and I wish English wine makers well. We will not create a major wine-producing market in this country, but we have a major spirits-producing industry that is under severe pressure.

Ultimately, I want the Government to equalise the tax on alcohol or alcoholic drinks on the basis of the alcohol content. The industry feels strongly about this issue. In the past, it was believed that the high tax on spirits was justified because they were considered to be "strong" drinks, as opposed to wine and beer which were not regarded as such. In fact, it is not the absolute strength of the liquid in the bottle that matters, it is the alcoholic content of what is consumed. For example, most people consume gin in a diluted form, adding tonic or other mixers, so that a gin and tonic is no more alcoholic than a glass of wine: indeed, probably less so than a glass of fortified wine.

A measure of whisky usually has an alcohol content of 40 per cent., but most people drink it with water: indeed, that is the proper way to drink it. In the export market it is drunk with the most extraordinary range of mixers. When I visited one of J'B's distilleries, I discovered the mysteries and wonders of the blend and the secrets that were locked up inside that wonderful spirit. But I was astonished to find out that its biggest single export market is Spain, where it is drunk with a bottle of coke, so there is no possibility of unlocking those delightful flavours and aromas. The important thing, however, is that the Spanish drink it in very large quantities, which brings foreign exchange back home to the United Kingdom economy.

It is logical to tax alcohol on the basis of the alcohol content. The spirits industry is asking for that, because it would put it on an equal footing with the wine industry. The amendment would not achieve that, but it moves in that direction and would have a beneficial effect. The whole economy would benefit from strengthening the home base for Scotch whisky. That would reinvigorate the export drive and protect jobs that have been lost at home, and the Treasury would lose far less directly through spirit duty than it thinks. 10 In those circumstances, I commenced the amendment to the Committee.
