Ms Dawn Primarolo
(Bristol, South)
: Press release 5/96 says that an amendment is required to cover the possibility of the Civil Evidence Act 1968 still being in force by the time the landfill tax is implemented. That is a rather odd explanation for an amendment, and I have a sneaking feeling that it has more to do with padding out the Bill than improving it.

Amendment No. 81 strikes out sub-paragraph (6) (a) on page 166 of the Bill. I would like to know why that sub-paragraph has to be struck out. Amendment No. 82 inserts a new sub-paragraph (7). As far as I can see the only difference is the word "computer", so why is it necessary? Does the Minister know whether the Civil Evidence Act 1968 is likely to be amended by this Parliament? If he does know, why did he not just say so, instead of referring to the possibility of it still being in force? If he does know, when can we expect such an amendment to come before Parliament? If he cannot answer any of those questions, will he explain why he is wasting the Committee's time by tabling amendments that appear to do nothing?

The Paymaster General (Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory)
: The purpose of the amendments is to ensure that the provisions of paragraph 2 of schedule 4 of the Bill take account of the Civil Evidence Act 1995, particularly with regard to the admissibility of computer evidence in civil proceedings in England and Wales. The amendments are necessary to align these provisions with the 1995 Act when it is enacted. Although we cannot take that for granted, it is reasonable to anticipate its enactment. They will allow computer documents to be admissible under the Civil Evidence Act 1968 until the 1995 Act is enacted, so the amendments are required in any eventuality.

Ms Primarolo
: The Minister says that he does not know but he probably thinks so and just in case he has tabled some amendments. He has still not said why it is necessary to strike out sub-paragraph (6) (a) as proposed in amendment No. 81.

Amendments Nos. 34 and 102 draw the attention of members of the Committee to the point about joint and several liability and have been tabled to draw out of me whether a unique power has been sought and JPEG 191 whether it is required in the circumstances. It is not unique, as my hon. Friend the Member for Beasonsfield spotted.

Such a power is used in the betting and gaming field, which has certain similarities with the tax that we are considering. A landfill site provides services, not goods, and in some cases will therefore have little by way of assets as compared with other businesses. If money has to be recovered in a case of insolvency, there may be few assets concerned other than a large hole in the ground with a lot of rubbish in it. The thinking behind the original clause was that it might be necessary to go behind the veil of incorporation and make the directors responsible.

Furthermore, the landfill tax due may represent a high percentage of the total value of a transaction. One can imagine an old landfill site charging only £1 a tonne for inactive waste, in which case the tax of £2 a tonne is 200 per cent. of the original charge. A much higher proportion of the tax could therefore be at risk than is the case with taxes such as VAT. For all those reasons, the Bill was drafted as it is. However, I am happy to accept the suggestions that are put to me and have already indicated my desire to do so.

I cannot accept the Opposition's amendment because it is technically deficient. In addition to proposing the deletion of sub-paragraph (2), it aims to amend sub-paragraph (1) be adding:

"Tax due from any person shall be recovered"

by the Crown, instead of its being "recoverable". That is a significant distinction because had the landfill operator disappeared or had no means to pay the tax, customs would be in breach of the law if its failed to recover the amount due. That is why the legislation uses the word "recoverable". There is a duty elsewhere in the Bill to seek recovery of any amounts due. I cannot accept an amendment that would mean that, in certain cases, customs would be in breach of the law if it failed to recover all the amounts due.

I am, however, happy to accept the amendment tabled by my hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield. It aims to achieve exactly the same thing, but does not suffer from the same defect.

Paragraph 27 imposes a separate interest charge on the following: tax shown on returns but not paid by the due date, running from the due date to the payment date; tax assessed because a return has not been made, running from the due date to payment date; other tax assessed, running from the date of notification of assessment or other dates specified by customs to payment date; and penalties assessed running from the date of notification of the assessment or other date specified by customs to payment date.

The interest rate is a penalty rate equal to the rate in clause 183, a new provision for setting the rate of interest for indirect tax purposes, plus 10 per cent., which is compounded monthly. The interest is not deductible in computing income or profits for direct purposes.

Clause 57
DISPOSAL BY WAY OF LANDFILL

Mr. Tim Smith
: I beg to move amendment No. 53, in page 38, line 21, at end insert

"except that a disposal by way of landfill shall not occur where such cavity is in the ownership of and under the control of the person who has produced the material deposited in such cavity and such disposal represents the best practicable environmental option for the disposal of such material and as a consequence of such disposal the material deposited will not give rise to environmental pollution.".

We are dealing with a special hole called a cavity. The remarkable thing about a cavity is that it is not on the surface, but a long way below the surface and therefore much more environmentally friendly than a hole in the ground. The British salt industry is especially concerned. When salt is produced, as with most mineral production processes, some waste is generated. The materials that comprise the salt purification residues are returned in a closed loop via pipelines to their place of origin in cavities several hundred metres below ground.

No environmental impact arises from that method of disposal. Indeed, the land above the cavities is farmed normally, so no one knows what is going on below ground. In those circumstances, it is JPEG 213 unreasonable to impose the landfill tax. Has my hon. Friend considered that matter?

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory
: Yes, we are considering that, and we have had discussions with the companies involved. In general, a cavity should not be exempt from the principle of the tax. It still has an environmental impact of some sort, although I accept, as my hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield said, that the impact is less than that of the more traditional landfill on the surface. I think that I can be helpful, if not by exempting the concept of a cavity, at least by reconsidering the sort of material that goes into cavities. The material that is pumped back into cavities would be classified as active waste because, although it is not too actively polluting, it has undergone a chemical reaction. Therefore, companies are not putting back into the ground exactly what they took out—minus the salt of course. We are reconsidering that.

It may be possible to recognise the non-polluting nature of the material and put it into a lower category, or there might be a way of examining the water content of the material, which has the effect of increasing its bulk and weight. We must decide whether to tax the solid material or the material in solution. One way or another, although perhaps not in the way the amendment suggests, I believe that we can respond positively to the points made by my hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield.
