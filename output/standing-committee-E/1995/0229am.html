Mr. Barry Legg
(Milton Keynes, South-West)
: I beg to move amendment No. 351, in page 372, line 10, leave out "in accordance with sub-paragraphs (2) to (6) below" and insert—

"(a) in relation to interest payable on an advance made on or after the day on which this Act is passed; and

The Chairman
: With this we may discuss amendment No. 350 in page 372, leave out lines 12 to 42.

Mr. Legg
: Schedule 33 changes the way in which interest payments are dealt with and amends section 349 of the Taxes Act 1988, under which exemptions from the deduction of tax on interest payments can be made gross in certain circumstances.

The amendments seek to cope with a potential difficulty in the schedule. Under the schedule, when interest payments are made on loans that have been transferred from a bank to a non-resident bank or other party that is not liable for corporation tax, such as an individual, the party that pays interest must do so under deduction of tax.

If there is sufficient publicity to make taxpayers aware of the proposals, they may work. I am, however, concerned that they will cause taxpayers problems with loans that have already been assigned by businesses in the United Kingdom that have carried out banking business. United Kingdom banks have frequently assigned loans to other parties, such as individuals, while overseas banks with offices in London have frequently assigned their loans back to their overseas parent company. That is particularly true of Australian state banks in the United Kingdom whose branches here have been closed down and whose loans have returned to their Australian parent state banks.

The Inland Revenue has assured taxpayers who have taken loans from such institutions that they can 572 continue to pay the interest gross without tax deductions. Many taxpayers are in that situation and many will be unaware of the proposals, which seek retrospectively to change the system under which they pay interest on loans that they have taken out and which have been assigned to bodies that are not longer subject to corporation tax.

The amendments would ensure that interest is payable under deduction of tax only if a loan is assigned after the Bill has been given Royal Assent. There would, therefore, be no element of retrospection, and taxpayers who have happily been paying interest gross on loans that were assigned many years ago can continue to do so. The amendment would greatly help the taxpayer.

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Mrs. Angela Knight)
: That is an important point and I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman and to my hon. Friend the Member for Milton Keynes, South-West for raising the matter.

I shall carefully outline the change. As my hon. Friend said, it is tied up with section 349 of the Taxes Act 1988 which allows yearly interest to be paid without deduction of tax when paid by a bank or payable on an advance from a bank. The wording of the section is causing problems; it means that relief is due only for interest payable in the United Kingdom. A Revenue statement of practice and extra statutory concessions have proved necessary to enable British businesses to pay interest gross on some foreign loans, because the effect of the measure depends not on the person to whom the interest is payable but on the one who made the original advance. It allows, sometimes inappropriately, gross payment where interest is paid to persons other than the original bank, which could include even concerns outside the scope of corporation tax. That is why we are revising the appropriate section to remove the requirement that interest be payable in the United Kingdom, but providing that gross payment of interest payable on an advance from a bank is dependent on the recipient being within the chartered corporation tax in respect JPEG 573 of the interests. That removes the current extra statutory concessions and clarifies matters.

The change does not affect banks carrying on business in Britain—their customers continue to pay gross interest—nor does it affect non-bank United Kingdom concerns that have taken over loans from banks. For some time, we have been consulting with the British Bankers Association on that and the various other questions that it raised, yet we are finding it difficult to identify anyone who is adversely affected by the proposed changes. We have seen no reason to continue to allow gross payments to overseas non-banks: such concerns have had a good run for their money.

The Law Society raised some points about the schedule and it was perhaps one of those points that my hon. Friend expressed in his example. [ Interruption.] It seems that it was not, but the principle is the same: where a company in Britain takes out a loan from a branch of an overseas bank and the bank then transfers that loan to another part of the overseas bank—for example, to Australia or back to head office in Germany. In future, interest would have to be paid net, although that does not provide such a problem as has been suggested. Where the United Kingdom has a double tax agreement with the country to which a loan was transferred, the payer in the United Kingdom can seek the Revenue's agreement to continue to make that payment gross. Our treaty network covers most major and minor countries, but does not extend to notorious tax havens such as the Cayman Islands and Panama.

The British Bankers Association does not consider that the amendment will pose any problems for syndicated loans markets and matters related to individuals—it has expressed no concerns whatever. Our proposed regime does not adversely affect individuals or companies that have taken out perfectly legitimate loans, but it catches those who have deliberately taken advantage of a loophole. Therefore, although I accept the concerns expessed by my hon. Friend the Member for Milton Keynes, South-West and by the hon. Member for Oxford, East (Mr. Smith), our investigations over a considerable period of time do not suggest that the adverse effects described by the hon. Gentlemen will result.
