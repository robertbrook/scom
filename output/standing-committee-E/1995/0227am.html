Mr. Darling

I also understand that when a closed company makes a loan, it is required to account for tax as if the loan were a dividend. That is an understandable, anti-avoidance measure. If the loan is repaid, the tax is repaid by the Revenue. At present, when a company makes a loan, tax on it is due 14 days after the end of the accounting period in which the loan is made. I understand the clause to amend that system with the aim of simplifying it and providing that taxes be paid or repaid—because the tax becomes repayable if the loan is repayable—at the end, not of a 14-day, but of a nine-month accounting period. Yet what is to stop a closed company making a series of loans over a period amounting to, say, eight months and 3 weeks and thereby altogether avoiding paying tax? I understand that the de minimis rule must apply at some point, but is it possible for companies effectively not to pay any tax because of the longer accounting period which means that the loans will have been made and repaid within the much longer period?

My second point arises from that. The Law Society has told us that in its view the clause does not make it sufficiently clear that if the loan is repaid within nine months there will be no tax due. If that uncertainty exists, we might be creating a larger loophole. If the measure simplifies the tax, reduces costs and makes the system more effective, that is fine but it would be helpful if the Financial Secretary—I presume that he is the Minister on the Treasury Bench dealing with the issue—told us what problem the clause is intended to remedy.
