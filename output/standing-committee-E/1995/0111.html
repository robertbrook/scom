Mr. Hughes
: Thank you, Mr. Winterton, for your greetings, which I reciprocate on behalf of the Committee. Your wish probably cannot be executed in every sense, although I hope that it can be in as many senses as possible.

78 I hope the Minister will be positive about amendment No. 18 because much of its substance derives from the recommendations behind the Bill. It could have been tabled as three amendments because, although it deals with the general proposition that the annual report of the commissioner should be expanded, three specific propositions follow from that.

The first is that the annual report should contain

"a list of the measures taken by the Commissioner in the previous year to advertise his work".

The second is that the report should contain

"details of the length of time between the commencement of each investigation and the granting of redress, if any".

The third is that the name of the relevant authority, board or trust should be included in the report.

The first proposition would ensure that the commissioner's work was known as widely as possible and that he told people of the extent to which he had achieved that aim. Much of our debate has been about the way in which the commissioner's work can become widely known, understood and used, and, therefore, achieve what Parliament sought to do in the original legislation and seeks to do in the Bill. The hon. Member for Cannock and Burntwood (Dr. Wright) has been the greatest contributor to that debate. One of the problems that we face is that people have traditionally been discouraged from becoming involved with a system that seeks to redress their complaints because they do not understand it. When the Bill is enacted, more remedies will be available and they will apply to wider areas of health service work. It is, therefore, important that people know that such services are available.

The argument in favour of the first proposition is backed by earlier work, and I shall cite pages 9 and 10 of the first report of the Select Committee on the Parliamentary Commissioner for Administration from November 1993. Paragraph 22 makes the case for the proposition, and I hope that it is even more persuasive than I would be if I were not supported by its authority. It states:

"It is vital that the Office of the Ombudsman is publicised effectively if appropriate complaints are going to reach him. We have, however, received evidence claiming that there are still many who are ignorant of his services. Justice believed that the MP filter resulted in a lack of visibility for the Parliamentary Ombudsman and the fragmented nature of the NHS complaints system resulted in a similar problem for the Health Service Ombudsman. The UKCC did not believe that NHS staff knew enough of the Health Service Ombudsman's work. The ACHCEW thought that 'the existing public awareness of the Commissioner and … his work is extremely low and … more could be done to publicise it'".

The next paragraph gives a useful comparative figure. It says:

"We learned that in the Netherlands a survey had been conducted into public awareness of the Ombudsman. 93 per cent. had some knowledge of his work."

I do not know what your view is, Mr. Winterton, but if 93 per cent. of electors in Bermondsey and in Macclesfield were aware of the work of the ombudsman we would be seeking a high honour in the new year's honours list. That figure is incredible, the like of which none of us would expect to achieve. It was achieved just over the water, so it can be done and it would be good if it could be achieved here.

JPEG 79 The argument goes on:

"In the United Kingdom there has been much speculation as to the level of public awareness of the Ombudsman. Too much comment in the past has, however, relied on supposition. There should be continuing research on the work and effectiveness of the Ombudsman system. We recommend … a survey."

The Committee went on to recommend a consumer satisfaction survey, but—this is the most important conclusion—in paragraph 25 it says:

"We would encourage the Ombudsman to continue his efforts to publicise his work both among Members and the public. We see value, for instance, in the publication of a periodic newsletter which will summarise cases of interest in a more popular and easily accessible form than can be the case in the report proper. We were disappointed to note that 54.3 per cent. of Members responding to the Questionnaire"—

I do not know who the 0.3 per cent. are—

" 'hardly ever' or 'never' read Ombudsman reports. … We do not believe that there is a single easy formula which will make the Ombudsman's Office generally known. We do consider that efforts in the past have borne fruit and that there must be a continuous reappraisal of how best to publicise the Ombudsman's services and an exploration of all those ways that might foster increased accessibility."

Let us continue always to find new ways of publicising what the ombudsman does and let us include that aim in the report so that the rest of us—the public and informed commentators—can assess and evaluate its effectiveness.

The National Consumer Council makes the same recommendation. I shall also cite another piece of persuasive evidence, that of the commissioner himself in oral evidence to the PCA Select Committee on 24 November 1993. At column 5 he says:

"there is one area in which I am wholly dissatisfied with my performance, that is, outreach to ethnic minorities. Next year when I have my leaflets reprinted I will have them done in a variety of ethnic minority languages".

He goes on to explain why that defect has occurred. The Chairman of the Committee at that time, the hon. Member for Rugby and Kenilworth (Mr. Pawsey), said:

"May I suggest that you also use some of the flourishing ethnic minority newspapers. Were you to use them you would certainly reach great chunks of the ethnic community."

On the issue of publicity, he suggested that the ombudsman should attempt to contact all race equality councils to give them advice and assistance. The commissioner said in reply:

"I think it is going to be one of the themes of the next world conference of ombudsmen: how to ensure our outreach."

The evidence is clear that there was concern about general and specific areas of information. The amendment would deal with that by requiring reporting back. I hope that the Minister will see the merit of writing that requirement into the Bill instead of leaving the matter to the discretion of the commissioner.

Proposed paragraph (b) of amendment No. 18 deals with the length of time taken in each investigation. That is always a matter of concern to the ombudsman and his staff as justice delayed is often perceived as—and sometimes is—justice denied. I refer the Minister to paragraph 12 of the first Select Committee report, which states: 80

"It is important that any complaint is investigated as speedily as is consistent with fairness and effectiveness. The results of the Questionnaire did not suggest that Members considered the time taken to be a significant problem."

In fact, 18 per cent. were not satisfied and 2 per cent. were very dissatisfied. The paragraph went on:

"The work of the ombudsman has, however, frequently been criticised for the length of time taken to complete investigations and issue reports and this criticism was repeated during the course of our inquiry. That was also the view of the Centre for Ombudsman Studies and the Association of Community Health Councils for England and Wales".

When the report was written, the average investigation period was 12 months and 13 days, and for various reasons that average has risen and fallen since then. Paragraph 13 of the report recommended:

"We consider, however, that investigations remain long and that every effort should be made to reduce still further the time taken, certainly to meet the nine months target"

—the target for completion that applied at that time.

The annual report contains a meticulous record of the events and facts of the investigation. However, it does not contain a record of the stages and timetable of the complaints process. That error could be rectified, although the amendment would not enforce a specific timetable for an inquiry. The theory is that charter-type targets, rather than a statutory requirement, would apply as they do in other aspects of public life.

I think that it is true to say that no investigatory processes in the legal, court or tribunal systems are time limited in the sense that they have to end by a prescribed date. Of course, the periods in which many such processes must be begun are often prescribed—for example, the Minister will be aware that in certain cases criminal authorities must take action within a certain number of months, and industrial tribunal applications must be made within a certain time. However, I do not think that an end-point is ever specified in those processes and the amendment does not propose a maximum time for an investigation—that must be left open in each case. However, a statutory requirement to publish information about the time delay that can be examined by the Committee and the House is the best lever that is available.

My final point is simple and uncontroversial. In practice, the name of the relevant authority—trust, board and so on—is now included in the report. That has not always happened and there is no requirement that it should. The amendment would insert such a requirement into the Bill.

I hope that the Minister will accept all the provisions in the amendment. I should be content if he were to say that he would prefer his officials to draft the amendment, making the same points but in different words. In any event, I hope that he will accept all our recommendations.

Mr. Barron
: I would defend your position as a member of the Chairmen's Panel to the last day of the year, Mr. Winterton.

I support the arguments of the hon. Member for Southwark and Bermondsey (Mr. Hughes) on amendment No. 18. Public perception of the new procedure will be vital.

Amendment No. 19 would require the commissioner to monitor and evaluate the new NHS complaints procedure as it relates to his increased work load for a fixed period following the Bill's enactment. It would also require the commissioner to involve all interested parties in the monitoring process and to make available to the House reports of his findings for each of the fixed periods in question.

The Minister said on Second Reading, rightly, that the Bill would bring about significant changes to the role of the health service commissioner; indeed, he described the changes as "radical", which is right. The new procedures are, however, as yet untested. It would therefore be sensible to monitor and evaluate the operation of the new procedures, thereby assessing the outcome for patients of the new system during the early years of its operation. Amendment No. 19 would require such evaluation and monitoring to take place over the first three years. That period would be sufficient for any likely problems and possible failings in the new procedures to be confronted and addressed by the commissioner and, if necessary, by the Government too.

We believe that the monitoring and evaluation process is not merely an internal one for the commissioner and his staff. I realise that he has independence, and rightly so, but evaluation should not be limited to the commissioner. Instead, it should be spread throughout users of the complaints system and should encompass interested and involved organisations outside the national health service.

The amendment would require three simple forms of monitoring. First, a complaints satisfaction survey would be conducted. A minimum of one survey in the first three years would assess what users of the new system think of it and of the service provided by the commissioner. That is vital; at the end of the day, the Bill and stages 1 and 2 are about giving users access to the correct complaints procedure, one which will give them satisfaction. A survey of users' views would be a good thing. After all, the commissioner is the ultimate authority for complainants using the system and it is therefore right that he should ensure that his work is responsive to customers' needs. He may even find that the satisfaction survey is so helpful to his work that he wants to conduct further surveys. There is certainly scope for that.

Secondly, the amendment would require a minimum of one meeting to be held in each of the first three years for relevant interests to discuss and evaluate developments and emerging issues as they affect service users and the commissioner. We envisage that such relevant interests would include patients organisations, the various professional bodies, the commissioner's advisers and consultants as well as national health service managers, health service bodies and family health service providers. The form of such meetings will be left to the commissioner's discretion. Perhaps different groups would meet separately with the commissioner in various circumstances or perhaps an annual meeting of users would take place. The aim is primarily to 82 gain the confidence of organisations that represent our constituents and are independent from the service providers in the health system.

Thirdly and finally, amendment No. 19 requires that a report of annual meetings he published along with the findings of the complainant survey and any resulting recommendations for amending the NHS complaints system.

Clause 10 already requires the commissioner to place annual reports and special reports before Parliament—I expect such reports as we propose in paragraph (c) of the amendment to be included in the special reports category and presented to the House. The amendment arose as a result of discussions with the National Consumer Council, which provides a vigorous and independent voice for consumers of all types of public service. The council agreed with us that the proposed new service is so different from its predecessor that certain checks and balances should be included for the first few years. The monitoring and evaluation provided by the amendment should add relatively little to the commissioner's costs or work load—certainly not sufficient to require an extra allocation of Government resources—but a small extra input by the commissioner has the potential to yield large additional dividends for the complainant system and for the NHS as a whole.

If the commissioner is to be his most efficient and to best serve the complainants who use his services, he needs to be able to assess how he makes decisions. Equally, if he is to hold and retain the confidence of health service bodies and family health service providers, he needs to know what they think of his work. The provision of complainant surveys will give the commissioner a much better view of how his service is received, while meetings with interested parties will allow him to hear from people and organisations with experience both of the older procedures and of a selection of cases with which the commissioner will have dealt under the new system.

Our proposals represent extra quality controls and are intended as guides for the commissioner. The provision of reports to Parliament is intended to maintain the commissioner's accountability to the House. I echo the hon. Member for Southwark and Bermondsey in asking the Minister whether, if the wording of the amendments is not to his liking, he will consider redrafting them, maintaining a quality control and a mechanism to gain the confidence of health service users—something that we all want—who might, from time to time, want to complain about how they have been treated in the system. I hope that the Minister will take that into account when he considers whether to accept amendments Nos. 18 and 19.

In the face of professional opposition, he has latterly made it his policy to name health authorities, which has been important. A great merit of the system is that it disciplines the providers. The naming of trusts that have been maladministrated enables others in the system—at national and local levels through providers and JPEG 83 purchases—to engage in conversations about maladministration. It is appropriate for us to give statutory form to good practice as it has evolved. We should expect routine self-evaluations of the effectiveness of the machinery. That is entirely consistent with the citizens charter enterprise—monitoring and evaluation by organisations of the effectiveness of their complaints procedures. Vigorous attempts should be made to advertise and publicise and reports should be widely considered.

On grounds of delicacy, I was not going to mention the point to which the hon. Member for Southwark and Bermondsey referred—on how well known is the ombudsman's work among Members of Parliament. Findings were dire. Asked if they were aware of these reports—after all, they are reports made to Parliament by Parliament's officer—Members produced a dismal response. Surely there are obligations on all sides here.

The hon. Member for Southwark and Bermondsey also mentioned the Dutch ombudsman. I vividly remember hearing the Dutch ombudsman talk about his job and his relationship to the Dutch Parliament. It was clear that the annual report of the Dutch ombudsman to the Dutch Parliament was the major moment for making judgments about the administrative record of Government Departments. An important debate takes place every year and the ombudsman's report is regarded as the key document. Here, Parliament does not insist on having an annual debate on the ombudsman's work, yet this institution set up the ombudsman to investigate maladministration in Government Departments. That shows, in microcosm how far we have to go in getting a grip on all these problems.

I support the amendments, which reflect good practice. There are difficulties, however, some of which have not yet been explored. If we ask the ombudsman to give a global account in aggregate terms of how long each investigation should take, we may encounter difficulties under the new system because there will be more than one sort of investigation. The ombudsman will be asked to do a number of things. For example, he will be asked to hear appeals from people who believe that they have been denied an independent review. Are we to ask the ombudsman to account for how long it takes him to decide whether there should be an independent review? That is a rather difficult enterprise. How much background will be required before the ombudsman can make that decision? If the ombudsman says that there should be an independent review, is it a recommendation, or is it obligatory for the provider to undertake such a review? If the internal procedure is said to be exhausted, what then happens if someone goes to the ombudsman to ask for a full investigation?

How much work on the part of the ombudsman is necessary for him to make a proper decision as to whether he should undertake a further investigation? That is a daunting prospect. How much of a previous investigation 84 has to be investigated before one can properly decide whether a further investigation should take place? Should the time taken to make that decision be counted as part of the ombudsman's time? So, there are real difficulties here, but it is nevertheless correct for us to try to establish how long each case will take.

In the spirit of building on existing good practice and taking the ombudsman system into the future, I hope that the Government will accept the amendments.

: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The new clause is just as good as all those to which we just agreed—the Government simply did not think of it. The new clause provides not a separate regional ombudsman system, but regional officers. Like all the arguments put by Opposition Members, the proposal is supported by the evidence given to the PCA Select Committee before we reached the proceedings of this Committee.

The new clause makes our proposal clear. It says:

"The Health Service Commissioner for England shall include among his officers officers who shall be based in each of the standard economic regions of England"—

I have taken the Government's definition—

"and who shall have responsibility for the investigation of complaints arising in each such region."

I use the argument put by the commissioner—I draw attention to the evidence he gave on 26 May 1994 at page 146 and on 27 June 1994 at pages 148–9, which is brief and in which the argument is clearly put. He said:

"If the Report's proposal that I should be able to investigate the actions of GPs, dentists and so on … were to be accepted ultimately by the Government"—

as it has been—

"that major extension of jurisdiction would have a significant effect on the staffing and organisation of the office. Some regional presence would seem essential if that were to happen; and the continuation of the HSC'S personal jurisdiction would be called in question."

I repeat:

"Some regional presence would seem essential".

It is not desirable, not on balance a good idea, not probably a good idea, but essential.

The Minister made it clear that the commissioner is fully aware that he must reorganise to cope with the work load and he wants to do that well. Members of the 90 Committee have every confidence that he will do as well as he can given the restrictions and the resources available.

The concluding paragraph of the appendices to the minutes of evidence, paragraph 14 states:

"It is axiomatic that widening of powers would affect the extent to which HSC could determine or even involve himself in every investigation."

I spent a relatively short, but formative period of my life working for the European Commission of Human Rights in Strasbourg. The reality is that the commission does not do all the work: the minor officials, such as I, did most of the work; the commission had a supervisory role to ensure that we did our jobs properly. That is how this system works: the commissioner has a supervisory role, but most of the work is done by officers. It is worth recording what paragraph 14 makes clear:

"It is axiomatic that widening of powers would affect the extent to which HSC could determine or even involve himself in every investigation. He would need to set down criteria to guide those who would be responsible for screening complaints. Given the steady increase in complaints already within existing jurisdiction and probable responsibility from early 1995 for dealing with unresolved complaints about disclosure of information by NHS authorities, the inclusion within the remit of clinical complaints and the administrative actions of GPs and other family practitioners would make inescapable a marked increase in staff and accommodation—and probably the creation of regional offices."

There was a discussion about that between the Minister and the hon. Member for Cannock and Burntwood. The Government have accepted the value of such an administrative check on the administration of the NHS. They have set up integrated regional offices—that has nothing to do with regional government or with changing the powers of Parliament, which is another debate for another place and another time and was alluded to in the Chamber yesterday. The new clause is about including in the Bill the structure that the Government use for the standard economic regions and applying it to the ombudsman.

People have more confidence in a local investigator. That is one of the reasons why people have resisted a national police force: they prefer to have a West Mercia police force or a Northumberland police force or whatever. The ombudsman should have a local presence: a local office with local officers who know Leeds, Bradford, Newcastle, Bristol, Plymouth or whatever and who know the score. They would know the trusts and would deal with them day to day. That would give people confidence in the system and would be in line with what the commissioner wants. I hope that the Minister will accept the proposition and that the new clause is the ideal vehicle for it.
