Clause 73
MEANING OF "LOAN RELATIONSHIP" ETC

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Mike O'Brien
: Several issues arise from the clause. I shall deal first with a point made by the Law Society. Various Conservative Members have declared an interest, so I must state that I am a member of the Law Society—at least I pay money to it, although I am unsure what I get out of it. In its submission on clause 73, the Law Society says: JPEG 611

"We find this a very difficult clause to interpret. This is a matter of concern, because it is the foundation of the new regime and the advent of self-assessment makes it particularly important that companies should be able to determine conclusively which of the money debts with which they are concerned give rise to loan relationships."

I find the clause difficult to interpret. The clause is meant to clarify and avoid doubt but my concern is that it creates doubt. Indeed, the Chartered Institute of Taxation was so concerned about it that it recommended its deletion to avoid confusion. That is not good basis on which to proceed. It seems to me—our briefing yesterday with civil servants touched on this—that clause 73 is modified to some extent by clause 92. Will the Minister clarify whether that is true because there does not appear to be any cross-referencing? I believe that there is a need to cross-reference but it might be possible to accommodate that simply by inserting after the first line of the clause "and section 92". However, I understand that that might not be necessary, so I hope that the Minister will clarify the issue.

Clause 73(3) is especially difficult to interpret. It applies,

"where an instrument is issued by any person for the purpose of representing security for …"

What is an instrument? I understand that the Stamp Act 1891 suggests that an instrument is any document. Furthermore, what does "issued" mean? Would a letter confirming a trade debt come within the legislation? Would a promissory note or an IOU count? Would an instrument representing security be one created by novation of an existing loan, or, as the Institute of Chartered Accountants asks, would it be a debt created in relation to the vesting of assets on privatisation? In particular, the use of the word "security" raises the question of whether this is intended to introduce the familiar concept of the debt on a security, or the much wider meaning of the phrase which has been favoured by the Revenue in the context of the phrase "deep gain security", or some new shade of meaning altogether. The Institute of Chartered Accountants is suggesting that there is a lack of clarity. On the grounds that ambiguity exists, and in the light of Pepper v. Hart, the Minister should clarify that point.

The Minister will say that the objective is clarity, but, as I have pointed out, various organisations doubt that. In its submission, the Law Society states that:

"The new regime is presumably intended to apply to loan notes issued on a takeover on the basis that the notes are instruments issued by the acquiring company for the purpose of representing the rights of the loan note holder. But what is the intended position if certificates are not issued? There will have been no lending of money, merely unpaid purchase money remaining outstanding. Although the holders' names are entered in the register of loan note holders, is the register thought to be an 'instrument issued' by the acquiring company? Even if certificates are issued, they will only be prima facie evidence of the loan note holders' entitlements, and it is arguable that they do not 'represent' anything."

I hope that the Minister will provide some clarification on that point.

It is also unclear whether "debt" includes a contingency debt. There is a body of case law in 612 respect of contingency debt that seems to suggest that it is included under the general term, "debt". Is that so? In the light of Pepper v. Hart, it might be useful to clarify that ambiguity. Does that affect in any way payments by guarantors under guarantees? Will the Minister clarify that point?

A note from the Law Society asks:

"Is it intended that a debt will be taken to arise from a transaction for the lending of money if the debtor is not a 'borrower' only if he issues some kind of instrument which 'represents' the debt, such as a bill of exchange or a promissory note?"

Under 73(3) reference is made to

"a transaction for the lending of money".

That phrase means a loan, does it not? The hon. Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Smith) fights for the cause of simplicity—for which quest he will, no doubt, go down in parliamentary history. Should we not pay heed to the need for simplicity? If "loan" is what we mean, we should use the word, "loan". Is there some reason why we should not? As far as I am aware, the lengthy phrase used is not one of art or of law, and it adds nothing to the precision of the Bill. Perhaps some alteration would be useful.

A further issue is what "money" means in this context. Few transactions involve coins or notes, so what is included in the definition of money? Again, Pepper v. Hart means that the Minister should use this opportunity to offer clarification.

The Institute of Chartered Accountants has given us some draft comments on amendments. The institute says in respect of clause 73(3):

"Probably the most important issue still outstanding is the uncertainty in the definition of 'loan relationships' … Nothing has yet been done to clarify whether ordinary commercial documentation, such as contracts and invoices, would amount to `an instrument … representing security' for the purposes of clause 73(3). We understand that such documentation is not in fact intended to be included, and that the Inland Revenue regard the phrase in question as broadly equivalent to 'a security' as defined in section 132(3)(b) TCGA 1992. That being so, we recommend that the latter definition should be expressly applied for the purposes of clause 73, so that the existing body of case law dealing with the phrase 'a debt on a security' can be used as an aid to interpretation."

Will the Minister deal with that point?

4.45 pm

Clause 73(5) deserves to be quoted in its entirety, before the hon. Member for Beaconsfield turns his attention to it, because it is amazing. It stated:

"For the purposes of this Chapter—

  1. references to payments or interest under a loan relationship are references to payments or interest made or payable in pursuance of any of the rights or liabilities under that relationship; and

Surely there must be a simpler way of putting that? In view of the resolution of the hon. Member for JPEG 613 Beaconsfield last year, can we not simplify it? If not, why not? What does it say about rights and liabilities that have to be dealt with in such a prolix way?

Mr. O'Brien

Mrs. Angela Knight
: The hon. Gentleman has not simplified but complicated the matter. I am aware that nit-pickers and hair-splitters have been raising some of those points, but they and the Committee need to consider the basic issue. We have taken full legal advice. I assure the hon. Gentleman that if we focus more narrowly and go through list after list, someone will find a way around the clauses that simplify corporate debt.

There are two essential ingredients in a loan relationship. First, is there a debt? Secondly, did it arise from lending money? If someone is paying interest on a debt, there is a loan relationship.

I shall try to address some of the hon. Gentleman's points. Clause 73 is not modified by clause 92 because clause 73 deals with loans for money whereas clause 92 deals with interest on things which are not loans for money. That same definition is used when considering the general list of every conceivable type of certificate, including those named by the hon. Gentleman. It does not extend to invoices or payments under guarantees, but it does extend to tradeable loan notes, for example when taken in exchange for a takeover bid. Whether promissory notes are covered by the new rules depends on whether there is a loan of money. If there is, the promissory note is covered; if there is not, then it is not. The answer to the question about certification is the same. Again, on contingency debt, one uses the same criteria.

Mr. Mike O'Brien
: On a contingency debt, how can one look at the same criteria? If there is a contingency, there is a contingency as to whether it arises.

Mrs. Knight
: It depends on the terms of the debt. If there is no certainty that any amount will be payable, then the answer is no.

The real issue raised by the hon. Gentleman and others is not that the definition of loan relationship is uncertain, but that it is unfamiliar. Because it is an unfamiliar term, people are not used to working with it. But like any unfamiliar term, as soon as one gets used to the idea of what the term is, it becomes as familiar as the old expression. If I may pray in aid the first time that I served on a Committee when I came to the House of Commons, other new Members and I 614 were amazed when the Chairman asked the Committee after half an hour or so to vote on something called "clause stand part". None of us knew what he was talking about. In the afternoon, a new Chairman came along and in his beautifully modulated tones, Mr. Hogg—he was a Labour Chairman—he informed us that we once again had to vote on "clause stand part". After another half an hour, during which we scratched our heads, we suddenly realised that we were voting that the clause stand part of the Bill. Now the phrase "clause stand part" is as familiar as any other.

The same is true of loan relationships. The phrase may be unfamiliar at present, but it will soon be as familiar in accounting and financial circles as clause stand part debates are to those of us who serve on Committee.

Mrs. Angela Knight
: Perhaps I may begin by discussing the generality of the distinction between the relationships outlined by the hon. Gentleman, and then move to the specifics. Loan relationships are divided into two broad categories—those for trade purposes and those for non-trade purposes. Where debts are held or owed for trade purposes, income and expenses from them are just included with all the other income and expenses of the trade in computing the overall profit or loss. If there is an overall profit, it is taxable, and if there is an overall loss it is relievable under the normal rules for trading profits and losses. One consequence is that that eliminates a longstanding discrimination between different sorts of borrowing for trade purposes, particularly in the rules determining the relief available when the result is an overall loss. In future, all trade borrowing will qualify for the favourable rules currently reserved for 616 borrowing from banks. We discussed that point this morning in a stand part debate on an earlier clause.

I have a personal ersatz example that helps me to distinguish a trading loan from a non-trading loan. A trading loan is made when a company borrows money to buy the lathe to make the widgets. A non-trading loan is made when a parent company—a holding company with a number of trading subsidiaries—borrows the money.

In practice, the difference between trading and non-trading loans is well known. The same distinction exists in many other places, including the legislation covering FOREX and financial instruments. It has not caused any problems there. That established definition runs through our tax law and is understood by financial professionals, both practitioners and participants, so is there a problem? When a known definition and a known distinction are used in a new context, it occurs to me that there is no problem of definition.

The hon. Member for North Warwickshire (Mr. O'Brien) made some specific points. I refer him to this morning's debate, when I laid out the three basic rules underpinning the clause. one is that the figures should follow those produced for non-tax purposes whenever that is acceptable. No prescriptive rules exist to say what tax treatment should apply, as tax treatment will follow the accounts. That is the beauty of the clause—it provides the flexibility that companies have sought.

Mr. Mike O'Brien
: I cited three examples of what the Minister suggests is an easy matter in respect of which there are many precedents. I did not fully understand her reply. The Minister may consider that other people should share her familiarity with the matter, but the Institute of Chartered Accountants—which will have to interpret the provisions—clearly does not. The Insitute asked what would happen when a company borrowed to pay a dividend; to acquire its original trade or an additional trade; or to acquire a subsidiary or a new trading subsidiary whose trade immediately hives up. How does the clause apply in each of those cases?

Mrs. Knight
: I thought that I had covered those points. No prescriptive rules are laid down—the tax treatment follows the accounts. That is, tax treatment on the specific aspects follows the accounting practices of the companies in question, and those accounting practices have to be within the two broad rules of accruals or mark to market. We shall come to those later. There are, of course, variations to cater for specific situations within those two broad principles and there are other matters to deal with for certain companies.

Those principles cover the issue as well as the specific points raised by the hon. Member for North Warwickshire: whether one treats a specific type of loan in a specific way depends on the accounting treatment that is used for a specific company. Clause 76
DEBITS AND CREDITS BROUGHT INTO ACCOUNT

Mrs. Angela Knight
: I beg move Government amendment No. 273, in page 48, line 26, after "method" insert "and when taken together".

The Chairman
: With this we may discuss Government amendments Nos. 274 to 276 and 175 and 277.

Mrs. Knight
: This is the first of a series of groups of amendments that deal with capitalised interest, bad and doubtful debts, aborted loan expenses, late interest payments, provision relating to insurance companies and anti-avoidance rules.

The amendments fall into two categories. The substantive amendment is amendment No. 277, of which the others—with the exception of amendment No. 175, which is a refinement that was proposed by the Law Society of England and Wales—are by-products. I shall therefore speak to amendment No. 277.

The amendment deals with capitalised interest. The reform is designed to ensure that borrowers receive relief for all their borrowing costs. We can generally achieve that by following the treatment in the company's own account. The Bill recognises that problems can arise when borrowing costs are capitalised into the cost of a fixed capital asset, which is not an uncommon practice. For accounting purposes, that capitalised cost would, in due course, be recognised in computing profits as the capital cost of the asset is depreciated. The deductions for depreciation are specifically not allowable for tax purposes. Without the amendments, there would be a risk that the borrowing costs would never be allowed at all once they were capitalised. That is a specialised but serious concern. It is particularly important for major capital projects, such as North sea oil production facilities.

The current provision allows tax relief in any period for the funding costs and incidental expenses that are capitalised into the accounts for that period, subject to the general rules about which accounting methods are acceptable and how they should be applied. Put another way, the interest is put into the accounts on an accrued basis and can therefore be offset against tax liability.

Mrs. Knight

We were first asked about allowing 620 non-interest funding such as debt issued below par. It was also suggested that relief for incidental expenses would not be allowed.
