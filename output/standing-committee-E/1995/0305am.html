Now is probably the best time to return to a matter of particular concern—project finance and the way in which it applies to the various accounting methods. The Government have set out in clause 77 two principal accounting methods, which members of the Committee will no doubt be familiar with: the mark-to-market basis and the accruals basis. We have had representations about the difficulties that the provision may cause for capital project finance in developing countries.

A British company that wants to become involved in a capital investment in a developing country or in eastern Europe also wants to ensure that it is protected. It is therefore likely to undertake its involvement in that country in such a way as to protect itself. That is normally by way of a loan investment, which means that the return on the investment—the interest on it—may not be payable for several years. It seems that the way in which the clause and other relevant provisions operate with respect to the two accounting methods will cause problems. They have been outlined in correspondence from Ernst and Young, which all members of the Committee have no doubt been sent.

The return comes after several years, but it appears that the taxation could come early—where it is calculated on an accruals basis, it could come almost straight away and the company could end up paying taxes before it has received any return. Ernst and Young has set out some examples of how that could arise, which raise several issues about the way in which the two accounting methods are being imposed.

I have already said—I want to make it clear—that there is great merit in having only two methods. However, there is no great advantage if we do not also allow a number of exceptions when it is valid to do so. The measure addresses circumstances that a Government would want to encourage; all parties would say that there is great merit in encouraging the development of third-world markets as well as investment in them, deriving a return from them and building trading partners not only in eastern Europe but in the third world, too. We want to encourage that and do not, therefore, want to undermine the basis on which companies undertake such loan relationships.

Companies undertake those loan relationships for a reason. We cannot just say, as I suspect the Minister might, that we do not want to subject the whole basis 640 of our legislation on loan relationships to accommodating capital project developments in other countries because that would undermine the basis of the taxation system that we are trying to create.

If that is the answer, we must behave with a great deal of care because there are stronger arguments for such developments than for other projects which companies undertake without good reason. Here, no doubt, we all think that there is a good reason for carrying out such investment and that we should not try to undermine it.

The reasons for using a loan investment are clear: to operate through a loan investment type of relationship gives investors a degree of priority over the cash flow that might come from the company in which they invested. Many countries have, if not a record of instability, the possibility of instability. Difficulties have arisen in Russia and in other countries where there have been levels of instability almost unprecendented in the past 50 years. In such circumstances, those who want to invest in eastern Europe countries in particular might want to use a loan investment, which might be undermined by the accruals basis of calculation of the taxation.

A further problem is that inadequate company laws might exist in the country where the British business tries to invest. For example, as Ernst and Young make clear, it was not possible to redeem share capital or to issue preference shares in Russia until relatively recently. In those circumstances, loan relationships might represent a way to protect the capital of the company investing from the United Kingdom. Such countries also often have currencies which are subject to fluctuation and that might be quite damaging to any investment undertaken by a British company. Again, that is a good reason why a company might want to undertake the investment through a loan relationship.

There is a whole series of arguments for saying that loan relationships are a good means to undertake invesment in an eastern European country or a developing country. There are strong arguments in favour of such investment and it should not, therefore, be undermined. At the moment, it is not being undermined. Schedule D, cases 3 and 4 show that taxation is based on the full amount of the income arising in any accounting period. So, if interest is received from an investment, tax will be paid on it—that is all fair and good. If no interest is received, no tax needs to be paid. However, clause 77 and the next couple of clauses appear to impose a straitjacket on the ability to carry out such investment by insisting that it be assessed either on an accruals basis or a mark-to-market basis.

A mark-to-market basis seems, on the face of it, to accommodate the difficulties, but there are restrictions in the Bill on how a mark-to-market basis can be used—perhaps the Minister could address that. The JPEG 641 only circumstances in which it seems to be allowed is where the business previously compiled its accounts on a mark-to-market basis or is engaged in a business that normally would do so. But capital investment projects in eastern European countries are often one-off by nature, put together by groups of investors who set up a company for the purpose. They therefore would not seem to be able to use the most appropriate system, the mark-to-market basis, for assessing tax.

I am in doubt about the Government's intention. Do the Government mean that any business may make a clear choice between the accruals basis or the mark-to-market basis, or do they mean that the mark-to-market basis can only be used if a company has a record of using it? That would restrict companies' ability to use that system, and the accruals basis could have a detrimental impact on an investment project.
