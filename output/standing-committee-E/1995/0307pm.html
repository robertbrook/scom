New Clause 2
CUSTOMS AND EXCISE TAX SIMPLIFICATION

"(1) The Commissioners of Customs and Excise shall prepare and present to Treasury Ministers a report on tax simplification.

  1. The report shall be laid before Parliament and published before 26 November 1996.
  2. The report shall give—
    1. an account of recent Customs and Excise tax legislation history;
    2. full details of recent annual additions to both primary and secondary Customs and Excise legislation;
    3. a summary of recent criticism of the complexity of Customs and Excise tax legislation including its effects on business and of parliamentary procedure;
    4. the advantages and disadvantages of possible solutions including a Royal Commission on taxation and a tax law commission.".—[ Ms Primarolo]

(Bristol, South)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

An amendment tabled last year by the hon. Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Smith) was passed as a result of which a report was published. It is an excellent, interesting report although it did not take us any further forward. I had assumed—as many people had—that Customs and Excise would be included in the review. Tax legislation has grown enormously over the past 25 years, which is acknowledged in the summary and in the background paper. We have constantly to strike a balance between simplifying the tax system without undermining the integrity of the tax base. Simplification is not always possible and there are times when tax legislation is bound to be complex, but it is in the interests of all those who follow these debates, regardless of their political affiliation, that we try to make the amendment of current legislation or the introduction of new systems such as self-assessment—which we have debated at length—as simple as possible.

The interim report on tax legislation from the tax law review committee said:

"We believe something more fundamental is wrong with our tax system than the rate at which it is being produced. The style in which it is written has been likened to a puzzle—and one which does not have a picture on the box! It is impenetrable; you cannot see even the broad outline of the picture until you have solved the puzzle. Worse still, there are times when the legislation is wholly incomprehensible. This is unacceptable."

 At times in our debate, hon. Members on both sides of the Committee have struggled with the complexity of some proposals. With the development of taxation legislation within Customs and Excise, there has been a 758 propensity to move to the use of statutory instruments: for instance, in the period from 1992 to the current year, 136 statutory instruments covering tax within Customs and Excise have been issued. We have considerably less ability to scrutinise and amend that legislation. Guidelines and further statutory instruments will be issued, on the landfill tax proposals, covering crucial aspects of the operation of the tax, with little time for Parliament to scrutinise them. The Budget changes made by statutory instrument already in the pipeline as a result of the Bill cover many areas still to be debated.

A number of reports have been produced on tax simplification, the principle of simplification as an objective and the balancing of simplification with the principles of tax collection. We must not encourage avoidance strategies. In the global market, simplification has to be balanced against international considerations. It is not always possible to achieve the straight objective of simplification.

The Public Accounts Select Committee, in its 41st report during the 1993–94 Session, considered the cost to business of complying with the requirements of value added tax, the complexity of that tax and the burdens placed on small businesses. The report of the Comptroller and Auditor General in March 1994 entitled "HM Customs and Excise: Cost to Business of Complying with VAT Requirements" took that debate a little further. The summary of its conclusions referred to points that should be considered when examining tax legislation, such as simplification, ease of reference and plain language drafting of current and future legislation.

The Comptroller and Auditor General's report says that we should safeguard the tax base and should avoid the additional risks to revenue collection from avoidance and evasion. It also says that as VAT is a self-assessed tax, it inevitably involves traders in extra costs. It makes various recommendations about the support that should be given to traders. It refers to balancing complexity with fairness and equity and says that it is not always possible to achieve the goal of simplifying the tax system.

The Comptroller and Auditor General acknowledged that there have been some

"notable achievements in working to simplify initial proposals"—

 on VAT legislation—

"tabled in Brussels for regulations to implement the Single European Market."

 He said that Customs and Excise had done a great deal in those negotiations to further the objective of simplification.

"key elements of any programme to ensure that traders' compliance costs are kept to a minimum"

 and to ensure that the tax base is protected. The report recommends a regular

"review of the regulatory framework, identifying the elements of primary law on which each regulation is based and possible areas of flexibility consistent with European Community law".

 It says that there needs to be

"an analysis of where the main compliance costs on traders arise and which elements of traders' costs arise from which aspects of the regulations".

It will take several years, but we have launched into the process with determination.

I would also mention that for any major changes to the statute or to new taxes, we always publish a compliance cost assessment. That in itself ensures that we are at least aware of the burden that we are placing on the business sector or on individuals through the tax system.

I am sure that the Government will have had time to deliberate on the contents of the clause since I first moved it last year, as have I.

I have to concede that there has not been a great deal of vocal agitation for the clause. There have not been 764 demonstrations in Parliament square, nor are our mailbags weighed down by representations from our constituents, but I make no apology for raising the matter; it is often sensible for us to be anticipatory rather than reactive in the changes that we make to the Taxes Act 1988. A few years ago, I proposed some changes to section 79 of a tax Act relating to the taxation of fund managers with overseas portfolios and to the tax treatment of certain derivative products. Again, the anomalies in the Act had not caused many practical problems, but I am convinced that had those changes not be made by the Government, people would have suffered from problems. Moreover, it is right for us to remove anomalies and unfairnesses, which may have unfortunate human and economic consequences, even if the numbers of people affected are relatively modest. In some ways so it is easier to do the right thing if the cost of doing so is modest, especially when the Government are rightly concerned not to lose any source of revenue and when, therefore, the bias will naturally be against accepting any changes that will have significant revenue costs.

The effect of the new clause can be described in a few short sentences. It would clarify, simplify, make more fair and rationalise—but not abolish—the two thirds rule applied by the Inland Revenue to ensure that pension provisions that qualify for the various tax inducements should not deliver pensions that are worth more than two thirds of the pensioners' salary. At present, that is defined as a spinal salary; the taxpayer may choose between taking one of any of the five years of salary prior to retirement, or an average of three out of the past 10 years.

If the taxpayer or pensioner had a higher income before that 10-year period—in other words, more than 10 years before retirement—that higher income is disregarded in relation to pensions. As I am sure that my hon Friend the Financial Secretary will remind the Committee, it is always open to employers to provide additional benefit by way of an unauthorised scheme, but such additional benefits will not qualify under existing tax inducements to ensure that employers and employees can provide for a reasonable pension; the cost to employers will be considerably greater, and they will not be able to offset the cost of such contributions as a business expense against income. That would be unfair and undesirable.

Last year, I illustrated the unfairness of the present regime by taking a fanciful example. I said that the chief executive of British Gas might decide to retire at the age of 50 with a substantial pension, which would be a six-figure sum, because it related to his final salary of many hundreds of thousands of pounds a year. He might then take a job for 20 years as a teacher, on £20,000 a year. If one works for 20 years as a teacher, one would normally qualify for a pension of about half the final salary—£10,000—so the former chief executive would find himself limited by the application of the two thirds rule and of the one sixtieth rule to a pension of £7,000, and would lose £3,000. That would hardly cause hardship in that case, but it would be anomalous, unfair and unreasonable.

In thinking further about the matter, I realised that I could take an example much closer to home. I am sure JPEG 765 that my hon. Friend the Financial Secretary will be, before long, a senior Cabinet Minister—he might be even more senior to that. If, after he had progressed through a distinguished career in the Government, he decided to return to the Back Benches for more than 10 years, he would find that the rule, which I am trying to modify to make it slightly more just and rational, would operate against his interests. The two-thirds rule would apply to his salary as a Back Bencher, not to the higher salary that he might have earned previously that would be unreasonable and unfair.
