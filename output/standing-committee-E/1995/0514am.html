Ms Jean Corston (Bristol, East): In drawing to a close my remarks on the amendments, I wish to focus a few issues. Much suspicion has been rightly felt by people who are critical of the Child Support Agency and the way in which it has dealt with child support. I refer to couples. one of whom is claiming welfare benefit, principally income support. Ministers have made it clear that when no one is receiving income support or drawing welfare benefit, couples are free to make their own arrangements about child support. Indeed, when that issue was raised at a recent all-party group, to which I referred at our last sitting, the Minister talked about savings to the taxpayer. No reference was made to family support and the eradication of child poverty.

My amendments, if properly incorporated into the Bill, would make it clear that adequate levels of child support are necessary for all families, not only those who are considered to be a drain on the taxpayer. Far too much emphasis has been placed on targeting women who are on income support and trying to bring up their children, rather than ensuring that all children of families going through the divorce process are properly supported.

I am grateful to the National Association of Family Mediation and Conciliation Services for making available some information about the impact of the CSA on the association's professional role as family mediators. It has made it clear that when couples use mediation to sort out such matters, mediators ensure that they are made fully aware of the actual or likely CSA calculations. They are told about the impact of the formula on all their arrangements and decisions.

Particular attention is drawn to the situation that will apply if either party is already on income support or may have to claim it in the future as a side effect, or consequence, of the divorce. The couple are told about the way in which the CSA operates. No calculations are made until an accurate figure is obtained by them from the CSA—although I hesitate to question what an accurate figure from the CSA might be, given that two thirds of its calculations are wrong. The possible effect of the operation of the agency upon the couple is always explored and a ballpark figure is put to them. There are, of course, consequences for second families.

National Family Mediation works within the Child Support Agency formula. Mediators help clients to understand the implications of the CSA and assist them to arrive at the so-called ballpark figure. The temporary figure becomes a benchmark for negotiation and, as an outsider, it seems to me that that is a determining factor in assessing the financial arrangements. Family mediators want to have an in-house facility so that clients can arrive at their own child support figure. Such a facility could be a kiosk or a software package, but that is not possible in the light of current funding.

It seems that the impact of the Child Support Agency on some aspects of family mediation has been malign. Disputes between couples, we are told,

Mr. Elfyn Llwyd (Meirionnydd Nant Conwy): I want to make a brief speech in support of the amendments. At our last sitting the hon. Member for Bristol, East spoke forcefully in support of her amendments. Clearly, she was speaking from considerable experience and what she said made much sense.

Under the old procedure under section 41 of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973, it was not possible to obtain a decree nisi unless the judge was satisfied that all the arrangements for the children were as they should be. In other words, the children's rights were paramount in any form of matrimonial law. That is not the current position, so children's rights have been eroded. The current law is less satisfactory because divorces can be granted without adequate attention being paid to the arrangements for the children.

In my experience, the statement of arrangements form, which solicitors complete, is the last item to be filed. The divorce petition is important; certificates on reconciliation slightly less so; and then there are the arrangements for the children. When we went to court, we often found that those arrangements were very different from those that were scribbled down at the start of the proceedings. Clearly, something must be done about that. Typically, the husband would give his former wife the matrimonial home and the equity therein, so the children would have a roof over their heads. The important point is that there would be no need for further, periodic referrals, perhaps every 18 months or two years. to the court, taking up its time and, worse, involving the children and the parties in further acrimony.
