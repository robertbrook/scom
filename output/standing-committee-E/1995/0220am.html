Mr. Mike O'Brien (North Warwickshire): The Committee will be glad that I have not brought "Butterworths" with me today, although I have brought another little text with me, to which I might occasionally refer.

The amendments demonstrate how self-assessment has proceeded in the past few years and substantiate Labour's case. Clause 115 amends the provisions for self-assessment in section 12B and schedule 1A of the Taxes Management Act 1970. Those provisions were inserted by schedule 19(3) of the Finance Act 1994, amended by section 105 of the Finance Act 1995 and Col. 366 are amended again by the Bill. We are now amending the amendments to the amendments, which shows where we have got to with the Bill. The Opposition do not object in principle to the Government amendments, but we should like some explanation of them.

The self-assessment process has become something of a mess. We have had representations from various organisations that consider the amendments in the Bill and those tabled by the Government as problematic. I propose to set out clearly the way in which the Opposition believe the process will develop.

Under amendment (a), an Inland Revenue officer could accept other documentary evidence, such as vouchers, as proof when original records of payment of overseas taxes through double taxation, for example, had been lost. No one wants to place extra burdens on business.

Image Col. 367 A person who is liable to make a tax return for a tax year or accounting period must keep all the records that he needs to make a complete and correct return. A trader must do so for five years from 31 January following the tax year, and a company must do so for six years from the end of the accounting period. A non-trader must keep records for one year from 31 January following the tax year if the return is made on time or for one year from the end of the quarter day on which it was made if it is late.

Under the existing wording of section 12B(4) of the Taxes Management Act 1970, copies of originals of the documents may be preserved. Failure to comply with record-keeping rules can result—under section 12B (5)—in a penalty of up to £3,000 regardless of whether any tax has been lost. A series of changes has been put forward by various legislation.

Subsection (3) of the clause inserts a new section 12B (4A) which covers subsequent records. It is worthwhile setting out exactly what is proposed. New subsection (a) (i) amends the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988 in respect of section 234 on dividend vouchers—statements of dividends and tax credits provided by companies when making distributions. New subsection (a) (ii) amends section 352 in respect of certificates of tax deducted on various provisions of the 1988 Act, such as donations to charities, annuities, annual payments, annual interest, building society interest, discretionary grants and so forth. New subsection (b) deals with payments to construction industry subcontractors; subsection (c) (i) with payments of foreign tax; and (c) (ii) with foreign tax deemed to have been paid on specific tax sparing agreements.

Under sections 234 and 352 of the Taxes Management Act 1970, the taxpayer is required to provide a document only when the recipient so requests in writing. Notes on clauses admit that clause 115

"extends the rules for keeping records".

The proposed change was not, however, referred to in the Budget day press release from the Inland Revenue, document No. 24, on self-assessment.

The Faculty of Taxation and the Institute of Taxation have questioned the need for retention of the original vouchers. The institute noted that the clause would cause

"a major compliance problem in practice"

and that it would be

"unworkable if the CREST system is adopted".

It recommends consultation so that requirements can be freely discussed with Inland Revenue. A similar point could be made about other documents specified under new section 12B (4)—for example, certificates of tax deducted at source, building society interest and so on.

Government amendments Nos. 107 and 109 now seek to redress the problem of retaining dividend vouchers and tax deducted certificates. The Col. 368 requirement to retain original records of foreign tax paid must, however, take into account the fact that the availability of such documents is dependent on the practices of the foreign tax authority, especially regarding the time taken to agree foreign tax liabilities. When foreign inquiries are made, several years may pass before appropriate records can be obtained. Does that mean that the original claim must leave out the foreign tax relief claim because the requisite record may not have been obtained by the filing date? The Minister should deal with that problem.

The company would be required to amend its return within the next 12 months using appropriate procedure under section 11 AA (2) (b) of the 1970 Act, as inserted by section 82 of the Finance Act 1994, if information became available within that time. Even after 12 months, some foreign tax relief information may remain unavailable. In that case, self-assessment would seem to require that the company make a claim to error or mistake relief within five years of the filing date. That process will be far more bureaucratic and time-consuming than the current method of agreeing foreign tax relief.

Labour amendment (a) to Government amendments Nos. 107 and 109 seek to probe the Government's view on the problem of record keeping generally and on claims not included in returns. Government amendment No. 109 requires that

"an officer of the Board is satisfied that any facts which he reasonably requires to be proved, and which would have been proved by the records, are proved by other documentary evidence furnished to him."

We believe that that is unnecessarily bureaucratic. The Minister may be able to explain why it is put in that way. Why not just provide that the record or other documentary evidence is acceptable? The words

"proved by other documentary evidence"

might allow individual officers to apply an overly high standard.

The amendments also seem inconsistent with the general approach of schedule 19, which aims to remove provisions requiring an officer of the board to be satisfied that certain conditions exist. Labour amendment (b) to Government amendments Nos. 107 and 109 would lower the standard of documentary evidence required for records in general and for claims not included in returns by changing "proved by" to "supported by". The documentation, taken with the other circumstances perhaps known to the Inland Revenue officer, may be enough to satisfy the Inland Revenue. That officer may have dealt with a particular business over a long period and may know that it normally deals with its affairs in an exemplary manner. There may be insufficient documentation to prove the case, but enough, taken with the overall record of a business, to support it and to satisfy the Inland Revenue; in such circumstances we would all think that that documentation should be accepted. So "proved" seems to set an unnecessarily high standard, whereas "supported" would be sufficient to satisfy the Inland Revenue—it would not accept the evidence if it were not. Our amendment would allow the Inland Revenue to be satisfied that there was sufficient Image Col. 369 documentation to satisfy it without requiring that extremely high standard of proof.

Those are the areas that we seek to explore with the Minister. I hope that he can comment on them so that we can decide whether to press the amendments.

At the heart of the amendments is a proposition that deals with the fundamental question of record keeping. To hear Opposition Members, one would think that keeping records was a brand new imposition on the taxpayer suddenly dreamt up for self-assessment. I think that everyone in this room is a taxpayer: certainly all hon. Members are and I imagine that every year they have to keep a record of official documents to justify the information that they give to the Revenue under the current arrangements.

Using common sense, we have sought to transfer the current arrangements to a system of self-assessment. The fundamental difference with self-assessment is that it puts the taxpayer in the driving seat, which is good. The taxpayer can base his self-assessment on his perception of his tax affairs and on his records, year in and year out. If he wants to know how much tax he will have to pay, he has to assume responsibility for the accuracy of the statements that he makes.

Our amendment recognises that human frailty may play a part in record keeping. Someone who keeps his tax records may lose an original document. Such documents may be pieces of paper that are effectively worth money, such as the vouchers relating to the receipt of a dividend from a company, which tell the Revenue that tax has been paid on that dividend. People will have to justify their self-assessment by supplying a piece of paper that says that they have paid tax on the dividend declared on their tax return. It is, therefore, an important document.

We were specific in clause 115, but we recognise human frailty and, as a result of our consultations with the self-assessment consultative committee, we have introduced an amendment dealing with lost documents. If, by any chance, a person has lost an original document, the inspector can ask him to show, Col. 374 in some other sensible way, that the tax has been paid on the amount in the self-assessment. If he can provide the inspector with a building society book, for example, or with further information or even with a replacement document from the building society or the company that has issued the original share dividend certificate, it is highly likely that the inspector will be totally satisfied with that alternative form of proof. We were anxious to introduce that provision in our amendments.
