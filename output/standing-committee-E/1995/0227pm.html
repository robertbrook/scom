Ms Dawn Primarolo (Bristol, South): Clauses 171 and 172 make several changes to the rules on stamp duty on shares and the stamp duty reserve tax. As the Inland Revenue press release of 4 January this year states:

"Most of these changes are designed to take account of the introduction of the paperless shares transfers under the CREST system, which are due to come in July 1996."

CREST is the replacement of the ill-fated TAURUS project and it is essential for London not only that CREST is a success—which TAURUS was not—but that ownership of shares can be transferred quickly, safely and with the minimum of formalities. CREST replaces TALISMAN, the existing settlement system for stock exchange transactions, so CREST in fact replaces the disastrous TAURUS, which was to replace TALISMAN. CREST will also handle many off-market transactions, many of which will not be sales. For example, executors could use the CREST system to distribute shares to beneficiaries when a deceased person's estate is wound up.

I should like to make some brief comments about the CREST system and ask the Minister some questions; giving that clause 172 relates to subsequent Image Col. 557 clauses, I shall also tag on some questions on the replacement of the stamp duty reserve tax for those transactions in the CREST system if you, Mr. Hogg, will permit me. The matters are complicated and I shall take them slowly and succinctly.

Clause 172 provides for the transfer of shares into electronic transfer systems such as CREST to be exempt from stamp duty. The fixed 50p stamp duty charge that would otherwise arise on deposits of shares into the CREST system will accordingly not arise, which seems sensible. Once shares are transferred into CREST, they will become dematerialised and transfers of legal title will take place electronically. Stamp duty will therefore not apply as it is a tax on instruments. The charge that will now apply once shares are held in CREST is the stamp duty reserve tax, hence the measures contained in the subsequent clauses. There are differences between stamp duty and stamp duty reserve tax and the Bill makes several changes to the latter to bring it into line with stamp duty, but does not do so in all cases. The Government seem again to be widening the scope of the stamp duty reserve tax—SDRT—in the interests of the Inland Revenue but not of the taxpayer. The SDRT applies to any oral or written agreement for the transfer of shares for consideration in money or money's worth, but not all those transactions will attract ad valorem stamp duty, which applies only where the consideration is money or shares. There are, therefore, circumstances in which the stamp duty reserve tax charge would arise, but an ad valorem stamp duty charge would not.

Moreover, there are situations in which there is a stamp duty exemption—for example, transfers between associated companies and company reconstructions—but no equivalent stamp duty reserve tax relief. It would appear from the material that has been put out by CREST Co., especially the white book that was issued last year, that no attempt will be made to replicate within the stamp duty reserve tax legislation the stamp duty exemptions from ad valorem duty. Indeed, the Bill as currently drafted does not do that either. That seems a little odd. To obtain the benefit of stamp duty exemptions effectively for stamp duty reserve tax, stocks will have to be dematerialised—they will have to be taken out of the system or documents will have to be created to back up transactions that have already been settled within the CREST system. That seems most peculiar given the emphasis that Ministers have placed on everyone being as efficient as possible and given the undertakings on deregulation. CREST participants will have to incur additional costs because of the failure of the Bill as it stands to replicate for stamp duty reserve tax the stamp duty exemptions. Perhaps the Economic Secretary could deal with that point when she comments later.

Moreover, it would appear from the CREST Co. white book that the Revenue is requiring CREST participants to give details of transactions where there is no consideration in either money or money's worth and where, therefore, there can be no question of a stamp duty reserve tax charge. For example, CREST participants might be required to state by means of a Col. 558 computer "flag" whether a transfer of shares is a gift, a distribution in liquidation or a transfer of shares from the executor to the beneficiary. That information is certainly needed for stamp duty purposes, but there is no statutory warrant for that information to be required where the charge is to the stamp duty reserve tax. The only information that the Revenue appears to be entitled to under the stamp duty reserve tax rules is confirmation that there is no consideration in money or money's worth. There is no need for the Revenue to have the further information once that simple fact has been established.

If the Economic Secretary believes that the Revenue should be given the additional information for stamp duty reserve tax purposes, the right course is surely to bring before Parliament specific proposals to do exactly that. That has not happened. Perhaps the Economic Secretary could undertake to consider that point, which is quite detailed.
