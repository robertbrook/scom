New Clause 15
ASSETS HELD FOR MORE THAN ONE YEAR


'.—(1) After Section 2 of the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992 there shall be inserted the following section.


"Assets held for more than one year.


2A—(1) Where a chargeable gain arises on the disposal of an asset which has been held by the taxpayer for more than one year, the amount of that gain shall be reduced by the percentage indicated in subsection (2) below.

  1. the percentage referred to in subsection (1) above is—
    1. 15 per cent. if the asset has been held for more than one year and less than two years;
    2. 30 per cent. if the asset has been held for more than two years and less than three years;
    3. 45 per cent. if the asset has been held for more than three years and less than four years;
    4. 60 per cent. if the asset has been held for more than four years and less than five years;
    5. 75 per cent. if the asset has been held for more than five years and less than six years;
    6. 90 per cent. if the asset has been held for more than six years and less than seven years.
  2. If the asset has been held for more than seven years no chargeable gain shall arise.
  3. This section shall not apply to reduce that amount of an allowable loss.
  4. This section shall not apply to a disposal which is deemed by this Act to take place when no actual disposal takes place if the taxpayer is also deemed to reacquire the asset; but in such cases the taxpayer shall be treated for the purposes of applying subsections (2) and (3) above on a subsequent disposal as having held the asset throughout the period for which he has actually held it."


(2) This section shall apply to disposals taking place on or after 6th April 1996 in the case of disposals by individuals and trusts, or 1st April 1996 in the case of disposals by companies.'.—[ Mr. Legg.]

Mr. Legg
: I should like to review the current capital gains system in the United Kingdom. Most members of the Committee will be familiar with the American saying, "If it ain't broke, don't mend it". I believe that our system of capital gains tax is broke, it should be changed and there is a consensus for change.

In the two weeks since I tabled my new clause, there has been much debate in the press about capital gains, all of which has been in favour of reform of the tapering system and of a lower capital gains tax. I am also pleased to have received much support from parliamentary colleagues on the Committee and in the House. I am grateful to many organisations that are interested in such matters: the Confederation of British Industry, the Institute of Directors, the Country Landowners Association, the Engineering Employers Federation, the Centre for Policy Studies, the Adam Smith Institute and Pro Share have all contacted me and support the new clause.

There appears to be a growing consensus in Parliament, in business and among investors for reform of the capital gains system. That consensus may even extend to Opposition Members. Things are changing on this front and there is a wider appreciation of the importance of capital having a reasonable tax regime.

The current problems of capital gains taxation stem from the capital gains changes that Nigel Lawson, now Lord Lawson, who made so many worthwhile reforms of our tax system, introduced in 1988. He made a mistake when he increased the rate of capital gains from 30 per cent. to 40 per cent. and accepted the argument of the Inland Revenue that capital should be taxed in the same way as income. He was wrong to accept the advice of tax gatherers, who may have JPEG 793 thought that the changes would make their task easier. The changes have not been good for business, for investors or for the Exchequer.

The yield from capital gains tax since Nigel Lawson introduced those changes in 1988 has fallen quite remarkably. The advice that he received from officials in 1988 when he presented his Budget was that increasing the rate of capital gains tax from 30 per cent. to 40 per cent. would produce more revenue. The advice from those officials was over-simplistic and they did not understand that changes in tax lead to changes in people's behaviour. Since the rate was raised to 40 per cent. people have been less willing to realise their investments. Instead of the yield from capital gains tax rising, it has fallen dramatically.

In 1987–88, the last year in which capital gains was at 30 per cent., the total revenue yield amounted to £2.2 billion. The following year, when the so-called reforms were introduced and capital gains went to 40 per cent., revenue fell to £1.8 billion. The next year it fell to £1.7 billion. The year after that it fell to £1 billion and the year after to £900 million. Since then it has stayed below the £1 billion mark. The effect of increasing the rate of capital gains tax has therefore been to cut revenue by some 50 per cent.

One of the organisations that has supported the new clause is ProShare, which is pressing for wider share ownership, particularly among individuals. Individuals would be happy to pay tax on all those investments that have risen in value if there were a more reasonable capital gains tax regime. If we cut capital gains tax and had a tapering system that lowered rates of tax, the total yield to the Exchequer would increase—people who are sitting on gains because they do not want to see 40 per cent. of them taken by the tax man would respond to a more reasonable regime.

The United States experience bears out my point that cuts in the rates generate more revenue. Much research has been done on the United States, which has had various tax rates over the years; the cuts in capital gains tax there in 1979 and 1982 resulted in more revenue. Every instance of a capital gains tax cut in the United States has been rapidly followed by a significant increase in capital gains tax realisations and by higher taxes paid on those gains. The lower tax costs of selling assets has increased realisations and tax payments.

Apart from the fact that people do not want to pay the current high rates and so hold on to existing assets, the JPEG 795 rates create several serious economic distortions. First, a bias against risk-taking! entrepreneurs are less likely to invest in risky projects if they believe that high rates of tax will be payable on the gains that they achieve, having risked their capital. Individual managers are less likely to move from a larger, cosier corporate environment to starting their own businesses if they know that they will be paying the same rate of tax on capital gains. So the current system imposes a restriction on entrepreneurs' ability to invest and expand. High taxes on savings, of which this is one, are also unhelpful to our economy, encouraging consumption and bringing down the overall savings ratio.

As well as the immediate tax consequences of capital gains tax rate reductions, there are the much more important consequences of the second-round tax revenue effects. If capital gains tax is set at more reasonable levels, more people will want to invest and that is important because it means more businesses, more prosperity and more jobs, which in turn create more revenue. The American experience is again relevant; the United States Joint Committee on Taxation, which reported on the proposed capital gains tax reductions that President Bush advocated, estimated that around $11 billion of revenue would be lost by those changes but that there would be a $100 billion increase in economic activity.

That shows how flawed was the advice given to Lord Lawson in 1988 when he increased the rate of capital 828 gains tax to 40 per cent. If hon. Members consult the Red Book issued then, they will find that the Inland Revenue advised the then Chancellor that more revenue would be generated from capital gains by that change. The reality has been the reverse; revenue has fallen against the background of an extremely buoyant equity market.

The property market has been mentioned. Most members of the Committee know that most properties in the United Kingdom are free of capital gains tax. Principal private residences, in which there has been a fall in value in recent years, are free of capital gains tax. The Inland Revenue's own figures show that revenue has fallen significantly against buoyant increases in asset value. Too many people will not change their current configurations of investment because they know that they will have to pay tax at 40 per cent. Unless we change that we will not benefit the United Kingdom economy.

The Chancellor would have more revenue, not less, from such a change, and would have more revenue for public services and to reduce taxes in other areas. As my hon. Friend the Member for Dover said when he spoke so ably, our problem is that this tax is a tax on success. To free people to be more successful and generate more revenue must be the message given by the Committee.

My hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield referred to a constituent who has fled to the Bahamas. We do not want successful business men to go to the Bahamas.
