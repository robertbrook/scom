clause Clause 64
title CHARGE AND RATES OF INCOME TAX FOR 1996–97

Mr. Malcolm Bruce (Gordon): I beg to move amendment No. 2, in page 42, line 30, at end insert—

'(4) In respect of reclaims by charities of basic rate income tax deducted from Charitable Covenants entered into before 28th November 1995, such income tax repayments to charities shall be calculated with reference to a deemed basic rate of income tax of 25 per cent.'.

This is a simple but important amendment and I hope that the Government will accept it or at least give it serious attention. Over the past few years the Government have pursued a policy that was once presented as a tax-cutting strategy. But it can no longer be presented as that because taxes have increased. It has been a tax-switching strategy—cutting tax on income and increasing indirect taxes, especially VAT.

Arguments about the impact of the tax burden may divide the Committee, but there has been a side effect which it may not have been the Government's intention to produce. I am talking about the impact of the tax changes on charities. Members of the Committee will be aware that a significant part of the income for charities derives from covenants signed by people to pay a certain amount of money over a number of years on which the charities can recover the tax that the covenator would have paid out of gross income.

A specific example may help the Committee. When a charity receives income under a deed of covenant for £75, it can reclaim £25 from the Inland Revenue. That £25 represents tax at the basic rate prior to this Budget Col. 220 of 25 per cent. on that part of the covenator's gross income of £100 out of which the £75 covenant has been paid. I trust that the Committee is with me thus far.

As a result of the reduction in the basic rate of income tax from 25p to 24p, a charity receiving a covenant income of £75 will be able to reclaim only £23.68 from the Revenue. For every £100 donation by covenant to charity there will be a reduction of 5.25 per cent. in total benefit. That equals a drop of about £260 on a charity's annual receipts of £5,000. There is some uncertainty about the total cost to charities. An early estimate put the figure of £17 million, but that has been revised to a more accurate £10 million to £12 million. That is not a huge amount of money to the Government, but it is a significant source of income to charities.

When I mentioned on Second Reading that I intended to table this amendment, I got what I thought—and hoped—was an encouraging nod from the Minister. I was not alone in thinking that that was the Minister's response: some people sitting in the Gallery thought so too. The Observer reported:

"The hope of a concession came last week as Malcolm Bruce, Liberal Democrat Treasury spokesman, tabled an amendment to the Finance Bill which would cut charities' losses.


The Minister nodded when Bruce proposed that the Government allow charitable covenants signed before the Budget should continue to qualify for relief at the old rate of 25 per cent. He said he would 'listen hard' to this proposal.


Members of Parliament, charities and tax reform groups have interpreted this as a sign that the Government may consider helping charities recoup losses incurred after the 1p tax cut."

As the Committee will recall, I tried to intervene on the Minister in his winding-up speech, but he would not give way. I wait in eager anticipation for his response to this constructive proposal.

If the Minister thinks that the cost of the concession might be a little high, I would point out that the hon. Member for Dover (Mr. Shaw) successfully tabled amendment on the Floor of the House to increase the concession on share option schemes from £20,000 to £30,000. This amendment would cost slightly less than that. It will be an interesting test for the Government. Are they prepared to put share option schemes ahead of a concession that would benefit charities? What value might be put on resistance to this amendment?

The effect on individual charities will vary according to circumstances. I have a letter from the National Trust, which is keen that the amendment is accepted. It states:

"The Trust notes the statutory provision in Clause 64 for a reduction in the basic rate of income tax from 25p to 24p in the pound, which is in line with the Government's long term strategy of reaching the 20p basic rate of taxation.


However, this does have serious implications for charities who gain a significant portion of their operating income by reclaiming the basic rate of income tax deducted from charitable covenants. The Trust greatly values this additional income derived from its covenanted membership subscriptions which is allocated to conversation work on the Trust's properties. We are already having to reschedule and prioritise essential maintenance and restoration work on the Trust's properties due to a reduction in grant aid."

Image Col. 221 Perhaps I should declare an interest as a member of the National Trust for Scotland, but there is support on both sides of the House for the work of that body and of the National Trust representing England and Wales. I am sure that Conservative Members will be especially worried that stately homes and important properties in the care of the National Trust may suffer damage as a direct result of the change proposed in the Bill and of the National Trust's inability to keep up the maintenance programme for properties. The letter continued:

"In the 1996–97 financial year…the Trust will lose income in the region of £360,000."

My amendment does not address that problem, but that extract shows what the charities have lost while this process has been going on and what they will lose if it continues. The Trust's letter states:

"Should a 20p basic rate of tax be achieved, in line with Government's long-term strategy, the Trust would suffer a permanent reduction in annual income of £1.8 million."

Clearly, this matter is of legitimate concern to the charities that are affected.

In campaigning or representational terms, charities are more concerned about the impact of the switch to VAT. The amendment does not deal with that, but to put it in context and show why this is a small gesture to charities compared with a large shift to their disadvantage, it is now estimated that non-recoverable VAT—for non-business, purely charitable activities—is running at about £350 million a year. One does not have to be a mathematical genius to calculate the difference between 17 per cent. and 8 per cent., which was the rate of VAT when the Government began this shift.

In the past couple of years the impact of the lottery has been mixed. Charities get some benefit from it, but that does not compensate them for their losses due to additional taxation. Moreover, charities have to compete with the lottery in the fundraising process.

The amendment would do one simple thing, so the Government could and should accept it. All it seeks to do is to allow those covenants that were signed and in place before the Budget to continue to receive tax relief at the rate of 25p in the pound as opposed to 24p in the pound. Charities are becoming more professional. When promoting covenants, they make the tax implications clear to potential covenators. When people write covenants, they make their donations on the basis of what they calculate the charity will receive. When selling the idea to potential givers, charities say, "If you want us to have this amount, you can sign a covenant for that much less and the tax relief will give us the net benefit." Many people make out their covenants to achieve the net effect for the charity that they want to support.

I shall round off my argument by referring to the impact of the change as calculated by a few other charities. The National Council for Voluntary Organisations, which initially estimated that the Col. 222 reduction would cost £17 million in lost revenue, on the basis of further research has now concluded that, although it is difficult to be precise, the relevant figure—I am sure that that is the figure that matters to the Government—would be between £5 million and £10 million. So it is at the low end of the scale compared with the share option proposal of the hon. Member for Dover, and I am sure that the Government will want to be at least as generous.

Oxfam calculates that the loss of revenue per annum would be £150,000. Other members of the Committee may be more familiar with what Oxfam does with its money, but we all know that £150,000 goes an awfully long way in an organisation like Oxfam, which has low overheads and a good record on the delivery of essential services.

The Royal National Institute for the Blind estimates a loss of £35,000, the National Trust a loss of £360,000 and no doubt others are paging us with details of their expected losses. The Cancer relief Macmillan Fund, which is highly valued in my constituency, expects to lose about £25,000. The amount varies from charity to charity, but there could be significant impacts on important charities, the work of which I am sure all members of the Committee value—the Government in particular recognise that they do important work.
