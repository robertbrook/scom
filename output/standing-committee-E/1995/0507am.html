Mr. Elfyn Llwyd (Meirionnydd Nant Conwy): Since I embarked on my contribution to the debate on amendment No. 30, I seem to have lost my voice. I shall, therefore, keep my contribution short this morning.

In our previous sitting, I referred to mediation. The National Association of Family Mediation and Conciliation Services recognises that legitimate concerns about power imbalances between divorcing couples must be taken into account in mediation. That is even more important when there is a history of domestic violence, when, at the outset, it should be known that mediation is hardly the course that should be taken.

Indeed, in a recent edition of "Mediation Quarterly", Joan Kelly, an authority on the subject, used the following definition of power:

"Within the mediation context, power can be defined as the ability of a person in a relationship to influence or modify an outcome. Power is the measure of the degree to which one can get one's needs or goals satisfied."

The operation of power between a couple is always highly complex. Put simply, however, I believe that it has two broad dimensions, the more important of which is the specifics of the divorce. By that, I mean the reasons why divorce is being sought; the level of anger and resentment that either spouse brings; and the decisions that must be made concerning children, the family home, finances, and so on.

It is important that the mediator redresses the power imbalance. When there are signs of intimidation, threats and non-negotiable demands, the mediator should interrupt, challenge and deal with such threatening behaviour. I am talking about typical cases when women are in fear of their husbands. The awareness and skills that mediators bring to bear on Col. 94 power imbalances will be of great significance to women because, in the main, such imbalances concern male against female.

I have received representations from Welsh Women's Aid about the contribution of the Lord Chancellor in another place. He said that domestic violence would be one of the "prescribed circumstances" in which the Legal Aid Board would not consider mediation more appropriate than proceedings. That is right and proper, and we should build on that statement during our proceedings. We should be wary of anything that places women in a less fortunate position. We are seeking parity in such circumstances. It is difficult when there is a dominant partner and a history of violence, and we should be ever mindful of that.
