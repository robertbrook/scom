Health Service Commissioners (Amendment) Bill

10.30 am

The Chairman: I welcome members of the Committee to the first sitting on the Health Service Commissioners (Amendment) Bill. I know that I speak on behalf of my co-Chairman, Mr. Donald Anderson, when I wish you a restful break when the House rises for the short Christmas recess. We look forward to you returning with your batteries fully charged for the Committee's remaining sittings.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Health (Mr. John Horam): I beg to move,

That during the proceedings on the Health Service Commissioners (Amendment) Bill the Committee do meet on Tuesday 9 January at half-past Four o'clock and, with effect from Thursday 11 January, on Thursdays and Tuesdays at half-past Ten o'clock and at half-past Four o'clock.

It is a privilege to serve under your chairmanship, Mr. Winterton—at least it is now, and I hope that it will remain so. You are extremely experienced in health matters, and this is a health Bill. The fact that you are chairing the Committee demonstrates the Bill's importance to patients, their relatives and to NHS practitioners and staff. You will be able more readily to separate the wheat from the chaff because of your background in health. We also welcome Mr. Anderson.

Hon. Members on both sides of the Committee made it clear on Second Reading that they supported the Bill's aims and that they expected constructive debate and scrutiny. I am sure, therefore, that both Chairmen will have an instructive and interesting time. I shall listen carefully to hon. Members' views and I hope that we shall be able to reassure them on the issues that were raised on Second Reading.

The sittings motion may seem overambitious—as does this Room, given that this is a tiny Bill. We adopted the present sittings motion not only so that we might have a good recess, but so that we could proceed speedily on a Bill that is widely desired. It will reform the national health service complaints system and implement widely welcomed proposals to extend health commissioners' jurisdiction to cover all complaints about services to NHS patients, wherever they are provided.

I hope that we shall make sufficient progress so that we do not have to use all the sittings that are provided for in the motion.

Question put and agreed to.

Resolved,

That during the proceedings on the Health Service Commissioners (Amendment) Bill the Committee do meet on Col. 4 Tuesday 9 January at half-past Four o'clock and, with effect from Thursday 11 January, on Thursdays and Tuesdays at half-past Ten o'clock and at half past Four o'clock.

The Chairman: Before I call the order of consideration motion, I remind the Committee that there is a financial resolution in connection with the Bill. Copies are available in the Room.

I should also call attention to the fact that adequate notice and, whenever possible more than the minimum notice period, should be given of amendments. As a general rule, Mr. Anderson and I will be extremely reluctant to call starred amendments.

Resolved,

I am sure that we all agree that this reform of the complaints procedure is long overdue. However, I am worried about certain aspects of the extension of the health service commissioner's remit. First, his new remit extends to family health service providers, from dentists and pharmacists to general practitioners, both fundholding and non-fundholding. Will the Minister tell us whether all those providers will operate the full new NHS complaints system, which is the cornerstone of the commissioner's new powers? If they are all running a complaints procedure, will they have time to administer to their patients, which is what they are mainly there to do? Has the Minister's Department undertaken an analysis of how much time family Image Col. 5 health service providers should spend on the new complaints and ombudsman system?

What will be the financial consequences of the extension of the health service commissioner's remit? We understand that it will cause marginal additional financial cost to the NHS. Has the Department carried out research on this matter? Costs might be marginal to the NHS as a whole—I would welcome any evidence for that assumption—but what effect will the new role have on small pharmacists, general practices or dentists still working within the NHS? A marginal cost to the service as a whole could be a real burden on an individual practice.

What of independent providers? The Bill makes it clear that those contracted to treat NHS patients will be liable to the same new complaints and ombudsman system as NHS providers. We welcome that, but how will it work? That is the question that many people, both inside and outside the House, want answered. Are independent providers to run complaints systems, and to be subject to referral to and investigation by the health service commissioner, only for some of their patients, as the Bill directs? Can such a system, with independent providers examining just one category of patients, be made to work?

What will happen if, as is inevitable, a private patient with the same grievance of injustice or hardship as a national health service patient in the next bed writes to complain that he has no method of complaining about the decisions made by the private provider? As Members of Parliament, we could all encounter that problem. Under the legislation, private patients have no recourse because they are totally private, but national health service patients in the same establishment have the right to complain to the ombudsman about their treatment. What will the Minister reply when the commissioner writes that he has investigated case A—a patient whom he can investigate—but that patient B, who has an identical complaint, cannot be investigated because he is excluded by the Bill?

What are independent providers supposed to do if the commissioner makes recommendations? Should they implement the procedures on behalf of some of their patients, or for all of them? Could national health service patients in the independent sector become more of a burden, as it were, than private patients because of the complaints procedure? That relevant question should be asked at the outset because the legislation affects the independent sector. The problem is that the extension of the remit reaches only halfway inside the independent sector.

What incentive is there for the independent sector fully to comply with the two-stage complaints procedure that will exist in the national health service? Many people want to know whether stages 1 and 2 will exist in the independent sector—will individual patients be able to receive such a service?

The statutory framework for compliance by independent providers is missing from the legislation. To some extent I understand why, if there has been maladministration or clinical misjudgment, national health service patients can involve the commissioner Col. 6 in the independent sector. But will stages 1 and 2 be written in?

Can the Minister explain how the Bill holds together? There seem to be inconsistencies and discrepancies, which should be addressed if we are to produce a workable and coherent legal framework for the new powers of the commissioner.

I hope that the Minister will consider my comnments constructively. The solution to the problems raised by my questions will ensure a better procedure for patients accessing the system for the first time to have their complaints heard and dealt with. The inclusion of the independent sector, as welcome as it is, introduces many questions about how private patients are to be treated. Opposition Members want those questions answered. What happens if there are great contradictions between national health service patients and private or independent patients in the same establishments? It is beholden on us all to try to get answers to those fundamental questions, as clause 1 breaks new ground, which we all welcome—it provides cover for our constituents when they are in the care of independent providers.
