EXERCISE OF CLINICAL JUDGMENT

(Strathkelvin and Bearsden)

The Chairman

"(3) In section 11 of the 1993 Act (procedure in respect of investigations) there shall be inserted—


`(7) The Secretary of State may by regulations made by statutory instrument make provision for the conduct by a Commissioner of any investigation in consequence of the exercise of clinical judgment.'.".

Mr. Galbraith
: The clause lies at the heart of the Bill; on its proper definitions and practices, the success of the measure will depend. There are three amendments to the clause, the first two of which are to be discussed now. Amendment No. 9 deals with the definition of clinical judgment and inserts the words of the Health Service Commissioners Act 1993 into the Bill. There is no definition in the Bill. Amendment No. 10 gives the Secretary of State the power to enable the commissioner to conduct an investigation following the exercise of clinical judgment.

I understand that the Bill exists largely to deal with complaints—that is quite right and proper. I consider it to have an additional function: to improve the standard of clinical judgment and practice in the national health service. The effect that the procedures could have on that necessary function of those involved has been underestimated. If the Bill is to fulfil that additional role, it must be held in esteem and respected by those who will be subject to its provisons.

To take doctors as an example of workers in the national health service, there are three procedures under which clinical judgment can be challenged. The first is through normal stage 1 and stage 2 within the NHS—I consider that the legislation extends that to the ombudsman—the second is through the General Medical Council and the third through the courts. Similarly with the General Medical Council, the 40 charge of serious professional misconduct—or the new charge in the Bill—is not related to individual items of misjudgment in clinical matters, which is what the ombudsman is concerned with. It is important, therefore, that those three areas continue to be kept separate and that that is clearly laid down in regulations.

With regard to who is to make the decision, it is again important that we get it right. The ombudsman, has agreed that he will not necessarily make it; he will have medical advisers who will decide in the wings. I am sure that his judgment will be correct.

A further question arises: will the person whose clinical judgment has been called into question have a say in who the advisers are and will he or she be able to appoint an adviser? We must not forget that such a process challenges the fundamentals of that person's clinical standards and profession. We are not challenging the process in cases of serious professional misconduct, because the person has access to a democratic process of redress, nor in cases that have gone to court, where the person can bring evidence. We are challenging an instance where an individual makes a charge that is based on various pieces of evidence. Again, that process must be clearly set out via a statutory instrument.

It is important that each of those procedures be maintained entirely separately. I do not like the suggestion that has already been made by the ombudsman that he might make judgments about how best such matters should be pursued. The procedures perform separate functions and that is why amendment No. 10 was tabled; so that the procedures are laid down by Parliament to ensure that they are followed. In a paper at the end of the year, the ombudsman proposed that he might not pursue a case if he thought that a separate channel, such as the courts, might be the correct one to follow, Such a decision is not within the ombudsman's judgment.

Let us consider the possibilities. If a case goes through the courts, it is considered a judgment against a case of negligence. It might be that no negligence was involved, but that an error of clinical judgment occurred. In other words, a case could fail in court, but could be found in the complainant's favour by the ombudsman. Therefore, it is not right for the ombudsman immediately to abrogate his responsibility to a court where the test of what went wrong is much higher than it is within his domain at that stage. The ombudsman should not be allowed the power to make a decision about whether there is a more appropriate arena in which a case could be judged. He must consider the cases on their own. It is for individuals and their advisers to decide whether to take the case to court separately.

We must also know what levels of judgment are to be considered. In other words, will the ombudsman judge a registrar case separate from a consultant's? From his recent paper, the answer is that he probably will. However, I hope that the ombudsman will not think that because a registrar may have made an incorrect judgment, he is not able to say that perhaps JPEG 41 someone other than the registrar should have made the judgment, thus allocating some responsibility to that other person.

The ombudsman's paper tells us that he will consider judgments differently in the light of whether they concern, for example, a district general hospital or a teaching hospital. I hope that that is not the case in this instance; that is one of the reasons we want Parliament to scrutinise the matter. There may well be different judgments, but the ombudsman must also decide whether it might have been more appropriate for the patient to be managed in a teaching hospital rather than in a district general hospital. It is not that different standards cannot be applied to different types of hospitals, but in deciding matters of clinical judgment, he must look up and down the line.

All such issues are important and need further airing. They must be brought before the House via a statutory instrument before we make our final decision. I want the ombudsman to stick to the NHS complaints procedure, which is fairly straightforward. If he does not and thinks that he can pass off decisions to the various courts, he will deny many patients and individuals what they want: someone to decide whether a misjudgment has been made and if so, to whom to apportion blame. We cannot allow the ombudsman to push that responsibility on to side courts.

We must also be clear about what the ombudsman can investigate in the NHS. He said that he would not normally investigate complaints that had not been through the NHS procedure. If he will not do so normally, on what occasions will he perform such investigations? What is the definition of normal in the paper? What cases will he examine even when they have not been through NHS procedure? Such issues require further clarification, because they are the nub of the Bill and essential for getting it right.

Those are not the subject of the Bill, but they are part of the package. Secondly, he can approach the General Medical Council. Thirdly, there are the courts.

I shall deal first with amendment No. 10, to which the hon. Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden addressed most of his remarks. The amendment would set out statutory regulations on the health service commissioner's approach to complaints. Two issues are involved here. First, the commissioner is a servant of Parliament and reports to it. He is overseen by the Select Committee on the Parliamentary Commissioner for Administration, which is an independent body and 42 part of Parliamentary procedures. He is not responsible to the Government and it is critical that he maintains that independence. I want to introduce as little statutory regulation as possible into the Bill and to preserve the HSC's total independence. That is the right starting point and I am reluctant to prescribe the commissioner's actions in specific circumstances. I am sure that the hon. Gentleman understands that it is wrong for the Government to establish too many statutory regulations.

Secondly, the hon. Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden is concerned about the link between a possible court case for negligence and the health service Commissioner's role. The Bill goes almost all the way towards meeting those concerns. While a court case is in progress, the commissioner will have to refrain from considering it. Once the case is over, however, he will be able to reopen it, if he considers that clinical misjudgment may be involved. He will be able to examine the case, regardless of whether negligence and damages were involved and regardless of what decisions were taken. He can step in and out of the process without any prejudice or hindrance.

That has been explored many times by the Select Committee on the Parliamentary Commissioner for Administration, and not only the current Select Committee. The issue was examined and recommendations to that effect made in reports in 1977, 1978 and 1990–91.

The most recent Select Committee report of 1993, entitled "The powers, work and jurisdiction of the ombudsman", again returns to the question of whether there should be a mechanism, involving the Select Committee, to enable cases to be put to the ombudsman for possible investigation. The report states:

"The Committee has in the past recommended that the Ombudsman be granted the power to initiate investigations. We continue to believe that on occasion this power might prove useful to the Ombudsman. We agree with Justice and the Centre for Ombudsman Studies that such an investigation should only be undertaken where this Committee has raised the matter formally with the Ombudsman as a matter of concern. Thus the Ombudsman's links with Parliament would be maintained. The Ombudsman would, of course, retain his discretion to refuse to investigate. He would initiate an investigation if the matter raised by the Committee seemed to him to provide reasonable grounds for believing that maladministration leading to injustice may have occurred. There would not, however, be the requirement for an aggrieved individual to present a case in which there is prima facie evidence of their having suffered maladministration leading to injustice."

The Select Committee concluded:

"We recommend that the Government introduce legislation to grant the Ombudsman the power to initiate investigations at the recommendation of this Committee."

The amendment is designed to transplant into the Bill that recommendation in relation to the health service remit of the ombudsman.

In 1966, Parliament set up an instrument to enable it to put in hand investigations of alleged maladministration and injustice. When we come across a case such as that of Mrs. Banks, where the whole country is screaming out for some rigorous, independent inquiry to find out exactly how such a monstrous thing could have happened, we have the instrument to hand. The result would be an enormous increase in public confidence in the instrument, and its visibility. In addition, the House's ability to investigate such matters would be hugely increased. The amendment simply inserts into the Bill an idea with a long pedigree that would open up another route into the operation of the ombudsman.
