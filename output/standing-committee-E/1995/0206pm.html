Question again proposed.

Mr. Ian Pearson (Dudley, West): The Government, who rightly want to tackle tax avoidance, will necessarily introduce complex legislation. It is important, however, that legislation dealing with a self-assessed tax is cast in simple terms that can be easily understood. It should also be targeted closely at the relevant abuse. The Bill, however, does not achieve either of those objectives.

The drafting is extremely detailed and difficult for the taxpayer to follow. In view of the Bill's complexity, I question the ability of Customs and Excise officers consistently to interpret and apply it. That must surely be open to doubt, and I would welcome clarification from the Minister as to how standardisation of interpretation can be secured. In view of recent calls from Opposition and Conservative Members to simplify tax legislation, the provision is a retrograde step. Paragraph 1 (4) (c) of schedule 3 provides a good example of arcane drafting.

Anti-avoidance legislation, as the Minister has recognised, requires balancing the needs of the Revenue with those of businesses in order to achieve a degree of certainty in the operation of the tax system. I am not sure that the clause will deliver that. It is wrong that a business man acting in accordance with the laws as interpreted by tax advisers should face the risk of customs rewriting the tax effect of a transaction up to six years later. That is especially worrying in the case of bona fide transactions.

Parliament has a duty to thwart any attempt to enter a tax avoidance scheme, but the Bill must be properly targeted so that business men are aware that its provisions might be used to combat a specific transaction. The clause is like the proverbial sledge hammer used to crack a nut, or perhaps it is like a scatter gun, because it gives licence to Customs and Excise to injure and maim companies at will, if it chooses to do so. I recognise that the Minister says that that will not be the case, but we are likely to have new Ministers in future and new Customs and Excise Col. 124 staff who will make new interpretations. The provisions therefore need to be more clearly targeted.

There is considerable confusion among businesses about the interpretation of the clause and, in particular, the Inland Revenue's application of it. It covers everyday transactions, which was perhaps not the original intention. The Minister said earlier that the main problem arose when companies entered or left group VAT operations. I appreciate that, but the Bill does not precisely target avoidance. Businesses will, therefore, be uncertain about which transactions the Customs and Excise will attack.

Customs and Excise powers must be clarified, and I want to draw several issues to the Committee's attention. Some of them are detailed, and I hope that the Minister will respond to those that he can and consider others in more detail later.

First, employee companies are affected by paragraph 1(1), 2(b) and 3 to schedule 3. Many companies—including those in the financial and public sector, such as insurance companies—operate with a trading company and a separate company that employs its personnel. They do that for sound commercial reasons. However, if companies were not VAT grouped the personnel company would have to charge the trading company VAT. The powers that clause 27 gives Customs and Excise could theoretically be used to disapply the VAT grouping, which would create a ridiculous scenario in which the trading company would have to employ its staff directly to avoid a VAT loss. We are in danger of enforcing a view of business organisation that favours divisional business structures, rather than group structures that involve different constituent companies.

Secondly, paragraph 2(3) deals with the effects on a company's VAT recovery position. If the Customs and Excise issue a notice that requires a company to assume that it is or is not part of a group for one transaction or for a particular period, what action will the VAT group's representative member have to take to adjust the partial exemption calculations—the method of calculating recoverable VAT in past periods? As a joint chief executive of a formerly partially exempt VAT group, I appreciate that that could be a problem.

The period during which a transaction occurs consists of several years prior to the issue of a notice of direction. Will all previous VAT returns be subject to recalculation and assessment? That needs to be clarified.

Thirdly, paragraph 3(1) to the schedule deals with the consistency of the Customs' approach to issuing notices. Because the Customs and Excise can identify transactions within a six-year period, businesses will require clearances from it to be sure that transactions will not be challenged later. It is, however, always difficult to obtain such clearances in an appropriate commercial time frame. Furthermore, it will be difficult to ensure that VAT offices pursue a consistent approach. A complex conglomerate may, therefore, Image Col. 125 have to deal with several different VAT offices, each of which gives different information.

Fourthly, paragraphs 1, 4, 4(b), 2(3) and 4(2) deal with the acquisition of a company. When a company purchases another company and joins another VAT group, can a notice of direction subsequently be issued, requiring a new company to be liable for the debts of the original group, which may be defunct? Organisations might change their practices if they are likely to be caught by Customs and Excise as a result of that acquisition transaction and buy only the assets of the company to minimise the risks of future liability.

Fifthly, charges between existing groups relate to paragraphs 1(1); 1(2)(b) and 1(3) to the schedule. Where a taxable trading company has a partially exempt subsidiary within the VAT group, which from 29 November 1995 started to make a management charge between the companies, will the management charge be attacked as a tax advantage under the definition of a tax advantage given in paragraph 1(3)(c)? That brings me to my sixth point. Does that definition refer to an absolute VAT saving or simply to cash flow? Most companies legitimately experience cash flow advantages—that is a normal part of commercial trading—through VAT grouping. That also requires clarification.

Seventhly, the definition of the term "full value" in paragraph 1(3)(c) is confusing. Tax advisers tell me that Customs and Excise will take it to mean VAT on the total consideration received whereas a business could easily take it to mean an attempt to impose a certain value on a supply. Could we have confirmation that Customs and Excise will take it to mean that and that that will appear in guidelines or a code of practice?

My eighth point concerns the definition of a genuine commercial purpose in paragraph 1(a). That can often be difficult to identify as it relies on what was in the mind of the member of staff when a transaction was undertaken; there can be cases in which there are at least two interpretations of what was in the mind of a person or group of people in such circumstances. Ninthly, transactions undertaken before implementation of the legislation need clarifying—that relates to paragraph 1(3)(a) and paragraph 5(8). Will a company that commenced a chain of transactions before 29 November last year be caught by the legislation, which was not known to it at the time of its decision to proceed? Would it be fair to penalise a company in such circumstances? I do not think that it would and I would appreciate clarification on that.

Tenthly—and lastly—I shall deal with transactions that have not yet taken place. It is difficult to perceive circumstances in which Customs and Excise could issue a notice of direction on a transaction that has not yet occurred but which is anticipated. Will the issuing of such a notice be withheld until the transaction has taken place or all the information has been disclosed to Customs and Excise? On the face of it, that would Col. 126 force the company to defend its actions in regard to transactions that had not even taken place.

Those are just some of the areas that need clarification. There are significant problems with the legislation; it is too complex in general and, more importantly, is not simple enough for the ordinary taxpayer to understand. It takes a blunderbuss approach instead of targeting the particular avoidance. The Institute of Indirect Taxation states:

"the extent of the powers taken is awesome in its extent"

—that is not very good English but it is a valid point. The Institute of Taxation states:

"These provisions are likely to cause uncertainty at a time when simplification is being encouraged."

So two august tax bodies are suggesting that the Government may not have got it right. The sheer volume of Government amendments to schedule 3 makes it apparent that they had second thoughts as a result of the consultation exercise.

I think that the taxpayer would consider such retrospection to be fair. It would be easily understandable and difficult for businesses to criticise. Will the Minister respond to that point?
