Mr. Boateng: Last night, the House had a wide-ranging debate on several amendments to the Bill. During the speeches that were made from both sides of the House, both for and against the amendments, and in the Divisions in the Lobbies, the House expressed a clear view that the Bill must contain provision for the saving of marriages that can be saved and also provision to make the needs and the interests of children paramount. Both sides of the House expressed the view that the Bill should not be simply a vehicle for the dissolution of marriage, but a means by which marriage might be supported. Indeed, as a result of amendments made in another place, the support of marriage as an institution is one of the guiding principles outlined in clause 1.

As a result of last night's vote, the time for consideration and reflection required before a divorce may take place is substantially altered. It is quite clear, and the Government conceded the fact yesterday, that a number of consequential amendments will need to be made. They will obviously be more than simple technical amendments.

The Minister will correct me if I am wrong, but the universal expression of opinion on the Floor of the House yesterday was that reconciliation should be dealt with in the Bill. When speaking to the first group of amendments yesterday, the Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster said in quite unambiguous terms that he readily acceded to that objective. However, the Bill contains only one reference to reconciliation. That reference is not to a mechanism to bring about reconciliation, nor to provisions for its funding; the Bill simply says that reconciliation should be kept in mind by mediators and lawyers. That is all very well, but it is not what the Government pay lipservice to nor is it what the House wants—which is a practical mechanism capable of delivering reconciliation, thereby saving those marriages that can be saved.

The Government have tabled no amendments to the Bill. We must therefore assume that they believe it to meet all the requirements and objectives that they have set out here and in another place. It was quite clear last night that the House does not agree with that judgment.

Before we consider the amendments tabled by me and my hon. Friends and those tabled by several Conservative Members, we are entitled to know the Goverment's intentions. We are entitled to know the nature of the technical amendments that they will have to make as a result of yesterday's vote. We are entitled to know what other amendments they wish to make and whether they are likely to accept amendments that have already been tabled to strengthen the Bill's support for the institution of marriage and for the protection of the interests of children. That is why my hon. Friends and I support the adjournment of the Committee today. It will give the Government an opportunity, over the weekend and on Monday, to consider their position, to table the amendments that Image Col. 5 they intend to table and, on Tuesday morning, to give the Committee a clear expression of their intentions so that we can continue our deliberations.

The importance of that is obvious. For example, the first amendment—No. 36—has been tabled by two Conservative Members. On first sight, that amendment has much to commend it. I fancy that Opposition Members will have no difficulty in supporting it and that it will therefore be carried. Given that, it would be helpful to know, when we come to consider that amendment, whether it is necessary.

If, between now and next week, the Minister tabled, or gave a clear commitment that it was his intention to table, a series of detailed amendments that provided for practicable steps to assist in saving marriages, or that set out how, for example, information was to be given to the parties on the implications for the children of the family of any possible divorce, or on the financial implications of a divorce, there would be no need to vote for amendment No. 36.

If, however, no such amendments were tabled and no such intention were signalled, there would be every need to ensure that, from the outset, the expressions of principle contained in the amendment tabled by the hon. Member for Gainsborough and Horncastle (Mr. Leigh) were included in the Bill. That is but one example of the importance of the Government making their intentions absolutely clear.
