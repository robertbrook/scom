Mr. Tim Smith (Beaconsfield): I beg to move amendment No. 29, in page 25, line 7, at end add

"(5) Inert material is material that does not physically or chemically react, decay or adversely affect other matter with which it comes into contact in a way likely to give rise to environmental pollution.".

Mr. Smith: Good morning, Mr. Hogg. The clause deals with the amount of tax that landfill operators are required to collect: a rate of either £7 per tonne or £2 per tonne. The change from an ad valorem duty to a charge on tonnage was a welcome response to points made during the consultation process. The distinction between the rates is given in subsection (4) which states that the lower rate will apply to

"material … of a kind commonly described as inactive or inert."

There have been several representations that the words "inactive or" do not add to the definition and that they should be left out.

The point that I wish to make on the amendment is one that I made on the Floor of the House when clause 36 was discussed; it was raised by British Alcan Aluminium, a company based in Gerrards Cross, in my constituency, which has an important production plant in Scotland. I am concerned this morning with the impact of the landfill tax on the Scottish plant because it is the sole United Kingdom producer of alumina chemicals. It produces 350 different specialised alumina products that are supplied to around 600 customers in 50 countries. British Alcan is a major United Kingdom plc and takes its environmental responsibilities seriously; it has invested significant capital in improving the environmental aspects of its production process.

The process at the plant in Scotland produces a waste product known as red mud, which is landfilled at an in-house site that is owned and operated by British Alcan. Red mud is an alkaline waste remaining after the extraction of alumina from bauxite, a naturally occurring ore. The company has already Col. 156 spent large sums on research and development but the recycling of waste has proved not to be an option, so it must be disposed of by landfill. Every effort is made by the company to minimise waste but every year approximately 115,000 tonnes of red mud are landfilled in a totally enclosed site that was formerly an abandoned shale mine. That is agreed to be the best practical environmental option. All effluent is returned to the plant where the maximum possible alkali is returned to the extractive process. No effluent arising from the site enters the surrounding environment and no methane or other greenhouse gases can be produced, That is the point of my amendment: the main environmental disadvantage of landfill is methane gas production but no gas is produced in British Alcan's process.

The example of British Alcan reveals one of the difficulties with the landfill tax, which I fully support, however, because I believe that it will, over time, lead to a desirable change in environmental behaviour. British Alcan produces a commodity product for a world market: I have already mentioned the number of products it manufactures and the number of customers and countries to which it sells. The market price of its products is a world market price. The effect of imposing an extra tax of £2 or £7 a tonne—certainly £7 a tonne—will be to render those products totally uncompetitive in the world market. As a consequence, there will be a genuine danger that British Alcan will no longer be able to produce at that plant and will have to make its products at an overseas plant. Up to 600 jobs will be put at risk.

I accept that the Government want to keep the tax as simple and straightforward as possible. As with any tax, the dilemma is that there may be some rough justice. If we try to achieve greater fairness, the measure immediately becomes more complex. The distinction between £7 a tonne and £2 a tonne will be dealt with in regulations. I hope that there will be full consultation on those regulations so that we can ensure that the important dividing line between £7 and £2 a tonne is seen to be fair to British industry and allows it to remain competitive while ensuring that the landfill tax secures its objectives.

The amendment is designed to introduce a new higher rate for toxic waste, which for definitional purposes the amendment refers to as "special material". Earlier, the Paymaster General spoke about the importance of regulations that interpret and give guidance to each of the categories. He also mentioned the consultation undertaken by Customs and Excise, and I shall refer to the report published by it on the responses received to the original consultation paper on landfill tax.

The consultation made it clear, as the Government subsequently acknowledged, that there should be a move from an ad valorem tax to a banded tax. The submissions warned, however, of the environmental damage caused by different types of waste. We believe it makes sense to have at least three tax bands for charging purposes to ensure that waste is charged according to the potential damage that it could do the environment. That approach is different from that contained in the amendments tabled by the hon. Member for Beaconsfield.

Various suggestions have been made about how to implement the proposal for more bands, and a number of organisations advocate a wider range of tax levels to accommodate acute toxicity, chronic toxicity, ecotoxicity, explosion risk, biological hazard, nutrient and biological oxygen demand and the atmospheric effects of greenhouse gases, ozone destroyers, particulates and so on. In addition, it was suggested that the tax should reflect commitments to control or eliminate specific substances and that it should contribute towards the achievement of national targets.

The amendment is much more modest. We endorse the approach of using a banded tax, on which Cambridge Econometrics has also commented. In its report "A Prospective Assessment of the Landfill Tax", that body stated:

"A banded tax with different rates according to the nature of the material being disposed of would ensure that the most inert materials would attract the lowest tax, while hazardous materials would attract the highest tax rates. The principle of banding according to quantities and types of waste has already been established in relation to the Charging Scheme for waste management licences."

In the report on the consultation about landfill, the Government confirmed that most of the responses supported a weight-based tax and favoured a banded approach, on the grounds that the tax should be paid and should relate to environmental damage caused by different types of waste.

The amendment would add £15 to the proposals for £2 and £7. That would begin to recognise environmental damage while balancing the clear difficulties of regulatory definition of the different bands. Moreover, the proposal would give industry in particular the opportunity to plan, on the basis of the Bill's declared intentions, for the eventual inposition of a landfill tax to fulfil those objectives.
