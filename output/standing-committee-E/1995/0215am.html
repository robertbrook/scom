clause Clause 69
title CHARGE AND RATE OF CORPORATION TAX FOR 1996

10.30 am

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Malcolm Chisholm (Edinburgh, Leith): The clause relates to corporation tax, and it would be appropriate to mention the undershooting of the tax, which the Treasury Select Committee report addresses. The Red Book gives the most recent Budget forecast for this year's corporation tax receipts as £26.4 billion, but the latest forecast is £2 billion lower. The projections for next year are also down on the original forecasts. Some changes in corporation tax rates obviously result from changing profit rates—because profits slow down this year, corporation tax will be a little lower next year.

The Select Committee dealt, however, with a second problem: corporation tax is declining as a percentage of profits. The Institute for Fiscal Studies recently noted that corporation tax was 50 per cent. of profits in 1990–91, but 47 per cent. two years later. I do not have more recent figures, but those that I have given encapsulate the problem.

The former Chancellor, the right hon. Member for Kingston upon Thames (Mr. Lamont), also alluded to the problem. He said:

"One of the questions I am interested in is to what extent our Corporation Tax reforms have actually lessened the yield from companies below what we might have expected".

The Treasury Select Committee raised the issue with Treasury officials before Christmas, when it studied the Budget. In response to question 178 on page 33 of the minute, an official said of the undershoot:

"As regards corporation tax, this is the most difficult to explain. As you probably know, main-stream corporation tax is paid with more or less a year's delay, so that the tax we are collecting this year derives from last year's profits."

The hon. Member for Stamford and Spalding (Mr. Davies), who is not yet in his seat, added:

"Which must make it in principle, one would think, to a simpleminded person like myself, rather easier to predict?"

The Treasury official replied:

"Yes, one might think that".

He then gave one explanation of why corporation tax is less than we might expect. He said: Col. 296

"Unfortunately, corporation tax is paid by 10,000-plus companies, all of whose tax position differs; they all have losses which they made previously and which they can set against corporation tax. They may have made losses earlier and they can set those against profits which they earn now. So the precise distribution of the aggregate profit figure across those 10,000-plus companies is absolutely vital to the corporation tax take."

The Select Committee was not impressed by that explanation, and page xxiv of its report noted:

"This does not adequately explain why corporation tax is lower than expected unless those companies with the majority of losses to relieve made the bulk of profits, and there is no reason why this should have occurred."

The Select Committee then considered other explanations, and asked the Government to tackle the problem seriously. It concluded:

"Clearly, on the basis of one year's figures it would be rash to suggest a fundamental change between company profit and tax yield, but the Treasury will no doubt want to consider whether companies are becoming increasingly aware of the tax implications of their operations, are managing to become more adept at employing tax avoidance mechanisms and whether measures introduced in recent Budgets to reduce corporate tax avoidance are generating the additional revenues forecast at the time of their introduction. The Committee recommends that, in its response, the Government clarifies the reasons for the lower yield of corporation tax and explains whether this yield is likely to remain below previous expectations in the longer run."

I hope that the Minister will be able to say something about that when he replies. No doubt he will say that the matter is being considered and that we shall find out what the Government think in due course.

The Select Committee suggests that tax avoidance goes on. Two other possibilities arise that should be considered. First, the period for trading losses set against corporation tax liabilities was extended in 1991 from one year to three years. That was thought to have led to a one-off decrease in payments of about £1 billion. Will the Minister say whether that was a one-off feature, or is it still having an effect?

The second matter relevant to the reform of corporation tax concerns the treatment of dividends. If dividends rise at a rate different from that of profits—or if dividends rise as profits fall, as has happened recently—the higher proportion of profits going in dividends means that less tax is paid, because dividends affect the tax credit for almost half of those who receive it. So the increase in dividends as a share of profits must have an effect on the revenue collected in corporation tax.

Some time ago the Government began dealing with a problem that is relevant to the way in which the tax system can be used more effectively to encourage investment. The previous Chancellor acted in a small way to deal with the problem when he reduced to 20 per cent. both the rate of advanced corporation tax and the tax credit. More radical measures may be required.

I wanted to flag up the problem that I have described, and I shall be interested to hear what the Minister says.

The Inland Revenue press release, which was issued at the time of the Budget, asserted that about 350,000 companies, representing 85 per cent. of the total that pay tax, would benefit from the reduction. The same press release said that the costs of the reduction would be negligible for 1996–97, £95 million for 1997–98 and £130 million for 1998–99. The Chancellor made a great deal of the 1p reduction. It is therefore worth considering, on the basis of those figures, what it is worth to the average company.

The effect of the 1p fall in the small companies' rate is that on average, according to the Government's figures, companies will enjoy next to no benefit in 1996–97, a fall of £271.43 in 1997–98 and a fall of £371.43 in 1998–99. I do not say that that is of no value, but I am sure that members of the Committee would agree that it is of modest value. One might argue that they are average figures; how do they look in an actual example? It is helpful to consider the position of a company making profits of, say, £1 million in a year. What does the company have to do to evaluate what that reduction is worth to it?

The formula is set out in section 13 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988. First, the company must decide whether its profits are below the lower relevant maximum amount, which is £300,000. If so, Image Col. 299 the 24 per cent. rate applies. Since the profits are clearly above that lower limit, the company must apply to its basic profits the full corporation tax rate and then reduce that amount by by another sum determined by the formula M-P x I/P where M is the upper relevant maximum amount, P is the amount of profits, and I is the amount of basic profits.

The formula takes the difference between the upper relevant maximum amount—£1.5 million, which has remained unchanged since 1994, as has the £300,000 lower limit—and the profits and multiplies the difference by the basic profits divided by the profits and a fraction which Parliament decides from time to time. In the Bill, the fraction is 9/400th. Last year, it was one fiftieth. Although basic profits and profits are often the same, they need not be. Other complexities arise for grouped companies. When the £1 million profit company has worked through all that, it will find that it has saved the princely sum of £1,250 for next year. I am not saying that that is of no value, but it is of modest value.

Because the formula is essentially a smoothing mechanism between the lower rate of 24 per cent. and the full 33 per cent. rate, it is inevitable that, in the intermediate range, the marginal rate of taxation is higher-35 per cent. last year and, I think, 35 ¼ per cent. this year. Of course, as the gap between the lower and higher rate widens, one would expect the marginal rate in the intervening range to widen. It is interesting to note that the marginal rate on extra profit in that range will be 35 ¼ per cent.

I quoted those figures by way of example to illustrate the modesty of the benefit and the complexity of some of the considerations involved. When the Minister replies to the debate, will he tell us why the Government have chosen the headline rate reduction instead of taking action on thresholds and why he thinks it a good idea that thresholds have been left unchanged since 1994? The Government must have a policy or philosophy underlying their approach to the threshold, and it would be useful if the Minister could tell the Committee what that is.

I want to probe the Government's thinking on the definition of what is known as the small companies' rate. Literally, it is not so much a small companies' rate as a small profits rate. It means that large companies making small profits will benefit although small companies making large profits might not. It has been argued that trying the rate to the profit level rather than to another measure such as turnover is not the best way to apply relief. I should be grateful if the Minister could say more about the Government's thinking on that.
