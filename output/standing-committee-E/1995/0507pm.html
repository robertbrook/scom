Mr. Paul Boateng (Brent, South): Before we rose for lunch, we were reflecting on the research findings of Professor Rebecca Bailey-Harris in an article in Family Law of November 1995. She raised an issue that we shall consider in our debate on reconciliation—bearing in mind the House's decision to extend the period for reflection and consideration from 12 to 18 months. When the article was written, the proposal was for a 12-month period of reflection.

The Committee will have very much in mind the question that was raised in the article when considering the role of counselling for reconciliation and when that should take place. Referring to the 12-month period, Professor Bailey-Harris asked:

"Will it be conducive to the reconciliation of those whose marriages are in trouble, or will it, conversely, have a hardening effect on attitudes? It would seem likely that in the vast majority of cases there will be no reconsideration by at least the spouse who has already taken the crucial and formalised step of initiating the process."

It is before that initial and formalised step has been taken—before the statement has been filed—that we believe that it is necessary to intervene strongly to create a pathway to reconciliation. Professor Bailey-Harris goes on:

"Whilst the initiating statement is presented in the White Paper as a neutral document in that no accusations are permitted to be contained in it, in reality, it can scarcely be so. The non-initiating spouse is likely to regard herself or himself as having effectively been served with a notice of the other party's intention to quit the marriage, and for reasons which need not be disclosed—a perception not conducive to generosity."

Professor Bailey-Harris makes a strong point. We understand the problems with the petition, but at least it gave a sense that there was a cause, whether or not that was accepted. The danger of the procedure in the Bill is that no cause has to be shown. It is just a statement that the marriage has broken down. It would be a mistake to believe that that, of itself, would necessarily be conducive to reconciliation; nor would it necessarily be conducive even to the absence of bitterness and enmity.

The advantage of counselling, whether for the purposes of exploring whether the marriage can be saved or for the purposes of coping with the trauma of divorce, is that it gives all the parties the opportunity to examine the underlying causes of the crisis in the relationship. It also gives the parties the opportunity to come to terms with those causes, either as a way of bringing them back together and saving the marriage, or of better equipping the parties to move forward into joint and agreed responses to the needs of the children, or into more successful second relationships. All the evidence shows that a second marriage is even more likely to fail than the first. That is a disturbing feature of Image Col. 133 relationship problems. Lessons are not necessarily learnt from the first experience of a broken relationship.

Mr. Boateng: I am not arguing for a return to fault-based divorce. I am simply pointing out the consequences of abandoning that mechanism, because they need to be recognised. The great problem with family law, which has beset every attempt this century to deal with family law reform, is that the rhetoric and the reality seldom meet. The circumstances of breakdown are not altered by changing the rhetoric. In the breakdown of any relationship there is a sense of guilt, fault and culpability.

I admire the fact that, in New Zealand and Australia, that has been recognised. Fault has been eliminated from the legal process of divorce in those countries, and it has been recognised that a new context must be put in place. Counselling provides that context. That is a strong argument for recognising what various forms of counselling have to offer.

Our plea, through the amendment, is that the Government should accept that a space, opportunity and pathways into counselling must be written into the Bill. From counselling one can best explore the hope of reconciliation, if such hope exists, and one can try to deal with the trauma that is the inevitable consequence of divorce.

We can offer them the opportunity to take counselling and to be aware of its possible benefits, but we cannot write into the Bill a procedure whereby someone can formally tell his wife that he is not very happy with the way that she is conducting herself, and that unless she mends her ways, he will consider making a statement that the marriage has irretrievably broken down.

Mr. Boateng: I would not choose to put the matter as the hon. Gentleman puts it. I urge him to recognise that when people initially consider divorce and take the first step, a proportion of them go to solicitors as a cry for help. We know that to be the case—it is not seriously Col. 134 disputed by anybody. Let us presume that, under the Bill, they go to the information session as a cry for help. We argue that, from the information session, there must be a clearly defined pathway into an opportunity to meet privately with someone qualified as a marriage guidance counsellor. The party having taken the initiating step, the counsellor can discuss what might be done in each individual case.

That is all that the amendment seeks. It would create a step between an information session and the statement of divorce. Knowing how things are, I suspect that the Minister will say that it is not practical. If he does, it will be incumbent on us to devise a mechanism that is practical. The Minister may suggest that we should examine again the content of the information meeting, but we believe that that meeting—or a meeting, however it is arrived at—should be generally available.

In November 1994, the National Association of Family Mediation and Conciliation Services laid down a number of criteria for what constitutes effective information meetings. The association's criteria were:

"a down-to-earth understanding of people's reactions to marriage crises and a trained ability to respond to them constructively…sensitivity and skill on the part of the interviewer as to where the marriage has reached—is it an early or late stage of dissolution. Timing and sensitivity are crucial as people are in a raw emotional state".

Another criterion laid down by National Family Mediation is

"the competence to identify domestic violence".

Opposition Members will remember how my hon. Friends and I voted on that issue this morning: the wider public will remember how a number of Conservative Members voted. National Family Mediation says that those entrusted with and exercising a function under the Bill should bear in mind and have the competence to identify domestic violence. That is precisely what we told the Minister this morning, yet Conservative Members rejected our amendment.

National Family Mediation give other criteria:

"local information, well presented … a sound and practical understanding of the legal process … a sound and practical understanding of children's reactions to marriage breakdown".

All those criteria cry out for a qualified person to be available. That is why the amendment stipulates that meetings should be available with a qualified marriage guidance counsellor—someone whose training and qualifications are approved by the Lord Chancellor. A way should be found to enable such meetings to take place and to give people the opportunity to participate in them. That may save some marriages. The importance of universal availability is that even when such a meeting does not save a marriage, it will ease the process and reduce the negative impact on the children and the parties themselves—an impact that will probably be with many if not all of those children in one way or another for the rest of their lives.

We have a duty to adopt a practical proposal to help to deal with the real worries of those engaged in the day-to-day work of saving marriages—people who see the benefit of saving marraiges. Our duty is to produce a practical mechanism by which that might be achieved.

It is important that I structure my speech so as to explain to the Committee how some of us who have taken a close interest in the matter have been trying to achieve consensus around the Committee. That would not constitute what I consider to be a serious information meeting.

Even if a serious information meeting were to be properly held—without videos who would hold it? Would it be someone who had a stake in the divorce process, such as a divorce lawyer or a mediator?

We are creating a new career structure. I have been told of family lawyers currently engaged in firms of solicitors who will become mediators. Like many other people, I am worried about whether information would be held by people with a financial stake in the process—I am not saying that that is improper—who would not go out of their way, any more than a solicitor does now, to encourage people to engage in marriage counselling.

I believe that it would be best to retain the compulsory information meeting, but that that meeting should be taken by a qualified and genuinely independent person. The Minister should decide, through regulations, who that person should be. We cannot decide that, although I have tabled an amendment that would ensure that the person was independent and qualified—for example, a registrar of births, deaths and marriages.

The information meeting should be held on a one-to-one basis. One would not attend it with a group of people, but singly or as a couple, although I accept that we cannot force people to go as a couple. At the meeting, people should be given the opportunity, and be encouraged—not compelled—to undergo marriage counselling.
