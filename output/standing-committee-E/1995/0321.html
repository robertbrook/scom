TRANSFER OF RESIDUAL LIABILITIES: ENGLAND AND WALES

(Rother Valley)

Mr. Barron
: First, Mr. O'Hara, I welcome you to the Committee. You will be pleased to hear that your attendance here is likely to be shortlived as we do not expect the Committee to have many more sittings.

The amendments question the fundamental purpose behind the Bill. They are probing amendments, in which we seek to identify exactly what a liability might be. The Bill seeks to amend the duties of the Secretaries of State for Health and for Scotland so that they must exercise their statutory powers to transfer the liabilities of health service bodies that cease to exist.

I shall return in a few moments to the question of why the Government are seeking to guarantee such liabilities, but first I ask the Committee to examine closely the wording of clauses 1 and 2. The clauses provide that the Secretary of State, when exercising his powers, must

"transfer property, rights and liabilities… so as to secure that all of its liabilities are dealt with"

I should like to know why that wording is used. The argument used by the Secretary of State and the Minister on Second Reading was that it was necessary 40 to tighten the wording of the National Health Service and Community Care Act 1990 because it was open to misinterpretation. The Act also left doubt about what would happen when a health service body no longer existed. We have already considered what it means when such a body "ceases to exist", in the words of the Bill. We have not been satisfied with the Minister's responses on that point, and we hope that we shall receive a better answer at a later stage.

Nevertheless, the Minister claimed on Second Reading that the tightening of the law was essential and that taking away the Secretary of State's discretion to transfer the liabilities was absolutely vital. Conservative Members claimed that there was a loophole and that the Bill would plug it. Hon. Members should read the Bill carefully. We should like the Minister to explain what will happen to each of the Secretary of State's discretionary powers. The Committee will particularly want to know what will happen to the Secretary of State's powers in respect of the property and rights of former health service bodies. If there is a loophole in the powers regarding liabilities, does not that mean that the same loophole exists with regard to rights and properties? If so, should not that also be plugged?

The answer is made plain in the notes on clauses which accompanied the Bill on Second Reading, although I accept that they do not constitute a statutory document. Note 8 says:

"The property and rights will remain subject to the Secretary of State's discretion to transfer"—

the note goes on to limit that discretion—

"to the extent that they need to be transferred".

I wonder why that is. The Government were given a brief to produce what is effectively a cast iron bale-out clause to the private sector and any private finance initiatives. We must ask whether that is what the Government have done in the Bill and whether they still have discretion regarding rights and properties.

We have already considered the £5 billion that the Government have landed on the taxpayers' collective doorstep as a result of the risk-free contracting in the new health service private finance initiative. I should like us to consider the property rights that have not been transferred. As I said on Second Reading, in the worst case the private contractor could contract with a trust to build for free a new health-care facility, if he wanted to. A few cost overruns dropped in along the way could recoup the contractor's money and perhaps bankrupt the trust in the process. The Secretary of State is certain to pick up the tab. The Bill was introduced to underwrite such cases.

Let us consider what may happen if a contractor builds a useless facility. That is not beyond the bounds of possibility, as the hon. Member for Southwark and Bermondsey (Mr. Hughes) can testify all too clearly. The Guy's hospital phase 3 building is three years behind schedule and five times over cost—18,000 variations have been made since the planning stage. In addition, an unknown bug is eating away at its copper JPEG 41 piping, £10 million worth of post-contractual changes have been needed and £500,000 was spent on legal bills in one year alone. The project director Mr. Graham Perry, described the phase 3 development as a "nightmare". In fact, it was so disastrous that, before his ennoblement by the Prime Minister, Sir Philip Harris, had demanded his £6 million back and refused to let the trust name the building after him.

The Philip Harris house saga holds an important lesson from which members of the Committee should learn. The building will never be used for its intended purpose. It may not be a white elephant, but it certainly is a white mouse. In an interview with the Health Services Journal on 2 November 1995, the project manager, Mr. Graham Perry, said:

"The initial 'Approval in Principal' was unreal, but politically acceptable. It may be that at the moment some people have no choice but to play the game. If they had put the big figure first, the project wouldn't have happened."

On Second Reading, the Secretary of State rejected my assertion that private contractors would underbid to secure work, but Mr. Perry clearly supports my view.

In the interview, Mr. Perry also refers to

"a lack of clarity, definition and control. The original sponsor was a new general manager with a purely medical background who had no contracting or construction experience whatsoever."

He adds that the manager's role was

"frequently passed across to a number of similarly inexperienced and inappropriate individuals."

He states that the whole system made for

"a dubious client control base."

The Guy's case provides a salutary example of what may happen. The Secretary of State may be left with property or rights that are as much liabilities as anything else, but he will have no obligation to transfer them.

I read Mr. Perry's interview soon after taking over the Opposition's health portfolio. It puts into perspective the fragmentation that is taking place within the health service and the responsibilities that are being given to individual trusts. By that, I do not refer to the responsibility for the management of hospitals that has been given to hospital managers. I clearly support that, and I hope that all members of the Committee do too. I refer to the added responsibilities that have been given to managers. For example, they have had to learn how to negotiate salaries at a local level for the first time. In addition, clinicians have suddenly been given managerial status inside the trusts and they have to take decisions on major projects. At one time, such decisions would have been taken at a regional level by people professional in that role. Clinicians now have to take those decisions and their professional status means that, in certain circumstances, they are inadequately trained to do so.

We must consider what a liability constitutes. I hope that Guy's will be able to pull through its present problems, but in such cases a trust may go out of 42 business. In the case of Guy's, responsibility lies with a number of players involved in the contract, but we must decide whether that project constitutes a liability. If it is not deemed to be a liability under the Bill, the private sector and the banks will like that one bit. They have clearly arm twisted the Government into effectively gold plating liabilities, but they will wonder whether they have covered them all. Discretion will remain in terms of property and rights.

Will the Minister tell us whether there will be empty or part-finished PFI projects because the Secretary of State has decided not to transfer them? Or, in the case of rights, will new medical techniques be unused because the Secretary of State had decided to keep them in limbo?

Has the Minister thought the question of the can of worms that the Bill is opening with regard to underwriting liabilities without discretion, whatever "liabilities" means as opposed to leaving discretion in properties and rights? Either the Secretary of State has discretion or he does not. This area, which has been left in limbo, should be cleared up.

The more I read the Bill, the more it appears to be a lawyer's charter and a last-minute, rushed and panic measure. If the Bill goes unamended and unexplained, is there not likely to be another round of legal wrangling as well as further discussion of the words we debated in the first sitting, "ceases to exist"? I think that there might well be and that lawyers will once again be rubbing their hands over consideration what the transfer of properties, rights and liabilities means and what discretion lies where.

The courts will want to know what constitutes property, rights and liabilities. None of those terms is explained in the Bill and no attempt has been made to provide a schedule detailing which is which. All three remain vague and undefined. Again, I submit that our learned friends could have a field day.

The Minister must tell the Committee in clear and unambiguous terms what is meant by a liability; whether property or rights will be classed as liabilities in future; and what measures he will put in place to ensure that he does not leave the Department open to endless litigation in the event of a health service body ceasing to exist.

I hope that the Minister is able to address my concerns and to explain in detail why the loss of discretion on liabilities still remains in property and rights and what that will mean in practice when interaction takes place between the public sector and private finance, the risks run by which he purports to want to underwrite by bringing the Bill before Parliament.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Health (Mr. John Horam)
: First, I thank the hon. Members for Rother Valley (Mr. Barron) for moving the amendment constructively. He seeks clarification, which is reasonable at this stage.

The hon. Gentleman asked what, exactly, we mean by liabilities. He sought to explore the distinction JPEG 43 between liabilities and property and rights. The first distinction is that property and rights are usually considered assets, not a liability, and, therefore, can be treated differently from a liability. A liability is usually an organisation's contractual obligation. On Second Reading, the Secretary of State used the example of a contract for the supply of potatoes.

An organisation might have contracts with all manner of people to supply services to the hospital trust. It might have liabilities which stretch into the future, obliging it to buy those services for some time. Those would be liabilities for an organisation in the normal commercial sense—they would have been entered into with another commercial party and the organisation would be honour-bound to fulfil them.

Mr. Barron
: Let us suppose that under the PFI, somebody took over the design, building and operation of a hospital. We understand that that would be a liability if the contract was to run for a number of years. Let us also suppose that the hospital ceases to exist, for whatever reason. If the purchaser—fundholders, if they still exist, or the general practitioner commissioning system, if that becomes the norm—decides to go through another hospital, will such a liability, contracted into under the PFI, be underwritten even if patients are not sent to that hospital for treatment because they get a better deal at a neighbouring hospital?

Mr. Horam
: No. I am saying that at the beginning of that process, when a PFI arrangement is entered into, there will be contracts governing who does what. The private consortium may contract with the hospital trust to provide certain facilities such as management. If the trust, for whatever reason, is unable to meets its obligations, it will still have to honour those contracts. It should not be allowed to walk away from contracts that have been reasonably entered into. As my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Health said on Second Reading, the public sector should not be allowed to behave like Barlow Clowes and simply walk away from a liability which has been properly entered into.

Mr. Barron
: Local purchasers might decide not to purchase at St. Thomas's ear, nose and throat department, for example—if St. Thomas's has such a department, that is—but to go five miles down the road. The contract to manage that department could run for 15 years, but if it was no longer functioning after eight years because the purchasers had decided to purchase elsewhere, would the contract be honoured, although people were going to another hospital department for treatment?

Mr. Horam
: No. That would depend on the contract. If the organisation does not exist, its liability is taken over by another organisation—a trust, or whatever the Bill specifies. Contracts properly entered into by the original trust will be honoured. If, however, an organisation cannot meet its ordinary obligations, 44 the contract entered into originally will apply. That contract might specify that there will be a break point, for example, in the facility's management. After two years, the facility's management may cease unless the contract provides that it shall carry on. That is a break point, which limits the contract. It is a question not of having an open-ended obligation but of meeting the contractual obligations that the original trust entered into—no more, no less.

The hon. Gentleman spoke about Guy's hospital phase 3, and the overrun of costs. That is an interesting example. It happened under existing conventional public finance. The point about the private finance initiative is that when a trust—St. Thomas's and Guy's trust in this instance—entered into a building arrangement with a private contractor, it would transfer part of the risk to the contractor, saying, "You will erect this building for £56 million. In so far as you exceed that cost, you take the risk." In practice, the risk taken by the public sector and the risk to the taxpayer will be less than it would be under the existing arrangements.

Chelsea and Westminster is one of a number of hospital building contracts that has been conventionally financed through the public sector. When problems arose, the taxpayer had to pick up the Bill in an open-ended way and bear the cost of finishing the project, whatever it was. That risk would be limited under the PFI by the original contract. Does the hon. Gentlemen understand my point?

Mr. Barron
: I do not agree with the Minister. It is because of the overruns and everything else that goes with the PFI that we are in Committee now. Where there have been overruns—and there have been many in the past—the state has renegotiated contracts anyway. Is the Minister saying that that would never happen in future?

Mr. Horam
: I am saying that in a privately financed contract of the sort that we envisage, contracts will be—and are already being—entered into that define the responsibilities and liabilities of the two parties. That will mean that the risk to the public sector—to the taxpayer—will be limited by the contract between the trust and the private supplier. That is a huge benefit. In the case of Guy's it might have meant all the overrun being taken on by the private builder who contracted to build Guy's phase 3 or provide the facilities for it. At the moment the public sector has to pick up the bill for the overrun.

In other words, the trust needs a contract with the private sector developer.

Mr. Horam

Mr. Hughes

Mr. Horam
: There is a contract now, of course, where the public sector buys services such as construction of a building from a private constructor, and that is, of course, subject to the usual commercial rules. However, the point of the PFI is that a consortium is formed not only to build or supply a particular service to a hospital, but to build, and supply facilities to, a major part of the hospital. It contracts to do so for a certain sum over a certain period. If it exceeds the cost, it bears the risk. That does not happen in the case of Chelsea and Westminster and it will not happen in the case of Guy's and St. Thomas's, where, in the end, the taxpayer, through the public sector—the NHS trust —will have to make up for things going wrong, costs being overrun, and the lack of a complete hospital for the original price.

The point of PFI is the transfer of risk from the public to the private sector. There would be no point in private finance arrangements if that were not so, because public money is cheaper than private money and to that extent there would be an advantage in wholly financing the new hospital from public funds. However, private finance gives a certainty about the cost to the taxpayer that cannot be obtained from the existing, conventional arrangements.

Mr. Simon Hughes
: I am conscious of your earlier concern about lengthy interventions, Mr. O'Hara, and I shall make only one point in explaining why the Minister is incorrect.

Under present arrangements, any hospital can enter into a contract that limits its absolute liability, 46 stipulating that it will pay the contractor no more than, say, £50 million, £100 million or £150 million, and that any cost above that will be the responsibility of the contractor. There is no difference if the entire contract for building and running is transferred. Everything depends on the terms of the contract. That is not the advantage of PFI. That is where the Government's view is flawed. Its advantage is, perhaps, the possibility of washing one's hands of management responsibility. There is no theoretical or necessary difference, however, between the contractual liabilities of NHS trusts in one arrangement or the other.

Mr. Horam
: There is, because the consortium in a PFI contract will, precisely, involve management as well as construction. I have spoken about supply of facilities. That is management. If, for example, the expected revenue is not received, the private consortium will bear the risk. That is the point of the PFI. It is a combination. To put it another way, it is not a matter, as it is at present, of build and forget. At present, a building is built and the builder goes away and forgets about costs and liabilities. Perhaps costs are greater than were originally foreseen, and the difference is borne by the taxpayer. Under PFI, the contractor will construct the building and be involved in the continuing management of its supplies and maintenance. Therefore, in so far as costs and revenue do not work out as planned, he will bear the risk.

Mr. Hughes
: I accept that continuing management costs would be transferred to a contractor from hospital trusts. But there would be no management costs for any of the relevant hospitals—management of the blessed things would not have started. Construction costs, however, could be protected under PFI entirely, as they are under the present system.

Mr. Horam
: The hon. Gentleman is wrong because in practice it is not possible totally to separate the management of a building from its construction. It may have happened in the past, with, in some instances, calamitous results. It is better to combine the two. Those who both build and maintain a building will take care about design and construction and about what will be necessary to maintain it. There will be a responsibility and incentive to build so as to minimise maintenance costs. There is a connection, and the hon. Gentleman is wrong to claim otherwise on the basis that management of the building does not begin until it is finished. Of course it does not, but none the less all aspects of the building should be thought about at the design stage.

The hon. Member for Rother Valley was speaking about liabilities. Liabilities, as I have said, are determined by contracts properly entered into by a trust and the private sector. The point of the Bill is that the public sector should not be able to walk away from such liabilities.

To make my point clear I shall give an example showing why it is necessary to retain a certain discretion over property and rights, and why the clause JPEG 47 is phrased as it is. Two trusts that are combined to form a new one, and therefore cease to exist, or are dissolved, to use the term that the hon. Gentleman prefers, may have owned a parcel of land that could be sold to raise money for the new trust, or for the NHS as a whole. It might be decided to do that, both in the general interest of keeping down the running costs of the new trust, which would thus not take on any debts associated with the land as part of its overheads, and because the trust does not need the land. The trust and the NHS would benefit from the land, the sale of which could be put towards new hospital building.

It would be wrong if the Bill provided that in such a case property had to be transferred with liabilities. That would be the effect of the amendment tabled by the Hon. Member for Rother Valley. it would make it necessary to transfer property and liabilities. In certain circumstances, which we cannot exactly envisage but which might well occur, it would be a mistake to do that, from the point of view of the economy of running the trust that was being set up. There might be, in certain circumstances, a difference between property and liabilities. Usually, property would be inescapably linked to liabilities—the whole lot could be transferred in one go, and that would usually happen—but there are circumstances in which that is not advisable. Therefore, the Bill is worded as it is.

Only the liabilities have to be dealt with; that is sufficient for our purposes—we do not need any more than that. If we tried further to constrain the Secretary of State, the Government or the national health service in the way that the amendment proposes, that might be to the disadvantage of particular trusts. We do not want that to happen. I understand why the hon. Gentleman seeks clarification of why there might be a distinction between property and liability. We have explored that, but the amendment might make managing the estate of the NHS more difficult.

(York)

That is increased flexibility. The hon. Gentleman was right to say that we need increased flexibility, and that is one reason why we have introduced the PFI.

Mr. Bayley

Mr. Horam
