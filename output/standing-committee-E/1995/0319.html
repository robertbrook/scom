10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary for Health (Mr. John Horam)
: I beg to move,

That during the proceedings on the National Health Service (Residual Liabilities) Bill the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock.

I welcome you, Mr. Hill, to the Chair of this Committee on our modest Bill. I understand that you have recently completed the proceedings on the Finance Bill, which must have been rather more indigestible.

The Chairman
: This Bill may be a reward.

Mr. Horam
: I hope that it is, Mr. Hill, and that we can serve up a modest dessert to follow your long main course on the Finance Bill. I am sure that my hon. Friends wish that to be the case and that the Opposition also feel that a modest dessert would be in order for you and your co-Chairman, Mr. O'Hara, whom we also welcome. I am sure that we shall make progress under your Chairmanship.

Mr. Kevin Barron
(Rother Valley)
: I also offer my thanks and hope that the dessert does not take too long, Mr. Hill, as you have sat through the proceedings on the Finance Bill.

I am surprised that the Minister has brought forward the proceedings in Committee so quickly. When I first came into the House of Commons, it was normal for there to be two weeks between the Second Reading and consideration in Committee. That procedure has been greatly truncated in recent weeks.

The sittings motion proposes that we sit both on Tuesday and Thursday mornings. I have had discussions with my hon. Friends and, although I do not know what the Minister's diary contains, many hon. Members on both sides of the Committee have events in their diaries to which they have been committed for a long time. Clearly, sitting on two days a week will interfere with those commitments. The Minister might reconsider and agree that, in the circumstances, the Committee might sit on Tuesday mornings only.

The Minister said that the Bill is modest, but we think it an interesting one. It does not stand on its own; statutory instruments that might be affected by the Bill are on the Order Paper almost daily. Indeed, there were two such instruments yesterday. We should like to consider the Bill in the context of other legislation that is being introduced into Parliament.

On that basis, I believe that it would be better if we sat on Tuesday mornings only until we are able to 4 reorganise our diaries or until we ascertain whether we need to sit for a long period, as sittings motion suggests, to consider a Bill that the Minister seems not to deem important. Perhaps the Minister will give us his views on the suggestion that we should sit only on Tuesday mornings.

Mr. Horam
: I had been under the impression given by the Labour party—that Opposition Members attached almost as much importance to the national health service as we do. I am surprised that Opposition Members' diary appointments are more important than the NHS. That is not the position among Conservative Members. We will break every diary commitment to be here to discuss the Bill because we attach great importance to it and do not in any way treat it lightly.

If there is a wish to organise the sittings differently, I am prepared to listen to what is said. If talks are held through the usual channels during the morning, we could reorganise the sittings. If my hon. Friend the Member for Croydon, South (Mr. Ottaway), the Government Whip, feels, after discussions, that progress can be made, I am not determined that we will sit on Thursday mornings as well as on Tuesdays. I must insist however, that the sittings motion is passed, until other arrangements can be made.

As Members, our job is to seek out information and to put it into the public arena so that the public can see the financial state of public services and how they are run. Officials and Ministers responsible for institutions such as the national health service should be properly accountable. Therefore, it is right that we should have the true picture of the debts faced by trusts and health authorities.

Conservative Members regularly say on the Floor of the House that they speak for business and for the private sector. I doubt whether that was true of their approach to the Asylum and Immigration Bill, in which the Government imposed a burden on small businesses. Those businesses will have to act as immigration police, but I am straying from the Bill by discussing that.

In this instance, I want to speak for private business. It has a right to information about the debts of health authorities and trusts. Before private business decides whether to invest in capital projects, it must have such information. It is no good selling the private sector a pig in the poke by asking it to invest money without it having the proper information. One of the Opposition's fears, however, is that the private sector will end up owning national health service hospitals and turning them into private hospitals. No doubt, we shall discuss that later in our proceedings. Nevertheless, private businesses have the right to know that the trusts with which they will 6 deal will not go broke as a result of severe debts.

I am not concerned about sitting on Tuesday and Thursday mornings. I would be quite happy to do that. Although the Bill is limited in its scope, we might have the opportunity to discuss many of the issues relating to the NHS and to debate private finance initiatives. I am worried, however, by what is being covered up or is potentially being covered up, and that clearly involves the problem of debt.

Before we agree to the sittings motion, I hope that the Minister will give us some idea of the true level of debt faced by the trusts. By that, I mean not the debt detailed in the statutory accounts—that is only one guide—but the debts revealed in the management accounts. As I said, in my health authority there is a difference of £8 million between the two sets of accounts. The private sector has as much right as the public sector to know the true picture. I hope that the Minister will provide information that will help to guide our debates on this important matter.
