Mrs. Angela Knight: I beg to move amendment No. 278, in page 215, line 29, leave out

"The Treasury may by order provide,".

The Chairman: With this we may take Government amendments Nos. 279 to 280, 312, 281 to 285, 313 to 315, and 317.

Mrs. Knight: The schedule is all about how to bring the taxation of collective investment schemes—including offshore ones—into the new rules. The amendments fall neatly into three batches, although they are all grouped together. I shall be brief.

Amendments Nos. 278 to 280 are the first group. Existing anti-avoidance legislation is designed to discourage investment in offshore funds purely to avoid or defer United Kingdom tax. The special rules do not apply when a fund distributes all or most of its income. To apply the test of distributing fund status, a rule is necessary to specify how distributable income is to be measured. Hitherto, the test was applied with reference to United Kingdom equivalent profits for tax purposes calculated as if the offshore fund were United Kingdom resident. That continues, but the amendment would make the calculation apply as if the offshore fund were an authorised unit trust rather than an investment trust.

Col. 674 Amendments Nos. 281 to 285 are about providing correct definitions of offshore funds. Alternative definitions exist relating to life insurance. They are satisfactory but only in that specific context. They have caught innocent situations when non-resident subsidiary companies hold debt for the purpose of their trade rather than for tax-avoidance motives. We have therefore amended the definitions accordingly.

Amendments Nos. 312 to 317 are the third set. They are technical amendments proposed by the Association of British Insurers. They would deal with the consequences when unit trusts or offshore funds move in or out of special rules for debt heavy funds.

Mrs. Angela Knight: The best way to answer the hon. Member for North Warwickshire (Mr. O'Brien) is briefly to explain the clause and schedule. The issues relate to how an offshore fund calculates its United Kingdom equivalent profits if it wishes to be a distributing fund—that is, if it distributes more than 85 per cent. of its United Kingdom equivalent profits.

We introduced amendments to enable, in certain instances, this aspect of equivalence to be calculated with respect to unit trusts rather than investment trusts. The unit trust method of calculation fitted rather better with the offshore funds than did the investment trust method. Investment trusts have moved towards an accounting practice known as SORP—a statement of recommended practice. Unit trusts have not yet moved towards a statement of recommended practice, but we anticipate that they will do so. Indeed, the unit trust industry is in the throes of sorting out a SORP for unit trusts.

That left us with two options: one was to rule out any changes that the unit trust industry might introduce, and the other was to provide a power so that it could be accommodated when its statement of recommended practice was resolved. That is some time off, but I cannot tell the hon. Member for North Warwickshire whether it will be in six months, a year, 18 months, two years or even longer than that. It will fit in with the new rules, however, in the same way as other collective investment schemes do—by tinkering to the right and tinkering to the left to make it fit as well as possible. We have taken the power to ensure that when and if the unit trust industry comes up with Image Col. 677 that particular accounting practice, we can accommodate it.

Clearly, the distribution of offshore funds is a different kettle of fish. The company's position is understood. We have tabled amendments to the legislation on offshore funds in response to discussions with the industry. The amendments do no more than meet particular and specific requirements.

The hon. Gentleman asked about non-qualifying investments. Securities mean bonds and debt instruments. The definition is no wider than that for direct holdings of loans. It reflects the definition in other parts of the Bill. The issue is the so-called 60 per cent. rule: whether an offshore fund has 60 per cent. or more of its investment in debt securities. If it does, it falls into one regime for corporate investors; if it does not, it is in the other regime. Put simply, if an offshore fund has more than 60 per cent. of its holdings in debt securities, the companies that invest in it will be considered under the tax regime as if they held that debt directly. If it has less than 60 per cent., they will not. That is one of the key issues that was addressed by the previous set of amendments. The provision is a response, not only to industry requests but to current industry practice.
