Family Law Bill [Lords]

(Except clauses 5 and 7)

clause Clause 1
title THE GENERAL PRINCIPLES UNDERLYING PARTS II AND III

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 30, in page 1, line 22, at end add—

"(iv) with due regard to the need to avoid the risk of violence being directed at either or both parties or any children affected".—[Mr. Boateng.]

4.30 pm

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

Ms Margaret Hodge (Barking): I started this morning by arguing how important it is that the amendment be accepted as a central part of the Bill. It would ensure that domestic violence is at its heart. Those of us who are concerned about such issues are anxious that an understanding should be reached in Committee about domestic violence.

The hon. Member for Batley and Spen (Mrs. Peacock) referred this morning to the number of people who issue injunctions about domestic violence and who then return to their spouses. That is right; about half of those who issue injunctions do so. We should not be happy about that. It is not that we do not want marriages to be preserved if they can be saved, but the behaviour that led to an injunction is unacceptable.

If our anxiety about domestic violence is placed at the heart of the Bill, we can then think about what action can be taken, especially in relation to concilation, to avoid domestic violence recurring. It is not good enough to say that, just because half of those who have issued injunctions return to their spouses, the matter should not be of concern to us. It makes it more important that domestic violence is the central issue; and it is why we should discuss what action must be taken to prevent it from recurring.

I was worried by some of this morning's contributions, which suggested that it might be better to keep families together when domestic violence had occurred, because if those responsible for it entered new relationships they were likely to take their violent approach with them. Such an attitude is deeply worrying. It is not an answer for those who are locked into violent relationships. Surely the solution is to decide what action to take to tackle the causes of domestic violence, so that people are not locked into relationships in which violence is a common part of their day-to-day life. The way this morning's debate was going it extremely worrying. It is not good enough Col. 72 for us simply to say that those who suffer domestic violence should stay with their partners because that violence would be repeated in a new relationship.

The domestic violence statistics are staggering; my hon. Friend the Member for Bristol, East (Ms Corston) referred to some of them earlier. The British Crime Survey referred to 500,000 incidents of domestic violence each year. It is worth reiterating that nine out of 10 of those incidents are against women. It was suggested this morning that women's equality was encouraging equality of attack, so to speak, in that women were now attacking men as much as women were being attacked by men. Well, the facts do not bear that out.

Mr. Julian Brazier (Canterbury): I am not sure whether the hon. Lady was referring just now to what I said this morning. I firmly believe that the largest proportion of domestic violence takes place against children, most of it unrecorded. It is significant that survey after survey has shown that children are vastly more likely to be abused—factors of betwen eight and 30 are cited by various surveys—by a new man in a relationship than by their natural fathers.

Ms Hodge: Clearly, we are all worried about the abuse of children. However, domestic violence refers mainly to violence between married partners.

Mr. Brazier: Why?

Ms Hodge: A different definition applies to child abuse. The physical and psychological damage to children is in a different arena. The hon. Gentleman may be right to argue that a man who is violent against his wife may also be violent against his children. However, the purpose of the amendment is to draw attention to the terrible and increasing incidence of partners in a marriage being violent against each other. There is also a growing incidence of child abuse; that too is dreadful and we need to tackle it. It is, in some ways, connected with the matter that we are discussing; but it is not to do with the main thrust of our argument; except that those who are violent against their spouses—mainly men—may also be violent against their children. The hon. Gentleman suggested that stepfathers tend to abuse their step-children. We need to tackle that—it is a serious problem—but it is not an argument for locking people into relationships where domestic violence occurs.

Mrs. Barbara Roche (Hornsey and Wood Green): In relation to the comments of the hon. Member for Canterbury (Mr. Brazier), would not my hon. Friend agree that one of the worst problems is the effect that violence has on the children—violence often directed by the father against the mother? Unfortunately that pattern of disrespect increases, starting a circle of violence that is tragically reproduced in the relationships formed by the children in later life.

Ms Hodge: I completely agree. I would have come to that point later in my speech, so I am grateful to her for having mentioned it.

It is worth reflecting on the statistics. The figures show that of the 120 women who were murdered in Image Col. 73 1991, 41 per cent. were killed by their partners. One in five of all murder victims are women killed by a partner or an ex-partner. Murder is the most serious of crimes. If we are worried about anything, it must be about that. It is another reason for making our concerns about domestic violence central to our consideration of family law. Indeed, the evidence suggests that our knowledge of domestic violence—as much of it as is reported—is only the tip of a much greater iceberg. The Association of Chief Police Officers—[Interruption.]

The Chairman: Order. If members of the Committee need to consult about the business of the Committee, I should be grateful if they did so outside the Room and not disrupt the debate.

Ms Hodge: The Association of Chief Police Officers said in evidence to the Select Committee on Home Affairs that domestic violence was "deep-rooted and widespread" and that known domestic violence was only the tip of the iceberg. ACPO said that the estimated extent of the problem was not based on reliable or accurate data.

We have heard that as little as 2 per cent. of domestic violence is reported. We also know that 20 per cent. of those who approach Shelter because of homelessness have suffered domestic violence. A high proportion of families are separating directly as a result of violence in the home.

It is ironic that women tend to be the most fearful of crime—for example, I will not walk far from my home on my own at night—and yet a woman is four times more likely to be subject to violence in the home than she is to be subject to violence in the street. That is an horrific figure, and it ought to suggest to us the importance of keeping the issue of domestic violence central to our consideration of the Bill.

The other reasons why I think the issue important relate to who the victims of domestic violence are. Again, the best available data come from the British Crime Survey. Victims tend to be younger women—I do not know whether that is surprising, but it is depressing. Poorer women are more likely to be subject to domestic violence than those in higher income brackets. Victims tend to be women living in council homes and women from lower socio-economic groups.

We have heard a lot about the desire to promote marriage. The women whom I have described are the most vulnerable to having their marriages dissolved. If we make that matter central to our concerns, we may go some way to ensuring that the proper structures and frameworks are in place to support marriages, rather than allowing them to disintegrate.

As we consider the amendment and parts II and III of the Bill, we also ought to consider the fact that conviction rates for domestic violence are abysmally low. Only 14 per cent. of reported cases—we must remember that reported cases are only the tip of the iceberg—lead to an arrest. Even fewer cases are prosecuted, and even where there is a prosecution, it tends to result in the perpetrator being either fined or bound over to keep the peace. That perhaps demonstrates a lack of understanding of how horrific it Col. 74 is to be the victim of domestic violence, and of the impact that it can have on people's lives.

I am concerned that the available data in the United Kingdom are so poor. We simply do not know how wide spread domestic violence is. I have been referring to various suppositions: evidence from the ACPO, suppositions that only 2 per cent. of cases are reported, and so on. The Canadian experience is worrying. Canada has made a more systematic attempt to collect the relevant data for well over 10 years—I think that work began in 1983. A national investigation has been undertaken to discover the extent of domestic violence. The results are horrific, and demonstrate why the issue should be central to our considerations.

Mrs. Roche: My hon. Friend will be interested to know that what she says about the data is borne out by the report of the Select Committee on Home Affairs, which says:

"We recommend that the Home Office upgrade the quality of national statistics on domestic violence which come to the attention of the criminal justice system."

So Members of Parliament across the political divide support my hon. Friend's argument.

Ms Hodge: I agree with my hon. Friend that improved data might develop people's understanding of the problems, which might lead to a greater understanding of why the Opposition believe that such matters must be central to the consideration of the Bill.

Canadian data have shown some horrific facts—for example, half of all women had experienced rape or attempted rape after the age of 16 and one in four had experienced violence from a current partner or former partner. That is an enormous problem. I was shocked when I considered my circle of friends and whether people to whom one feels close are revealing data about their relationships with their partners. We need proper data if we are to consider the impact of domestic violence on families. I was delighted that the Select Committee on Home Affairs agreed with that when it considered the matter.

4.45 pm

We should also be concerned about the fact that domestic violence has an impact on a range of services. We know that women who suffer domestic violence are more likely to make a claim on the health service, for example. Extensive reseach shows that 40 per cent. of abused women find difficulty in sleeping and 46 per cent. feel depressed and lose confidence; therefore, they are likely to seek medical assistance. One study carried out in 1984–12 years ago, but it contained the most recent data that I could find—showed that 80 per cent. of abused women seek medical help at least once and that 40 per cent. sought medical help on a minimum of five separate occasions.

If we are to ensure that domestic violence does not destroy families—this is not only an issue of maintaining the family, although that is important—we must consider how to prevent the growth of his horrible crime, which individuals commit on those whom they are supposed to love. If we can prevent such violence from happening, we can have an impact on other public services.

Image Col. 75 Members of the Committee have stated that children must be central to our considerations. Some children experience terrible domestic abuse. Two out of three of the women who go to refuges to escape domestic violence have children. Probably all hon. Members have women's refuges in their constituencies and I hope that most visited them before sitting on this Committee. It is always heart rending to talk to women in those refuges and see the impact on their children. As a London Member, I know that many of those women come from parts of London.

Mr. Andrew Rowe (Mid-Kent): I do not want to undermine the course of the hon. Lady's argument, which is especially powerful and important in relation to the usefulness of women's refuges. However, the other day I was appalled to discover that a woman in my constituency took her children to a refuge, and was admitted, entirely as a ploy to strengthen her case for taking custody of the children. In fact, she was moving straight from the refuge to a home that her lover had set up for her.

Ms Hodge: I cannot comment on that particular instance. Most of us who have experience of seeing in our surgeries people who have been subjected to domestic violence, or of visiting women who have sought refuge in women's refuges, know that, on the whole, those women have suffered in terrible trauma. I hope that the case that the hon. Gentleman refers to is the exception that proves the rule. I should hate to think that we make judgments on the basis of the abuse of abuse, so to speak, which seems to have happened in that case.

I reiterate what my hon. Friend the Member for Bristol, East said this morning. Two thirds of children living in violent relationships have themselves witnessed the violence, which clearly has an impact on how they will grow up, the sort of relationships they will form and the sort of marriages they will enter.

I welcome part IV of the Bill, which deals with some of the legal issues associated with family law and violence. We should welcome the fact that it reinstates the Family Homes and Domestic Violence Bill. I welcome the fact that it will implement some of the Law Commission's recommendations.

The purpose of our amendment, however, is to make the avoidance of domestic violence an integral part of the Bill. Its inclusion in clause 1 will ensure that it is a matter of central concern both to us during our deliberations and to those who will administer the Bill when it is enacted—people in conciliation, mediation or information meetings and the lawyers and judges who will be involved in the process of divorce, should that become necessary.

The example cited by the hon. Member for Mid-Kent (Mr. Rowe) suggests that there may be cases in which a woman leaves the violent home easily or enthusiastically to gain something else. However, the decision to leave the home is incredibly difficult for all those involved, especially as these people tend to be poor. They tend to be locked into council housing and to have less flexibility and fewer choices open to them. We must recognise that people who have reached the stage at which they flee the home because of domestic Col. 76 violence—and therefore embark on the path to divorce—are, in most cases, acting in desperation. It is a difficult decision.

Such people are escaping not from poverty, but to poverty. Those of us who are trying to sort out social security benefits for women in refuges have great experience of that. Those women have to cope with children on their own, rather than with their partner, which causes enormous stress. They will probably have had to move from the area where they had friends and a network of support, which imposes additional stress. They may also have had to move the children from their schools, which affects the children.

Those women move into homelessness. There are a growing number of cases in my constituency in which women get locked into the refuge system because local authorities have less and less accommodation to rehouse people fleeing from domestic violence. Some women also lose their jobs because, in moving from their homes and areas and having to take sole responsibility for the children, they cannot cope with work. That locks them into a further cycle of increasing poverty. It is because of that that so many go back, even after they have obtained an injunction against their husband.

We should also remember that those who suffer from domestic violence are beaten down psychologically, not just physically. Their self-esteem is probably at rock bottom, so their ability to deal with many issues in their relationship is severely curtailed. When we consider matters such as conciliation, mediation or attendance at the information meetings, we must be conscious of the psychological state of women who suffer domestic violence. We must ensure a fair system that does not disadvantage them when they try to disentangle themselves from an unhappy relationship or, if there is any hope, when they try to bring the marriage together.

Mrs. Teresa Gorman (Billericay): I entirely sympathise with the hon. Lady. Does she agree that the concept of domestic violence being treated as something different from ordinary violence is a great disadvantage to women? In common law we have provision for dealing with violence against the individual, whether within marriage or without. We should make better use of that, instead of treating domestic violence as something different and perhaps not quite as important as the violence that occurs when a person comes up to someone in the street and slugs him for the hell of it. We would thereby remove part of the emotional element from the issue, which I entirely agree with the hon. Lady is an extremely important social issue. Perhaps the Bill is not the right place to emphasise that. We should stress that the police should deal with the problem as they would deal with any other form of grievous bodily harm.

Ms Hodge: I agree with the hon. Lady's intent, but domestic violence is almost worse than common assault. Too many women are too frightened to admit that they are subject to domestic violence. We must create an environment in which women who feel so low because they have been subject to it feel confident enough to come forward so that the police can take the necessary action.

Ms Jean Corston (Bristol, East): Does my hon. Friend agree that one of the reasons for the Bill is that many of the provisions of common law are not available to some women who are faced with domestic violence? Those who have represented women trying to obtain injunctions—for example, tort injunctions—have found that they are simply not available to women in some circumstances. The common law has failed women in many ways.

Ms Hodge: I thank my hon. Friend.

Mr. Elfyn Llwyd (Meirionnydd Nant Conwy): Over the past five or six years the police attitude towards the subject has changed for the good. We are dealing with a culture in which, for the past 30 or 40 years, whenever anything happened in a so-called domestic framework it was considered not to be actionable by the police. That culture must be changed.

Ms Hodge: I agree entirely with those comments.

If the Committee accepts the horrific nature of domestic violence, we cannot deal with it as an afterthought or an appendage to the law. It must be integral to our deliberations and central to our work in considering and amending the Bill through the due processes of the House. Let me give a few examples of the ways in which it could influence our consideration of the Bill.

Mr. Peter Bottomley (Eltham): I think that I am right in saying that the amendment is to part I, which sets out the principles for parts II and III. Part III is about legal aid for mediation and part II is about orders for separation and divorce. It might be helpful to the Committee to know whether the hon. Lady's remarks are directed to part II on separation and divorce, or whether they refer to matters to which we may wish to return on part IV, which deals with the occupation of the family home.

Ms Hodge: I am assured that my remarks relate to both part II and part III.

Let me elaborate on examples of action that we could take. Violence in a marriage is often the result of men feeling that they are the strong, macho, assertive partners in the relationship. The violence almost reinforces that macho image. In an odd way, that was somewhat evident from some of the speeches that we heard this morning. We need to challenge the traditional concept of power relationships in marriage if we are to prevent the massive incidence of domestic violence. If we took action that started to challenge those traditional relationships, we might do a lot to promote marriage, which concerns all of us in the Committee.

My hon. Friend the Member for Bristol, East talked about the zero tolerance campaign that originated in Edinburgh, which were a critical part of trying to reeducate people to the idea that as a society we will not tolerate domestic violence, and that power relationships within marriage need to be different from those that have been accepted in the past. Such campaigns tell people that the authorities—the police, the courts and those involved in conciliation and mediation—take the Col. 78 matter seriously. They lay out clearly the criminal aspects of domestic violence for both the victims and the perpetrators, which is an important aspect of zero tolerance campaigns. I read somewhere that such campaigns stop domestic violence being a low-risk option for perpetrators, which I thought explained it rather well. I am saddened that there has not been greater support from the Government for zero tolerance campaigns to ensure that the insidious crime of domestic violence is tackled.

Ms Janet Anderson (Rossendale and Darwen): Does my hon. Friend agree that it is largely Labour local authorities that have pioneered the zero tolerance campaigns?

Ms Hodge: I agree, though it is difficult nowadays to find a Conservative-controlled local authority—and after Thursday, it will be even more difficult. Many of the campaigns were started two or three years ago when there were more Conservative-controlled authorities, but it was the Labour-controlled authorities that encouraged them. I understand that there was pressure on the Government at the time to run a national campaign, but they chose not to do so. I hope that the Government regret that.

There are other things that we could do. Some of the work done in our education system to reduce bullying in schools may break people out of the cycle of violence, especially those who experience violence at home, which leads them to repeat the pattern. I have heard of some interesting projects, one of which was the Change project run by Stirling council, which involved work with men as the perpetrators of violence; some lessons could be learnt from that. The project involved only men who had been convicted of domestic violence and work was done to change their attitude to relationships, which was an effective mechanism in trying to break the cycle of domestic violence.

We should support such projects and those involving both men and women—we shall be talking about conciliation later in the Bill. If men and women together can develop strategies to help them cope and handle conflict in their marriage, perhaps the incidence of domestic violence could be diminished. I hope that the whole Committee would support that.

We have heard about the good job done by domestic violence units and the police should be supported for the spread of such units. They are good and their expansion should be applauded by the Committee, but equally we must show some concern over the tendency, which I have witnessed on one or two occasions, among the police services in various London boroughs to refer a matter to a unit and marginalise the issue by leaving it to the particular policemen and policewomen who pick up allegations of domestic abuse.

There is terrible difficulty in tackling an issue of this sort because we are fairly new to knowing how to cope with it as a society. We tend to marginalise the issue; we must take care not to do that.

We must consider domestic violence when we deal with the arrangements for children. It cannot always be presumed that the best solution for children is to have access to both parents. All of us will know of children who are petrified of their dad, because it is usually the Image Col. 79 dad who has abused both them and the mother. In the courts, there is a quite proper desire to ensure that contact with the father is maintained, but instances of abuse by the father are often overlooked and wrong arrangements made for the children.

Although we want contact with the father to be maintained, we must be careful that we do not provide greater distress for the children should the father be dreadful. Access to children and the protection of their welfare must be linked to safety. I hope that later in our consideration of the Bill we will discuss the development of child contact centres as a mechanism for ensuring access in an appropriate setting, given violence against the mother or the potential violence that might be inflicted on the children should there be no centre available—and there are not many around.

Refuge provision and access to safe houses should also be considered.

Mr. Brazier: I am most grateful to the hon. Lady for giving way again. I was at a child contact centre last weekend. We all support their excellent work. However, once again I urge her to think about the relevance of her general speech to the clause. The fundamental fact, as survey after survey in this country and abroad has shown, is that the principal danger to children comes not from their natural fathers or parents, but from stepfathers. Promoting the clause, which does not refer to the domestic violence section of the Bill and specificially excludes part IV—it refers only to the marital sections of the Bill—does nothing to advance the case that she has been making.

Ms Hodge: With respect, that assertion is not borne out by the facts. There is violence against and abuse of children by people close to them. I am not disputing that such a person could be a stepfather, but the assertion that violence and abuse come principally from such a situation is wrong. We must be concerned with the increasing incidence of domestic violence by natural fathers in families with children and a mother. The issue is not just one of stepfathers, although there might be abuse when a step-parent is involved.

I wish to make a further point to the hon. Gentleman, to which he will no doubt reply. If physical violence formed part of a first relationship, it may well occur again within a second relationship. That does not mean that we should not deal effectively with the domestic violence that was displayed in the first relationship, however. We should take far more seriously the prevention of domestic violence, and that is something that my contribution attempts to do. We can do that through zero tolerance campaigns and by working with those men who tend to be violent in relationships.

Mr. Brazier: Those matters are dealt with in part IV of the Bill, and the clause does not relate to that. However, study after study has shown that the dangers from a stepfather in a second relationship are many times greater than they are from a natural father.

Ms Hodge: There is a danger of our ping-ponging the argument across the Committee, but I must tell the hon. Gentleman that my prime concern is for women and children. They must be at the forefront of our minds and at the heart of what we are considering.

The hon. Gentleman cites research, but he should come forward with that research. No doubt, he will have the opportunity to do so. If Canadian research is correct, one in four women experience violence from their partners or former partners. The Canadians have far better data than we do because they have sought them. If those findings are true and British experience is similar, the issue of domestic violence should be central to our consideration of the Bill. The problem goes far further than being something that occurs when a stepfather is around—it is much greater than that. That is why I am anxious that it should be covered in clause 1.

Mr. Peter Bottomley: Will the hon. Lady tell us whether the Canadian information on violence in the household splits the figures between those who are married and those who are not?

Ms Hodge: I did not hear the question.

Mr. Bottomley: Does the hon. Lady know what proportion of those experiencing violence in the household are married and what proportion are not?

Ms Hodge: I have not put aside the paper, but such data appear in the British Crime Survey. I shall share that information with the hon. However, the British Crime Survey provides the best data on how much of the problem occurs within marriage and how much it affects those who are not married. Perhaps it is more likely to affect those who are not married, but it is equally an issue for those within marriage. The British Crime Survey contains the evidence.

Domestic violence is one of the greatest crimes of the late-20th century. We are learning more about it and it is more acceptable to talk about it publicly and for women to reveal it to the police or others. We are also learning more and more about its impact on the family.

Mr. Paddy Tipping (Sherwood): I am delighted to have an opportunity to support this important amendment, particularly so early on in the life of the Committee. We are on part I, clause 1.
