Clause 1
THE GENERAL PRINCIPLES UNDERLYING PARTS II AND III

Amendment proposed [ 25 April]: No. 36, in page 1, leave out line 12 and insert—

"(i) encouraged to take all practicable steps to save the marriage, through marriage counselling or otherwise; and

Mr. Bill Michie
(Sheffield, Heeley)

Is it a genuine attempt to help couples through a crisis or will it be another stumbling block intended to delay the inevitable, thus creating more misery and frustration? That, to some extent, depends on resources being made available and on the sort of conciliation or mediation that is offered.

Last night, I was considering the difference between conciliation, reconciliation and mediation—three words that will not necessarily be interpreted in the usual way by courts and certainly not by lawyers, who seem to have their own interpretation of many English words. Who will be offering such mediation? I agree that those people should be professionals, but where will we find them? What will be their mandate? On the implications of divorce for the family, I am not sure that anyone is qualified to make that sort of judgment and be certain that they are right. The children, quite naturally, may be upset at the time, which could make the couple feel so conscious of their actions that they withdraw from divorce.  That is extremely important. I have a real problem with the Bill because it is cost neutral. We are told that savings can be made from the legal aid budget that can be refocused on mediation—and, perhaps, reconciliation. I find that hard to believe. My experience of social policy and social change is that if major changes are made—and the Bill is certainly a major change—they cost money in the short term. I hope that the Minister will talk to us during our proceedings about the financial assumptions behind the Bill. I would be very worried if, when the Bill completes its passage, it is still considered to be cost neutral. Almost by definition, it will cost more in the short term.

The amendment refers to "marriage counselling". I am worried that we shall move from a system to end marriages that is primarily guided by the legal profession to one that makes greater use of counsellors and mediators. That raises a big resource issue. There are not enough qualified people at present. A great deal of work needs to be done, first to train people and, secondly, to ensure that sufficient resources are available.

During our previous sitting much was said about appropriate, well-qualified, professional people being available. That was contrasted with what was called an JPEG 43 Auntie Wu approach to counselling. Perhaps I come from the Auntie Wu tradition of counselling, but in my experience marriage counsellors and mediators are, by and large, white, middle-aged, well educated and professional. That creates a stumbling-block that prevents some people from taking part in such services. We must consider the matter more widely. My experience of working with community groups and voluntary organisations suggests that we can add value to people—we can train, dare I call them, "ordinary" people and give them expertise. They may not be able to perform a totally professional job, but they can offer support and help.

I would be a little chary of any move to a highly professional counselling service. I know that organisations such as Relate aspire to that and achieve it, but we need to encourage other people, including from the black and Asian communities to come forward to give counselling. Very few people from those communities work as counsellors for Relate. We need to work hard at that problem and that, too, raises a resource issue.

There is a real danger of confusing our terms. The Bill is underpinned by the notion of mediation. As I understand it, mediation is a process in which parties can engage to find a way out of their difficulties and to discuss the practicalities of resolving them. Some people might want to take that approach; others will want to have family counselling, which is different from mediation. It is a process in which people come together, talk and consider positive, practical steps to try to save their marriages. I want to see more about that in the Bill.

At the moment, the Bill is about the mediation process—the practical steps to get out of a marriage. I want to see more in the Bill about marital counselling and reconciliation. There will always be other people who require another sort of counselling—divorce counselling. Coming out of a relationship is painful. Even when the practical matters are sorted out through mediation, people feel much hurt and pain. Those people need support, too, so there is a need for divorce counselling.

Those are issues for the professionals, for people such as Relate and other counselling organisations. However, they are also important issues for us. In essence, we must make the resources available. Clearly, the Government cannot deal with such matters. First, they must use the powers of grant aid in the Bill to put money into making the system work to save marriages. Secondly, time must be given to allow resources to be built up so that professionals can be put in place.

All of that happens at the end of marriages, but the most important part of clause 1—the key principle of the Bill—is

"that the institution of marriage is to be supported."

 That headline statement appears at the beginning of the Bill, but there is little in subsequent clauses to support it. The Bill depresses me because it has been referred to as a Bill about saving marriages that should be saved. If we are serious about saving marriages, we should do much work at the start of marriages, not at the end of them.

44 I am concerned that all political parties seem to treat family policy as a bolt-on—a tagged-on extra. In a sense, families are the key to our society both for us and for future generations. I should like this and future Governments to focus on the family and to create a ministerial group that works together, draws civil servants together and puts forward punchy and positive proposals to promote marriage, partnerships and the bringing up of children. The Bill is devoid of that. I should like to see much more work done on that issue.

Amendment No. 36 deals with the importance of making people

"aware of the implications of a possible divorce for any children of the family".

 It is often said that children are the losers in separation and divorce. I hope that we shall support the amendment and that we shall consider ways to protect and enhance the rights of children. The law is clear—the interests of the child should be paramount.

I hope that we shall consider the work that, for example, family court officers could do to hear the voices of children. Children have a right to be heard when they are involved in separations. The difficulty is to know how that should be done. Although there will be some feelings and tensions, the problem is not so great for older children—at least there is a system for allowing them to be heard. However, the problem is more difficult when dealing with younger children. I hope that during our discussions we will consider amendments that will protect the right of children.

In an earlier sitting the hon. Member for Eltham (Mr. Bottomley) was kind enough to speak about the work of family centres. That work demonstrates that family policy must be worked out at both national and local level. We must listen to the voices of children. The work in Nottingham, to which the hon. Gentleman referred, clearly demonstrates that, with adequate resources and good professional help, families can be kept together. Children could be kept with their families. My experience working at the centre showed that over a period of two years, with intensive help and support for families, children were not taken into care on that estate. Before the family centre was established on the estate, eight children a year were coming into care. The work of the family centre reduced that to one child coming into care over a period of two years.

That is important for the child and the family. It is also important to us as legislators—first, because of the social issues involved, and secondly, in terms of a cost-benefit analysis. It costs more than £200 a week to keep a child in care. By making resources available and investing in the family and in children, by good preparation and by looking to the future, we can support partnerships and the institution of marriage.

That is what I hope we will get from the Bill. There is a great deal of amendment to be done, from clause 1 to the last clause, to ensure that the Bill focuses on families, helps them to survive if they can survive and have the will to survive, and gives a careful, thoughtful and helpful way out to people who face difficult and painful situations after they have reluctantly decided to end a relationship.

The Parliamentary Secretary Lord Chancellor's Department (Mr. Jonathan Evans)
: I begin by saying something about the principles clause. As the hon. Member for Hornsey and Wood Green (Mrs. Roche) said, it is rare to begin legislation with a principles clause; most legislation deals with the detail of what we are endeavouring to bring within the law. As I understand it, the precedent was set in Bills of a constitutional nature, but there have been a couple of examples of principles clauses in more recent years—most notably, in the Legal Aid Act 1988.

I should explain how a principles clause came to be included in the Bill. Members of the Committee will be aware that some people—outside, rather than within the House, I hope—have portrayed the Bill as damaging to the institution of marriage, and have claimed that that was one of the purposes of the Law Commission's initial proposals for legislative change in this area, made at the beginning of the 1990s.

When the Bill was considered in the other place, two Members of that House proposed that key principles contained in the White Paper published by the Lord Chancellor should be incorporated in the Bill, in order that there should be no doubt about the Bill's purpose. 48 The argument was that that would help the courts and others who exercise functions under the Bill to understand its principles. Clause 1 states:

"The court and any person, in exercising functions under or in consequence of Parts II and III, shall have regard to the following general principles".

 I would hope that the principles contained in the Bill are generally unobjectionable. That was the view in the other place, and I am sure that anyone who reads the list in clause 1 would also take that view. You, Sir Patrick, have said that you are prepared to let us have a wide-ranging debate that considers not only the amendment but the general principles of clause 1.

May I answer the charge that it was hoped that the Government would, on reconsideration, feel that the inclusion of clause 1 improved the Bill? Although it is correct to say that two Members of the other place introduced a principles clause, the clause now in the Bill was drafted by the Lord Chancellor's Department and proposed by the Lord Chancellor. I certainly give due credit to those Members of the other place who raised the issue, but there is no doubt that the Lord Chancellor and I have supported the insertion of a principles clause in the Bill.

I take on board the Committee's view that marriage counselling should be elevated and mentioned in clause 1. I have more difficulty in accepting the other two proposals in the amendment. Paragraph (i) states that couples should be

"encouraged to take all practicable steps to save the marriage, through marriage counselling or otherwise".

 The hon. Member for Sheffield, Heeley, my hon. Friend the Member for Mid-Kent (Mr. Rowe) and the hon. Paragraphs (ii) and (iii) of the amendment present greater difficulty.
