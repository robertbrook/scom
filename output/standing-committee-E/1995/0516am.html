Mr. Boateng: In returning to the subject of domestic violence, we are emphasising the extent of the problem. Concern about the matter is not only felt here. Out there in the country, one of the most significant cosmetic retailers is running a campaign entitled "Blow the Whistle". It is geared specifically to heightening public awareness of the scale of the problem of domestic violence—so much so, that the company is receiving postcards, at the rate of 150 a day, highlighting the concern of members of the public.

The amendments would dispel the widespread perception that the law does not deal with domestic violence. I am referring to the practical application of the law. Its accessibility and flexibility in dealing with a complex and high volatile phenomenon is at a premium. The law counts for very little unless it can provide a practical remedy for those seeking redress.

In their entireity, the amendments seek to preserve the courts' use of the undertaking, making it available to victims of domestic violence and enabling a power of arrest to be attached. The amendments seek to deal with a real worry—not only among professional advisers but among those working in the women's refuge movement and elsewhere—that the Bill may make undertakings less readily available and less effective in dealing with the problems presented by domestic violence.

One thinks, in that regard, particularly of the remarks and writings of District Judge Stephen Gerlis, who in praising the effectiveness of undertakings, expresses the view that the Bill may have inadvertently—almost by a side wind—blown them away as an effective remedy. We seek to emphasise their importance, to make them available where at present they might not be available, and to enable a power of arrest to be attached to them. The reason for that additional power is that solemn undertakings should be backed up, to provide a speedy and effective redress. The notion that an undertaking can be taken lightly—that breaching it will not have serious consequences—must be dispelled. We seek to do just that.

Amendment No. 19 proposes the use of magistrates courts as a venue for obtaining orders. That, too, was referred to by District Judge Stephen Gerlis, in the January edition of Family Law. He said:

"There are very good reasons for this"—

he is referring to the use of magistrates courts—because

"the procedure in the magistrates' courts is very much simpler than in the county court".

He went on to say that the Domestic Proceedings and Magistrates Courts Act 1978 led to an attempt Image Col. 309

"to persuade applicants and their legal advisers to head in the direction of magistrates' court for their injunctions rather than the county court because the costs involved appeared to be less."

That is another attraction of using magistrates courts.

The fact that the procedures are simpler means that magistrates courts are more readily usable by those who do not, for one reason or another, have the services of a lawyer, and we should take that, too, into account. The cuts in eligibility for legal aid have left some women especially vulnerable to spouses or partners who are prepared to exploit that lack of means and ready access to professional advice and assistance because they themselves have difficulty in funding representation.

Magistrates courts are attractive to many who do not have lawyers because their procedures are simpler, there is not the reliance on affidavit evidence that there is elsewhere, and it is possible to give evidence on oath in the witness box to obtain an order. The court draws up the papers and arranges the necessary documentation.

There is one disadvantage to magistrates courts, in that their power to commit for contempt is less useful in exercising a deterrent effect, because the order is limited to two months as opposed to the County Court's two years. Nevertheless, magistrates courts are an increasingly attractive venue, and the amendments would ensure that they had the power to accept undertakings.

The amendments would allow us to use every venue and mechanism available to law in order to get to grips practically with the menace of domestic violence. It is in that spirit that I commend them to the Committee, and I trust that they will find widespread support and acceptance.
