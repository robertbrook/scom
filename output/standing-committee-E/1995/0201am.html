Clause 4
RATES OF DUTY AND REBATE

Amendment proposed [ 30 January]: No. 5, in page 2, line 36, to leave out from the word `above;' to the end of line 39 and insert the words—


`(c) it is delivered for home use as petrol which is suitable to be used as fuel for engines for which leaded petrol is suitable by virtue of being leaded; or


(d) it is delivered for home use under such a description, or in such a manner, as tends, in the circumstances, to suggest that it is—

  1. petrol satisfying the conditions set out in paragraph (a) above; or
  2. petrol suitable to be used as fuel for engines for which leaded petrol is suitable by virtue of being leaded.


(1C) The method of testing unleaded petrol for ascertaining, for the purposes of this section, its research octane number or motor octane number shall be such as the Commissioners may direct."


() In subsection (2) of that section (meaning of "unleaded"), for the words from "or, if" onwards there shall be substituted "; and petrol is 'leaded' for the purposes of this section if it is not unleaded.".'—[ Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory].

Mr. Robert Ainsworth
(Coventry, North-East)
: Does the clause, in its amended form, remove the fiscal incentive to buy super unleaded petrol instead of leaded four-star petrol? Members of the Committee do not know exactly what the Minister is proposing. I should be grateful if he could clarify his intention.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory
: Super unleaded petrol falls into the category of unleaded petrol, which, for some years, has attracted a lower excise duty rate than leaded petrol. The lower rate was introduced deliberately to discourage the use of leaded petrol. The Government are satisfied that the use of super 40 unleaded petrol is low, and declining. As I mentioned earlier in the week, very few vehicles require it, they are getting older and, in due course, they will be scrapped. As I have already said, the use of super unleaded is therefore declining and as those vehicles are scrapped it will be phased out. In addition to that the greater use of catalytic convertors means that benzene emissions, which are associated with the use of super unleaded petrol, are already declining. The clause and the amendment forestall a potential greater use of super unleaded petrol by some companies by combining unleaded petrol with other mixtures to make it suitable for use in cars that use leaded petrol. As a result of our change, super unleaded petrol will bring in additional revenue and there will be associated environmental improvements.

Mr. Robert Ainsworth
: I have no desire to detain the Committee, but the Minister's comments do not appear to correspond with what he said on Tuesday. I have a simple question. Is it the Government's intention to remove all fiscal incentives to buy super unleaded as against leaded four star petrol?

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory
: I do not want to repeat myself, but unleaded petrol attracts a duty rate less than that of leaded petrol. Therefore, the position remains exactly the same. I cannot repeat that again.

Mr. Ainsworth
: I have no desire to detain the Committee. Some years ago the Government introduced an incentive for people to buy unleaded petrol. That was a successful campaign. We have now discovered, however, that super unleaded petrol is being used, which has damaging consequences for health. I want to know whether the measures in clause 4 and the amendment moved by the Minister will remove entirely the incentive for using super unleaded petrol, because at present people are being encouraged by the taxation system to use such petrol. Are the Government intending through the amendment to remove completely the incentive to buy super unleaded petrol as against four-star petrol? I cannot ask a more simple question. I would like a simple yes or no answer to that question. Are the Government proposing to remove the incentive to buy super unleaded petrol as against leaded four-star petrol?

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory
: The price of fuels is not fixed by the Government.

Mr. Ainsworth
: The tax is.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory
: The tax element is designed to be an equaliser. I can give the hon. Gentleman no assurance that the market will deliver the price equality that he seeks.

As regards to the duty element, by equalising the gap we are moving towards removing the incentive for some petrol companies to use the status of super unleaded petrol to make it more attractive and suitable for cars that currently use leaded petrol. By doing that we can forestall the use and prevent the increase in use JPEG 41 of super unleaded petrol. That means that associated benzene emissions will be removed from the environment.

Mr. Malcolm Chisholm
(Edinburgh, Leith)
: We welcome clause 4. The idea of putting up tax on petrol and diesel seems environmentally sound. It would be even more environmentally sound if more attention was paid to promoting public transport.

Subsection (2) of the clause relates to road fuel gas, a tax that applies to cars as well as buses. The use of such road fuel gas is one development that we would like to see promoted by the clause. The other development relates to diesel but before I talk about the tax on diesel, I should make it clear that I support the tax changes as they relate to cars. In the past three years, however, tax rises have also affected public transport with the effect that the tax increases relating to diesel are now much higher than those relating to cars.

I am sure that hon. Members recall that until 1993 buses doing local journeys received a rebate for the diesel fuel tax paid and until then paid no duty at all. Following the 1993 Budget, the duty rate on diesel per litre was 2.56p. The following year it went up to 5.3p per litre—an increase of 107 per cent. A year later the tax went up 6.18p—a further increase of 17 per cent. This year, the tax is set at 9.16p—an increase of 48 per cent. That represents a massive increase in the duty paid by public transport as well as by cars. The fact that public transport will be faced with a tax of more than 9p this year will cause serious problems for bus companies. That does not seem to me to be a sensible way to encourage public transport use.

When the Chancellor introduced the 1993 Budget in which he proposed his environmental measures, he said:

"Any critic of the Government's tax plans who claims also to support the international agreement to curb carbon dixoide emissions will be sailing dangerously near to hypocrisy"—[ Official Report, 30 November 1993; vol.233,c.937]

As we all know, the word hypocrisy is used rather loosely by Conservative Members.

As we are aware, the freezing of the fuel rebate for buses made public transport more expensive and therefore less attractive to people. That was not taken into account by the Chancellor.

I have been in touch with representatives of Lothian Regional Transport and they have told me that their fuel costs have risen by 53 per cent. and that they now stand at £4.5 million—an increase of more than £1.5 million in the past two years. That has created problems because those costs are passed on in higher bus fares and other additional costs. If the Government are serious about being green and encouraging public 42 transport, they need to look again at the rebate for public transport.

The second argument in favour of the change used at the time of the 1993 Budget was that the tax would be an incentive for people to use fuel efficient vehicles.

However, some of the additions required to make engines more environmentally friendly also, unfortunately, make them less fuel efficient. The Government's argument at the time was not particularly sound.

Even more desirable than reducing the diesel rate for buses would be to introduce measures to encourage buses to convert to using road fuel gas. Cars could also convert to that. The most disappointing aspect of the clause is that even after the proposed decrease of 15 per cent., the cost of road fuel gas and petrol and diesel will be roughly the same. That is not a satisfactory state of affairs, especially if we want vehicles to be converted to use road fuel gas.

Before the Budget, road fuel gas duty was at 400 per cent. of the minimum level stipulated by the European Union. There is obviously room to offer more incentives to get the market for road fuel gas off the ground. Many other EU countries have already ensured that the gas duty is at the minimum level.

Some calculations have been done that identify that to convert a small or medium-sized vehicle to run on gas would cost around £2,000. The duty rate for gas and diesel are currently at similar levels—about 5.09p per mile for diesel and 5.03p for gas. Assuming parity of fuel prices, it has been calculated that it would take 3.8 million miles to offer a simple payback to operators to cover the conversion investment. That is no incentive. It has been calculated that if the duty were reduced to the EU minimum level, operators would receive a payback from conversion within two to three years. That represents a good incentive to operators and would apply to cars and buses.

One major European city has converted its bus fleet to run on natural gas. I was told that by some councillors from Lothian region who went over to Austria recently. Lothian region is a environmentally sound Labour council and it has taken many positive steps to improve the environment and transport facilities. Those councillors thought that they might suggest to their counterparts in Vienna that they should consider converting their bus fleets to natural gas, which Lothiana region is considering. They were rather stunned when those councillors in Vienna told them that they did that in 1978. All the Viennese bus fleet runs on natural gas. The significant fact for our purposes is that no tax is levied on liquid gas for public transport use. The price of diesel is four times the price of liquid gas in Austria because of that tax measure.

The Government should consider that Austrian example and encourage the development of that environmentally clean mode of transport. I am sure that hon. Members are aware of the advantages of natural gas against petrol and diesel—carbon monoxide emissions are lower by 76 per cent. as JPEG 43 against petrol and diesel; nitrogen oxide emissions by more than 80 per cent; non-methane hydrocarbons by 80 per cent.; carbon dioxide emissions are reduced, albeit by smaller amounts, but they are still significant; and benzene emissions are reduced by more than 90 per cent. There are many environmental improvements to be had from such conversions to natural gas. I hope that the Government will be more bold in subsequent Budgets—if they have any—and encourage such conversions.

Clause 6
RELIEF FOR MARINE VOYAGES

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Ms Primarolo
: Will the Paymaster General explain a little more clearly than the notes on clauses the exact purpose of clause 6? The clause puts into legislation a concession introduced in January 1995 in respect of relief from duty on oil used in commercial vessels. Previously, relief from duty on all fuel oils was granted only to the Royal National Lifeboat Institution JPEG 49 lifeboats, most fishing boats and to commercial vehicles using heavy oil in home waters.

As a result of clause 6, relief from duty applies to all mineral oils used by commercial vessels during marine voyages. The main beneficiaries of the concession and clause 6 are small petrol-driven boats used in diving support, marine surveying and commercial transport.

Will the Paymaster General explain this measure which is relatively small but which raises a number of questions about how the system will be policed, whether the system of reliefs and rebates in the area are unnecessarily complicated and whether a more straight forward manner of dealing with this could be found?

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory
: The clause replaces an extra-statutory concession, which is desirable in itself. It is always better to put those things into law. It extends the duty relief which has up to now been available for heavy oil, or diesel commercial vehicles, to include, from now on, similar petrol-powered craft. The sort of vessels that will be helped are those operated by fish farms, or diving support vessels, and other similar craft used by commercial operators. It will not extend to pleasure craft.

This is a simplifying measure in that it extends a concession more rationally, to include all such small powered craft used in these waters, regardless of whether they fall into the previous category. For instance, there was no particularly good reason to exclude those visiting and servicing fish farms to which the concession was already extended rather than similar commercial vehicles used in other ways. I hope that this simplifying measure will have the more general support of the Committee.

The report to the chairman of Customs and Excise 1994–95—it is from the south Wales and the Borders collection executive unit, but it is not unusual in what it has to say—at page 8, said:

"Large scale cigarette smuggling was a significant development towards the year end."

50 We are aware from information from the tobacco industry that exceptional difficulties are being experienced as a result of the taxes on hand rolling tobacco. In the United Kingdom those taxes are the highest in Europe at £5.40 on a 500 g pouch, more than four times higher those of continental neighbours, such as Belgium, where it is £1.13. The closeness of Belgium and the huge variation in the duty levels, which affect the price of the product, create an opportunity for smuggling on a scale that is intolerable for health and revenue reasons and for policing reasons in terms of the development of criminal activity and fraud in this area.

Smuggling of that product increased by 50 per cent. last year, with a substantial revenue loss to the Government which would otherwise have been obtained had the products been purchased here for consumption.

I am not advocating smoking, but I want to make it clear that we are concerned about the duty rates because of their implications for health, tax—by which I mean revenue to the Exchequer—and policing.

The most frequently quoted product in the handrolling tobacco market is called Drum—I could not name any of them. Drum is not legitimately sold in the United Kingdom, but more than 11 per cent. of the population who smoke apparently smoke it most often. It is the third most popular brand for hand rolling tobacco, but it is not even sold here legally. Unless an awful lot of our citizens, instead of going to the corner shop, are going to Belgium to purchase their tobacco, I can only assume that it is coming into the country by another means. The United Kingdom manufacturers sell to Europe, so they are not losing anything and are protecting their profit. They know full well that their products will come back into the United Kingdom. The British tobacco industry sales to Belgium increased fourfold in 1995. Unless the Belgians have suddenly developed an enormous preference for smoking hand rolled tobacco, it is unlikely that that product is being domestically consumed in Belgium. The consumption of hand rolling tobacco in the United Kingdom is increasing because smokers are migrating—I am not sure that that is the right term—to cheaper continental tobacco, which is becoming widely available. It is also interesting to note that sales of hand rolling cigarette papers in the United Kingdom were stable until the relaxation of border controls and the establishment of the single market in 1993. Since then, those sales have increased significantly. Some people say that sales are eight times greater than the necessary number if one measures the sale of cigarette papers against the equivalent legitimate sale of hand rolling tobacco. Unless those cigarette papers are being used to smoke something else, it is highly likely that they are being used to smoke hand rolled tobacco smuggled into the United Kingdom.

The duty paid in the United Kingdom on hand rolling tobacco sales has consistently and dramatically JPEG 51 fallen since 1992. The difference is mainly due to smuggling. JPEG 69 The clause reduces vehicle excise duty on electric motor cycles to £15—the same rate as for petrol motor cycles of under 120cc. Electrically assisted pedal cycles are exempt from duty.
