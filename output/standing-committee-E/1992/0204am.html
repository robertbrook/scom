Mrs. Taylor
: Clause 244 is one of the important clauses that still needs clarification. The Government must say how it will work in practice.

The clause gives the Secretary of State the power to allow local education authorities to supply specified goods and services to grant-maintained schools in their own or adjoining areas.

As drafted, the clause allows local authorities extra freedom to provide these services, but in doing so would create significant anomalies. Worst of all, it could threaten some of the services which are so important to every school in an area, especially the local authority schools, whose services could be destroyed if local authorities do not have certain necessary freedoms.

I hope that the Minister will use the debate on the first amendment to explain the way in which he thinks that the clause will work in practice. The Minister will have had many representations on the clause because it is causing so much concern among the people who are responsible for essential school services.

The amendment would allow local authorities to provide services without needing an order from the Secretary of State. Various questions arise from the amendment, so I hope that the Minister will take the opportunity to make an early speech.

We need clarification of the Government's interpretation of trading at the margins. Over the past few months, there have been different interpretations: suggestions include a 10 per cent. limit and, in a letter to an outside organisation, it was suggested that the measure would apply only after 50 per cent. of schools in an area have become grant maintained. If so, there are still problems in the definition. Are we discussing half the schools in the area, or half the pupils in the area?

1348 The clause gives the Minister a great deal of power to specify the goods and services that may be provided. What goods and services would the Secretary of State think appropriate? We need further information about the definition, "adjoining area". Why are Ministers being so specific about the trading that local authorities can do? It is surprising that Ministers should adopt such an approach. They are telling us that there should not be restrictions in the market and yet, at the same time, they are including in the Bill restrictions on local authorities' ability to enter such markets.

The clause limits the freedom of grant-maintained schools to buy services wherever they wish, which seems a strange measure from this particular Minister, who has been talking about the new-found freedom of schools that decide to change to grant-maintained status.

I hope that the Minister realises the genuine concern about the future of many important services. We have had representations about the clause from a range of interests, from those with experience of providing services to schools. Our amendments cover many services, including outdoor centres and school libraries. The Minister should recognise that these are not fringe interests; they are extremely important. In particular, the amendment dealing with school libraries, which I hope that we will discuss later, illustrates the damage that could be done if the clause is not amended. Any threat to the school library services will weaken the help, inspiration and back-up that those services often provide.

When I read the reports and briefings I assumed that that Act was restrictive, in that it prevents local authorities from trading with non-public services. I was assured by my hon. Opposition Members are afraid that it will lead to disruptive and complicated legislative procedures to interpret the Act and to clarify the meaning of "at the margins".

Government policy has produced fragmentation and has created difficulties in the interaction between LEAs and the grant-maintained sector.

We all want to provide a high standard of service to support children's learning in all schools. If the clause is passed as drafted, there is a risk that we shall have the worst of both worlds. We shall lose efficient and effective local services, educational expertise and local knowledge, to the detriment of our education system.

Many services depend on a good relationship between client and provider. If the service is suddenly snapped off with no concern for the customer, there will be a lot of disgruntled parents and governors. The Government should bear that in mind, especially considering the time scale of these operations. We expect the two-year period to begin in 1994, so by 1996 we may be facing even more disgruntled parents and governors than now.

The agency was asked what it knew about terms and conditions of service for teachers and it said that it had never seen them—and could it please have a copy of them? That shows the level of service that private agencies can offer. Their approach is to learn as they go along. They cannot compete with the high level of competence and experience that has been built up by LEAs in the past few years.

LEAs are particularly good at helping schools to respond quickly to the many changes and untested ideas that the Government are trying to introduce to schools. In Cambridgeshire, St. Ivo school, which is in the Prime Minister's constituency of Huntingdon, recently suffered a devastating fire. It burnt down the science block and destroyed much of the work that the children had done for GCSE, including project work. The local authority came in quickly the next day and helped the school staff to rescue much of the children's work. It also gave advice on how to start temporary laboratories so that the children's education could continue with as little disruption as possible. That shows the real value of local education authorities. They could never be replaced by private agencies or insurance companies providing insurance cover. They provide the real help that schools need and they give it as quickly as possible.
