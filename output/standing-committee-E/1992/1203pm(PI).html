Tragically, the Bill does not refer to nursery education and its vital contribution, both educational and vocational, to successful schooling—[Interruption.] This is a serious amendment. I hope that my hon. Friends will do me the courtesy of letting me put the case for it.

Nursery education lays the foundations for all that follows. In Britain, it is a bit of a lottery, the chances of receiving any form of nursery education depend less on the child's needs than on where he or she lives. I hope that hon. Members will agree that such an important learning experience should not be left to chance and that all parents should have the right to send their children to a nursery school if they wish.

Amendments Nos. 83 and 105 would ensure that there is a coherent approach to nursery education so that all children under five could attend local community nursery schools and that local education authorities could establish and maintain such schools. Nursery education should be expanded and integrated with the day care provision that is available in local authorities. That could best be achieved if local education authorities took the strategic role in that regard alongside their responsibility for related services.

Amendment No. 83 would remove the administrative confusion and chaos that would ensue if the responsibility was shared with the funding agency. If the local education authority has specific funding—whether it has a commitment to nursery education or whether it makes little provision at present—it could expand or establish nursery education within its area. However, the White Paper "Choice and Diversity" refers only briefly to nursery education. It does not mention the continuing power of local authorities to provide such education in their areas. In his introduction to the White Paper, the Prime Minister states:

"The Government are"—

Image Col. 465 I apologise for the grammar, but it is the Prime Minister's, not mine—

"determined that every child in this country should have the very best start in life".

However, the White Paper does not ensure that nursery education is universally available; nor does it propose a coherent extension of nursery education. As I said on Second Reading and again today, nursery education is not mentioned in the Bill. Given the extensive research that shows the value of nursery education to young people through their school lives, that is a grave omission. I hope that the Committee will help to rectify that by supporting the amendments.

According to "Choice and Diversity"—a misnomer—the legal position is that primary education includes full-time nursery education. Therefore, schools can continue to provide full-time education for under-fives when they become grant-maintained. However, grant-maintained schools can provide part-time nursery education only as an agent of the local education authority. Nursery education is not part of the mandatory system. It is often provided on a part-time basis, especially where demand outstrips supply. It is appropriate for grant-maintained schools to continue to act as agents for the local education authority in providing part-time nursery education. Local education authorities will thus continue to be responsible for the overall service.

Different LEAs have different policies for the provision of nursery education—some provide no places, while others accommodate many of their children. How can the funding agency decide on the appropriate provision in a particular authority? The Bill does not state at what level such provision is to be made. There are two possibilities: one is that the funding agency would be forced to apply different policies in different parts of the country, depending on the LEA's policy in the area concerned. The other would be to establish a national policy for nursery education, by which provision in a particular area would then be judged. The latter possibility may have some attraction, but is not it a rather strange position for the funding agency to be in? There is no legislative basis on which it can determine the appropriate level of nursery education across the country. It must be more appropriate for the LEAs to continue to hold responsibility for providing nursery education, even when the funding agency takes full responsibility for primary education.

Paragraph 6(2) schedule 2 excludes special schools. The amendments would ensure that the same exclusion would apply to nursery schools. I presume that special schools have already been excluded because of the LEA's ability to respond to the required level of provision. Since nursery education is not mandatory, LEAs should be expected to determine the appropriate level of nursery provision in their areas.

Sometimes nursery provision can be part of a programme that reflects the particular circumstances and local needs. LEAs might be required to provide nursery places as an agency implementing national or urban inner-city programmes. Nursery education should be treated in the same way as special education.

Paragraph 6 of schedule 2 defines the responsibilities that are to transfer to the funding agency from the LEAs when either the 75 per cent. exit point is reached or the LEAs ask the Secretary of State to make such an order. LEAs may not establish or begin to maintain a school unless they have already ceased to maintain other schools. LEAs are still multi-service authorities and may be providing nursery education as part of a programme, reflecting local needs and local circumstances. They might provide such places, particularly now, to carry out the responsibility of making and maintaining statements for children under the age of five with special educational needs.

Paragraph 7.11 of the White Paper recognises the key role that LEAs play in the provision of nursery education by allowing grant-maintained schools to make such provision only as agents for the LEA. That must underline Image Col. 467 the LEAs' responsibility in that area. The LEAs may also have to deal with that when a grant-maintained school ceases to provide nursery education.

"Education for under fives … can not only enrich the child's life at the time but can also prepare the child for the whole process of schooling."

"It should be the objective of both central and local government to ensure the steady expansion of provision of nursery education until it is available to all 3 and 4 year old children whose parents desire it for them."

The Select Committee went on to recommend that

"Future Public Expenditure White Papers should make higher provision for under fives."

One of the most important recommendations was that every LEA shoud have an early-year adviser.

France supplies places in nursery schools, kindergartens or similar establishments for one third of all two-year-olds, while Belgium, Finland and Norway provide for one fifth.
