Clause 71
MAINTENANCE GRANTS

4.30 pm

Mr. Win Griffiths
(Bridgend)
: I beg to move amendment No. 566, in page 42, line 33, at end insert—

"(2A) Grant regulations in respect of maintenance grants—

  1. shall include provision for taking into account, in the case of each school required to be covered by the grant regulations in any financial year, the numbers and ages of registered pupils at that school on such date or dates as may be determined by or under the grant regulations in relation to that year;
  2. may include provision for taking into account any other factors affecting the needs of individual schools which are subject to variation from school to school (including, in particular, the number of registered pupils at a school who have special educational needs and the nature of the special educational provision required to be made for them); and
  3. may in particular include provision, in the case of any school required to be covered by the grant regulations in any financial year, for any amount of maintenance grant determined with respect to the matters mentioned in paragraphs (a) and (b) of this subsection to be adjusted after taking into account other factors affecting the financial resources made available to maintained schools in the area of the school in that year or (in the case that there are no other maintained schools in that area in that year) previous years."

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to take the following amendments:

No. 538, in clause 72, page 43, line 16, after 'grant', insert

"provided that expenditure is for a similar purpose, and at the same level, as expenditure in local education authority maintained schools which is supported by grant from central government.".

No. 540, in clause 73, page 43, line 27, after 'bodies', insert

"provided that the aggregate sum disbursed by the funding authority to grant maintained schools within any local education authority area in any financial year as a proportion of total capital expenditure approved for all the maintained schools within that local education authority's area does not exceed the proportion of grant maintained schools as a proportion of all maintained schools within the same local education authority area.".

Mr. Griffiths
: Clauses 71 and 72 deal with the type of grants available to grant-maintained schools. The purpose of the amendments is to find out how the system of maintenance and special purpose grants will work in schools. The Government are obviously not aware of the great controversy that has surrounded the funding of grant-maintained schools, especially the question whether their additional costs are a true reflection of the costs that the local authority would normally bear for the schools in its area.

892 The evidence suggests that if a large number of secondary schools had already opted out and the rest were to follow in fairly quick succession, local authorities—such as Hillingdon and Havering—could find that the money allocated to them for education purposes through the standard spending assessment was insufficient to cover the funding for grant-maintained schools and for the schools that remained under LEA control. The Bill should provide that the funding for grant-maintained schools fairly reflects that for LEA schools and that any additional money GM schools receive truly reflects the costs that the LEA would normally have borne.

We tabled amendment No. 566 to probe the Government on special educational needs. The Government's consultation document on the common funding formula examines the problems and suggests ways in which they might be overcome. There is still a strong feeling, however, that the schools that have already become grant maintained will not lose out in the future, because of the way in which they were initially funded. They were given a clear, short-term bribe of additional expenditure considerably in excess of what they needed to cover the local authority costs. The few schools that became grant maintained in the initial wave will be funded in a more favourable way than schools that became grant maintained later and schools in the local authority sector. From memory, I believe that a first wave school—perhaps a London borough comprehensive—will spend £100,000 more than a similar school in the second wave. There will be similar advantage to a school in the third wave.

We have always said that the grant-maintained system depended on bribes that were excessive and that would have to be tapered off as more schools became grant maintained. If there is to be parity and justice in funding schools, whether they are grant maintained or not, there should be no financial benefit—additional funds given to a grant-maintained school should reflect what that school cost the local authority, which would no longer be responsible for it. We hope to explore that issue.

We also want to discuss special educational needs. The funding issue ought to be clear in one respect: we want existing best practice to be enshrined in legislation if a child has a statement that is written precisely and is made under the terms of later clauses. There would then be an almost automatic financial implication to supply what the statement provides for. If a statement applies to a child with special educational needs, it ought not to matter whether the child is in a grant-maintained or a local authority-maintained school—funding would be automatically available. Provision may be made under the common funding formula for payment to be made automatically from a special, set-aside fund. I would not be happy with a provision based on averages, with a school receiving an amount of money because it has had a historic role. A specific and clearly identified amount ought to be made available for children with statements, if only because the parents of such JPEG 893 children and those involved in their education would want to be sure that that provision was being made.

The wider issue of children who may not have a statement is more difficult to tackle. Many children with severe problems do not have statements. In a comprehensive school, perhaps 18 per cent. of pupils will require additional educational support which will not be covered by a simple capitation allowance. I hope that the Minister will tell us in detail how the formula will allow for that, as much thought must be given to the funding of schools.

There are problems on both sides of the fence. It could be argued that schools may be keen to establish a special educational need, which may require a statement, in order to get the extra funding and the use of that money in the school may become blurred. Children may be excluded because of problems caused by their behaviour. Additional support may be needed to prevent their exclusion, so that they may continue their education in a mainstream environment.

People in many organisations working on special educational needs are worried by those matters. There is special anxiety that children without statements may be squeezed in grant-maintained schools that want their national curriculum test results to show them in the best light. Already, there is some evidence of an increase in exclusions in some schools. It is not a general problem, but it might become so if schools feel that they must show the best results in published league tables. They may come under pressure to drop pupils who are a little awkward and whose learning problems mean that they do not adapt quickly in normal lessons. Alternatively, schools may seek additional funding for smaller classes for such pupils. If 18 per cent. of a school's population must be taught in smaller classes, the sums of money involved across the country as a whole could be considerable.

Under the Education Act 1944 and the Education Reform Act 1988, local education authorities and schools—whether maintained or grant maintained—are obliged to provide in the best way that they can for all pupils so that all of them have equal acess to the national curriculum. For that to happen for some children, we would need smaller classes than can be found in most comprehensive schools.

JPEG 895 If conditions remain the same, there will be moves—particularly in grant-maintained schools—to exert pressures, which could result in more children being excluded. Some provisions in the Bill relate to the ability of an LEA to send a child to a particular school, although governors will have the right to appeal: that may be a way to prevent too many exclusions.

Funding is important for children who are not statemented but who have special needs that may require the provision of equipment. Do the Government intend to make specific provision to allow grant-maintained schools to buy equipment that may be needed, for example, by a child who has a slight hearing impairment or by a child who needs specific equipment because of a physical disability? Will there be a banded system, in which the child will be categorised and the school will receive a certain sum of money, depending on the number of children in specific categories, but without statements?

The issue is important—perhaps the most important one that we shall have to resolve. There is a severe danger that the Government may be tempted to maintain something akin to the additional expenditure that is currently provided for grant-maintained schools—the schools think of that as a short-term bribe, which will perpetuate injustice. Alternatively, because of financial pressures, the Government will be forced to bring the two systems closer together. Therefore, the original perceived advantages of the extra funding will quickly disappear. We hope that the Government will study the issue seriously and tell us how they hope to maintain fairness between grant-maintained schools and LEA schools.

Rather than being a snowball rolling down a hillside, about to turn into an avalanche, the grant-maintained movement is stuttering along. A few schools will make applications but there is good evidence that most are rejecting grant-maintained status. Such provision will not prove to be a huge problem but, in fairness to all schools, the Government must resolve it. I hope that they will make it clear that future spending on grant-maintained and local authority-maintained schools will include fair and sufficient provision for children with special needs.

We need to know the Government's intentions on funding because we have seen what has happened over the past two or three years to the schools that have achieved grant-maintained status. Let us consider the early schools that achieved such status, specifically in relation to the annual maintenance grant, with which the amendments deal. The first group of schools to achieve grant-maintained status—those that opted out before April 1991—were given a generous deal—a cash-protected, central, annual maintenance grant at either the 1990–91 level, or the 1991–92 level, whichever was greater.

The second wave of schools, which opted out between April 1991 and April 1992, were cash protected for their central, annual maintenance grant JPEG 897 at the 1991–92 level, even if that was lower than the 1990–91 level. Those schools had no choice; the generous deal was not on the table for them. So, the treatment of the second wave was slightly less generous.

The picture for the third wave was even worse. For schools that opted out after April 1992 and were in the first 15 per cent. of schools within their local authority area to do so, their annual maintenance grant was tied to 15 per cent.

The bad news for schools in the fourth category—those that opted out after April 1992 but were not in the first 15 per cent. to do so—was that their central, annual maintenance grant was based on the actual level of delegation in their local education authority area, which could have been more or less than the 15 per cent., but with no protection.

Originally, generous amounts of cash were on offer, but there are now four different categories of grant-maintained school within a local authority area. The way in which the criteria for those categories were arrived at may seem abstract and dry, so a real example may be of benefit to the Committee. It comes from an outer London borough and shows that a school in the first wave of schools to opt out received a central, annual maintenance grant of £320,000. A similar school opting out today would receive only £220,000—£100,000 less than it would have received had they been in the first wave of schools to opt out. Therefore, the generous amounts on offer just two or three years ago are no longer available.
