Clause 28
EXPENSES IN CONNECTION WITH PROPOSALS FOR ACQUISITION OF GRANT-MAINTAINED STATUS

Mr. Byers
: I beg to move amendment No. 466, in page 14, line 29, at end insert

'provided that no such expenditure incurred under subsection (6) of Section 20 exceeds the limit set out in subsection (3) below.'.

The Chairman
: With this, we may take amendment No. 467, in page 14, line 32, leave out subsection (3) and insert—

'(3) A local education authority who have received from a governing body written notice under section 17 or section 18 of this Act that a ballot is to be held under section 20 of this Act shall neither pay any sum nor incur any expense for the purpose of influencing the outcome of that ballot in excess of such maximum amount as may be prescribed.'.

Mr. Byers
: Clause 28(3) provides for restricting the amount of money that a LEA can spend on activities that relate to ballot about grant-maintained status. It sets out an aggregate sum of money that a LEA cannot exceed in any year. I presume that, at the beginning of every year, the Secretary of State will specify to each LEA a sum of money, which will later be capped. LEAs will be unable to exceed that figure in providing information about grant-maintained status.

While accepting the principle of restricting the amount of money that a LEA can spend on information when an opt-out ballot is held, the amendments would provide a more practical method of achieving that. They would link LEA spending on information with the amount of money spent by individual school governing bodies. The amendments would provide that, if an opt-out ballot takes place, the Secretary of State will grant an earmarked sum of money for the governing body to spend on information. The LEA will be allowed to spend the same amount of money on information.

The amendments are a more practical method of achieving the Government's political objective of restricting the amount of money that LEAs can spend on information. Some of us would question whether LEAs 764 have spent a lot of money on the process. However, the Government believe that that has happened. We dispute the fact that there has been abuse, but we acknowledge that such a view has been taken. We are trying to put forward a more practical way to limit the amount of money that can be spent.

The wording of clause 28 causes great difficulty and raises the question of the way in which the aggregate money will be calculated. It will often be difficult and certainly unpredictable to calculate a year in advance exactly how many ballots there will be in an LEA area. In my own LEA authority of north Tyneside, the Government might say that £25,000 is a reasonable amount of money, but there has been only one parental ballot to opt out. In Kent or Essex, where there have been many parental ballots, obviously a different figure would have to be calculated. How would the Secretary of State calculate the aggregate figure in clause 28(3)?

Would it not be far better to adopt the approach taken in the two amendments? The balloting process would be studied and once the school began to ballot, that would be the time to say that the school could spend a certain amount of money and the LEA could spend the same. A fair formula could be worked out: if the school had 1,200 pupils, 50p per pupil could be spent, so that each side of the argument could spend a maximum of £600 as part of the process of providing the information. A simple formula could be devised, which would allow both parties to express their views and provide the information to the parents within an amount set down by the Secretary of State, through the amended clause 28. The amendments provide a simple, practical and effective method of achieving the Government's objectives of restricting LEA expenditure on the provision of information.

I must admit that the wording of the amendments is not original. The Opposition have taken it from Government legislation—the Self-Governing Schools etc. (Scotland) Act 1989. That Act puts a similar restriction on LEA expenditure during the balloting process and section 17 provides the same formula for restricting local authority expenditure as in these two amendments.

What is good enough for Scotland is good enough for us. As clause 28 is presently worded, it will be almost unworkable in terms of the Secretary of State providing a global figure for an LEA well in advance of the period for which it will be effective. The figure will be unpredictable and it will be difficult to calculate how many schools will be balloting. The formula that the Government proposed in the 1989 Act as it affects Scotland would be a far better way to carry forward the stated intention of the Government.

If, for some reason, the Minister feels unable to accept the amendments, we shall be listening carefully to hear why the formula that was acceptable in the 1989 Act for Scotland is not acceptable for a Bill affecting England and Wales.

Mr. Forth
: In some ways, the argument for the amendments is seductive but it is not well-founded, despite the claims of the hon. Member for Wallsend (Mr. Byers). The fairly obvious reason comes down to economics. I do not expect Opposition Members to understand it too well, but I shall do my best.

Local education authorities can reap the economies of scale—I suspect that I have lost most Opposition Members already—because they can produce material in bulk and JPEG 765 relatively cheaply for the ballots held by different schools. That was suggested by some Opposition Members—semi-facetiously in one case—but the principle stands. Each school must produce its own materials in the course of the balloting procedure, so the level playing field so ingeniously suggested by the hon. Member for Wallsend may not be so level after all.

There must be a formulation to take account of that inegality. The parity at which the amendment is aimed would seem to produce a level playing field, but in practice it would do nothing of the kind. This part of the Bill aims to place reasonable limits on what LEAs are able to do and to facilitate as far as is possible the provision of proper information to parents by school governors, so any result such as I believe that the amendment would produce would be unfair in the way that I have described. The playing field would be tilted—ingeniously, but unfairly—against schools. That is the flaw in the amendment. I hope that all hon. Members share my wish for as much parity as can be achieved in the circumstances—I concede that, given the nature of the players, exact parity would be difficult to achieve. However, although the hon. Member for Wallsend, as ever, presented his clever amendment smoothly, it will not do. Without wishing to overstate the matter, I fear that it may undermine the true purpose of the clause.

Mr. Byers
: Being called smooth by the Minister is certainly a compliment. However, two matters must be dealt with before the Minister sits down. First, clause 28 refers to the "prescribed amount" for each LEA. How will that amount be calculated? What factors will the Secretary of State take into account?

Secondly, on the wider issue of information from the LEA, the Local Government Act 1986 lays down strict requirements about the nature of information that local authorities, including LEAs, may give to people in their areas. Do not the provisions of that Act cover the points raised by the Minister?

Mr. Forth
: On the second question, it is not the nature of the information as much as its volume that is relevant here. The Bill would ensure that the amount of information is reasonable, regardless, for the moment, of its content.

The hon. Member for Wallsend, not unreasonably, asked about the "prescribed amount". I envisage that, in due course, a consultation document will be produced outlining some possible different approaches to the problem which will draw on the Scottish experience Inevitably, however, the formulation will have some regard to a basic amount allowable, plus an amount per school that could be adjusted according to the number of pupils per school. There would be an attempt to take account of the authority and its nature, the number of schools involved and the number of pupils in the schools. The approach should probably be along those lines, but I do not want to pre-empt the discussion.

The Department for Education will approach the matter, as ever, cautiously and carefully, by consulting, listening and moving towards a consensual system of a kind with which members of the Committee will be familiar. I cannot answer the hon. Member for Wallsend in detail, but I should have thought that such a general approach would commend itself to the Committee. I shall 766 have to ask the Committee to bear with me. I do not think instant answers will be expected from me at this stage. The objectives are laid out in clause 28. Consultation will follow. We shall then put together a formula which, I believe, will achieve the objectives of the clause. The amendments would undermine those objectives, which is why I ask the Committee to reject them.

Mr. Byers
: I am disappointed by the Minister's response. He should have dealt with several important issues in replying to the debate, which was very brief. He referred to economies of scale. I think I was with his argument at that point—had he gone much further into the economic debate I might have had some difficulty. I understand the economies of scale argument. Probably one of the best examples of the use of economies of scale is his own Department's use of massively increased publicity budget in the past few years to issue the kind of booklet that was paraded before the Committee earlier today. That booklet, which sets out how schools can become grant-maintained was produced by the thousand by the Minister's Department. No local education authority is spending at such a level. There have been mailshots to chairs of governing bodies, advocating the benefits of grant-maintained status and letters to head teachers of schools arguing the case. Those are the economies of scale operated by the Department.

There are two aspects to this pair of amendments. Amendment No. 466 would provide equality of spending between the school governing body and the local education authority. The important factor provided by amendment No. 467 is that the limit will apply to individual ballots. Both sides would then be restricted in relation to individual ballots. The Government's stated intention is to restrict expenditure on information by a local education authority, and that is most crucial in relation to ballots at schools. To parents, it is the arguments about the advantages and disadvantages of opting out for their school which are most important. The amendments would limit expenditure in that context. They go no further. They would lay down merely that if Wallsend high school—there is no such school—is balloting to opt out, the high school can spend £600 on its campaign and the local authority can spend £600 on its.

I am disappointed that the Minister is unable to accept the amendments. We have taken the wording produced by the Government two years ago in the 1989 Act in Scotland. I do not know of any examples of abuse resulting from that legislation, which seems to have worked effectively. Can the Minister give examples of how it has not worked effectively? I can think of none and I doubt that he can. It is a workable system that has stood the test of two or three years, it seems to be fair and it has merit.

I am disappointed that the Minister feels unable to respond positively. We may return to the issue later, perhaps on Report, but for the moment I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

I refer to the £760,000 that the Government are giving this year to the Grant Maintained Schools Centre, which was known originally as the Grant Maintained Schools Trust. It was set up in 1988, at the time of the Education Reform Act 1988, by the now hon. Member for Epping Forest (Mr. Norris). At that time he was not a Member of Parliament. He was such a great man that the people of Oxford, East had voted him out of office in the 1987 general election. However, it was second time lucky for him when the people of Epping Forest elected him at a by-election a few years ago.

The hon. Member for Epping Forest was responsible for establishing the Grant Maintained Schools Trust in 1988 for the purpose of advocating grant-maintained status for schools. It was to advise schools that wanted to become grant maintained and to campaign for the view that such status was the correct way forward. The organisation sought public funding, but was denied by the then Secretary of State, the right hon. Member for Mole Valley, because part of its remit was to campaign and as the right hon. Gentleman rightly said, campaigning would be a misuse of public money. It applied also in 1988 for charitable status, which was refused by the charity commissioners because of its partisan nature and campaigning activities.

As a result of those two refusals, the Grant Maintained Schools Trust split into two apparently separate organisations. The Grant Maintained Schools Centre was specifically set up with a narrow remit, which was to advise schools that had already voted for grant-maintained status and had had their applications approved by the Secretary of State. Once that process had been completed, the Grant Maintained Schools Centre could give advice on establishing a grant-maintained school. On that basis, the House voted public money to support the Grant Maintained Schools Centre because it was no longer involved in campaigning. A separate body called Choice in Education was set up alongside it. It still exists. Choice in Education clearly and proudly states that its role is to campaign for grant-maintained status.
