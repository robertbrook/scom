Late last year I did a survey of health authoritiy attitudes to and policy on community continence services. One in 20 people in this country suffer from continence problems; some 2.3 million people. It is not a high-profile subject and I pay tribute to the dedicated professionals who provide those services for our fellow citizens. There is much ignorance about that sort of medical need, particularly among general practitioners. However, I shall concentrate on the health services' attitude to people living in the community but usually outside their own homes—that is, in residential homes.

I asked health authorities what I thought were five simple questions. However, each question had two subsections to it and it has taken me about four months to analyse the response that I received. I had responses from 86.5 percent of health authorities, or 165 authorities. That was a substantial wad of detail. I want to share with the Minister the broad statistical breakdown because it highlights a problem relevant Image Col. 43 to the type of residential accommodation that we are discussing.

I asked health authorities whether they supplied continence aids to patients who lived in residential homes and whether they sold aids to the owners of nursing homes. There is a legal difference, so I concentrated on residential homes. Eighty-three authorities, 51 percent of those responding, supplied named patients in homes, not the homes per se; 20 authorities—12 per cent—supplied patients in both residential and nursing homes; 12 authorities supplied patients in nursing homes, but not residential homes; 44 authorities, 28 per cent, did not supply patients in residential or nursing homes.

I take a simplistic view about the matter. There is considerable divergence of policy among district health authorities. That is partly caused by the operation of circular HN (88)26, which reminded authorities that they can supply patients in their own homes for free and that they could supply patients in residential care. Authorities, because of the use of the words "could" and "may", withdrew supplies to patients in residential homes across the country. That has caused considerable difficulties.

I asked health authorities whether people living in residential homes should receive equal treatment in respect of a public service, irrespective of whether they are in local authority, private sector or voluntary sector homes and irrespective also of whether they are self-funded—mainly by the use of disposable assets, such as the family home—or funded by the Department of Social Security.

For people in residential homes, such accommodation is their home. They may not have their own front door with a letter box, though some do. We are discussing people who are in residential homes, as opposed to those in residential care, and who have more facilities and independence than people usually have in traditional residential care establishments. Even to pose the question of whether people who live in residential homes should receive the same public service as people living in their own homes is to answer it. Such people should not be treated any differently.

Some health authorities had doubts, but I shall not go through my summary of quotes. I am talking about supplying named patients in homes who have had proper nursing assessments.
