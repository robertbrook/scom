: I welcome you back to the Chair, Sir John. It is reassuring to see you presiding over the Committee again.

In some ways, the amendment is unnecessary. The simple and straightforward reason is that the nature of the process—by which a school holds a ballot and presents proposals that are considered by the Secretary of State and are then subject to a period of objections—means that there is hardly an element of surprise when that school secures final approval and is able to fulfil the conditions of clause 7. That cannot happen suddenly or overnight.

In addition, paragraph (b) of amendment No. 137 would mean that, given certain timings of the holding of a ballot and the submission of a proposal, there could be a possible delay of 14 or 15 months before the process is started. I am sure that the amendment was not tabled with mischevious intent but there are certain circumstances—I have not worked out the exact number of days that would be involved—in which, if a ballot were held in the early JPEG 511 part of the year and the process were finalised just after April, it would effectively be another 12 months before the Secretary of State could invoke the provisions of clause 7. That is an unreasonable and unacceptable delay in what would be a familiar process, in which the prevailing local circumstances would be well understood. Clause 248(4) provides for the Secretary of State to make different arrangements where it is necessary to take account of special circumstances. That allows a flexibility for local circumstances that members of the Committee should find reassuring.

The smooth and effective working of clause 7 is in the Secretary of State's interest. When we reach the final stage of the schools' proposals and their approval by the Secretary of State, he will wish to ensure the efficiency of the arrangements that invoke clause 7. We wish to probe the 10 per cent. point because a number of its factors must be clarified.

We should like to know at what point in the year the 10 per cent, would come into operation: would it be as a result of the annual returns of form 7? Once the forms were in, the Secretary of State might study them. In an LEA in which more than 10 per cent. of the pupils were in grant-maintained schools—probably in the secondary sector as it is more likely to happen there, and has happened there already—would that be the set of figures on which the Secretary of State would make a decision? Would he wait until September, when the schools have done their own enumeration of the number of pupils in schools at that point? Would he use a September enumeration as confirmation of the form 7 returns?

What would the Secretary of State do if the number of pupils making up the 10 per cent. was on the margin? In the London borough of Lewisham LEA, one of the 12 schools constitutes 15 per cent. of the secondary school population. If one school out of 12 constituted more than 10 per cent. of pupils, would the Secretary of State be prepared to make an order to give the funding agency joint responsibility for providing school places? Would he restrict the order to the secondary sector, or would it apply to both the secondary and the primary sector? Does he intend to maintain a separation of the two sectors when making such orders?

Lewisham also has three secondary schools that are on the verge of constituting 10 per cent. of the pupil population. In the form 7 returns for January 1992, the secondary roll in Lewisham was 10,528. One school had 1,115 pupils, another had 1,008 and another had 1,099. In September 1992, the actual number in those schools was JPEG 513 1,192, 1,131 and 1,199 respectively in a secondary school roll of 11,276. Each of those schools is at the margins of the 10 per cent. trigger point. The three schools experienced a reduction in numbers as the term went on: some of the pupils at 16-plus decided to leave school when they found jobs or places on a training course. On top of that, the overall numbers in Lewisham increased because places had been filled in other secondary schools.

Ten per cent. is a low figure and in a small education authority, several secondary schools could have pupil numbers on the margins of 10 per cent. of the total pupil population. School populations fluctuate so that the pupil population of grant-maintained schools may be above and below the 10 per cent. mark during the year. We believe that a higher trigger point of 25 per cent. would be better. There would be less of an argument at the margins because there would be more pupils in the grant-maintained sector.

It is not clear from the clause which secondary school pupils would count. Local authority schools, grant-maintained schools and voluntary-aided schools would clearly be counted, but would city technology colleges and sixth form colleges be included in the LEA figure—many comprehensive schools have sixth forms, but there are also many sixth form colleges? What about pupils of secondary school age who are in special schools? Which pupils will be included in the count, especially in the secondary sector?

What date will be used for the 10 per cent. trigger point? Will any other considerations be taken into account for the mechanism to be brought into action? I hope that the Minister will clarify the Department's thinking on those matters.
