Mr. Griffiths
: The amendment is crucial to the clause, which clause repeats the provisions of the Education Act 1981. It instructs local education authorities to

"keep under review the arrangements made by them for special educational provision."

Given the extra thought that the Government appear to have given to part III, some of the concessions that they have already announced and some of the amendments that they have tabled, we thought that they might have reconsidered clause 142. We believe—as do the Special Education Consortium and all the organisations across the 1082 spectrum of special educational needs and activities that belong to it—that restricting the review of the arrangements made by the LEA leaves provision that may be made in grant-maintained schools in limbo. It could be argued that such provision will be covered by the duties laid upon governors of grant-maintained schools to ensure that the special educational needs of children attending those schools are catered for. But we believe that a gap appears between the provision made in grant-maintained schools and that made by the LEAs, under their responsibilities for special educational needs, as set out in the 1981 Act.

When a child has a statement, it might be argued that the problem does not arise. The child will have been assessed and statemented. A grant-maintained school may have been named in the statement and it would have to accept the child. In that sense, we are fairly sure and would hope that, given good will on all sides, the process will work. However, we believe that there is the potential for confusion. Given that far fewer schools are likely to opt out at primary level than at secondary level, it is also possible that although needs may be met at primary level such provision may not survive easily at the secondary level. Therefore, amendment No. 422 requires the LEA not simply to review its provison but to plan it and, in so doing, to consult every body, including grant-maintained schools, that has an interest in ensuring that in any local authority area proper provision will be made for all children with special educational needs.

I am surprised that the Department for Education has not produced a more expansive amendment. Circular 7/91 provides a good framework for a

"clear and coherent authority-wide policy".

Without the involvement of grant-maintained schools in planning and provision, the local authority's policy will be fatally flawed. The Government have admitted that local authorities should have a major role, and that more than one authority should co-operate in the provision of special education, especially if it is highly specialised. The Government hope that when the grant for education support and training is no longer available, local authorities will make their own arrangements. Some local authorities are already considering that possibility.

It is crucial that the Government accept amendment No. 422 at least in principle so that there is proper overall provision. Deaf Accord has given the example of a shire county which recently identified the need for an upper school to provide total communication in order to take children currently educated in a total communication primary school. If all the upper schools were grant maintained and none of them could provide total communication techniques and support, it is likely that they would be unable to take those children unless the local authority approached them and obtained prior agreement. If provisions for such planning were enshrined in the Bill, including consultation with grant-maintained schools, it would ensure that even if all secondary schools were grant maintained, special provision at primary level would also be available at secondary level.

Deaf Accord and other organisations involved with special educational needs are worried that there could be a breakdown in provision between primary and secondary level. The amendment recommends that the reviews to be carried out should be co-ordinated along the lines of section 19 of the Children Act 1989. The Special Education Consortium and its members—I mention Deaf Accord in JPEG 1083 particular because it has raised the point—are worried about the high incidence of abuse of deaf children. The National Deaf Children's Society has a project entitled "Keep Deaf Children Safe". That organisation believes that the amendment's provision for a review would ensure that representations from, for example, the local authority social services department and the health authority, were taken into account. The amendment refers to health authorities, but trusts now exist in many areas. The amendment should, therefore, state, "health authority or trust". I am sure that the Government will accept the principle of the amendment and improve it. Deaf Accord and the National Deaf Children's Society support the collaborative review arrangements set out in the amendment.

The Special Education Consortium fears that unless something more than a LEA review of its own arrangements is established, gaps in the provision for special educational needs will appear as more schools become grant maintained. The amendment seeks to overcome the problems and to set up a planning process, which was suggested by the Select Committee on Education, Science and Arts in 1987 when it said:

"Our experience is that it is important for an LEA to have a clear statement of its policy on integration, developed in consultation with parents and professionals, as part of its overall policy on special education. Parental choice is a necessary qualifier of, but not a substitute for, sensible strategic planning by an LEA."

We should allow a reasonable time to discuss all the important issues that we must tackle today. I hope that the Minister will either explain how the Bill already covers the points that I have raised or accept an amendment to clause 142 to provide for comprehensive provision, planning and a review, which would incorporate grant-maintained schools and other organisations, such as local authority social services departments.

Like many other members of the Committee, I have been approached by constituents who have spoken of the difficulties they encountered when trying to meet the needs of their children. The amount of time taken to determine whether children have special educational needs and the provision that must be made for them must be kept under 1084 constant review. Parents can experience terrible frustration at the long delay in the process. As months slip by perceptive psychological and educational damage is caused to pupils. We have heard nothing to suggest that the process from referral to the making of a statement will not remain a long, drawn-out affair. It is important to bear in mind that the rationale for that process is the protection of children, because we are concerned about their protection and right to appropriate provision when that is necessary.

Welsh Office and Department for Education guidelines recommend that the time from referral to the preparation of the draft statement should be six months. However, I am told that the median for England and Wales is about 12 months. One LEA has been cited as requiring as long as three years for the process to be completed.

We must also bear in mind that at the time of referral, when the process is initiated, the child is already experiencing difficulties. That is why the referral takes place, it usually happens only after parents have made several visits to a school to raise the issue. In most cases, the process is initiated by the parents. A constituent of mine described how the process took two years and three months. It started when the child was nine and a half-years-old, when the parents initiated the process after the child had suffered a crisis. It was 12 months before the child saw a psychologist, I understand that it is not unusual for six months to pass before that occurs. The child was nearly 12-years-old before the first draft was provided and then it was unsatisfactory. There was a long wait for a psychologist, another for a medical report and further delay before a statement was drafted.

There is no doubt that a great deal can be done, even within existing resources, to tighten the procedure to ensure that everything is better co-ordinated. It is essential that we should recognise that as a priority, even within the available resources.

I acknowledge that the present resources are inadequate but that is not a sufficient reason or excuse for the delays that often occur. The establishment of tribunals will expedite the appeals procedures but I emphasise that, when the code of conduct is published, it must set down strict guidelines for the time taken for the process to be completed. It is essential for LEAs constantly to review their procedures, as the amendment advocates. It is for local authorities to establish the right practice, then it is up to society—all of us—to ensure that the resources are provided. Society must take that decision. In its scale of values, the needs of those pupils must receive higher priority.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Schools (Mr. Eric Forth)
: The amendment touches on a vital part of the provision for special educational needs. At the outset, I should remind the Committee of the philosophical and practical basis for the concept of grant-maintained schools, lest we have forgotten that because it seems so long since we debated it. Grant-maintained schools are given the option, after parental ballot, of leaving the control of their local education authority to become more autonomous and independent. In doing that, they are charged with JPEG 1087 continuing to make proper provision for children with special educational needs. That is an important and continuing responsibility for grant-maintained schools.

Such schools will come increasingly under the aegis of the funding agency for schools. Therefore, depending on the stage reached in a particular area—we considered stages 1, 2 and 3 earlier—it is logical and correct for the funding agency, the local authority or both, when appropriate, to have responsibility for planning the provision of places for children with special educational needs and for providing the necessary funding to support those places. If we start from that position—I would characterise it as the mainstream provision—the provision of places for children with special educational needs will be under the two different regimes that we discussed earlier: local education authorities and the funding agency for schools.

I recognise the points made by the hon. Member for Bridgend. If you will allow me, Mr. Martin, I shall leap to consideration of clause 144 and our proposed amendment to it. We shall create a powerful new tool in the requirement on all schools, during the annual reporting process, to report on the implementation of their governing bodies' policies for pupils with special educational needs. Schools will be required to identify clearly and to detail the provisions that they are making for special educational needs pupils. That is an important element in the process.

We have already considered clause 137, which is entitled

"Provision of information by governing body".

It should be recalled that subsection (4) states:

"The governing body shall make such reports and returns, and give such information, to any local education authority by whom any functions are exercisable—

  1. in relation to the school, or
  2. in relation to registered pupils at the school,".

Self-evidently and importantly, that will include the special educational needs provisions to which I referred.

A picture is emerging that I believe goes all the way towards meeting the concerns expressed by the hon. Member for Bridgend. We have detailed provisions for schools to report on their provision for special educational needs. The overall and overriding requirement is on the governing bodies of grant-maintained schools, in particular, to give such reports and returns as are necessary to the local education authority. That covers the provision of information.

The co-ordination element comes in two different forms. First, the funding agency for schools will identify needs and make provision for them through the funding mechanism. Secondly, under, I think, clauses 5 and 14, LEAs and the funding agency for schools will, in turn, have to provide each other with information and to act co-operatively in some circumstances.
