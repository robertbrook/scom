Mr. Win Griffiths
(Bridgend)
: On a point of order, Sir John. I seek your advice on a matter relating to the financial memorandum to the Bill. In Committee on 28 January, the Under-Secretary of State for Further and Higher Education referred to the cost of tribunals. There is an issue at stake on which I require your guidance, Sir John. The Under-Secretary said:

"We estimate the annual cost of setting up a tribunal at £1 million a year."—[ Official Report, Standing Committee E, 28 January 1993; c. 1181.]

 However, in the financial memorandum to the Bill, on page xvii, it states:

"The costs of the Tribunal will be fully covered by savings in the running costs of central Government and in the administration costs of local education authorities whose appellate functions will transfer in full to the new Tribunal."

 Therefore, I asked the Department and the Welsh Office how many appeals were handled by local authorities in each of the past three years. The reply from the Department for Education was:

"The information requested is not available centrally."

 And the reply from the Welsh Office was:

"Information is not held centrally on the number of appeals against the provision in a statement of special educational needs which went before a local appeals committee nor of the costs of running the system in each local education authority. However, the number of appeals against statements to my right hon. Friend are shown as follows".—[ Official Report, 5 February 1993; Vol. 218, c. 347–64.]

 There have been none this year, and there were three last year and two the year before.

How can the financial memorandum state that the costs will be financially neutral, when the Department for Education does not know what it costs local authorities to run their appeals system? On top of that, the Department for Education seems to have plucked a figure out of the air. How can the statement in the financial memorandum be correct? Where does the Department for Education get that estimate of £1 million? It says that it has estimated the cost of running the appeals in each local authority in each of the past three years, but it has no information on that.

I seek your advice, Sir John, on how we can get to the bottom of this. How does the Department for Education know that the cost will be financially neutral when it does not know what it costs local authorities to run their appeals system?

The Chairman

Mr. Griffiths
: It is a pity that the Minister did not explain new clause 22 to the Committee. I understand that it provides for the funding agency to take over the responsibilities of the Department for Education for funding city technology colleges under section 105 of the Education Reform Act 1988. Under that section, the city technology colleges are funded through a formal agreement between the Department for Education and the proprietor of each college. The amount of funding is based on the estimated cost of pupil funding in a group of LEAs. However, CTCs have no access to the special purpose 1434 grants that other schools may receive. The Government seem to intend CTCs to be funded in the same way as grant-maintained schools.

City technology colleges have been funded more generously, in terms of capital costs, than any grant-maintained school, despite the initial bribes to persuade maintained schools to opt out. Middlesbrough CTC managed to attract only £1·5 million of private money out of the £6 million that was needed to set it up. The Government's contribution of £4·3 million was far more than the £3·6 million that Cleveland county council received in the same year for 238 schools.

Perhaps the Government believe that the new clause will mitigate the embarrassing failure of their policy for CTCs. The so-called Gerbil, envisaged that there would be 20 CTCs by the early 1990s. The then Secretary of State spoke of the Conservative party conference about the funding of the 20 CTCs. He stated that

"all or a substantial part"

 of the capital costs would come from private sources. The reality has been exactly the opposite. Only 16 CTCs are running and the Government have had the devil's own job trying to get industrialists involved in their sponsorship.

The new clause gives the Government the opportunity to get themselves off an embarrassing hook. They are subsuming the funding of CTCs into the funding agency and the grant-maintained sector. I do not know whether the existing CTCs should be alarmed for their financial future; even if the Government had kept the CTCs separately funded from the funding agency for grant-maintained schools, they could still, no doubt, decide to change the funding formula to bring it in line with grant-maintained schools.

At the moment, the capital funding of CTCs is 79 times the funding provided for local authority schools. It is phenomenal to think of money being wasted on a totally unnecessary experiment. If the money had been available to the mainstream sector of education, far more could have been done with it. Nationally published test results and inspectors' reports show that the experiment failed: CTCs had poor results in technology and there were critical reports from the inspectorate on the delivery of technological subjects. Perhaps, over time, the name of CTC will be changed and an inglorious experiment in English educational history will end. I say English educational history because the experiment has not been conducted in Wales or Scotland, for which the Celts are thankful. I hope that the corpse will be buried and that the funding will keep on a more even keel for maintained schools as well.
