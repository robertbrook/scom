On Second Reading, I quoted from the evidence of the Welsh joint education committee. That body also stated:

"The Joint Committee sees no justification for yet further centralisation of powers and decisonmaking by the Secretary of State for Wales. It views with deep concern for the future health of the democratic process within Wales, the creation of yet further nominated no-elected bodies".

The phrase "deep concern" is certainly no exaggeration. On Second Reading, I said that the removal of democratic accountability will lead, inevitably, to greater insensitivity on the part of public servants, who currently must be constantly aware of the options of elected members and who may become increasingly arrogant and heedless of public opinion without elected members breathing down their necks.

The amendment proposes that eight members of the funding council would be county councillors. An alteration to that figure after the local government reorganisation would have to be discussed, but that difficulty demonstrates how unreasonable it is to introduce the Bill ahead of local government reorganisation. Those councillors would reflect the views of their councils and therefore of their electors. They would provide a channel of direct communication about circumstances and priorities in their areas. They would also provide a link with the local bureaucracies that will spring up once the funding council is established and begins to gain in importance. The amendment also allows for the co-option of members. It recognises that there is a useful role for people with professional qualifications and experience who can be selected according to their expertise in and experience of aspects of educational provision such as special needs, school inspection and finance. The amendment does not change the Bill's conditions for membership, although I can see great merit in the other amendments in this batch which do.

My amendment is a less than satisfactory way in which to represent democratic opinion, but it is less unsatisfactory than the Bill, which seriously undermines the democratic process. Its far-reaching effects can only be imagined now. The amendment would reduce the damage and make the creation of the funding council less unsatisfactory. I commend it to the Committee, along with 168 the other amendments in this group that are compatible with the structure of the council that I have described in amendments Nos. 39, 42 and 52.
