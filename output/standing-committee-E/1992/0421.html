Bail (Amendment) Bill

10.30 am

Mr Michael Stephen
(Shoreham)
: I beg to move,

That if the proceedings on the Bail (Amendment) Bill are not completed at this day's sitting, the Committee do meet on Wednesdays at half-past Ten o'clock.

 I introduced the Bill because my constituents and, I am sure, those of all other hon. Members are seriously concerned about offences committed by persons on bail. In any constituency, one finds on inquiring that those offences are committed by a small group—usually of young men—who, having been arrested and let out on bail by magistrates, re-offend time and time again before their trial for the original offence. It is not unknown for one young man to have committed more than 200 offences before he reaches the age of 20.

The majority of those offences relate to burglary and car crime. Statistics are hard to find, but Northumbria police has done valuable work in this area under the supervision of Professor Openshaw of Newcastle university. I wish to acknowledge their contribution to this important matter.

Since 1989, Northumbria police has kept records of offences committed on bail and it has found that in case of theft or the taking of a motor vehicle, 44.6 per cent. of detected crimes are committed by a person on bail. In the case of house burglaries the situation is worse, with 53.5 per cent. of all detected crimes committed by a person on bail.

Clause 1
PROSECUTION OF RIGHT OF APPEAL

Mr. Stephen
: On a point of order, Mr. Hill. Amendments Nos. 1 and 3 to 9 would remove the words which restrict the right of appeal to the decisions of magistrates and apply it also to the decisions of Crown courts in bail cases. I believe that that would be a desirable amendment because, although much rarer, mistakes in bail cases do occur in Crown courts. I have, however, taken the advice of experienced colleagues who have rightly pointed out that if one seeks to achieve too much in a private Members' Bill one may achieve nothing in the end.

Amendment No. 10, which is grouped with amendment No. 13, would specify the courts to which appeals would lie for magistrates and Crown Courts. However, in view of what I have said about the Crown Courts, I am content with the existing wording of clause 1(1) which provides that appeals should lie with a judge of the High Court or a Crown Court. Therefore I do not intend to move amendment No. 1.

The Chairman
: Very well.

Mr. Stephen
: I beg to move amendment No. 2, in page 1, line 6, after "with", insert "or convicted of".

After conviction, magistrates may remand a defendant for reports or other reasons and they may decide to remand that person on bail. If the prosecution is to have a right of appeal when bail is granted to a person who is charged, there is an even stronger case for it to have a right of appeal after that person has been convicted but before he has been sentenced.

Mr. Michael
: I beg to move amendment No. 16, in page 1, line 7, after "more", insert

"or an offence of taking a vehicle without the owner's consent, and it is alleged by the prosecution that there is a risk to the public of serious harm if bail is granted".

 I hope that the Committee will be business-like to enable the hon. Member for Shoreham to return to the House with an improved and effective Bill. Amendment No. 16 is especially important in that regard. Clause 1 applies to offences punishable by a term of imprisonment of five years or more. That sounds perfectly reasonable as a starting point, until two things are realised. First, less serious offences would be caught by that requirement. For instance, the theft of a milk bottle is technically an offence that falls within the clause. I am sure that hon. Members would agree that the theft of a milk bottle even if repeated and further offences were committed by the accused while on bail, is not a crime of the seriousness of others for which the Bill is intended.

Secondly, the offence of taking a vehicle without the owner's consent does not carry a penalty of five years or more and therefore would not be caught by the Bill. Theft of a motor vehicle would be covered, but to prosecute successfully for theft, the prosecuting counsel would have to prove an intention permanently to deprive the owner of the vehicle. Because of the difficulty of proving that, the charge of taking a vehicle without the owner's consent is the charge more normally brought in cases related to the serious business of death riding. I am sure that hon. Members on both sides of the Committee would agree that death riding is a more appropriate term than joy riding.

Often, offences of taking without the owner's consent, even if they are not immediately dangerous or life threatening, are dangerous to the public because the person who takes a vehicle does not have insurance. If an accident occurs, a person can be injured, maimed for life or can lose property for which there is no recompense. I know that the hon. Member for Shoreham takes that offence seriously and amendment No. 16 allows for it to be included.

The amendment is balanced by ensuring that the prosecution needs to allege that there would be a risk of serious harm to the public if bail were granted. By providing a proper safeguard, the amendment would tighten the clause and make it more effective. Hon. Members may question the use of the term "alleged" rather than "proved". If risk to the public had to be proved before there could be a right of appeal, it would place an onerous restriction on the clause and that is not my aim. Obviously, if the prosecution felt that there were a serious risk, it would need to allege it. The matter would then be raised on appeal in the appropriate court, where the right weight would be given to evidence of likely further offending and any risk to the public. The need to allege would not obstruct the application of the clause in circumstances in which the police and the prosecution felt that the granting of bail was likely to lead to the type of problems to which the hon. Member for Shoreham referred earlier. Therefore, it would allow action on issues which have—I agree with the hon. Gentleman's opening remarks—caused the public and the police grave concern.

I hope that the amendment will be seen as a constructive contribution towards achieving fair and, 6 effective legislation, which will assist the courts and the criminal justice system generally.

I am confident that the Bill will reach the statute book and I am delighted to see it proceed. I congratulate the hon. Member for Shoreham on introducing it. My confidence allows me to say that the Bill will reach the statute book. However, the evils which it addresses will not have a remedy in one of the jurisdictions of the United Kingdom. The problem that the Bill will tackle exists in Northern Ireland to the same extent that it exists elsewhere. It is true that in Diplock cases there are restrictions on the right to bail, and properly so. However, in other cases, we suffer from the same problem as the rest of the country, and in some respects it is worse. When professional criminals come before the courts, there is a tendency for people to say, "Oh, these are ordinary, decent criminals. They are not nasty terrorists." There is a greater readiness to grant bail. That is a serious problem.

The problem is serious in my constituency. On a couple of public housing estates, there are grave problems with repeat offenders, who carry out mainly burglaries. They have destroyed—by that I mean demolished—90 per cent. of one estate. They have now started on another. Hundreds of houses have been pulled down as a result of the activities of such gentlemen. They will supply kitchen or bathroom suites. If none are to hand, they will lob a few petrol bombs into a house to clear out the people, strip out the goods and sell them.

Such people are brought regularly before the courts and regularly given bail. It would be delightful if the Bill's provisions were extended to Northern Ireland. The thought of tabling an amendment to that effect crossed my mind, but I realised that that would delay proceedings. It would be delightful if the Minister would convey to his colleagues in the Northern Ireland Office the message that we want equivalent legislation because there will be an anomaly. Such an anomaly should be tackled.
