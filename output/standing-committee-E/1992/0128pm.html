Mr. Stephen Byers
(Wallsend)
: I beg to move amendment No. 644, in page 93, line 20, at end insert—

'() for the provision of legal advice and assistance by way of representation under Part III of the Legal Aid Act 1988, for representatives who appear on behalf of the child or the child's parents.'.

 The amendment would deal with some of the difficulties that might arise as a result of some of the decisions taken by the Committee this morning, especially that to keep the requirement in clause 158 that the chairman of the tribunal should have at least seven years legal qualification. There is genuine anxiety that, because of that decision, tribunals will become overly legalistic.

Many Members of the Committee will be aware that when tribunals have been established and the chairman is required to be legally qualified, that has encouraged them to become legalistic in their approach. Those tribunals that are chaired by a lay person are more informal and not so legalistic … Many hon. Members will have had that experience.

Mr. James Clappison
(Hertsmere)
: I agree with the hon. Gentleman's point about legalistic tribunals. All lawyers would do well at all times to steer clear of being legalistic—[HON. MEMBERS: "Hear, hear."] I consider it a serious allegation to call a lawyer legalistic.

I understand the spirit of the hon. Gentleman's remarks, but does he consider there to be a distinction between the chairman of a tribunal who takes a legalistic approach—that would be an unhappy development—and one who applies his legal training and experience to do justice in a case and to help the parties as much as possible? It is possible to apply legal training while, at the same time, taking a humane and practical approach to the merits of a case and the sensitivities of those involved.

Mr. Byers
: I thank you, Sir John, for that guidance. I am trying to show why legal aid would be necessary for representation. If the tribunals consider cases legalistically, the case for legal aid for the persons appearing before them is strengthened.

There is a common agreement on both sides of the Committee that tribunals should act informally and that the experience of the parents appearing before the tribunals should not be threatening or daunting. We must 1188 recognise that the tribunals will have to consider many difficult issues. It is important to ensure that parents are given proper and appropriate support.

The hon. Member for Hastings and Rye (Mrs. Lait) pointed out this morning that the tribunals would be seen as a "court of the last resort". That explains in simple terms the importance that will be attached to them.

The amendment recognises that the tribunals will often have to deal with difficult issues. It is, therefore, appropriate that legal aid is available for representation at them. It is strange that legal aid will be available for advice to parents in preparing their case to put before the tribunal, but that it will be denied for the representation itself.

Many hon. Members feel that the advice procedure will be less daunting and complex for parents than the presentation before the tribunal.

Our debate this morning identified that the tribunals will be subject to the provisions laid down by the Council on Tribunals. As a result, they will be judicial in nature—whether we like it or not—and the proceedings will be complex and daunting. No matter how much we want the proceedings to be informal, they can never be given the powers that the tribunals will have. They will have the power to summon witnesses, to discover and produce documentary evidence. They will also require people giving evidence to swear an oath and, if somebody refuses, the person will be committing a criminal offence.

We are not talking about a cosy, fire side chat. The tribunals will have all the powers of a court of law, namely, to compel appearance of a person; to discover documents; and to charge the individual with a criminal offence, if he refuses to co-operate. That is why we feel that legal aid would be appropriate for representation.

It is not only the proceedings that will be daunting; the law will also be complex. On Tuesday, we began to identify the difficulties that might be caused because of the application of the law. This morning, the hon. Member for Hertsmere (Mr. Clappison) said that points of law would be raised in the tribunals.

Mr. Clappison
: The hon. Gentleman may have misunderstood me, or I may not have expressed myself sufficiently clearly. I referred to the same material to which he has just referred relating to the operation of the powers of the tribunal—which I suggest are very important—rather than to it taking points of law. I hope that the tribunals will steer away from the creation of precedent and the like.

Mr. Byers
: I am sure that we would all welcome that, if it were possible. I am afraid that, from our experience of tribunals, that is not the case. Tribunals operate in several different areas and precedents are set. If there are legal chairmen, they will talk to each other—we know the nature of the legal profession—in various establishments and precedents, either formally or informally, will be duly established.

The proceedings will not only be daunting, but parents will have to deal with complex legal issues. I do not want parents to be left alone or with the support only of a friend in such circumstances. It is important to provide parents with the support of legal aid for representation. That suggestion would not break new ground. There are tribunals in which legal aid is available for representation, for example, in the Lands Tribunal; in employment appeal tribunals and in mental health review tribunals. All types JPEG 1189 of legal aid are available for those tribunals, which begs the question of why we should treat appearances before the special education needs tribunal though it was a second-class tribunal. The Committee recognise the importance of setting up that tribunal. It should be valued and given sufficient support.

In its 33rd report, the Lord Chancellor's advisory committee suggested that six areas should be considered when deciding whether legal aid should be applied to a tribunal's work. Four of those areas apply specifically to special education needs tribunals. The committee believes that legal aid should be available, first, if a high proportion of cases involve difficult points of law. Tuesday's debate showed that that is likely. Secondly, it should be available where the issues being considered involve complex or disputed facts. That will often be the case as disputes will be between a local authority and a parent. Thirdly, it should be available where a substantial proportion of respondents are represented—local authorities will have legal representation and, fourthly, where the tribunal's decision may lead to a loss of livelihood or sole source of income. It is important to children with special education needs that we decide on the right provision to ensure that such a thing does not happen.

The Lord Chancellor's advisory committee has identified four priorities which, I believe, the special education needs tribunal satisfies. A local authority will always have representation. No matter how much we would like both parties to go to the tribunal seeking consensus and compromise, the tribunal will be the last act of a difficult and drawn-out process. It would be useful to write a process of arbitration into the Bill, but we have not tabled an amendment to achieve that which has been accepted by the Chairman. Local authorities have large law departments and will brief experienced barristers, if not Silks, who take a case on their behalf.

The costs should come as no surprise. If the tribunal decides that a statement should be amended—it has that power—to provide education in an independent boarding school, the cost to the local authority will be between £20,000 and £25,000 a year. Substantial sums of money will be involved. A local authority will brief counsel for a two or three day appearance so that it is properly represented in view of the large costs involved. I do not want a lone parent, perhaps supported by a friend, to appear before a tribunal and be faced by a local authority and all the powers available to it. Legal aid should be made available to such parents to ensure proper representation.

Evidence of and research into the work of tribunals clearly demonstrate that applicants with support and legal representation have a far higher success rate than those who appear alone or are supported just by a friend. The Lord Chancellor's Department commissioned research from Hazel Genn, reader at Queen Mary college, university of London, on the effectiveness of representation at tribunals. It was published in 1989 and examined three tribunals that are relevant to the special education needs tribunal. It was found that 42 per cent. of appeals to the social security appeals tribunal were successful when applicants appeared alone. Over half were successful when there was legal representation. Only 9 per cent. of immigration appeals were allowed in total or in part if the individual was alone or backed by a relative or friend. If 1190 a solicitor appeared alongside the applicant, 37 per cent. were successful. Over half the cases brought before industrial tribunals in which applicants had the support of a solicitor were settled or allowed. The success rate of individuals appearing alone or with a friend or relative was down to one third. The Lord Chancellor's Department's research shows that providing legal representation boosts considerably the chances of success. That begs the question, should not the Committee try to support parents and improve their chance of success? If we do not support the amendment, we shall vote to accept a system that will make fewer parental appeals successful than if legal representation was granted.

The Government wish the chair of a tribunal to be legally qualified. The law will be complex, and procedures daunting. The Lord Chancellor's advisory committee recommends support by legal aid in those circumstances. We must create opportunities and increase parents' success rate. Legal aid would do that.

Our amendment is relevant. If the Government refuse legal aid representation to applicants for tribunals, they will deny parents their legal rights. The Act will provide parents with the opportunity to achieve justice while denying them the means of securing it. We hope that the strength of our arguments will find favour with the Minister and that he will respond positively to our amendment.
