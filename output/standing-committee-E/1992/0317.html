As a 114 result of the constructive debate last week, I should like to withdraw amendments Nos. 111 and 112 and in due course formally to move amendments Nos. 122 and 123 in their place.

Amendments Nos. 122 and 123 would require the owner of the land where an offence had been committed by the destruction or impairment of the quality of a hedgerow to replace that hedgerow with one that consists of shrubs and trees of an appropriate species that will correspond to it. I listened carefully to the concerns raised last week by the hon. Member for Denton and Reddish (Mr. Bennett). Like him, I certainly do not want local authorities to insist that tall, gappy hedgerows should be replaced by similarly tall, ill-managed shrubs. While the Bill cannot ensure that hedgerows are well managed, it is certainly appropriate that a replacement hedge would get the best possible start. I agree with the hon. Gentleman that, in the interests of good management, it will usually be appropriate to replant with short shrubs, which can be properly managed as they grow to give a good bottom to the hedge.

I believe that those aims are entirely consistent with my original amendments, but I am persuaded that, in the interests of clarity, those amendments should be revised. That is why the new amendments do not contain any reference to the size of the replacement shrubs. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will, therefore, be reassured on that point. I think that all members of the Committee are agreed that there needs to be a duty to replant trees and shrubs of an appropriate species. By maintaining that duty, the Bill will ensure that the replanted hedgerow has the obvious potential to grow to match the quality of the original. Of course, in most cases, it is not possible to plant a new hedge that will immediately have the same qualities as the one which was destroyed. To that extent, I am in agreement with the views of my hon. Friend the Member for Cirencester and Tewkesbury. A replacement hedge of healthy young plants needs time to grow to match the stature of the mature shrubs that it is replacing. That is why my amendments refer to trees and shrubs that will correspond. That is, future tense.

My hon. Friends' amendments Nos. 119 and 120, while very similar to my own amendments, still contain a reference to shrubs or trees that will "in time" correspond to the originals. The Committee has spent considerable time discussing semantics and I am reluctant to burden it any further, but I should like to make a further point on that subject. I realise that the intention is to make it clear that local authorities should not expect farmers and landowners to replant hedges using mature, 10 ft tall plants. However, the danger is that the phrase "in time" will mean at some undefined point in the future. The farmer or landowner then need only plant some new shrubs and leave them, arguing that, at some point, they will correspond with the hedgerow that has been lost, even if that is in 200 years' time. The problem is that "will" is, in any case, the future tense and so the addition of "in time" would appear to add nothing. However, as hon. Members know, the courts do not regard words in legislation as unnecessary, but strive to give them meaning. There is, therefore, a JPEG 115 danger that "in time" will be regarded as meaning rather more than "in the future".

My amendments already make clear that we are talking about plants that will correspond. That recognises that plants need time to grow. However, it also implies that there is a duty on the landowner to ensure that the hedgerow will correspond with the original rather than leaving it to nature and the passing of time. That is essential to the effectiveness of the Bill. I hope that my hon. Friends are reassured that farmers will not be required to replant fully-grown mature shrubs. Should further reassurance be needed, I feel certain that the guidance notes to be provided by my hon. Friend the Minister will make the matter abundantly clear. I was making that point as we concluded last week's sitting. I hope therefore, that my hon. Friends will withdraw the amendment.
