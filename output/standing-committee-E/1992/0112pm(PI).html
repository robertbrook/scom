Mr. Don Foster (Bath): I think that you, Mr. Martin, will agree that this morning I was able to make strenuous argument for the amendment. On the strength of that, I hope that it will gain the Committee's support.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Schools (Mr. Eric Forth): The amendment represents a considerable departure from what we have become accustomed to. No doubt that was the intention of the hon. Member for Bath (Mr. Foster). However, because it takes the Committee into such new territory, hon. Members would be well advised to pause and consider it carefully. The whole thrust of the Government's legislation—as it exists up to now, as it is presented in this Bill and as it is envisaged in the future—is that parents are the key to the crucial, school-by-school decisions about whether grant-maintained status should be sought.

The amendment would broaden the mechanism and would therefore affect that principle. Superficially, it appears attractive, but we must consider the merits of taking the direction that it opens up. Is the amendment necessary? Would it add anything to the process? More importantly, would it detract from the process? The hon. Gentleman, whose remarks, perhaps inevitably, were somewhat brief, given that we were approaching the break for lunch, did not make as comprehensive a case as he might otherwise have done. That is a pity, as it is important that the Committee understands all the ramifications of the amendment. The list of parents' names and addresses has been, and sometimes still is, the subject of contention. The proposal to broaden that list and make it available to school employees represents a considerable departure. What is the purpose of that proposal? Conservative Members will want to stand firm on the principle that the electorate who will make these vital Col. 732 decisions will remain the registered parents at the school. To make their details available to others—even to people who are as closely involved in the school as its employees—would be a departure that I am reluctant to allow.

The proposal to involve the head teacher belongs in a slightly different category and could complicate the matter and muddy the waters. I do not see the necessity for it. The existing procedures have worked satisfactorily. To introduce the new dimensions suggested by the amendment would open up the process beyond what has been envisaged in the Bill and the Government's policies up to now, and could complicate the matter unnecessarily. I accept the hon. Gentleman's earlier remarks at face value. He said that he was not in the business of complicating matters.

We rest our case on parental involvement and responsibility. However attractive some of the ideas behind the amendments may be, the Committee should reject them for the reasons that I have given.

Mr. Foster: I am somewhat surprised by the Minister's statement that the amendment takes us into new territory and that it could introduce new dimensions and complications. That is his main reason for opposing it. The Bill contains 255 clauses and 17 schedules. It is an enormous measure that is enormously complicated. It takes us into many new territories. I wish that the Minister would follow his own advice and not take such a measure any further. Sadly, he will not do that.

There is clear opposition to the amendment, and the Committee needs to make progress. We may gain the support of the Government on other issues. Therefore, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.
