Before dealing with the specifics of the amendment, a general point must be made about the acceleration in the rate of loss of hedgerows in recent years. During the past six years, about 7,200 miles of hedgerow have been grubbed up every year—that is a rate of 20 miles of hedgerow per day. If disintegration of hedgerows through neglect is included—my hon. Friend the Member for 12 Denton and Reddish (Mr. Bennett) will refer to that when we consider later amendments—the net loss amounts to about 12,500 miles per year after taking account of new planting. That means that almost 10 per cent. of hedgerow cover in England and Wales disappears every year. As legislators we must be concerned about that because hedgerows are such important features of our landscape and wildlife habitats. The purpose of the Bill is justifiably and rightly to provide additional protection for hedgerows.

This group of amendments explores why it is not possible to extend the scope of the Bill to cover hedgerows which are not included in its provisions. The Bill excludes small hedges of less than 20 m, and none of us has any argument with that Clause 1(2) states that a hedgerow will be protected only if it

"(a) is on or adjacent to land used for agriculture or forestry"—

that presumably means that it is currently used for agriculture or forestry—

"or,


(b) is on common land, protected land or a golf-course or racecourse."

It is obviously important to protect such locations, but hedges in a multitude of other locations are equally important. One of the most important is urban areas. There may be no agriculture or forestry use and no adjacent common land or golf course, but none the less, hedges may be of value. I shall identify one or two of the hedges that fall outside the scope of the Bill but which should be covered, and I am grateful to the Royal Society for Nature Conservation for providing the information.

In Fryent park in the borough of Brent in London there are about 10 km of hedgerow. Much of the pattern has changed little from the parish maps of 1597 and they contain the nationally rare narrow-leaved bittercress.

In Leicester there are hedgerows composed of hawthorn, dog rose, sycamore, elm, blackthorn, crack willow, elder, holly and field maple. They are south of the adventure playground at Western park. Also in Leicester there is hedgerow bordering a cycle way on a disused railway line where the adjacent land is unmanaged open space and disused allotments. There are other hedgerows within and surrounding a disused sports field north of Dorset avenue, Eyres Monsell.

In Peterborough there is ancient hedge in Longthorpe, one of the older parts of the city and close to the city centre, which contains woodland plants, including dog's mercury. It is adjacent to a school playing field and housing estate. Also in Peterborough there is a mixed hedge in South Bretton alongside a footpath on a housing estate. It was part of the original old parkland and maintains a link with past landscapes. It includes mature oak, hawthorn, field maple and rose.
