Education Bill

Clause 15
"GRANT-MAINTAINED SCHOOLS"

Amendment proposed [10 December]: No. 262, in page 8, line 38, at end insert—

'or


(d) in pursuance of proposals for the purpose published under section (Proposals by governing bodies) of this Act for two or more existing grant-maintained schools to be conducted by a single governing body.'.—[ Mr. Don Foster.]

10.30 am

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman
: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking new clause 13— Proposals by governing bodies—

'(1) Regulations may make provision for two or more grant-maintained primary schools to be conducted as a group by a single governing body where the governing bodies of those schools have made joint proposals in a prescribed manner to the Secretary of State in accordance with this section.

  1. Any provision of this Part of this Act may apply, with or without modification, to—
    1. procedures prior to the making of such proposals, or
    2. such proposals, or
    3. the conduct of such schools in such manner as the Secretary of State may determine.'.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Schools (Mr. Eric Forth)
: I am grateful to the hon. Member for Bath (Mr. Foster) for tabling this amendment. I remind the Committee that clause 114 allows grant-maintained schools to propose to join an existing group of schools and for two groups to propose to merge to form a single group. Those provisions will cover unusual circumstances and will help grant-maintained schools. The amendment and the new clause set out a similar process to allow existing grant-maintained schools to propose to form a group, which is a variation on the theme. I accept that there is no reason in principle why they should not be permitted to do so. We did not allow for that because we thought it unlikely that two or more existing small grant-maintained primary schools would want to form a group or would be conveniently located to do so. We thought that it was more likely that an existing grant-maintained school might be close to an established group formed as a result of the Bill. Clause 114, will enable it to join such a group.

However, if two or more existing grant-maintained primary schools see the benefit of forming close ties in a formal group under the Bill, that is a matter for them. I am content in principle to accept the intent and the thrust of the amendment and the new clause tabled by the hon. Member for Bath. However, I should like to study the wording which probably needs to be refined. For example, it may be important to make it clear that only existing grant-maintained primary schools could form a group, just as only LEA primary schools can. I want to examine that aspect. I hope that the hon. Gentleman accepts that I 672 am content with the principle of the amendment and the new clause. I ask him to withdraw the amendment and I will undertake to introduce a new provision—I hope during the Committee stage and perhaps under a subsequent clause—to give effect to the intention of the amendment.

Mr. Don Foster
(Bath)
: Given the Minister's acceptance of the underlying principle of the amendment and the new clause and his agreement to introduce new wording later, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

The strategic role of the LEAs' planning and their service to schools have been undermined. The demands of the national curriculum require the necessary resources to be made available. The amendment would ensure that such resources were available to the grant-maintained schools from the funding authorities. All children, no matter how rich or poor their parents may be, must have books and equipment for their education. If the teachers do not have the tools, they cannot do the job for which they are paid. In 1991, four out of five schools had fewer than 10 books per student, which is well below the recommended minimum. Last year, additional spending in schools ranged from about £1·20 to £41·20 per pupil on books and materials. How can our children have a fair chance and an equal opportunity when such wide variations exist in our different LEAs?

I forgot to mention that the environment and location of schools makes a difference. Issues such as the location of the school and the type of parents who send their children to that school will determine how easy it is to raise funds. For example, my school used to educate subnormal children. We took children from poor backgrounds. We found it difficult to raise funds, whereas other schools in more affluent areas found it easier because the parents were more affluent. That produces discrepancies in different schools.

Last Wednesday, we went on a Select Committee visit to Hertfordshire. We were pleasantly surprised at the provisions made by the LEA. One school for the physically handicapped that we visited raised £80,000 for the school fund—that is almost as much as some schools get in their capital allocation allowance. Some schools raise funds and can give their kids a far better chance while other schools find it harder to raise funds. I do not begrudge that money going into that school, but, it is so unfair and makes life difficult for other schools that are trying to compete in the same atmosphere.

Most importantly, the introduction of pupil-led funding has exposed the disparities in levels of available finance to schools. The available money varies significantly between schools—even between schools of the same size. I shall give examples of that; they have been carefully researched to ensure that the criteria was exactly the same for each secondary school in different LEAs. I shall pick one school at random: The Haydock high school in St. Helens has 738 pupils. It has no sixth form and its budget is £1,421,441. Holgate school in Barnsley has 792 pupils JPEG 675 and no sixth form and its budget is £1·3 million—a difference of £106,000 between two schools in separate authority areas. Resources for Haydock school are greater than those for Holgate, yet they are similar schools, providing a similar service, with almost the same number of pupils.

I have another example, in which the discrepancy is even greater. The Marches school, in Shropshire, has 1,146 pupils. Again it has no sixth form, and its budget is £2·26 million. Tameside local education authority's Two Trees school has 1,122 pupils. Its budget is £1·744 million—a difference of £500,000. How can it be regarded as fair distribution of resourcing if one school has £500,000 more to spend than another school in another LEA? Those are two examples of secondary schools. There are similar astronomic differences in funding for primary schools.

Member for Rugby and Kenilworth (Mr. Pawsey) because of his intervention. 676 The absence of clear guidelines on the Government's proposals for funding grant-maintained schools is hardly helpful.

The amendment would ensure that, whichever mechanism is chosen, the funding will have regard to the minimum amount of resources that a school needs to deliver good quality education. The amendment would limit the size of classes because the larger the class the less attention a teacher can give to a child.

The size of classes is increasing. The hon. Member for Croydon, North-East (Mr. Congdon) told the Committee that, according to statistics from Smith square, pupil numbers were declining. If that is so, why are class sizes increasing? There is now an opportunity to make classes smaller and give pupils far more individual attention.

The Government offer ever more information about the size of classes—the key to improving education—but class sizes continue to rise. The Prime Minister told the National Union of Teachers in a letter last year:

"To lay down class sizes would make for inefficient use of teachers. Schools that manage their own budgets have every incentive to make the best use of available resources."

Yet local management of schools requires that schools be funded almost entirely according to the number of pupils on schools' rolls. For each child admitted, a fixed sum is added to the school budget. Schools are therefore faced with the difficult choice of allowing class sizes to rise or turning away money to fund schools. This development justifies further acceptance of class size limits, which have been agreed between all teachers organisations.

More than 25 per cent. of primary school-age children are in classes of more than 30. Even in secondary schools, where the number of pupils is falling, 10 per cent. of pupils are in classes of more than 30. Limits are necessary to give all children a fair chance under the national curriculum and the testing procedures. Lower-size classes relieve stress and mounting pressure on teachers. This is an important factor because stress and pressure have a detrimental effect on the ability to teach and on standards in the classroom.

In 1986, there was an estimated £3 billion backlog of repairs and replacements necessary to put schools in a decent condition. Current estimates are as high as £6 billion. Although grant-maintained schools have done well, if more schools opt out of LEAs and move to central control, funds will dry up. I do not think that the 300 per cent. increase in capital expenditure on grant-maintained schools—as opposed to the 10 per cent. increase for LEA schools—can continue. The difference illustrates the Government's unfair allocating of capital resources. The system will not continue because when more schools opt out—which I hope does not happen but which I suspect will because of the bribery aspect—the vast difference in allocations will quickly cease as less money becomes available.

The National Association of Inspectors and Educational Advisers recently said:

"Hundreds of schools should be closed because of the serious defects, if they were subject to the same standards used to declare homes unfit for habitation."

Many of the schools in which our children are educated are unfit for human habitation.

JPEG 677 That is not merely a one-off occurrence in my constituency—there are schools like that throughout the country. All hon. Members know of similar examples in their constituencies.

We should not expect our children to work in dangerous buildings that are not waterproof and that are inadequate to meet the needs of the national curriculum. Standards for maintenance, repair and replacement of school buildings must be enforced.

I shall quote from a report by the National Audit Office entitled, "Repair and Maintenance of School Buildings," published on 25 July 1991. It is a long passage, but it is so important that I shall put it on the record. It states:

"A survey based upon local education authorities' own assessments and published by the Department of Education and Science in 1986 reported that 38 per cent. of primary schools sampled and 24 per cent. of secondary schools sampled needed work on roofs, or heating systems, or walls and windows. The cost of bringing school buildings up to the accommodation standards denned in the School Premises Regulations for 1991 pupil numbers and to an acceptable state of repair was estimated to be £2 billion at November 1987 prices. The cost of structural repairs and maintenance alone was estimated to be £730 million to £995 million at November 1987 prices.


Her Majesty's Inspectorate of Schools consider that good education can occur in some of the poorest accommodation. But they have commented in their reports over the last decade on the adverse effects on the quality of education caused by buildings which are unsuitable for current educational demands, and badly maintained accommodation. They have suggested that these circumstances may adversely affect morale and do nothing to stimulate and encourage pupils to give of their best and raise their levels of achievement, or to attract to the teaching profession able and enthusiastic teachers."

That report is an indictment of our education system. It is deplorable that enough money cannot be put into the education service to ensure that our children are educated in reasonable accommodation.

Conditions facing children in schools every day include outside toilets, no running water, rooms for practical activities without power points, water running down the walls into electric wires. Any allocation of funds to grant-maintained schools must take account of the necessity for decent standards of school buildings to end the decay and inadequacy that Her Majesty's Inspectorate identified in 1987 and in every year since then.

Regulations governing school buildings were laid down 11 years ago by this Government for the protection of students and teachers, but they deferred until 1996 the enforcement of those regulations for schools built before 1981. In other words, it does not matter that children continue to be educated in the most foul of conditions; the Government are not prepared to give schools the resources that they need.

The amendment would ensure that, at least, the Government are forced to fund grant-maintained schools to minimum standards. Guaranteed standards for all schools must be laid down and resourced, so that every child has a fair chance and equal opportunities. Minimum standards of provision must apply to all schools. However, the funding agency should determine funding levels and grants to schools to ensure that grant-maintained schools attain certain levels of provision.
