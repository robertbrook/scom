PROPOSALS FOR ALTERATIONS TO VOLUNTARY SCHOOLS BY LOCAL EDUCATION AUTHORITY

Mr. Forth
: I regret that there are a large number of Government amendments and I apologise to the Committee for that. However, their essence can be stated fairly simply and straightforwardly.

The amendments reflect the further consideration that we have given to the proposals in the White Paper to give local education authorities the power to bring forward proposals to make a significant change of character or significant enlargement of voluntary schools. On Second Reading, we said that we were always prepared to listen carefully to representations. I am sure that all members of the Committee welcome that as they have constantly urged it on my hon. Friends and me since we started.

We recognise that concerns have been expressed to us about that radical departure from what has been the previously established policy, so we decided to table the amendments. Their effect will be to remove the provision from clause 205 that local education authorities should be able to propose the significant change of character or significant enlargement of the premises of a voluntary school.

As amended, the provisions give the Secretary of State new powers, were school places in the area are excessive, to direct the governing body of a voluntary school to make proposals for the significant change of character or significant enlargement of the school under section 13 of the Education Act 1980.

The Secretary of State will also have the power to direct the governing body of a voluntary school to bring forward proposals for the alteration of the school at stage 3 where the provision of places in an area is not sufficient. Our commitment to the removal of surplus places remains unchanged, but there are varying views about the best way 1272 to achieve this. The clause as amended will ensure that the voluntary sector does not stand in the way of sensible reorganisation proposals.

I stress that the local education authority will be free to bring any desirable changes to the Secretary of State's attention, and he will be able to consider the overall situation in the area before deciding whether or not it is necessary to direct a voluntary school to bring forward proposals. If the Secretary of State decides that the governing body of a voluntary school should be directed to bring forward proposals under section 13 of the 1980 Act for a change of character—but not religious character—or enlargement, the proposals will require the approval of the Secretary of State, who may reject them, approve them without modification or, after consultation with the persons by whom they were made, and the LEA, approve them with modifications.

As with a direction under clause 207 to the LEA or the funding agency for schools, if the time allowed under the order expires and no proposals have been made by the governing body of the school, the Secretary of State may make his own proposals for the change of character or enlargement of the school. If any objections to the Secretary of State's proposals are made by those who are listed as statutory objectors, including the local education authority concerned, the governing body of the school and those responsible for the appointment of the foundation governors, the proposals will be subject to the public inquiry procedure outlined in the Bill.

The amendments balance the competing claims of those who would seek to give LEAs complete control over voluntary schools and those who would give voluntary schools complete autonomy regardless of the overall situation in the area. I commend them to the Committee.

Mr. Byers
: On numerous occasions the Committee has discussed the additional and centralising powers that will be given to the Secretary of State as a result of the Bill's provisions. For many of us, the power that will be given to him by clause 207 is probably the best illustration of the enormous extension of the Secretary of State's powers that will result from the Bill.

The clause is stark in its provisions. It says plainly that the Secretary of State will have the power by order to direct the local education authority or the funding agency to issue proposals to remove excess capacity in the school sector in a particular local authority area. The clause states that, in making such an order, the Secretary of State can lay down the principles that should be applied when the local authority or the funding agency presents proposals in response to that order. It would be helpful for the Committee to hear from the Minister examples of the considerations that will be covered by those principles.

We are worried that the clause gives the Secretary of State power by way of order. The order does not have to come back to Parliament, but can simply direct the local authority or the funding agency to make reorganisation proposals.

Clause 207 is carefully worded—the Secretary of State has ensured that the holder of his office will not be involved in any local controversy. The order will direct the local authority and will not name individual schools. That will be the difficult task of the funding agency or the local education authority. Those of us who, in a previous political life, were responsible for piloting through reorganisation proposals involving the closure or merger of schools know all too well the political difficulties and the local hostility to which such proposals give rise. I understand why the Secretary of State does not want the holder of his office to be involved in such arguments.

It is unfair that the Secretary of State can, by order, require reorganisation proposals to be made, but will not specify the schools that are to be the subject of such proposals. He will take a broad-brush approach, while the funding agency or the LEA will do the detailed work and will put proposals before the Secretary of State. That procedure requires clarification. First, how will the Secretary of State identify a surplus or excess capacity in a particular local authority area? The figure of 1·5 million surplus places in England has been bandied around in Committee. The Minister has said that there are up to 1·5 million. That is a large number: I recollect that it first came from the Audit Commission. We should be clear in our own minds about how that figure is to be calculated.

In the autumn, the Department for Education estimated the surplus capacity in individual LEA areas. It was a difficult exercise, especially for local authorities. In the consultation document, the Department's estimate of surplus capacity seemed to be based on two separate methods of calculation. One method used the more open enrolment capacity formula, and the second calculation was based on the school's actual standard number, which was multiplied by the number of age groups to produce an estimated capacity for the school as a whole. Those two formulae give different figures for local authorities. The calculation of the number of surplus places must be clarified.

JPEG 1277 One local authority reported that under one formula it would have to implement a reduction of 8,730 places, involving the closure of seven or eight schools; but under the other formula it would have to build three extra schools because there would be a deficit of 3,310 places. That is why we need a clear illustration of how excess capacity will be calculated.

Opposition Members see some merit in flexibility and in having surplus capacity in the system. Clearly a surplus of 1·5 million is untenable and indefensible; we do not need that degree of over-capacity. But if all the 1·5 million surplus places were removed, thousands of schools in England and Wales would be closed. The Minister should tell the Committee whether the Secretary of State wants thousands of schools in England and Wales to close. If he moves to require local authorities or the funding agency to remove up to 1·5 million surplus places, that will be the consequence. The Minister should make his intentions clear today.

Clause 207 is unacceptable because in practice it will make local authorities and, indeed, the funding agency the mere tools of the Secretary of State. He will be able to require them by order to remove excess capacity. That is not an appropriate way of achieving an objective that we share—surplus places are not good for the system; they are inefficient, a waste of valuable resources and they need to be taken out. Diktat by the Secretary of State as provided for by the clause is inappropriate even in this Bill.

They will make it difficult for teachers who have been successfully teaching English for many years to go on teaching English in that proven 1304 way and still prepare pupils adequately for the tests. They rely on the traditional method of testing, which demands rote learning and knowledge of the right answer and gives no marks unless the answer required by the exam board is written down on the paper.

    There is a variety of methods of assessment and of teaching that should be used in our schools—for example, drama, discussion, reading Shakespeare texts, seeing how those texts have been interpreted on film by actors and actresses, co-operative learning and folder work. In past years all those have led to improved standards of English in our schools. It is a tragedy that, by being so prescriptive about the format of testing at key stage 3 English, the Department of Education will almost outlaw that type of teaching. Teachers will not be preparing students sufficiently for the exams at key stage 3 in English.
