Mrs. Taylor: The amendments are important because we are seeking to clarify the role of the funding agency for schools by exploring with the Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Schools why he has chosen the title of "Agency" rather than "council". We wish to ensure also that there will be further parliamentary scrutiny of the proposals, as we do not believe that the Minister can be trusted to get everything right about this matter.

On Tuesday, much was said about the brevity of the consultation period, which has led to many difficulties for everyone, including Ministers. The more parliamentary scrutiny of the proposals that there can be, the better will be the chance that the provisions that are introduced will be practical and workable. They must not create more difficulties for everyone involved, including those schools in the grant-maintained sector.

The need for further consideration and parliamentary scrutiny of the matter is made even greater by the fact that clause 1 is being debated in the absence of the consultation document from the Department for Education on the common funding formula. That document is expected to be ready before Christmas, when we shall be able to see the Minister's proposals. However, we must examine the clause today without that information. That makes the provision of proper safeguards and opportunities for future parliamentary scrutiny essential, because we do not know the details of the way in which the new system will work.

I hope that the Minister will tell the Committee a great deal more about the way in which the funding agency will Col. 36 work. The information on that so far has been very limited. The notes on clauses make it clear that that agency will not be a quango but that it will be free-standing and that it will have its own powers. However, there is nothing in the Bill that sets out clearly the funding agency's function. Therefore, our proposal that the body should be a funding council is a means by which we can explore why the Minister has chosen instead to establish an agency and what its role and powers will be. One of the new funding agency's tasks will be to distribute funds to grant-maintained schools. That is a cheque-book function. Ministers will lay down a formula to prescribe how funds are to be distributed to grant-maintained schools. Will the funding agency have much independent power and discretion, or will it simply form another bureaucratic layer to carry out the Secretary of State's intentions? Clause 1 does not specify the funding agency's powers. The authority will be a hived-off division of the Department for Education. The use of the word, "agency" should ring bells, because it often leads to privatisation. The Secretary of State will make decisions that that new funding authority will simply administer. That begs the question of who would wish to serve on such a body.

If power is to be centralised and the funding agency is to have a bureaucratic and administrative role, serious problems will arise. In the past 24 hours, we have seen that the Secretary of State cannot even present statistical information that is of any value. If the Secretary of State cannot publish accurate information on examination results, how can he run every school in the country successfully?

Centralisation in education will not work. Nothing in the clause persuades us that its provisions will do anything other than create a new bureaucratic organisation by which the Secretary of State can use his power. That raises important questions about the autonomy of the funding agency, and about its role. The Minister should clarify matters as quickly as possible. I hope that he will accept our reasonable request for another opportunity for parliamentary scrutiny of the proposals in the absence of full information.

Mr. Stephen Byers (Wallsend): I support the amendments, which fall into two categories. Amendments Nos. 5 and 7 would mean that the creation of the funding agency would be subject to an order under the affirmative procedure. Amendments Nos. 1 and 9 would change "agency" to "council".

Amendments Nos. 5 and 7 deal with accountability. We shall discuss that issue in more detail later when we consider other clauses and amendments.

The Bill challenges the basis of local democratic control over an important public service. We must acknowledge that, whether in London, the metropolitan districts or the county councils, the provision of education is a major function of local authorities. It is an important part of our democratic process that such an essential public service should be under democratic, elected control. Many of us think that that is best achieved through local accountability.

Under the Bill, that accountability and democratic control would not even be taken over by national Government, but would be pushed down to individual school governing bodies, by the strange concept of subsidiarity. That is superficially attractive, but the fact that a transitory group of parents should have control over Image Col. 37 important public assets—literally billions of pounds of public money—is dangerous. It is far better that such money should be subject to elected, democratic control. A school belongs to the whole community, not just a small group of parents. To give power to one group of parents may be superficially attractive. It is similar to the argument that states that league tables are attractive; they may be attractive to a supporter of Newcastle United—

The Chairman: The hon. Gentleman is going wide of the subject of the amendment, which deals with the funding agency.

Mr. Byers: I am sorry, but the fact that Newcastle United is 12 points ahead in the first division is too tempting to ignore because it is so unusual.

Education is an important public service and clause 1 would transfer the responsibility for education from local education authorities to a funding agency. Before that part of the Bill comes into force it must be subject to an affirmative resolution to allow further consideration of the important issues.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Dewsbury (Mrs. Taylor) said, we still do not know the full picture. It is like a jigsaw, certain pieces are in place, but we do not know about the most fundamental issue behind the operation of the funding agency and how it will allocate its slice of the cake to individual schools that may have achieved grant-maintained status.

We are told that a consultation paper will come out sometime before Christmas, with consultation taking place into the new year. What concerns us is that that consultation may still be going on when the Bill reaches the statute book. Therefore, we think that an affirmative order would be appropriate.

There are further dangers in giving the responsibility for such an important service as education to a funding agency. Education has developed in many positive ways over the years because, as a local service, it has responded to local needs and aspirations. The needs and aspirations of people in Wallsend will differ from those of Worcester, which we should acknowledge.

The positive initiatives that have been taken in education over the years represent a response to local demands. For example, comprehensive education was not a decision made by central Government, but was decided by Conservative-controlled local authorities, Labour-controlled local authorities recognised the wisdom of that decision. It was wrong to condemn the majority of children aged 11 as failures and the authorities responded to the needs of local people.

Provision for special needs has developed because of the way in which local education authorities were able to respond. Many organisations and many members of the Committee are worried that, in terms of special needs provision, a funding agency would not be in such a strong position to respond to local needs and aspirations. A local body has flexibility, but a central funding agency would impose a straitjacket of central control and the positive developments of many years might not continue.

It is worth contrasting the Government's proposals with what is happening in other European education systems which, traditionally, have had a central funding agency exercising central control. France, Germany and Col. 38 Italy have centralised education systems but they are moving away from them because they know of the deficiencies and the rigidity that can occur. They are devolving power to locally elected democratic bodies. We are moving in the opposite direction and that is another example of the way in which the Government fail to learn from their mistakes and fail to appreciate the benefits and advantages of a decentralised education system, as recognised by other European countries. We are worried about the straitjacket of control that would result from a funding agency.

The confusion over the financial arrangements under which the agency will operate also worries us. An affirmative order would allow us to consider the consultation documents that the Government are about to publish. "There can never be a level playing-field between grant-maintained schools and local education authority maintained schools, because grant-maintained status gives such clear benefits and advantages."—[Official Report, 10 November 1992; Vol 213, c. 836]

Amendments Nos. 1 and 9 propose changing the title of "agency" to "council". I spent an interesting hour in the Library last evening looking at Stevens and Borrie's "Mercantile Law", 17th edition, which took me back to my student days.

It is useful because it defines an "agent" as:


"he who is employed to do anything in the place of another".

The provisions for Wales allow the Principality to have a council, which is an interesting distinction.
