Mr. Jeff Rooker

: I, too welcome you, Dame Janet, to the Chair of the Committee. I hope that you enjoy our deliberations. I know that you take a keen interest in this subject.

The sittings motion is fairly normal, as one would expect, but it is not a normal Bill. If this measure were needed tomorrow, the Bill would go through on the nod. There is no opposition to the Bill from the Labour party, or, as far as I am aware, from the Liberal Democrats. We have made it our business—as shown by our comments on Second Reading and by the amendments that we have tabled—to use the Bill to explore the progress made in community care plans and the changes that are due to take place in April 1993. This is probably the only opportunity that Members will have to debate the issue. It took more than a year to have a debate in the House on community care. This is not a contentious issue so I hope that we can take a fairly relaxed view. We want to make progress, however; we are not going to mess about.

I want to make a point, not so much to the Minister but to his colleagues sitting behind him. Just for once, I would like to serve on a Standing Committee and participate in a proper debate. I would genuinely like to know Conservative Members' views on community care policies in their areas, and not just have a ding-dong between Labour Members and the Minister about what is happening in our own backyards or areas with which we are familiar.

The Bill relates to a good cross-section of areas in England, and there is a good smattering of new hon. Members on the Committee. Therefore, I hope that Conservative Members, who I suspect have been instructed to keep their mouths shut and their backsides on their seats, will not take the advice that they have been given. We welcome the opportunity for some dialogue on what progress is or is not being made with community care plans, which affect millions of our fellow citizens. That is why the amendments have been framed in the way that they have.

It is clear that the legislation was not scrutinised in the other place. In fact, the Bill was considered in the other place twice—once before the election and once since. That can hardly be described as scrunity of legislation or as their Lordships often loftily put it, more non-partisan, more detailed consideration of legislation than takes place in the House of Commons. They have not done their job, so it is left to the House of Commons to do what is required.

I hope that Conservative Members will take that in the spirit in which it is intended. We have plenty of time. The provisions of the Bill are required to be in force before 1 April 1993—not that we shall sit until 1 April 1993. However, bearing in mind recent reports that the House may adjourn for the summer recess on 10 July, rather than on 17 July, and if we are about to embark on a 16-week recess it would be good if new Conservative Members were able to say that, for a few weeks they had done a job of work in Committee and had actually pulled their weight in the House of Commons. I hope that we will make reasonable progress.

I am not working to any particular timetable, although I suspect that the Minister has been told to finalise the Bill in one morning—clearly, that cannot be done—but I should have thought that a couple of weeks, give or take a sitting or two, would allow plenty of time for our deliberations. That is not an offer or a commitment, but it shows the spirit in which Opposition Members enter into the discussions.
