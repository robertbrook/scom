Amendment No. 58 deals specifically with race relations and equal opportunities. It would require the Secretary of State to appoint to the funding agency people who have a background in those sectors. We should take note of a court case between the Equal Opportunities Commission and the city of Birmingham. The commission believed that the city of Birmingham was acting against the letter and spirit of the Sex Discrimination Act 1976 by maintaining single sex schools. The court ruled in favour of the Equal Opportunities Commission. I shall read the concluding comments from the case, which are relevant to our deliberations. The court ruled as follows:

"Section 8 does not impose a duty to provide secondary schools. The duty is to secure that sufficient schools shall be available in the area for providing secondary education. It followed therefore in discharging that 'securing function' the council has to have regard to the availability of educational opportunities provided by all the schools within its area, including relevant places in grant-maintained schools and even relevant places in independent schools.


The council could not ignore the existence and discriminatory effects of some 120 grammar school places being available at Handsworth grammar school for boys merely because the school has changed its status and become a grant-maintained school. Handsworth grammar school remained a part of the pool for public sector grammar school education within the council area."

The point is that, whichever the funding authority—the local education authority in that example—it will have to take account of that judgment in its responsibilities for the opening and closing of schools and the provision of school places in the future.
