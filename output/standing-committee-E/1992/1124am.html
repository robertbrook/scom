Clause 2
SCHOOLS FUNDING COUNCIL FOR WALES

Mr. Paul Murphy
(Torfaen)
: I beg to move amendment No. 30, in page 1, line 27, at beginning insert

'Subject to subsection (1A) below'.

The Chairman
: With this, we may take amendment No. 31, in page 1, line 30, at end insert—

'(1A) This section has effect when not less than 20 per cent. of the pupils for whom education is provided in county, voluntary and grant-maintained schools in Wales are registered pupils at grant-maintained schools.'.

Mr. Murphy
: I had not anticipated that we would reach clause 2 so quickly this morning. It gives us the opportunity to study some of the most important aspects of the Bill that affect the Principality. I am delighted to see that the Minister of State has returned from Milan, where he was at the weekend, and I hope that he has brought back some jobs for Wales.

Under amendments Nos. 30 and 31, the Welsh funding council would be set up only when 20 per cent. of Welsh pupils are in grant-maintained schools. There are three reasons for that. First, it is important to avoid the unnecessary cost and bureaucracy of setting up a funding council for Wales when few schools have become grant-maintained. The Committee will be aware that the history of the Welsh aspect goes back several months, when the Government's original intention was for grant-maintained schools to be funded and administered not by a schools funding council, but by the existing Further Education Funding Council for Wales—at this stage, it might be appropriate for me to declare my own interest as a consultant to the National Association of Teachers in Further and Higher Education.

JPEG 137 People in the education circles in Wales believed that the Further Education Funding Council for Wales would deal with the grant-maintained schools—until the Bill was published. For some reason, the Government changed their mind. Initially, they said that the small handful of schools that had become grant-maintained would be run directly from the Welsh Office, which would not be too difficult as there are only three such schools. Then, the Government said that when sufficient schools—there is no sign of the precise number—in Wales became grant maintained, the schools funding council would be set up in Cardiff to run what the Government believe will be an increasing number of grant-maintained schools in the Principality.

During the debate on the sittings motion, I said that we should examine carefully the setting up of the funding council because of local government re-organisation in Wales. The Secretary of State for Wales has adopted a different stance from that of his English counterpart. We are told that he proposes introducing a White Paper between now and January 1993 on the structure and future of local government in the Principality.

Last week, leaked documents from the Welsh Office—a matter on which the Minister is not prepared to comment but which is of interest to the Committee—came to the notice of my hon. Friend the Member for Cardiff, West (Mr. Morgan). The documents, which seemed genuine to me, revealed that the Government are considering the establishment of 24 rather than 23 unitary authorities for Wales. So far as we are aware, they will be local education authorities; at present there are only eight authorities in the Principality.

We do not yet know what education function those councils will have. We are told that some, the larger ones—say, Cardiff, Newport and Swansea—will be called lead authorities. They will take the lead—presumably in different advisory services—in the support services that the LEAs now administer. The other, smaller councils, apparently will not have those functions. While all this is in the melting pot, so to speak, it seems insane for the schools funding council for Wales to be established without our knowing precisely what the pattern of local government in Wales is likely to be.

If the Government were to accept the amendment, it would be convenient to wait until 20 per cent. of Welsh pupils were in grant-maintained schools before setting up the council. This would enable them to examine closely the implications and impact of local government reorganisation in the Principality.

A second reason why it is imperative for the Government to rethink is the distinct lack of interest in grant-maintained schools in Wales. We have heard much from the Secretary of State for Education and his junior Ministers about the great upsurge in grant-maintained schools in England. The Minister of State for Wales spoke on Second Reading about the great upsurge in Wales—where there are about 2,117 schools of which three have opted out so far. Two of those decided to opt out because the local authority threatened to close them, acting in response to a specific directive from the Welsh Office to reorganise secondary schools in the county of Gwent to 138 save surplus places and help save some of the £23 million costs, and the 118,000 places. That was a laudable objective.

No other reason would have justified it.

The Welsh Office has talked for some years about optimum numbers for schools. If has referred to 800 or 900 pupils in schools for 11 to 16-year-olds being the right size for a comprehensive. The numbers at Cwmcarn school were reduced and it was threatened with closure as a result of the local government reorganisation proposals. The Government should have taken note of that, but they did nothing of the sort.

JPEG 141 The third reason for the amendment's importance is that it would set up another organisation dealing with education planning. In Wales, education is run by the Welsh Office education department and the local education authorities. Under those organisations, governing bodies have been given greater powers—I think that local management of schools is a good thing—but the Bill would mean that education in Wales would be run by local education authorities, the FEFC for Wales, the schools funding council for Wales, the Welsh Office education department, the grant-maintained schools themselves and the education associations that take over so-called failing schools. Not many Conservative Members have spoken about the pupils. The are best served by the local education authorities, which, with the help of the Welsh joint education committee, for many years have provided a first-class education service. Setting up the funding council too early will mean that all the arguments about quangos in Wales will get worse.

The Secretary of State for Wales had appointed nearly 1,000 people in a country that is proud of its democratic background and heritage. Sir Wyn Roberts
: I do not want to be distracted from the main theme of local government reorganisation and education.

Over the next three years there will be a significant transformation in the management and administration of education in Wales because, by 1995, all schools in Wales will have delegated budgets and will assume greater control over their priorities, with LEAs retaining less power at the centre for the provision of services which they think that the schools need.

By April 1995, I fully expect a significant number of grant-maintained schools in Wales to exist because that will follow from the further delegation of powers under LMS.

11.15 am

In that event, the LEAs would have a more diminished role than at present and it would be for them to determine how to fulfil their responsibilities. The same principle would apply to the new unitary authorities, which could pursue several options to meet their responsibilities, including mutual co-operation. They would still have the responsibility for education taken by the county LEAs.

The amendment would ensure that the schools funding council for Wales would not be established until at least 20 per cent. of pupils in Wales were in the grant-maintained sector, which is equivalent to about 95,000 pupils. Is the hon. Member for Torfaen suggesting that, until that 20 per cent. were achieved, existing grant-maintained schools would continue to be directly funded from the Welsh Office? If he is, I note his point, but the Opposition have challenged our proposals to establish a council because it would lead to confusion about and duplication of the responsibilities of the LEAs. The amendment is a recipe for chaos and exemplifies the Labour party's muddled thinking.

We have not had an adequate explanation of how the Opposition have arrived at the magical figure of 20 per cent. nor any indication that they appreciate the full implications of their proposal. The amendment ignores the possibility that the distribution of grant-maintained schools may not be even across Wales. It does not take account of the possible distribution of grant-maintained schools between the primary and secondary sectors.

Let me give an example of what the 20 per cent. threshold could mean if one linked the primary and secondary sectors. If all pupils in Gwent and Powys, or Gwynedd and Dyfed, attended GM schools, the 20 per cent. threshold would still not be reached. It would be a nonsense to pretend that the LEAs concerned should retain complete responsibility for the provision of JPEG 145 sufficient school places. I admit that that is an extreme example, but we cannot rule out the uneven distribution of GM schools across Wales and across the primary and secondary sectors. That is why clause 2 does not specify a particular point at which the schools funding council for Wales would be established. We want to examine what pattern emerges in terms of numbers and distribution and then we will decide, on the basis of a sensible assessment, on the need for a council.

As I said, the schools funding council will be established in response to the wish of parents to have a significant grant-maintained sector in Wales. It will be established only in those circumstances. The target of a precise number of grant-maintained schools has not been set at which the schools funding council will be set up. The establishment of such bodies takes time. We cannot make the council appear at the touch of a button, or say that it will come into being when 17 or 20 schools have become grant-maintained. We shall monitor how many applications are made for grant-maintained status over the next few months and study the pattern that emerges as to location and distribution of those schools among the primary and secondary sectors.

The decision of the outstanding pioneer, Griffiths Jones, to make Welsh the teaching medium in his circulating schools was very influential. JPEG 153 The break with that tradition in the voluntary provision of education occurred with the establishment of the state system by the Education Act 1870. That had a devastating effect on the Welsh language at a time of rapid social and demographic change, setting in motion what sociologists of language would call language shift. That process has changed the Welsh language from being the language of the vast majority of the Welsh people in the middle of the last century to being a language of the minority, although not an insignificant majority in any sense of the word. The fact that the Welsh language gained first a toehold—at elementary and primary level—and then a foothold—at long last, at secondary level—in schools made possible the survival of Welsh as a vigorous language.

The establishment of Welsh as a subject and then as a teaching medium—as a factor to be taken seriously in Welsh education—was a long and gradual process. Mr. Dafis
: This group of amendments relates to the composition of the schools funding council for Wales. They are designed to make that council more representative of the various people and groups that the schools are intended to serve. I hope that the term "service" is not redundant in relation to schools.

Amendments Nos. 39, 42 and 52 would make the schools council more democratically accountable by including among its members representatives of local government and county councils. Amendment No. 39 does not, however, suggest my approval of the establishment of the funding council, and I shall vote against the inclusion of clause 2 in the Bill.

I believe that local authorities are by far the best organisations to have responsibility for education in schools. They are democratically accountable and close to the communities that they serve. They have intimate knowledge of the social, geographical, cultural and linguistic considerations of communities as well as the variation between them. To tackle the problem of local variation, the funding council will have to spawn regional, and even local, bureaucracies. A bureaucratic structure will be established that is not vastly dissimilar to the present one, but without the component of democratic accountability.

I have already said that I accept that LEAs are not perfect. It is worth reminding ourselves that imperfection is part of the human condition: this is as likely to be expressed through the democratic process as through any other. Occasionally, we find intransigence and slow progress in democratic local authorities.

I have already mentioned the failure of West Glamorgan, since 1972, to establish a single Welsh medium primary school. However, such deficiencies should be tackled through the enhancement and further refinement of the democratic process, not through its JPEG 161 elimination. There is no reason why people's rights to Welsh medium education should not be established by statute, that is one way to ensure that local authorities may not frustrate people's aspirations.

Clause 2 recognises that different circumstances apply in Wales because we are a nation. I prefer the appellation, "nation" to that sentimental and rather meaningless appellation, "Principality". I have little warmth towards the latter description. In Wales, we have a radically different cultural and social tradition. I suppose that that is reflected in the fact that we shall have a "council" in Wales, rather than an "agency". We are not particularly enthusiastic about that point either, because, in reality, the council and the agency will have exactly the same power. It is interesting that the clause relating to Wales states:

"The Secretary of State may by order make provision for the establishment of a body corporate".

I am not sure whether that shows that the Government and the Welsh Office are uncertain about the extent to which grant-maintained status in Wales will become the norm. Does it show less enthusiasm from the Welsh Office than from the Department for Education concerning grant-maintained status?

Let us assume the worst—we have to assume the worst in relation to the clause. I fear the combination of the Bill and the reorganisation of local government.
