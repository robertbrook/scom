Mr. Griffiths
: The amendment would help to clarify the position of a school that became grant maintained by including the word "exclusive" in clause 30(2)(a). What 12cconstitutes school premises is not clear. The Department of Education wrote to Gloucestershire county council in December 1990, and said:

"There is no clear definition of what constitutes 'school premises'".

The amendment would help to make clear that a grant-maintained school should not necessarily take over all the land and buildings on a school campus. Many schools are not used exclusively for education, but include youth facilities, and adult and community centres, which operate on the school campus under the aegis of the local education authority. Those centres provide a service to the community beyond that for which Education Acts provide, and beyond the statutory provision of education for children between the ages of five and 19. We hope that the Government will respond positively to the amendments.

Government amendments Nos. 296 and 297 suggest that the Government acknowledge that there may be disputes about what should be part of a grant-maintained school. I shall examine what constitutes school premises, what should be allocated to a school that becomes grant maintained and what should remain under LEA control. We have tried to make the clause more precise by including the word "exclusive" in subsection (2)(a).

I dare say that every member of the Committee has one or more schools, especially secondary ones, in his or her constituency, which offer youth and community facilities. Cynffig comprehensive school in my constituency has a youth wing and some parts of the main school building are used for the same purpose.

Barry boys' comprehensive school, where I used to work, had a youth wing on the school campus. I was a 776 member of the Vale of Glamorgan borough council in the early 1970s. The council entered into a joint agreement with South Glamorgan county council to develop community facilities, to upgrade the swimming pool and to build a new community centre on the site of Llantwit Major comprehensive school. Those joint-use facilities were developed because of the efficient use of resources by two tiers of local government which ensured that the community was even better served by the councils than before.

In other cases, there have been disputes between grant-maintained schools and the LEA about what is school land and what is owned by the local authority. In Leeds, 50 school playing fields are also used for community activities but clarification is needed about whether the playing fields should stay under local authority ownership and be used by the school during the day and by the public at weekends and in the evenings.

Some disputes have been resolved satisfactorily: the site of the City of Leeds school and the Notre Dame school, which became a sixth-form college, independent of the local authority, had a sports hall built 50 years ago, which was the subject of discussion about its use. The freehold was kept by the local authority and a user agreement was reached with the Notre Dame college, which was happy with that arrangement. However, if it had not been satisfied it might have challenged the local authority and the sports hall might have become that college's property.

A campus in Milton Keynes was home to two schools, a sports centre and other leisure facilities, which were used by the public on evenings and at weekends. In that case, the grant-maintained school was given the freehold and the local authority granted a 99-year lease on the site. I believe that that arrangement should have been the other way round, but it shows that there are disputes about such matters. That is why it is essential that the Bill makes it clear that the property or land that is transferred to a grant-maintained school is used exclusively by that school and has no other general youth or community purpose.
