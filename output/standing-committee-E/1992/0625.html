Community Care (Residential Accommodation) Bill [Lords]

clause Clause 1
title ARRANGEMENTS FOR PROVISION OF RESIDENTIAL ACCOMMODATION IN PREMISES MANAGED BY VOLUNTARY ORGANISATIONS ETC

Amdt. moved [23 June]: No. 25, in page 2, line 34, at end insert—

'(IZC) Arrangements shall only be made by virtue of this section where the relevant authority is satisfied that the views of any person to be placed in accommodation have been taken into account.'.—[Mr. Rooker.]

10.30 am

Mr. Jeff Rooker (Birmingham, Perry Barr): I was on my feet at our last sitting, having formally moved amendment No. 25. I want to add a few words on that important subject. However, so that it is on record to ensure that the Minister and Whip have no fears and to show that my word is my bond, my hon. Friends and I will ensure that the Committee stage is concluded this morning.

The key issue about consultation is users' views. The amendment is narrowly drafted. Sometimes one cannot separate the user—the person who wants or needs care—from the carer who has been involved. That is one of the great issues that was tackled by the Select Committee. If the users' views are to be taken into account, they must be informed views, based on knowledge of the alternatives and options available for a particular person. Those people must therefore have the choice, if possible—and every case must be examined—of continuing to live independently in their own homes. That is the first option that should be available for people and it should be fully explored before they are moved to residential care of any description.

People may not appreciate the options and range of facilities that are available. There lies the dilemma between giving the user choice and independence, a paradox that was explored in the Social Services Select Committee's sixth report, way back in May 1980. That was a special report on choice for service users as part of the community care programme. It made 17 recommendations, most of which will be accepted by local authorities. The Government have shown their support for many of those points, though not all, because one of them referred to sections 1 to 3 of the Disabled Persons (Services, Consultation and Representation) Act 1986.

However, choice must be informed. Information, therefore, must be available. I do not want to discuss information services, but there can be no choice unless information is available. It is incumbent on the local authority involved, as the lead agency—until the Government takes that role away—to provide information on what is available in its area. I do not want to plug my local authority, the biggest in England, too much but late last year it produced a substantial information file and Col. 108 guide to care in the community. It had chapters on money, housing, health, help in the neighbourhood and at home, getting out and about and leisure. It is a mine of information about contacts in the public, private and voluntary sectors. Many people, when they come to require care, will not have had the opportunity to plan for it. They are given no option and decisions are sometimes made quickly. That is a tragedy. I suspect that most members of the Committee have come across cases of people in residential accommodation who should not be there. When my hon. Friend the Member for York (Mr. Bayley) was busy with the closure of 17 homes in the Leeds area, I expect that he came across examples, as I have in my constituency when homes have been closed or amalgamated. When that happens, there is an assessment of every person living in a home. It is rare not to come across one case about whom, after the assessment, the social services department asks why that person was in the home in the first place. Sometimes it results from a lack of information available to the carers and the person concerned.

Every local authority should ensure that there is as much information as possible available on the situation in the area. That is a departure for many local authorities. They are not used to advertising, like the private sector, or to working closely with the voluntary sector. Birmingham has given a lead, with its wide dissemination of information that I hope others will follow.

When the choice is made between independence and residential care, it is not just the services that the user requires at home that should be considered. I have to be careful how I put this point because I could be misunderstood by some of my friends in the trade union movement. The first priority is the user. To gain maximum independence it is always preferable to provide aids and adaptations. For example, flexibility in the kitchen would enable people to make a choice to cook their own meals rather than receive a meals-on-wheels service. To be able to do things for themselves gives independence; to rely on services leads to a degree of dependence.

People have to be informed about the range of adaptations and aids available so that they can, without going into care, have their homes converted using modern technology, such as movable sinks and worktops to accommodate wheelchairs. People do not appreciate what is available because it is not the sort of thing that one shops around for. By the time the aids are needed, people might not be physically able to shop around. That is why it is crucial that the care managers or the social services go out of their way to provide maximum information so that informed choices can be made. I can do no better than to quote the Association of County Councils document, published this year, on care in the community which stated:

"the establishment of an effective user and carer-centred perspective also demands that actual and potential users and carers are informed of what services are available."

That is the climate at the moment. I hope that the Government will seek to perpetuate it, so that people are given a genuine choice.

By the time that an assessment is necessary, it is likely that the assessment will lead to residential care—for the majority of cases at a low level, such as the narrow type of housing dealt with in the Bill. Nevertheless, as a later amendment will make clear, choice has to include the Image Col. 109 possibility of deciding that the move has not worked out. I do not want to go into that aspect yet, because we shall deal with the issue of trial periods shortly.

The Government probably do not want to amend the Bill. I hope, however that they will make the right noises and accept that choice has to be based on information. I hope that they will do what they can to encourage local authorities to maximise the amount of information available.

I read some of the draft community care plans last year. I do not wish to single any of them out, but I am not happy with one or two of them because they do not intend to maximise the information available from all sectors. There will be a cultural change in the way in which social services departments operate from next April. Some of them are moving in that direction now. They will genuinely see themselves as the enablers, planners and organisers, and they must provide the user with information so that he can make an informed choice. No one else can take that role. Only local government can collate, publish and disseminate the information to GPs, carers and potential users.

Even insurance companies are moving into this field by encouraging people to plan for possible future care costs and arrangements. Previous and present generations never planned to be in care. No one planned for that, or made arrangements, so there was no demand for information. Now there is such a demand, and I hope that it will help match the carer to the services in a much better way than before. If people are able to make those choices, it is less likely that they will end up in the wrong kind of care in the wrong part of the country. That point cannot be overstated. We shall constantly return to this matter as the implementation of these measures—next April, we hope—comes closer, and from then onwards. It will be crucial because, as Members of Parliament, we shall be involved at the sharp end when things go wrong. We are like firefighters, usually called in when it is too late. Our job is to encourage the services to provide information so that people do not feel the need to call on firefighters.

It is difficult to accept the way in which the Government use the term "freedom of choice" in relation to community care. They have suggested that the advent of income support payments to elderly people to support them in private care homes has given many thousands of people great freedom of choice which had previously been denied to people with insufficient income to exercise purchase of care in that way. The argument is that, since 1981, the extension of income support payments has given people a range of choice previously denied them. In reality, the exercise of the freedom of choice to which the Government refer is a sham and a myth. I shall explain my reasons for saying that, because they are central to our argument for amendment No. 25.

After 25 years of direct or indirect involvement in social work, I have yet to meet one elderly person who has made a positive choice to enter institutional care when that person had a range of alternatives. I do not say that people do not positively choose to enter institutional care, but I have never met someone who has positively chosen to enter residential or nursing care when that person has had a genuine choice of alternative domiciliary services. It is nonsense to talk about freedom of choice for the elderly. That is why amendment No. 25 is necessary.

For most people considering entering care, the choice is between the same form of care from a range of establishments. The choice that the Government offer is narrow; it is a choice, by and large, or one form of care—institutional care in a care home or a nursing home. That is not freedom of choice. That implies a wider choice from among a range of services and alternative forms of care.

Mr. Alun Michael (Cardiff, South and Penarth): My hon. Friend said that he did not know of cases in which people have chosen residential care. Image Col. 113 The point that undermines the Government's argument about freedom of choice is that, if people are to choose to enter care, they must consider the availability of such care in their area. The amendment deals with flexibility and ensuring that opportunities can be taken in an appropriate way. It involves defining the fine line between sheltered accommodation and residential accommodation. The Government recognise the need to ensure that arbitrary definitions do not limit the capacity to provide appropriate care. That is why the Government have introduced the Bill and why we are discussing it in Committee. The purpose of the Bill is to ensure that an inappropriate definition does not prevent an appropriate placement. It bends the law sensibly to bring Abbeyfield and similar unregistered or small homes within the range of premises with which residential care arrangements can be made.

The amendment seeks to ensure that the consequence is not to bend another line so that such accommodation is excluded from the category of sheltered accommodation. It seems sensible to have an overlap rather than a gap between the two types of accommodation. The definition should describe how the accommodation is used and what leads to a client going into a particular place.

How would it work in practice? If the unit is defined as sheltered accommodation, a resident would still be able to receive care. The care element would be funded as part of a package of community care, if that was a direct alternative to residential or nursing home care. The same applies to various adult family placement schemes and other forms of minimal care in provided accommodation.

We welcome any measure that gives greater flexibilty—which is the the purpose of Bill—but we want to be careful not to increase financial responsibility and expectation out of the same pot of money. That would undoubtedly be the case, if there were an expectation that social services departments would meet the costs of the Abbeyfield society and other organisations in providing homes for people who would not otherwise be likely to go into residential or nursing home care. All that social services departments will receive from the Benefits Agency is an estimate of what would have been spent on supporting people in private and voluntary residential or nursing homes. It needs to be used Col. 130 for that purpose, or for a package of community care as a direct alternative. If the net were to be widened, and if the type of premises that could be used were defined as those premises capable of being treated only as residential accommodation and not as sheltered accommodation without additional resources being provided, that could lead to increased expectations which social services departments would be unable to meet.

The amendment would allow flexibility for the accommodation for which arrangements could be made under the Bill. It could be defined as sheltered accommodation or as residential accommodation, depending on the circumstances. It would be a sensible and British compromise which would avoid the anomaly that could arise if an inappropriate dividing line were enforced on the basis of unfeeling logic rather than on the basis allowing the public bodies involved to exercise humanity and flexibility in the way in which they provide care.
