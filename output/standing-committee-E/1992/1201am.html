SUPERVISION OF FUNDING AUTHORITIES

Mr. Don Foster
(Bath)
: I beg to move amendment No. 71, in page 3, line 7, at end insert—

'(2A) In respect of the exercise by the funding authority of functions in respect of any grant-maintained school, the Secretary of State shall make arrangements to secure that a copy of each direction is

  1. delivered to such representative organisations of grant-maintained schools as he thinks fit; and
  2. made available to the governing body of every grant-maintained school.'.

 I have already expressed my concern about the powers that the Bill will give the Secretary of State. Earlier, we discussed the power of the Secretary of State to appoint members of the funding authorities. Now, clause 6 gives him absolute power over the members of the funding authorities, and he will have the right to dismiss them should they take decisions of which he disapproves. One might say that, that is not just the belt and braces approach, but he is even using a piece of string.

In that context, it is vital that the directions issued by the Secretary of State are consulted on as widely as possible and that the process is as open as possible. Like many Opposition members, I would ideally like a requirement that ensured that, before directions affecting graint-maintained schools were given, the Secretary of State discussed his proposals with the governing body of each GM school. However, I recognise that with the centralised structure that the Government are trying to impose through the Bill, consultation with so many groups would be impractical. Such a problem does not exist under the present arrangements for local education authorities and their schools. For example, in the major revision of the local managements of schools arrangement, LEAs are required to consult all of their schools. That is covered in section 34(4) of the Education Reform Act 1988.

A major problem of the Bill is that the structure proposed by the Secretary of State could ultimately lead him to have direct, personal control over all 24,000 schools; consultation would be administratively impossible. We have heard that the Secretary of State likes to think of himself as the nation's headmaster but, unfortunately, his door does not seem to be open to many people and it is unlikely to be open for consultation with the governors of all grant-maintained schools. It is inauspicious that he has recently moved to a sanctuary.

Amendment No. 70—which we discussed last sitting —and amendment No. 71 are designed to introduce at least some of the features of openness and consultation that are already required by LEAs. There is genuine 332 openness in the work of the LEAs: they consult, they are accountable and they have public meetings. They must openly reach the decisions that affect children in LEA schools. The Bill would provide no guarantees that the Secretary of State would operate in the same open way for grant-maintained schools.

At our last sitting, the Minister accepted the arguments for amendment No. 70, which dealt with the need to consult the funding authorities before issuing directions. It would have gone some way towards open decision making about grant-maintained schools.

Amendment No. 71 also deals with openness. It distinguishes between the two sorts of direction under clause 6. The amendment does not cover directions for purely internal management issues that affect the work of the funding authority. However, the Secretary of State will also issue directions that affect the interests of individual and collective grant-maintained schools. The amendment would ensure that such directions to the funding authority were delivered to organisations that represented grant-maintained schools and made available to the governing bodies of every grant-maintained school. If individual grant-maintained schools cannot be consulted about directions, they should receive details of directions that affect them as soon as possible. That will enable them to look ahead and plan effectively. Early notification about details of directions means that grant-maintained schools will receive them before the funding authorities have interpreted them. The schools may thus be able to influence the funding authorities' interpretation of the directions. The amendment is uncontroversial. It would increase open decision making. Just as the Minister accepted the argument for amendment No 70.1 hope that he will accept the argument for amendment No. 71.
