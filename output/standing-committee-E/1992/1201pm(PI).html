    Mr. Stephen Byers
    (Wallsend)
    : When we adjourned, I had made my preamble to the amendment, which would tackle the issue of the efficient provision of education in response to parental choice. The difficulty is to reconcile the Government's objective of extending parental choice with the removal of surplus places. The aim of the amendments is to reveal the problems that will arise as a result of that stated objective.

    It will be difficult to achieve the efficient delivery of an education service when the Bill would require the local education authority and the funding agency to have dual or parallel responsibility during the time when between 10 and 75 per cent, of pupils are in grant-maintained schools. That will result in the inefficient provision of an education service because it will be difficult for either the LEA or the funding agency to take the lead either in removing certain surplus places or in extending parental choice.

    What the Minister said this morning made matters more confused, but the amendments make it clear what should be the objectives in the provision of education and parental choice.

    The Government are committed to the removal of the 1·5 million surplus places that they identified. That objective will be difficult to achieve and it will considerably 366 reduce parental choice. The desire to extend parental choice and the recognition of the fact that there must be some reduction in the surplus capacity in schools are laudable objectives, but the dead hand of the Treasury is involved in the aim to remove such a large number of school places. If so many places are taken out of the school system, parental choice will be greatly reduced.

    The grant-maintained sector has expanded in only a handful of LEAs. It has caused difficulties in respect of admissions to individual schools and dissatisfaction among parents. Members of the Committee will be aware of the problems created in Hillingdon, Ealing and Kent by trying to give parents the schools of their choice. The expansion of the grant-maintained sector has not increased parental choice in those authorities: indeed, in many cases that choice has been denied.

    If surplus places are removed on the scale proposed by the Government, parents will be struggling over a limited number of places. Schools will choose the pupils rather than parents choose the schools. Some local authorities have taken the initiative to remove surplus places. A survey carried out by the Association of Metropolitan Authorities 12 months ago found that there had been a drop in successful primary school choices from 91 per cent, to 79 per cent, in one year in a west midlands local education authority, and a local education authority in Tyne and Wear had seen a decline from 83 per cent, to 63 per cent, in successful first choices over three years specifically because of the removal of surplus places.

    The paradox created by the Bill is that the removal of surplus places will deny parental choice. Small rural schools will face difficulties because of the Government's commitment to remove a large number of surplus places. Many authorities have done that already; they are often Conservative-controlled LEAs that wish to placate their masters in Central Office. Those small rural schools will be threatened with closure.

    Marsham county primary school in the constituency of the right hon. Member for Mid-Norfolk (Mr. Ryder), the Chief Whip, is a typical Victorian primary school. It is built around two classrooms and according to the Government and the LEA has a capacity of 64 pupils. At present 30 pupils attend the school; it is only half full. From a bureaucrat's view, a large number of surplus places should be removed, because those 30 pupils are taught in classes of 15 or 16. The school has the full support of parents; 18 sets of parents choose to send their children to it. But that does not matter to the Government or to Norfolk education authority because that school is to be closed as part of the exercise to remove surplus school places. The education committee voted for closure by just two votes, but that decision has now been referred to the full county council meeting in January for further consideration.

    That is a good example of the difficulties that occur when the desire to remove surplus places is the overriding consideration. That small village primary school has the strong support of the local community and parents, but parental choice is being denied because of the removal of surplus places. The Bill would provide for both parental choice and the removal of surplus places, but we believe that that is an impossible objective.

    We are worried that the grant-maintained sector could develop and expand with no parental support. It might even be expanded in the face of opposition from parents. The Government have always prided themselves on the JPEG 367 fact that the grant-maintained sector is built on parental choice. They argue that parents voting for schools to become grant maintained is a strong demonstration of the democratic process. Ministers point out proudly that the turn out in ballots for grant-maintained status is often higher than for local authority elections. The Bill allows the funding agency to expand the grant-maintained sector with no reference to the views of parents generally or those who have sponsored the establishment of a grant-maintained school.

    Money will be the key to the development of the grant-maintained sector. Last week we discussed the revenue that the Government would provide. The Under-Secretary of State for Schools said that the link between the amount of revenue that a grant-maintained school receives and an LEA's expenditure on education was important, reflecting, as it did, the amount that grant-maintained schools currently receive. He said that that may change, that the link may be broken if the number of grant-maintained schools increases. However, we must await the promised consultation document on revenue, which should come to us around Christmas.

    We know the Government's plans for the capital regime because the autumn statement made it clear how it will operate for the maintained and the grant-maintained sectors. The autumn statement revealed that, over a three-year period, a little more than 20 per cent, of the total capital for schools would go to the grant-maintained sector.

    That begs the question of what the funding authorities intend, given that they will have access to large capital sums from the Government. They will also have powers under clause 40 to establish afresh grant-maintained schools and under clause 86, to decide on "a significant enlargement" to a grant-maintained school—all that can be done without the obligation either to consult parents or re-ballot them on whether they want the number of pupils at a school to be doubled. All that is offered is some woolly wording about consultation taking place. If it is anything like the consultation that took place on the White Paper, it will not be worth much to parents. We shall discuss the issue of shared responsibility, which causes great difficulty, on later amendments.

    The Bill gives powers to the funding agency and the finance will be provided to it through the capital regime. The Bill allows for the possibility of the grant-maintained sector expanding, not because of parental choice or rights, but because the funding agency will decide, for whatever reason, that new GM schools need to be set up or that existing schools need to be enlarged. There could be circumstances in which the funding agency would decide that a grant-maintained school that currently offered comprehensive education should offer a specialism. The agency would then fund a specialist block in that school. There could be circumstances in which the agency might decide to set up a new school and propose that the Secretary of State remove surplus places in the same LEA area. All those powers have been given to the funding agency. We are worried that the Bill will not require that the exercise of those significant powers be dealt with efficiently or in a way that takes into account or respects 368 parental choice. That is why we have tabled the amendments in this group, and I recommend them to the Committee.
