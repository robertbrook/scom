Mr. Forth
: I think that I should now turn to a matter that has been mentioned frequently—the alleged apparent contradictions between the concept of surplus places, and parental choice. The White Paper identified up to 1·5 million surplus places in England—the "up to" is quite important. My Department, in its dealings with local education authorities up to now, has received returns of figures from them. When aggregated, those figures produced the calculation of up to 1·5 million surplus places. The LEAs' own figures therefore produced that total.

We are now engaged in the next round of the process. We have invited LEAs to identify how they will deal with the surplus places as a result of figures thrown up in their area. When that is known, we shall find ways of tackling the problem on an authority-by-authority basis. So far, that has been relatively straightforward.

The other day I received a deputation from an LEA, accompanied by two Members of Parliament—one from each major party. 396 My hon. Friend the Member for Battersea asked the same question. Put simply, surplus places are the total capacity of schools minus the pupils on roll. That is self-evident.

The key point is how we quantify the capacity. There are two ways of doing this: the standard-number-times-a-year group basis and the more open enrolment calculation. Both may produce different answers. Whichever formulation we use, we still throw up a large number of surplus places, LEA by LEA. The way in which the Department has calculated that capacity is detailed in circulars. I shall be happy to give my hon. Friends copies so that they can see the basis upon which my Department sets about calculating school capacity, measuring it against numbers on roll and arriving at the difference which, by definition, is a surplus.
