Education Bill

Clause 65
REPLACEMENT OF PROPOSED INITIAL

FIRST OR FOUNDATION GOVERNORS BEFORE INCORPORATION

10.30 am

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mrs. Ann Taylor
(Dewsbury)
: I welcome you, Sir John, back to the Committee. [Hon. Members: "Hear, hear,"] It is good to start on a note of agreement—an unusual occurrence in this Committee.

I hope that the Under-Secretary of State for Further and Higher Education will explain the Government's thinking behind clause 65, which, like the other clauses listed for debate today, raises significant and detailed questions. It provides for the replacement of

"proposed initial first or foundation governors before incorporation"

 in certain circumstances.

Obviously, the Bill, or other regulations, must cover every eventuality and so must contain a provision such as clause 65. However, the question arises as to who should nominate governors in such circumstances. In addition, who should nominate the governors of the grant-maintained school in the first place? The proposal that a small group of people whether it be the governing body that exists before the move to grant-maintained status, or, in the case of a voluntary aided school, the foundation—should determine who the new governors of the grant-maintained school will be reveals a narrow view of what a school is about. The provisions in the clause replicate those in other legislation, but they exhibit that narrowness of view of who should be responsible for appointing a school's governing body.

All hon. Members agree that the role of a governing body is very important, as are the nominations to it. Therefore, I hope that the Minister will acknowledge that a governing body should represent a community as widely as possible. That may not always happen if an existing governing body nominates its successors. Although that may not appear to be the biggest problem with the Bill, it is indicative of the approach adopted by Ministers in the weeks of our deliberations.

856 The move to grant-maintained status is very important. Opposition Members do not welcome it. However, if it is to happen, we must ensure that the right people become school governors. By "right people", I mean people who have an overview of what is involved in education, not people who represent various narrow interests. The governing body of a school that has sought grant-maintained status may have been split on that matter. Perhaps only part of the governing body will be willing to serve on or suggest nominations for the successor body. That would be a shame and would mean that the new governing body would be narrowly constituted. I hope that the Minister would not be happy with that.

For grant-maintained schools to be successful, their governing bodies should represent a wide range of people. I am worried that the clause provides that only a narrow range of people will choose the governors of a new grant-maintained school. I hope that the Minister will, from the outset, state that members of governing bodies should represent wider interests and experiences. The clause focuses on what would happen in specific circumstances, however, its principles apply to the entire Bill. It highlights the problems that we should tackle.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Further and Higher Education (Mr. Tim Boswell)
: It is a privilege to speak before you, Sir John, for the first time.

The hon. Member for Dewsbury (Mrs. Taylor) has exposed a difficulty that has been caused by rapid consideration of prior clauses during the previous sitting. Her points apply to earlier clauses, however, as she said, the principle behind the clause applies to the entire Bill, for example, clause 68, which deals with initial governors for new grant-maintained schools. The hon. Lady's argument deserves a response.

Clause 65, in accordance with the precedent set by the Education Reform Act 1988, provides for the replacement of proposed initial governors in the case of casual vacancies between nomination and the implementation of the proposals. Such vacancies could be caused by death, disqualification or withdrawal. I am sure that the Committee will not wish to resile from that principle.

The clause, and its concepts, in a narrow compass, are consistent with the 1988 Act. We have had experience of the implementation of that Act and the hon. Lady's fears have not been borne out. The establishment of first governors, their work on behalf of the school and the community, and the role of grant-maintained schools in the community have been positive. It is our intention and profound wish to replicate that under the Bill.

The hon. Member for Dewsbury rightly said that nominations to the governing body are important. They should not be taken lightly. Existing governors will take account of that as they nominate the first JPEG 857 governors of the new school. In considering how that should be done, there is a dissonance or tension between two views of the governors. First, they have certain duties, which we would all agree are extremely important. Whether or not they are in sympathy with the grant-maintained philosophy, although I imagine that they will be, the governors have a duty to the school. I am sure that, as well as carrying out their more precise duties, they will do their best to ensure that the school fulfils its role in the community. Secondly, in the view of what the hon. Member for Dewsbury, said, the governors should represent the community.

If we created a shopping list of all the possible interests in the community and sought to replicate them in the governing body, we would either find the task impossible or the school would be unmanageable because of the many interests for which we sought to cater. In the clause, based on the precedent of the 1988 Act, we wish to find a balance between prior representative interests, the need to run the school in an effective manner and the duties of governors, whoever they are, to that school. I believe that we have the balance right.

I remind the Committee that the first governors—both the initial ones and those who may succeed them—should include two representatives of parents from the school. Together with their children, parents are, after all, customers of the school and therefore, should be represented among the first governors.

Although it does not arise in the clause, the hon. Member for Dewsbury will be aware of the provisions for teacher governors, including the head teacher. The main interests of the school are directly represented on the governing body; that mechanism has proved to be successful under the terms of the 1988 Act.

It would be difficult to accommodate wider representation of interests, whether or not it arises directly on the stand part debate. I agree with the hon. Member for Dewsbury that it is important and desirable—and from the evidence it is happening—that governing bodies should represent their school and its interests. They should seek to do that in the context of the wider community and the public interest. The evidence that we put forward in our previous sitting suggests that a positive role is already being played by grant-maintained schools with such a constitution in the work of the community. I have addressed the hon. Lady's general considerations and I take them to be in sympathy with the clause, which I commend to the Committee.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 65 ordered to stand part of the Bill.
