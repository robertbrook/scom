Mr. David Jamieson
(Plymouth, Devonport)
: I hope that this group of amendments finds a resonance with members of the Committee. The Minister has said that the amendments challenge the definition of "special educational needs" that has existed since 1981, but that is why they have been tabled. We recognise that gifted or particularly able children should be included in that category.

Amendment 660 deals with the child who

"has displayed a consistent capacity to learn exceptionally quickly."

The Education Act 1944 affords

"For all pupils opportunities for education offering such variety of instruction and training as may be desirable in view of their different ages, abilities, and aptitudes."

Article 29 of the United Nations convention on the rights of the child states:

"the education of the child shall be directed to the development of the child's personality, talents, mental and physical abilities to their fullest potential."

Those definitions of children's needs stretch far beyond those special needs for which the 1981 Act provides.

Changes in the education system and the prevailing general social attitudes have sometimes diverted our attention from the needs of individual children. We shall fail in our duty and will deprive society at large if we do not provide the fullest opportunities for personal fulfilment for all children. Children of exceptional ability can be just as much a challenge, at home and at school, as those with learning difficulties. They have just as much right to have their needs defined and provided for as children with difficulties.

Children who have special talents in sport and music are often well catered for in the education system. Their talents are obvious and mechanisms in the system ensure that such talent is used to full effect. Children who are talented at sport find themselves in teams for competitive sports, or participating in individual events where they can show their prowess, which is recognised by their peers and others. Children with special abilities in music can play instruments and join groups and orchestras. Those facilities are easily available and are well documented.

The children who concern me most are those in the category of the intellectually gifted whose gifts lie in mathematics, science or technology—the more esoteric areas of learning. Such children may be pushed into taking up swimming, tennis or music so that those talents are used, although their true talent may lie elsewhere.

Society has found pejorative terms for intellectually gifted children. We speak of boffins; a word often used as a source of fun when describing especially clever people. I found the word egghead in a thesaurus. The word "swot" is a comic book term. All those words are pejoratives for intellectually gifted children with special abilities. I challenge members of the Committee to find a pejorative for children who are gifted in music or sport—society does not use such words for children with those gifts.

Children who are not given challenging work may become disinterested in the classroom curriculum and consequently their attainment may drop to the average of their peers or even below it. Teachers will then not regard 1056 such pupils as especially able and will not give them more advanced work. A vicious circle or downward spiral develops.

Children with particular gifts may feel ostracised by their peers. Occasionally they become disruptive or badly behaved. Tragically, many children—especially girls, according to research—will conceal their talents to avoid being ostracised.

A few years ago, a colleague who was an adviser in a local authority was called to see a seven-year-old girl with behavioural problems who had just begun her first year in junior school. The climax came during a maths lesson. The girl was drawing clocks and putting times on them. She suddenly tore up the paper and jumped on the desk. She was referred to an educational psychologist who sent her to my colleague. The girl was found to have ability in mathematics equivalent to a first-year mathematics undergraduate. That had not been recognised in the school. It took an educational psychologist, doing the equivalent of statementing on the girl, to establish her talent and special need. When that had been done, a programme of work to challenge her talent was formulated, with the help of a local secondary school and the local polytechnic. Had the girl's talent not been recognised, and action taken, I wonder what would have happened.

Such stories worry me. How many children whose ability is not recognised sit quietly in school and do not tear up paper and jump on their desk? That child made an outward demonstration of her feelings. I wonder how many do that. Children should not need to behave in that way. Their ability should be recognised by appropriate systems. I reject the suggestion that the assisted places scheme is an adequate device for that purpose. So many children have differing talents and abilities that if we attempted to find schools for them we would need as many schools as there were children. Such ability should be catered for within mainstream education and in comprehensive and the new grant-maintained schools. I do not suggest that those children should be statemented. The Bill must recognise what those children need. We tabled the amendment for that purpose.

Members of the Committee who are members of the Select Committee know that recent evidence to it challenged the need for statementing as the best method for all children. That was suggested by Mary Warnock in her evidence to the Committee and it is contained in its report, to be published later this year. We shall discuss it later. I would not take that route, but legal recognition of special ability is needed and the needs of those children must be identified.

It would be more appropriate to consider a number of devices, such as courses on the characteristics and educational needs of gifted children. They should be increased and included in syllabuses for all types of teacher training. All secondary schools should be screened to assist in detecting those with exceptional ability. Links could be established with colleges of further education and polytechnics to provide an enriched curriculum. All those avenues should be explored.

The National Extension college should be approached to see to what extent its courses could be used for similar purposes. The practice of accelerating gifted pupils into older age groups should become more general, and schools JPEG 1057 should be encouraged to help gifted children to take more GCSE examinations, or to sit them when they are younger than the normal age.

We could also consider work experience schemes to provide gifted children with the opportunity to experience demanding occupations that require rigorous intellectual and creative thought. The amendments would have an important effect on the Bill. I am sure that much of what I have said will find some resonance with members of the Committee, and I commend the amendments to the Committee.

The Audit Commission report "Getting the Act Together" discussed Clwyd, which has developed a register of pupils with special needs and is now piloting guidelines for entering pupils on it. This highlights good practice, one to which the Audit Commission felt it important to draw attention. The report states:

"The register, originally designed to encourage primary school teachers systematically to identify pupils with special needs, forms part of the LEA's scheme for the local management of schools (LMS). The register has two levels which relate to the severity of need in the child. Entry onto the first level of the register is a mark of a school's concern for a child experiencing difficulty in learning. A child can only be registered following discussion between the school and an LEA representative—usually the educational psychologist. Schools provide the extra help for pupils on level one of the register.


Level two of the register is for pupils with more severe special needs. The LEA is currently piloting guidelines for JPEG 1059 teachers which indicate the level of need in a child which triggers inclusion at level two. These guidelines are the outcome of co-operation between the LEA and head teachers. They do not outline rigid categories of special need, but give schools a measure with which to assess whether a child's difficulties are severe enough to trigger the provision of extra help from the LEA."

The report continues:

"Entry to level two of the register attracts additional funding for the school. The LEA has to agree that entry to this level is merited, and, as with entry to level one, this is done by educational psychologists who independenty assess whether a child's level of need is broadly consistent with the pilot guidelines."

That is a far more satisfactory way of going about things than that suggested in the paper that we received from the Secretary of State on 18 December on the common funding formula for grant-maintained schools, which discussed using a "proxy measure" for additional educational needs, perhaps

"based on the number of pupils at a school eligible for free school meals."

The paper continued:

"Using free school meal (FSM) numbers as the measure of CFF entitlement for additional educational needs seems to be the best option currently available."

If we can ensure that our definitions are correct, we should not need to use such proxy measures. We need to direct funding to and identify it more closely with children's needs. That could be achieved by adopting an approach similar to that of Clwyd.

That is a complex mouthful. That is absurd. According to the Education Act 1981, my constituent Stephen Hawking has a learning difficulty. That is a good example which shows how absurd the definitions are. We need to separate learning difficulties from the provision of access to schools or of equipment to enable a physically disabled student to have access to the curriculum. The failure to provide lifts or accessible doorways and toilets prevents disabled teachers and support staff, as well as disabled parents, grandparents or other members of the community, from entering schools. Are all such people to be thought of as having learning difficulties?

We are trying, in a small way, to correct that situation through the amendment.
