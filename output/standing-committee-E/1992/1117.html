Education Bill

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Schools (Mr. Eric Forth)
: I beg to move,

That, during the proceedings on the Education Bill, the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock, and with effect from Thursday 19th November at half-past Four o'clock.

May I say, Sir John, on behalf of the Committee, what a pleasure it is to find you in the Chair and how much we look forward to your traditional firm but fair guidance in what will no doubt be the long passage of this bulky Bill? On the occasions when you cannot be with us, we look forward to serving under the chairmanship of Mr. Martin, whose fairness and firmness are almost the equal of yours, Sir John.

I hope that it will be self-evident to the Committee that, given the length of the Bill and its detailed nature, we want to retain as much flexibility as possible on the sittings motion at this stage to assess, as progress is made, whether sittings should be held in the afternoon and, perhaps, even in the evenings. The sittings motion is framed in that spirit, in the hope that members of the Committee, under your guidance, Sir John, will want to make steady progress and to consider it in as much detail as the Committee believes is necessary.

I hope that the Committee will support the sittings motion.

Mrs. Ann Taylor
(Dewsbury)
: For once, I can agree with what the Minister said—1 am not sure how often that will happen during our sittings—in welcoming you, Sir John, and Mr. Martin to the Chair of the Committee. The Minister described you, Sir John, as being firm but fair and Mr. Martin as being fair but firm. I am not sure of the difference between those two approaches: we look forward to trying to detect any difference.

The Minister said that there should be some flexibility about the Committee's sittings. In the present political climate, I can understand why a Minister believes that flexibility is needed—as Ministers never know which crisis will hit them next. There may be occasions when members of the Committee will feel that it is inappropriate to sit in the afternoon because there is an emergency debate or, who knows, a vote of confidence.

Leaving aside the need for flexibility, I am anxious about some aspects of the sittings motion. I shall make only one or two brief comments on the subject because, as the Minister said, the Bill has much substance and we are keen to make progress and to discuss the detail. First, if the sittings on Tuesday and Thursday are inconvenient to the Secretary of State and that is the reason why he is not with us, we should be perfectly prepared to consider sitting on other days to facilitate his attendance. It would be proper for the Secretary of State to be a member of the Committee: indeed, it is insulting to the Committee and to the education world that he is not. It is surprising that the Secretary of State is not present because he claimed 4 personally to have written the White Paper on which the Bill is based. He said that the Bill would set the education agenda for the next 20 years; on Second Reading, he said that this was the biggest-ever Education Bill. Unfortunately, he was equally quick to let it be known that he did not intend to serve on the Committee. Opposition Members do not understand why he cannot find time to be with us.

Mr. James Pawsey
(Rugby and Kenilworth)
: The hon. Lady should be aware, given her long experience in the House, that the Labour Administration of 1974–79 established the precedent that Secretaries of State did not automatically serve on Standing Committees. I remind the hon. Lady of two Standing Committees on which Labour Secretaries of State did not serve—those considering the Community Lands Bill of 1974–75, presented by Tony Crosland, and the Aircraft and Shipbuilding Industries Bill of 1975–76. The hon. Lady is not being hypocritical—that is not a parliamentary word, so I shall not use it—but if she is not two-faced, she is certainly looking both ways.

Mrs. Taylor
: The hon. Gentleman shows the debating tone that he would like our proceedings to have. I do not recall any other Secretary of State making such arrogant and boastful claims about his authorship of a Bill and then declining to see the Bill through all its stages. The Secretary of State has boasted so much about his role in writing the Bill and about its effect on the future of education that serving on the Committee is the least that he could do. If some sittings prove to be inconvenient, Opposition Members will be willing to show the flexibility of which the Minister spoke earlier.

However, the Secretary of State may consider the Bill so perfect that he can forget about it. I should be more convinced about that had his Department not been writing amendments after the Bill had been sent to the printers. That shows that the Bill was far from perfect then. The sad truth for the junior Minister may be that the Secretary of State knows that the Bill is a mess and will need a great deal of amendment and that he has left the job of picking up the pieces to the junior Minister who has the pleasure of being with us this morning and for a long time to come. Time and time again, it has been shown that the Secretary of State does not like to be put on the spot and questioned. That was evident in the debate on the Floor of the House, in which the Secretary of State was reluctant to give way. That resistance was clear to Opposition Members and, as a result of the Secretary of State's absence from the Committee, the Minister will have to answer many questions on his behalf.

The second reason for my reservations about the sittings motion is that it is somewhat unusual to resort to afternoon sittings so early in the proceedings. I am sure that the hon. Member for Rugby and Kenilworth (Mr. Pawsey) will jump up any minute now to tell me that it is not unprecedented. I do not claim that it is: very few matters in the House are without precedent. However, I am sure that even the hon. Gentleman will acknowledge that it is highly unusual to move to afternoon sittings so early in the consideration of a major Bill. The last piece in the so-called jigsaw of education reform by the Government was not presented in the same way. Not only was there more time for consultation, but for a while the Committee sat in the morning only, to ensure that hon. Members had enough time to consider what amendments JPEG 5 might be necessary. That leads me to the obvious conclusion that the Government are intent on railroading the Bill through the Committee and the House as soon as possible.

Perhaps our worst fears will not be realised; perhaps Ministers do not hope to clock up a certain number of hours before implementing a guillotine, regardless of outstanding issues. I fear that, in moving the sittings motion, the Minister signalled his intention of simply getting the Bill through as quickly as possible rather than exploring the issues as fully as possible.

The third and final issue that I wish to raise about the sittings motion is the difficulties that have been caused by the time scale of the Bill's introduction. The White Paper was published at the end of July when Parliament was in recess and schools had started their summer holidays. The cut-off point for consultation was the end of September. That left little time for people to absorb the full impact of the proposals. The Bill was published on 29 October and Second Reading took place on 9 November. Today, 17 November, we are in Committee to begin detailed consideration of the Bill. If the Committee is to meet in the afternoons as well as in the mornings, we may have little time to consult people outside the House who wish to propose ideas for amendments. A great deal of anxiety has been expressed, both inside and outside the House, by those who believe that there has been insufficient time for consultation.

On Second Reading, the hon. Member for Rugby and Kenilworth said that the time that we took to consider the Bill was part of the consultation process.

Mr. Pawsey
: Shock, horror!

Mrs. Taylor
: The hon. Gentleman is shocked by that revelation, but it is in Hansard. When the hon. Member for Rugby and Kenilworth made those remarks, the Secretary of State nodded as if to agree with him. If the period of consultation is to stay open for the duration of our consideration of the Bill, that raises some interesting points, which should make us cautious about proceeding too quickly.

If consultation continues, that raises problems about the status of the clauses. Are we going to rush to consider the early clauses that cover the funding authority—an arrangement that the Minister has not thought through? If we rush the Bill through without giving people outside the House a further opportunity to consider it, the clauses will be far from perfect.

If the consultation process officially remains open, the Minister should confirm that so that interested groups know that they can make representations and that Minister will consider them. I do not say that Ministers will listen, because they rarely do that on education. Ministers have not accepted constructive criticisms of the Bill. They have not listened to the vast majority of people who are worried about education. They have not listened to parents, governors, teachers or to the professionals in the education world. They are becoming very isolated, and if we rush the Bill through, that isolation will create many problems in education for a long time to come. On that basis, I think that it would be most unwise of the Committee to meet in the afternoons as well as in the mornings, certainly until we have had proper time for further consultation on the Bill.

Given that the Government, Opposition parties and the various organisations that are genuinely concerned about education wish to comment on what the Government have described as a very important Bill, it is vital that we allow adequate time for consultation and do not at this juncture agree to sit in the afternoons.

Mr. Paul Murphy
(Torfaen)
: I welcome you, Sir John, from a Welsh point of view, to the Committee of a Bill that covers the Principality as well as England, although not, of course, Scotland.

I support the comments of my hon. Friend the Member for Dewsbury. The hon. Member for Bath (Mr. Foster) also made interesting comments about sittings. Given your long experience, Sir John, you will recall that over the years Governments have tended to use Standing Committees in different ways. Before the general election the Government decided to change the regulations governing local government finance and to introduce a council tax. I mention this because the Government decided that the Committee stage of the relevant Bill would last only three weeks and would meet on Tuesdays, Wednesdays and Thursdays—which it did, and deep into the night.

In the last two years of government before the general election, the Government took it upon themselves to manipulate Standing Committees to ensure that proper scrutiny of Bills did not take place. I fear that the Government have not yet realised that times have changed. They have a heavily reduced majority and they cannot rely on a number of their own Members of Parliament, so they can no longer steamroller as they used to do; nor can they decide to do anything that they wish.

Many issues in the Bill require greater attention and consultation, as my hon. Friend the Member for Dewsbury said. This is especially so from a Welsh viewpoint. I am pleased that the Minister of State, Welsh Office is present. I am sure that, as is usual, the Minister will give members of the Committee written information about the separate provisions for Wales and about the separate powers of the Secretary of State for Wales under the proposed legislation.

There are two reasons to doubt the need for the sittings motion proposed by the Government. First, in the 8 Principality there is universal agreement among people in education—whether from local education authorities, teaching unions, or from academics who teach education in universities in Wales—that there has been inadequate consultation about a measure—especially the provisions on grant-maintained schools—that has extremely low support among people of Wales. Of just over 2,000 schools in the Principality, only three have decided to opt out, so there is no reason for separate legislation for Wales to introduce grant-maintained schools. The consultation exercise was wholly inadequate and the Bill is built on shifting sand.

Secondly, we should consider local government reorganisation and how education is administered in Wales. It is highly unsatisfactory, to say the least, to discuss such a large Bill, many clauses of which apply to local education authorities in the Principality, when at the same time the Secretary of State for Wales promises the House of Commons and the people of Wales a White Paper, possibly within the next few days, on the new shape and structure of local education authorities in Wales. We understand that the Secretary of State for Wales intends to introduce a unitary system of local government.

Mr. Win Griffiths
(Bridgend)
: My hon. Friend has raised a vital matter. There is a strong case for proceeding with only morning sittings until we have had a chance to read the White Paper, and for us then to consider whether it would be appropriate to have afternoon sittings. Mr. Murphy
: I agree entirely with my hon. Friend the Member for Bridgend (Mr. Griffiths): the proposal is for about 25 to 30 new unitary authorities in the Principality, all of which will include local education authorities. Some of the provisions of the Bill that we shall discuss may be meaningless until we know the exact shape, structure, functions and nature of those new unitary authorities. It is premature for the Government to introduce these education measures before we know the likely shape of local government in Wales. That is not untypical of the Government because in the past few years they have thoroughly messed up the system of local government in Wales and in England. They have no time for local government and the way in which they are processing does not surprise me.

The Bill will be badly affected by changes to the local government structure in Wales. I hope that the Minister of State will tell us—this morning if possible—whether the Government are to make an announcement in the next few days about the shape of local government in Wales and, if so, whether that impinges on our discussions of the Bill.

The other aspect of the Bill that affects Wales is the setting up of several quangos in the Principality. Except for the six Tory Members of Parliament in Wales, we are all deeply opposed to the extension of that system of government. The consultation process has been too short and has not taken into account the objections to those quangos in Wales. In two weeks' time, the Public Accounts Committee will be looking carefully at the workings, or the shenanigans, of some quangos in the Principality.

For those reasons, the points made by my hon. Friend the Member for Dewsbury are valid and I hope that the Government will reconsider the sittings motion.
