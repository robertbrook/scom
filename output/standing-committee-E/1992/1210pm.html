Mr. Win Griffiths
(Bridgend)

Friend the Member for Wallsend (Mr. Byers) how the Secretary of State, given his well-known prejudice and bias towards grant-maintained schools, could retain that independence in a dispute between a grant-maintained school which wanted to double its intake and a local authority that protested that that would damage education in one of its schools.

Mr. Stephen Byers
(Wallsend)
: I have had time to ponder that question. Even for the present Secretary of State it must be difficult to be impartial. He is a man of great and wondrous ability, but even he may have problems in discharging that task in the quasi-judicial manner that is his statutory responsibility. We already know the difficulties that were caused by his off-the-cuff remarks in Kirklees. As the Mirfield Reporter reported, he implied that an application would be successful. That is difficult to reconcile with a commitment to examine all factors and matters in an even-handed way.

That is the problem with clause 13. The Secretary of State makes the rules and appoints members of the funding agency. Clause 13 states that, in a dispute over functions between a democratically elected LEA and a funding agency appointed by the Secretary of State, the Secretary of State will have the power to make the decision and resolve the dispute. That is neither fair nor just.

Disputes over functions that will be referred to the Secretary of State involve difficult and contentious issues. For example, the Minister spoke earlier today about what constitutes a significant change of character. That is the type of dispute between the funding agency and an LEA which may be referred to the Secretary of State. I was interested to hear the Minister say that he had no doubts about the matter. He gave the Committee a guarantee that a proposal to introduce selection into a school's admission policy would amount to a significant change of character which would have to be referred to the Secretary of State. There was no qualification as to the subjects that would be part of such a selection process.

That guarantee is interesting in the light of a letter from the Department for Education to Langley Park school for boys in Bromley. A civil servant called Mr. John Cairncross in the Department wrote:

"The Department takes the line that the introduction of any degree of selection in conventional academic subjects, 642 even if limited to a certain proportion of places at the school, amounts to a significant change of character and should be the subject of statutory proposals. On the other hand, we take the view that the introduction of a degree of selection on the basis of aesthetic subjects or sport need not be the subject of statutory proposals."

In many respects that differs from the Minister's observations this morning. It raises the question of whether the Minister's statement was incorrect. That would be unusual. It would be interesting to hear the Minister's definition of "conventional academic subjects" and to know what would come under the category of "aesthetic subjects" on the basis of which selection will be permitted. Those vital issues need to be addressed.

The Bill causes confusion. The Minister, in trying to be helpful, has confused hon. Members even more. The letter from Mr. Cairncross does not help to clarify the matter. Such disputes should not be resolved by the Secretary of State. We hope that the Minister will clarify what is meant by "selection" in that context.

It is bad law and I hope that the clause will not get past the Committee.

Mr. Foster
: Clause 15(2)(c) provides for primary schools to operate as a group. A single governing body would conduct the group's affairs. The subsection refers to chapter IX, which provides other options for clustering.

The Bill provides many options for groupings or clusterings. For example, clause 105 provides for

"two or more existing primary schools to acquire grant-maintained status and be conducted as a group by a single governing body."

That refers to primary schools that are initially LEA-maintained. I am not happy with that option. Before a school is allowed to have grant-maintained status, it should demonstrate its ability to stand on its own two feet.

In other parts of the Bill there are various options for groupings or clusters. For example, under clause 114, a primary school eligible for grant-maintained status, or one that has already opted out, may become a member of an existing group. There is the possibility of two existing groups joining together and of a school leaving a group. All those options are, properly, subject to a range of caveats.

668 However, the option for two or more existing grant-maintained schools to join together to form a group is missing from the Bill. That is likely to arise from proposals made by the schools themselves.

Clusters or groupings of that sort may be important, perhaps for the survival of one or more schools, especially in rural areas. The arguments about the importance of maintaining small schools in rural communities are well known to the Committee. If one or more of those schools is in difficulty, forming a cluster or group may help to save the school and thus a rural area's educational provision. That can be done in two ways—first by a voluntary move and secondly, by the more precise procedure of a group having a single governing body. That is possible for LEA schools and, in some circumstances, it has benefits for the survival of a school, especially in a rural area.

It is strange that the option is not available to grant-maintained schools. Much as I dislike the concept, if there are to be grant-maintained schools, those in a rural area may need to take that option either to survive or to provide more efficient and effective education for the pupils in that area or geographical vicinity. That it does not may be merely an omission and the new clause and the amendment provide an opportunity to explore that issue with the Minister. He may argue that the wording of the new clause could be improved, but I hope that he will respond positively to my proposal.
