I do not mind which of the amendments the Committee favours as long as Conservative Members have sympathy with one of them, or at least with some of my arguments.

The main point that I was making last week is that the Bill makes no direct reference to enclosure hedgerows. It can be argued that without such reference it weakens the ancient and historic protection that hedgerows should enjoy. I hope that the Committee understands that thousands of miles of hedgerow are involved.

It was suggested last week that because there are 4,000 separate Acts covering commons enclosure, there is vast diversity in the language of those Acts. At the beginning of the week I obtained a copy of an article by Mr Luke Clements in an ecological magazine. It referred to the common nature of a very large proportion of the enclosure Acts and the allotment clause, which often states:

"Plot [number …] shall be allotted to [name …] who shall make and forever thereafter maintain the hedges [detailing which] and the duty shall pass to his heirs and assign."

Many acts of Parliament confirming the enclosure of areas of common land in Britain contain the same terms, so the argument on excessive variety does not apply. In those days Parliament recognised that it was taking land from the community, giving it to a few landowners and farmers, and placing a specific and strict duty that they and anyone who succeeded them in the possession of that land should maintain the hedgerows for ever more.

I have said before, but it is worth repeating, that during the 30 or 40 years in which hedgerow destruction 74 proceeded apace in Britain—it is still proceeding—many enclosure hedgerows were destroyed by default. It will be interesting in due course to hear what the Parliamentary Commissioner says about a case that I presented to him a few days ago. Having observed the imagination which villainy often possesses in exploiting loopholes in legislation to conserve our natural and national heritage, I fear that the omission of a reference to enclosure hedgerows in the Bill will result in some judge or magistrate mistakenly or mischievously failing to take the matter seriously and allowing that omission to weaken the historic protection that enclosure legislation should afford. I do not mind which of the two amendments the hon. Member for Surrey East (Mr. Ainsworth) accepts. However, I firmly believe that, to ensure that no loophole or excuse for hedgerow destruction is created, one of the two amendments should be accepted.

Late yesterday, I passed a copy of Mr Clements' article to the Minister, who perhaps not this morning but on Report, may offer the informed view of himself and his colleagues in the Department of the Environment. They cannot afford recklessly to ignore the legal provision that has been established.

Last week, I said that it was a pity that a test case has not been brought. I am sure that all members of the Committee will hope that the Bill reaches the statute book in reasonable condition, but once it does so the need for a test case will become even stronger.

Conservative Members often talk about the need to observe the rule of law, although occasionally some of them are prepared to accept commercial interests that do not. We should not enact one law that ignores another. Therefore, if the amendments are resisted, similar amendments will have to be pursued at a subsequent stage, but I hope that that will not be necessary.

Mr. Ainsworth
: Clause 4(5) has provoked considerable controversy and a great deal of thought. As members of the Committee are by now aware, the Bill aims to provide controls on hedgerow removal so that the most valuable examples may be retained in the public good. To expect individual farmers or landowners to bear the costs of this in the interests of the wider public good is not a new concept. For farmers in particular, it can be argued that the high level of agricultural subsidy paid from the public purse entitles the public to some protection of the environment in return.

The Bill recognises that there may be circumstances in which the cost to the individual concerned may be so great that it outweighs the value of the hedgerow to society. Clearly it is a difficult balancing act. The decision we are left with is how best to achieve this balance.

The intention behind the Bill is to encourage landowners to examine hard ways of operating their businesses which make use of the land without removing valuable hedgerows. That contrasts to the present situation in which hedgerow removal may be the first and most obvious measure contemplated.

I want the system to be fair, but it would not be consistent with the aims of the Bill or with general good practice to create a situation in which almost every case of refused hedgerow notification could plausibly go to appeal 82 because some economic consequence could be shown. Clearly, there will be an economic case of some sort for most hedgerow removals.

Where a local authority's decision to refuse a notification leads to an appeal, clause 4(5) empowers the Secretary of State to allow the appeal if he is satisfied that the proposed work is necessary to secure the viability of the business. That test has been regarded as too severe, as it would effectively prevent almost any appeal from being successful. That is certainly not the intention of the Bill, so I have reconsidered the point.

Amendment No. 38 provides that an appeal may be allowed if

"any business … would be seriously impaired if the work in question were not carried out".

I shall set out briefly why I prefer that test to the measures in the other amendments tabled by the hon. Member for North Cornwall and by my hon. Friend's the Members for Cirencester and Tewkesbury and for Hexham.

Amendment No. 37 suggests that an appeal should be allowed where

"retention of the hedgerow … would pose a significant threat to"

the viability of any business. I accept that that is a less severe test than that contained in the Bill, because there would no longer be a requirement to show that it was necessary for the work to go ahead in order to secure the viability of the business—only a threat to viability would need to be demonstrated.

Amendments Nos. 66 and 16 weaken the test to an unacceptable level. Amendment No. 66 does so by including two additional criteria: that the work is necessary

"in the interests of good husbandry"

of the land, or

"in the interests of the sound management of the land". Expressions such as "good husbandry" and "sound management" have an appealing resonance, but those criteria are so wide that they could probably be applied to justify almost any case of hedgerow removal. For example, it could be argued that the removal of all hedges was in the interests of the good husbandry of oilseed rape or wheat.

Amendment No. 16 substitutes the concept of viability with a test based on efficiency, by providing that an appeal could be allowed if it would

"prevent the efficiency of any business … from being substantially impaired".

"Efficiency" is capable of wide interpretation—so wide that the amendment would create the risk that a vast majority of cases of refused hedgerow notifications would go to appeal, at the taxpayer's expense. Efficiency implies a ratio of output to input, which can be measured in many different ways. For example, in recent years farming has become highly efficient in terms of labour, but that has been possible only because of the increased use of machinery. In terms of fossil fuel use and the cost of unemployment, the efficiency of agriculture presents a very different picture.

It is relatively easy to be efficient if the cost to the environment is ignored. The concept of efficiency, therefore, is insufficiently clear to appear in the Bill.
