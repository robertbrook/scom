10.32 am

The Minister for Corporate Affairs (Mr. John Redwood)
: I shall certainly take the hon. Gentleman's comments into consideration.

I am pleased to introduce the Department's explanatory memoranda and the draft directive, in its various versions. I shall clarify what technical issues I can in answer to questions and hon. Members may find a discussion on them helpful.

As many members of the Committee will know, I believe strongly in the importance of the role that Parliament plays in appraising and in commenting on draft European legislation and I have found the comments of many hon. Members valuable in forming the Government's negotiating position on this important matter. A scrutiny reserve remains in place around the political agreement that we reached—somewhat to our 2 surprise—on 18 and 19 December and the purpose of today's sitting is to decide whether, in the light of our discussion, that scrutiny reserve should be lifted.

The original Commission proposal of 9 July 1991, set out in draft instrument 7573/91, has been tabled and we have sent out explanatory memoranda. The essence of the proposal was that it promoted D2-MAC—a means of broadcasting—by making it mandatory for all new service providers to use that format. We objected strongly to forcing a specific new format on satellite broadcasters, especially as it is a format that has so far been far from successful in the marketplace. We disliked the fact that the proposal would impose an additional cost of £50 to £100 on the purchaser of every television set throughout the Community to pay for the compulsory decoders that are necessary to receive D2-MAC broadcasts. As many purchasers of television sets would not intend to pay for such a service, decoders would be redundant and there would be an additional cost to consumers of conventional PAL television broadcasts.

We were also against the restriction implied by the draft directive on all digital technology. At the moment, broadcasting in the European Community uses primarily the PAL system, which provides normal television services. The Americans and Japanese have been working hard on a digital system of transmission. In future, a digital system might take over from the present system.

The draft directive arising from the Commission's proposal of 9 July 1991 would have prevented European companies from responding to new digital technology. It would have forced them to develop the D2-MAC and HD-MAC technology preferred in the proposal. We want to remove that restriction. One cannot be sure how the market will develop, but it could be extremely dangerous to European companies if they were debarred from a major new technology that could prove to be a world winner. That draft directive would have meant that European companies would be backward in the market, unable to meet the possible world demand for digital technology.

We were prepared to have HD-MAC—a new product—as the standard for high-definition television within the Community, because technical experts think that it is a sensible way to service that need. We were also prepared to allow D2-MAC—an intermediate technology—to be developed in Europe for 16:9 format televisions—cinema-widescreen televisions of which a few models are available at considerable expense. D2-MAC is a sensible way of fulfilling the need to broadcast cinema-wide, rather than on the 4:3 dimensions of traditional televisions.

We consulted widely on the proposal and our scepticism was reflected in the consultations. Several Members of Parliament have raised points with me. On 18 and 19 December we met to consider an amended Commission proposal—the subject of an explanatory memorandum of 11 December 1991 on EC document 10157/91—which went some way in our direction but still fell well short of our negotiating aims on the directive. Officials and I went to that meeting with a heavy heart about the draft proposal; we thought that it would be impossible to reach an agreement. However, many member states agreed with the British Government's reservations and supported some of our proposals to write a more acceptable measure.

The final text of our work of 18 and 19 December was received only recently and it was circulated by the JPEG 3 Department as soon as possible. I believe that it—the draft minutes of the 1549 Council meeting on telecommunications held in Brussels—has now reached every member of the Committee.

The agreement that was reached will not make D2-MAC compulsory for transmissions to 4:3-style televisions until 1 January 1995 and even then it will be conditional on EC funding being available for both simulcasting in PAL—the present system—and D2-MAC.

Under the draft directive, decoders will not be compulsory, except for widescreen televisions, as they need decoders. We do not mind that, because there will be no surcharge on the price of standard televisions which receive traditional services.

The draft directive explicitly excludes digital technology, allowing companies and broadcasters to develop digital technology in the European Community if they wish, so we shall no longer be vulnerable to a technical challenge from Japan and the United States of America which we would have been unable to answer under the original draft directive.

I hope that the Committee agrees that the document represents dramatic progress, faced as we were with an inappropriate draft directive which would have done great damage to broadcasters, satellite service providers and many in the industry producing the equipment. It is important that European industry be geared to products that are saleable in the world markets and to debar digital technology could have done us damage.

Following political agreement—if the draft directive goes through as a common position and becomes a directive, as it is likely to do, given the voting balance in the Community on the issue—there will be two other matters to sort out. First, by voluntary initiative, a memorandum of understanding will be signed by the companies and parties interested in promoting the D2-MAC and HD-MAC technology, which our companies can enter if they think it right to do so. Secondly, the Community will consider whether funding should be made available to promote that technology and to promote simulcasting.

In some Community circles and in the press, there has been mention of figures as large as 1,000 mecu being available for those purposes. The Government have made it clear that we want an extremely good proposal, well costed, specifying the benefits and explaining why Community money, as opposed to commercial money, is necessary for such a venture. We would also want to be sure that any such monies would be spread around the Community, rather than being concentrated in one or two companies or countries, which would be undesirable.

Unless there is vigorous justification of a sensible-sized programme, the Government will not give their consent. We believe that the funding proposal will come forward under article 235, so it will require unanimity and we could block it if we did not like it.

The final document before us is 8122/91, which is a small measure to promote a stock of material in the 16:9 cinema screen format for television. The Government have been influential in discussing how that might be arranged and in limiting the sum of money required. We did not think that it would be sensible, for example, to commission an expensive European soap as a series of programmes. 4 We tried to confine the issue to support where there is a genuine need for additional expense in producing material in a new format, and for training, research and so on.

I would be happy to answer questions.

Mr. Redwood
: I sympathise with my hon. Friend's points. Had Britain been on her own in the negotiation, we would not have proceeded with the directive. But there was a clear wish on the part of many Community countries to have some sort of directive and many of them agreed with us that grave dangers were inherent in the original draft. When we first considered the original draft, it was not clear that we would receive such support within the Community. Indeed, we worked hard to obtain support for the justifiable fears that we and many people in commerce shared about the proposal.

There is an argument for establishing a recommended system for widescreen television. Standards must be laid down for national or international systems. Widescreen television is not up and running anywhere in the Community, so there is a case for such technology to be used to develop it. It is important, however, not to debar ourselves from a future technology that could be superior to others and could be developed worldwide. That is the reason behind the digital exemption.

We have also narrowed the impact of the directive by confining the dates of the proposal from January 1995 to 1998, so that the Community will have the opportunity to reconsider the matter. In the period before the directive becomes fully operational, it is up to people to set up services in any technological format they like which will allow the market to develop further.

A community directive issued on 5 December 1991 says in the third paragraph on page 2:

"Whereas HD-MAC has been developed as the European transmission standard for HDTV services based on those criteria of compatibility and evolution and D2-MAC offers the best available path towards the development of HDTV with wide-screen 16:9 D2-MAC as the main step in that direction".

That sums up the Commission's attitude on the subject. It has made an historic commitment to the MAC transmission system, so we must ask ourselves why we are where we are.

Throughout Europe, major political and technical reputations have been staked on the MAC format. Two very large companies, Philips and Thomson, have committed vast resources to the development of the MAC JPEG 11 format and have been backed, through the Eureka programme, by the European Community. Some of the best technical players in the United Kingdom have also thrown their weight behind it.

The MAC programme, a superb piece of engineering, is about as much use now as the Brabazon or possibly even Concorde, two superb pieces of engineering which led to many other major developments but which, in themselves, were not successful worldwide. Some might quarrel with my assessment of Concorde, but my comments are certainly applicable to the Bristol Brabazon.

Hon. Members may remember from the early days of video recorders a box about 8 in by 2·5 in, which was a Philips video cassette. Philips put enormous sums of money into the development of what quickly became a totally redundant video recorder. The company lost. It put its eggs into that basket and lost great sums of money. That is not to say that the engineers were not good or that they did not put sufficient expertise into it. Even the Beta system fell by the wayside and most people in most countries now use VHS for domestic purposes—although not exclusively for professional purposes, where Beta is used. The others failed.

MAC is in this category. The time has come for Europe to acknowledge that fact and to get on the right bandwagon. The hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, North (Mr. Henderson) was correct to say that there is a grave danger that Europe, not just the United Kingdom, will miss out. All is not lost, however. In the development of the MAC system, which stretched the narrow screen to a wider cinema format screen, the engineers had to use digital technology to fill in the edges, so we do not lack digital technology in the United Kingdom and in Europe. That is the path that we must tread.

We must now discard the wrapping and get rid of MAC. We must recognise the fact that some people made wrong decisions. We all make wrong decisions from time to time, on both sides of the House and throughout Europe. There is no shame in acknowledging that, but there is some shame if we nail our colours to the wrong mast and do not recognise our mistakes.

Friend the Member for Bury, North (Mr. Burt), I wonder what a politically correct soap opera would comprise, but I fear that I should not enjoy it very much and, I suspect, nor would many of my constituents.

My hon. Friend the Member for Bury, North said that it was important that the Community should not make decisions to subsidise. I reassure my hon. Friend that the decisions before us today do not include a decision to subsidise anything. That will come later, when we have the JPEG 23 Commission proposal for funding. That proposal must be under article 235, which requires unanimity; that will give us considerable leverage—rather more than we had in these negotiations, which were conducted under qualified majority voting. Therefore, we always had to be careful to ensure that we had several countries in agreement with us to enable us to move forward.

I agree with my hon. Friend the Member for Bury, North that it is usually right to leave technology to the marketplace and that is largely what we do. Perhaps my hon. Friend would agree, however, that sometimes it is sensible to establish a standard to which people can produce. It is always difficult to judge exactly when that standard should be set and there will always be a row about what it should comprise. If it is done too soon, what the market might produce is not known; if it is done comparatively late in the process, there will be quite a lot of losers who will have backed other technologies that have not been incorporated. It is a difficult judgment to make. I hope that we are making it in the right way in this draft directive.

I remind my hon. Friends the Members for Thanet, North and for Bury, North about the dates. Under the draft directive, new services are mandatory under the MAC system only from 1 January 1995 to 31 December 1998, when the directive will need renewal or it will expire. That is very different from the original proposal, under which the mandatory requirement on a much wider range of services would have come in at a much earlier date and have lasted for much longer. I hope that that helps my hon. Friends who were worried about that aspect.

My hon. Friend the Member for Eltham (Mr. Bottomley) said that often British commercial interests can be both practical and farsighted. I agree with him. We have tried to reflect that in our negotiations in the European Community on this and on many other directives. We want to discuss the detail and the practicalities and not vague, general theories. I agree with my hon. Friend that, whenever possible, we want enabling measures—ones that open up opportunities—rather than restrictive measures that can become constraints on trade and, indeed, can damage the future economic and industrial development of the Community.

I have learnt from this debate that there is a strong scepticism in the Committee about subsidy of any kind. I shall take account of that view in our negotiations on the money issues. I shall also ensure that any proposal is sent to the correct authorities in the House for early scrutiny. We should lift the scrutiny reserve on the draft directive because we cannot improve on its shape and, as my hon. Friend the Member for Thanet, North pointed out, there are many ways in which it could be made worse. If we keep it in this form, it has a good chance of being accepted, whatever the European Parliament may say. However, if we try to tamper with it further, we could end up with a worse proposition.

Some of the comments in the Committee have renewed my worries about incorporating exclusive technologies in legislation, particularly if the legislation has a long shelf-life and there is no obvious way of amendment or revision. I want the United Kingdom presidency of the 24 Single Market Council to consider as a priority whether the legislation that has been agreed so far is made too restrictive by imposing exclusive technologies, and whether that could become an anti-competitive problem and a danger for Community-based industries that are debarred by directive and Community law from developing technologies that will have wider global appeal. That could apply across the board. I hope that the Committee will endorse the Government's action so far and is happy with those replies.
