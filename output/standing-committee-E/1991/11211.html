Fitment of Speed Limiters

10.30 pm

Mr. Peter L. Pike
(Burnley)

The Minister for Roads and Traffic (Mr. Christopher Chope)
: There are two Commission proposals before us today—8046/91 on the fitment of speed limiters and 7816/91 on the technical standard for speed limiters. the proposal on fitment has been the subject of an intensive series of meetings by officials in the Council's transport working group and it will be put to the Transport Council on 16 December. The technical proposal will go before the Internal Market Council on 19 December. I shall direct most of my remarks to the fitment proposal, although there are some important points to be made about technical standards, especially with regard to enforcement.

There is no doubt about the Community's competence in the sector of vehicle standards. Indeed, many of our vehicle manufacturers are strongly in favour of harmonised European Community rules because they say that domestic standards, which differ from the European norm, can add to their costs. The Government have already provided for the fitment of speed limiters on coaches and new heavy goods vehicles, and we welcome moves towards more widespread use of speed limiters in Europe. One obvious advantage of that is that visiting vehicles from Europe will be speed limited, and, that therefore, our hauliers, operating in competition with foreign hauliers, will not be disadvantaged.

Speed limiters would not be needed if lorry and coach drivers did not drive faster than the law allows. Their use could also be avoided if enforcement by the police forces of the Community could ensure 4 that drivers did not exceed speed limits. But that is impractical. In the United Kingdom, 40 per cent of HGVs, weighing more than 7.5 tonnes gross weight, break the speed limit of 60 mph on the motorways and more than 20 per cent exceed 60 mph on dual carriageways, where the lower speed of 50 mph applies. That is why we have already provided for the fitment of limiters to new HGVs that are first used on or after 1 August 1992.

Speed limiters offer both road safety and environmental benefits. Casualty savings are available because limiters prevent vehicles from travelling above the legal limit, and excessive speed is a factor in causing road accidents. Environmental benefits arise from a reduction in the consumption of fossil fuels, and a reduction in the emission of gases, which contribute to global warming. Although fitting speed limiters represents an additional cost to vehicle operators, there are offsetting benefits, too, which mean that many operators stand to recover their outlay in a fairly short space of time through reduced fuel consumption and less wear and tear on their vehicles. Those operators who only employ responsible drivers, who never travel at illegal speeds, stand to gain little from limiting devices.

The scope of the proposal from the Commission on the fitment of speed limiters, document 8046/91, is wider than we would have liked, particularly on coaches. However, it is probable that the text to be put before the Council will be more restricted. I understand that the Dutch presidency is suggesting that limiters should be required only on coaches over 10 tonnes maximum weight. It is also likely that fitment to existing vehicles will be less demanding and that vehicle operators would have until 1996 to comply. That would be more acceptable, and it would give us plenty of time in which to comply.

By far the most difficult aspect of the proposal is that it seeks to prescribe Community-wide speeds at which the limiters should be set. The proposal would have the effect of limiting the speeds of lorries and coaches covered by the directive to speeds below the current United Kingdom limits for those vehicles. We have repeatedly said in the transport working group that this is not acceptable to us, and we have placed a reserve on the proposal for that reason.

We believe that speed limits should remain a matter for individual member states to decide. They design and maintain their own roads, they have their own rules about driving standards, and casualty statistics differ widely between them. It is true that the United Kingdom has some of the highest speed limits in the Community for coaches and HGVs, but we also have a better road casualty record than most of our European partners.

To limit vehicles to our current maximum speed limits is one thing, but limiting them to below that speed—thereby incurring a significant cost penalty for no good reason—is going too far. Operators would not be able to recoup the cost of fitting speed limiters if they had to travel at 10 mph below the speed limit. I cannot estimate the precise cost to individual operators because circumstances vary, but it would certainly be significant. The Freight Transport Association—the FTA—suggests that the cost to JPEG 5 operators would be between £250 million and £500 million, of even £700 million.

As things stand, none of the other member states seem to have any fundamental difficulty with the speed settings limit proposed by the Commission. That means that there is little room to negotiate. The proposal is subject to majority voting.

I mentioned the proposal on technical standards, document 7816/91. That is based on a standard agreed by the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe, a wider group of countries than the EC. The UNECE has been active for many years on vehicle standards and has good liaison arrangements with the EC. The British standard on speed limiters is also based on the same principles. The technical proposal is therefore acceptable as it stands, except that it, too, has references to set speeds. Those will be dropped from the final text; there will instead be a cross-reference to the limits in proposal 8046/91.

Both proposals lack a clear provision on enforcement. If we are to see the full benefits of speed limiters it is essential for member states to introduce adequate arrangements to ensure that they will be properly installed and secured against tampering.

It is with some surprise that we find it difficult to get support from other countries that are in favour of speed limiters, but which do not want them to be tamperproof. I leave it to members of the Committee to draw their own conclusions. However, we shall take up the argument with the Transport Council.

Ultimately, we should like to see a common EC standard to enable member states to use diagnostic equipment to check at annual roadworthiness tests that limiters are working and are correctly calibrated.

The hon. Lady asked how many vehicles would be involved if extra vehicles had to be put on the roads because each existing vehicle could not be used so much during the day. The FTA has sent the Government and, I imagine, others as well, its estimates of the implications of the draft directive. It carried out a survey of a cross-section of its members. Of those who make regular use of motorways, 20 percent would expect to reduce their use of those roads by an average of 13 per cent if a 50 mph limit were introduced, and 31.3 per cent of those surveyed would expect to increase their vehicle fleet by an average of 8 per cent to compensate for a lower motorway speed limit.

By applying those figures to all the contributors to the survey, the FTA calculated that the total HGV fleet would increase by 2.8 per cent, which is equivalent to 13,000 extra HGVs on our roads. Co-incidentally, that is almost exactly the number by which we have been able to reduce the incidence of such vehicles on our roads by increasing the weight limit from 32 tonnes to 38 tonnes. The benefit achieved by reducing the number of HGVs on our roads, which was generated by raising the weight limit, would be removed at a stroke by the introduction of a speed limit of 50 mph on motorways.

On road safety, the hon. Lady will know that, as confirmed in the FTA letter, vehicles will be diverted from the safest roads, which are our motorways, to less safe roads, because there would be no speed advantage in using the safer roads. That would cause added congestion and danger, so the proposal entails a road safety disbenefit. Although savings in the form of greater fuel efficiency would result from the reduction in speed, there would also be disbenefits, 8 such as greater congestion on other roads as a result of the diversions that I described.
