legislation European Community Documents Nos. 4173/91, 4174/91 and 4175/91 relating to Copyright, Lending Right, Rental Right and Accession of Member States to the Berne Convention

10.30 am

The Paliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Industry and Consumer Affairs (Mr. Edward Leigh): The motion arises out of an ambitious programme of work which the European Commission has embarked on regarding copyright.

The Commission's programme forms part of its work towards the completion of the internal market. It wishes to ensure that those who create, produce and perform works of literature, art and music have the necessary legal rights to combat piracy of their works throughout the Community. It also seeks to harmonise those rights, where necessary, to ensure fair competition in the supply of goods and services that are protected by copyright.

The Government fully support those objectives. The United Kingdom is in the fortunate position of having many talented authors, artists, composers and performing artists whose work is in great demand throughout Europe. However, they do not enjoy the protection in all countries of the Community that our domestic law provides. We therefore broadly welcome the Commission's Initiatives and intend to play a constructive part in negotiations about them. We do, however, recognise that copyright law must balance the rights of copyright owners and performers with the needs of society as a whole. Furthermore, not all aspects of copyright are relevant to the workings of the internal market, and we shall examine the Commission's proposals carefully to ensure that they do not extend into areas which should properly remain within the competence of member states.

Document 4173/9 sets out the Commission's working programme about copyright and so-called neighbouring rights. I should perhaps explain that there are differences between member states about what those expressions cover. In the United Kingdom copyright protects literary works, musical and artistic works, films, sound recordings, and broadcasts. Performers' rights are protected separately as a neighbouring right. However, in several other countries, sound recordings and broadcasts fall within neighbouring rights rather than copyright. The point should not cause any substantive difficulties in the negotiations nor will it, I trust, in this debate.

Col. 4 The working programme is, as I have said, ambitious. It envisages proposals for legislative action in six areas this year, and a programme of studies and other actions is to be completed by the end of 1992. Two of the legislative proposals have already been made and are part of the subject matter of our debate. Of the remaining four proposals, the Government welcome the Commission's intention to harmonise the term of protection for copyright and certain neighbouring rights. We feel that there is a particular need to ensure that sound recordings are protected in all countries of the Community for 50 years, as in the United Kingdom. We also accept that there are some uncertainties at the European level about the rules concerning copyright and neighbouring rights applicable to satellite and cable broadcasting and we therefore support Community action about that.

We are, however, less persuaded that action is necessary in respect of the home copying of sound and audiovisual recordings, and shall therefore closely consider any Commission proposals about that. We look forward to the results of the studies which the Commission intends to carry out regarding moral rights, photocopying, resale rights and collective management of copyright and neighbouring rights.

The Second Document, 4174/91, is a proposal for a Council decision, which would require all member states to accede to and comply with the Berne copyright convention and the Rome convention on neighbouring rights. The Commission sees this as providing a basic level of harmonisation on which it will be possible to build in the future. I have to say, however, that the Government have some doubts about whether the proposal is necessary or desirable. The United Kingdom already complies with that decision. Indeed, all but two member states, Belgium and Ireland, have acceded to the latest text of the Berne convention, and those which have not belong to earlier texts of that convention. The degree of copyright harmonisation which would result from that decision is therefore very small. Admittedly, greater harmonisation of neighbouring rights would result, because five member states have yet to accede to the Rome convention. However, wide differences would remain because the Convention contains many provisions that are discretionary or may be reserved.

Furthermore, we and other member states have yet to be satisfied that the treaty base cited by the Commission is adequate for a decision which would affect member states' external treaty relations with third countries and would result in a substantial transfer of competence from the member states to the Community. We are continuing to examine those questions and await an opinion from the legal services of the Council and a response from the Commission to the questions that have been raised in discussions, in a Council working group.

Document 4175/91 is a proposed directive that would require member states to confer on authors, performers and producers of films and sound recordings an exclusive right to authorise or prohibit the commercial rental or the public lending of their works. Member states would, however, be allowed to have public lending right schemes, such as in the United Image Col. 5 Kingdom, in respect of book lending from public libraries, rather than exclusive rights, provided that authors are equitably remunerated. The directive also proposes exclusive rights for performers, producers of films and sound recordings and broadcasting organisations in respect of the reproduction and distribution of their works.

There is much in the draft directive that we support. The Government recently legislated, in the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988, to introduce an exclusive rental right in respect of films, sound recordings and computer programmes in recognition of the fact that rights owners need to be able to control that activity, partly to ensure proper remuneration and partly to help to minimise the unauthorised copying of borrowed material. We feel that such control should be available throughout the Community. Authors throughout the Community should be remunerated in respect of public library lending and that performers, broadcasters and record producers need Community-wide reproduction and distribution rights.

However, we question the need for the directive to create rental rights in respect of other works, such as works of art, because we doubt that problems in that area need action at Community level. Nor are we persuaded that it is necessary or desirable to create a separate rental right for each performer, author, composer or artist who contributes to the production of a record or film. That multiplicity of rights could result, for example, in video rental outlets requiring licences from several different categories of rights owners, each possibly subject to different licence conditions and methods of payment. We shall continue to make our doubts known to the Commission and other member states.

Questions of legal base and competence also arise in connection with the draft directive. In particular, it is not clear whether the cited treaty provisions justify the inclusion of Article 3 of the proposed directive, which is intended to assure authors and performing artists an adequate share of income from rental. The practical effect of such a measure is of some concern, because a potentially large number of authors and performers would apparently acquire the right to challenge contractual provisions that they had freely entered into. That could be particularly troublesome in the case of session musicians or film extras who are normally paid a single lump sum for their services at the time that a record or film is made and well before any rental income has accrued. Again, we are discussing our worries within the Council and are carrying out a thorough analysis of the legal basis for certain of the directive's provisions.

I recognise that these matters are somewhat complex, but I hope that I have given the Committee a clear idea of the content of the Commission's proposals and of the Government's general attitude towards them. I shall be happy to deal with any specific points that members of the Committee may raise.
