10.32 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Employment (Mr. Robert Jackson)
: May I start with some background information, so that we have the framework and context in our minds. In answer to the point raised by the hon. Member for Greenock and Port Glasgow (Dr Godman), in 1990, long before the social charter, the Government supported a Council resolution on the protection of the dignity of women and men at work. That was an Irish presidency initiative, not a Commission initiative. Among other things, it called on the European Commission to draw up a code of good conduct based on best practice in member states and to provide practical guidance to employers, trade unions and employees on how to combat and deal with sexual harassment at work.

Ms Quin
: The Minister spoke of a Council resolution of 1990, and said that it was supported long before the social charter. Surely that is a mistake. The social charter came into existence in 1989, or even earlier. Perhaps the Minister meant the social chapter?

Mr. Jackson
: The hon. Lady is quite right; she calls on her expertise as a former member of the 2 European Parliament. I cannot remember the exact year in which the social charter was adopted. It may have been 1990. However—social chapter or social charter—it is clear that the hon. Member for Greenock and Port Glasgow was wrong to suggest that the subject arose under the social charter. It arose from an Irish presidency initiative. Its legal basis has nothing to do with the social charter.

The code comes forward as an annex to a Commission recommendation, urging member states to promote awareness of the code generally, and especially to encourage its adoption in the public sector. Both the code and the recommendation, which was approved by the Commission on 27 November, urge recognition of the fact that sexual harassment is a form of sex discrimination and as such may contravene the equal treatment directive. That is another aspect of the legal base that I draw to the attention of the hon. Member for Greenock and Port Glasgow.

The code and the recommendation are not binding on member states. However, member states were asked to express their support for the code and recommendation at the Labour and Social Affairs Council on 3 December by signing a declaration. That declaration is designed to give political and moral weight to the recommendation and code. The Committee will be pleased to hear that at the Council, the Government maintained the United Kingdom's parliamentary scrutiny reserve on the declaration, pending today's debate.

Mr. Jackson
: I had been speaking for only a minute, so the point of the hon. Member for Greenock and Port Glasgow was very unfair. I say to the hon. Member for Stretford (Mr. Lloyd) that I am happy to answer his questions during my speech, although it might be more helpful if I were allowed to set out the background to the proposal and if the Committee were then to make use of the question and answer procedure. The answer to the hon. JPEG 3 Gentleman's question is that it is notoriously difficult to arrange a synchronisation of the timetable of the Council of Ministers and the timetable of the Committee. All that we can do, if the timetables cannot be made to coincide, is to maintain a parliamentary reserve, which is what we have done. That is a perfectly acceptable and straightforward way of dealing with the matter.

It is essentially the declaration to which the parliamentary scrutiny procedures apply and which therefore was the main focus of the Department's explanatory memorandum. Members of the Committee have a copy of that document. Of course, for the purposes of debate it is impossible to consider the declaration in isolation from the code and recommendation. I shall say more about those documents later.

I shall give a brief resumé of the 1990 resolution and each of the documents before us. I re-emphasise their non-binding nature. All the documents cover all conduct of a sexual nature that violates the dignity of women and men at work.

As I have said, we were pleased to support the resolution, because we condemn all forms of sexual harassment. The resolution calls on member states to develop, in particular, awareness campaigns, based on best practice, to counter sexual harassment and remind employers of their responsibility to seek to ensure that the workplace is free from sexual harassment and victimisation. So the emphasis is on increasing people's awareness. During negotiations on the resolution the United Kingdom made a number of suggestions for improvement, which were accepted, I am happy to say.

The code of practice was formally launched by the Commission on 27 November. It is based on best practice in member states and provides practical guidance to employers, trade unions and employees on how to deal with and, better still, to prevent sexual harassment at work. For the purposes of the code, sexual harassment is defined as

"unwanted conduct of a sexual nature, or other conduct based on sex affecting the dignity of women and men at work. This can include unwelcome physical, verbal or non-verbal conduct".

The code goes on to give advice on how best to pursue positive measures in that connection.

In the area of prevention, employers are asked to consider issuing policy statements about sexual harassment at work and to ensure that employees have the opportunity to complain.

The setting up of a proper complaints procedure is recommended, as are undertakings that allegations of sexual harassment will be dealt with speedily and confidentially, and that employees will be protected against victimisation or retaliation for bringing a complaint. The complaints procedure should specify that appropriate disciplinary measures will be taken against employees found guilty of sexual harassment. Of course—and this is crucial—the code recognises that informal solutions are to be preferred wherever possible, and it sets out a range of ideas about how that should be given effect.

Trade unions are recommended to formulate and issue policy statements to raise awareness and help 4 to create a climate in which sexual harassment is neither condoned nor ignored. They are urged to raise the matter with employers, as appropriate. The code asks unions to treat complaints sympathetically.

The code also places obligations on employees. They should ensure that standards of conduct for themselves and colleagues do not cause offence. They can make it clear that they find such behaviour unacceptable, and they can support colleagues who suffer such treatment and are considering making a complaint.

The Commission recommendation accompanying the code recommends that member states—that applies to the United Kingdom—take action to promote awareness of the kind of conduct specified as being unacceptable, and that we implement the code of practice in the public sector to serve as an example. Employers generally, and employee representatives, should develop measures to implement the code, as it applies to them.

The draft declaration registers the council's endorsement of the general objective of the Commission recommendation and, among other things, invites member states to develop coherent, integrated policies to prevent and combat sexual harassment, taking account of the Commission recommendation.

The non-binding nature of the documents means that there is no obligation on the United Kingdom to implement faithfully every suggestion contained in the code of practice. The declaration reflects our view that policies in such an area are for development and implementation by member states acting individually. This is the principle of subsidiarity. This is an eminently sensible and practical way to proceed, which respects the principle of diversity of approach among member states. This brings me to the legal position. Reference has been made to the possibility that sexual harassment may be contrary to the equal treatment directive. What must also be said is that only in the United Kingdom and the Republic of Ireland is there clear judicial acceptance in our national law that proven sexual harassment may in certain circumstances be unlawful sex discrimination. The Commission themselves has acknowledged this.

Under our sex discrimination legislation not only are employers responsible for their own conduct but they are also liable for any discriminatory acts done by their employees in the course of their employment, unless the employer can show that he took such steps as were reasonably practicable to prevent them. This can act as a powerful impetus to employers to take sexual harassment seriously. Equally importantly, the law also protects complainants and anyone else giving evidence in a case against any subsequent victimisation by their employer.

There is a growing body of case law in the United Kingdom to support the view that sexual harassment constitutes sex discrimination under the Sex Discrimination Act, 1975. What has emerged from this is that this country, along with Ireland, is in the vanguard in the Community in providing a remedy at civil law for people who wish to bring complaints JPEG 5 of sexual harassment under the sex discrimination legislation.

In general terms the various initiatives call on member states to raise awareness of the problem of sexual harassment. This is a sensible step with which we agree. I believe that there is already a high level of awareness, especially in the wake of recent, highly publicised examples, particularly in the United States. We need to see that this is translated into a spreading of good practice—of which I am pleased to say there are some notable examples among leading United Kingdom employers, including the Civil Service, British Rail, British Telecom, the high street banks, ICI, Shell, ESSO, to name but some. These organisations already have well-developed arrangements to deal with complaints of sexual harassment from members of staff. I know from first hand that the civil service practice is well in line with the measures advocated in the codes. So we do have excellent models of good practice to commend to employers.

Of course, we must be realistic about how much we ask businesses to do when it comes to devising and implementing policies, and we must bear in mind that smaller employers do not have the same administrative back-up as the pioneering organisations I have just mentioned. The Commission's code certainly recognises this. On the other hand, this kind of discrimination is abhorrent and all reasonable steps need to be taken to deal with it, where it exists.

Mr. Jackson

: The hon. Member for Gateshead, East (Ms Quin) is an expert in such matters. She tempts me into discussing subsidiarity. We shall probably define that concept as we go along. No one has promulgated an authoritative definition. I do not know whether anyone is capable of doing that, except perhaps His Holiness the Pope. The phrase originated in the discussions about the internal constitutional structure of the Roman Catholic Church. The Roman Catholic Church discussed whether decisions should be taken at papal, episcopal or a more local level. That is the historical background to the phrase. Nobody has defined subsidiarity exactly. For the purposes of the Community it governs whether decisions should be taken at Community level or by the member state. I agree with the hon. Therefore, it shall act within its competence—that is a distinct concept. The article continues:

"In the areas which do not fall within its exclusive jurisdiction, the Community shall take action, in accordance with the principle of subsidiarity, only if and insofar as the objectives of the proposed action cannot be sufficiently achieved by the Member States and can therefore, by reason of the scale or effects of proposed action, be better achieved by the Community.


Any action by the Community shall not go beyond what is necessary to achieve the objectives of this Treaty."
