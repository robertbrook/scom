The December council concluded that, in addition to national programmes, some measures need to be agreed at Community level. These include a strengthening, where necessary, of Community programmes. Among these are SAVE, a programme to promote energy efficiency improvements, THERMIE, a demonstrative programme to encourage the adoption of new and better energy technologies and JOULE, a research and development programme covering energy-saving technologies and energy options that are economically viable and environmentally safer.

The council also referred to the development of new and renewable energy sources, which would include the proposed ALTENER programme on renewable energy. Such measures are an integral part of a co-ordinated Community-wide approach to tackle CO
2
emissions.

The joint council concluded that these specific measures and the national programmes were unlikely to be sufficient in themselves to stabilise Community CO
2
emissions and that higher energy pricing through fiscal instruments was likely to be needed. This fits with the United Kingdom Government's recognition in the environment White Paper that, in the long term, meeting the United Kingdom's conditional target will inevitably require increases in the relative price of energy and fuel, perhaps by taxation. A Community-wide carbon/energy tax, as suggested by the Commission, could therefore have an important role to play in limiting emissions.

The introduction of a carbon/energy tax raises a wide range of complex issues which need to be considered, including the economic and social consequences of such a tax, its implications for energy policy, the most appropriate base for the tax and how it is to be administered. The communication does not have much to say on these issues. The December council recognised the need for further analysis of the issue. This approach was endorsed by ECOFIN. The Commission is now to put forward formal proposals for a Community strategy by May, taking account of the further work required.

The Commission recognised that the unilateral imposition of a carbon/energy tax by the Community could damage the competitiveness of energy intensive industries engaged in international trade. It suggested that such effects might be alleviated through a system of exemptions. However, we do not believe that this idea is sensible. It carries the risk of creating a set of undesirable distortions and placing the burden of a tax on to other sectors of the economy.

4 The United Kingdom continues to believe that it would be sensible to implement a carbon/energy tax in the EC only if other countries took similar action. Concerted international action is the best way to proceed to limit global emissions—indeed, action by the Community alone might deliver no significant reduction in emission in the world as a whole.

That is where the current negotiations on a framework convention on climate change are so important. These were set in motion in December 1990, when the United Nations established an Inter-Governmental Negotiating Committee to draw up a convention in time for signature during UNCED in June. The fifth meeting of the negotiating committee is currently taking place in New York and the United Kingdom is playing a leading role in the negotiations. There is still some way to go, but we are still hopeful of a successful convention, containing real commitments, being ready for June. That will form a sound basis for continuing efforts to mitigate the threat of climate change in the year a head.
