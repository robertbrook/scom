Mr. Curry
: I shall explain the policy background to the report, comment on its conclusions and explain the key issues for the United Kingdom.

The sugar regime is not time limited, nor are the 100 or so Council and Commission regulations that derive from it. Quotas are set for five years and it is usual for the regime to be reviewed at the end of each five-year period.

In 1990 the existing regime was rolled forward for a further two years to June 1993 so that the outcome of the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, which was expected imminently, could be taken into account. A full review of the regime is due to be concluded under the United Kingdom presidency later this year and implemented from 1 July next year. We do not yet have any Commission proposals. The review will deal with beet sugar, and cane sugar, for which arrangements must be changed following expiry of the Portuguese accession period on 31 December. It is worth emphasising that the entitlement to import 1·3 million tonnes of sugar from the African, Caribbean and Pacific countries is not part of the renegotiations. That is consolidated in European legislation and does not have to be the subject of new negotiations.

The sugar regime was established in 1968 and was directed at the interests of EC beet growers, although it covered also sugar growing in French overseas terrritories. Cane sugar from the French overseas teritories and overseas departments is consolidated within the EC quota and is treated as EC sugar. The quota system enabled high guaranteed prices to be offered to growers while limiting the amount of sugar that would benefit from it. Market prices to consumers rose to or above the high support price level.

2 Countries receive an A quota, which broadly represents their consumption and a B quota, which covers the remainder of their production. The cost of exporting quantities of sugar in excess of EC requirements is met by a levy on growers and processors, which is shared in the ratio of 60 to 40 of 2 per cent. on A quota sugar and up to 39·5 per cent. on B quota sugar. The national quotas have increased in successive reviews so that they now generate a considerable export surplus. Growers may grow sugar for export, which is C sugar. That does not fall within the quota and it is without a price guarantee. Price guarantees are significant enough on A and B sugar to enable C sugar to be grown profitably on their back.

On the whole, quotas have encouraged over-production, especially in the most efficient areas of France and Germany, and they have tended to maintain production where it would not otherwise be profitable. That applies notably in Italy. The Court of Auditors notes that the Community is approximately 150 per cent. self sufficient in sugar, so it is clear that the quota is too large. Reform of the common agricultural policy and GATT will generate pressure for cuts to be made.

The basic institutional price in the sector is for white sugar—the processed product—and not for the agricultural product, as in other regimes. All other prices are derived from the intervention price for white sugar.

We have argued for many years that the institutional price for sugar is too high. We have supported the Commission's attempts to reduce it, which largely have been unsuccessful because of resistance to cutting the price of sugar. There is no stabiliser other than the quota, and support prices have been cut once only—by 2 per cent.—whereas in most of the rest of the arable sector prices have fallen by approximately 20 per cent. In comparative terms, sugar has been favoured.

The argument against that is that the cost is budgetarily neutral. It is true that the levy system keeps the bulk of the cost out of the budget as such, but there is a charge upon consumers by a different mechanism. The European Agricultural Guidance and Guarantee Fund—FEOGA, the Community budget—pays only for exporting the tonnage that is equivalent to ACP imports and part of the subsidy on sugar that is devoted to the chemical industry.

When the United Kingdom entered the Community in 1973, we brought out a commitment to the old Commonwealth sugar-exporting countries and agreed the sugar protocol. It is without a time limit and it provides for the exporting countries to send 1·3 million tonnes of sugar to the Community at prices guaranteed within the range of Community prices, which, practically, means raw sugar intervention price. It has been important for those countries and for the United Kingdom as it has enabled us to maintain a port refinery industry that supplies half our needs. Eighty per cent. of this sugar comes to the United Kingdom for refining, and it provides significant employment.

It is important that the sugar matches EEC prices: the Council of Ministers has been unanimous about this. If prices diverge, the Community cane refineries in France and Portugal, and in the United Kingdom—Tate and Lyle—will find their processing margins squeezed, and the refining of cane sugar could cease to be viable.

The review must tackle events in Portugal. At present, Portugal imports some of its sugar from four African countries; the rest is imported at the world price under a JPEG 3 preferential regime. We should like the security of those supplies, and supplies to the United Kingdom, to be assured more effectively. When some ACP countries failed to deliver their full supplies in the past, the United Kingdom felt the pinch. We did not have the required amounts for our refining capacity, which resulted in under-utilisation.

The United Kingdom is alone in having equally important beet and cane sectors, and we have tried to maintain a balance between them. Although the Community support system favours beet interests, the Council recognises the need to redress the balance between beet and cane-refining margins; adjustment aids have been provided for the purpose.

The GATT disciplines—we hope that we are in the final stages of an agreement—will apply to all sectors, including sugar. Agriculture Ministers spent six hours around the lunch table on Monday with trade Ministers trying to thrash out the issues, and the Community has put its proposals on the table. We shall be part of the negotiations. The Dunkel text had problems that were common to sugar and other sectors because of an imbalance between numbers, but we hope that this can be resolved.

Sugar is not part of the CAP reform proposals, but the Commission will have to introduce proposals for review. These are likely to be part of the United Kingdom's task during its presidency.

Mr. Curry
: I shall summarise briefly the report of the Court of Auditors. It states that quotas are too large and that they lead to an unacceptably high surplus to be exported. We agree. It says that prices are too high, which leads to distortion of competition with competing sweeteners and an unnecessarily high cost to consumers. We agree. The report states also that national aids ought to be removed. We agree. It says that the regime is completely insulated from world or EC market pressures, which must also be true. It states that too much non-quota C sugar is produced.

We are thoroughly sympathetic to the comments of the Court of Auditors on the beet sector, but we do not agree with its remarks about the cane sector, where it has the wrong end of the stick. We simply do not accept the cop out that cane sugar should be removed from the regime and that we should pay a redundancy benefit to the ACP countries to put that sugar on the world market instead of sending it to the European market. That is an entitlement: it is part of the trading benefits that those countries receive, and it makes no sense to transfer it to the budget. We have fought that proposal consistently—I remember fighting it years ago in the European Parliament. Most people wanted to get rid of the problem by switching it to a different part of the budget and categorising it as something else. That does not make the slightest sense. We are hostile to that part of the report. It is unhelpful. It is not in the interests of the growers and suppliers. Nor is it in the interests of refiners in the United Kingdom and other countries. We dissent markedly from that part of the report.

I apologise to the Committee for a long summing up. Given the complexity of the regime, I hope the Committee found it helpful.
