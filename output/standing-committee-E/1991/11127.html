Child Care

10.30 am

The Minister for Health (Mrs. Virginia Bottomley)
: I welcome this occasion to explain in more detail why the Government feel able to support the recommendation on childcare in principle. The recommendation is not legally binding. In negotiations in Brussels, we are seeking to ensure that the terms are not such that the Government are recommended to introduce legislation. This is a statement of the European Community's policy objectives in this area.

The recommendation is about ways of helping women with dependent children to take part in the labour market. That must always be a matter of individual choice for parents. For many employers, there are sound business reasons why it is sensible to consider how best to develop family-friendly personnel policies so that they recruit and retain good quality staff.

We are pleased that the recommendation recognises that there are several ways of helping parents reconcile work and family responsibilities. We also know that in the United Kingdom we have already done much on that front and we have something positive to contribute to the EC about good practice. For example in the public sector—the civil service and the national health service—we have a major programme designed to help the work force combine work and family responsibilities. We know that it makes sense to go for packages of arrangements. It is not simply a question of setting up a work place nursery and an employer then describing himself as family-friendly. It is a far more complex and subtle process.

We welcome the general approach in the recommendation, but we have one major reservation which applies to three of the Articles—the wording of the introductory sentences for articles 3, 4, and 5. The terms seem to imply that the Government should introduce legislation. We could not support that. I am happy to say that in negotiations in Brussels, other member states shared our view that the recommendation should not be worded in that way. So we may now able to negotiate revised wording that we can accept.

The four elements covered in the recommendation are child care, leave arrangements for employed parents, the organisation and structure of the work place, and sharing family responsibilities between men and women, Article 3, which deals with child care, seems generally to be on the same lines as Government policy. It talks about flexibility, diversity, parental and local community involvement as well as the quality of services. We have two reservations, however.

2 Paragraph (a), uses the words, "this requires", before listing the main issues about patterns and access to child care. "This requires" is inappropriate language for a recommendation. Secondly, paragraph (c) recommends that public funding should make an essential contribution to child care. It is not for the EC to say anything about how we should spend taxpayers' money or about our priorities for public expenditure. We made those points during the negotiations and they were sympathetically received.

Article 4, which is on parental leave, poses problems, as its wording strongly implies that a statutory minimum of parental leave is required. Parental leave arrangements should remain a matter for employers and employees to decide in the light of their individual circumstances. A wide range of practices exists in this country, which could be jeopardised by the imposition of one set of arrangements by law.

However, the Government play an important role in that area. First, we set an example of good practice in the public sector: both the civil service and the NHS have negotiated flexible leave arrangements and career break schemes. Secondly, we emphasise to employers that generous and flexible parental leave will enable them to recruit and retain able staff, and we publicise good practice in the private sector. We believe that that is the right way forward, and we shall be seeking changes to article 4 to make it compatible with our approach.

Article 5 deals with the work place. It recommends member states to

"encourage both sides of industry to promote action, inter alia, within the framework of collective agreements, to make the environment, structure and organisation of individual workplaces supportive of the needs of all working parents".

 Examples of good practice that are cited in the Commission's explanatory memorandum are the hours at which meetings are held to avoid incompatibility with the need to collect children from creches, the importance of giving adequate notice of the need to travel and the need for flexible working hours.

The Government take every opportunity to encourage employers to adopt a flexible approach to working arrangements. In July 1991 the Secretary of State for Employment published a good practice guide, "The Best of Both Worlds", illustrating the benefits of a variety of flexible arrangements for employers and employees. The Government, therefore, support the broad thrust of article 5. We accept that we have a role to play in raising awareness but, at the same time, believe that it must be for both sides of industry to agree such matters between them.

We are opposed to paragraph (b) in the article, which singles out people working in child care services as requiring special consideration. It is unacceptable for the EC in a recommendation to do that. We know that research findings suggest that poor terms and conditions of employment affect the quality of child care services. It is, therefore, reasonable for providers and those responsible for regulating private and voluntary provision to pay attention to this issue. The Government should not be involved in any way, except to JPEG 3 establish the framework for the quality of child care, which has to be registered under the Children Act 1989. Volume 2 of the guidance spells that out in some detail.

Article 6 recommends member states to stimulate initiatives to encourage men to play a greater part in family life. That causes us some anxiety. It is highly intrusive for Government to intervene in parents' arrangements for reconciling work and family responsibilities. It is also not practicable. Nevertheless, no one would disagree with the general principle of more equal sharing of domestic and family responsibilities between men and women. Negotiations in Brussels suggest that other member states recognise the ethical and practical difficulties that the article poses. We therefore hope to resolve the problem in a way that we can accept.

In conclusion we want to be able to sign the recommendation, but that is provided that we negotiate changes to the text which meet our anxieties. It deals with a matter about which the Government have been active. We recognise the general need for all who are involved—employers in the statutory, private and voluntary sectors, employees, parents and statutory authorities concerned about the quality of child care and services—to work together to ensure good practice and sensible development.
