Tobacco Products

10.31 am

The Parliamentry Under-Secretary of State for Health (Mr. Stephen Dorrell)
: Sir Michael, it may help the Committee if I make a brief statement setting out the Government's view on the draft directive.

The Government have consistently opposed the proposition that it is a legitimate exercise of the Community's powers for it to impose a legal requirement on all member states to ban tobacco advertising. The Commission has introduced the draft directive under article 100A of the Treaty of Rome. That article is concerned with the completion of the internal market. Doubts have been expressed on whether the draft directive is even a legal use of powers, although I am not qualified to express an opinion on that. However, I consider it an unnecessary use of the power vested in the Community under article 100A because I do not accept that uniform conditions on tobacco advertising are a necessary precondition to the completion of the internal market. As the purpose of article 100A is to give the Community the power necessary to create a proper and competitive internal market throughout the EC, I do not consider it a proper use of the powers under the article to seek to achieve a different objective. The motivation of the draft directive is not the easing of non-tariff barriers to trade, or the creation of a more competitive market in tobacco products.

Instead, it is health oriented, because it concerns the reduction of tobacco consumption and, consequently, the reduction of harm to health caused by the consumption of tobacco.

Although I cannot express a view on whether the draft directive is a legal use of powers under article 100A, I believe that the motivation of the Community in the exercise of this power is nothing to do with free trade in tobacco products or with the creation of a single internal market in advertising services. It is health oriented and therefore not a proper use of the power vested in the Community under article 100A, even if, at the extreme, it is a legal use.

: I suspect that I was at fault, Sir Michael, in giving way to my hon. Friend the Member for Stafford (Mr. Cash), but I always find an intervention from him irresistible. I am aware of the memorandum to which he referred and of the legal opinion that supports the opposite argument. I am seeking to avoid expressing an opinion on that subject. Whichever side of the argument is supported by the legalities, I believe that the proposal is not a proper use of the Community's powers, because its motivation is different from what was envisaged when the original power was granted under the Single European Act.

I have argued that the proposal has been introduced to achieve a public health objective. The Committee must therefore examine the merits of the argument that the measure is necessary to achieve the very desirable objective of reducing tobacco consumption. That at least is not in dispute among parties interested in the subject. The Government recognise and warmly embrace and endorse the desirability of further reducing tobacco consumption both in this country and throughout the Community. Tobacco is a major killer. JPEG 3 It is responsibly for 80 per cent of the 41,000 deaths annually from lung cancer. We believe that it is responsible for 20 per cent of the 187,000 deaths from heart disease each year and for 80 per cent of the 31,000 deaths each year from bronchitis and other diseases that cause obstruction of the air ways. There is also a serious health hazard if tobacco is consumed by a pregnant woman. There is no argument about the desirability of substantial further reduction in tobacco consumption.

The Committee must consider whether a ban on tobacco advertising is the most effective means of achieving a further reduction in tobacco consumption and, on that issue, the evidence is equivocal. The most compelling evidence that I have seen in my 18 months as a Minister in the Department of Health concerns the member states of the European Community. It is odd that people should consider countries outside the Community in assessing the effect of a proposal that will apply in the Community. Compared with all but two of the other member states, this country has an extremely good record in reducing tobacco consumption. It fell by 29 per cent in the United Kingdom in the 12 years from 1975 to 1987. That is the third largest reduction in tobacco consumption of any member state. The greatest reduction—41 per cent—was achieved by the Dutch, and the Irish achieved a 31 per cent reduction. It should be of at least passing interest to the Committee that we and the Dutch followed precisely the same policy on tobacco advertising. We feel that the best way to minimise the effect of tobacco advertising and to achieve the objective of reducing total tobacco consumption is by voluntary agreement with the industry. The British, Dutch and Danish Governments pursue the same policy in this respect; that is why those three Governments and the German Government are sustaining a blocking minority on the proposal in the Council of Ministers.

The Government's view is that our voluntary approach since 1979, inherited from our predecessors, is working and is delivering the shared objective of a reduction in tobacco consumption. It is not our only step in achieving the important objective set out in the Green Paper "The Health of the Nation". We agreed, in the context of the European legislative process, a rota of some of the toughest health warnings to be printed on cigarette packets and tobacco packaging of any member state. There was a significant increase in taxation on tobacco products in the last Budget. There is little dispute about the effect of price on tobacco consumption.

We argued vehemently in the Council of Ministers, not merely as a debating point, that it is absurd for the Community to hold itself up as an institution interested in reducing tobacco consumption while it hands out subsidies for tobacco production under the common agricultural policy. The Government's position is that this proposal has been introduced under an inappropriate Community power under article 100A. When the substance of the case in support of the proposal is analysed, it is revealed to be a weak proposal which is illegitimate under article 100A.
