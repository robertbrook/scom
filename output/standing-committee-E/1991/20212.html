10.31 am

Mr. Patrick McLoughlin (The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Transport)
: I understand that I have to give a brief outline of the documents. If hon. Members have looked through the papers, they hardly need me to tell them that airport slot allocation is a complex subject. Members of the Committee will appreciate that it is not easy to condense into a few minutes the work that the Department and its consultants have carried out over many months. But I shall attempt to give an overview of the issue and to explain how the Government have arrived at the position outlined in the two memoranda that hon. Members have received.

The Government have always fully supported the principle of liberalising air transport in Europe, and the United Kingdom has worked hard to push through change. With the third liberalisation package under discussion, we are now within reach of a Community single market in aviation, which will benefit users and provide exciting opportunities for United Kingdom airlines.

Like the Commission, the Government regard slot allocation as an important element in the structure of the industry. Some two years ago we decided to examine alternatives to the present system, because we were concerned that it might be a barrier for carriers wishing to mount competitive services. The benefits of lifting restrictions on airlines could be at risk if they found themselves unable to get into airports to offer services.

At the same time, we knew the strengths of the International Air Transport Association system. Worldwide civil aviation scheduling is a complex operation and must, in the end, rest with experienced professionals. Remember that aircraft take off from Heathrow, during operating hours, at an average rate of more than one every two minutes and Heathrow is part of a worldwide network of busy airports. The last thing that the Government want is to become involved in detailed scheduling. The present system has evolved 2 over many years. It is certainly not what one would have designed from scratch, but it works. The procedures enable carriers to put together scheduling networks with the minimum bureaucracy and the maximum flexibility. The airlines have an incentive to sort out their differences in a spirit of compromise and without needing outside agencies. Our consultants, SD-Scicon, found that the existing system worked a lot better than they had expected.

As the Committee knows, we appointed SD-Scicon to examine the scope for change, including moving towards a market-led system of slot allocation. The study was the first of its kind to analyse slot allocation from first principles and attracted a good deal of interest both here and abroad. Though Scicon did not come up with workable options, its study focused minds on how the present system works and the implications of change.

Scicon examined two options in depth, one a modified version of the existing system, the other a more radical scheme involving the auctioning of slot leases. Both options turned out to be complicated, putting at risk the flexibility of the IATA system. Both would have involved excessive intervention by Governments, which we want to avoid, not only because it would go against the general thrust of our civil aviation policy, but because of the danger of abuse elsewhere in the world, with bad results for United Kingdom airlines.

Scicon's work also revealed how difficult it would be to introduce money into the allocation of slots, for example through auctions. Auctioning slots would almost certainly lead to allegations of a breach of international agreements on airport charging. We have the current major arbitration with the United States of America over the legality of charges at Heathrow as a warning of that. Furthermore, the consultants could not find acceptable solutions to the problems of grouping slots for auction.

The Secretary of State was clear that Scicon had not come up with workable proposals; indeed its work confirmed how difficult it would be to devise arrangements preferable to the existing procedures.

Meanwhile, the IATA procedures have got better. About the time we commissioned our consultants, IATA introduced into its guidelines some priority for new entrant airlines to obtain slots. IATA is now looking at slot wastage. There is evidence that even at the most congested airports a certain number of slots which the holding airline decides not to use are either returned to the pool later than they might be or are not used at all. IATA is considering ways of amending its procedures so that such slots are reallocated in good time to other carriers. The Government welcome that development.

The change in the London area traffic distribution rules in early 1991 provided an opportunity for the IATA system to prove its adaptability. At a stroke, airlines that had been barred from Heathrow were permitted to apply for slots. Not only did this increase demand for slots at the busiest airport in Europe, it also happened after the scheduling conference for the summer 1991 season had taken place. Yet the system coped. Eighteen airlines previously barred from JPEG 3 Heathrow are now operating there. They may not all have slots at the times that they wanted, but they have a foothold at the airport, which they can improve as time goes on.

Those were the circumstances in which the Secretary of State considered the Government's approach to slot allocation. He was not convinced that a better alternative to the existing arrangements had come forward. There was no evidence that the schemes proposed would lead to user benefits. By reducing the certainty with which airlines could plan their investment and scheduling, and by increasing bureaucracy, they were likely to harm the long-term interests of the industry and of users. United Kingdom airlines based at Heathrow would be affected disproportionately, with little immediate prospect of any advantages, either to users at Heathrow or to the airlines themselves at other airports.

The existing system continues to evolve. The Government believe that further improvements are possible and desirable, and will maintain the pressure on the industry to make changes. We will continue to keep the situation under review, but for the present, we believe it is good common sense to look for gradual change to the existing procedures, and the Government are encouraged that the Transport Select Committee came to similar conclusions in its recent report.

Mr. McLoughlin
: I beg to move,

That the Committee takes note of European Community Document No. 4496/91 and the Supplementary Explanatory Memorandum submitted by the Department of Transport on 3 February 1992 relating to the allocation of slots at Community airports; notes that the principle of efficient, neutral and transparent airport coordination is in line with the Government's aims; notes the strengths of the existing worldwide system of slot allocation; and endorses the Governments' intention to respond to the draft Regulation on the premise that any change to the system should benefit users without creating practical difficulties for the civil aviation industry, and without substantially extending the role of governments in slot allocation.

 The Commission has produced a draft regulation on slot allocation at Community airports. The contents of the proposal are summarised in the Department's explanatory memorandum, produced this time last year. We are waiting for an amended draft from the Commission, in the light of the European Parliament's opinion, given in December. The Department's recent supplementary explanatory memorandum summarises the UK industry's reactions to the proposals.

I should say at once that the Government are not convinced that the Community needs to take action on slots. As I explained earlier, we are looking for gradual JPEG 11 change to the existing system, and this can occur regardless of Community action. We know that other member states are not keen on making substantial changes by a Community regulation. But we have to face the possibility that momentum for change may build up, perhaps because of the threat of action from the competition directorate, when its current block exemption expires at the end of this year. The competition directorate has the power to include slot allocation rules in its next block exemption without reference to the Council of Ministers. If Community provisions on slot allocation procedures are to be made, the Government would prefer to see them in a regulation adopted by the Council of Ministers.

There are a number of uncertainties about the progress of the proposals, which makes it difficult to be firm at this stage about the Government's tactics. The work that we and our consultants have done over the past two years has put us in a strong position to influence the debate, but we need to keep our negotiating options open.

We cannot be sure how energetically the current Presidency will pursue the matter. The Portuguese are reported to want to conclude it by the end of June, but given the lack of detailed debate so far and the worries of member states about the contents of the regulation it is difficult to believe that they can meet that timetable. Beyond June, the UK's Presidency will condition our approach to the debate. Progress on the liberalisation package will also affect our negotiating position.

The proposals are something of a mixed bag. A number of provisions within the regulation aim to improve the quality and openness of the scheduling process, which the Government strongly endorse. Coordination of congested airports in the UK is carried out in an independent and efficient manner, and these proposals would bring practice in other countries more into line with United Kingdom standards. That could benefit the industry and users generally, and the UK airlines in particular.

We are concerned that the present proposals imply more Government involvement than we have in the UK, and more than is consistent with the general move in Europe towards less state intervention in aviation. We need to consider whether the regulation offers the best way of achieving some of the objectives.

The most controversial elements in the draft regulation are those on forced surrender of historic slots, in certain circumstances, on a route-specific basis. That would represent a substantial departure from past practice, and—not surprisingly—is strongly opposed by the industry.

Question put and agreed to.

Resolved.

That the Committee takes note of European Community Document No. 4496/91 and the Supplementary Explanatory Memorandum submitted by the Department of Transport on 3 February 1992 relating to the allocation of slots at Community airports; notes that the principle of efficient, neutral and transparent airport coordination is in line with the Government's aims; notes the strengths of the existing worldwide system of slot allocation; and endorses the Governments intention to respond to the draft Regulation on the premise that any change to the system should benefit users without creating practical difficulties for the civil aviation industry, and without substantially extending the role of governments in slot allocation.
