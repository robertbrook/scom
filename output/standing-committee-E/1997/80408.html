debates
legislation Convergence of the Telecommunications, Media and Information Technology Sectors

[Relevant document: European Community Document No. 12793/97, a Commission Communication on the information society and the protection of human dignity.]

10.30 am

The Minister for Small Firms, Trade and Industry (Mrs. Barbara Roche): I welcome the opportunity to discuss the Commission's Green Paper on convergence. Unfortunately, my hon. Friend the Minister for Arts, who together with me signed the explanatory memorandum, cannot be with us today—he and my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Culture, Media and Sport are hosting the United Kingdom presidency's European Union audiovisual conference in Birmingham.

As my hon. Friend the Minister for Arts and I stated in our explanatory memorandum, the Green Paper aims to start a debate on issues raised by convergence. Chapter I of the Green Paper considers the scope of convergence; chapter II deals with its impact on telecommunications media and information technology sectors and its social and economic context. Chapter III examines existing and possible barriers to convergence, chapter IV deals with regulatory implications of convergence and chapter V considers principles and options for future regulation.

The Green Paper poses a series of questions. My hon. Friend the Minister for Arts and I have provided the Committee with a draft outline of the Government's reponse. The Commission's paper covers some important issues that are also being considered in the United Kingdom—including of course, by the Select Committee on Culture, Media and Sport—so this debate is timely. The Commission's consultation period extends until the end of April; the Government are reflecting on our reply to the Commission on questions raised by the Green Paper and we will submit a response by the end of the month. As I mentioned, I have provided the Committee with the most up-to-date draft of our response—I emphasise that it is a draft—and we will ensure that Parliament receives a copy of the final version. The timing of this debate allows us to take the Committee's views into account when drafting the final version.

In our response, we will also take into account the views of industry, users, consumers and other interest groups—we asked for their views by the end of March. Several broad themes emerged in the responses that we received: regulation should be consistent, transparent and fair; we must avoid dividing society into the Col. 2 information rich and the information poor; regulatory bodies should be based at national level, but there should be strong co-operation between regulators in different member states; and public service broadcasting is important.

Understandably, views on future regulatory models differed. Several respondents commented that it is important to keep an open mind, as the converged environment is still developing. None the less, broad support emerged for an evolutionary approach that does not rush into a totally new framework but builds on existing structures.

Views also differed on the question of whether regulation should be horizontal or vertical. In the former, content is regulated separately from the means of delivery; the latter applies different rules to content delivered by telephone and by broadcast media, for example. Several respondents thought that broadcasting—public service broadcasting in particular—should be a special case. Others were keen that sector-specific regulations should not stifle the growth of new information society services.

During the coming weeks, we will be revising the draft outline in the light of the comments already received—and of course the comments of the Committee.

Under the United Kingdom presidency, the Green Paper has been discussed at the Telecoms Council meeting that I chaired on 25 February and at an informal seminar for Ministers responsible for culture and audiovisual technology, which my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State and my hon. Friend the Minister for Arts hosted in Macclesfield from 15 to 17 March. Public consultation on the Green Paper continues until the end of April. The Commission then intends to produce a report on the outcome of that consultation by the summer break. It has said that it is likely to prepare what it calls a convergence action plan by the end of the year. On that basis, proposals for Community legislation, if there are any, will not be drawn up before next year.

The Select Committee on European Legislation suggested that we include in this debate the Commission's communication on the response to the Green Paper on human dignity and the protection of minors in audiovisual and information services, including a proposal for a Council recommendation.

For the new audiovisual services to reach their full potential, there must be a climate of confidence. That will be achieved only if public interests such as human dignity and the protection of minors can be secured. The draft Council recommendation will foster international co-operation and promote a self-regulatory framework, which should help develop that confidence. I hope that it will be adopted by the Council of Ministers at the Audiovisual and Culture Council on 28 May.

As I have already said, major cultural issues are clearly linked to the convergence debate. Indeed, the Select Committee on Culture, Media and Sport considered those matters last week. However, I am obviously interested to hear the Committee's views, and I will happily pass those on to my hon. Friend.

The Chairman: Thank you, Minister. The Committee has until 11.30 am at the latest to question the Minister. I ask hon. Members to keep their questions brief and to ask them one at a time. There is ample opportunity for all hon. Members to be called.

Mr. Boswell: In the explanatory memorandum, Ministers place a heavy and entirely welcome emphasis on subsidiarity. Does the Minister see any advantage in having a co-ordinated European Union approach, rather than allowing the bilateral evolution of UK and other national policies or taking a more global approach? What is the particular added value in the European dimension?

Mrs. Roche: The hon. Member for Daventry (Mr. Boswell) raises some important points. On some issues, an EU approach is correct—a good example is telecommunications. Our presidency has coincided with the single market in telecoms, which came about on 1 January. Indeed, what the EU has achieved in that respect—a driving forward of the agenda—is widely appreciated because broadcasting and telecoms are important.

The hon. Gentleman rightly asked what was important at the national level. Matters such as the granting of licences are clearly for national jurisdiction, as is detailed enforcement. However, some European Union recommendations may be helpful, as, for instance, was the EU recommendation on harmful material on the internet.

The hon. Gentleman also asked what global considerations were important. Copyright is a good example, as it is a matter to be considered world wide, not only by the UK or the EU. Another subject for global consideration is electronic commerce, and a conference in Ottawa in October is concentrating on that enormously important subject.

Mr. Hopkins: I welcome my hon. Friend's opening statement, particularly the Government's emphasis on subsidiarity and on taking broadcasting separately. Where convergence is seen to be advantageous, will the Government seek to ensure that best practice is pursued rather than having to make unacceptable compromises; and, if best practice is UK practice, will the Government press the UK's case hard in discussions with other member states?

Mrs. Roche: My hon. Friend makes an important point. The audiovisual and culture council noted that cultural diversity is strong and that people clearly want to enhance it. However, this is an odd area for politicians because technology is driving the debate forward at an enormous pace. We have yet to see how individuals, communities and countries respond to that. My hon. Friend's remarks about best practice are important. We shall endeavour to ensure that those remarks are kept on board.

Mr. Boswell: The Minister has very helpfully tabled the draft United Kingdom response to the Green Paper and, in her helpful introductory remarks, given us an indication of the flavour of responses. Given that the documents were prepared a week or two ago, can Col. 4 she update us on any new responses or on any additional thoughts that she might have had? Can she say more about the definition of "information rich" and "information poor"? I sought to obtain from her colleague, the Minister for Science, Energy and Industry, similar definitions of fuel poverty, and it would be helpful to know what she has in mind.

Mrs. Roche: On the hon. Gentleman's first question, we are still in the response period and we are analysing the responses that have come in, which are in line with the draft recommendations. The recommendations are in outline, almost note, form, but I was anxious to give the Committee as much information as I could. Some of our first thoughts touch on my answer to the previous question in that convergence is more a technical than a market phenomenon. We must have a coherent response. We shall seek to develop the principles of coherency in economic regulation and of consistency in content regulation. I need not remind the Committee that this is a complex and evolving matter.

The hon. Gentleman's second question deals with an important point about perhaps one of the greatest challenges for all of us. We are in the middle of an information revolution that will affect not only our economic life—electronic commerce will alter how we do business—but our social and cultural life. Indeed, it will affect everything that we do. Therefore, it is important that we develop existing initiatives so that everyone is included in the information society. We cannot truly have an information society until everyone is included.

I shall do my best to give a definition, but this is a difficult matter. As the hon. Gentleman will know—he had the pleasure of being a schools Minister—it is important that everyone starts IT training at an early stage, and we are trying to ensure that that happens. It is also important that teachers keep themselves updated. That is why my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Education and Employment has proposed the national grid for learning, which involves not only school equipment, but the role of teachers and keeping them constantly updated.

We have other proposals, such as the Department of Trade and Industry's "IT for all" initiative, which was started under the previous Administration. We have continued to support that initiative indeed, we have provided extra funding for it in the past financial year. Under that initiative, we seek to provide hands-on experience for people who are perhaps apprehensive about new technology or find it a little frightening. It is difficult to give the hon. Gentleman an exact definition, but I have mentioned some of the steps that the Government are taking.

Mr. Timms: My hon. Friend the Minister has mentioned a couple of times the problem of harmful material on the internet and the draft Council recommendation on the protection of minors and human dignity in audiovisual and information services. Can she tell the Committee a little more about what is envisaged in that recommendation? In particular, is it intended to adopt at European level the Image Col. 5 self-regulatory approach to tackling this problem, which I think is widely recognised to have been effective in the United Kingdom?

Mrs. Roche: My hon. Friend raised the important issue of harmful material on the internet and referred to the related document that has been tagged for this debate.

We have a duty to tackle the problem of harmful material for two main reasons. First, such material needs a policy to deal with it. Secondly, although the amount of material is quite small in relation to the overall content of the internet, it gives those who are not familiar with the internet an impression that we would not want to give. We want to spread the message that use of the internet can bring great advantages.

We have therefore built on an approach which, although self-regulatory, is based on the existing law of the land. I receive quite a lot of correspondence from Members of Parliament passing on letters from constituents who are worried about the internet being totally unregulated. I always reply that there is no difference between the law of the land on line and off line: the law is technology neutral. Harmful or illegal material will be dealt with by the law of the land.

The Committee will be aware that the Government support the Internet Watch Foundation, which works with industry and works very closely with the Home Office. It was backed by the previous Government and we have continued to support it. Indeed, I have asked for a review of its activities, to see whether we can extend them. Therefore, the police, the Home Office, the Government and the industry are involved in the industry's self-regulatory approach. We always keep an open mind and shall ensure that further legislation is introduced, if necessary.

The industry has taken a similar self-regulatory approach to audiovisual and information services. As my hon. Friend will know, there is a live debate on the subject in Euorpe at the moment. I am glad to say that it is a key area where the UK is providing a valuable lead.

Mr. Boswell: It is indeed an important subject, and the Minister has answered helpfully so far. We readily make allowances for the fact that she is operating at the margin of her departmental interest, but can she clarify—although it is pretty clear from the explanatory memorandum—that the Government are happy with the idea of a European Council recommendation on the protection of minors and human dignity and that they hope to secure an agreement on it during their presidency?

However, there is one glitch in relation to the legal base. The Council's Legal Service has queried the use of article 130(3) because it is a competition article. Can the Minister tell the Committee whether that glitch has now been ironed out?

Mrs. Roche: The hon. Gentleman is right. The matter was raised, but I understand that, following discussions with the Commission and the UK presidency and some amendments to the text of the Col. 6 Council working group, the Council's Legal Service has now withdrawn its reservations and is content. I certainly hope that we can make progress and reach agreement. I shall check on the progress so far and make it known to the hon. Gentleman and other members of the Committee. We are keen to make progress—as we shall with the Telecoms Council in May—because of the effect on the use of the internet. Ministers with responsibility for telecommunications are also keen to make similar progress.

Mr. Rammell: These issues are of hugely significant importance to millions of people, yet it appears that they are largely unreported. How would the Minister suggest that the Government work with industry and the media to open the debate to the public?

Mrs. Roche: My hon. Friend has posed one of the key questions that faces us. The document has many ramifications and covers many matters. Quite apart from the inevitable changes in our social and cultural life, it proposes considerable changes for our economic life. One effect of one part of it could be that the increasing convergence of IT industries, telecoms and broadcasting will affect our economic and business life and result in many new industries being established.

There is also a good opportunity for the UK to win a strong competitive position for itself, because convergence will mean that all those content industries will become increasingly important. Historically, the UK has been strong in its content industries. We have only to think of our traditional skills in film making. There are probably few American blockbuster films to which British firms have not contributed. In answer to my hon. Friend the Member for Harlow (Mr. Rammell), this could be one way of engaging public debate. We shall soon hold our own consultations, and I hope that that will add to public discussion. My hon. Friend is right to say that the subject is vital to the country's future; I agree that it needs the widest possible debate among the public and industry.

Mr. Hammond: The paper focuses on competition in the European Union. It refers to opportunities for job creation and growth through convergence. Are the Government also alert to the international dimension and the potential for the export of jobs and growth through the increasing use of electronic commerce and electronic media? Mrs. Roche: The hon. Gentleman poses an interesting question. I chaired the Telecoms Council in February. There was a fascinating discussion among Ministers from all member states on what approach to take. There was a desire for differing approaches and for a light regulatory touch in some areas. Member Image Col. 7 states feel that if the matter is handled correctly there is potential for wealth and job creation. There will be many opportunities for entrepreneurship.

I take a more optimistic view of electronic commerce than the hon. Gentleman. There are many challenges—let us make no mistake about that. However, if we get electronic commerce right, smaller UK companies, our small and medium-sized enterprises, will be able to take advantage of the new technologies to export and expand. It will revolutionise the way in which we think of the supply chain.

We shall need many discussions on electronic commerce, in relation to such matters as cultural diversity, which is a strong and understandable desire of many member states. It is important for Ministers in the European Union to understand that the new technologies and the digital revolution will benefit everyone.

Dr. Ladyman: The Minister said that the law is technology neutral. Does that also apply to the laws governing competition and monopolies? Does current or forthcoming legislation deal with the issues that we are discussing today? Was the competition legislation that the House will be debating over the coming months framed with such issues in mind?

Mrs. Roche: Yes. The Competition Bill that is currently going through Parliament was framed to take account of these matters. That will be useful, as will the responses to the Green Paper on the utilities. Furthermore, we shall introduce a consultation document, which will engender public debate, and will ensure that we adopt the correct framework. That is important not only for the present, but for the future. We face that challenge in the Committee and in Parliament as a whole. It is a fast-moving area and we must get the framework right for the future.

Dr. Palmer: Is the Minister aware of the media speculation that tax revenue in the developed world might emigrate through the internet? Companies could trade from low-tax areas in far away countries. Has this possible long-term trend been discussed at the European level?

Mrs. Roche: As we have said, those issues are relevant at a European and a global level. I am sure that they will be raised at the Bonn and Ottawa conferences. We start from a position that is technology neutral, but we must look carefully at such matters as convergence and the speed with which transactions can move across national frontiers and beyond European Union boundaries.

Mr. Letwin: Will the Minister explain interconnection to the Committee? It is not clear from the documents. Does she agree that one of the drivers towards convergence will be switch-based resellers? They will offer a range of different technologies—perhaps single switches, voice-fixed data and mobile—and interconnection will be important. Will fully Col. 8 distributive costs or incremental costs form the basis for designing regulations for interconnection between existing mobile networks?

Mrs. Roche: I do not think that I can give a technical opinion, but the hon. Gentleman is right to mention interconnection. The deregulation of the telecoms market, not only in the United Kingdom, but elsewhere in the European Union, partly drives the debate. The hon. Gentleman will have seen the interconnection directive. The single market must be fully implemented for that to work. New entrants must be able to move into new markets, and interconnection should be as swift as possible. The date of 1 January 1998 has passed, and must be followed by implementation.

The Commission's great task—and that of the UK presidency, which will be handed on to our Austrian colleagues, and all member states—is to ensure that implementation works in principle. Therefore, we must support the Commission when it makes its regular reports on implementation. The hon. Gentleman made an important point.

Mr. Coaker: I ask the Minister to comment on an important aspect of our debate. We have considered information and technology skills, which are important to access. But the pricing framework is also important. Will that be discussed in the negotiations to ensure that it allows access to as many people as possible?

Mrs. Roche: Access is important. The answer that I gave to the hon. Member for West Dorset (Mr. Letwin) is relevant to the question of my hon. Friend the Member for Gedling (Mr. Coakes). Access is connected to liberalisation, which we should encourage because it leads to falling prices. They encourage access to the internet.

Liberalisation is helpful, but it is not the only answer. We must show the economic, social and cultural benefits of the internet. Therefore, programmes such as "IT for all" are important. The initiative, "Programme for Business" has learning centres, often in the business link network, and encourages small businesses to use new technology.

Prices are not the only barriers. Often, small firms have new technology, but do not use it fully. They are so busy with the day-to-day running of their businesses, that using IT sometimes seems like a bit of a luxury. We need to convey the message that IT is central to a business. Learning centres can help to promote that. We need to pursue several factors in that context. However, the Government can add some value by speaking about and promoting IT. That is why we are pleased to have conducted benchmarking studies of the subject.

Mr. Hammond: To follow on from the question by the hon. Member for Gedling and to keep questions focused on the perhaps rather mundane aspect of the business comprising the local loop and access to the network, I wonder whether I am correct in thinking that while BT still charges substantial premium prices for the installation of ISDN lines in the United Image Col. 9 Kingdom, in other European Union states ISDN provision is universal. Do they see it as a potential impediment to the growth of those technologies in the United Kingdom, likely to place it at a competitive disadvantage in the European Union?

Mrs. Roche: No, I do not think so. In a comparison of the penetration of internet use in this country and in the rest of the European Union, the United Kingdom does well. As for transmitting by ISDN, we shall be able to receive internet services and digital technology by a variety of means. We are keen to promote competition in this sphere. Promoting competition in infrastructure services will enable us to achieve the take-up that we want.

I remind the hon. Member for Runnymede and Weybridge (Mr. Hammond) that, as I told my hon. Friend the Member for Gedling, it is important to communicate the case for use of the new information and communication products. That is vital to the achievement of the necessary take-up and penetration.

My saying that the United Kingdom is doing very well does not mean that we should not learn from what is happening in the rest of the European Union. I have been impressed by some of the projects in other member states. Sweden and Finland provide good examples, particularly in work done with schools, which has demonstrated the information revolution. The United Kingdom is playing a full part in what is happening.

Dr. Palmer: On a point of information, I do not think that many countries have universal ISDN. [HON. MEMBERS: "Germany."] Germany—possibly.

"Browsers and search engines are inherently independent … Recently, however, competition concerns have been raised about the possibility of Browsers being packaged with other software or even becoming fully integrated to the software itself."

Mrs. Roche: The relevant areas of competition policy are important. My hon. Friend refers to a Department of Justice case in the United States, which is still pending. As he said, the Green Paper raises an interesting issue. This is another example of how good Col. 10 the Green Paper is in focusing the mind on complex issues. Friend the Member for Broxtowe (Dr. Palmer) and the Committee may also wish to know that when Telecoms Ministers meet at the Council, we are fortunate in that both Commissioner Bangemann, who is responsible for telecoms, and Commissioner Van Miert, who is responsible for competition policy, frequently attend our deliberations, which is always helpful.

Mr. Letwin: At various points in the helpful notes in preparation for a response that the Minister has provided—she will have noticed that Conservative Members find much to commend in the Green Paper—there are some references to national contexts and others to international, global contexts. How does the Minister envisage convergence, not only of technologies and services but between the World Trade Organisation, the International Telecommunications Union and the European Union? What role does she see the EU playing in attempting to achieve global liberalisation on a basis that will allow convergence of mobile telephone systems—the "mobile satelite—and others to work, not only in the EU but globally?

Mrs. Roche: The hon. Gentleman has raised an important point, as did the hon. Member for Daventry earlier. Some issues are rightly viewed as a matter for national regulation. Other issues, such as copyright and intellectual property rights, are clearly world wide.

Much discussion is currently taking place between the EU and the OECD about preparations for the Ottowa conference, in which the issues raised by the hon. Member for West Dorset are relevant. In this sector there are many regulatory bodies, and the UK is actively involved with them. For example, the Radio communications Agency takes a leading role in representing the UK and also in the implementation of policy. I thought that the hon. Gentleman might have referred to third generation mobile phones, but I shall not take the liberty of replying if he did not. However, if we can get the world standard right in this sector, it would be advantageous not only for the EU but for UK industries, which is why we are keen to promote it.

There is no room for complacency. We want the existing law of the land to apply. We always keep an open mind about whether there is any need for regulations. Mr. Hammond: Staying on the same theme, may I take the discussion on not to harmful or illegal material, but to material that infringes the law in one member state or another? Mr. Derek Twigg: I was reassured to hear the Minister speak in her opening statement of information-rich people and information-poor people. Will she say a little more about the Government's thinking on that subject?

Mrs. Roche: Yes; it is probably one of the biggest questions that faces us, and it affects several Departments. The Department of Trade and Industry has the "IT for all" initiative and the "Programme for Business" initiative, which is often located within the business link network.
