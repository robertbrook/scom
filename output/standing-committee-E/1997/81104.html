The Minister of State, Department of Trade and Industry (Mr. Ian McCartney)
: Good morning to you, Dr. Clark, and to my fellow members of the Committee.

We have the opportunity to debate the European Commission's proposed social action programme for the three-year period from 1998 to 2000. I look forward to a constructive discussion.

I was privileged to speak for the Opposition when, in November 1995, this Committee debated the previous programme for 1995 to 1997. However, I should say that it is much better to speak as a member of the Government. Those of us who are veterans of the 1995 debate will remember that I spoke then about the United Kingdom's dismal employment record, about the lack of investment in training and the lack of decent minimum standards in the workplace.

Much has changed since then. We have signed the social chapter, and we are implementing four directives under it. We have also implemented the working time and young workers directives. Moreover, we are introducing legislation on fairness at work to fulfil our manifesto commitments.

The social action programme, which will in effect, run for two and a half years, and which reflects the UK's influence and input, sets out a framework for the Commission's proposed actions. The programme was introduced to the Social Affairs Council by the Commission on 4 June; the Council subsequently debated the matter on 27 October and at Pôrtschach.

The Council's role is not to endorse the programme per se; and it did not do so. The Council will, of course, give full and proper consideration to those actions that require decisions as and when the Commission makes specific proposals, considering each proposal on its merits.

We shall have ample time and opportunity to discuss some of the issues, and I shall be glad to do so. However, it is important to put this debate and the social action programme in context. There are 18 million people employed in Europe. That is why the issue of jobs is at the forefront of the European 2 agenda, as the social action programme correctly recognises, and why the European Union is acting to remedy this appalling tragedy by developing new employment strategies.

In June 1997, we began to see real change at the Amsterdam European Council, where a new employment chapter for the EU treaty was agreed and, quite remarkably, given immediate effect. That text emphasises the need for modern work forces and flexible labour markets.

At the end of 1997, after the special Luxembourg jobs summit, the Council agreed a set of detailed employment guidelines to be followed by member states when implementing employment policy, under the four main themes of employability, entrepreneurship, adaptability and equal opportunities. Those pillars are a result of the major influence of the UK Government.

By the end of April this year, all member states had submitted national employment action plans that describe how each country implements the guidelines. This country was the first to submit such a plan.

The Cardiff European Council, which the Prime Minister chaired in June, and which rounded off the UK presidency, acknowledged the contribution made by the action plans and agreed those areas where further work is needed under the four pillars. The agreement on employment guidelines for 1999 will reflect that agenda, and will be a key task for our Austrian friends during the remainder of their presidency.

That is the backdrop against which the Commission has produced its new programme, and against which we shall judge it. Do the proposed measures promote employability or enhance adaptability? Do they encourage entrepreneurship or provide greater equality of opportunity? Will they lead to more flexible labour markets? Will they enhance competitiveness and job creation?

The three main themes of the programme—jobs, skills and mobility; the changing world of work; and an inclusive society—suggest that the Commission has broadly recognised those imperatives.

In summary, the Government commend a great deal of the programme, much of which sits foursquare with our policies. However, we shall not be able to support some of the Commission's actions, either because of a proposal's substance or because it offends against subsidiarity. One such measure relates to the provision of information and the consultation of workers at national level. UNICE decided on 16 October not to enter negotiations with the European Trades Union Congress about a possible agreement. We, therefore, expect the Commission to propose a directive. I noted in my memorandum that a proposal on healthcare may also offend against subsidiarity.

The programme simply sets a framework. We will form a clearer view and seek to influence decisions when issues are clarified. For example, the Commission is and will be discussing specific issues with the social partners, as provided for under the social chapter, before deciding whether to proceed. My ministerial colleagues in other Departments and I JPEG 3 will come back to the Committee over the next two years or so with memorandums on specific proposals. In the meantime, I am happy to hear the Committee's views, to respond to questions and, when appropriate, to put the motion.
