Image Col. 5 The European Community preliminary draft budget for 1999 is a serious and important document, especially as we look forward to the new challenges that the European Union faces. I am sure that members of the Committee have read the views that the Government have outlined in two explanatory memoranda. Therefore my introductory remarks will be brief.

The 1999 budget covers the last year of the current financial perspectives—the headings under which EU finances are set. Therefore it presents an especially difficult problem for budgetary discipline, but also an important challenge for the future.

The problem is caused by the weight of past commitments on the structural funds. As members of the Committee know, the current financial perspectives were agreed at the European Council meeting in Edinburgh in 1992. As my constituency is close to Edinburgh, I may add that that city did not make its usual impact in terms of budgetary rigour. Some decisions meant that, unlike other items in the budget, structural funds would have a privileged status. The Council promised to make available a specific amount of funding during the period of the current financial perspectives. The Council also decided that the funds would be made available at the rate at which member states said that they were able to use them. That has important implications for the budget that we are considering today.

Partly because of hold-ups in 1993 in the Council and Parliament's agreeing the implementing regulations for the structural funds, but also because of difficulties on the ground, member states have been slower than expected to implement their allocation of funds. Consequently in 1999, the last year of the current financial perspective, the budget will have to bear the weight of a planned increase of some 2·5 billion euros of commitments originally foreseen in 1992, as well as an additional 3 billion euros of commitments that were not spent in the years 1994–1998. So altogether, the Council and European Parliament—the budgetary authority—have no choice, because of the decisions taken in Edinburgh in 1992, but to agree to a total increase in the 1999 budget of some 5·5 billion euros in structural funds. As a result, even if all other parts of the budget were frozen, the overall budget would still increase by some 6 per cent. in commitments and 3 per cent. in payments.

That presents a significant challenge to budgetary discipline. As the Committee knows, over the last two years, the annual increase in budget payments has been restricted to about 1 per cent. Budgetary discipline is important, because limiting resources has a powerfull effect on improving the use to which those resources are put, and thereby helps to cut out mismanagement and fraud. It is also important because European expenditure places considerable strain on national budgets and cannot be exempt from the same rigorous approach that we apply to national expenditure.

Especially this year, as we look forward to the new financial perspectives, budgetary discipline is crucial in view of the financial burdens that enlargement will place on the Union. Total EC expenditure is currently Col. 6 limited by the own-resources ceiling of 1.2 per cent. of member states' GNP. Over the past four or five years, a disciplined approach to EC budgeting has enabled significant headroom to be opened up under this ceiling so that the Commission's proposals for 1999 now represent only 1.11 per cent. of member states' GNP. It is clear that for many member states, including ourselves, 1.27 per cent. represents the absolute limit for contributions to Europe.

There is broad agreement among member states about the importance of enlargement—not only because of its economic impact but because of the wider significance of bringing the former east European bloc countries and others into the EU. If we are to make enlargement work and ensure that the own-resources ceiling remains durable for the next financial perspectives and beyond, we must do what we can to preserve and even increase that headroom in the final year before the new financial perspectives.

The European Commission has proposed an increase of 6.47 per cent. in commitments and 3.38 per cent. in payments. By taking a firm approach and focusing on the real priorities, we can and must do better. That will be my priority when I attend the Budget Council in Brussels next Friday. However, I repeat that the burden of the structural funds causes an enormous problem for the 1999 budget and for that reason it is highly unlikely that we will do better than the 1.4 per cent. increase agreed last year. However, we shall do our best to improve on it.

Finally, I should like to speak about the scrutiny procedure. It is a year since our last scrutiny debate, during which we heard some helpful suggestions—not least from the right hon. Member for Cities of London and Westminster (Mr. Brooke), a distinguished former Treasury Minister—for improving the scrutiny process. I agreed with his perceptive remarks. Following last year's debate on the preliminary draft budget or PDB, as it is known, I agreed that there must be a better way of scrutinising budget documents. The Select Committee on European Legislation has adopted a new approach, based on the Government's recommendations, aimed at providing more timely and helpful information to Parliament.

Our aim is to increase transparency and to get away from the mountain of often undicipherable paper work visited on members of the Committee—sometimes of such a volume as to inhibit proper scrutiny.

The new arrangements include a separate explanatory memorandum focusing on revenue and fraud. Though the annual budget process cannot change the rules that govern the way in which the cost of the EC budget is shared between member states, we have an opportunity today to find out in more detail what is happening in the European budget. In the new process we have attempted to draw hon. Members' attention to the key parts of the PDB documents and to the Commission's budget overview in particular. I hope that hon. Members will as a result feel that they are better informed for the debate.
