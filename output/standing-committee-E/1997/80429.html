Water Policy and Bathing Water

[ Relevant document: European Community Document No. 6260/98, further proposed amendments to Annex V of the draft Water Policy Framework Directive.]

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Angela Eagle)
: I am glad to have the opportunity to take part in the debate on European Community water policy. I am sure that none of us is in any doubt about the significance of water policy. Water is of the utmost importance for human life and health, for aquatic ecosystems and for the quality of the environment in its broadest sense.

Water is also of tremendous economic importance. A great deal of water policy and legislation is set or influenced at European level. We must strike a balance between different needs for water and different ways of meeting those needs. Therefore, it is vital that we get Community water policy right.

Moving to the specific subject of today's debate, I will introduce the Commission's proposal for a directive which establishes a framework for Community action in the field of water policy, or, to use its less cumbersome title, the water framework directive.

The proposed water framework directive is a major piece of Community environmental legislation. It has a high profile, having been prepared at the specific request of the Council and the European Parliament.

The directive goes to the heart of Community water policy and offers a pioneering approach for developing water legislation in line with advances in scientific and technical knowledge. The emphasis is no longer only on chemical standards—those lists of numbers that often characterised older water legislation. By contrast, the directive encourages a broader ecological perspective that takes account of the chemical and physical aspects of the water environment. It measures net effects on the health of biological populations.

The directive will also overhaul existing Community water legislation, much of which was passed in the mid-1970s and is now out of date. It will provide a framework which will accommodate newer legislation, such as the urban waste water treatment directive.

The water framework directive has a broad scope. It will establish a common approach to environmental objectives for all ground and surface waters in the Community. It will also establish a common method of implementation, based on the principle of river basin management. Its objectives on sustainable water use will require member states to bring their rivers, lakes, 2 estuaries and coastal waters up to a standard that allows typical biological populations to survive and reproduce. That will ensure that water of a suitable quality is provided for the various uses to which the water environment is put.

Quantity objectives for ground waters have sustainability at their heart. Member states will have to restrict abstraction to quantities that do not result in long-term depletion of ground-water levels or significant ecological damage to surface water systems that are connected to ground-waters.

Clearly, there are challenges associated with such a wide-ranging and ambitious directive. We need to ensure that we do not sign up to unnecessary or impracticable objectives, and so the emphasis on framework is important. We must strike the right balance between European Community prescriptiveness and a flexibility that will enable member states to implement the directive using their own systems. There must also be sufficient comparability between member states for us to be confident that we are working towards common goals. We must make sure that the directive provides a lasting framework that we can use to protect Europe's water for the foreseeable future.

A great deal has been achieved during negotiations on the framework directive, especially since the United Kingdom assumed the EU presidency. We still have some way to go but, subject to the views of this House and another place, we are optimistic that we will make real progress on bringing this important directive to a satisfactory conclusion.

I will now address the Commission's amended proposal for the bathing water directive. In 1995, the House of Lords covered the ground thoroughly: they had a full debate and the Select Committee on European Communities produced two reports. Their Lordships drew attention to the need for a full discussion on acceptable levels of health protection for sea bathers. However, they were unconvinced that there was justification for imposing on the general public significantly higher costs in order to reduce somewhat the risks of self-limiting illness associated with bathing.

On the basis of the available evidence on health effects, the previous Government shared those views, and the present Government see no reason for taking a significantly different stance. The Government support revision of the directive where it would allow better targeting of resources without weakening standards of protection. However, we do not believe that the revision proposed by the Commission in 1994, nor the 1997 amendments, meets those objectives.

On the understanding that the directive deals chiefly with the protection of public health, we question the health-related scientific basis for the new mandatory quality requirements proposed for some of the microbiological parameters. Those requirements would pose considerable compliance problems—and expense—for all member states. It is surely significant that, since publication of the proposal, none of the successive presidencies has given the proposal sufficient priority to include it on the Environment Council agenda.

The Commission's amended proposal, which contains some of the amendments from the JPEG 3 consideration by the European Parliament, includes even tighter mandatory standards and takes little account of previous criticisms. That proposal would pose even more difficulties and expense, and it is likely that the majority of member states will have similar objections.

There has been considerable opposition to the proposed revision. Moreover, during its presidency no member state has taken up that revision since its introduction in 1994. The Commission should therefore seriously consider withdrawing the proposal. It should then begin proper consultations on what member states would like to see in any new revision. It should seek to improve the revision's scientific basis, and should consider a new approach that involves better consultation between the Commission and member states, and which takes fully into account states' experience of implementing the existing directive.

I would now be pleased to answer any questions that hon. Members may have on either of the proposals.

Angela Eagle
: I do not think that any Members of Parliament would say that they opposed raising the quality of bathing water, drinking water or any of the waters around the country. However, the question is whether the 1994 EU bathing water directive and the suggestions from the European Parliament in 1997 are sufficient for the task.

The Government consider that the measurements chosen for the bathing water directive by the European Parliament and the European Commission do not do the job. We have particular reservations about the much tighter mandatory limits on enterovirus, which is very small and hard to spot: sampling water is not guaranteed to produce conclusive results. Much more important is that water should contain no pathogens damaging to human health.

The Government agree with the 1995 House of Lords report, which stated that the mandatory standard for enterovirus measurement is a poor gauge of whether water poses a risk of ill health. We believe coliforms offer a much closer guide to possible problems with bathing waters.

Mr. Paul Clark
: The worldwide debate about global warming and climatic change has many angles and is affected by many of the decisions taken by Governments. Will my hon. Friend explain how the water framework directive might contribute to that discussion?

Angela Eagle
: The new concept of ecological health offers a much better way to decide whether a water 6 environment is safe than can a measure of chemical health alone. A safe water environment will permit a healthy ecology, but plant, insect and animal life will decline in a heavily polluted environment. The concept of ecological health is a better guide than a mere list of chemicals for which one must search in a given volume of water to say whether it meets the required standard.

Sometimes, however, we must measure what chemicals are present in water. We expect that the framework directive will contain a priority list of toxic substances, such as organophosphates and pesticides, which may be put on a list and then measured for. The twin concepts of ecological health and chemical quality together allow us to assess whether we have a healthy water environment.

The step change implied in the concept behind this directive is that it covers the whole topic of river basin management. It is not confined to a stretch of water in a certain area, but includes ground water and the health of rivers, estuaries and coastal waters. It is a huge improvement on the old European Union directives of the 1970s, which merely contained lists of chemicals, pollutants and pathogens that had to be found and measured.

The aim of the framework directive, if successfully agreed and put into operation, will be to achieve good water status in river basin management areas. That good status will depend on a combination of chemical and ecological good health.

The idea of a common level of ambition demonstrates that this is not a prescriptive, centralising directive—unlike the bathing water directive, which contains mandatory targets for several main substances.
