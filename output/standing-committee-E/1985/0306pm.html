Financial Services Bill

Clause 96
POWER TO TRANSFER FUNCTIONS TO DESIGNATED AGENCY

Mr. Bryan Gould
(Dagenham)
: I beg to move amendment No. 254, in page 41, line 40, leave out subsections (1) and (2) and insert—

"(1) There shall be a Securities and Investments Board, established in conformity with the requirements of Schedule 5 to this Act, which shall carry out the functions to which this section applies.".

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to take the following amendments:

No. 245, in page 71, line 41, leave out paragraph (a) and insert—

"(a) that the Securities and Investment Board is able and willing to discharge all or any of the functions to which this section applies; and".

No. 246, in page 72, line 7, leave out second "a" and insert "the".

No. 255, in page 72, line 31, leave out subsections (6), (7), (8), (9), (10) and (11).

Mr. Gould
: The amendment takes us to one of the Bill's central issues. It may help the Committee and the Minister if I repeat what I said on earlier amendments. The amendment carries no implication that we are unhappy with the broad structure and principles of the Bill or with the broad balance struck between statutory rules and self-regulation. I hope that my remarks on the amendment will be seen in the context that we approve the notion of a statutory framework within which a substantial degree of self-regulation is permitted and, indeed, encouraged.

However, the Bill is not clear about who will run the body of statutory rules. The Bill is curiously drafted in that respect and is surprisingly coy about who will do the job. Not until clause 96 is a hint of an answer offered and even then it is in rather elliptical language. Clause 96 says:

"If it appears to the Secretary of State—


(a) that a body corporate has been established"

he may make a designation order.

Nowhere in the Bill do the words "Securities and Investments Board" appear, which is a curious piece of drafting. It leaves a gaping hole at the heart of the Bill. It is unnecessarily devious to fail to identify what body is contemplated to exercise the powers.

520 We have ended up in a curious state, with all the paraphernalia and most of the reality of statutory regulation but no statutory body to undertake responsibility. The reason is that when the Bill was drafted and its shape was being decided, the Government simply lacked the courage of their conviction. The Government's courage failed them. They knew perfectly well that what was required was a straightforward system of statutory principles to be run in a straightforward way.

I always feel obliged to make it clear, lest hon. Members do not understand me, that, as established in the Bill, the statutory framework should contain the role for self-regulation with which we are all familiar. However, at the apex of the pyramid surely there should be a clear-cut body identified in statute with its powers and composition defined by statute.

I believe, that as with Professor Gowers's report, it was feared that if that recommendation were put forward, the City would not wear it and that, if the City's acceptance of the proposal was prejudiced, the Bill might be jeopardised.

Oddly enough—or perhaps not—City opinion has moved ahead faster than was foreseen. City opinion has largely outstripped the Government's rather pusillanimous view in the Bill. There is probably a majority opinion in the City, at least within the younger, more progressive elements, that a clear-cut statutory body is desirable or, at the very least, inevitable. That majority opinion, which is growing all the time, comes from some surprising people. Unlikely bedfellows such as Ian Posgate and Sir John Nott show how wide is the range of opinion, in favour, for varying reasons perhaps, of a statutory body.

I think that that opinion is correct. The pace of change in the City is frantic, the changes are unpredictable, the old safeguards are being swept away, the market place which is the City has altered, and, more than ever before, we shall need clarity and certainty in the way that the City is regulated.

After all, that is largely why the Bill is before us. The Government are to be commended for bringing it forward, and, as we have often made clear, in broad terms we support it. But the purpose of the Bill is to lay down for the City, in difficult and unpredictable circumstances, a clear and effective system of regulation, and if that is our objective, at the heart of the structure there should be a statutory body.

I have been looking at foreign models, and I find that they encourage that view. I have never thought in terms of the Securities and Exchange Commission as others have, partly because I think that it has a bad reputation. That may be undeserved and in recent years it has operated more acceptably than when it was first set up.

However, if the choice is between an SEC and a voluntary body, the City might be tempted to go for the latter. But that is not the option: a body with statutory identity need not be an SEC in every respect. There are more acceptable and workable models available under other jurisdictions. Virtually every other important capital market has a statutory commission of some JPEG 521 kind. Two of the better examples are the Ontario Securities Commission and the National Companies and Securities Commission of Australia.

Through the good offices of the stock exchange, I have obtained details of how those bodies operate, and they appear to be admirable models. The chairman of the Australian body, having described the way that his commission operates, says:

"This has resulted in a regulatory system which is largely satisfactory to both the exchanges and the Commission."

A similar view is given on the Ontario commission. Therefore, the notion of a statutory commission is not only well established but well accepted in almost every other jurisdiction faced with the problem.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Trade and Industry (Mr. Michael Howard)
: Can the hon. Gentleman tell the Committee by whom, and in what terms the same point was made about the Ontario commission?

Mr. Gould
: I cannot guarantee that I can find the exact phrase in this letter which comes from the chairman of the Toronto stock exchange, but I shall pass a copy of the letter to the Minister if he wishes to see it.

I hope that I have established that in other jurisdictions a statutory commission is acceptable and works well. Therefore, does it matter whether we have one? I believe that it does because if we do not, we shall face the over-complicated business of creating the powers, vesting them in the hands of the Secretary of State, delegating them on certain conditions, making provision for taking them back and so on. A voluntary body would, therefore, operate at one step removed from the creation of the powers in the statute. That is the first unnecessary complication.

But there are more serious problems. Although everyone understands the point, it is worth repeating to show how odd it is. The Securities and Investments Board, which is now a private company limited by guarantee, and will remain so, will exercise those powers. It is odd that that body, having received delegated powers, should be able to make rules with legislative effect. That has emerged in the questions of my hon. Friend the member for Workington (Mr. Campbell-Savours), and on other occasions.

That power will be, to some extent, unfettered when the designating order is made. The Minister may withdraw designation, or dismiss members of the council, but, after designation, that private company, limited by guarantee, will have a fair degree of independence in making rules of legislative effect. Provisions in schedule 7 require it to give notice of its intentions, but those who see themselves as potentially subject to those rules are anxious.

The hon. Member for Bournemouth, West (Mr. Butterfill) has tabled an amendment, drafted by the stock exchange, requiring SIB, when making such rules, to consult those who may be affected.

Mr. Gould
: That shows the element of unease about the unusual principle that an ability to make rules with legal effect should be placed in private hands. I say unusual rather than unique, but there are few other such examples, so perhaps the Minister will draw our attention to them if he wishes to claim that the position is not as unusual as it appears.

The peculiarities rest not so much with the proposed structure—the ability to make rules, for example—but with those issues inhibited by the peculiar structure. In normal circumstances, some aspects of the operation of SIB would have been regarded as straightforward and almost axiomatic, but, because of the peculiar position of a private body exercising those powers, the Government have not included them in the Bill.

We had, for example, an interesting debate on whether SIB should have the power to impose changes in the rules of SROs. The Bill was drafted as it was because it looked odd to give to one voluntary body the power to impose rule changes on other voluntary bodies. That weakness in the relationship explains many of the Bill's provisions and weaknesses. The Committee said, rightly, "No. If that regulation is to be effective, SIB must have those powers." The Minister eventually acceded to that reasoning, and we look forward to seeing his proposals in that respect. We encountered that problem, and needed the debate, because the relationship between a voluntary body and other voluntary bodies regulated by it is an odd one and leads us into difficulties.

The hon. Member for Chichester (Mr. Nelson) raised another aspect of a similar problem, concerning SIB's powers to investigate SROs and other authorised people. He drew attention to a limitation built into the Bill on that power of investigation, and, again, we can see why it is there—it is odd to give one voluntary body the power to investigate the activities of others. If SIB were idenditifed in the statute, no one would have thought further about it, as it would have been a normal aspect of the operations of a regulatory body.

There is also the more controversial question of the power of SIB to initiate prosecutions. Such power does not currently exist and I do not necessarily think that it should. I suspect that our debate has again been inhibited because SIB is not a statutory body. It is in a different position from the Bank of England which has the power to initiate prosecutions—with the DPP, in many cases—under the Banking Act.

4.45 pm

If we were serious about running the City as effectively as the Bank of England is supposed to run the banking system we would not be so hesitant. But because the Government have chosen this curious solution, matters are becoming unnecessarily snarled up.

The leitmotiv running through our debates has been the problem of the relationship between SIB and the SROs. How can SIB regulate the SROs effectively? We come up against the problem so often because it is very difficult to allow one bunch of chaps—that is what they are; a bunch of chaps on whom the Secretary of State's eye happens to fall—to exercise the power of JPEG 523 effective regulation over the SROs. Indeed, some of my hon. Friends and I could set ourselves up as a body corporate. We could say to the Minister, "How about us? We are a likely-looking bunch. We could do the job." But that body, if it were private and voluntary, would face the same regulatory problems as SIB.

There are other—not unimportant—matters, such as SIB's accountability to Parliament. The provisions for that accountability would flow more naturally if SIB were set up under the statute and had to make to Parliament reports which would be open to debate.

There is also the question of how to fit a body like the Takeover Panel into the structure of the Bill. The panel is not mentioned, but in the light of recent developments, perhaps we should consider whether it should be brought within the Bill's scope. That could be done only on a statutory basis. But how could we make the Takeover Panel a part of the Bill's statutory identity, and how could we define its relationship to a non-statutory body which is supposed to have an overall supervisory function?

The financing dichotomy is not as clear cut as the hon. Member for Yeovil (Mr. Ashdown) suggested. As a voluntary body, SIB has to be financed through fee income. It would be sensible for the Government to say, "Here is a body that is entrusted with statutory functions. It needs resources. We shall provide those resources, but let us give it the power to charge fees to cover its costs." Although that might not make much practical difference, SIB could not then be accused of having its level of activity determined by the fees charged to those whom it would be regulating. Under a proper system of financing, SIB's level of activity would be determined by its appreciation of what was required and it would have the power to fix its fees and cover expenses incurred by and agreed level of activity.

There is one argument for keeping SIB as a voluntary body and that has been put forcefully by those involved in the organisation at present. While they recognise the strength of many of my arguments, which I hope will have weighed also with some Conservative Members, their major worry is that if SIB is to be made a statutory body in any shape or form and, as a consequence, is to be financed at least in principle from the public sector even though it involves no net cost to the Exchequer, it would become subject to Treasury control and would always be in the list of those bodies that had to be cut back when the Treasury axe was wielded and so, in the end, would not have the resources to do the job that is necessary. For example, it could not pay the salaries that now, perhaps, it has to pay, given the outrageous salaries in the City.

I understand the argument, but it is a shocking indictment of our system of government that what is, on all other counts, a sensible and straightforward way of proceeding is to be frustrated and inhibited by this ridiculous argument which could be resolved at the stroke of a pen. In saying that, I am quoting one of the Minister's right hon. Friends who made that point on the Floor of the House a week or so ago in relation to the water boards.

Mr. Gould
: The hon. Gentleman makes a fair comment. It is inevitable that any institution set up to undertake any form of public purpose is rightly always at the mercy of the political views of the Government. We live with that situation, but it is not an argument in every case for failing to do now what is right.

I turn to the point about civil servants. When Professor Gower wrote part I of his report, he asked whether this area should be regulated by civil servants or by a self-standing commission. That was the choice. At that point I did believe that civil servants were the right people for the job and I remain of that view.

I am grateful to the hon. member for Yeovil because I want to make it clear that I am talking about a commission whose independence of both the City and the Government is as well-established as possible in the Bill. With all respect to the civil servants present, we are not talking about a branch of the DTI. They do a perfectly good job as civil servants, but they are not adequately equipped by training or experience to regulate the City in the way that we would want an JPEG 525 independent and self-standing commission to do.

The Bill involves a hard practical operation, setting in place real powers, but there is also an element of presentations and psychology. If the legislation is to work, we must convince those who deal in City markets and institutions that we mean business. We shall give the wrong impression to all those contemplating coming to London or continuing to trade in London if we put in place a great structure of regulation with, at its heart, a private company that is indistinguishable from any other pirate company and which does not even appear in the Bill.

Many people from other countries, who will be increasingly important in the City of tomorrow, will fail to understand our intention and will receive the wrong message. They will believe that the Bill contains a clear indication that the Government and the City of London are not serious about effective regulation. That would be most unfortunate.

It would have been better to set up in the early clauses a securities and investments board not very different from the one already planned. It would at least have appeared in the Bill so that its powers could be defined.

My amendment is very much a second best and would not solve the problem. I accept fully that the peculiarity of the structure would remain even if the amendment were accepted. But if the amendment were accepted we should least achieve the identity of the SIB within the Act. The damaging question, "Who is to run it?" would be given a proper answer. If we leave the vacuum in the Bill we shall hopelessly prejudice the system's chances of working effectively.

Mr. Anthony Nelson
(Chichester)
: I believe that the Committee owes a debt of gratitude to the the hon. Member for Dagenham (Mr. Gould) for the compelling and moderate way in which he advanced his arguments. The atmosphere in which our debates are taking place is radically different from that in which they might have been conducted some years ago. That difference reflects not only the common desire to institute a durable system which will be respected in the City and outside, but the modus vivendi that can operate in Standing Committees. Although we discuss the Bill from different perspectives, we nevertheless find common ground. I have a preference for my own amendment, but I agree with the main import of what the hon. Member Dagenham said.

It is important to remind ourselves of the history of this gorup of amendments, which are very significant and which would change the structure of the system of regulatory control. Before we advance detailed arguments for or against the amendments, we must recall that we are here principally to try to improve investor protection. The whole rationale of the new regulatory system, and of any powers employed by bodies set up under it, is principally not to ensure the better oiled and more efficient working of City institutions, but to improve investor protection. Above all, it should act as a more effective preventive medicine against ugly situations that may arise in the future.

The ugly situations in the past—which gave rise to the initial inquiry and the appointment of Professor Gower, and to successive inquiries into the scrutiny of City affairs—were horrendous. During a time of booming stock markets, when it is easy to make up uncovered situations, it is easy to believe that such scandals will not recur and people will not be defaulted in the same way. Sadly, I predict that whatever we pass in the Bill they will occur. In company law, or in this legislation, there is no way that we can ensure a watertight sytem, but we have a common obligation to do the best we can.

I hope that hon. Members on both sides of the Committee, even if they do not support my arguments, will at least appreciate my credentials. I worked for some years in the City in the investment divisions of merchant banks, and I have taken a close interest in that aspect of regulation over the past few years. I submitted evidence to Professor Gower even before his interim report was published, proposing the regulatory system that I reiterated on Second Reading and which I am trying to introduce in this amendment.

Political and technical arguments can be adduced in support of amendment No. 245, which enjoys the signatory support of some of my hon. Friends. Having made some pleasant remarks about Opposition Members, I do not wish to strike too strong a note of dissension, but I believe that the tactic displayed this morning was rather shallow and unhelpful. While signatories to any amendment are at liberty to withdraw their signatures from, not to support, or, indeed, to vote against an amendment, Conservative Members do not need the blandishments of Opposition Members, who implied that in some way our arms were being twisted.

I believe that you would agree, Sir Michael, that Conservative Members have displayed exemplary independence and responsibility it their approach to a series of amendments—notable the question of the rule changes that can be imposed by the SIB. I am happy to trust their objectivity and judgment in assessing the arguments that I wish to present.

I believe that political arguments in favour of statutory recognition of the designated agency come first. To recognise the designated agency specifically and in law would send the right messages to the public. I believe that investors and the City would be reassured if they felt that that body had a place in law and was recognised, and that the legislation fulfilled what they understand to be undertakings by the Government, in successive reports, to institute a regulatory system and not a discretionary system allowing the Secretary of State to delegate this or that power to this or that body.

Like the hon. Member for Dagenham, I believe that public opinion, City opinion and international opinion have moved markedly during the process of public discussion of this subject not only over the last two and a half years, but interestingly, quite rapidly over the last six months or so. I am sure that my hon. Friend and lady Friend the Under-Secretary—who has excellent contacts and has developed an excellent reputation in a JPEG 527 short period of time in office for consulting closely with bodies in the City—will be aware that while some of them have misgivings about what I am proposing other authoritative and responsible bodies support it. They have changed their minds in its favour and none of them can be classified as people who do not support in any way the ideals of a capitalist and free system.

Some people believe that there is a limit to what we can do in legislation by instituting a regulatory system, or laying down provisions of law. That is what I call the Stanley Clinton Davis theory of company law. He believed that, although we have a responsibility to define as closely as possible and prescribe the law, we are capable only of sending out a message about what we think is permissable, recognising the limitations of law and the sophistication of lawyers, enabling them to get round a system. A good example of that is insider dealing, because, however much we may legislate or speak against it, the practice is difficult to control. Those hon. Members who took part in the passage of the Companies Act 1980 will know what I am talking about.

We have a responsibility to draft our legislation as clearly and specifically as possible, and I am not in favour of having it made subsequently by order. My hon. Friend the Member of Bournemouth, West (Mr. Butterfill) supported the criticism that I voiced on Second Reading about the lack of detail in the legislation. We are sending a message to the City and the public to inform them or our anxieties and seriousness about instituting a system of statutory and permissive control. The recognition of the body in law would reassure professional operators in the City and provide reassurance and protection for the investing public.

The second political argument that I adduce in favour of my amendment is that it would enjoy political support on both sides of the House, and be more enduring than other legislation in recent years. The idea of common cause and middle ground does not necessarily appeal to everyone, but we have a responsibility to ensure that the City of London and the financial services industry remain a vital and contributory part of our economy, vibrant and prosperous, and a significant contributor to our balance of payments. Any inhibition on their ability to do so, through uncertainty about the future, or by the diversion of business to other markets, which may operate on a more fair, overt and controlled basis, would be detrimental to the City's prospects.

Therefore, it may be argued, politically and economically, that there is a strong case in favour of a statutory recognition of SIB, to ensure that we have a clear system that will attract money, give people confidence and endure the passage of time.

We should not underestimate the importance of proceeding in the House, wherever possible, on a bipartisan basis of support. On such occasions, we should state our anxieties frankly. Many of my hon. Friends on the Committee and in the parliamentary Conservative party are anxious that statutory recognition of SIB might be used by a future Government, perhaps of a different political persuasion, as a Trojan horse for the institution of Securities and Exchange Commission- 528 type system in this country.

The principal political argument in favour of recognition is that, if we fail to institute a system of regulatory control at this stage in law, there is a danger that, at a later stage—perhaps in a few years' time, and under a Government of a different political complexion—we shall have a more draconian system of control over the City and the financial services industry.

Conservative Members are anxious about the matter, and, if we fail to seize the opportunity to institute now a system of statutory regulation, which enjoys a progressive and responsible body of opinion support in the City, there is a danger that, in very changed circumstances, with a bear market and a series of scandals, we shall have something vastly different. We may have the repeal of the Bill and total control of the City. The prospects for the City would be disastrous and we should have to hold ourselves accountable for not instituting now a system that might have prevented that eventuality.

I read chapter III of Professor Gower's report with particular interest. It sets out the arguments for and against the DTI having a self-standing regulatory commission. Professor Gower, whom most of us know and respect, came down in favour of such a commission, but felt that in the political atmosphere existing then— some time ago now—it was not on the cards. He said that the decision was finely balanced and that he wanted to get it right, but he could not, on balance, support such a commission. The professor felt that a statutorily recognised body would be more in touch with what was going on and could offer attractions and a career structure to able people.

Many people who heard that the Bill was being brought in believed that it would provide investors with significantly greater protection, and I believe, in many respects, that it will. I fear that unless the amen-dements are accepted, we shall fall short of many expectations outside the House.

I shall try to explain the technical reasons. Even if the Secretary of State is satisfied that SIB can fulfil the functions laid down for it, and he delegates all power that he can to it, the board will still not be able to investigate insider dealing; it will still not be able to 530 obtain information from unauthorised people in investigating their activities or those of authorised persons; it will still not be able to change certain rules on advertising by unauthorised persons; it will not be able to recognise investment or collective schemes in the European Community or other countries. The SIB investigator, inquiring into an authorised insurance and investment business and its tied salesmen, will not be able to obtain information from anyone other than an authorised person, and SIB will not be able to exercise certain powers towards insurance companies—particularly with reference to schedule 8 of the Bill.

That would be the position even if the Secretary of State devolved to SIB the maximum powers allowed under the Bill. Why is that so? Most people believe that once the system is in place, the body will be a watchdog, responsible for policing and overseeing the activities of all those in the investment field, and preventing the worst sort of activities from taking place.

There is one simple reason why SIB will not work like that. Apparently SIB is regarded as a private sector body, and as such cannot undertake any of the functions I have listed. Members of the key features of the arrangements proposed in the Bill.

The Secretary of State may transfer powers to an agency only when he is satisfied that specified criteria are met. Those criteria relate, first, to the constitution of the body. Its constitution must provide for members to be appointed by, and to be removable by, the Secretary of State and the governor of the Bank of England acting jointly, and those members must include practitioners, users and others. It must have proper procedures for taking decisions. Those provisions ensure that the powers can be transferred only to a body that is properly organised and run by people who have between them the range of experience to do an effective regulatory job.

Secondly, the agency must have a satisfactory system for monitoring and enforcing its rules and for investigating complaints and it must be able and willing to promote and maintain high standards of integrity and fair dealing. Thirdly, rule-making powers may be transferred only if the rules that the agency proposes to make would provide adequate investor protection and comply with the principles set out in schedule 6. The Secretary of State must also be satisfied that the rules will not restrict competition to an extent greater than is necessary in the interests of investor protection.

Those are tough requirements, designed to ensure that powers can be transferred only to an agency that has the resources, will and ability to maintain the 534 highest standards in the carrying out of investment business. The transfer order is subject ot affirmative resolution, so powers can be transferred only if Parliament, as well as the Secretary of State, is satisfied that it is right to do so.

Which powers can be transferred? We shall debate the question later, but the short answer is that virtually all powers relating to authorised persons and exempt representatives, except those concerning the scope of regulation under the Bill can be transferred. The latter are reserved to the Secretary of State, and I believe that they should be reserved to him, whatever the nature of the central body.

Therefore, the Bill enables the agency to exercise its powers flexibly and quickly, so that it can respond rapidly to changing circumstances. In addition, the Bill allows the agency to determine its own budget, free from external controls, so that it can decide what resources and staff it needs to function properly.

The Bill provides four methods for maintaining effectiveness and accountability, after the transfer of functions. First, the Secretary of State and the governor, acting jointly, can remove the agency's chairman and board members. Secondly, if the agency fails to meet any of the requirements for the transfer of functions, the Secretary of State can withdraw powers—in whole or in part—from the agency, and can either exercise them himself, or transfer them to another agency that would do a better job. Thirdly, the Secretary of State can direct the agency to alter its rules or practices if he believes that they are unduly anti-competitive. We would move inescapably down the road to a fully statutory body in the sense that the council of the stock exchange is a fully statutory body. It would derive its powers in detail from the Government, from statute and from further Government intervention. That would happen eventually, if not immediately, and would be contrary to the fundamental strategy of the measure. The measure seeks to establish broad principles and criteria within which the agency would have to work, but recognises the enormous importance of flexibility and adaptability.

We are designing a scheme of practitioner-based regulation within a statutory framework, which will be infinitely preferable to a scheme in which the agency's powers are derived in detail from legislation. I believe that if the amendments were accepted we would move inevitably down that road although, as the hon. Member for Dagenham said, it is a matter of judgment.

The agency's powers have been made strong enough to enable it to impose rules on SROs. I note that Sir Kenneth Berrill accepts that he does not need the powers to initiate prosecutions which were mentioned earlier. He believes that he will have enough powers on prosecutions by working closely with other authorities and with the strengthened provisions for the prevention of fraud. He believes that, because of the agency's powers of authorisation and its ability to satisfy itself that the rules of SROs are stringent and adequate, his powers will be sufficient.

I part company with Opposition Members who see demerit in the fact that the measure is constitutionally innovative. It will meet a new situation and is well judged to do so. It may be a matter of political tempra-ment, but I see such innovation as a positive attraction. A voluntary body working in conjunction with other voluntary bodies to achieve a satisfactory scheme of regulation—no one supposes that the SIB will be a soft touch—will be greatly preferable to the highly bureaucratised and legalistic scheme that would flow from the amendments.
