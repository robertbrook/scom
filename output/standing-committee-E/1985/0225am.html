Mr. Gould: Clause 30 and succeeding clauses take us to the part of the Bill that attempts to give Col. 316 effect to our obligations under the EEC treaties. Broadly speaking, they oblige us not to impose constraints within the EEC on the conduct of business or the carrying out of trade or of a profession, so we are obliged to seek ways of authorising in this country the carrying out of investemnt business by those who are similarly authorised in other EEC countries.

It is widely believed, not just by me, but by others outside the Committee, that clause 30 takes us a good deal further than is required by our obligations under the EEC treaty. Nothing in the amendment is designed to derogate from our treaty obligations, but we feel that by having the provision as wide as it is at present we are unnecessarily creating problems. Indeed, those problems have been described by the Securities and Investments Board as a "major absurdity". I shall explain shortly why SIB holds that view. In broad terms both the stock exchange and the Consumers' Association agree with the objective of this group of amendments. Those bothes do not always agree, and when they do, the Minister and the Committee should take account of their views.

As the clause is drafted, someone authorised to carry out one type of investment business in one EEC country will be authorised to carry out all forms of investment business in this country. That, of course, is subject to certain conditions, such, as that he does not have a permanent place of business in the United Kingdom. I ami sorry to pick on the Greeks in this respect, but one can see why it is a telling example; a Greek insurance broker authorised for the purposes of Greek law as a fit and proper person to carry out insurance business in Greece will, by virtue of that authorisation and by virtue of clause 30 and succeeding provisions, be authorised to carry out all forms of investment business in this country. He can manage investment funds, offer investment advice, run collective investment schemes, and so on, on the basis of that limited authorisation for one purpose and in one place; that authorisation will permit him to carry out the full range of investment business in this country.

Furthermore—to some degree this point is addressed by amendment No. 219—the authorisation that is required to bring a Euro-person—if I may use that term—will be concerned solely with whether he is a fit and proper person.

The authorisation required and identified under clause 30 is imply the equivalence of rules established under chapter III which deals solely with the process of authorisation and the requirement of fitness and properness, and has nothing to do with the range of other rules and regulations which will be made under chapters V and VI, particularly the conduct of business rules.

The authorisation which is all that is required to enable an EEC business man to carry out the full range of investment business is not only limited to> his own country and a particular sort of investment,, but refers to business for which he must be fit and proper. However, there need be no corresponding Image Col. 317 provisions through our own conduct of business rules. Therefore, it is a limited authorisation which is a gateway into a virtually unregulated ability to carry out investment business in this country.

The ability of the SIB or other designated agency to enforce the rules will be limited in this case. Almost by definition, a person who seeks and obtains authorisation under this provision will be substantially out of the jurisdiction so that the capacity of the authorities to enforce conduct of business rules could be limited. Even the provisions on giving notice under clause 31, without which a criminal offence would be committed, will be difficult to enforce if the person concerned stays outside the jurisdiction.

Does any of that matter? It matters because someone who obtains authorisation for these broad purposes by means of going through this narrow gateway may be providing and carrying on a range of investment services that do not match up to the conduct of business rules applied to indigenous operators.

That means that the investor may not be offered the protection mat he has a right to expect under these provisions. In addition, UK operators and business men offering investment advice and carrying out investment business may find that they are subject to unfair competition because they have to compete with people who are not necessarily obliged to meet the requirement laid down by the Bill.

The Minister might object that the amendment would involve us in a conflict with our EEC obligations. I am advised that that is not so, as nothing in the EEC treaties requires us to assume that our EEC partners have the same rules and regulations as us. We are obliged simply to have an equivalence of requirements so that they apply equally to nationals of each EEC country. Nothing in the amendment would cause problems over that.

The Secretary of State or designated agency will have the power to require people authorised in the UK to limit themselves to particular kinds of investment business—clauses 10(3), 15(2) and 45(2Xa) all allow for that. However, under clause 30 that power will not apply to Euro-persons authorised by this route. The amendment is designed to make it possible for the Minister to limit the authorisation obtained under the provision to the particular kind of investment business for which authorisation has been secured in the country of origin.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Trade and Industry (Mr. Michael Howard): Important points have been raised and I hope that the Committee will forgive me if I reply to them at length.

The amendments have a common theme, but treat it rather differently. The theme is that a person authorised by clause 30 should be allowed to carry on in the United Kingdom only the investment business for which he is authorised in the member state in which he is established, which, for convenience, I shall call his home state.

The variation proposed by the hon. Member for Dagenham (Mr. Gould) treats the question directly. Both amendments Nos. 100 and 33 would limit the authorisation conferred by clause 30 to the extent of the person's home state authorisation. If a person authorised by those provisions were to carry on investment business here, outside the scope of his home state authorisation, he would be committing a criminal offence.

Col. 320 The Committee will recall our debate in the first and second sittings about whether authorisation should be limited to specified types of investment business or whether the necessary limitations on an authorised person's activities should be contained in rules. The same arguments that applied in that general case apply in this specific case, and I need not repeat them.

The essential difference between us is whether a criminal offence should be created or whether there should be no criminal offence, but all the civil remethes to which I referred should be available.

But there are three additional reasons why the approach based on rules is right. First, the distinction between different kinds of investment business may be different in other member states from that prevailing here, whether the distinction was made in legislation or in the rules. Under the amendment, a United Kingdom court might have to decide whether, say, a French company carrying on investment business in the United Kingdom was carrying on business of a kind for which it was authorised in France. That would involve the United Kingdom courts in interpreting French law.

Secondly, it would be discriminatory to impose a criminal sanction on persons authorised by clause 30, who strayed over the boundaries of a specific type of investment business, but a civil sanction on directly authorised persons and members of recognised self-regulating organisations and professional bothes. It would also be disproportionate to the end to be achieved, which, as I shall explain later, can be achieved without criminal sanctions. In order to be compatible with the EEC treaty, however, restrictions on the right to provide services must be proportionate and applied without discrimination to all persons operating in the member state in question.

Thirdly, it would be a strange approach to impose criminal sanctions in a case where, by definition, the person has no permanent place of business here, but to rely on civil sanctions for persons established here.

Those difficulties would result from the amendment proposed by the hon. Member for Dagenham.

The variation embothed in amendment No. 145, proposed by my hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Smith), and also in amendments Nos. 101 and 102 when considered in isolation from amendment No. 100, is more subtle. These amendments all concern the notice which a person authorised by virtue of clause 30 is required to submit before he starts to carry on investment business here. The notice has to include information about the investment business which the person proposes to carry on here. The amendments provide that this business would have to be business of a kind which he was authorised to carry on in his home state.

The effect of the amendments would therefore be that a person authorised by virtue of clause 30 would break such rules if, without previously notifying the Secretary of State or designated agency, he carried on investment business of a type for which he was not authorised in his home state. That approach avoids many of the problems attaching to that underlying amendments Nos. 100 and 33, but it would still give the prospect of United Kingdom courts having to interpret the law of other member states, and in any case a person could avoid its intended effect by submitting at the same time as his initial notice a revised plan outlining other types of business he intended to carry on.

I should say that in many ways I sympathise with some of the sentiments underlying all these amendments. If a person is authorised in another member state to sell, say, life insurance, the Bill should not give him carte blanche to come here and sell gold futures. But the Bill already provides two key safeguards against this: the first is that the conduct of business rules will apply to persons authorsed by virtue of clause 30 just as they apply to others. So in the example, the foreign insurance salesman selling gold futures here would be subject to exactly the same rules as everybody else selling gold futures here.

The hon. His amendment would not resolve that difficulty.

The second safeguard lies in the powers of intervention that the Secretary of State or designated agency will possess. A notice submitted under clause 31 will have to contain details not only of the investment business which the person wished to carry on in the United Kindom, but also of his authorisation in his home state. If the business which the person proposed to carry on was different from that for which he was authorised, that could well raise doubts in the mind of the Secretary of State or designated agency as to whether it was desirable in the interests of investors to let him proceed. If not, he or it could issue a restriction of business order under clause 60 prohibiting the person in question from carrying on investment business other than that for which he was authorised in his home state. The person could refer the matter to the tribunal, but the order would stay in force while he did so.

Any breach of the order would attract the same penalties as a breach of the rules under the approach envisaged in amendments Nos. 145, 101 and 102: civil suits by investors, injunctions and restitution orders under clause 56, and termination or suspension of authorisation under clause 32. I believe that these provisions provide sufficient Col. 322 safeguards to meet the concerns that have been expressed, and that the amendments are therefore unnecessary.
