Dr. Marek
: There is much unease within the profession about whether appeals will be organised locally or nationally. My sympathies he with an impartial national system of appeals. However, I recognise that that could create a large bureaucracy, staffed by 20 or 30 people and occupying two or three floors of a multi-storey building in London.

Could we introduce an independent national appeals system that necessitates only three or four extra staff? I believe tha such a system would be preferable, if the applicant is turned down by an FPC sub-committee, the regulations say that the appeal would be considered by an appeal committee, which would be comprised of members from more than one neighbouring pharmacy sub-committee.

Circular HCFD 85.15, states on page 5, paragraph 33, that:

" If in the opinion of the chairman of the Appeal Panel (to whom the appeal will initially be referred) or subsequently of the Appeal Panel itself the notice of appeal discloses no reasonable grounds of appeal or its vexatious or frivolous (e.g. the grounds consist of unsubstantiated allegations against the persons who considered the application) the appeal can be rejected forthwith."

 JPEG 69 Paragraph 34—it is important to put this on record —continues:

" The appeal system exists to ensure that obviously wrong or perverse decisions are changed; it does not exist to overturn without good cause local judgement on the necessity and desirability of any proposed changes in NHS pharmaceutical services. Minor errors in PPSC procedure on the arrival of new information are not decisive; a PPSC's decision should be altered only if the Appeal Panel is satisfied that the errors or the new information are of such magnitude that the original decision cannot be sustained."

 There we have the nub of the argument. The right to appeal should not be limited only to cases where clearly a wrong decision has been made by the original pharmacist sub-committee—it should be given to everyone. There should be a national body to which an appeal could be sent if it did not clearly and unequivocably show that it was not vexatious, capricious and so on, was not based on some narrow principle of law, was not an appeal or interpretation of the regulations, and was not an appeal based, perhaps, (on an interpretation of the original decision. Therefore, if it were not clearly shown that the original decision was completely unreasonable, the appeal could be dismissed immediately. There would be no need for any hearings.

The regulations talk about local appeal committes perhaps having oral hearings and moving from place to place. There would be no need for that. The papers could be put on someone's desk, looked at to find out if there were an apriori case for the appeal, and a decision could then be made about whether there was something clearly and obviously wrong with the original decision.

I would not want that responsibility in the hands of one person because that is dangerous. Indeed, the first sentence of paragraph 33 allows that. It says:

" If in the opinion of the chairman of the Appeal Panel (to whom the appeal will initially be referred) or subsequently of the Appeal Panel itself the notice of appeal discloses no reasonable grounds … the appeal can be rejected forthwith."

 Even if the Minister gives no undertaking to think about this again, and insists on local appeals. I think it is wrong to allow an appeal to be " rejected forthwith " by the chairman. Therefore, I hope that the regulations will be re-written.

That will also apply if a national appeal body is introduced, which is what the amendment seeks to achieve. There ought to be one, two or three people to whom the appeals could be sent and when the chairman is minded to dismiss an appeal because it does not come within the tight terms of reference drawn for the appeal committee, he should pass it to his colleagues and say that unless he hears from them within a week the appeal will be dismissed. I am sure that could be done.

That measure is important not only because Opposition Members tabled the amendment, but because it is one wanted by the industry. It is wanted by the small pharmacists and also by the large retailing pharmacists. I believe that hon. Members on both sides of the Committee have received letters from Underwoods, the British Retailers Association 70 and from the PPSC on behalf of small pharmacists, which desperately want a national committee.

The reason for that is that there is likely to be a " You scratch my back and I'll scratch yours " attitude if such matters are left to the local committee. That is the problem. If appeals are undertaken by committees from surrounding areas, the committee members will know -that one day there will be an appeal for an application in their area and members from the original committee will sit in judgment on their decision. That will be difficult, and with the best will in the world people are only human and may not take an independent and objective view about whether an original decision was one that no reasonable committee could conceivably have reached.

We are not arguing for a massive new bureaucracy, but a small unit that will sift those appeals. If the regulations were seen to be drawn tightly, applicants would know that when a decision had been taken there would be little point in appealing as it would need to be based on stringent criteria.

The appeal committee should be as independent as possible. At present, it consists of three lay members, three pharmacists and an independent chairman. It would be useful if at least one of those pharmacists did not have an NHS contract. He should have no financial involvement in a large undertaking and should not practice in the area for which he sits on the committee. That would improve the appeal committee. The system will work only if the public knows that the committee will do its job dispassionately, independently and without having different vested interests.

I believe that a committee which decides who should be able to open a pharmacy should consist of lay members only. Pharmacists should only advise. However, it is normal practice to have a 50:50 make-up in such committees, with an independent chairman.

We must show the public that the committee's decisions are absolutely above board and that there are no vested interests. If it is thought that there are vested interests, people will appeal and there will be much more of an outcry when those are turned down. If appeals are allowed only on a strict ground, the original decision must be seen to be above board.

We are discussing an important matter. Our amendment may be defective and may not meet the case completely, but the appeals procedure will be detailed in regulations. However, a helpful reply from the Minister would be welcomed.
