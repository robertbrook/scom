Amendment No. 16 touches on a matter that has become tied up with the abolition of the Greater London council.

The 1976 joint working party's report "Time on our side? A Survey of Archaeological Needs in Greater London" recommended that an index of sites and monuments be created for the whole of Greater London. The GLC is working in partnership with the Museum of London, the Passmore-Edwards museum and the Kingston heritage centre to develop a Greater London sites and monuments record, which includes both archaeological sites and historic buildings. Given the history of our town, it is remarkable that Greater London was almost the last county to set up such a record and that the collection of data only began in autumn 1984.

The sites and monuments records—SMRs—are intended to bring together essential information about known and potential sites in a consistent and easily retrievable form. The main aims are to help assess the known and suspected archaeological evidence; to assist in the allocation of priorities and resources for archaeological excavation and recording; to answer inquiries about planning and land management, a point that I shall return to later; to help meet the information needs for research and publication; and to assist in education and leisure, Col. 102 including publicity, exhibitions and the dissemination of information to the general public.

The Historic Buildings and Monuments Commission is likely to regard SMRs as a key element in the proposed enhancement of the number of scheduled ancient monuments nationally.

SMR compilers are employed by the three museums on 12-month contracts and are funded by the Greater London council. Seven staff are working on archaeological data and three on architectural data. There is also a map co-ordinator. Each archaeological compiler covers the area of one unit of the Greater London archaeological service. The department of urban archaeology is collecting its SMR material in the same way.

Compilers start with the units' existing records and then move on to other sources, such as national and local journals, aerial photograph collections, museum records and unpublished research and surveys.

I want to stress that arrangements exist already whereby archaeological interests are fully taken into account.
