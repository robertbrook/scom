Col. 390 Let me set out the very grave reservations that we have about dealing with the affairs of Lloyd's under this Bill, if we do come to the conclusion that major changes at Lloyd's are necessary. These reservations rest on the considerable problems of reconciling two statute-based systems of regulation which would deal with a largely common area from two quite different standpoints, namely the interests of policyholders and the interests of names. These are not merely technical details, but fundamental problems with far-reaching consequences.

The Lloyd's Act 1982 reformed the constitution of Lloyd's and gave very wide powers indeed to Lloyd's and its various governing bodies. These powers serve two key purposes. First, and of paramount interest, they protect the interests of Lloyd's policyholders, by, for example, laying down the capital required by names, providing for assets to be held safely in trust funds to meet claims that might arise, and limiting the amount of business that names can underwrite. That protection under the Lloyd's Act 1982 is buttressed by provisions under the Insurance Companies Acts but policyholders protection is the key responsibility of Lloyd's.

Secondly, the powers under the Lloyd's legislation also regulate the varying interests of the community of Lloyd's, including the people who provide the capital for the insurance business and stand behind the insurance—the underwriting members. Examples of that are the rules on admission and the recent provisions on what are known as preferred syndicates.

It is difficult to distinguish neatly and clearly between those two purposes. It is not a matter of saying that rules about names are for the protection of names and not for the protection of policyholders. That would be obvious nonsense, as it is the assets and resources of names which provide the protection for policyholders, and Lloyd's has detailed rules about those assets and resources.

Sometimes, rules and procedures, such as the monitoring of names' overall underwriting exposure, may serve to protect both names and policyholders. What Lloyd's is regulating, under the totality of its powers, is the conduct of business at Lloyd's in the fullest sense, to protect policyholders and members of Lloyd's.

Furthermore, I am not aware that anyone has suggested that the powers provided by the Lloyd's legislation are inadequate. They are acknowledged to be wide and were updated only four years ago. That is in almost complete contrast to the financial services sector covered by the Bill, where legislation dated back to the days immediately before the second world war, and where many areas were entirely unregulated.

We have, therefore, legislation providing comprehensive powers, vested in Lloyd's governing bodies, enabling them in a consistent and coherent way to protect policyholders and names.
