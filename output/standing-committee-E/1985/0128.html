Mr. Gould
: On a point of order, Sir Michael. I fear that I must at this stage disrupt the atmosphere of harmony that has been established in the Committee.

I raise a point of order, not out of any animus against the Minister, the hon. and learned Member 6 for Folkestone and Hythe (Mr. Howard) personally, or out of any desire to embarrass him, but because we must raise the question of the Minister's interest —which he very properly declared to the House— in a matter of great contention in the Bill.

The problem is ironic and acute because we are dealing with a Bill whose raison d'être is to minimise and deal with conflicts of interest. I do not hold the Minister personally responsible for the problem that we face, but nevertheless the hon. and learned Gentleman appears, from his declaration of interest, to be involved in a conflict of interest of precisely the sort that the Bill seeks to outlaw.

It is common ground that the primary concern of the Bill is conflict of interest. Many of the scandals and frauds in the City which are, in many ways, the genesis of the Bill, involve conflicts of interest, although they are dressed up in other terminology. The problems at Lloyd's for example, have involved conflicts between the interests of the underwriters and those of the investors on whose account they manage funds. The problems of Halliday-Simpson on the stock exchange, of commodity dealers, and the problems caused by the flotation of British Telecom shares, to which I have tried to draw attention, have all involved conflicts between the personal and direct interest of professional people operating in the City and the interests of those on whose account they are supposed to be acting.

Of course, it is not our purpose to suggest that the Minister as a member of Lloyd's, as I understand him to be—he may wish to elucidate his position— is acting in an either potentially or actually fraudulent way, or that there is perhaps even a real conflict of interests. Nevertheless, it is scandalous that the Government should put forward to pilot the Bill a Minister who has a clear personal interest in its subject.

I cannot help but feel that if these matters were being arranged now, and not, as they were, two or three months ago, a somewhat chastened Government might have tried rather harder to avoid the problem. We are still living in the aftermath of a typical piece of Government arrogance: two or three months, or even longer, ago, when the Minister was asked to take on the Bill, the Government thought that they could sweep aside the rules that would ordinarily apply. However, I suspect that in today's climate they would have taken more care over what is clearly an extremely sensitive issue.

It is indisputable that one of the major issues in this Committee stage will be whether Lloyd's should be brought within the ambit of the Bill. I say that, although it is my contention that Lloyd's is already within the ambit of the Bill. The question is whether Lloyd's should be subjected to the regulatory framework created by the Bill. The terms of clause 40 clearly show that Lloyd's is already within the Bill. Clause 40 makes it clear that, but for its specific exclusion, the investment business carried out by the council of Lloyd's or by underwriting members of Lloyd's would fall within the Bill. We must therefore confront the question whether Lloyd's should be within the Bill. On clause stand part, quite JPEG 7 apart from any amendment which might be tabled, we have to say yes or no to the proposition in clause 40, that Lloyd's should be excluded.

It is not the Opposition, in a spirit of mischief, who raise the issue; it is raised for us by the structure of the Bill and by the Minister's interests as a member of Lloyd's. The question whether Lloyd's is to be regulated as at present, under the Lloyd's Act 1982—a private Act and therefore an example of self-regulation—or brought within the regulatory framework of the Bill matters enormously to Lloyd's as an institution as it has made very clear. It therefore also matters to every individual member of Lloyd's. The Minister has very properly declared his interest. He is such a member of Lloyd's and is therefore inescapably affected by the decision that the Committee and, eventually, the House will have to take.

The general principles that are thought to govern such cases—the Minister's predicament is not unique, although it has some unusual features—are well established. They go back to a body of rules which, in their modern form at any rate, were first enunciated by Asquith and were reformulated in 1952 by Sir Winston Churchill. They have since been endorsed by successive Prime Ministers of many Governments and, for the sake of convenience, can be called the Churchill-Butler rules. They were certainly endorsed by Harold Wilson.

I believe that I am in order to quote from those rules, but I shall nevertheless paraphrase them. I make that point simply because they are not the subject of some controversy about their confidential nature. They are on the record and have been published in Hansard. In broad terms, the opening principle of the rules says that a Minister must arrange his affairs so that no conflict between his personal interests and his public duties arises or is seen to arise. That latter phrase is important. We are suggesting not that there is a practical conflict, but that it looks extraordinarily peculiar that, given his interest, the Minister should be dealing with the Bill. As one of the major purposes of the Bill is to ensure that everything both is and appears to be as seemly as it ought, the Minister is bound to take account of the fact that this conflict of interests looks remarkable.

Those principles have been endorsed by a number of Prime Ministers. But we are fortunate—I am not sure that that is the right word—to have further illumination of the rules that might be said to govern the Minister's predicament. My right hon. Friend the Member for Chesterfield (Mr. Benn) recently put in as evidence to the Select Committee on the Treasury and Civil Service a document issued by my right hon. Friend the Member for Cardiff, South and Penarth (Mr. Callaghan) when he was Prime Minister. Although that document was marked " confidential" it has been published by my right hon. Friend the Member for Chesterfield because it has been made availabel to those who re- 8 requested sight of it. In legal terms that would be sufficient publication.

The preamble makes it clear that the body of rules contained in the document has evolved over a long period and has been endorsed by successive Governments, so we are not talking about an innovation made by a relatively recent Prime Minister.

The interesting part of the document is paragraph 76, which specifically refers to the position of Ministers who are names at Lloyd's. It states flatly that no one holding the office of Prime Minister, Chancellor of the Exchequer or Secretary of State for Trade and Industry should be a name at Lloyd's. It then says that there may be other Ministers who should not undertake underwriting business in any Lloyd's syndicate and that there may be particular Ministerial duties with which membership of a Lloyd's syndicate would be inconsistent.

I accept that a distinction is drawn between active underwriting membership of a syndicate and membership of Lloyd's as an institution. However, I suggest that those who framed the rule in paragraph 76 did not necessarily have the niceties of such a distinction in mind, but were simply drawing attention to the inconsistency between a connection with Lloyd's in the form of membership and certain ministerial duties. The paragraph concludes by saying that in all such cases or in any case of doubt the Prime Minister's approval must be obtained.

There is an essential difference between the approaches suggested by the Bill and the amendments. The amendments rely on the criminal law while the Bill relies on the enforcement of rules.

An applicant can be granted direct authorisation if he appears to be a fit and proper person to carry on the investment business described in his application. Under the Bill, the Secretary of State or a designated agency will be able to require him to give notice if he wants to undertake any activities not covered in the application. If the supervisor is not satisfied that the person is competent to carry on the new activities and the person concerned is not prepared voluntarily to drop his plans, the supervisor can use his intervention powers under Chapter VI to stop the person undertaking those activities.

Breach of either such a prohibition or the notification requirement would be grounds for disciplinary 34 action by the supervisor, up to and including withdrawal of authorisation in a serious case. It would also give rise to a right of action by any investor who had suffered loss as a result of the breach of the rule or prohibition. Alternatively, the supervisor could seek an injunction or restitution order from the courts. There is therefore quick and effective action that the supervisor can take to enforce the rules restricting the activities carried on by an authorised business.

Similarly, recognised self-regulating organisations and professional bodies will be required to have, and to enforce, rules restricting the activities carried on by their members. That is the system proposed in the Bill.

The alternative approach contemplated in the amendments is to rely on the criminal law. An investment business could be required to obtain separate authorisation for each of its main types of activity. Alternatively, as proposed in the amendments, it could be authorised to carry on all types of investment business except specified activities. In either case, breach of the restriction would be a criminal offence, and a new authorisation would be required before the business could legitimately undertake any activities even marginally outside the existing authorisation.

Let me explain why we have rejected the approach that relies on the criminal law. First the divisions between the various kinds of investment business inevitably involve an arbitrary element. One type of investment activity may merge with a similar one, as in the case of investment advice and investment management. There are few clear-cut boundaries, and we feel that vague boundaries should not form the basis of the definition of a criminal offence.

Secondly, one has to consider the severity of the penalties involved. If the categories are defined in statute and someone inadvertently strays across an arbitrary boundary, he will become an offender liable to criminal penalties, and the contracts in which he has engaged will be rendered unenforceable, with all the uncertainty that that entails.

In our view, criminal penalties should be reserved for the more serious offence of carrying on invest- 36 ment business without any authorisation. Once a business has been authorised, the more flexible range of penalties for breaches of rules are more appropriate and can be adapted according to the seriousness of the breach.

Thirdly—and most important—enforcement will be quicker and more effective if it relies on disciplinary and similar remedies rather than on the criminal law, with its higher standards of proof, strict evidential rules and the need to go to the courts.

Our final reason for rejecting the approach suggested in the amendments concerns its administrative implications. If a business has to obtain separate authorisation for each of its main types of investment business, unnecessary bureaucracy and form-filling will be involved.

We therefore believe that the approach adopted in the Bill, which relies on the regulator's decision about whether an investment business should be authorised to carry on certain kinds of investment activity is to be preferred to an approach relying on the criminal law.
