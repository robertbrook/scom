FORM AND CONTENT OF PROSPECTUS

Mr. Yeo
: I beg to move amendment No. 335, in page 102, line 17, after "with", insert

"any rules or requirements of any recognised investment exchange or with".

 The amendment would allow compliance with the requirements of any recognised investment exchange to be regarded as compliance with the Secretary of State's rules. For example, the amendment would enable compliance with the requirements of the stock exchange on prospectuses for unlisted securities market companies to be regarded as sufficient if the Secretary of State so wished. The provision could be extended to cover any recognised investment exchange for example, a market on which Eurobonds were traded—thus providing greater flexibility in the more sophisticated markets.

Mr. Howard
: There is much to commend the idea behind the amendment. It is not, in fact, a novelty that the listing requirements of a recognised exchange should have equal validitiy with statutory prospectus requirements. Until the listing legislation came into force on 1 January 1985, the stock exchange had the power under section 39 of the Companies Act 1948 to grant certificates of exemption from the statutory prospectus requirements. Nobody questions the fact that the stock exchange's requirements, whether for the main market or the USM, are considerably higher than those laid down in the Companies Act 1985, which have changed little in half a century.

There is also an important distinction between the listing requirements of an exchange and prospectus law. Admission to a market involves more than the publication of a document. It is the first step into a system of continuous disclosure, while the publication of a prospectus and the offering of securities outside a market is an isolated event, albeit an important one.

It will be best to undertake further consultations before deciding how best to give effect to idea underlying the amendment. My hon. Friend's approach has the great merit of simplicity, but prudence suggests a closer look before concluding that it is sufficient. I ask him not to press the amendment to a Division, on the assurance that I shall reflect and consult further and, if appropriate, table an amendment.

The present rules under sections 428 and 430 of the Companies Act enable an offeror company that has secured acceptances from the holders of at least 90 per cent of the target company's shares—excluding shares held by it or its subsidiary—to buy out the dissentients; 870 they also enable the dissentients to require the offeror company to buy them out once it holds 90 per cent of the shares, including those held by it and its subsidiary.

The principle underlying the provisions is that the rights of dissentients should be adequately safeguarded; that they should be able to resist compulsory acquisition on the terms offered if they have reasonable cause for doing so; and that they should otherwise be treated no less fairly than those shareholders who accepted the offer.

Provisions in the schedule improve the position of dissentients by toughening up the definition of the 90 per cent threshold triggering the offeror's right to buy them out so as to exclude shares held by persons acting in concert, and his associates in general, and not just subsidiary companies. It goes on to alter the timetable governing the process of compulsory acquisition so that dissentients are informed of their rights earlier and allowed to exercise them without undue delay; and enourages dissentients with an arguable case to take it to the courts, by protecting them against orders for costs.

Over and above those provisions, the schedule represents a real attempt to bring the law into line with current practice and to remedy the deficiencies and inadequacies of current provisions.
