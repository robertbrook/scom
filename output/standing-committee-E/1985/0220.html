The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Trade and Industry (Mr. Michael Howard)
: It is, of course, important that investors who deal with members of recognised professional bodies are no less well protected than those who deal with other authorised persons. The special regime for recognised professional bodies is intended to make the best available use of existing regulatory structures, not to provide a soft option. Therefore, the clause provides that a professional body cannot be recognised unless its rules afford to investors at least equivalent protection to that afforded by or under chapter V.

A recognised professional body will have to have rules on the same subjects as a recognised self-regulating organisation. Those rules will be measured against the rules laid down by the Secretary of State or designated agency, and the same equivalence test will be applied as that applied for recognised self-regulating organisations. Some of the Secretary of State's or designated agency's rules, including those on clients' money under clause 50, may apply directly to members of RPBs as well as to members of RSROs. If they do not apply, the RPB will have to have equivalent rules. Therefore, I do not believe that the first half of the amendment of the hon. Member for Great Grimsby (Mr. Mitchell) would add anything to the Bill.

The second part of the amendment deals with a recognised professional body's disciplinary arrangements. They should include a significant independent element. The purpose of subsection (2)(c) is to secure a proper balance between the interests of members of RPBs and those of the public. It will be difficult to satisfy The Secretary of State or designated agency without a significant independent element in the disciplinary arrangements. I shall consider how that requirement for an independent element can be spelt out more clearly.

However, to require the disciplinary procedures to be wholly independent would, in my view, go too far and tip the balance that has to be struck. It would also mean that the disciplinary procedures could not rely on the expertise of those who know the profession best, and it could lead to parallel investigations being made, one by the professional body and one under the independent disciplinary procedures, into closely related breaches of rules.
