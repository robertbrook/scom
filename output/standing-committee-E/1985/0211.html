We cannot legislate to deal with all contingencies, but, by the same token, it is important to devise proper criteria. The phrase "fit and proper person", which I wish to be included by reference to what is contained on page 2 of " Regulation of Investment Business: The New Framework " published in December 1985 by the SIB, could be further defined as "honest, competent and solvent ".  166 I do not wish to spend too long on my argument, but it is important to get this right. Section 3 of the Insolvency Act 1985 deals with the test for a "fit and proper" person. The most important part of the provision is the requirement that in addition to being "fit and proper persons", people who are to be insolvency practitioners must

" meet acceptable requirements as to education and practical training and experience."

 That is, in effect, the competence test that my amendment refers to. It is as relevant to the provision of financial services as it is, for example, to the provision of licensing services under the recently enacted Administration of Justice Act 1985. I served on the Standing Committee of that Bill with at least one of my hon. Friends and perhaps two Opposition Members.

Only people who are "honest, competent and solvent " should be allowed—licensed, controlled or regulated—to provide services, as the SIB also recommended. Moreover, there should be a proper test of competence. If amendment No. 154 were accepted, paragraph 7 of schedule 2 would provide:

" The organisation must be able and willing to promote and maintain high standards of integrity and fair dealing and competence ".

 However reasonable we may seek to be, we must meet the bottom line in the amendment.

It could be argued that the expression "fit and proper persons " already includes the criteria that I mentioned, but it would be as plain as a pikestaff to anyone, lawyer or non-lawyer, that the provisions of the SIB document would not necessarily be imported into the individual rules of each SRO. I am anxious that proper guidelines should be given and that they should be provided by Parliament, not just referred to by the SRO at its own whim, as will happen if the amendment is not accepted.

The constitutional question of what is contained in the memorandum and articles of association, whether limited by guarantee or other arrangement, will be prescribed by the SRO. It is important to get the criteria right in the legislation for the sake of members of the public.

I shall not dwell on Rex v. The High Justices, 1912, No. 1 King's Bench Division, because I should rapidly bore the Committee, but I want to place it on record because it deals with the definition of a "fit and proper" person. It is abundantly clear, as a matter of law, that there is no fixed definition of a "fit and proper" person, which could cause a lot of argument about its meaning. In simple language, I want to ensure that Parliament gets the definition right in the legislation. I see no reason why my amendment should not be accepted, and I intend to press it to a Division.

If that could happen with other SROs, we would find the Establishment resorting to a series of curious devices in order to overcome the difficulties. We do not want that.
