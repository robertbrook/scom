Clause 1
APPLICATION OF FOOD LEGISLATION TO HEALTH AUTHORITIES AND HEALTH SERVICE PREMISES

The Chairman
: Before I call amendment No. 7, I remind the Committee that copies of the financial resolution in respect of the Bill are available in the Room. Adequate notice must be given to amendments. As a general rule, I do not intend to call manuscript or starred amendments.

Mr. Frank Dobson (Holborn and St. Pancras)
: I beg to move amendment No. 7, in page 1, line 14, leave out 'in' and insert 'to extend'.

Clause 1(2) (b) goes to the heart of the proposition announced by the Government when they said they were accepting the disappearance of Crown immunity from hospital kitchens. I believe that nearly everyone welcomed that decision, but under the Bill as drafted, there is a great danger that the good intentions of the Government would be immediately undermined. Subsection (2)(b) seeks to

"make such modifications of the food legislation, in its application to health authorities, as appear to the authority to be appropriate",

 which made me ponder who the authority would be. As you, Sir Philip, were in the Chair, I even wondered whether a quango had been invented for such a role.

However, the next page of the Bill shows that the appropriate authority would be no other than the Ministers. When Ministers introduce a Bill that imposes obligations on them and then take unto themselves the right to set aside those obligations when it appear to be appropriate, we must look more carefully at the proposals.

The amendment seeks to ensure that the modifications that the Ministers might make would only apply in one direction. The amendment would introduce a sort of legislative ratchet in that they could extend the food regulations in respect of hospitals, but could not retract them. We think that that is wholly appropriate.

I do not wish to enter a premature debate on amendments Nos. 8, 9 and 10, but they intend to identify certain shortcomings in the existing food hygiene regulations which will still leave hospital JPEG 7 kitchens subject to various problems. For instance, pigeons in the kitchens of Charing Cross hospital will not be subject to the legislation unless it is specifically extended to include them. While the present, inadequate food hygiene regulations need to be modified, they should be modified only by way of extension rather than by chopping out embarrassing bits that are difficult or expensive to implement.

What will the Minister bear in mind when he decides whether something is or is not appropriate? The Explanatory and Financial Memorandum explains that:

"Clause 1 has no implications for public expenditure."

 We know that money needs to be spent on some hospital kitchens that are a stinking mess, and it is difficult to believe that such expenditure will have no implications for public expenditure.

The only way that such costs would not have implications for public expenditure is if the Minister exercised his powers under subsection (2)(b) and made modifications because the money was not available. That is not in line with the general proposition that the Government claim to have accepted when they said that they were willing to lift Crown immunity from hospital kitchens.

The Minister may say that all sorts of unforeseen difficulties may arise if the legislation is to apply to Health Service kitchens. But unspecified, unforeseen, and apparently unforeseeable difficulties should not be used as an excuse to give the Minister such sweeping powers that will allow him to set aside the main provision in this part of the Bill. What sort of unforeseen difficulties have his advisers suggested justify him taking those powers? If such difficulties are unforeseen and unforeseeable, they are usually dealt with when they arise. That is how such difficulties should be dealt with in this case.

To what extent can clause 1 be applied? It is not clear to me, but that is probably a shortcoming on my part rather than on the part of the parliamentary draftsmen. Can the Minister say whether the lifting of Crown immunity in respect of food hygiene, unmodified by the Minister, applies only to food in hospital kitchens? Will it apply to food when it is brought in to be used in the kitchens? Will it apply to food after it is cooked and when it is taken to a ward or to a nurses' home? Will it apply when food is on the ward and in the nurses' home? Will it apply to food that is left over and uneaten in the ward, the nurses' home or in the surgeons' mess? Will the measure cover all those circumstances or will it apply only to hospital kitchens?

If lifting Crown immunity will apply only to hospital kitchens, substantial applications in the process of new food techniques will not be covered—for example, cook and freeze methods where food may be kept in a freezer which is not in the hospital kitchen. Will the food hygiene legislation apply to that? Will the legislation apply to food that is prepared by one hospital kitchen for a group of hospitals and which is stored off the premises where it is cooked or where it will be eaten?

It is absurd to say that we wish the rigours of the 8 food hygiene regulations to be applied to food while it is being cooked in a kitchen but that once it is on a trolley going to a ward, a staff residence, and so on these rigours will no longer apply. The general idea is for the food to be healthy and not to undermine the health of patients, staff or, presumably, the occasional visitor. Our objective is to ensure that the food is safe.

We want an assurance from the Minister that the unmodified application of the food hygiene regulations in clause 1 will cover all those circumstances. If not, what was trumpeted forth in the House about the regulations will not be wholly true.

The main point of amendment No. 7 is that subsection 2(b) could be applied to undermine and dispense with the admirable decision taken by the Government to apply food hygiene regulations. Opposition Members are not satisfied to leave in the clause the wording,

"make such modifications of the food legislation … as appear to the authority to be appropriate."

 If the DHSS suddenly finds that the health budget is skint, it may find it inappropriate to apply the full rigours of food hygiene legislation.

Is this a general power? Will those modifications apply generally to all National Health Service premises? The present unrestricted powers sought by the Minister will allow him to say, "'Well we notice that it will cost £680,000 to do up the kitchens of Stanley Royd. The Yorkshire health authority don't have the money and so we will modify the application of regulations to Stanley Royd hospital, Wakefield."

A general power is fairly unacceptable, but to leave the Minister with the power to modify the application of the legislation to premises in a certain health district, in one country—Scotland—or those within a health region is more unsatisfactory than the general power. It will be recalled that some of our ancestors went to considerable lengths to minimise the dispensing powers of Governments.

Our amendment seeks to modify the clause to prevent the Government having the power to find something "appropriate"—which usually means something inconvenient—to dispense with the basic proposition of the Bill. We look forward to the Minister's explanation of what he and his colleagues are up to in this respect and, providing we receive solid assurances, we may be prepared to withdraw the amendment.

He also asked whether it would be possible to diminish the impact of the Bill by the use of those powers. It was proper to raise those questions. Indeed, I initially queried why the provision was necessary. I was advised by parliamentary counsel that the clause needs to be included as a prudent contingency provision to cover the possibility that the application of food legislation may not fit the way in which health authorities are constituted. Lawyers, from their vast experience of legislation, have seen that difficulties could arise over implementing a provision to remove Crown immunity from food legislation.

I am advised that the amendment would not have a ratchet effect, as the honourable Member for Holborn and St. Pancras suggests. I agree with the honourable Member for Ipswich that if it were felt that the provision was wholly inappropriate, it would be best to remove the clause from the Bill.

I can give the assurances that the Committee seeks. The Government have no intention to use the provision to evade the spirit and intent of the Bill or to reintroduce Crown immunity by the back door. We could not use that power for a selective operation. It was suggested that we could discriminate and differentiate between the application of the provisions from one area to another. That does not apply. One could not do so. I am advised that the power to modify could be exercised only in relation to health authorities generally. It could not be used selectively.

I have sought to give the strongest possible reassurance about the Government's intentions on the issue. It is clear that the power could be exercised only subject to the affirmative procedure of both Houses. The Government cannot exercise the power by the back door, as it were, not even by using the negative procedure. An affirmative resolution of both Houses would be required if the power were to be used. I can undertake now, if the Committee so wishes, to look at it again. I understand the worries that have been expressed and I want to do all that I can to make it absolutely clear that there is no resiling from what my right honourable Friend the Secretary of State announced following the Stanley Royd committee of inquiry's report and from what I said during the debates on the private Member's Bill introduced by my honourable Friend the Member for Aldridge-Brownhills (Mr. Shepherd). There is no intention to resile from the consensus that was established in the House on the matter. I am prepared to look again to see whether a way can be found of retaining the prudent contingency, so that it is clear that the power cannot be used in the way that has been suspected.

As for whether the measure applies only to hospital kitchens and not to other parts of the hospital, I assure the Committee that the Bill applies to the premises—the hospital and the NHS premises JPEG 13 concerned. Therefore, the regulations would apply to health authorities and hospital premises to exactly the same extent as they would apply to everyone else. The regulations require those who handle food to protect that food from the risk of contamination, and they include requirements about personal cleanliness, the carriage and wrapping of food. Certainly, they are not limited to hospital kitchens. The regulations cover the handling of food at all stages.

The honourable Member for Holborn and St. Pancras asked a fair question. I hope that my explanation will allow him to withdraw the amendment. I assure the Committee that I shall look again to see whether there is any way in which the wording of the provision can be altered to reinforce the undertakings that I have given in this short debate.
