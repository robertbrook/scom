Mr. Howard
: Government amendment No. 274 is a transitional provision, arising out of new clause 5. I shall, therefore, describe the new clause first.

The Insurance Brokers Registration Council was set up under the Insurance Brokers (Registration) Act 1977. The principal provision of the Act is to make it an offence for a person to call himself an insurance broker unless he is registered with the Insurance Brokers Registration Council. Those registered with the council are subject to its code of conduct, and breach of the code leads to disciplinary action.

The Act applies to all insurance broking, that is, to both long-term insurance as an investment and to general insurance, such as motor insurance and insurance of the contents of a house. The Bill will introduce a new regime for the regulation of the broking of long-term insurance as an investment.

The fundamental purpose of new clause 5 is to prevent undesirable dual regulation of the broking of long-term investment insurance, that is, to prevent regulation by both the Insurance Brokers Registration Council and by a self-regulating organisation in the area or by direct authorisation under the Bill. Accordingly, the principal provision of the new clause is to restrict the scope of the Insurance Brokers (Registration) Act 1977 to non-investment business, that is, to mainly general JPEG 939 insurance. That will leave room for the new system of regulation for investment insurance.

The IBRC will continue uniquely to be able to confer the privilege of using the term "insurance broker". In addition—and I return to Government amendment No. 274 to the schedule of transitional provisions—existing brokers who do only investment business will be able to remain on the register and to continue calling themselves insurance brokers.

The IBRC has expressed some worry about the new clause, and it is reflected in the amendments of my hon. Friend the Member for Stafford (Mr. Cash). I am grateful to him for raising the issue of whether the IBRC should continue to regulate life insurance broked by its members as well as general business. The question is complex, and there is no single answer.

I am aware of the worry that the IBRC has expressed about the exclusion of life business from the scope of the Insurance Brokers (Registration) Act and I should be happy to consider further what alternative approach we could devise to meet those worries. It is complex, and we should all hesitate at the prospect of life insurance intermediaries being subject to two systems of regulation, one under the Bill and one under the Insurance Brokers (Registration) Act. Therefore, one needs to ensure that, in meeting the worries of the IBRC, any possible conflict between the two systems and any needless duplication is avoided. On the other hand, if businesses that arrange only life insurance are to be able to join the IBRC and call themselves insurance brokers, one will have to see that that right of access is fair and reasonable, and that it will not call into question the authorisation of businesses under the Bill.

I shall carefully consider what alternative course we can pursue to meet the worry of the IBRC and shall consult it and other interested parties.
