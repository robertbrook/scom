Clause 67
INTERPRETATION

10.30 a.m.

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Dr. Oonagh McDonald
(Thurrock)
: I have only one question on clause 67. It is about time sharing homes, which are excluded from the clause. Will the Minister consider whether it is justified to exclude them all? Some time sharing schemes, such as holiday homes, are in part investment in property and involve an investment element. Will the Minister examine whether all such schemes should be excluded?

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Trade and Industry: (Mr. John Butcher)
: I think that I can address my remarks to the hon. Lady's point. She rightly identifies the fact that time sharing is not primarily an investment product and, accordingly, it would be quite inappropriate to regulate these arrangements under the Bill. However, arrangements of the time sharing kind will be exempt only if their predominant purpose is personal use or enjoyment of the property. If time shares are sold as an investment, they will be caught under these provisions.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 68
RESTRICTIONS ON PROMOTION

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Dr. McDonald
: Again, I have just one brief point to raise. It is covered by Professor Gower in part II of his report, when he casts doubt on the wisdom of subsection (2), which excludes the application of subsection (1) to an advertisement issued

"to an authorised person; or

( b) to a person whose ordinary business involves the acquisition and disposal of property of the same kind as the property, or a substantial part of the property, to which the scheme relates."

 Will the Minister say whether he is entirely sure that such an exclusion is justified? There may be risks involved because, although the advertisement may be issued to "an authorised person" or to

"a person whose ordinary business involves"

 that type of property, such people might sell on suspect schemes to the innocent who were not involved in such business or who were not authorised persons.

Mr. Butcher
: The basic system of regulation of collective investment schemes is found in this clause, which 492 governs the promotion of schemes by authorised persons. In promoting these investments, authorised persons will, of course, also be subject to the general conduct of business rules.

The clause is concerned with schemes, and not with persons. Schemes authorised or recognised under the Bill may be promoted to the general public but, as a general rule, other schemes may be marketed only to other authorised persons or to business investors.

To answer the hon. Lady's question, that means that the Secretary of State or the designated agency will have powers to make regulations allowing authorised persons to promote selectively schemes which are neither authorised nor recognised.

Later, the clause provides for a professional business that may wish to market to another professional business something that is not marketed to the general public. For example, a property company may wish to buy units in a property scheme. That is general practice, and we want to exclude such operations from the rules on marketing schemes to the general public. I hope that I have answered the hon. Lady's question.

Dr. McDonald
: I shall have to think further about the Minister's remarks and possibly return to them on Report.

Question put and agreed to.

"or it satisfies the conditions of any regulations made for the purpose under section 73 below".

 This is a technical and merely enabling amendment. Clause 70 could limit the action that the Secretary of State may wish to take in future with regard to novel forms of unit trusts. The specific kind of unitised trust that I have in mind in tabling the amendment is unitised property. At present, institutions and members of my profession are considering the creation of a unitised property market. A working party has been set up, including members of the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors, Rowe and Pitman, Phillips and Drew and Mercantile House Holdings. Unitised property would enable such institutions, and individuals, to take a share in large buildings, which they could not otherwise afford. A market could be established, which would deal adequately with that type of investment.

The interesting concept of unitisation could lead to a new form of investment, which would be available to institutional and private investors alike. It could do much to open up new opportunities for property-based investment. However, it is still in its early days and much needs to be done to make the idea on the drawing board a reality.

A crucial element, as the supporters of unitisation readily acknowlege, would be the formation of a new market on which units in individual properties would be traded, in the same way as shares in companies are traded on the stock exchange.

I sympathise with the intention behind the amendment, which is to ensure that the criteria for authorising unit trust schemes, as laid down in clause 70, do not prevent the Secretary of State or the designated agency form authorising a single property unit trust. 494 However, my hon. Friend's amendment would commit me to operating within the framework of a system devised for the regulation of authorised collective investment schemes and, with the conventional form of unit trust in mind, we should be wiser to avoid that.

Conventional schemes provide investors with the opportunity to invest in a professionally managed portfolio of assets, which spreads risk and enables the investor to redeem his units at a price closely related to net asset value. The unitisation schemes that are being worked up envisage single unit trusts as an entirely different sort of creature, which would work differently.

Single property unit trusts should not be dealt with in a system of regulation designed for a different type of investment product. That would not ensure the proper protection of investors. Many of the safeguards for investors in single property schemes would not be provided by the schemes themselves, but by the availability of a secondary market on which investors could trade units. We must look closely at the implications of those major differences and, if necessary, adapt the Bill to take account of them.

: I apologise to you, Sir Michael, for not being in the room when my amendments to the clause were reached. I do not feel strongly about those amendments, but I shall rehearse the arguments for them.

My amendments would allow any self-regulating organisations, professional bodies, investment exchanges, or clearing houses that had not been recognised under the legislation to appeal against the refusal to recognise them.

My hon. and learned Friend the Minister may argue that there is no need for such a provision, as such bodies could make representations and individuals could pursue other routes to authorisation, either through another SRO or through direct authorisation from the board. Mr. Howard
: I hope that my hon. Friend the Member for Stratford-on-Avon (Mr. Howarth) will forgive me, if I deal first with the point that my hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Smith) raised. There is a natural transition from his point to the point made by my hon. Friend the Member for Stratford-on-Avon.

The purpose of the tribunal is to provide a safeguard against the use by the Secretary of State or the designated agency of their executive powers against an authorised business or individual. Those powers can directly affect the ability of those involved to earn a living through investment business.

The decision to refuse or revoke a recognition order would not have the same effect on people's livelihoods. If a self-regulating organisation or a professional body were not recognised, businesses could seek authorisation from another SRO or professional body. In all cases authorisation could be sought from the Secretary of State or the designated agency. Therefore, the need to safeguard against loss of livelihood does not arise on refusal or revocation of a recognition order.

Nevertheless, such revocation raises worries for those involved, so there are procedural safeguards. The Secretary of State or designated agency could not, except in an emergency, revoke recognition without giving those concerned the opportunity to make representations.

Such decisions require the assessment of many complex and wide-ranging criteria, so they are best left to those who are continually involved in regulation. The procedures before revocation and the availability of judicial review of a decision to refuse or revoke recognition provide adequate safeguards.

The distinction can be summarised thus: the decisions on matters such as revocation that are left to the Secretary of State or the designated agency are in the nature of the exercise of regulatory powers, which should be in the hands of a regulatory body; the decisions that the tribunal will have to make are analagous to judicial powers, as they will affect the fate of individuals. That is why we have not given a right of appeal 502 to the tribunal in the cases described by my hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield.

The same logic answers the point that my hon. Friend the Member for Stratford-on-Avon made. The Bill does not enable an appeal to be made to the tribunal against the direct, ruling power. That power itself is not yet in the Bill, but we have undertaken to consider it in the light of the views that Committee members expressed in the debate on clause 12. We have yet to decide what is the best way of providing that power and the safeguards that ought to be associated with it.

For those reasons, it is perhaps unlikely that we shall decide to give the tribunal a role here. The tribunal is better with questions affecting individuals than with purely regulatory functions which are for the Secretary of State or designated agency, with an ultimate right of appeal to the courts.
