Mr. Frank Dobson (Holborn and St. Pancras): I beg to move amendment No. 38, in page 6, line 19, at end insert

" and only if the proposed adjustment of remuneration does not involve reopening closed negotiations.".

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to discuss the following amendments:

No. 50, in page 6, line 19, at end insert

" provided that such different information was not and could not have been available and known to the determining authority at the time of the earlier determination ".

No. 42, in page 6, line 25, at end insert—

"'different information" means only such supplementary information relating directly to the information on which the earlier determination was made and which was available to the determining authority at the date of the earlier determination but to which the determining authority did not have regard.".

Mr. Dobson: I do not wish to detain the Committee long and I hope that the Minister will assist me by accepting our amendments. They are intended to ensure, first, that, where there have been negotiations about a settlement with the professions, the negotiations will not be reopened as from the start in the light of new information, because we think that that is wrong and, secondly, that only changed circumstances which are germane to the contract will be considered.

As we read the clause, it would permit Ministers to adjust remuneration in a way that amounted to the total reopening of negotiations which everybody thought were closed and satisfactory. We accept, as I think the professions do, that it is not improper, in intention anyway, to have an adjustment at the end of the period of a negotiated arrangement, but it would be improper for the Government to wish to reopen the negotiations on the basis of changed circumstances that were not wholly relevant. As far as we can see, a shift in the price of the dollar, of seed corn in Guatemala or shrimps in Bangladesh could be used by the Government as a reason for reopening the negotiations on glasses, which we do not think is a sound way of going about things.

We also do not believe, as should be clear from amendment No. 50, that, where the Government might reasonably have predicted before the nego- Col. 98 tiations finished that something would happen, they should suddenly remember those factors and benefit from them. If something was known to the Government when the previous negotiations closed or was reasonably predictable, the Government should not be able to drag that in at a later stage, saying "Oh dear, somebody has just found a file. We want to knock a few quid off, so we have thought of this, that and the other."

In short, we believe that it is right and proper, if there are changes in factors which are directly relevant to the negotiations and which could not reasonably have been foreseen, for the Government to seek to make an adjustment at the end of the period, but not in any other circumstances. We do not want the Government to come back for a second bite at a cherry which they knew about in the first place; we do not want them pleading totally irrelevant bits of information; and we do not want them reopening the negotiations.

Subject to my usual caveat about the inadequacy of our drafting amendments—we do not necessarily expect the Government to accept these amendments as worded; they may be faulty and may not achieve what we are seeking to achieve—we hope that the Government will accept the principle behind them and be prepared to limit the very wide scope which the Bill would give them.
