Mr. Ashdown
: The argument here is much the same as my argument on amendments to clause 2. The purpose of these amendments is to ensure a debate in the House should the Minister wish to extend the exemptions under the Bill.

We have argued long and hard about the exemption of Lloyd's and other bodies. Exemption from the Bill is a privilege not lightly to be conferred. The negative procedure is inadequate because it can be used to prevent the House from debating the issues fully.

Why does the Minister wish to allow exemption on the negative procedure? It may be an adequate procedure for making a further exemption of a purely 430 technical nature, such as those granted under clause 42, but how does he know that some future Administration would not misuse the power to add exemptions on a broader scale? What kinds of exemptions does the Minister have in mind? Can the clause be tightened up so that such exemptions granted under the negative procedure are of a purely technical nature? This is a matter of principle. We cannot allow the granting of exemptions to the Bill without the proper, full parliamentary procedures.

Mr. Butcher
: In legislation in which exemptions are delineated in one clause, there is normally another clause dealing with the removal of exemptions. We have incorporated within the Bill a constitutional safeguard which will allow the Government of the day to choose different options to suit different circumstances. If we had not included clause 42 we might have prejudged some fundamental issues, but, in addition, we must take into account some of the technical matters hinted at by the hon. Member for Yeovil (Mr. Ashdown).

As my hon. and learned Friend the Parliamentary Under-Secretary said when the hon. Member for Yeovil proposed a similar amendment to clause 2, Parliament should be able to consider any proposals to alter the Bill's scope—in this case, by exempting people from its provisions or removing exemptions conferred under it. Where the effect of an order is to remove an exemption conferred by chapter IV, the Bill's scope will be extended to affect different people. That sort of extension should be made only on a positive decision of Parliament, and the Bill so provides.

As the hon. Member for Yeovil rightly inferred from the Bill, different considerations apply in the case of additional exemptions, which may be purely technical. For example, one of the statutes specified in clause 42 could be repealed or amended so that someone other than the person specified in clause 42 could carry out the statutory functions described. In such a case—as I am sure the hon. Member for Yeovil would agree—the affirmative resolution procedure would be inappropriate.

Where a proposed exemption seriously affected the rights of investors, the order could be considered by Parliament, and, if Parliament disagreed with it, annulled. However, such consideration would not be necessary or desirable in every case.

The hon. Member for Yeovil made a good libertarian, as opposed to Liberal, point when he said that a future Administration—he may mean the current one because it will be the future Administration—might take advantage of the powers given in clause 43. Under the negative resolution procedure it is easy for the Opposition to pray against the order in the usual way.

Clause 43 gives the House the opportunity to decide what is and what is not important, and to use its procedures to execute its traditional constitutional duties. To an extent, the debate that we are having must take place in many Standing Committees, but, having examined the issue as the hon. Member for Yeovil would wish the Government to examine it, I feel that the constitutional safeguards that he values are maintained. However, the clause allows us to avoid wasting the House's time by having to use the mandatory affirmative resolution procedure on a purely technical detail.
