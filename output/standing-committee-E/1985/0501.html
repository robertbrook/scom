National Health Service (Amendment) Bill

Clause 1
APPLICATION OF FOOD LEGISLATION TO HEALTH AUTHORITIES AND HEALTH SERVICE PREMISES

Of course no one can deny that money must sometimes be laid out to improve working practices, but if a health inspector says that a cooker, for example, is out of date and no longer hygienic, the matter can hardly be rectified by improving the working practice; the cooker must be replaced. The honourable Gentleman may be suggesting that the hospital board should see whether the Salvation Army can provide one that can be installed for nothing, but I do not think that the health inspector would view that as the right way to deal with the problem. I think that he would say, "You will have to spend some money and buy the best modern cooker", which is what one expects in a hospital.

I accept that some improvements in working practices are needed, but so is there a need for 34 capital expenditure. If the honourable Gentleman thinks back to what I said, he will recall that I was objecting to the Government stating in the explanatory and financial memorandum that clause 1

"has no implications for public expenditure".

I do not see how that can be the case. First, there are financial implications for health boards and health authorities in terms of the money that they will have to spend in improving some of their hospitals' kitchens. Certainly, in Scotland, this is not a matter merely of hospital kitchens and working practice; nurses' homes should be included as well. If three or four nurses' flats are served by one kitchen, which may be only a poky little hole in the wall, presumably money would need to be spent to improve it. In Scotland, the small canteen facilities in health centres would be included, because they are part and parcel of health board property, which I gather they are not in England and Wales. Money will be involved from the health boards' point of view.

Money will also be involved from the local authority point of view, and there will be manpower implications. My other objection is that the Bill has no manpower implications. In my own local authority—Glasgow district council—the environmental health department will have to carry out regular inspections of the major hospitals, the teaching hospitals—Glasgow university has a medical school and many of the large hospitals in Glasgow are teaching hospitals for the university— the smaller hospitals, health board accommodation, nurses' homes and health centres. All of them in the area will need to be inspected.

Do the Government believe that that burden of extra work can be achieved without any extra manpower and that not one extra person will be put on the payroll to carry out that work? I cannot accept that. How can a local authority be expected to carry out that work without taking on extra manpower? Local authorities will have to spend money employing extra people, so the Bill is bound to have financial as well as manpower implications for local authorities.

I hope that the Bill has manpower and financial implications for local authorities as I wish to see food regulations applied to all Health Service institutions. However, if money is not spent on improving kitchens or on extra staff, we are wasting our time. There is no point in our discussing a Bill that will be unenforceable, once enacted. Unfortunately, we tend to spend too much of our time passing laws that cannot be enforced. I hope that the Bill will not fall into that category and that it will be properly applied.

I hope also that the Minister will define the phrase:

"premises used by a health authority"

The clause does not specify premises "owned by" a health authority. For example, the Greater Glasgow health board may wish to rent some council flats from Glasgow district council for the use of doctors or nurses, who will live in them rent free. Will such premises fall within the terms of the clause? Of course, if they were merely privately rented flats, JPEG 35 they would not be covered by public authority food regulations. However, the clause implies that a food inspector can say to a nurse living in one of those flats, "Sorry, you are not keeping your kitchen tidy enough". Perhaps the Minister could spend a little time explaining the term "used by" a health authority.

I hope that the Minister will consider that point in relation to Scotland. Scotland has a unitary system of health control and the health board controls both primary and secondary health care. That does not apply in England and Wales.

I believe that the principal ethos which drives the present Administration is that they do not want to spend public money unless absolutely forced to do so by public opinion. Moreover, the Government were forced in this instance. I do not tar every Conservative Member with the same brush, and I am aware that many do not hold the view which I have outlined, but the Government do. When a Conservative Back Bench Member brought forward a Bill that would lift Crown immunity, the Government were spurred into action. The Government asked themselves, "What can we do? Can Crown immunity be lifted so that the Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974, the Public Health 36 Acts and the Food Acts apply to NHS hospitals? Oh dear me, no. That would cost far too much money. We don't want to spend money on the public. We want to keep that money for our supporters." Therefore, the Government proposed a compromise.

Clause 1 does not do even the barest minimum. It does not do enough. It makes an attempt but it is only a fig leaf to cover NHS legislation. The clause provides that only the Food Acts will apply to the kitchen and food preparation areas of hospitals, and that is not good enough. It is pathetic and abysmal but it is better than nothing. One must be grateful for crumbs from the table of this Government, with their huge majority. It is better to have a crumb than nothing, however, so I shall not vote against the clause. But it is important that the public realise exactly where this measure can be placed among a panoply of measures that the Government could have brought forward if they had so wished.

In our debate on Tuesday, I argued that simply to try to keep pest infestation from the kitchens is impossible unless hygiene regulations are applied throughout NHS premises. Cockroaches, feral cats and mice do not recognise boundaries. They do not agree to remain in the kitchen. Nor do they say, "If you eradicate us from the kitchen, we shall not enter it from outside." Such a suggestion is ridiculous. Pests will stay in the ducting system and in the wards and it will be ineffective simply to try to apply the food regulations and their cleanliness aspect only in the kitchens.

Did the Minister reply to my argument? The answer to that is no. He merely said something about the innappropriateness of the amendment and expressed sorrow that other amendments had not been selected. Of those that were selected, he said:

"but they would not have any effect on the Bill except to introduce some rather superfluous wording."— [Official Report, Standing Committee E, 29 April 1986; c.23.]

I find the Minister's argument dismal. Surely, he recognises that there is a serious problem of vermin infestation in our hospitals, that the clause deals with it only partially and that its provisions will not cure the problem. He could have said that he would consider the matter further and, perhaps, give more powers to environmental health officers so that our hospitals can be rid completely of vermin infestation. But he did not do so. He hid behind the technicality of the amendment by saying that it was defective and that some amendments that relied on it were not selected.
