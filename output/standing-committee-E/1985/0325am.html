The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Trade and Industry (Mr. Michael Howard)
: I should like to say a few words about the point raised last week by my hon. Friend the Member for Suffolk, South (Mr. Yeo) on which I have been reflecting. [Laughter.] It is a most important point, well worthy of reflection, and I am surprised that the Committee should consider it as a matter for anything other than congratulation.

I said previously that it would be unreasonable and damaging to our markets to forbid authorised United Kingdom businesses from transacting any bargain where they could not ascertain the ultimate principal. There are many countries where shares are not registered and where law and custom take it for granted that the beneficial ownership of the company is unknown. An overseas bank may, with the best will in the world, be unable to discover the beneficial owners of the company for which it is acting, but that does not mean that the company is a front for wrongdoers.

The vast majority of companies formed in countries such as Switzerland and Belgium are formed by nationals of those countries for the pursuit of legitimate commercial objectives. If we refuse to do business with them unless their Governments, in effect, adopt British company law, we may frustrate a few insider dealers, but we shall also risk driving away millions of pounds of legitimate business. However, a different situation obtains where suspicion of insider dealing already exists, and where transactions in the weeks preceding an announcement are being looked into and disclosure is sought to identify a possible offender. I agree entirely with my hon. Friend the Member for Suffolk, South that in that situation pressure should be applied to the fullest extent that proper limits of UK jurisdiction allow to obtain disclosure of the true principles.

Such pressure could take the following forms. First, it could prohibit authorised United Kingdom firms from undertaking any further business with an offshore intermediary or counter party who refused to disclose JPEG 903 the principals substantively behind the suspect transaction. Secondly, it could impose sanctions up to and including deauthorisation if the recalcitrant intermediary was authorised to carry on investment business in the United Kingdom. Such measures should be taken only with due regard to the possible limits to the knowledge of the persons from whom disclosure was sought. For example, it would be unreasonable to penalise a Swiss bank which was reasonably dealing when it knew no more of the Swiss company for which it had acted than that it was a wholly owned subsidiary of another company registered in the Netherlands Antilles, but it might be reasonable to penalise it when it could have known and chose not to.

Finally, there is increasing recognition in the financially important countries that insider dealing is growing and there is readiness to collaborate in repressing it. The Government are playing an active part in following these efforts. Clause 149(6) is designed to enable the United Kingdom to disclose information in pursuance of any arrangements that result. I shall consider what additions to the Bill may be necessary to enable the pressures that I have mentioned to be exerted. Clause 60 already provides restriction orders and would allow prohibition to be placed on further dealings with specified persons; otherwise, it is a matter of ensuring that the designated agency and the self-regulating organisations have appropriate rules.

Clause 150
OCCUPATIONAL PENSION SCHEMES

Mr. Yeo
: I beg to move amendment No. 290, in page 113, line 41, at end add—

"(3) Any person who engages solely in activities falling within subsection (3)(a) below shall not be treated for the purposes of this Act as carrying on investment business.


Activities carried out in the capacity of a trustee (other than a pensioner trustee) of a small self-administered pension scheme being a pension scheme in which the Board of Inland Revenue has required the appointment of a pensioner trustee.


For the purpose of this subsection, a pensioner trustee is a person or body who satisfies such conditions as the Board of Inland Revenue shall from time to time determine in connection with the approval under the Finance Act 1970 of self-administered pension schemes with fewer than twelve members.".

The amendment recognises the difficulty that the clause may create for small, self-administered pension schemes. Such schemes normally have fewer than 12 members, and frequently only one or two. The members are the shareholders and the directors of the company that establishes the scheme.

I declare an interest, as I was, and still may be, a beneficiary of some such scheme, set up long before I began my parliamentary career.

The members of a scheme are the main influence on its investment policy, and the companies are usually family businesses or businesses with a small number of shareholders. There are several thousand such schemes.

The superannuation funds office of the Inland Revenue insists that such schemes should have a pensioneer trustee, which can be a corporate trustee. Pensioneer trustees are widely involved in pension matters in a professional capacity and they have to be approved by the superannuation funds office. Therefore, all the small, self-administered pension schemes have, in the form of a pensioneer trustee, an expert among their trustees, but they differ from large pension schemes in that the members are, in effect, investing their own money on their own behalf.

Clause 150 would require all the trustees, not just the pensioneer trustees, to be authorised persons. That would be a cumbersome process, involving a tremendous number of applications from people who do not wish to carry out investment business in any other role than as a trustee of a small, self-administered pension scheme. That process is especially unnecessary as each scheme has a pensioneer trustee who will be authorised.

Mr. Howard
: It may help if I explain briefly why the clause is in the Bill. The Government believe that JPEG 911 members of a pension scheme are, in effect, investors in that scheme and that they deserve the protection that the Bill will extend to investors. Many pension schemes, both personal and occupational, would be covered by the Bill without the clause. The clause ensures that members of in-house managed schemes—that is, schemes in which the assets are managed either by trustees or by an employee of the company concerned—are also entitled to the protection that the Bill affords.

I have received a number of representations that suggest that the clause is not quite right. I am considering those representations carefully and will bring forward whatever amendments may be necessary.

I agree with my hon. Friend the Member for Suffolk, South (Mr. Yeo) that small, self-adminstered pension schemes should not be discouraged. I also agree that, where such a scheme had a pensioneer trustee who manages the scheme's assets, the desired object could probably be achieved by requiring only the pensioneer trustee to be authorised. However, although many pensioneer trustees offer a professional management service, not all do and they are not required to do so. Their only formal duty is to prevent the winding up of a scheme in circumstances that would abuse the tax advantages that it enjoys. It is therefore quite possible that some or all of the non-pensioneer trustees of a small, self-administered scheme could themselves manage the scheme's assets. I am not convinced that in such a case it would be right to exclude the non-pensioneer trustees from the need to be authorised. However, we are considering carefully the scope of the clause and I will bear my hon. Friend's arguments in mind.
