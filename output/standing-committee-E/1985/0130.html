The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Trade and Industry (Mr. Michael Howard)
: When we adjourned on Tuesday, we were discussing whether the powers in clauses 59 and 60, which enable the Secretary of State or the designated agency to restrict businesses from engaging in certain kinds of activity, should have the immediate backing of the criminal law.

There are two important points underlying our view that that would be unnecessary and undesirable. First, criminal offences should be defined clearly and precisely. Vagueness about the boundaries of a criminal offence should, as far as possible, be avoided. Secondly, criminal offences should not be created unless they are clearly necessary. The amendments would create criminal offences which do not meet either of those tests. As I explained on Tuesday, the boundaries between different types of investment business are inevitably arbitrary and it could be easy unwittingly to stray across the boundary between what was permitted and what was not.

No one has yet demonstrated why a criminal offence is needed here. It has not been suggested 44 that the system of rules and the civil and disciplinary sanctions that the Bill provides for breach of such rules are inadequate. The rules will require an authorised person to keep to the activities that he is considered fit to undertake, but there will be effective discipline and civil sanction if he nevertheless undertakes other activities.

As the hon. Member for Dagenham (Mr. Gould) pointed out, the civil and disciplinary sanctions will be underpinned by the criminal law in that, if a person transgresses the rules seriously enough, he will lose his authorisation and will be committing a criminal offence if he then continues to carry on investment business. But that criminal offence will be the straightforward one of carrying on business without authorisation. We believe that that reliance on civil and disciplinary sanctions, with the criminal sanction only as the ultimate back-up, is the best way of achieving that objeotive, which all Committee members agree that they share.

The hon. Member for Yeovil (Mr. Ashdown) has suggested that the Bill should nevertheless give the Secretary of State a reserve power to impose restrictions with criminal penalties for breaching them. He has not given any examples of situations in which such a power might be needed. He appears to have advocated that power on the basis of " You never can tell; it might come in handy sometime." That is not an adequate basis for taking a power with a criminal sanction.

Hon. Members have also raised a number of other matters, including the problems associated with overlapping self-regulating organisations and the absence of a power for a designated agency to fine authorised business. We can discuss those matters more fully when we come to the relevant provisions in the Bill.
