I shall not rehearse our earlier arguments; we have already stressed the need to broaden our discussion. At present, one body represents a major section of London, which is ILEA. The hon. Member for Welwyn Hatfield (Mr. Murphy) argued that there were other groupings outside the Inner London boroughs that were connected with the museum. At that time, everyone recognised that the Government's proposal for the abolition of the GLC was not related to a certain body. There were many areas to which that decision was not specifically related, but which had to be dealt with because problems arose,

The previous Minister, Lord Gowrie, said that he recognised that the GLC was not abolished in order to safeguard the arts but that any problems that arose must be dealt with. We are trying to do that. The hon. Member for Welwyn Hatfield said that a problem existed. A variety of people from many areas use the museum—he mentioned tourists, too. He said that he 68 was not proposing that they should be represented, but that if people from outside London used the museum, why should they not be represented by another body. In other words, appointments cannot be made from a representative body because other communities of sections are around the perimeter.

If we follow the logic of that argument, it means having autocratic appointments only. That is wrong. We should attempt to make the appointing body as representative as possible. As my hon. Friend the Member for Newham, North-West (Mr. Banks) said, the GLC did not make appointments that were in line only with the political complexion of the appointing body, but at least it had the sense and strength of being representative. The hon. Member for Welwyn Hatfield would not have complained about non-representation if the responsibility were left to the GLC; he would have said that it was a sensible perimeter of representation. But that has been abolished. We have lost a body which at least would have appointed a third of the governors.

I am not making a political point. I am trying to deal with a situation that has been caused by the Government who, to save costs and because they dislike Ken Livingston, decided to abolish the GLC.

There is a good case for the ILEA. It is the most representative educational body within the perimeter of London. The Museum of London is not a gallery as is the National Gallery. The museum has educational connotations. Even the National Gallery recognises its own education functions. It is not merely aesthetic. The Museum of London has a specific education function. It is related to the past and the present and to the social, political, and aesthetic history and to the fashion history of London. That is closely integrated with education.

The amendment would further the case for both the democratic accountability and educational function of the museum. It would be a gesture towards alleviating the anxieties, criticisms and bitterness that many people have about the Government's decision. The Bill is not a good example of the consequences of the GLC's abolition, and if even moderate suggestions are rejected, it shows that the Government have entirely ignored the consequences of that abolition. For all those reasons, I hope that the Minister and the hon. Member for Welwyn Hatfield will support the amendment.

Mr. Tony Banks
(Newham, North-West)
: I apologise for missing the last part of Tuesday's sitting. I have read the Hansard report very carefully and I should like to take up some points made by the Minister.

The amendment seeks to prevent the City and the Government from acquiring an increasing share of governors on the museum board. We believe that the ILEA should have a right to nominate governors to the board, because, after the abolition of the GLC, the ILEA will be the largest democratically elected body in London.

There is a certain element of dejá vu in this because the ILEA covers precisely the same area as the old JPEG 69 London county council. I declare an abiding interest in the LCC. My hon. Friend the Member for Newham, South (Mr. Spearing) taught in an LCC school. I was educated in LCC primary and secondary schools, and I like to think that I benefited from that education. Those who attended such schools had a great deal of loyalty to the LCC and those who taught in those schools.

If the ILEA had the right to nominate members to the board of governors, it would restore some of the links that used to exist with the London county council.

It is also worth while noting that the problem need not have arisen twice. The LCC was abolished by a Conservative Government in 1964 when they set up the Greater London council. The Conservative party is well known for setting up local government bodies and then demolishing them. The GLC was set up so that the Tories of the day could obtain political control of the wider body. However, their attempt to gerrymander local authority boundaries to secure that control did not work because some Tory districts did not wish to be included in the London area. Instead of the new GLC in 1964 being Tory, it was Labour.

The present Secretary of State for Education and Science was the prime mover behind the local government reorganisation in 1964 which set up the GLC. I am glad that the issue has come back to haunt him because he deserves it.

The Minister said last Tuesday that representation should be linked to those who paid the costs, and there is some superficial appeal in that argument. However, that is not always the case. I cite the example of the Lea Valley park authority. The GLC provides 80 per cent, of the revenue of that authority, but 50 per cent, of the representation on the governing body comes from district councils outside Greater London because they have a close interest in what happens in the Lea Valley area. It was decided that because of that close interest, their representation should be disproportionate to the amount of money they contributed.

The Minister's desire to maintain a close link between revenue contributions and nominating rights can be challenged on the grounds of past investment. It causes unhappiness to Labour Members, especially to my colleagues and me in County Hall, that the investment we made on behalf of London ratepayers—for example, for Kenwood, Rangers House in Blackheath and Marble Hill House, for which we provided capital revenue to acquire artefacts, works of art, and so on—is being taken away from the ratepayers and given to a quango. That quango could, no doubt, dispose of the assets in those historic houses if it so wished. That is wrong, because ratepayers' money was used to provide those works of art.

I do not know why the Government believe that public assets are second rate and private assets are almost sacrosanct. If a future Labour Government renationalised without compensation—I have always maintained that that should be done, although I believe that my colleagues do not support that principle—there would be cries about the state grabbing and 70 taking away private property. However, conversely, if the state hands over over public assets, not a whimper comes from Fleet street and hardly a word of objection is raised within the Conservative party. There is a peculiar attitude towards public assets versus private assets.

It could be argued that Inner London ratepayers who are now represented by the new Inner London education authority, have past investment in the Museum of London. That should be recognised. Whatever else has been said, as the ILEA represents the largest concentration of London ratepayers together in one democratically-elected body, surely Conservative Members must recognise that it is appropriate for the ILEA to have nominating rights to the Museum of London's board of governors.

The Minister's second argument on Tuesday was that the Museum of London was not primarily an educational institution. I do not believe that the Museum of London is any more or any less an educational institution than the Geffrye and the Horniman museums, which are now to be transferred to the Inner London education authority. The Minister said that the Museum of London had strong links with the adult education authority and that that was why the Ministry's arrangements would be different. I remind the Minister that that was not the Government's original position. The original proposals for the disposition of the assets of the Greater London council were for the Geffrye and the Horniman museums to be given to other museums. As I recall it, the Geffrye was to go to the Victoria and Albert museum and the Horniman to the British museum.

I am sure that, being sensible, people will try to operate the chaos being willed upon the government of London by the Government and will try to make things work after the abolition of the GLC. People in this country have the capacity to muddle through despite all the odds, but we make much of the muddle ourselves. The other main reason for our desire to achieve the amendment relates to my first argument. We do not want the City of London to have further nominating rights to the museum's board of governors. The argument is a democratic one. We do not believe that the City of London is the most appropriate body to represent London local government.

I said on Tuesday that I had to leave early because I was to lunch with the Lord Mayor of London It was a delightful lunch. Of course, the Lord Mayor was as kind, gracious and hospitable as ever. I should like to think that there is a great deal of affection between me, as chairman of the Greater London council and him, as the Lord Mayor of London. So, anything that I now say about the City has nothing to do with my personal feelings for the Lord Mayor and the Lady Mayoress.

 This amendment may be a little more probing than the previous one but I may be able to anticipate what the Secretary of State will say.

It is common knowledge that disentangling the manifold powers of the Greater London council has been more difficult than the prime Minister ever dreamt when she decided to include that issue in the conservative party manifesto. Some arguments about who shall do what have still not been properly resolved—for example, collection and disposal of rubbish, maintenance of roads and ferries, and so on. The London Residuary Body that was set up under the 1985 Act was given responsibility for the important jobs of paying pensions, looking at investments, and other matters central to the former statutory responsibilities of the GLC.

In addition, section 95 of that Act establishes a co-ordinating committee of successor councils—the existing London boroughs and the Common Council of the city corporation. The co-ordinating committee must be set up before 1 September 1985 to

"consult and co-operate, as respects matters affecting those councils JPEG 75 or their areas, with the new authorities, the residuary bodies and any other body to which functions or property of the Greater London Council or the council of that county will be transferred by virtue of any provision made by or under this Act and with the staff commission."
