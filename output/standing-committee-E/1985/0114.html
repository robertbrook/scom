Museum of London Bill

Clause 1
COMPOSITION OF BOARD OF GOVERNORS

Amendment proposed [10 December], No. 1, in page 1, line 9, leave out "cease to be" and insert "be reappointed as".— [Mr. Buchan.]

Mr. Mark Fisher
(Stoke-on-Trent, Central)
: In moving the amendment before the Christmas recess, my hon. Friend the Member for Paisley, South (Mr. Buchan) raised several interesting questions that the Government should answer. When replying to the debate, I hope that the Minister will tell us why he seeks a change in the board of governors. He must establish that need for a change and convince his hon. Friends of it—he will certainly have a job convincing Opposition Members. I presume that the hon. Gentleman's advice is to resist the amendment and that he will recommend his hon. Friends to vote against it.

Mr. Fisher

The Minister must convince all Committee members that there is a need for change. If he believes that there is, presumably he has some criticism of the individuals presently serving on the board of governors, the way in which they are selected, or the groupings which they represent. Unless he has criticisms of them as individuals or as representa- 34 tives, it seems strange and perverse for the Government to seek to change the museum's board of governors purely wilfully and without any just cause. Therefore, Opposition Members expect the Minister to give some coherent and convincing reasons why he sees it necessary to seek that change.

We should be especially grateful if the Minister addressed himself to the present make-up of the board, because it contains some extremely interesting people who have given good service to the museum. If he has criticisms, the Committee is the proper place for him to bring them forward. It will be interesting to know whether he has criticisms of the board and why he believes that the changes envisaged by the Bill will improve the quality of the government of the museum. Unless he can convince us that the quality of governorship of the museum will improve, there seems little point in introducing the changes.

I managed to obtain a list of the membership of the board of governors. The membership is wide-ranging and seems distinguished, with some members possessing skills and experience which surely must be recognised by all members of the Committee as contributing greatly to the judgment and consideration of matters affecting the museum when the board of governors meets.

As a general example, I cite the fact that Sir Ashley Bramall—the present chairman of the staff committee—is on the board. I am sure that leaving politics aside hon. Members will recognise the enormous contribution that Sir Ashley has made for many years—presently as chairman of ILEA and throughout his career—to education in London. Surely, all members of the Committee would believe it to be an impoverishment of the board of governor's quality if someone with the educational experience, especially in terms of London, of Sir Ashley were not on the board to ensure that the way in which the museum developed embraced the full understanding of the educational needs of museums and the way in which museums can and do educate young people about the history, culture and a range of past and present activities of our capital city.

I am sure that there can be no criticism of such people as Sir Ashley. The Minister might say that some of the appointments have been politically motivated. Opposition Ministers probably would recognise that that was true and consider that to be a good reason for appointing governors. I am sure that the Opposition and the Government have a difference of opinion on that issue. We stress that political appointments made by the Conservative party — some GLC appointees on the board of governors are Conservative members of the GLC and have been appointed by the Labour controlled GLC — and by the Labour party have a valuable part to play. Moreover, there should be intelligent and reasoned political dialogue about the nature of London and its history. It would be foolish, pointless and wrong to try to say that the presentation and interpretation of the history of London by the Museum of London was non-political. It is not. JPEG 35 How one interprets history is a highly-charged political issue, and it is fair and proper that the full range of political attitudes towards the history of London should be presented in the museum, both in its choice of archives and in its presentation of permanent and other exhibitions.

The Opposition would stress the political nature of history. It makes a positive contribution, not solely in a partisan sense but in terms of our belief that politics plays an important and crucial part in history, especially in the interpretation and presentation of that history. Therefore, it is impossible logically to present a non-political, objective view of history. By its very nature, the presentation of history is subjective. I believe that the range of subjective attitudes towards history should be reflected on the board of governors.

For that reason it is important that such people as John Gorman, who is an extremely distinguished Labour-Socialist historian, has published such works as "Images of Socialism" and is a design artist of great distinction, represent a particular political viewpoint on the board of governors and contribute in that way, just as there is room within that presentation of history for a more academic and objective approach towards history.

Returning to the amendment, there is room on the board of governors not only for partisan and strongly-held political views but for more academic and slightly more objective views on London and its history. Dr. Valerie Pearl is the president of New Hall, Cambridge and an extremely distinguished historian, especially in the history of London. She has been a governor for seven or eight years and is valuable to the board of governors because of her ability and experience. Members of the Committee may be aware of her works, such as "London and the Outbreak of the Puritan Revolution" and her studies in London history. It is vital that historians of her range and special interest in London are kept on the board of governors.

There are others with perhaps less historical or political involvement in London but who have a wide understanding of the way of life in London at present. I refer to Simon Jenkins, who is the political 36 editor of The Economist and a former editor of the London Evening Standard in which he wrote a column about living in London, which some members of the Committee will recall. It was a well-researched, amusing and wide-ranging column that was enjoyed by people living in London. Recently, he wrote a companion guide to outer London. Although my hon. Friends may not agree with Mr. Jenkins' political views, as expressed when he was editor of the Evening Standard—seldom has that paper had sympathy for the Labour party—we recognise that such people, with their knowledge and proven interest in London, have an enormous contribution to make.

If the Minister tries to persuade the Committee that the make-up of the board of governors should change, he must tell us why people such as Dr Pearl, John Gorman and Sir Ashley Bramall, the latter two being Labour sympathisers, should not continue to make their excellent contributions to that board.

If the Minister changes the present structure of the board of governors, he should make clear what criteria will be used for future appointments. People in London need reassurance that there will be a balanced board of governors which reflects the needs and interests of everyone and all cultural activity both past and present. It is important that the Minister justifies his reasons for changing the board of governors and clarifies the basis on which he will formulate that change.

The Minister has a difficult task ahead of him in convincing fair-minded hon Members that the present board of governors has not done a good job, and that it cannot and should not continue to do that good job.
