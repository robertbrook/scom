Mr. Gould: I beg to move amendment No. 43, in page 140, line 22, leave out from 'State' to end of line 23. The amendment illustrates the difficulties identified in our point of order.

Schedule 5 deals with the composition of a" designated agency on the assumption that there may be many candidates for the role. The schedule lays down ground rules for the composition of such a designated agency, which might one day catch the eye of the Secretary of State, and be made the subject of a designating order.

The reality is that we know that at least for the time being, we are not discussing any old designated agency, but the Securities and Investments Board. What we do not know is whether SIB will remain a private company limited by guarantee or whether, as a consequence of further amendments, new clauses and new schedules it will become a body set up under the Bill. Therefore we are discussing schedule 5 in limbo, as we do not know what sort of body we are contemplating.

However, in the light of your ruling, Mr. Lamond, and of the Minister's reply to the Committee, we are obliged to go through the motions of examining schedule 5 on an undefined basis. I assume that we are discussing some form of SIB which is, for the time being, a private company limited by guarantee, but which must comply with the provisions of schedule 5, yet it is the only body that could be required to do so. One sees immediately the difficulties, doubts and uncertainties that face us.

The schedule provides that members of the governing body of the designated agency should be appointed—and dismissed—jointly by the Secretary of State and the governor of the Bank of England. My amendment seeks to remove the role played by the Bank of England from that provision. The amendment deals with a precise point and has little to do with the broader issues that we have been debating. However, I think that the Committee should take a view on it.

The purpose of the provision is to try to ensure that there is some co-ordination of views by the Secretary of State and by the governor of the Bank of England on how the SIB should operate. The two supervisory agencies should not be working at cross purposes. I have some sympathy with that aim. But the coincidence of the two supervisory agencies is likely to be assured more by practical considerations than by any artificial provision.

The Secretary of State or the SIB will be administering the Bill's provisions across the whole range of financial services. At the same time, and over a similar area, the Bank of England will be supervising all institutions which provide banking services. The City of London will in future be composed largely of sizeable financial conglomerates, many of which will be offering banking services. And the Bank of England has made it clear that where it supervises an institution offering banking services, it Col. 712 would be prudent—in the light of Johnson Matthey, no doubt—to supervise the institution's related activities as well.

That could result in the very duality of supervision across the City which schedule 5 is presumably designed to implement. In other words, the provision in schedule 5 will play little part in affecting what is to happen anyway. It may be desirable or it may not, but most financial institutions in the City will be subjected to at least two separate forms of supervision, administered on the one hand by the Secretary of State or the SIB and on the other hand by the governor of the Bank of England.

If that is so, we do not need the provision; but there are also other objections. It is a curious attempt to harness two horses which may not always wish to run in the same direction. There may be confusion and difficulties; there may be room, not for harmony, but for disharmony, if the two agencies are compelled to agree. There is no provision for which agency's decision should pre vial if they cannot agree. While I am sure that the Secretary of State or Sir Kenneth Berrill and the governor of the Bank of England will always seek to agree, that cannot be guaranteed. Any disagreement would be a recipe for confusion.

The Bank of England has already shown its limitations as a supervisory authority. We need recall only the difficulties that it faced in deciding what to do about Johnson Matthey. More important were the difficulties that it encountered in supervising and monitoring activities at Johnson Matthey before the problem was made public. We cannot say, from what we know of the Bank of England's record as a supervisory body, that we have any confidence in injecting it into this area as well. The Minister would have to show what the Bank of England could bring to the supervisory function that is not already provided by the Secretary of State or the SIB. Underlying the doubts and difficulties about the Bank of England as a supervisory authority is a third and more important objection.

By its very nature the Bank of England should not be a supervisor here. There is a very good reason which was confirmed to me only yesterday when I discussed the matter with some eminently placed people in the City. The Bank of England is a major client and customer of the City, especially in sales of gilts, the management of the whole gilts market and in financing the Government's borrowing requirements. The Bank is a major player and customer and is in the wrong stance and attitude to be a supervisor at the same time.

It was exactly that kind of difficulty that got the Bank into trouble over Johnson Matthey. The Bank was trying to fulfill two roles as a central bank and a lender of last resort to the banking system and as the prudential supervisor of the banking system. Because the Bank of England could not resolve those two separate and distinct responsibilities it got into difficulty. When it was finally faced with the mess it felt obliged as a central bank with a loyalty to the banking system to pick up the pieces at Johnson Matthey whereas an effective regulatory system would have required such incompetence, not to say Image Col. 713 fraud, to be rewarded or punished by fate. That would have happened if the Bank of England had been simply a supervisor.

I fear that that duality of responsibility disqualifies the Bank of England from fulfilling a supervisory role in financial services. The Bank is both client and supervisor and it cannot be expected to fulfil both roles objectively and properly at the same time.

When Professor Gower surveyed the possible supervisory agencies in part I of his report he naturally placed most weight on the Department of Trade and Industry as he then envisaged it and on the self-standing commission. He also made a passing glance at the Bank of England as a possible candidate and I was relieved that he did not pursue that line.

My reaction was that the Bank of England was simply the wrong institution. Although it falls outside the competence of the Committee, I would go further and say that the distinction between the Bank of England's various roles should disqualify it from a supervisory role in the services covered by the Bill and that disqualification should be carried through into the banking sphere. We should be looking at a separation of the roles of central bank and supervisory authority, as the Americans have, but I concede that that remark goes rather wide of the debate.

An objection to removing the Bank of England from its role of jointly appointing, and therefore dismissing, members of the governing body of SIB, is that it is feared that appointments to SIB would become political matters under the control of Ministers. That may or not be the case, but the problem arises with many appointments made by Secretaries of State to public positions. I cannot believe that it is sufficiently strong ground for wiping away the whole structure of appointments which applies across the board in virtually every other industry.

It may be argued by the Minister and by other hon. Members that the fear that appointments may become a political football is a fundamental objection to removing from the Bank of England its power to make appointments. If so, we have gone further down the road of political appointments under the Government than many of us feared. The Minister may be better placed than we to understand how far the Government are prepared to use political preference to make such appointments. If the Minister makes that argument he will reveal more than he intends.

I hope that no reliance will be placed on that argument and that the Minister will try to deal with the objections to the proposal that I outlined.
