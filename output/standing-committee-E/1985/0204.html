The Chairman
: At the last sitting of the Committee the hon. Member for Yeovil (Mr. Ashdown) asked me to reconsider my ruling, in connection with amendment No. 48, that liabilities are not investments. The hon. Member pointed out that in clause 1 "investment" is defined as meaning

"any asset, right or interest".

 I have given further thought to this matter, but I can find no grounds for changing my ruling. A liability as such cannot be regarded as an investment, or as a right or an interest. A Lloyd's underwriter could, of course, be held to have an investment in the profits of his syndicate. There is a conceptual difference here between the two. For this reason, the hon. Member's amendment No. 97 is also out of order. I am sorry to have to give the hon. Member that information but, as promised, I have announced my decision at the beginning of the sitting.

Mr. Bryan Gould
(Dagenham)
: It may help the Committee if I restate briefly the nub of my argument in support of the amendment.

I believe that I had made it clear that the Opposition were entirely in favour of employee shareholding and recognised the advantages of providing a mechanism by which that could be facilitated.

However, I sought to persuade the Committee that, for perhaps three reasons, it would be unfortunate to leave employees in a share shop without recourse to independent advice. First, those employees were likely to be among the least experienced of investors. Secondly, they were operating in a context where they were likely to be at their most vulnerable, because it would be a familiar context and they would be meeting other workmates and perhaps superiors. Thirdly, it was a context in which there would be a great possibility of insider information being available to some of those trading. For those reasons, it seemed to me that special account had to be taken of the need to provide independent advice.

The Minister made a number of points in his reply. First, he said that the Bill was not primarily —indeed, at all—intended to regulate ordinary transactions between private individuals. Of course, I accept that. But the purpose of setting up an employee share shop is to make that not an ordinary transaction; it is to provide, in a sense, a form of market place, the mere provision of which would, in ordinary terms, be regarded as conducting an investment business. That is the reason for the precise exclusion in paragraph 19.

The Minister cannot simply take refuge in the notion that those are transactions between two private shareholders. It is a special situation which the Minister is quite rightly anxious to encourage. I am equally anxious to ensure that that is nevertheless properly regulated.

The Minister's second point concerned the requirement that independent advice should be provided. He said that that would add to the cost of the operation and would, therefore, be a disincentive to those who wanted to set up such a share shop. My rejoinder was, as I recall it, that all forms of protection for the investor and regulation of markets cost something. If we were simply to accept the cost argument, we would never have set out on the course in the Bill. I am not, therefore, persuaded by that argument.

Otherwise, again, we would not have embarked on the course set out in the Bill.
