The drafting of the Bill is such that even lawyers well experienced in construction law have difficulty in understanding its meaning and significance. A number of peers complained of its complexity and obscurity, including Lord Denning, Lord Wilberforce and Lord Edmund-Davies.

    Of particular concern are the complex provisions in new sections 14A(3) to 14A(9) which refer to the starting date for the three-year limitation period. The date depends on the results of a succession of tests that have to be applied to determine when the plaintiff first had sufficient knowledge of the facts to bring his action. The interpretation of these provisions and their application in practice will present great difficulty, and recourse to the courts is likely to be necessary on this account, let alone on account of the claim itself. These tests are based on those for personal injury contained in section 14 of the Limitation Act 1980 and it is not clear why this should be considered suitable for latent defects in the very different area of construction work or for the work of solicitors or accountants.

    The question of the commencement date is of importance for the practical running of a business so that people know when they should date their papers. As someone who practises in the engineering contract industry, I am concerned that there may be difficulty in keeping sufficient records and having access to them. The burden on the industry will be considerable. At the moment people keep their records for six years. To be asked to keep records for 15 years will impose a massive constraint on an industry because most of the people will have moved to other occupations before 16 years is up, which will make it very difficult to look back.

    Under the present law the date of accrual of a cause of action is when the damage occurs, whether the plaintiff was aware of it or not. This remains unchanged, and the six-year period within which an action must be brought runs from that date. Where the damage is latent, however, the plaintiff is also entitled to have three years in which to bring the action from the date when he is deemed to have knowledge of certain facts or material relevant to his claim according to the tests prescribed. The tests for determining this date are complex and uncertain and likely to give rise to difficulty. In some instances, it will also be necessary to determine the date of accrual. This may also pose 6 difficulties, but it is not clear just what "damage" means in this context.

    Let us take the example of a crack in concrete. Is the relevant date when this first happens but is invisible to the naked eye, when it can be seen and measured; or when it is of such magnitude that damp can penetrate and cause corrosion to the reinforcement steel within the concrete?

The first difficulty is that the date will have to be determined about 15 years after the alleged act or omission, by which time memories will have faded and the people involved and the records and other information may no longer be available.

That is normally recorded as a matter of course in the form of a certificate, usually signed by the supervising architect or engineer. That would give a certain date which could be determined immediately from routine records. As Lord Hacking and others pointed out, other countries faced with the same problem use the completion date. As I understand it, within the EEC the accepted period is 10 years.
