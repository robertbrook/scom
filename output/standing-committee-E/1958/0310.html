Coming to the more radical Amendment of the hon. Gentleman, he is right in his prophecy that I am reluctant to accept the powers embarrassingly proposed for me, because I am satisfied, on a careful consideration of this matter, and having regard both to experience of the past, where we have had central scrutiny and to the measured recommendations of the Royal Commission, that it would not be appropriate to substitute central scrutiny for hospital level scrutiny as proposed in the Bill.

Perhaps I may explain this to the Committee by seeing first of all what the Col. 386 examination consists of, and what the objects are, because there are two objects of the examination of documents. The first object is to ensure that the documents are technically correct and that they provide good documentary authority for the detention. The second object is to ensure that the documents accurately describe the condition of the patient.

The first object is achieved by a clerical check of the documents, and the second object requires a medical check of the documents. The clerical check will ensure, for example, that the application is filled in with all the statutory particulars described under Clauses 25 to 29 which we have been discussing: that no essential part of the document is left blank; that they are signed by people who appear to be qualified in the way that they are supposed to be, and that the dates are correct—for example, that the medical examinations are not more than seven days apart, as required by Clause 28 (1). All that is a clerical examination.

On the other hand, the medical examination concerns the description of the patient according to the category of mental disorder as defined earlier in the Bill, the particulars in support which are required to be made by regulations under Clause 26 (2, a), and the reasons in support of the opinion that detention is necessary in the interests of the patient, which are required under Clause 26 (2, b). Those are a medical and not a clerical form of examination. The medical object of the examination can, of course, be achieved only if it is done by a doctor who has himself seen and examined the patient, and that is the root of this matter. Scrutiny by people who have not seen the patient can fulfil only the first or clerical object, and cannot fulfil the second or medical object.

The Royal Commission was very definite about this matter and devoted a lot of attention to it in paragraphs 747 to 762 of its Report. It said this at paragraph 749:

"No routine scrutiny of the documents alone by a person who has not examined the patient can provide any check on their accuracy nor any safeguard against the deliberate submission of false documents or against errors of information or of diagnosis. In so far as the central scrutiny of documents may have come to be regarded as a safeguard for the patient from this point of view, we should make it clear that this is quite illusory."

Image Col. 387 Then it goes on at paragraph 751:

"In our view, it should be made clear in future that the primary responsibility for ensuring that there is proper documentary authority for the use of compulsory powers lies with those who use those powers and who would be responsible if they were improperly used, that is to say, the hospital authorities and local health authorities …"

It reaches its conclusion at paragraph 760 in these words:

"The present system of submitting all documents for routine central scrutiny is unnecessary and should be abandoned."

The same point is made under sub-paragraph (g) of paragraph 761 in the summary of conclusions.

The hon. Gentleman pointed to the experience of the Board of Control in this matter. Of course, it has great experience, and it said at paragraph 99 of its evidence to the Royal Commission:

"The number of documents received by the Board of Control each week is shown in Appendix E."

They are very voluminous indeed, as the hon. Gentleman will recall. It went on:

"It is doubtful whether the time spent in scrutinising and filing these documents is justified by the value of the scrutiny which, as we have suggested, may be more apparent than real."

As I have pointed out, that is because the medical value of the examination depends upon its being done by a doctor who has seen the patient. All that does not, I hasten to say, for a moment mean that scrutiny is not an important aspect of this mental health procedure and that it should not be done by people best able to secure the two objects that I have defined. It is certainly possible to give central guidance from the Ministry as contemplated by the Royal Commission, and we would certainly have that in mind. The documents will also be available to the tribunal on an application to it and in regard to discharge.

Therefore, the conclusion to which I think the logic of the matter drives us is that it would be wrong to transfer responsibility for the scrutiny of these documents from the hospital authorities, where it lies under the Bill, to the Minister, where the hon. Gentleman seeks to put it. Both the objects of the scrutiny—the clerical and the medical objects, which, I say again, are very important—can be satisfactorily achieved and, I believe, only satisfactorily achieved Col. 388 on the basis as prescribed in the Bill, fortified by the central guidance from the Ministry where proper and required. For that reason I would recommend the Committee to leave the matter as it stands, and not to accept the Amendment.
