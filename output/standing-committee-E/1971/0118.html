Mr. Anthony Crosland: We resume our discussion on Amendments Nos. 73 and 74, which are the major Amendments relating to the surplus. In practice, the major debate upon the principle of the surplus appears to be taking place upon these two Amendments Col. 570 rather than upon the Question, That the Schedule stand part of the Bill.

The surplus is built up as a result of all the changes in subsidies, rebates and allowances, so we must see the total picture of all three if we are to see how it is accumulated and what its effect is. I begin by quoting from an article in the Local Government Chronicle of 7th January, by its Financial Editor, Mr. George Forster. He says:

"Proceedings on the Housing Finance Bill resemble a British rather than a tropical dawn: the light comes slowly, illumination is gradual, and not always distinct."

That seems to be a major understatement:

"It is fair neither to Parliament nor to local authority advisers that forecasts of financial effects have not been made available, at the outset and in convenient form, of a measure of such complexity and magnitude. The Bill should have been accompanied by a White Paper."

That echoes what we on this side have said repeatedly since the start of our debates. However, in the absence of a White Paper, we must do our best to summarise the Bill's arithmetic as we painfully drag the information out of the Minister at sitting after sitting. Only so can we assess how the surplus is built up and what will be the consequences.

I start with housing subsidies strictly—not with subsidies related to need, poverty, or the maintenance of incomes, but to those which go to help the housing programme. I take the year 1975–76, which throughout our debates has been taken as the mean year, and, where the Minister has given brackets, I take the middle figure. In 1975–76, the total of housing subsidies is £100 million, as shown in c. 115 of the Official Report of our debates. It is made up of the residual subsidy of £5 million, the transition subsidy of £10 million, the operational deficit subsidy of £15 million, the slum clearance subsidy of £15 million—incidentally, a much smaller figure than one would have imagined from the extravagant claims made for this subsidy in Ministerial speeches—and the rising costs subsidy of £55 million.

As the Financial Memorandum tells us, the figure of £100 million may decline still further after 1975–76. and we know that some of these subsidies will disappear in succeeding years since some are purely transitional. Even the others will probably be abated as rent income Image Col. 571 and local authority surpluses grow. Therefore, the figure of £100 million overstates the position as it is likely to be in the years after 1975–76.

We therefore have £100 million of subsidies going to housing, as opposed to need, in 1975–76. In order to compare this with the present position, let us ask ourselves what today's position is.

In 1970–71—the latest figures we have—total Government housing subsidies were £157 million. As we are frequently reminded, 10 per cent. of these went to cover the cost of rebates and the remaining total of about £140 million represented the strict housing subsidies. Therefore, we have a likely figure of £100 million in 1975–76 compared with £140 million even today, though, with a house building programme for the public sector of any decent and reasonable size, the present figure of £140 million would have risen sharply.

Under the Bill, we shall have a housing subsidy of £100 million in 1975–76, as compared with a figure much greater than £140 million had the present system of subsidies continued. As a result, we have a cut of £40 million even on the present figure, and a much larger cut on the figure which would have been achieved had we continued with the present system.

It is, of course, here that the Minister will achieve by far the greater part of the £200 million cut in subsidies as compared with what would have been paid under the present system. In c. 84 of the Official Report he quoted the figure of £200 million, and it has been repeated more than once since. After all, he cannot achieve his £200 million saving out of the rebates and allowances part of the subsidy bill since, with fair rents as high as they will be, we shall have to spend a good deal more public money on rebates, allowances, supplementary benefit payments and so on. The greater part of the £200 million will therefore come from a cut in the building subsidy part of the total subsidy bill.

What will the consequences be? The first and most obvious will be a transfer of up to £200 million from the council housing sector to the national taxpayer and, as a consequence of this transfer, there will, I believe, be a further decline in public sector house building. Indeed, Col. 572 the White Paper on Public Expenditure, to which we have referred more than once in our debates, makes it clear that in 1975–76 there will be a decline in public sector house building even in relation to the figure for 1970–71, which both sides of the Committee agreed was too low.

It is worth noting that the most recent figures produced by the Department of the Environment show that in the third quarter of last year starts in the public sector were down 18½ per cent. on the third quarter of the preceding year. They were the lowest that I can trace since 1961. Starts in the public sector of housing are now lower than at any time for over a decade. That is the background against which we are discussing what will be a drastic cut in Government help to the council house sector.

The first effect of Part I, leading up to the surplus in Schedule 1, will therefore be a drastic cut in subsidies, a large redistribution of income from council tenants to the national taxpayer and, consequently, a fall in public sector house building when we desperately need a rise.

I turn now from housing subsidies—subsidies to aid building—to those intended to relieve poverty and need and to maintain income. Again, I take 1975–76, and middle figures where a bracket has been given by the Minister. Most of my figures have been taken from cc. 430 and 458 of our debates before Christmas.

The total payments made for the relief of need under fair rents are enormous, because fair rents are so high. We shall have Supplementary Benefit Commission payments of £80 million in 1975–76, we shall have private rent allowance payments of £40 million, and we shall have rent rebate payments of £260 million, of which—and we cleared up this point earlier—some £70 million will be taken over from the Supplementary Benefit Commission. The total payment for the relief of poverty or need or maintenance of income is £380 million—a gigantic total, due to the fact that fair rents are so high.

Who pays? Who is responsible for meeting the national problem of millions of tenants whose income is too low to afford fair rents? The Supplementary Benefits Commission will pay the £80 million to which I have referred. The rent allowance subsidy will cover the bulk of the £40 million going to private rent allowances. The rate rebate subsidy will cover £120 million. This adds up to a Image Col. 573 total of £240 million to set against the £380 million, which will be the total of rebate, allowance and supplementary benefit payments. The £240 million roughly corresponds to the Minister's figure of £200-£250 million out of the total in that year which will go for the relief of need and the maintenance of income.

On top of the Government payment of this £240 million in these ways, rates will cover a further £40 million, making a total of £280 million from public funds, as compared with the payments which will have to be made of £380 million. In other words, we are left with a gap of £100 million, and this is what will be paid by the council tenants out of their fair rents. Council tenants will be taking over from the national taxpayers £100 million of the costs of relieving low incomes.

It is this situation which has created the fury in the local authority world. I quote again—it is a recent quotation, and there have been many others—from the Local Government Chronicle of 7th January:

"No wonder that it is here, and in connection with rent allowances, that the local authority associations are most dissatisfied. To shift burdens from the Exchequer in respect of assistance to low-inoome tenants, on to the shoulders of other local authority tenants appears to be indefensible…'

Let us see where we have got to now as we lead up to the surplus described in Schedule 1. The council tenants have paid their ordinary taxes, like everybody else. They have paid their ordinary rates, like everybody else. They have saved the taxpayer up to £200 million on housing subsidies, and over most of the country they are paying the full cost of their own council housing. They have contributed £100 million to help their poorer fellow citizens—first, the poorer citizens in council houses and then the poorer tenants in private houses.

But we have not finished yet. Over much of the country, on top of all that, they will contribute a surplus, which the Minister has put at some £30 million in 1975–76, although it is worth noting that at this time we shall begin to see the Government's policies unfolding. If this Government were to stay in power, this year would be far from being the end of the road: this £30 million surplus would grow rapidly in the years after 1975–76.

Of that surplus, part goes to relieving Col. 574 the national taxpayer, once again—in other words, we should take £15 million off the figures which I have been given for the cost of subsidies from the Government—and the other part goes to relieving the ratepayer and paying for benefits of concern to the whole local community, because even the half that is paid back to the local authority is not paid back to the housing revenue account but to the general rate fund, thereby relieving the ratepayer of expenditure which strictly should fall on the whole community and not just on the body of council tenants in that area.

This is a fantastic turnabout from the situation that most people imagine exists in council housing today. Far from the council tenant being universally subsidised, as many people wrongly imagine he is today, the better off of my council tenants in Grimsby, for example, after paying the full historic cost of the council housing in Grimsby, will pay for the rent rebate to their less well off neighbours and then rent allowance to private rented tenants in Grimsby. On top of that, the Government have now introduced, for the first time in our history, the concept of a landlord's profit into public sector housing. We are now to have a strict landlord profit in council housing.

The result of this will be that the council tenant will produce this landlord's profit, part of which goes to relieve the local rates—in other words, we have here a system of double rating only of the council tenant—and the other half goes to the national Exchequer to relieve the national taxpayer. There is a form of double taxation on the council tenant. This is really what the surplus which we are discussing in Schedule 1 amounts to. For the first time in our social history we have the imposition of double rating and double taxation for the council tenant, and the amounts will go far beyond the figures which we are discussing for 1975–76.

In passing, I should say that this is an extraordinary contrast with the position of the owner-occupier who, far from paying a double tax—that is to say, his ordinary taxes and another housing tax—is actually receiving, with the agreement of both sides of the Committee, a universal subsidy from the Exchequer.

How does the Minister justify this remarkable new principle which is being Image Col. 575 introduced into council housing and which has never been properly discussed in this Committee? Sometimes he says that that is not really happening at all. He argues that because fair rents will be lower than market rents the council tenant is still being subsidised—an argument, incidentally, that he does not use about the owner-occupier when he talks about tax relief. But, of course, ignoring that, his argument will not stand up, because over much of the country the scarcity element in fair rents will not be of any significance; it will be of significance only in the areas of greatest stress. Therefore, over the greater part of the country, geographically, fair rents and market rents will be broadly the same, and this alleged element of subsidy will not exist.

Then the Minister produces an even more remarkable justification for this state of affairs which appears in paragraph 64 of the White Paper:

"The Exchequer will relieve ratepayers of most of any deficits in the Housing Revenue Account and initially of the whole cost of rent allowances. The Government therefore considers that the Exchequer should share in any surplus arising in that Account".

It is really the most monstrously dishonest argument to try to give the impression that the Government are now giving so much additional help to council housing that they deserve a right in the surplus at a time when the burden on the rates is to remain broadly unchanged, according to the Financial Memorandum, at a time when Government subsidies are to be cut by anything up to £200 million as compared with what they would otherwise have been, and at a time when the sole relief to deficits on the housing revenue account will come from council tenants in the form of higher rents—in other words, when the Government are drastically cutting their help and commitment to council housing.

To say then that they are morally entitled to share in a surplus to which they have contributed nothing and in which they have no rights, a surplus which is solely due to the imposition of a much higher and highly inequitable level of council rents, is a highly hypocritical argument. It is interesting to think what this argument would lead to if it were applied to the whole sector of owner-occupation; if the Government were to argue that the total tax relief which they give to the owner-occupier justifies them in some- Col. 576 how sharing in the surplus that the owner-oecupier receives, but naturally we do not hear anything about that.

The Minister says that this whole new system, including the surplus, is justified by the massive redistribution of aid towards the areas of greatest need which is allegedly taking place under the Bill. But, unfortunately, one of the outstanding and depressing features of all the debates we have had so far on the Bill has been that we have scarcely been able to find a single area outside inner London which will gain significantly from this alleged redistribution of help to the areas of greatest need. Again and again we have been given figures from Manchester, Liverpool, Birmingham, Swansea and other places suggesting that this redistribution is largely a myth and that no substantial extra gain will go to any area, whatever its housing needs, outside inner London.

It is true that we have found a great deal of redistribution that will go on under the Bill. It is, as my hon. Friend the Member for Swansea, East (Mr. Mc-Bride) has frequently reminded us, redistribution from low wage, low rent areas to more prosperous high rate areas; from areas with a large stock of rented housing to areas with a much lower stock and, proportionately, much more owner occupation, and, above all, from the council tenant to the national taxpayer. However, what all these forms of redistribution have in common is that they are all in the wrong direction.

None of these justifications is the real reason why this concept of a surplus and the whole concept of fair rents and the change in subsidy is being introduced. The real motives are to improve the competitive position of private housing and to reduce the part played by public sector housing. This came out perfectly clearly in the notorious speech of the Secretary of State to the Housing Research Centre conference in 1969 when he said:

"The ratio of council housing is much too high at the present level of wages".

It is that statement of his, that policy of his, which is embodied in the provisions of the Bill. The Government believe that the proportion of council housing should decline and the Bill, with the concept of a surplus and the double taxation, is a deliberate attempt to weaken the Image Col. 577 competitive position of the public sector of housing against the private sector. That is the first essential motive.

The second essential motive is to save large sums of public expenditure by a huge cut in the total of housing subsidies as compared with the present system. The third essential motive, which is really the means by which the other two objectives are to be achieved, is to impose the principle, for the first time, of an additional rate and an additional tax on council tenants, over and above the ordinary rates and the ordinary taxes which they pay along with any other citizen. In other words, this introduces the principle of double rating and double taxation on the council tenant.
