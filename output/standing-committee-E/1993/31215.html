FISHING FLEETS CAPACITY

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Michael Jack)
: The Scrutiny Committee has recommended that, alongside the documents referred to in the motion which we shall debate later, we should consider a commission working paper on the measures that member states are taking to meet their current multi-annual guidance programmes. I hope that my comments on it this morning will be helpful to members of the Committee.

The first document that we shall consider is the European Court of Auditors report on the implementation of measures for the restructuring, modernisation and adaptation of the capacities of fishing fleets. The report reviews the structural measures in place throughout the Community during the period 1987–91 and examines the efficacy of the multi-annual guidance programmes for managing structural policy. The report includes an audit of the effectiveness of the different forms of aid, including the decommissioning grant. The criticisms to which it refers are now being considered by the Commission and member states through the framing of new measures, such as the proposed regulation on structural assistance. More generally, the report displays a certain scepticism about the value of decommissioning expenditure on its own. Given last night's debate in the House, members of the Committee will not be suprised to learn that the Government share those views.

I shall quote one important paragraph from the report. I referred to it in July when we debated the Fishing Vessels (Decommissioning) Scheme 1993 and again in last night's debate. However, for the sake of honourable Members who were not in the Chamber last night, I shall repeat the paragraph, which is relevant to the matters that we shall be discussing. In the report, the Court of Auditors said:

"It must be said that since the CFP was instituted the technological changes in the fishing sector had been so big that catch and detection facilities have now reached such a level of perfection that the traditional balance between fishing and resources has been destroyed. Any resource can now be located and exploited with an efficiency that has never been known in all the time that man has been exploiting fish resources."

That quotation lays specific emphasis on the responsibility that we all have to bring back into a more sensible balance the relationship between catching capacity with in the Community and the volume offish in the sea.

The second document that we shall debate is the Commission's report on the results of the transitional guidance programme, which was designed to fill the gap between the 1987–91 multi-annual guidance programme targets and the current multi-annual guidance programmes, which were not agreed until the end of last year. The report is factual and shows that performance against MAGP targets is monitored carefully by the Community institutions. We must bear that in mind as we 2 consider how we can achieve the targets in our current MAGP. Any shortfall will be picked up in future reports and we must recognise that if we do not meet our targets, our negotiating position on other fronts may be weakened.

The third report is a Commission paper on national measures to control fishing effort. It reflects a major innovation in the current MAGP targets—namely, the requirement to restrict fishing effort to bring it into line with what the stocks will bear. That implies reduction in capacity and an adjustment of activity. At least 53 per cent of the overall targets must be achieved by capacity reductions. The remaining 45 per cent may be achieved by measures to reduce activity. It is worth noting that even member states intending to meet their targets by capacity reductions alone must monitor the activity of remaining vessels to ensure that overall effort does not increase.

The report describes the measures that member states are taking to control effort and divides them into three groups: those operating or intending to operate days-at-sea regimes, such as the Netherlands, those operating closures, such as the weekend bans on fishing in Greece, Spain and Portugal, and other member states which are not controlling effort at all except indirectly through licensing and quota restrictions.

The document was prepared at the start of the current MAGP and does not give complete information on member states' plans for meeting their targets. However, we now know that all member states are likely to be operating decommissioning schemes to a greater or lesser extent. The Commission insists that all member states should submit data on the activities of their fleets.

The United Kingdom target under the current MAGP is a 19 per cent. reduction, which we had aimed to achieve by a combination of capacity reductions of 10.5 per cent. and activity reductions of 8.5 per cent. As I said last night, the Government are suspending days at sea and will now consider the way forward. I also made clear last night that I cannot anticipate the outcome of those considerations. However, it is clear from the documents and from what I have said this morning that we face a conservation problem, that fishing effort must indeed be reduced and that the task will require a combination of measures. That is spelled out in the motion, which we shall soon debate.
