10.30 am

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Michael Jack): Thank you, Mr. Cook, for calling me to put some points before the Committee by way of explanation on this matter. I am grateful for the opportunity to discuss with the Committee the Commission's proposals for the distribution of extra quota to Italy and to explain the Government's position.

Our considerations centre on the Commission's additional report on the application of the Milk quota scheme in Italy and the associated explanatory memorandum this report was considered by the Agriculture Council in Luxembourg last month in the context of the Commission's 1994 common agricultural policy price-fixing proposals. It will be discussed further by the Agriculture Council later this month.

The background to the report is the fact that until very recently none of the three countries has made a serious attempt to implement the milk quota system in accordance with Community Rules. In Spain and Italy that resulted in milk production significantly in excess of quota. Their response was to ask for an increase in their national quotas in exchange for a commitment to reduce production and implement the quota system properly. In the case of Greece, where actual production was not substantially over quota, the request for an increase in quota was based on the country's very low level of self-sufficiency in milk and reflected the difficulties Greece was having in importing milk from the former Yugoslavia.

Provisional increases in quota were granted to Italy, Spain and Greece last year and the Commission was charged with the task of monitoring progress and reporting to the Council in March this year. That it has done. The report identified shortcomings in the implementation of the quota system in all three countries. The most serious of those were in respect of Italy, where it appeared that the overall target for reducing production would not be met. In view of that, the commission proposed that Italy's additional quota of 900,000 tonnes for 1993–94 should be reduced by 350,000 tonnes for 1994–95.

Within a matter of weeks the Italians presented revised milk production statistics to the Commission. On the basis of that new information, the Commission drew the conclusion that Italy had in fact taken the necessary steps to apply the milk quota system and reduce surplus production. As a result, the Commission has amended its original proposal and is now recommending that Italy should receive the full 900,000 tonnes of additional quota on a provisional basis for 1994–95, but that about a third of this should be held in a special reserve for allocation to producers who are currently in dispute with the Italian authorities over their individual quota awards.

Col. 2 The speed with which the Commission changed its original proposal in favour of Italy is, to say the least, suspicious. And the new figures which have been presented by the Italian authorities give us cause for concern on two counts. First, the amended figure for Italian milk production in 1991–92—the base year on which Italy's additional quota was assessed—has been revised downwards. The Commission claims that that is the result of detailed checks which the Italians have only just completed. That may well be the case. But if the revised lower figure had been used as the starting point for the assessment of additional quota in December 1992, it is doubtful whether a figure of 900,000 tonnes would have been justified.

Secondly it is not clear to us from the figures that the Italians have explained precisely how they have fulfilled their commitment to reduce milk production by 1.6 million tonnes. We have the impression that their claimed reduction may be more apparent than real since it seems to rely, at least in part, on the withdrawal of quota from farmers who have not produced a drop of milk for the past five years. In view of those concerns we will continue to have reservations about the Commission's revised proposal.

In addition to those technical points, we have a more fundamental reservation about the allocation of additional quota to Italy—and to Spain and Greece for that matter. The Commission has made this proposition in the context of a package of proposals for CAP prices and related measures, which is unsatisfactory to us for a number of reasons. In particular, it would involve a 1 per cent. quota cut for all member states from 1994–95. We are opposed to that. It is not justified by the Commission's own analysis of the market situation in the dairy sector. Since the United Kingdom does not have enough quota as it is, a further arbitrary reduction, equivalent to about 150,000 tonnes of milk a year, would deal the British dairy industry a heavy blow. At a time when producers in the Mediterranean countries stand to benefit from additional quota, it is unacceptable that producers elsewhere should be facing a cut.

We are not alone in making that point. The Benelux countries share our view as does Denmark. It is, of course, difficult to predict the outcome of the negotiations. However, I can assure the Committee that the Government will in no way support a price-fixing package that provides additional milk quota for Mediterranean milk producers while damaging the interests of British farmers—in particular by imposing a further arbitrary cut in milk quotas.

However, there is a new Italian Government and a new Italian Minister of Agriculture, and my right hon. Friend the Minister will be interested to hear what he has to say about this matter.

Mrs. Browning: Will my hon. Friend give further information on how the Government are challenging the statistics and accounts of the Commission? I understand that the accounts for 1989 are being challenged in the European Court of Justice and that other challenges are being made to undo the Commission's decisions about not just Italy, but Spain and Greece as well. Mr. Jack: I am grateful to my hon. Friend for her question. It is important to distinguish between the action that the United Kingdom Government can take in all such matters and what other European institutions are actually doing. I do not want to detain the Committee on the detail, but the Court of Auditors report 4/93, which was published in the Official Journal, includes an important commentary on the accounts and on the qualifications and doubts. That points out the Court of Auditors' reservations about the way in which the statistics and levy calculations were conducted in all three countries.

On the question of action, I must tell my hon. Friend that it is for the Commission to follow through action regarding the qualification of the accounts. Italy was subject to a disallowance of 56 million ecu in 1988, 220 million ecu in 1989 and 295 million ecu in 1990. The United Kingdom has taken action on the Commisssion's revision of its assessment of disallowance. That is why we have taken the Commission to the European court. We do not accept the basis of the calculation that the Commission has followed in determining the revised level of disallowance to which Italy should be subjected. We do not think that it is using the right figures. It is using too low a base and, on the basis of that calculation, has revised the Italian milk production figures. Therefore, we think that it has miscalculated the disallowance by about a factor of a half.

We have been robust in taking the Commission to the European court on the basis that it was going soft on Italy. So, where we can act, we have done so strongly in the interests of proper accounting.
