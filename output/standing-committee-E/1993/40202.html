The Minister for the Environment and Countryside (Mr. Robert Atkins): With your permission, Mr. Jones, I should like to make a short statement about the proposals from the European Commission for a Council directive on the landfill of waste.

It may be helpful to the Committee if, at the outset, I give a brief summary of the history of the proposal. In its resolution of 7 May 1990 on waste policy, the Council of the European Communities invited the European Commission to propose criteria and standards for the disposal of waste by landfill. The Commission made its original proposal for a Council directive on the landfill of waste—reference 5953/91—on 22 May 1991. The Department's explanatory memorandum dated 30 June 1991 deals with that in substance. The Commission's proposal was subsequently discussed by the European Parliament at its October 1992 plenary session. The Parliament tabled several amendments to the proposal, many of which were incorporated by the Commission in the revised draft of the proposal—reference 7506/93—which was published on 22 June 1993.

The Department's explanatory memorandum dated 16 July 1993, which has been supplemented by the memorandum and compliance cost assessment of 10 January 1994, deals with that revised proposal. The June 1993 revised proposal helpfully shows the amendments made by the Commission alongside its original proposal. The Committee may therefore wish to concentrate on that version of the draft which is the subject of negotiations within the environment working group in Brussels.

The proposal relates to wastes covered by the EC waste framework directive whose final destination is landfill disposal. It provides for landfills to be classified according to the types of waste to be disposed of and sets out waste acceptance criteria for each class of site. It includes a detailed permitting system for landfill sites, and sets out the operational measures to be taken to ensure a high level of environmental protection.

The Department's explanatory memorandum of 16 July 1993 explains that the Government have two major worries about the latest draft proposal put forward by the Commission. I should like to say a few words about them. The first concerns subsidiarity. We place great importance on that principle; that is, that the Community should only act when the objectives of the proposed action can better be attained at the level of the Community than at the level of individual member states. The original proposal for the directive was drawn up before that principle was enshrined in the Maastrict treaty and endorsed at the Edinburgh Council. We are not convinced that the proposal in its present form takes full account of that principle.

Col. 2 My predecessor, the hon. Member for Suffolk, South (Mr. Yeo), argued strongly at the Environmental Council in October last year that no convincing case had been made for Community action and that the proposal should, therefore, not be taken forward. Other member states, however, were keen for the proposal to proceed, and for the question of subsidiarity to be considered in relation to the detail of the proposal. We remain to be convinced. However, in the light of the support of other member states for the proposal, it is now clear that our overall objectives in the negotiations will be better served by concentrating our subsidiarity arguments on the provisions of the directive, rather than on the proposal as a whole. Indeed, our subsidiarity arguments have already led to suggestions from the Belgian presidency and the Commission for much of the detail in the annexes of the directive to be removed.

Our second worry relates to the future of co-disposal under the directive. That is the new treatment of certain difficult industrial wastes by interaction with biodegradable municipal wastes in a controlled landfill site. Co-disposal makes use of the natural processes which take place in a landfill site when biodegradable waste decomposes to render harmless the potentially hazardous element of certain difficult wastes. The Commission's 1991 draft of the directive provided for co-disposal to take place when environmental benefits would be achieved. However, following the European Parliament's discussion of the directive, the Commission accepted an amendment proposed by the Parliament which would lead to a ban on that practice once the directive has been in force for five years.

Co-disposal is a vital means of waste disposal in the United Kingdom. About 2.8 million tonnes of difficult industrial waste are disposed of in that way—about 70 per cent. of the annual total. Properly controlled, it is an environmentally sound practice. Uncontrolled co-disposal practices in some other European countries have led to quite different preceptions about the values and dangers of that form of waste disposal. Several countries are therefore strongly opposed to the practice. We have no wish to force other countries to adopt the practice, but we seek to convince our European partners that strictly controlled co-disposal is a valuable and safe method of waste disposal.

We are also concerned about the economic consequences of a possible ban on co-disposal. The compliance cost assessment for the proposal shows that we can expect industry's waste disposal costs to increase by about £160 million if the co-disposal option is no longer available.

I hope that I have given at least a brief insight into the proposal and those issues that concern us. As most members of the Committee will know, this is a new, interesting and somewhat challenging area of policy for me, but I shall do my best to answer all questions.
