10.30 am

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Nicholas Soames): Dr. Blackburn, may I say what an honour it is to sit at your feet? I have not had the pleasure of being a member of a Committee chaired by you before, but I am sure that it will be conducted with despatch and good order. I look forward to your guidance. It is retrograde step, however, that we have paper cups in the Committee Room; I hope that we shall soon return to glasses.

Thank you, Dr. Blackburn, for giving me the opportunity to say a few words about this important issue. I must stress that the motion should refer to protecting the health status of the United Kingdom, not just Great Britain. Before commenting on the proposal, I wish to explain briefly the background to Council directive 64/432/EEC, which governs intra-Community trade in cattle and pigs and say why it is necessary to amend it.

The directive lays down detailed animal health rules, including a requirement that cattle exported for breeding or production originate from herds that are free from tuberculosis, brucellosis and enzootic bovine leukosis and that pigs for breeding come from brucellosis-free herds. In addition, the testing of cattle for tuberculosis and brucella, and pigs for brucella, is required if the animals are more than a certain age and are exported for breeding or production.

Testing for EBL is required for cattle more than 12 months old that are not intended for meat production, and which originate from a member state or region that is not free from the disease. Trade in calves that are less than 15 days old is not currently subject to directive 64/432, but is subject to rules agreed bilaterally between trading countries. All consignments of cattle and pigs must be accompanied by official health certification signed by a veterinarian with a competent authority in the member state of origin.

European document 4168/94 is a proposal for a Council directive to amend and update the earlier directive. Consolidation of that 30-year-old directive and the 40 or so amendments made over the years is long overdue, and we generally welcome it. The Commission considers it appropriate to propose several new amendments to the directive that are in keeping with the single market philosophy of liberalising trade. Unfortunately, in undertaking the amendment exercise, the Commission failed to provide a summary of the numerous consequential amendments, to other directives. As the Committee has rightly pointed out, the absence of such a summary does not facilitate transparency and ease of use.

Col. 2 Directive 64/432 is an animal health directive; it does not deal with animal welfare. That is, of course, covered by other Community rules. We have fully implemented Community welfare legislation, supplemented by our national rules, which are extremely strict. I assure the Committee that we shall continue to press the Community to adopt additional rules that reflect our high standards of care.

As for the proposed amendments, the Government support the principle of removing unnecessary barriers to trade and support the initiative taken by the Commission in drafting the proposal with a view to facilitating trade. However, what may be good for United Kingdom exporters may not necessarily be good for importers and the industry generally. It is, therefore, important that the individual provisions are assessed carefully to ensure that they do not increase the risk of disease being spread between member states.

We also know that, understandably, the industry wishes to proceed with caution and avoid the risks of the spread of disease into the United Kingdom. The proposal to reduce from six months to 30 days the period of residency in the exporting member state is of major concern to the industry. Although it is not completely opposed to reducing the minimum residency period, it considers that 30 days is too short a time. Similarly, the proposal to remove altogether the pre-movement testing of animals being moved from regions officially recognised as disease-free may be premature while pockets of disease continue to occur within the Community and in certain third countries.

Those two proposals would certainly benefit the United Kingdom's export trade, particularly by eliminating pre-movement testing for brucellosis. It is essential, however to measure those benefits against the possible risk to this country's high animal health status, to which we all attach much importance.

The proposal that has met the greatest reaction from industry is the intention to bring trade in calves less than 15 days old within the scope of the directive. About 450,000 calves were exported last year, mainly to the Netherlands, France and Belgium. It is extremely important business. Among other things, the calves must come from the herds where there has been no outbreak of EBL during the past two years. Under the proposal, however, calves for export would have to come from herds situated in an EBL-free member state or region or from herds that were individually EBL-attested. To gain attested status, herds would need to be EBL tested with negative results twice in 12 months. That could be achieved by herds joining the Government's excellent cattle health scheme or by a comparable private arrangement.

As great Britain is unlikely in the short term to be recognised by the Commission as being EBL-free, all 30,000 dairy herds and a few beef herds producing calves for export would therefore have to undergo routine testing for EBL. It would also be necessary to keep activities on farms and at markets involving export status calves separate from activities involving non-export status calves. That would initially involve some capital outlay, particularly by market operators, to provide the necessary facilities. Where tasks had to be carried out twice as a result, staff costs would be doubled. In addition to the extra resources required to deal with a large number of applications for EBL testing under the cattle health scheme, the state veterinary service would be required to Image Col. 3 approve markets handling calves. There would also be a need for official verification of the status of calves at those markets.

Finally, the Government and industry are concerned that the proposed list of diseases for which additional trade guarantees may be requested does not include warble fly. The United Kingdom has already submitted a case for such guarantees in the absence of appropriate harmonised control measures to maintain the country's warble-free status. Our argument that additional trade guarantees for warble fly are necessary if our health status is to be sufficently protected has been strengthened by the recent imports of French cattle found to be infected with the parasite. As an interim measure, we have been able to deal with the problem under national rules. As permitted under EC rules, cattle that have been found to be clinically infested on import have been returned to France.

The proposal that we are debating raises several important issues and the Government welcome the opportunity to hear the Committee's views.

I have listened carefully to the views that have been expressed in the Committee this morning. I want the hon. Lady and the Committee to know that our continuing objective during further negotiations will be to ensure that the rules will continue to protect the health status of the United Kingdom and the Community as a whole while sustaining our valuable and important export markets. The hon. Lady knows how important those are for our farmers.

We must not fail to recognise that today we have been considering only one element in the prevention of the spread of animal diseases. In addition, all member states now operate a system of intensified veterinary checks on animals at the point of origin to ensure that only those complying with Community rules may enter intra-Community trade.

As a further precaution, member states of destination may conduct spot checks on imported consignments at the point of destination or, where there is suspicion of irregularity, at any point in the transport chain, including points of entry.

Whilst we believe that all the controls, when taken together and implemented conscientiously and correctly, as we do in this country, provide adequate safeguards against the spread of disease, we are not in any sense complacent. I give the hon. Lady my personal and categorical assurance of that. We cannot be complacent. The warble fly episode Image Col. 13 shows clearly that are we not complacent, and that we are prepared to act with vigour. We have powers to take immediate safeguard action if we have reason to believe that there may be a risk in importing animals from certain areas. We will not hesitate to do so. However, in the light of concern about a number of recent animal health problems in which imported animals have been involved, my right hon. Friend the Minister announced at the end of last year newly strengthened measures to maintain Britain's high animal health status. They include the instigation of 24-hour periods of blanket surveillance at all south and east cost ports that cover exports as well as imports, the targeting of consignments with up to 100 per cent, checks when there is a risk from particular animal health disease and the stepping up of checks at the points of destination to between 50 and 60 per cent, on average. Such measures have been augmented as a result of our continuing assessment of disease risk and we shall continue with that approach.

We have taken several steps to deal with particular disease risks. The frequency of blanket surveillance for 24-hour periods at south and east coast ports has been increased since February of this year. The level of post-import checks on imported poultry has been stepped up because of the threat of Newcastle disease. All pig imports are checked to keep out the threat not only Aujeszky's disease, but swine fever. That is of critical importance to our hard-pressed, nevertheless fundamentally successful pig industry.

Following the 1993 warble fly outbreaks, all cattle that are imported from France are now checked and, without fail, clinically infested animals are returned to their country of origin. We have maintained our post-import quarantine requirements for captive birds imported from other member states.

Only a small number of health problems have so far been found as a result of checks on animals imported in 1994; 41 cattle from 10 consignments imported from France were found to be clinically infested with warble fly and are being returned to France. Two stallions, imported from Russia and Sweden, were found to be seropositive for equine viral arteritis and were, in the immortal word of my hon. Friend the Member for Little Pig and Saddlecock—Littleborough and Saddleworth (Mr. Dickens)—"casterated".

Col. 14 Responsibility for safeguarding our high animal health status does not, however, rest solely with the Government. In accepting the greater freedom that they have as traders under the single market, importers must assume responsibility for knowing what they are buying and under what conditions they should be buying. We have sent all livestock farmers a copy of our "Don't Import Disease" leaflet, which sets out their responsibilities with regard to the import of animals with great clarity. It is in farmers' interests to ensure that they follow those rules so that, together, we can continue to maintain the high animal health status that this country has for so long enjoyed, and with it the advantage that it gives to our exporters.

It is not just a matter of words; we all attach great importance to the issue. I want the Committee to know that we shall continue to do so and continue to put the entire resources at our disposal into ensuring that such a level of high animal health status is maintained.

I acknowledge that we have difficulties ahead in the working group and in the Council, but we shall negotiate at all times in Britain's interests. I am confident that we shall reach a deal that will protect the country's high animal health status and of which the Committee will approve.
