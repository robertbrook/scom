[DR. JOHN G. BLACKBURN in the Chair]

Weights and Dimensions of Vehicles

10.30 am

The Minister for Roads and Traffic (Mr. Robert Key)
: May I start by saying what a pleasure it is to be in a Committee chaired by you, Dr. Blackburn.

The primary aim of the proposal from the Commission is to harmonise the national weight and dimension limits of goods vehicles over 3.5 tonnes and of buses with more than nine seats.

The Commission first made proposals on vehicle weights and dimensions in 1971. There followed a long period of discussion and debate. Revised proposals were produced in 1979 and in 1981. That finally led to the agreement, in December 1984, of directive 85/3/EEC, which set limits on some weights and dimensions—but only for the heaviest lorries that were used on international journeys between member states.

The limits agreed were 40 tonnes on five or six axle vehicles for general haulage, with 44 tonnes for vehicles carrying containers on combined transport. In contrast to the current proposals, the combined transport vehicles can operate on five or six axles, but the tractive unit must have three axles. The provision for the three axle tractive unit was to ensure that the drive axle loads were kept as low as possible as it had not been possible to agree to specific drive axle limits. At that stage the United Kingdom secured the derogation to maintain our 38 tonne limit. Later, in 1986, the Council agreed on a drive axle limit of 11.5 tonnes for those vehicles. Again, the United Kingdom secured a derogation to limit axle loads to 10.5 tonnes.

The derogations were intended to give us time to assess and, and where necessary, to strengthen our road bridges, before accepting the increased weights. They allow us to keep our weight limits until 1999 and are maintained in the proposal from the Commission.

Subsequent amendments to the directive introduced weight limits for two, three and four axle vehicles; revised dimensions for the width of refrigerated vehicles; and revised length for articulated vehicles and drawbar combinations. The last amendment—in 1992—introduced a technical definition for road friendly suspension for the drive axles of some of the vehicles in the directive.

To date, all the limits in the directive refer to vehicles that are used on international transport in the Union. The protracted discussion that has taken place since the early 1970s shows how difficult it is to harmonise the divergent standards that are applied in each member state. Following the completion of the internal market and the abolition of border controls in January 1993, however, the Commission now argues that widely differing national weight limits should be harmonised to facilitate equal competition in the internal market.

2 The effect of the proposal for the United Kingdom would be to increase the maximum permitted weight of certain six axle vehicle combinations to 44 tonnes, to increase the width of non-refrigerated vehicles by 50 millimetres to 2.55 metres and to introduce a height limit of 4 metres. The Government response to that proposal is detailed in the explanatory memoranda—dated 24th February and 29th March.

We do not accept the Commission's reasoning. The directive sets weight limits to allow the movement of goods vehicles and buses across all member states. For purely domestic journeys, there is no reason why a vehicle should not be loaded to different weights. Many vehicles that are limited to 38 tonnes in the United Kingdom can be safely loaded to 40, 44 or even 48 tonnes in those member states that allow such weights. More importantly, as we pointed out in the explanatory memorandum, a move to permit 44 tonne vehicles for general transport would completely undermine the measures that have been taken by the United Kingdom and other member states to encourage combined road and rail transport by allowing a higher weight limit. That would mean that, in terms of actual payload, combined transport would be at a disadvantage relative to pure road movements.

The United Kingdom is not alone in opposing the Commission's proposal. The Netherlands and Denmark, which allow weights in excess of 44 tonnes, do not wish to impose burdens on their operators by restricting the weight at which they operate. Several Countries, including Belgium, Luxembourg and Italy, allow 44 tonnes for general transport on five axles and do not wish to force their industry to re-equip with sixaxled vehicles. Germany and France, like the United Kingdom, wish to encourage combined transport by allowing the additional payload of the 44 tonne vehicle.

There is also opposition from those countries which are seeking to join the Community. Sweden and Finland have sound economic arguments to retain their existing high limits of 60 tonnes on both weights and dimensions. Austria, however, wishes to restrict the use of road vehicles in favour of rail and combined transport.

The limited discussion that has taken place on the proposal to date indicates that any agreement on weights is a long way off. The issue of dimensions is far less contentious. But, as we have stated in the explanatory memorandum, there would be significant problems with an overall height limit of 4 metres.

This is a complex and technical matter but I shall be delighted to do my best to explain the rather abstruse points to members of the Committee, should they so wish.

Mr. Key
: The hon. Lady is absolutely right about the time scale. The European Parliament will be well into its next session before it is ready to debate the issue. The Council of Ministers will wish to debate it too, and I foresee that it will run and run and because there is little agreement among Council members.

Although it would be possible to renegotiate derogation, we have no plans to do so on the grounds that the reason for derogation will no longer exist after 1999.

If the hon. Lady was arguing on the grounds of subsidiarity and policy, that is a different argument which I should like to think about further. The issue arises from the policy of encouraging more transport of heavy goods onto the railways.

Mr. Lidington
: Will my hon. Friend say more about the problems with height limits? Are double-decker buses the only category of vehicle to be adversely affected?

Mr. Key
: We would have some difficulty with height limits. The height limit is 4.57 metres for a double-decker bus and 4.2 metres for lorries, but that applies only to vehicles running at more than 32.52 tonnes. The decimal figures are a result of the difference between metric and imperial measures. The intention was to prevent heavier lorries from becoming taller and that was the minimum height required to carry a standard 40 foot container on the lorries of that period.

Some people may think that the 4 metre vehicle height helps to overcome the problem of bridge bashing. I am not convinced about that. The real problem is the driver's lack of perception of the height of the bridge and his lack of knowldge of the height of the vehicle that he is driving. That is not as silly as it sounds. A driver may be used to driving vehicles with different trailers of different heights. As the average maximum height of our bridges is about 5 metres, there would be serious implications for double-decker buses that are higher than 4 metres.

Mr. Harris
: What representations has my hon. Friend received from amenity groups and local authorities, and from road haulage and freight transport organisations? I imagine that there is a great divergence of opinion about the advisability of the proposal.

Mr. Key
: My hon. Friend is right that we have received many representations. The Freight Transport Association has campaigned for a general increase in lorry weight to 44 tonnes on six axles because it recognises the advantages of six axles over five axles for road wear and for loading bridges, especially small ones. We are grateful to it for that. Its campaign included articles in the trade press and letters to Members of Parliament, which my hon. Friend and I, and no doubt you, Dr. Blackburn, have received.

Local authorities, environmental groups and many members of the public expressed concern at the proposed increase for combined transport, which they feared would open a back door to a general increase to 44 tonnes. There 4 was widespread opposition to that. Some local authorities were worried that there would be an increased incidence of damage to bridges. However, all those anxieties did not take into account the fact that an increase to 44 tonnes on six axles would not do more damage to roads or bridges than the present 38 tonne, five axle vehicles.

I suspect that there is still a perception among the electorate that a heavier lorry automatically means bigger dimensions. It does not, with the tiny exception of the increase in width of less than 2 inches, which was allowed for refrigerated vehicles because of insulation factors and because of the proposal in the directive which would allow for a slight width increase of about 2 inches to take account of new methods of stacking pallets inside containers. That would be much more efficient.

The present regulations take account adequately, and largely without complaint, or occasional overhang loads that are carefully regulated. Therefore, I do not think that the anxiety is as great as it was. That is a summary of the responses to the consultation on the proposals.

Mr. Barnes
: The Minister said that five different attitudes had been adopted by 11 different nations, including some of those which had applied for membership of the Community. Is it therefore likely that the Commissions initiative will be taken on by the presidency, which will work out a presidential compromise between the different viewpoints?

Mr. Key
: That is likely, but I think that it will be years before the matter is resolved. Britain's argument is that these are issues of subsidiarity and that we manage perfectly well now. I have already explained that a vehicle that is allowed to load to only 38 tonnes in this country can safely load to 44 tonnes or more in countries in which it is allowed to do so. That introduces the issue of cabotage, to which hon. Members may wish to refer. As we already work perfectly well with different lorry weights, and as operators and drivers are used to that and it causes little trouble, I suspect that the argument on subsidiarity will win the day—I certainly hope so, because that is what we all want to achieve. That is particularly important because we are dealing not so much with a technical argument as with an argument of policy. We are trying to get more appropriate freight on to railways in a number of different ways.

Mr. John D. Taylor
: The Minister mentioned the economic implications of this measure with regard to the strengthening of bridges. Would the increase to 44 tonnes have any adverse effect on the Eurotunnel or on the ferries betweeen Scotland and Northern Ireland?

Mr Key
: On the question of ferries between Scotland and Northern Ireland, the directive is not intended to apply to Northern Ireland. However, the Northern Ireland Office is considering making separate proposals and that will be a matter for my right hon. and learned Friend the Secretary of State for Northern Ireland.

Freight transport through the channel tunnel is important. We anticipate that the 44 tonne limit for combined transport will be very popular with operators who want to route long-distance freight through the tunnel. There will be no change for ferries. We thought carefully about whether we should include the 44 tonne limit for ports and, on balance, decided against that. It was not an easy decision. We reckoned that containers going to JPEG 5 international destinations by sea would continue to reach the port by rail. If we allowed 44 tonne lorries to travel from the freight depot at Folkestone straight through the channel tunnel it would increase the number of 44 tonners on the roads through Kent, so we have said that they will have to load on to the train at the Willesden depot. That is the present position, but it may change with the Ashford interchange. There will be no change for shipping: 38 tonners are allowed to go to ports.

Mrs. Gillan
: I want to return my hon. Friend's attention to the fear of a general increase to 44 tonnes, which would have a detrimental effect on our integrated road and rail transport policy. Have any calculations been made of the amount of freight that could switch from rail to road? If so, can he tell us about that, because it is of great interest to the Government and to the Opposition, who support a combined rail and road transport policy?

Mr. Key
: By raising those points my hon. Friend shows how perceptive she is. One of the most extraordinary things about the Commission's proposed directive is that at no point—not even when it refers to combined rail and road transport—does it mention railways. It appears that the Commission has totally ignored rail transport. May I draw the Committee's attention to the proposal for a Council directive, which is in the document dated 15th December? Paragraph B.I(e) on page 3–1 believe that I am not allowed to quote in French so I will translate it—asks about the positive effects of the proposal. It then states:

"The positive effects of the proposal will be threefold. Firstly, it will simplify the possibilities for cabotage".

Secondly—I paraphrase—it will specify road-friendly suspensions and, thirdly, since the maximum weight would be increased in seven member states, the proposal would result in fewer road journeys. It does not mention railways.

My hon. Friend asked me about figures. The latest statistics that I can offer are as follows: in 1992, of the 94,800 articulated tractor units registered in Great Britain, 27,300 were three axled vehicles; and of the 240,000 trailer units on the road that year, only 75,600 were three axled. The majority of existing six axled articulated vehicles would be able to operate at up to 42 tonnes without modification but, were they to take full advantage of the proposed increased weight limits, the use of specially adapted semi-trailers would be necessary because of our restrictions on axle spacing and loading. That is another technical difference between us and the Commission. The axle spacing of the trailers is crucial, especially for short-span bridges.

The estimated additional cost of a semi-trailer capable of operating at the new weight is between £2,000 and £3,000. An adaption of existing semi-trailers is possible but at a cost of between £3,500 and £4,500 each. Special designs would not be required for drawbar vehicles but relatively few existing vehicles are capable of operating at 44 tonnes.

On that basis, less than a third of present tractor units would be able to take advantage of the proposed weight increase and approximately one third of existing trailer units would be able to operate at up to 42 tonnes but not at 44 tonnes.

Those are important details because we are considering the extent to which there will be any saving in the total number of lorries on the road as a result of the increase to 44 tonnes. That is difficult to quantify. In so far as present cargoes are restrained by weight, an increased payload 6 would, theoretically, reduce the number of lorries for a given amount of freight. Many cargoes on the roads are already constrained by volume. In such cases, no advantage could be taken of a higher payload. It is all very well to talk about 44 tonnes but, if a lightweight commodity such as boxed kitchen chairs is being carried, the load will not add up to anything approaching the maximum weight allowed—nor would feather pillows. Volume is important in both of those examples.

Present estimates indicate that between 4,000 and 5,000 lorries might be saved by a general weight increase to 44 tonnes, but that saving takes no account of the risk to the viability of the rail freight market. That would be significant, especially for intermodal traffic where road and rail are in direct competition. Again, that is difficult to quantify but the effect on rail container-carrying operations could be substantial and might offset the number of lorries saved by as much as 50 per cent. That would not be compatible with the Government's objective of maximising the use of rail for freight movements.

Can he tell us how such work has been done if money has not been allocated to ensure that the programme has been carried out consistently? Mr. Key
: I am happy to address those important points. On lorry weights and bridges, we have already taken account of the bridge-strengthening operation. That is important because the stress on bridges relates primarily to axle weight, but it is also important that the load distribution on bridges is acceptable. That is why I said that short-span bridges pose a special problem. It is important that the spacing between axles is controlled because such bridges are more common. United Kingdom regulations include detailed axle-spacing requirements, but they are not all covered by the Commission's proposal. In particular, there is no Commission requirement for a minimum space between the last axle of the tractor and the first axle of the semi-trailer in an articulated lorry. That is an important factor. On longer-span bridges, the total weight of the vehicle is more important, but we have a lot of shorter-span bridges, such as those that cross railway lines.

Until our programmes of bridge assessment and strengthening is complete, we cannot allow axle weights greater than 10.5 tonnes. Good progress is being made with trunk road structures. Spending of nearly £50 million is planned this year and we are confident that the job will be finished by January 1999. All costs incurred by local authorities when assessing and strengthening bridges carrying local authority roads are eligible for transport supplementary grant. Resources allocated to local authorities in the past few settlements have remained constant at £137 million, despite reductions in other expenditure categories this year.

JPEG 7 It is true that we have made comparatively little progress on privately owned bridges carrying local authority roads, such as those owned by British Rail—now Railtrack. Funds for assessment are being channelled through the local authorities, giving them a greater say over their local priorities. It is in the interests of local authorities to complete the scheme. Otherwise, they will find that heavier 44 tonne lorries will not be able to use those bridges in 1999 and they will have to be diverted on to less suitable roads.

There is a question about lorry routes. Local authorities already have powers to ban certain classes of vehicles from sensitive roads. A general ban would cause many major problems for access and enforcement and might give rise to the need for trans-shipment in one form or another. However, the siting of railheads and goods depots would make such a general restriction on 44 tonne lorries for combined transport impossible. Good road access to the terminals is very important.

The Secretary of State also has powers under the Road Traffic Regulation Act 1984 to prohibit or restrict the use of specified sections of motorways and trunk roads by certain classes of vehicle. The same legislation gives local highway authorities the power to restrict or prohibit certain classes of vehicle from using certain stretches of road where there are clear reasons for doing so. That Act also gives authorities the power to specify through-routes for lorries, or to prohibit or restrict their use on certain roads or within zones, to preserve or enhance the amenity of an area. Both those provisions are subject to procedures requiring public notice to be given. In some cases, a public inquiry may be necessary.

I hope that the hon. Member for Stoke-on-Trent, North (Ms Walley) will agree that the 1984 Act contains significant powers. A national lorry-routeing system would be very difficult to devise and enforce, because many heavy goods vehicles will be destined for places outside the route, such as particular factories or locations. Is the hon. Lady suggesting that a massive new bureaucracy is needed to permit vehicles to deviate from specified routes? That would be a nightmare and enforcement would be very difficult and expensive for all concerned. I am not convinced that we need a national system of lorry routes. I would prefer to work to encourage combined road-rail traffic and to get appropriate freight traffic off roads and on to rail where possible.

Mr. Barnes
: What are the implications of heavier loads for road accidents? Is it worse to be squashed by a 44 tonne load or a 40 tonne load? Have studies on that been carried out?

Mr. Key
: I reassure the hon. Gentleman that the increased weight limits will have no effect on performance, because the same stopping distances must be achievable with heavier weights. There should therefore be no safety risks, even on matters such as that of mirrors, where we have introduced changes; we are seeking further changes at a European-wide level on mirrors to ensure that blind spots both backwards and downwards on the near side do not occur. As far as accidents are concerned, the vehicle inspectorate is very anxious to enforce the regulations and it is important that we pay tribute to the extraordinary work it undertakes. For example, it anticipates examining a minimum of 1.4 million heavy goods vehicles and 145,000 public service vehicle tachograph charts this year; it will also weigh a minimum of 115,000 goods vehicles and have at least 90 per cent. weighbridge equipment available nationally at any one time. Enforcement is a very serious issue and it is working. That is important.

Mr. Key
: If I am allowed to depart from the subject matter of our debate, I shall answer that question.

There will be no change in the standard of enforcement of continuous operator licensing. To suppose that is to miss the point. The traffic commissioners will have exactly the same powers: the proposed change will simply cut down bureaucracy. I know that the hon. Lady and her party have argued against that change—it is merely a facet of the difference between us—but there is no evidence or reason to suppose that there will be any diminution of standards of inspection or performance by operators as a result. The same is true of the London lorry ban. The passage of vehicles through the City has not changed—the only change is in the number of bits of paper that are generated to operate that ban.

JPEG 9 I met the traffic commissioners last week and I have the highest regard for them. I wish that more people knew about their work. Changes have been proposed regarding their work, and my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State is considering changes to the organisation of the traffic area offices, which interact with the vehicle inspectorate in an important way. We are still considering those important matters. Safety—for other road users and for the industry—must however, come first.

Mr. Barnes
: What is being done to encourage the noble idea of combined road and rail transport? Does any research show what inter-connections can be made between road and rail transport faculties to enable quick loading and transfer from lorry to rail? Could all freight points be used to enable that?

Mr. Key
: May I try to explain what this argument is about and invite members of the Committee to consider it from a different perspective? Instead of thinking about a lorry, we should think about a container, because the issue is all about the comparative weights of containers.

The standard international 40 foot container has a maximum payload of 30 tonnes. The maximum contents would be 25 to 26 tonnes of freight, because the container weighs 4 tonnes. Given the 38 tonne limit, a full load cannot be put in such a container and be carried on a road on a 38 tonner. That can only be achieved if an extra 6 tonnes is allowed for the total weight of the tractor, trailer and container. An extra 6 tonnes, not five, must be allowed for, because 1 tonne must be allowed for the axle. If a vehicle that goes to the railhead has the advantage of those few tonnes, it will have the same payload as a container on a train. If we continue with the 38 tonne limit, we shall give an unfair competitive advantage to a container that is taken by road, That is what we seek to eliminate.

We want to encourage hauliers to apply for licences to replate their vehicles up to the 44 tonne limit for combined operations. Since the regulations came into force on 24 March, we have had more than 100 applications from the freight transport industry to do that. That will mean that companies in the industry will be at an advantage.

When we see lorries thundering down the road, carrying freight that we might think should have been carried by train—such as aggregate for road-building—we should bear in mind the fact that the industry can already operate so efficiently with 38 tonners that it can undercut rail transport.

The hon. Member for Derbyshire, North-East is right to say that, unless there are proper interchange facilities between road and rail to make the transfer of container loads quick, we shall not reap the advantages that we envisage. That is why the Government have invested large sums of taxpayers' money in the Eurotunnel terminals around the country. They will be efficient and have been designed to allow the swift transfer of goods from road to rail, as the hon. Gentleman wants.

Ms Walley
: Will the Minister say how much freight has been lost in the past five years from rail to road? There has been a large move in that direction in freight in cement, chemicals and cider. What investment has been made in the railways given that current technology permits piggybacks and the kangaroo methods to which the Minister referred? 10 He referred also to new depots for the channel tunnel, but there is no evidence that rail terminals around the country can accomodate a return to rail freight.

Mr. Key
: I shall try to bear in mind the range of questions asked by the hon. Lady, despite the faulty memory that comes with the onset of old age.

It is difficult to quantify rail-to-road losses. My Department's economists estimated that the direct effect on train-load freight in the United Kingdom could be equivalent to 50 million additional lorry miles. The effect on container-carrying operations could also be severe, with a large proportion of existing traffic lost to road. Whatever the numbers, the effect on freight rail is likely to be damaging. In mainland Europe, 44 tonne lorries operating internationally are likely to pose a considerably greater competitive threat to freight rail than the present 40 tonne vehicle. That does not sit well the European Community's oft-declared aim of encouraging combined transport.

We are limiting the measure to lorries carrying containers and swap bodies and not applying it to semitrailers carried piggy back on railway wagons. Road-rail operations are likely to be confined to the carriage of such units. Whole vehicles cannot be carried by rail in the United Kingdom at the moment. Semi-trailers carried piggy back do not suffer from the 4 to 5-tonne payload disadvantage applicable to vehicles carrying containers and swap bodies.

I do not have to hand details of the amount of investment made in freight terminals, but that information is easy to obtain and I shall give it to members of the Committee in writing.

Mr. Key

Mr. Barnes
: A possibly far-fetched scientific solution to the road-rail link problem would be a technology that would turn lorries into wagons and wagons into lorries. Such a solution would help to ensure that rail was fully utilised. The idea is not science fiction, as developments along those lines are currently under way in America.

My question concerns subsidiarity, to which the Minister referred. Does the case for subsidiarity depend on the nature of the road and transport system in this country, or on the state of the vehicles that operate here? I understand some of the subsidiarity arguments, but a great deal of coordination is needed to develop the European Union.

Mr. Key
: The answer is, disappointingly, a mixture. As I have said, different countries have different reasons for objecting to the proposals. In Italy, for example, 44 tonne vehicles have five axles only. We believe that that is not justifiable in terms of wear to the road. The proposal also has implications for transport policy in view of the fact that we are trying to integrate road and rail to a greater extent. Countries with high limits, such as Sweden where it is 60 tonnes, do not want to force their industries to reduce that limit.

JPEG 11 The health of the freight industry is an important matter of public policy. Of course, how regularly operators and owners of freight vehicles, wish to purchase new vehicles that are built to different standards, or whether they wish to adapt existing vehicles, which can be very expensive, is a matter of commercial judgment for them. A vehicle can be adapted by putting a steering bogey on a third axle of a trailer, which can then carry the 44 tonne limit. That is expensive and complicated, however, and cannot be done to all makes of trailer. Thus there is a mixture of motives.

There are three reasons why we find the Commission's proposals regarding maximum weights hard to accept. First, we see no reason why lorry weight limits for purely national, domestic journeys need to be consistent throughout the Community. Member states should be able to set their own rules to suit their own circumstances. Lorries designed for operation at higher weights in one member state can be used at lower weights in others simply by carrying less. The Commission's argument that harmonisation is necessary to ensure fair play in cabotage is a red herring.

Second, the Commission proposals seem to fly in the face of all that the Community has been trying to do in recent years to promote the use of rail and promote combined transport—journeys by combination of road and rail. There is recognition that the unbridled growth of road transport is not an option for the future. Of course, for many journeys for which goods have to be conveyed there is no sensible alternative to road transport. But for longer distance journeys, or for large flows of goods conveyed in JPEG 13 bulk over comparatively short distances, alternatives to road transport need to be developed if the Community's trade is not to be gummed up in ever growing traffic congestion.

In Britain, we have taken a recent lead in measures to encourage more freight on to the railway. Among them are regulations to allow 44 tonne lorries for combined transport, so that lorries carrying containers or swapbodies, which make most of their journey by rail, are able to carry roughly the same weight of goods as can be loaded into an articulated semi-trailer making the entire journey by road. If the maximum weight of lorries were to be increased to 44 tonnes for general road haulage, that would entirely negate the impact of our special weight concession for combined transport. It would also give a competitive boost to road transport in the bulk freight markets, such as aggregages, coal and steel. That would inevitably take traffic away from the railways in Britain and elsewhere in Europe, which is the opposite of what we, and I think most other member states, want.

Thirdly, the 44 tonne vehicles that the Commission's proposals would oblige us to accept on our roads are not the same as the 44 tonne vehicles that we now allow on our roads for combined transport. The Commission's proposals would allow a maximum weight of 11.5 tonnes on the drive axle. Our present limit is 10.5 tonnes until January 1999, so that we can check and strengthen our bridges where necessary. The "Commission vehicle" would impose more wear on our roads than the 44 tonners that we have been prepared to allow.
