[SIR DAVID KNOX in the Chair]

Fisheries

10.30 am

The Minister of Stàte, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Michael Jack)
: I welcome the opportunity to set out the Government's views on these important issues. However, before I do so I should like to deal with a procedural matter.

Hon. Members may have noticed on today's Order Paper that the Government have already tabled a motion reporting that the Committee has supported the Government's motion. I want to make it clear that I do not wish to pre-empt the outcome of this morning's debate. However, as hon. Members will appreciate, to complete the full scrutiny process of the documents during what remains of this Session of Parliament, a motion requesting the approval of the House must be tabled today. The Government judged it right to hold the debate before Prorogation to ensure that the House had the chance to express its views in full before the next Fisheries Council on 23 November, when the integration of Spain and Portugal into the common fisheries policy will be discussed again. It is possible mat decisions will be reached then, although I believe that the matter will not be resolved until the December Council.

I previously dealt with the matter in Parliament when I gave evidence before the Select Committee on 25 May. The April 1994 Fisheries Council had already adopted the guidelines to be found in document 15274. Those guidelines, published on 30 May as Council regulation 1275/94, established a clear set of principles that the Commission should follow in drawing up detailed proposals.

Those principles were that there should be no relaxation in the current Iberian rules until detailed replacement rules had been adopted by the Council; that relative stability should be fully respected; that there should be no increase in fishing effort in any area; that specific reference should be made to the Irish Box—under the Iberian Act of Accession, it lapses on 31 December 1995—as a sensitive area requiring special consideration thereafter; and that there should be no obligation to grant Spain access to other areas, including the North Sea.

On 25 August, three months after the 1 June deadline, the Commission published the two proposals before us today. That is why today is the earliest practicable date for debate. The first proposal sets out rules of access to specified western waters, from the Faroes to the Azores and along the Atlantic coast of north Africa. Under that proposal, member states' access would be limited to a number of standard vessel days, defined by species and by area. However, that proposal did not set out how those standard vessel days should be allocated by member states 2 to their fishermen. The second proposal sets out detailed arrangements for monitoring compliance with the access proposal.

Those proposals were founded on dubious assumptions. They were excessively complex and bureaucratic. They flouted the Commission's undertaking, which I had secured at the June Council, that the proposals must minimise bureaucracy. As a result, I was in the forefront of the argument that the proposals were unacceptable. The majority of member states took the same view. The general opinion was that it would not have been possible to implement them fairly or to enforce them properly. The failure to address adequately the commitment to give special protection to the area of the Irish Box was a key concern to the United Kingdom and the Republic of Ireland. There were also serious doubts about the methodology adopted by the Commission in working out the detail of the access proposal.

At the Fisheries Council on 28 September, the presidency invited member states to put forward alternative ideas for a much simpler and more operable system for the presidency and the Commission to consider. I had full and extremely useful discussions with the British fishing industry, which was united in its views on the matter.

Taking those views fully into account, I put forward an alternative approach based on key objectives: no change to relative stability and no increase in fishing effort; access to western waters to be limited to vessels with estalished rights in the areas concerned; the United Kingdom to have an effective role in monitoring the access arrangements for vessels of all nations in its own western waters; special treatment for highly sensitive zones, of which the Irish Box must be one; to assist enforcement, larger vessels to be subject to reporting requirements when entering or leaving the areas covered by the regulations and to report catches, and technical conservation measures to be strengthened. The presidency is currently considering our ideas and those of other member states, and technical discussions are continuing. As I have said, the Council will next address the issue on 23 November.

As the Committee has noted, of relevance to the debate are the Commission's proposals for setting specific management objectives and strategies for a four-year period. Those proposals have been on the table since December. Discussion is not far advanced and decisions are not expected before next year. However, for the Committee's information, the ideas under discussion are for sophisticating the approach to fisheries stock management. They include the new concept of total allowable effort, multi-annual and multi-species total allowable catches and quota flexibility between years. There are some ideas of value, but further and more focused technical consideration is required before we can accept them as a basis for taking management decisions. The Commission has already acknowledged that the 1995 TACs will be agreed along traditional lines, thereby allowing more time for further detailed assessment of the proposals.

The protection of sustainable fisheries arises also in another document that we shall consider today, namely the Commission's report on the progress of member states under their multi-annual guidance programmes. Those programmes originally focused only on capacity, but they now address the broader concept of fishing effort, with a view to achieving a better balance between the fishing effort JPEG 3 exerted by the fleet and the stocks of fish in the sea. Each member state has been set a target to be achieved by the end of 1996. The document sets out the progress that has been made.

I accept that I have spoken for perhaps a little longer than is normal, but we are discussing complex and important matters. Several documents are before us, and I hope that my introduction has been helpful.

Mr. Jack
: The hon. Member for Crewe and Nantwich [Mrs Dunwoody) has put her finger on what I consider to be one of the most important issues. Our industry can have no confidence in whatever successor regime results from the current proposals unless it can be properly enforced. I assure the hon. Lady that I shall pursue that theme as vigourously as I possibly can. I want to be able to tell our fishermen that enforcement can be properly carried out. We have made some progress in that respect. There is a strong emphasis in the Council's thinking on ensuring that the flag states—the member states whose surrounding waters will be affected by the proposals—will have a key part in the enforcement process. That is important in building confidence in the industry.

The hon. Lady asked me whether the proposal would cut the access of the Spanish fleet. The problem is that the present restrictions on the Spanish fleet's access to, for example, the Irish Box, result from the treaty of accession signed for Spain and Portugal. Our task is to control the effort of the Spanish fleet when there is no Irish Box and no basic and periodic list. The Commission chose the route of standard vessel days; we have proposed something much nearer to what the hon. Lady would like, which is in various sectors of the western waters to control the Spanish effort that could be applied.

Mrs. Ewing
: On monitoring the Spanish or Iberian fleet in western waters, how does the Minister define the effective role of the United Kingdom? Who will be responsible? Will it be the Community or the United Kingdom?

Mr. Jack
: Effectively, for western waters, monitoring will be the role of each member state in its own waters. It is not proposed to have a Community-wide monitoring arrangement, but I draw the hon. Lady's attention to the control regulation which came into force on 1 January, which gives Community inspectors the ability to inspect at random and without prior notice the way in which enforcement is being carried out by Member states. I attach great importance to that, particularly in respect of enforcement activities in Spain. That will be an important point to develop.

The proposals for enforcement are not yet fully worked out. The papers before the Committee this morning are proposals that have now been set aside by the Fisheries Council. New proposals are now under active consideration by the presidency, with a view to presenting them to the Council on 23 November. It is certainly helpful to have heard two members of the Committee highlight the importance that they attach to enforcement. I assure them that I shall properly reflect that in our remarks on whatever revised proposals come before the Council on 23 November.

That is another reason for our saying that the original idea was grossly unfair to the United Kingdom. There were serious doubts, especially when you make comparisons with the number of standard vessel days that had been allocated to the Irish Republic, for example.

As to relative stability in fishing effort, you are right to underscore—

Member for Pembroke, and I had prefaced my remarks by referring to him by name. I apologise if I deviated in your direction, Sir David. I shall try to focus on the hon. Gentleman's question.

The hon. Gentleman is right about relative stability and no increase in fishing effort, which we prayed in aid as fundamental principles. In the regulation that the Fisheries Council agreed to, it underpinned the important relative positions of member states under the new arrangements. The difficulty is that, because of the treaty that I mentioned in response to the hon. Member for Crewe and Nantwich, the Irish Box, as a line on the map, lapses at the end of 1995. We fought hard for the co-ordinates that are the Irish Box to be specifically defined in the regulation as a sensitive zone. Our proposals recognised that we needed a policy to deal with the Irish Box and certainly with the Irish Sea, where stocks are under great pressure.

The hon. Gentleman will see, if he examines the detail of our proposal, that we have said that there needs to be a policy for sensitive zones, of which the Irish Box is one. There are other sensitive zones: for example, Portugal is concerned about Madeira and the Azores, and France is concerned about the bay of Biscay. The Council must return to the matter, for precisely the reason that the hon. Gentleman mentioned—the protection offish stocks.

We share common cause in our anxieties about the Irish Box as a sensitive zone. I assure the hon. Gentleman that we shall press the matter hard.

Mr. Wallace

Mr. Jack
: Loss of days is not an issue, as we have challenged the proposition that would deal with the allocation of standard vessel days. We are relying on the ground that that is not what we want. The majority of member states in the Fisheries Council registered their varying degrees of concern. The Commission has set the 6 matter aside and has invited alternatives. There are variations on the theme of alternatives, but we are moving away from the allocative mechanism that was represented by standard vessel days in the thinking of other member states. I cannot anticipate what the presidency will suggest as a final proposal for the Council on 23 November, but my short answer is that the United Kingdom will make it clear that standard vessel days are not acceptable. I shall do all that I can to lobby other fishery Ministers on that point.

On quotas, it is certainly my view that there should be no loss of the ability to take quota. It is important for the hon. Member for Orkney and Shetland (Mr. Wallace) to understand that the quotas will be the subject of a separate negotiation in the December meeting of the Fisheries Council; they are not part of these proposals. In our criticism of the Commission's original proposal, we made it absolutely clear that questions had been asked about the number of standard vessel days available with regard to taking existing quotas. We pointed out that such a formulation was utterly unacceptable if it meant that we could not take quotas. I believe that that line of argument has been generally accepted.

When we are in the middle of negotiations, I never like to give unequivocal guarantees that certain things will happen, but within the balance of probabilities, I cannot foresee a formulation that would not enable our fishermen to take quota. Bearing in mind that Spain is seeking to be the beneficiary of the changes, I hardly think that the Spaniards would want to sign up to a mechanism that would not enable them to get whatever quotas had been allocated to them.

Mr. Jack
: The hon. Lady asks a pertinent question. Last December, we had agreed a regulation that might have given us a more relaxed timetable for those events. I was very satisfied with it. As a delegation we had worked hard to change the Commission's starting point on the arguments, which was a so-called "blank-cheque policy" acknowledging that changes would occur but offering no detailed proposals. We secured what I have described as a stepping-stone framework regulation, which enabled us to take the matter forward on a more measured time scale.

Mr. Wallace

Mr. Jack

Although I can be precise in my terms and make a case that defends the UK's interests, other members of the Council will take a different view on how the removal of the Irish Box should be handled. The first proposal considered by the Council was the Commission's. UK Ministers have given reasons for their view that the proposal was flawed; one was that the Commission did not define the Irish Box as a sensitive zone. It had a more fluid formulation for attempting to deal with the restrictions on Spanish fishing activity under new arrangements. We found those arrangements flawed and put forward our own proposals. I think that I have made it clear to the Committee that other member states are now reformulating their positions in the light of the Commission's challenge: "If you don't like our idea, find another one." We now have the opportunity to find out how other member states view the proposal.

The question asked by the hon. Member for Orkney and Shetland touched on an important point. We shall continue to stand our ground, but we must realise that there may be 11 other versions that will have to be debated in the Fisheries Council. Our task will be to plot, through the various formulations, the best way in which to secure the interests of our fishermen. I know what my objectives are JPEG 9 but, as the hon. Member for Orkney and Shetland will understand, we are in a negotiation. It is unlikely that everyone else will sit back and say that the UK has come up with the best possible idea, and agree to it without any amendment or debate. That would be an unrealistic view. I hope that I have made it clear where we stand in the negotiations; I shall do my best to sell our proposal to fisheries Ministers of other Community countries.

Mr. Wallace

Mr. Ainger
: The Minister told us that the United Kingdom proposals to the Fisheries Council on 23 November will include a proposal that we should play a significant part in monitoring technical changes, conservation, size of catch and so on. Mr. Jack
: The control regulation that took affect on 1 January already provides enhanced rigorous procedures to monitor catches, which we have accommodated. The monitoring of the arrival of catches at the quayside needs to be considered separately from the reporting requirements mentioned earlier. Those are subject to discussion.

The Commission's proposals were onerous. That is partly why we said that we did not want such an approach. It would have been complex and bureaucratic and would have been a considerable burden on member states. I cannot anticipate what the next stage will hold for us because we have yet to see clearly what the presidency compromise proposals will be. We have opted for a simple regime, that can be accommodated within our existing mechanisms for fishery protection. However, we emphasise that coastal states should have a powerful role. I say with some pride that we have an efficient enforcement mechanism. However, if the arrangements are ultimately changed, we want to be able to keep a close eye on matters. We would look carefully at our patterns of monitoring and enforcement—I know that other member states, including the Irish, will do the same—to ensure that whatever comes about can be properly monitored.

I must tell the hon. Member for Pembroke that another method of enforcement is in the distance—that is, the advanced electronic monitoring of the location of fishing vessels. The hon. Gentleman knows that about 40 United Kingdom vessels are taking part in Community trials on real-time satellite monitoring. Friend the Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland and I, who will attend the Council, to point out that when our Parliament considered the matter, whatever differences there may be between us on United Kingdom fisheries policy, there was no difference in our wish to see the interest of Britain's industry defended in the negotiations. In the days to come, if members of the Committee who have contacts with European Members of Parliament wish to add their weight of support, I should be happy to brief them in more detail if that will enable them to influence the outcome. The more people who shout our message the better.

The common theme that must be underlined is enforcement. I refer to the remarks of the hon. Member for Crewe and Nantwich. I do not pray in aid high technology as the total solution to any problem. It is very expensive and untested as a provable, reliable system. I have said that we have pressed enforcement extremely hard in the Fisheries Council and I take the matter seriously. I pointed out that, with the advent of the control regulation, trial satellite monitoring is now being taken more seriously at Community level. Anecdotal reports by television presenters, programmes, and industry representations to ports in northern Spain, which bring back stories about prosecutions, as referred to by the hon. Member for Pembroke, build the picture that not everybody plays by the rules. It is worth reflecting that not every fisherman in every member state plays by the rules. If we are to have strength to our elbow on this argument, United Kingdom fishermen must reflect on that matter. The fewer prosecutions we have the whiter the picture we can paint of our position. I assure the Committee that the enforceability of future policies will be uppermost in our minds. On deploying resources on protection and enforcement, we shall examine current patterns carefully in the light of new regimes.

There was a second theme in our debate involving some criticism about the multi-annual guidance programme. With regard to the comments of the hon. Member for Glanford and Scunthorpe about what we are trying to do to meet our target, on the subject of nephrops—trawls and shellfish mobiles—we are presently negotiating with the Commission for reductions in our sectoral target. We are reflecting the technical nature of those fisheries, which is a way into the arguments on technical conservation. I am optimistic that we shall achieve an allowance that we can post against our target.

We are also seriously considering improving the way in which licence aggregation works. What set out to be a promising source of a reduction in effort has not been taken up by the industry with quite the enthusiasm for which I had hoped. In discussions, representatives of the industry have given me the idea that by making some adjustments, which we shall discuss with the industry, there may be opportunities to see two objectives realised. There could be 24 a reduction in effort and some modernisation. It is interesting to note that, despite some of the gloomier statements about the industry, this year there have been orders in Scotland for 15 new fishing vessels. I was pleased to note that some are to be built in Scottish yards. So, perhaps the picture is not quite as bad as it is always painted.

Comments were made about the attitude of the Foreign and Commonwealth Office. In its defence, in the enlargement negotiations it was anxious to ensure that fisheries issues were given proper weight. The fact that we gained extra fish in the discussions on cohesion cod and fishing in north Norway underscores its importance. However, I shall draw the attention of my colleagues in the Foreign and Commonwealth Office to hon. Members' remarks on those important points.

I am pleased that hon. Members have acknowledged the difficulties of the negotiations on which we are about to embark. The hon. Member for Crewe and Nantwich chastised me about our regard—according to her interpretation—for the scrutiny process. I take such matters extremely seriously. Had the Commission not been tardy in keeping to its original timetable, we would not have had to put a presumptive motion before the House. I understand the line she has taken, but I write to the Chairman of the Scrutiny Committee to keep him posted as regularly as I can. In a fast-moving situation, it is not always possible for official papers to catch up with real world events.

Mr. Jack
: I understand the point made by the hon. Member for Crewe and Nantwich. However, we face some timetabling difficulties; this year, there are German elections and the German presidency. The Fisheries Council was, therefore, moved from October to September. However much one brutalises Parliament—as the hon. Lady suggested—it might not always be possible to have a confluence of events to enable our Parliament to have an advance say on such matters. Ministers try hard to keep all parties informed, officially or through unofficial networks.

The hon. Member for Crewe and Nantwich said that we must not be sidelined in the negotiations; she emphasises, with her long experience of such matters, an important point. I hope that everyone who reads the report of the Committee will reflect on it. If I do not engage in negotiations on behalf of the United Kingdom, we shall be sidelined. People have often said, "Can you just say no?" If I do that, I will be out; I will not be part of the negotiating process. I do not underestimate the problems that we shall face, but I shall not be sidelined. The fact that I am engaging in a broad range of bilateral discussions with fellow fisheries Ministers demonstrates my intention to put forward the UK's position in clear, unequivocal terms.

My hon. Friend the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland and I will do our best, but we shall be engaged in negotiation. Our starting point accords with the industry's JPEG 25 view. As the hon. Member for Falkirk, East remarked, we have tried hard to say in touch with the industry on such matters.

I have noted other points raised by hon. Members and I assure them that I shall reflect on them carefully when considering the line to take in the Fisheries Council. My hon. Friend the Member for Falmouth and Camborne (Mr. Coe) used a telling phrase when he said that this matter was not a minor irritation. He was right to emphasise that. He and my hon. Friends the Members for Scarborough and for St. Ives have been doughty defenders of the industry in putting forward its case. We shall do that as vigorously as possible in forthcoming negotiations.

I hope that the Committee, given those explanations, will endorse the motion and send me into the negotiations with unanimous support; that would be helpful.
