Transport of Animals

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Nicholas Soames)
: The subject of our debate is one that the Government consider to be of the highest importance. Animal welfare is a matter of great, justifiable and instinctive concern to the British people. The Government share those feelings in every particular and to the utmost degree. Our concern is not only for animals in this country, where their welfare is already subject to excellent and rigorous safeguards, but to extend our standards to the rest of the community. That is particularly necessary for animal transport, because animals can now be freely traded within the Community.

We need to ensure a common set of rules, so that all member states operate to the same high standards of care. However, it is not enough to adopt a set of rules if there is no intention of applying them rigorously. No amount of hand-wringing or vapouring will make any difference to the welfare of animals in Europe if the rules are not properly enforced. The strongest possible enforcement is needed and we are doing our utmost to ensure that that is achieved. We are clear in our own minds that enforcement standards are nowhere near high enough. We seek new measures that will be easy to enforce, and we shall continue to impress on other member states and the Commission the need for proper and rigorous enforcement throughout the Community.

The proposals are designed to cover issues that were left outstanding when the original directive on animal transport was adopted in 1991. That directive required the Commission to produce a repeat and, where necessary, proposals on maximum journey times, feeding, watering and resting periods, loading densities and vehicle standards. Document 8441/93 contains those proposals. It is accompanied by an explanatory memorandum in which the Commission explains the basis on which the proposals were drawn up, including the detailed advice that the Commission received from the Scientific Veterinary Committee.

Pending the implementation of the further Community rules, member states are permitted to operate national rules in certain areas. The United Kingdom has taken advantage of that provision. In particular, it has maintained a 15-hour maximum feeding and watering interval for farm animals in transport.

The Government are aware that differences in welfare standards between member states can put our industry at a competitive disadvantage and, most important, deny animals the welfare safeguards that we know to be suitable and essential. We therefore welcome the Commission's proposals as a starting point for discussion. The Government invited more than 500 interested organisations and individuals to comment on the 2 proposals. We received 86 replies and a full set of those responses has been deposited in the Library. The responses have been summarised in the supplementary explanatory memorandum submitted on 19th November, which also summarises the progress made in the negotiations.

The negotiations are exceedingly difficult and complex. I need hardly point out that some member states do not give animal welfare the high priority that is instinctively given by the United Kingdom. Many difficulties have arisen over detailed aspects of the Commission's proposals, which were themselves complex and in some respects inadequately justified scientifically. For example, the proposals on poultry transport were generally considered to be totally impractical and they are expected to be dropped.

The main issues, however, relate to the treatment of the main livestock species during transport. I am glad to say that the principle of giving animals proper care, including water, food and rest, has not been disputed, although the detailed form of those controls remains to be resolved. As I said, those negotiations are now being conducted in Brussels.

A particular issue of the highest importance is the question of limits on long journeys. I am fully aware that the absence from the Commission's proposals of a specific journey limit for slaughter animals has disappointed many people. There are, however, several forms that such limitations can take and the idea of a simple limit on journeys of animals to the slaughterhouse was rejected by the Commission because it would be too simple to evade. The Government's top priority is to find a legal framework of proper welfare safeguards that will work in practice and be easy to enforce.

We also wish to ensure that the Community rules are comprehensive and, in that context, I am pleased to say that we expect the proposals to be amended to include sheep in the new requirements. We have fought hard to achieve that, given the extensive trade in sheep in the Community for which proper welfare standards are clearly required.

The timetable for the adoption of the new measures has, to some extent, been driven by the prospect of Greece taking over the presidency on 1 st January next year. Greece has already stated that animal welfare issues will not progress while it holds the chair. Therefore, the current discussions are regarded as a window of opportunity that may not occur again for some time, and we are supporting the Belgian presidency in its efforts to reach agreement at the Agriculture Council in the week beginning 13th December. I am especially grateful to the Committee for agreeing to hold this debate at a date that will enable us to meet that timetable.

To conclude, I assure the Committee that the Government are pressing with great vigour for measures that will lead to major improvements in animal welfare in the Community, and which will be properly enforceable and reflect the great concern that exists in the country that animals should be afforded the highest possible standards of protection.

We want the rules to ensure proper enforcement. I do not think that there is a hair's breadth between the hon. Lady and I in terms of what we should like to achieve.
