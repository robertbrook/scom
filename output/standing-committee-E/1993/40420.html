Market in Raw Tobacco

10.30 am

The Chairman
: I call the Minister to make his opening statement, which I trust will be relatively brief. After that there is time for questions. Together, questions and the statement can last for up to one hour, after which I shall call the Minister again to move the motion.

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr Michael Jack)
: I shall indeed be relatively brief, Mr. Winterton, but this is a complex subject and I welcome the opportunity to discuss with the Committee the Court of Auditors report on the tobacco regime.

Of all the common agricultural policy regimes, the tobacco regime has rightly attracted the most criticism and the deepest frustration at the huge sums of money involved. The report pulls no punches in confirming that the regime has been the subject of fraud, irregularities and maladministration. It cries out for reform. The United Kingdom has played a leading role in maintaining the pressure for such reform.

The regime began in 1970 when the Common Market organisation, meeting the objectives of the EEC treaty, was introduced, bringing together the national market systems of the then producer member states and recognising GATT obligations with respect to tariff levels. With the accession of the new member states, particularly Greece, the costs, complications and problems of the regime grew. Today, seven of the 12 member states produce some form of tobacco, but the Community's consumption tastes dictate that it must import two thirds of its requirements.

The regime's principal aims were to support production in disadvantaged farming areas where there are few if any alternative crops and work opportunities, to maintain farmers' incomes and to reduce unwanted surpluses by adapting production to the varieties in demand. In practice, the regime led to significant overproduction of unwanted varieties and escalating expenditure to finance intervention and export subsidies.

The regime became the 10th most costly, accounting for just under four per cent. of CAP expenditure, but it is the most costly in terms of support per hectare. In 1994 it is likely to cost more than 1·2 becu—about £930 million. That is too much and it is unacceptable to the Government.

While there were adjustments to the regime in the 1980s, a complete reform was not agreed until 1992. The reform has several implications, from the 1993 harvest onwards. Production subsidies will be limited—the tonnage of 370,000 will be reduced to 350,000 from 1994. More marketable varieties will be encouraged. Intervention and export refunds have been withdrawn, although they could be reinforced in exceptional circumstances. The main producer states have had to establish control agencies to oversee the scheme. The Committee might like to know that similar agencies established for olive oil are already 2 showing results. Last year, Italy reported more than 336 cases of irregularities—a more than threefold increase in the level of reporting.

The Commission estimates that by 1997 expenditure should have been cut by 25 per cent. as a result of the reform. That is a step in the right direction, but it does not go far enough.

Members of the Committee have before them copies of the Court of Auditors report, and they will see that it relates to the period before the reforms. It makes horrifying reading, but the regime has been significantly changed since then, so the Commission has correctly argued that many of the criticisms levelled by the report have already been met.

The report centres on the budgetary and financial aspects of the regime and draws attention to a considerable, but unspecified, sum of Community funds that may have been paid out incorrectly or without adequate safeguard under the old regime, the Commission is investigating those claims, with a view to making arrangements for necessary recoveries.

The Government strongly support the work of the auditors in highlighting such instances of poor financial management or control, and we therefore stress the importance of Commission and member state cooperation. Furthermore, we will seek to ensure that all these investigations are concluded speedily and effectively.

The report demonstrates the importance that the Government attach to improving and tightening controls on fraud. It is worth stating that, as a result of the Maastricht treaty arrangements, there is now a comprehensive financial management framework, which includes an explicit requirement for member states to treat fraud perpetrated against Community budgets as vigorously as fraud against national budgets. That framework also requires the European Court of Auditors to provide a statement of assurance on the reliability of the accounts and the legality and regularity of the underlying transactions. That independent assessment of the Community accounts should identify any problem and enable the Government to press for urgent, remedial action. Under our presidency, we pressed for a refinement in the approach to fraud, especially in risk analysis.

The report focuses on some of the broader principles and it shows the large amount of CAP support per hectare for tobacco. We agree that that support is well out of line with subsidies for other crops and that it should be reformed.

We recognise that the tobacco regime provides a form of income support and employment in isolated areas with very small holdings and few, if any, alternative crop opportunities. But the support is 23 times per hectare that given to cereal production, which is not acceptable or justifiable.

The report draws attention to the apparent anomaly of supporting both tobacco production and anti-smoking initiatives, but with annual Community consumption running at 670,000 tonnes, over two thirds of which is imported, stopping support to tobacco producers will not, in itself, deal with smoking problems.

The Government broadly support the Court's conclusions on those issues. We have always been highly critical of the tobacco regime. Indeed, it was largely because of our efforts—with little support from other member states—that the 1992 reforms were achieved.

JPEG 3 More can be done to improve the regime. There is a Council commitment for review in 1996. But I am sure that the Committee will agree that the report provides an important channel to stimulate work, as the interim focus for the development of future reforms of the regime.

Our policy will be to continue to press for further fundamental reform, especially the reductions in support to which the general criticisms of the report point. The report is a way of putting further pressure on the Commission and other member states. But I do not want to leave the Committee in any doubt about the difficulty of that task, as other member states are not as keen as we are to change the regime.

We shall closely monitor the effectiveness of the new regime, but that will not dissuade us from our efforts to continue to press for further reductions in the amount of budgetary support and to ensure that the new regime is effective.

The Chairman
: Before I call members of the Committee to put their questions, I shall give them some guidance. Having studied the document and sought advice, I should point out that all aspects of Community support for tobacco production, including the social and health costs and tax revenue implications, will be appropriate matters for both question and debate.

I am sure that the Committee is aware that the Select Committee on European Legislation has suggested five issues that this Committee might explore: the use of such large European Agriculture Guidance and Guarantee Funds in support of Community tobacco production; the extent to which the control problems associated with the old tobacco regime have been rectified in the new regime; whether an alternative system based on direct income support would be preferable and workable; the possible health costs associated with Community support for tobacco production; and the tax revenue implications of any change in approach.

I hope that that provides a guide to members of the Committee on the wide nature of the subjects that can be tackled in this morning's debate.

Mr. Jack

I was left scratching my head to discover that, while there is pressure on people to dissuade them from smoking, we have an inordinately expensive regime to produce tobacco in the Community.

Council regulation No. 2075/92 of 30 June 1992 details the reforms of the common market in raw tobacco. I remind the hon. Gentleman of one of the "whereas" clauses which gives a clue as to how we got into this situation:

"Whereas it is the purpose of the common agricultural policy to achieve the objectives of Article 39 of the Treaty, and, in particular in the raw tobacco sector, the stabilization of markets and a fair standard of living for the agricultural population concerned; whereas these objectives may be achieved by adapting resources to requirements, in particular by means of a quality policy."

 Article 39 of the treaty of Rome refers to providing income for rural communities.

When the Community came into existence, four of the six original members produced tobacco. It has been described as an allotment industry. Tobacco is grown on holdings that are usually less than 1 hectare in size. There are 200,000 growers in parts of the Community where it is difficult to see what alternative crops could be grown commercially to provide a living. As the Community seeks to harmonise that which is there, it invented a tobacco regime as part of the common agricultural policy, and tobacco is a legitimate agricultural crop. As the Community has enlarged, the regime has been built on; and now even Belgium and Germany, as well as hot countries such as Greece and Italy, have a small amount of tobacco production. Difficult rural areas are involved and that support has been maintained. We played a leading role in challenging what is clearly outrageous expenditure of public funds.
