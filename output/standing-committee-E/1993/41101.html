Transport of Animals

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mrs. Angela Browning)
: I am pleased to open the debate on such an important subject.

In the matter of animal welfare there is, quite rightly, no subject of more concern to right hon. and hon. Members than the transportation of livestock. Most particularly, that concern has focused on consignments exported to other Community member states, a trade in which there have been several cases of insufficient attention to animal welfare, Frequently, those carrying animals have broken the rules on resting, feeding and watering and have not followed the journey plans declared before travel. Under current EC provisions there is an unacceptable lack of control and enforcement once animals reach the continent.

There is an overriding need for the United Kingdom, together with other northern member states, to bridge the cultural divide and convince certain intransigent Governments that animal welfare is a subject worthy of concern and action. The proposed measure that we are debating today is central to those concerns.

Directive 91/628, which is already in force, lays down some general welfare requirements for animals in transit. However, it does not address the key issues of feeding, watering and resting requirements and journey times, explicitly leaving such subjects to be covered in a further measure. To meet our concerns, such a measure must set the highest possible standards for livestock travelling in the Community and provide effective enforcement mechanisms on which we can rely to ensure that the requirements are observed.

Members of the Committee will, I am sure, be aware of the state of play. The Commission's original proposal has been significantly modified as a result of discussions in the Council of Ministers.

In June, a compromise proposal was on the table which, for adult animals, would have required watering to take place after 15 hours and feeding after 22 hours, following which a rest period would also have been necessary. The United Kingdom joined several other member states in withholding agreement to the proposal which, in our view, did not go far enough. In particular we wanted the Council to examine the possibility of absolute limits on journey length for animals travelling to slaughter, as was reported in our second supplementary memorandum on document 8441/93.

The German presidency made welcome efforts to take the subject forward, setting up a high level group of chief veterinary officers to identify ways of improving the June compromise package, especially on the issues of 2 enforcement and journey limits. The group made some extremely useful progress on some aspects, but disappointingly little progress in other respects.

In Luxembourg last week the Council of Ministers was again divided, depressingly, on the usual lines, and I cannot report that there is any new package or text on the table. Agreement appears attainable on tighter requirements on the training of those who transport livestock, as well as on the introduction of arrangements to license transport companies. That would mean that a member state in which companies were established would have the power to withdraw licences if those companies infringed the directive's requirements anywhere in the Community. There is no agreed new text on such matters and some member states still have problems, but there are signs that the ideas are at least negotiable.

The German presidency's initiative has helpfully enabled discussions to take place on absolute journey limits for slaughter animals.

Unfortunately, the discussions have confirmed that there is a fundamental divide among member states. Southern member states argue that they need to import live animals and that an absolute journey limit would cut off their supplies and close down slaughterhouses. Those member states comprise a substantial blocking minority and I foresee little likelihood of early progress on that issue.

There is, at least, some good news on UK concerns. A licensing requirement for livestock transport companies would be an important gain for the UK. It would help our problems with exports and we have pressed hard for it. However, we believe that discussion on journey time must continue, although it is evident that progress on that will be difficult. The German presidency has declared its intention of persevering in its quest for consensus and we shall continue to fight for the best achievable Community measures. Meanwhile, we shall also press ahead with our own plans to amend our domestic legislation to create offences that may be prosecuted in a British court when exporters fail to follow the export journey plan that they have declared to us. That will be a significant improvement, but it in no way alters the fact that, in the longer term, an effective and enforceable European Union measure must be the overriding priority.

We now look to the resumption of discussions, probably initially at working group level. We need to bear in mind that failure to agree a Community measure during this German presidency is likely to result in a substantial period of inactivity at Community level. There is no doubting the overriding importance of securing an enforceable Community measure on the subject. There is a narrow window of opportunity to secure a satisfactory agreement during the German presidency, given that the presidency then passes on to France and, thereafter, to Spain and Italy.

Mrs. Browning
: We are looking for higher standards of welfare through compliance with approved journey plans. Those plans will involve feeding, watering and resting of animals, and they will incorporate current legislation about the pre-inspection of journeys, to ensure that animals are properly rested and fed before a journey, and they will ensure that animals have been deemed by a vet to be fit and healthy enough to travel. More importantly, once animals have crossed the channel and journey through other countries—which is where most problems have been experienced—we should ensure that the journey plan has been put into effect. The plan should be approved, and checks should be made to ensure that each resting place on each part of the journey accords with the plan. The plan should be returned within 10 days of completion of the journey, so that one can check whether the plan has been followed. If it had not been followed, we should want the opportunity to bring the offender before the courts.

(East Lothian)
: I agree with a certain amount of what was said by the hon. Member for Ribble Valley, except that it is even more important that Scottish meat should be labelled "Scottish meat" to be sold to our oldest historical allies across the water in France, where I am sure it will continue to sell very well.

This is the first European Standing Committee that I have attended and it has been an interesting and worthwhile experience. I join my hon. Friend the Member for Falkirk, East in complimenting the Minister for the capable way in which she has responded to questions. I hope that she will recognise that strong representations have been made, from both sides of the Committee, by Members from rural and urban constituencies about the important subject of the transportation of live animals.

My hon. Friend the Member for Glanford and Scunthorpe mentioned the dramatic reduction in the number of slaughterhouses that operate in the United Kingdom. As I understand it, that cut has largely been because of the high cost of raising slaughterhouses in the United Kingdom to the European Community's high standards. That has been a painful exercise for the operators of slaughterhouses, who have had to spend much money so that they can have access to the mainland European market for meat. It is absurb that, having made those improvements, we are still indulging in the primitive and crude business of exporting livestock on the hoof, so that animals can be slaughtered at premises at the other end of the European Community, which may not be up to the same standard as our own. There is an inconsistency in the way that the Common Market has dealt with the market for meat. The Minister may care to consider that matter in more detail.

Much has been said about the cultural differences between the north and south of Europe, particularly about people's attitudes towards animals. The hon. Member for Ribble Valley said that people in southern Europe might be critical of our cultural attitudes to animals—and rightly so. There are major differences in the pattern of agricultural production; there are fewer extensively reared grazing animals in southern Europe, for obvious reasons. It is hotter in southern Europe than northern Europe, and there is less rainfall, so people from the south might not be as familiar with the livestock that we have been talking about.

It is important to realise that if people from southern Europe feel that they are being browbeaten by arrogant politicians from northern Europe—who might say that the southerners are primitive and get things wrong—they will react negatively. There are better ways of persuading people that better practices are available. One way is to use influence of the market, to use one of the Government's arguments. If southern Europeans discovered that they 24 could buy meat that had been slaughtered close to the point of production—whether England, Scotland, Ireland or northern Europe—and transported cheaply, on the hook in chilled containers, to southern Europe, they might realise that such practices were not such a bad idea after all. Why not use the market argument to overcome the problem? A persuading job needs to be done. Politicians and the animal welfare lobby might consider the way that they go about persuading people.

On my travels during the recess, while I was driving a lorry all the way from Edinburgh to Yugoslavia—during the journey, by the way, nobody checked by tachograph—I was surprised to see advertising material in motorway service stations in Germany campaigning against the transportation of live animals over long distances. Pressure is building up.

The European Commission needs to take an overview of transportation policy. I hope that it will take a lead and make constructive recommendations to Governments. New regulations should enforce tolerable standards for the transportation of animals. I can think of no one better to do that than the new European Transport Commissioner.

I wish that more people would stand back and realise that it is not a straight choice between the transport of live animals for slaughter as fresh meat near to consumption on the hoof, and frozen meat on the hook. 12.8 pm

Mrs. Browning
: I am grateful to hon. Members on both sides of the Committee for a constructive debate on an emotive subject. We have covered the more practical aspects of how progress can be made in solving the difficulties and problems that we have encountered with animal transportation.

The issue of enforcement has been raised many times this morning. Whatever improvements are made to regulations, it is important that they are enforceable, both with our proposals in the United Kingdom and, more importantly, on an EU-wide basis. Our first step is to ensure that the requirements are agreed so that they are applicable throughout the Community. Next, we need tighter obligations on enforcement written into the regulations. We are all aware that it is all very well to have regulations written down, but practical enforcement is often a problem.

Once we have achieved that, it is important to get member states to act by establishing bilateral contracts. We must also press the Commission inspectorate to play an active role in ensuring that the regualtions are properly enforced and to oversee that enforcement.

A licensing system for transport companies is also important. The hon. Member for Glanford and Scunthorpe mentioned existing cases of companies with a 26 bad track record. I repeat my reassurance that, though current regulations do not enable us to take action, in our proposed reforms for the United Kingdom we want to be able to point the finger and act against individuals or the licensing of their companies rather than simply look at the journey plan as we are now obliged to do.

Several hon. Members have mentioned labelling. As I have said, existing legislation does not enable us to enforce or insist on country of origin or methods of production, but I assure the Committee that I will examine the matter in a Euro-wide context, as several hon. Members requested.

The hon. Member for Glanford and Scunthorpe also mentioned trans-shipment. It is a problem, but the 1991 directive requires the Commission to lay down detailed requirements on facilities and activities in the staging posts at various points on the journey. Although trans-shipment is not the main purpose of the directive, it could be covered under the regulation. We have pressed for that and will continue to do so.

We are determined to stand our ground and achieve measures that will set the highest possible standards for animal welfare in transportation. Tighter enforcement measures are critical and we shall seek tough sanctions that we shall be determined to use and that we shall require others to apply to ensure that the necesary standards are observed. I assure hon. Members that my right hon. Friend has worked, and will continue to work, vigorously in the Council to make sure that the United Kingdom's aims come to fruition.

Question put and agreed to.
