Trans-European Transport Networks:

Guidelines

10.30 am

The Minister for Roads and Traffic (Mr. Robert Key)
: Good morning, Mr. Martin. May I say once again what a pleasure it is to serve under your chairmanship.

Following the entry into force of the treaty on European Union last November, the European Community treaty has a new title covering trans-European networks, or TENs. TENs are infrastructure networks in transport, telecommunications and energy. In the single market, linking infrastructure networks at the European level is now of considerable economic importance.

According to the treaty, a series of so-called guidelines is to be adopted by the Council of Ministers. They are to include specific projects of common interest, which the Community may support financially. The Commission's proposals for guidelines in three sectors—telecommunications, energy and transport—are now under consideration in Brussels.

In the case of transport, decisions covering combined transport, road and inland waterways were adopted on 29 October 1993. The Commission's current proposal is aimed at integrating the network guidelines of different transport modes into a single transport trans-European network. The proposal covers airports, ports, rail, and shipping navigation and air traffic control components and updates the three networks agreed last year.

Separately from the work on the guidelines, the Christophersen group has been working to identify priority projects in transport and energy. In its interim report to the Corfu European Council in June, the group identified a first list of 11 priority projects in the transport sector. The projects were endorsed by the European Council, and the member states were asked to make every effort to ensure that they were begun during 1996 at the latest. At present, we have two projects of United Kingdom interest in the top priority list—the proposed new channel tunnel rail link and a railway link from Larne via Belfast to the Republic of Ireland. The Christophersen group will submit a final report to the next European Council in Essen in December, by which time projects may well have been added to the list.

Although designation as a priority project by the Christophersen group has no legal significance, it is likely that those transport projects will form the core of the so-called "projects of common interest" to be adopted by the Transport Council later this year.

2 TENs projects will be financed from a mix of public and private sector sources. There will be three primary sources of EC grant support for TENs: the existing cohesion and structural funds, and finance under a proposed TENs financial regulation covering interest rate subsidies, the payment of loan guarantee premiums, support for feasibility studies, and, in exceptional cases, cofinancing of projects. EC loans and guarantees will also continue to be available from the European investment bank and European investment fund respectively. None the less, the private sector and national Governments will continue to be the most important sources of TENs finance.

I should emphasise that designation by the Christophersen group of a priority project does not guarantee EC money. Projects will have to be considered on a case-by-case basis against the criteria and funding rules established for each of the applicable funding instruments.

The Corfu council did not endorse the Commission's view that new Community financial instruments were necessary to fund the priority projects. It decided instead that the financing needs of each of the projects should first be examined on a case-by-case basis. That work is now getting under way in a series of study groups. The Christopherson group report was considered by European Standing Committee B on 6 July together with the proposed TENs financial regulation.

The present proposal seeks to define the objectives, scope and priorities of the transport network and to establish criteria for the selection of projects of common interest. The scope of the network is defined in terms of the individual transport modes so that there is a network for roads, another for rail, and so on.

The proposal also covers related management and control systems, such as air traffic control and the vessel traffic management information system.

The components of the network guidelines are at various stages of development and the Government have proposed several amendments, particularly to the rail and road networks. The proposal defines airports of common interest based on a set of criteria. There is no proposed network for ports, but the Commission has instead put forward a proposed set of criteria for assessing ports projects. As I have said, the Commission's objective is to integrate the network guidelines for all transport modes into a single trans-European transport network.

The Government consider that the establishment of trans-European transport networks will make an important contribution to the functioning of the single market and thereby to the competitiveness of European business.

We have made efforts to ensure that sufficient UK routes are included in each of the networks. That is important to ensure that the UK has the opportunity to obtain a fair share of Community funds.

There are several ways in which the proposed decision could be further improved. First, in accordance with the JPEG 3 principle of subsidiarity, it should be made explicit that decisions on infrastructure development must remain the responsibility of the individual member states, as is the case for the three transport guidelines already agreed. Secondly, private as well as public sector transport projects should be eligible. Thirdly, the definition of the term "project of common interest" needs to be clarified; there needs to be a clear understanding of which projects are eligible for support, and which are not. Finally, provisions should be included which will ensure that, as far as possible, distortion of competition is avoided, particularly in the ports and airports sectors. That is important for the UK because of the high proportion of facilities in private sector ownership.

Since the decision was adopted by the Commission, member states have met three times at official level to discuss it. Some progress has been made on some of the points that I have just mentioned and we have received good support from other member states, but much work needs to be done.

In view of the commitments made by Heads of State and Government at Brussels and Corfu, I hope that members of the Committee will agree that the proposal is important, that its adoption will make a substantial contribution to the establishment of a trans-European network in the transport sector and that the Government's proposed approach to negotiations on the draft decision is the right one.
