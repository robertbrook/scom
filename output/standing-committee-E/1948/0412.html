    3. 1102
  3. while receiving mental treatment as a voluntary patient under the Mental Treatment Act, 1930, or under any such law as is mentioned in paragraph ( b) of this subsection, being treatment which follows without any interval a period during which he was detained as mentioned in paragraph ( a) or paragraph ( b) of this subsection."

The Chairman
: That will be convenient to the Committee.

Mr. Manningham-Buller
: The object of these Amendments is to provide that the rights of a spouse to divorce the other spouse on the grounds of insanity shall not depend upon where the other spouse may happen to have been detained in the British Isles. As the law now stands a court can grant a decree of divorce on proof that a person is of incurably unsound mind and that that person has been continuously under care and treatment for at least five years immediately preceding the petition. "Care and treatment" is defined, and one meaning of that phrase is detention pursuant to an order under the Lunacy and Mental Treatment Acts, 1890 to 1930; but if the detention is under the Lunacy (Scotland) Acts, that detention cannot count as part of the period of five years which it is necessary to establish for the purpose of obtaining a decree in the English courts.

The Committee will see that by subsection ( a) of the second Amendment it is intended to provide that in future detention under the Lunacy (Scotland) Acts shall count towards that period of five years. This is really only bringing the law of England into line with that of Scotland because detention under the Lunacy Acts of England counts if proceedings are brought in Scotland for divorce on grounds of insanity. No one will object to that. Subsection ( b) is intended to make the same extension with regard to Northern Ireland, the Isle of Man or the Channel Islands. If we are to include the period of detention in Scotland, it seems a little illogical that detention in the Channel Islands, the Isle of Man or Northern Ireland should be excluded.

I do not think that I need do more than draw attention to the fact that there is, in subsection ( b), the phrase:

"treatment as a person of unsound mind or a person suffering from mental illness …"

The inclusion of the phrase

"a person suffering from mental illness"

JPEG 1103 does not extend the law at all. In this country, when a person is having temporary treatment and then an order is made for him to continue receiving that treatment, the detention for the period for the purpose of obtaining a divorce will start from the date of the making of the order. The effect of subsection ( b) is to provide that the same will happen in Northern Ireland, the Isle of Man or the Channel Islands. It cannot happen in Scotland because there is no provision there for voluntary treatment similar to the voluntary treatment under the 1930 Act.

Subsection ( c) merely brings this part of this provision into line with the 1937 Act, which provided that voluntary treatment following immediately upon detention within the meaning of the Act could be counted towards the five-year period. These Amendments are therefore merely intended to make it possible to obtain a divorce for insanity even though the spouse happens to have been detained outside England but in some other part of the British Isles. I think that this proposal will be entirely uncontroversial. I hope no one will oppose it. It will do considerable good in a small number of cases, and it is a change in the law which is worth making.

Clause 3.
—(WARDS OF COURT.)

Mr. Manningham-Buller
: I beg to move, in page 1, line 18, at the beginning, to insert:

"Subject to the provisions of this section."

It would be convenient if we could also consider the next Amendment, in line 18, to leave out from "Court," to the end of the Clause, and to add:

"except by virtue of an order to that effect made by the Court.

  1. Where application is made for such an order in respect of an infant, the infant shall become a ward of Court on the making of the application, but shall cease to be a ward of Court at the expiration of such period as may be prescribed by rules of court unless within that period an order has been made in accordance with the application.
  2. The Court may, either upon an application in that behalf or without such an appli- JPEG 1107 cation, order that any infant who is for the time being a ward of Court shall cease to be a ward of Court."

It may surprise some hon. Members to know that any British infant can be made a ward of court by merely settling a small sum of money upon that infant and then starting an action to administer the trust. That can be done without the consent of either parent, and it can be done in relation to any child which is British. It may be that a child who has never visited this country can be made a ward of court by that simple operation under the law as it stands, without the consent of the court, and then all the consequences of being a ward of court will flow directly that child happens to be brought within these shores. Once a ward of court, as the law stands, always a ward of court until the child reaches the age of 21. It cannot thereafter be taken out of the jurisdiction without the consent of the court, and the court has control over the education and marriage of that infant.

This procedure, which was originally intended to benefit the interests of the infant and to be for the protection of the infant, is liable to abuse and has been abused. It is possible, for instance, for a lady who has spent the whole of her life in a Dominion to come over here, leaving her husband and bringing the child of the marriage with her, and, by the simple action I have described, to make the child a ward of court, whereupon the husband will have great difficulty in getting the child hack to the country where it has been all its youth. The procedure as it now stands can be used as nothing more nor less than a trap, and, without notifying the child or the other parent it could be done at any time. For instance, where a husband invites his wife, from whom he was divorced, say, 10 years ago, to bring their child into this country for a short holiday of a fortnight or so, undertaking that he will pay all expenses, he can make the child a ward of court and make it impossible for the child to be taken out of the country back to the country where its home is, without the consent of the court first being obtained.

The object of these Amendments is to ensure that that sort of abuse cannot be carried on in future and to ensure that while a child can be made a ward of 1108 court by an application, that will not last long unless an order of the court is obtained. Whereas the application will have the effect of making the child a ward of court for a short period, that period will end unless the order of the court has been obtained and, of course, the court will have regard to the infant's interests. The proposed change carries out, in proper language, the provisions of Clause 3, which provides that if a child is to be a ward of court for a long period the consent of the court must first be obtained. It may be very important in the interests of the child that the court shall have power to terminate the period during which the infant is a ward of court. I am sure these Amendments will be welcomed in all branches of the profession. The Denning Committee reported in favour of the change and the Bar Council are also in favour.

New Clause.
—(EVIDENCE OF ACCESS.)

(1) Notwithstanding any rule of law, the evidence of a husband or wife shall be admissible in any proceedings for divorce to prove that marital intercourse did or did not take place between them during any period.

(2) Notwithstanding anything in this section or any rule of law, a husband or wife shall not be compellable in such proceedings to give evidence of the matters aforesaid.—[ Mr. Manningham-Buller].

Brought up, and read the First time.

1112

Mr. Manningham-Buller
: I beg to move, "That the Clause be read a Second time."

I expect most hon. Members of the Committee, and all the legal Members, fully appreciate the effect of this Clause, and that most of them are familiar with the decision in the case of Russell v. Russell in 1924 in the House of Lords. That case decided that:

"Where a child which might have been conceived in wedlock has been born alive to the wife, neither the husband nor the wife is permitted, with the object of proving that the child is or is not the child of the husband, to give evidence showing or tending to show that they did or did not have sexual relations with each other at any time during wedlock when the child might have been conceived."

That is a statement taken from a leading textbook—Rayden on Divorce—and it correctly sets out the effect of that decision, which has been valid since 1924. Its effect has not been fortunate, because it has meant, not that evidence cannot be given of non-access, but that the parties have to incur great expense in proving non-access by calling other witnesses. The Denning Committee, in its final Report (Cmd. 7024), recommended that this rule in the case of Russell v. Russell should be abolished. It has also been recommended by the Bar Council and the Law Society, and I do not think there is any body of lawyers which would not now wish to see the rule which was established in that case rescinded.

This matter is dealt with in subsection (1) of the new Clause. The effect of this subsection is that the evidence of the husband or wife shall be admissible in any proceedings for divorce to prove that marital intercourse did or did not take place. When we make the evidence of a person admissible, there arises the question of whether that person can be compelled to give evidence, and we take the view that it would be quite wrong to say that either the husband or the wife should be compelled to go into the witness box and say "I have (or have not) had relations with my husband (or wife)," as the case may be.

Subsection (2) did in effect over-rule the decision of the Court of Appeal in a recent case, Tilley v. Tilley, which was decided in 1948, where it was held that the wife, in the circumstances of that case, at the instance of the co-respondent, JPEG 1113 could be compelled to answer whether or not, after the commission of adultery with the co-respondent, she had had intercourse with her husband. If this new Clause is passed in its present form, the wife would not be compelled to give evidence in that way, and I think that would be a very good thing indeed. I, therefore, commend the new Clause to the Committee.

Sir T. Moore
: I would like to congratulate my hon and learned Friend on his wisdom in seeking to reverse the rule of law which has existed since the Russell case was decided. It would be improper to allow the discussion to proceed without mentioning the fact that the rule of law in the Russell case has never been accepted and has never operated in Scotland. Although I would prefer this to be made clear by the Lord Advocate, it should be on record that this is just one more of those instances where Scotland has given a very profitable and proper lead to England. As in many other cases, England is now prepared to alter the law or to bring the law of England into line with the operation of the law in Scotland, and I would like the Lord Advocate to confirm that that view is correct.

The Lord Advocate
: These Private Members' Bills provide strange bedmates, and, for the first time in my career, I find myself in complete agreement with the hon. and gallant Gentleman. What he has just said is yet another illustration of the great missionary work which we do in Scotland. We show the light, and eventually it penetrates, and this is one of the instances of penetration. We should accept this admitted superiority of the Scottish law in this connection, and I suggest to English lawyers that if they look back further they will find many other cases in which they might profitably adopt Scottish law.

Mr. Selwyn Lloyd
: Had we been entirely a male gathering. I could have made some comments on what has been said by the Lord Advocate and my hon. and gallant Friend. New Clause.
—(ADDITIONAL POWER OF COURT TO MAKE ORDER FOR MAINTENANCE.)

Mr. Manningham-Buller
: I beg to move, "That the Clause be read a Second time."

A good deal was said about the importance of the previous new Clause. In my view this new Clause is perhaps even more important, or at least equally as important, as the last one we considered. The Committee will be aware that an English woman who marries a man whose domicile is outside England acquires by marriage the domicile of her husband, and that, apart from one exception made by the 1944 Act, in consequence of taking her husband's domicile she is debarred, in most cases, from recourse to the English courts for a divorce or a decree of nullity.

Attention was drawn to this—attention has been drawn to it more than once, of course—by the Denning Committee, which said, in paragraph 82 of its final Report:

"This rule acts with particular hardship on English women who marry men from the Dominions or from places abroad. A wife by law takes the domicile of her husband. If the marriage breaks down, her natural tendency is to remain with, or return to, her relatives in this country, but nevertheless in order to obtain a divorce, she has to proceed in the courts of the country where her husband is domiciled, and this is so even if they are both resident here."

The Report goes on to point out that the rule has been modified in a few cases under the Matrimonial Causes (War Marriages) Act, 1944, and says:

"These modifications do not in our opinion go far enough. In particular, the 1944 Act does not meet the case where an English woman has married a man from over- JPEG 1121 seas and after living with him a short time in his home land is deserted by him and, being left stranded, returns to her relatives in this country. As the law stands at present she cannot obtain a divorce here but has to go to his country for the purpose."

Well, that Report correctly states the law as it is today, but I hope that after the passage of this Bill, with the addition of this new Clause, that law will be altered.

The effect of this new Clause will be that any English woman resident in this country, who has been ordinarily resident here for three years immediately preceding the commencement of proceedings, shall have recourse to the courts of England, even though her husband is domiciled outside England, provided that he is not domiciled in some other part of the British Isles. It is quite right to exclude that particular category, because Scotland has a law of its own, in which it naturally takes great pride, and there would be some difficulty in making this new Clause apply where the domicile of the husband is Scottish and that of the wife English.

This new Clause will deal with a large number of cases where, for instance, the wife has married someone—maybe of foreign nationality—whose home is in some far distant part of the world, where the marriage has broken up and the wife cannot get her freedom without making a journey to that distant and remote place. I myself think that this new Clause will make a very great improvement in the law. I am glad to know that it has the backing of, not only the Denning Committee but the Bar Council and the Law Society. I therefore commend it to the Committee.
