Major Legge-Bourke: I beg to move, in page 1, line 17, at the end, to add:

(4) Nothing in this section shall prohibit or make unlawful, or render any person liable to any penalty in respect of the docking of any heavy horse in accordance with the Animals (Anaesthetics) Act, 1919, or any statutory modification or re-enactment of that Act for the time being in force.

I did not take any part in the Second Reading proceedings, and I feel that I owe the Committee an explanation. I should like to make it clear that I think docking is a very unpleasant thing that has to be done in certain cases. I have seen it in a military capacity and in a civil one, and I should say that where docking is done solely from the point of view of fashion it is quite reprehensible and that there is no justification for it. I felt that when this Bill was presented to Parliament it would probably meet with considerable sympathy from all parts of the House, as I believe it has, and that it Col. 1464 was certainly one which I should feel it would be right in principle to support.

At the same time I felt that farmers might have a different point of view, and very soon after it was presented on 28th January, I did get in touch with my local farmers' union but at that time all I could get was an expression of opinion by the secretary that he thought that probably most people would object to the idea that there should be no more docking—in the Fens anyway—but that there had not been a meeting of the union about it. Unfortunately that meeting was not held until after the Second Reading. In the meantime I had a word with the promoter of the Bill, and I informed him that I thought my local farmers' union might be opposed to it. He told me that he understood that the breed societies were not objecting, and were rather glad that a decision was to be made either way. I passed that back to my local farmers' union, and they definitely protested at once, but unfortunately, that protest did not arrive before the Second Reading. Had it done so, I should have said then pretty much what I am saying today in Committee.

I wish to make that one comment on the hon. Member who moved this Bill on Second Reading, because he did say, as reported in col. 751 of the OFFICIAL REPORT on 4th March,

"I have received no objections from any source since this Bill was published."—[OFFICIAL REPORT, 4th March, 1949; Vol. 462, c. 751.]

I accept his assurance, but I think he will recall that I did mention to him what my local people were feeling, and on the day of the Second Reading I did say that I had not had a definite confirmation one way or the other. Naturally there is a general feeling that docking ought to be prevented, certainly so far as riding horses are concerned, and horses which are docked only for show purposes, but we have to ask ourselves whether what we are doing will interfere with the working of horses by people who know far more than we know about the problem. It is because I am convinced that there is a case to be made out on those grounds that I am moving this Amendment.

I am impressed by the unanimity of opinion which exists in the breed societies. The Under-Secretary of the Home Office was good enough to receive a deputation Image Col. 1465 from the breed societies, which I attended, and he did hear their case. I do not want to weary those who know the case very well by putting it all over again, but I do think these figures, which were not known at the time, should be known. The Shire, Clydesdale, Suffolk, and British Percheron horse societies each circulated all their members with a view to finding out how many were opposed to the Bill or in favour of it. The Shire Horse Society, with a membership of 3,500, has eight members who took the trouble to write in and say they were in favour of the Bill. In the case of the Clydesdale Society, with a membership of 3,500, six members wrote to say they were in favour. Out of 1,000 members of the Suffolk Society five wrote in, and. in the case of the British Percherons, again five wrote out of a membership of 1,000. In other words, over 99 per cent. of the members of the heavy breed societies have not opposed the action which those societies took in approaching the Home Secretary and myself.

Mr. Moody: How many have objected to the Bill?

Major Legge-Bourke: I gave the figures of those who have written in to say that they approved of the Bill.

Mr. Moody: Is the hon. Member saying that only 1 per cent. have written in to object?

Major Legge-Bourke: It appears that less than 1 per cent. have written in to say they are in favour of the Bill.

Colonel Clarke: Could the hon. and gallant Member read to us exactly what question was put to the members, what information was asked for?

Major Legge-Bourke: I have here the letter which one of the heavy horse societies, the Shire Horse Society, sent round to its members, and I think that it would be only fair to read it:

"Docking and Nicking of Horses Bill. 'You may be aware that this Bill, now before Parliament, would make it illegal (except in case of injury or disease certified by a qualified veterinary surgeon), under pain of a penalty of £25 and

or three months' imprisonment, to dock or nick any horse. It is also proposed to prohibit the importation into the United Kingdom of all docked or nicked horses. This Society, in collaboration with the other Heavy Horse Societies (Clydesdale, Suffolk and British Percheron) has taken Col. 1466 steps to draw attention to the very weighty objections to prohibiting docking of the working heavy horse, and to the very serious results which it is feared would inevitably follow the passing of the Bill into law in its present form. It is now proposed to take further steps to oppose the Bill in Parliament, with the support of the members of this Society; and it is accordingly desired to ascertain their views on the matter. I shall be very much obliged, therefore, if you will let me know, as early as possible, whether you are in favour of, or against, the docking of heavy horses. The grounds which the Society propose to put forward in opposition are shortly set out overleaf. If you approve the action which the Society is taking you can assist by writing to your local Member of Parliament (at the House of Commons, London, S.W.I), enlisting his support in opposing the Bill."

Having read that, I had better put before the Committee the statement which accompanied that letter, setting forth the objections to the Bill at the headquarters of the breed society itself. They are:

"(1) increased accidents, especially on the farm, caused by reins getting under un-docked tails, particularly where teams are worked with single lines.

  1. increased accidents, due to long tails getting caught in tip carts, chains, etc.
  2. sore docks due to contact with shafts or other parts of carts or implements, and to dirt and grit picked up (especially in winter) by a long tail.
  3. increased difficulties in getting labour to work undocked horses and extra work and time in grooming them.
  4. discouragement of the use of the heavy horse, owing to extra risk of accidents and work involved, and consequential decline in working horse population, loss of land fertility, etc., which would be in direct conflict with the expressed policy of the Government.
  5. insufficient advantage to the horse itself, inasmuch as the tail could be close clipped and the animal thereby deprived of the means to deal with flies (the preservation of which means is said to be the principal reason for the Bill), while the practice of plucking or thinning an undocked tail can be extremely painful to the horse and may itself result in sore or tender docks."

That was what the Shire Horse Society sent out to all its members, and I think it is in line with the letters and memoranda sent out by the other breed societies to their members. As regards the reins getting under the tail, it can be argued that if a line or rein gets underneath a docked tail it can cause that horse to start kicking and probably to injure itself, but I think what we have to accept is that when a horse has been docked there is less chance of the line Image Col. 1467 getting under the tail than there is when the tail has been left long. I think that is an objection which has been raised in the past to the case put up on behalf of farmers to the effect that the line getting under the tail is one of the major dangers. On the matter of cleanliness, it is perhaps forgotten that the horse-keeper is on the decline, quite definitely, in the countryside. In other parts of England he is known by a different name, but in the Fens he is the horse-keeper. Some farmers find it difficult to get any horse-keeper. There is no doubt that in nine cases out of ten a horse-keeper who was asked for his opinion would say that he would prefer to look after a docked horse than an undocked horse, because a tail does require a good deal of attention, especially in areas where the ground becomes very muddy in winter. The tail can be plaited, but if a horsekeeper is looking after a good number of horses he might have to spend the best part of a couple of hours before his day's work has begun in plaiting horses' tails. There is nothing worse, in my military experience, than leaving a horse with its tail plaited indefinitely. The tail should be left free to allow the air to get into it, and by leaving it plaited one is inviting a form of dirtiness in horse management which is quite unpermissible to anyone who has the welfare of horses at heart. There is the danger of getting lice in the tail unless it is well washed out. I do not know if hon. Members know what a Fen drove looks like in Winter-time. I have had letters saying how difficult it is to get horses down a drove in these times and when they are used in Winter-time a horse's tail has often to be washed out.

11.15 a.m.

The hon. Member who moved the Second Reading of the Bill said that the main purpose of docking was fashion and for the purpose of showing horses. I cannot agree with that. To my mind there is no question that originally it was done to keep the tail out of the way in farm work and also for military use. Only when horse shows began to be held and farmers wanted to show their horses did horses with docked tails come into the shows. I think it wrong to say that it was originally done because of fashion. Incidentally, the word "dock" is, I understand, an Icelandic word which Col. 1468 means a short tail. Probably in Iceland they cut them because of ice and snow as we do in the Fens because of mud, and it is a practical operation. It would be a great mistake to assume that all people who dock their horses in rural areas are doing so purely in order to be able to show them. I am absolutely convinced that is not the case.

I fully accept that most of the horses in the show ring are docked, but that does not prove that they are docked in order to get into the show ring; in fact, I know they are not. I ask the Committee to believe that there is a very real problem for the farmer to get his labour and to avoid having accidents on his farm and keeping his horse in a clean condition, particularly in regard to its tail.

One point I should also mention is the matter of flies. In the Second Reading Debate hon. Members mentioned the fly muscle. It is true that the fly muscle covers the majority of the body, certainly all the body which a full-length tail will not reach. I have had considerable experience of horses suffering from flies because, in 1940, we went out to Palestine with our horses and they became more out-of-date every day that we stayed there. I can assure hon. Members that we had some docked horses, in fact I had one, for many uses, and they managed to get through that period. I grant that they must have suffered something, and I believe that if we had let the hair grow long they would not have suffered so much as they did. But they got through and got used to it. When they first went on the open horse line shackled by one leg, we found they were very irritable to begin with, but, after about a month, they acted exactly as horses with long tails. We must not overlook the fact that there are now dressings which can be put on to keep flies away to some extent, although in my experience nothing will keep away the horse fly, neither a long tail nor anything else. If he makes up his mind that he is to bite, he will do so.

A few days ago I was driving through my constituency and saw four young Suffolk horses turned out. All had been docked and the hair on the tails had been allowed to grow after docking. It was the sort of day that we have been having in the last three weeks or so and there were many flies about and practically no wind. It was blazing hot, but Image Col. 1469 the horses were grazing in the field perfectly quietly and seemed able to flick off their backs all the flies which they could not get away by means of the fly muscle.

I am convinced that these farmers have a very real case. The Ministry of Agriculture have put forward a strong plea to encourage horse breeding and I have heard the Minister congratulate the breed societies on the work they have done in improving the breeds. The breed societies are convinced that they are right in docking. If some hon. Members feel that perhaps the way they went about obtaining the opinion of their members was a little rushed, I am certain that my own farmers and others in other constituencies did not know that the Bill was coming on when it did. Some of the breed societies' headquarters had considered it, but the ordinary members in the constituencies had not realised it was coming on as soon as it did. They have been given an opportunity of saying that they approve and I have given the number who say they approve of the Bill. I hope the Committee will feel that it is right at this time—I do not say for ever—for us to exclude the heavy horses of the pedigree stock such as those administered by breed societies.

May I touch on the subject of foals and old horses? I am assured by my local farmers' union, and it is certainly the custom in the Isle of Ely, that when foals are born in the Summer their tails are left on until the end of the Summer to help them through the fly period. There is no question that docking can be carried out quite painlessly, and the Section of the Anaesthetics Act to which reference has been made makes it illegal to dock without a general or local anaesthetic, rendering the whole operation painless to the horse. I hope hon. Members will realise that the operation is painless. I hope I have convinced the Committee in regard to old horses by the illustration I gave of the four Suffolks I saw the other day, that allowing the hair of the docked tail to grow and cover that part of the back not covered by the fly muscle is the rule. It ensures that the old horse does not necesarily suffer misery when turned out in its old age. I ask the Committee, as a practical proposition, to accept the Amendment because I believe it is essential for the cause of agriculture at the Col. 1470 moment, although I believe that perhaps at some future time we may find some way of overcoming these difficulties.

Mr. M. Philips Price: I am quite prepared to admit that, certainly as far as convenience, the passage of this Bill will make it more difficult for farmers under certain conditions concerning the use of horses on the farms. But I feel there is no excuse whatever for insisting upon those conditions and inflicting what we are forced to the conclusion is a certain amount of suffering on the animal. There is no excuse for that, even if it means a little more difficulty in the management of horses under certain conditions. Nor am I so much impressed by the statistics and arguments which the hon. and gallant Member for the Isle of Ely (Major Legge-Bourke) has given in regard to the position in the Fens. Farms are more mechanised now, and I have only one horse, whereas I formerly had six and I have four tractors and formerly had none. But I have frequently driven a horse on a side rake, or an ordinary rake, and known what it is to get the driving rein under the tail. It is a nuisance, but if one holds the rein in a certain way one can generally avoid the trouble. Generally it happens when one is watching something else for a moment and lets the rein get loose so that the tail comes over it. One has to be continually watching and keep one's attention on driving. If it does happen one has to stop the horse, get down and lift the rein over the tail and start again. It means a little more trouble. One can have trouble with a tip cart. When the cart is tipped back the rein may come under the horse's tail. One has to see that it is slowly tilted back and that all is clear. There is more trouble about keeping the horse's tail clean and it can get very dirty, but, if one keeps the horse, one must look after it and do everything possible to avoid any kind of cruelty. The curse of flies at a time like this is so Image Col. 1471 great that there is no trouble which is too much to be taken to prevent this suffering. The hon. and gallant Gentleman certainly put his case extremely well and I congratulate him on it, but he has not convinced me and I do not think he has convinced the Committee.

Brigadier Thorp: I am glad my hon. and gallant Friend the Member for the Isle of Ely (Major Legge-Bourke) put down the Amendment, because we must look at the matter from a realistic point of view. The point is whether we are going to lose more horses off agricultural land which, I understand, is against the wishes of the Minister of Agriculture. Members that getting something under a horse's tail cannot always be prevented by the word "Whoa." I remember on occasions when my polo stick caught underneath the pony's tail and it did not stop it. In fact, I remember occasions when the only answer when I was on my back was to pull the polo stick out. Naturally, I would not oppose the Bill on that ground. I want only to get a realistic view in the case of horses working on farms.

11.30 a.m.

The hon. Member for the Forest of Dean (Mr. Philips Price) mentioned that he did not think the Fens were very different from other parts of the country. I do not know about that. I know the Fens slightly, but I know my own part of Northumberland better. If he will come with me he will find it very hard to find a cart horse or a horse working on a farm, either cross-breed or thoroughbred, that has not been docked. Therefore, up in Northumberland, where it may not fee such deep going as in the Fens, the farmers and the workers think it is necessary to have the horses docked. I admit that in probably all cases hair is allowed to grow on the dock, which covers most of the area which the fly muscle does not cover.

As regards keeping horses clean the hon. Member for the Forest of Dean was applauded when he said that when a horse is kept it should be looked after. Col. 1472 I could not agree more with him. but in these days of shortage of labour and lack of knowledge of horses, it is not always easy for the owners to impress that necessity on the people who actually tend the animals. The cleaning of a horse's tail to keep lice away is a big job. I have a great deal of experience of that, because the horses in my regiment, which hon. Members have often seen on the streets of London, always had to have their tails in absolutely perfect condition. This business of washing a horse's tail is not like washing one's hands before going to lunch. It is a much bigger job than that. That has to be done to keep the lice away. Most hon. Members would agree that the streets of London are slightly better than the Fens, but when horses came off an exacting parade or guard duty the bottoms of their tails had to go into (buckets of warm water to get the dirt of London's streets off them. title —(RESTRICTION ON LANDING DOCKED HORSES.)

(1) No docked horse coming from a place outside the United Kingdom shall be landed from a ship or aircraft unless the landing is permitted by an officer of Customs and Excise or licensed by the Minister of Agriculture and Fisheries.

  1.
