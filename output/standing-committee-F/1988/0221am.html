Mrs Beckett
: I am glad that you are in a good mood, Mr winterton; I am not sure that other members of the Committee feel the same.

These amendments are particularly important. Throughout our proceedings, we have been discussing and will discuss, how the Bill affects particular groups, and the Opposition are most interested in how it affects vulnerable people. The Government's propaganda is pretty good and is given an amazingly uncritical reception in the press. Therefore, the Government are good at finding reasons, explanations and excuses for many of their actions. When with each succcessive Social Security Bill, they make a net saving on expenditure, it always turns out to be at the expense of those who are most vulnerable.

These amendments concern 16 and 17-year-olds. The propaganda theme of this Bill, particularly in clauses 7,9 and 10, is that the unemployed are to blame for their lack of work, and that if they would only bestir themselves, there would be no difficulty. Last year's Social Security Act could be seen as a dry run for this year's Bill. Its message was that the young unemployed were responsible for their circumstances and that large numbers of them were wilfully opting for unemployment. During the general election compaign, and in 618 the rush of excitement that afflicts Conservative Secretaries of State at party conferences, it was proposed that benefit would be withdrawn from those young people who were wilfully refusing either to seek work or to take up one of the many different kinds of training places that the Government were offering, which were all described in extravagantly flattering terms.

However, that is not what happened. The proposal was that benefit would be withdrawn from young people who were choosing to lie idle in bed in the morning, even on beautiful mornings like this, instead of looking for training places and for work. That is not what the Government did. Instead, having withdrawn basic and underlying right to benefit from every 16 and 17-year-old, they made some minor concessions to some 16 and 17-year-olds. The rate of benefit that such young people received stemmed not from the Social Security Act 1988 but from the way that income support was structured under the Social Security Act 1986. It introduced for the first time a sort of junior rate of benefit which related not to circumstances but to age.

The small number of 16 and 17-year olds who are still entitled to benefit include single parents and people suffering from a handicap or disability. The Government's assumption was that the majority of young people would obtain a place on a youth training scheme. They assumed also—it is the basis of their approach—that the vast majority of young people not only do but should live with their parents, that they are welcome and properly cared for in their parental homes and that there is sufficient income to look after them. We would all wish that happy picture to be the case for all 16 and 17-year-olds, but unfortunately, it is not the reality for some, and they are the most vulnerable.

Some young people who cannot live with their parents can receive income support, but only for a short time after leaving school—for up to a maximum of 16 weeks and then only so that it will give them a breathing space to secure a YTS place. Once that limited entitlement has expired, the only recourse for someone who has not obtained a placement and is left without money is to apply to the Secretary of State to use his discretion to say that they should receive income because otherwise they will face severe hardship. Because the payment is discretionary, there is no right of appeal against a refusal.

The problems that have arisen as a result of that aspect of the legislation are not new in the sense that they were unforeseen. They were precisely foreseen in the Committee stage debates on last year's Act. I have not been surprised, but I have been incensed, by the way in which the Government and some of those who voted for their legislation last year have pretended that the resulting difficulties are a surprise or a mistake. Some well-meaning and worthy bodies—chartiable organisations such as Barnado's, children's homes and so on—have been forced to draw attention to what has happened in practice as a result of the Government's legislation because of the great harm that they see. They have done so more in sorrow than in anger, which implies that somehow it is an unforeseen side effect of the Government's proposals. We must put it clearly on the record—I feel confident that it will be place on the JPEG 619 record more than once—that there is nothing unforeseen about what is happening to vulnerable 16 and 17-year-olds. It is precisely what we predicted. We warned the Government of it and they chose to ignore it because of the substantial savings that they were able to make by ignoring it.

We asked what would happen if insufficient training places were available or if, although training places might be available, some of the young people registered for YTS were not successful in obtaining such places. My hon. Friend the Member for Halifax (Mrs Mahon) moved an amendment which related to the fact that many of the most vulnerable young people—those most in need of support, an assured income and some security—are exactly the young people least likely to be attracted to the managing agents or employers involved in YTS projects. That was ignored.

We sought to write into the legislation that suitable training places, not just any places, should be available. We pressed the Government on whether there would be enough YTS places where young people are living—bearing in mind that the thrust of the legislation is that young people are expected to live at home. All we got from the Government was an assurance. That assurance was couched in sensible terms by the Minister of State, who merely told us that the Department of Employment had insisted that the places would be available, therefore they would be. He was careful—wisely—not to take any personal responsibility. The then Under-Secretary who—perhaps partly for that reason—is no longer with us, was more unwise and gave his own unconditional guarantee that such training places would be available throughout the country. That guarantee has proved to be utterly worthless. Some young people have great difficulty because training places are not available and their benefit entitlement is running out, because they are not accepted for the available training places, or because they leave them after a short time.

Even the Government recognise that young people with no living parents or guardians might be entitled to the short period of income support as of right. That shows the Government's realism; they pass legislation which assumes that young people live with parents, but even they are forced to recognise how unrealistic that is if parents are no longer alive. Young people who do not live with their parents or guardians and who have been in care or custody immediately prior to their 16th birthday, those who are living in accommodation as part of a rehabilitation or resettlement programme under the supervision of a local authority social services department, those who are living away from their parents to avoid physical or sexual abuse and those who are living alone due to their parents' unavailability—whether the parent is ill, in custody or unable to enter the United Kingdom—cannot get benefit after the extension period expires unless they are considered to be experiencing severe hardship. Merely falling into one of these categories is not hardship enough. There must be further proof that some further difficulties may arise at the end of the extension period, if they do not have a place on YTS. If severe hardship is accepted, they can continue to receive benefit for limited 620 periods, although it is discretionary, without the right of appeal.

Again, I recall last year that we urged the Government to accept that if an award of severe hardship is made, it should be made with some secuity, and should not be withdrawn at a moment's notice. I seem to recall the former Parliamentary Secretary telling us that the provision was necessary because a young person who made a good case for receiving benefit on the grounds of severe hardship could return to his or her parental home. The Opposition expressed grave doubts about that and pointed out that even if that happened, the benefit could be questioned because circumstances had changed. Making the benefit available on only a discretionary basis and for a relatively short time was likely to perpetuate the uncertainty experienced by many young people.

Reports from all over Britain and from a wide variety of statutory and non-statutory agencies, such as social services departments, Barnardos, the Church of England Children's Society, the National Children's Bureau, highlight major problems caused by the legislation. The charitable organisations that offer hostel accommodation to the young homeless and young people leaving care also have major problems because they see increasing numbers of young people who cannot get onto YTS or cannot continue it. Those circumstances apply to young people who have been in care, and to those who may not have been in care immediately prior to their 16th birthday, but who nevertheless have many problems maintaining a relationship in their parental home.

About 8,000 young people a year leave care and the majority have either no home, or no suitable parental home, to which to return. About 70 per cent of those who leave care go to live on their own, compared to about 0.2 per cent of the general population. The reasons for that are clear. It is also clear that, although young people live on their own because that is the best and most realistic option, nevertheless, they may be unprepared, lonely and vulnerable, particularly if they have lived in care for years. They often have to find their own accommodation. We hope that they will at least start off in some of the hostels that offer emotional support, care and assistance. Of course, there are too few such places, and many young people end up in board and lodgings, bedsits and temporary squats, or they sleep on floors in friends' houses. Successive surveys have shown that young people who have left care are over-represented among the numbers of homeless people. In 1988, a Centrepoint study estimated the figure at 25 per cent and some years earlier a Department of the Environment study estimated it at 33 per cent.

Although everyone knows that young people are more likely to be destabilised, emotionally disturbed or insufficiently mature to cope with living independently, that is not heeded in the Bill. The Bill requires that they cope with the problems of finding a home, establishing themselves and adjusting to a different way of life, and with the progression from care to full-time employment or to YTS. That is why those young people are often less attractive to those who run JPEG 621 YTS projects. It has now become clear that many young people are failing to get YTS places and are being left without resources.

Several local authorities have noted that they must keep young people in care for longer than they should. They must re-admit young people into care—sometimes into inappropriate placements—not because that is the best way to prepare them for the future, but simply because of the benefit changes. One local authority social services department commented that the benefit changes greatly increase the pressure on vulnerable young people to make major decisions about their lives very quickly. There is a likelihood of increased crime to maintain income, and a feeling that they have nothing much to lose.

A charity that provides hospital accommodation points out:

"As a small voluntary agency we cannot afford to meet the charges and allowances for hostel residents. Nor do we wish to be in the position of having to evict someone because they cannot meet the charges. We are very concerned about these very vulnerable young people. Some will have nowhere to live at a time when they are facing severe emotional stress."

Those problems are not confined to young people who have been in care. A local authority in the north of England has reported increased contact with young people aged 16 and over, who have never been in care but who, following the benefit changes, now seek assistance, saying that they cannot return to the family home as they were subjected to abuse there. Under the previous benefit system, that would not necessarily have come to light because young people could establish themselves independently. That option is no longer open to them unless they bring into the open the difficulties that they have been facing.

Another charity project notes:

"The Government knows through its own research on child abuse, alcoholism, crime, drug addiction etc. that some families are unfit places for young people to be and that consequently staying at home is not an option for some. Now it must be made to see that present arrangements are grossly unfair and damaging to some of society's most vulnerable and worst treated people. This project saw 138 16/17 year olds in the final quarter of 1988. None of them had trivial reasons for leaving home and most faced with the facts chose acute poverty rather than staying with their families."

Although we regret the new provision, amendment No. 73 does not seek to overturn the provision that young people must be pushed on to YTS. It seeks to offer to the group of people who fall within the five separate definitions—to whom the Government initially gave a small entitlement to income support—a right to full entitlement benefit for as long as they need it, even if they fail to obtain a YTS place. We do not suggest that they should not be required to register for YTS. That is a simple, basic proposition.

I should like to examine how the decisions are made. They should not be discretionary and young people should not have to go through the process that I am about to outline, or to face the kind of decisions that are being made. None of this is theoretical any more. Unfortunately it is all too concrete.

We received a report of a particular research project carried out by an organisation in Scotland, in the constituency of my hon. Friend the Member for Livingston (Mr. Cook), about young people living in an emerg 622 ency hostel for the homeless.
