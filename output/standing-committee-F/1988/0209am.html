Mrs. Beckett
: We are now embarking on clause 9, and in our debates on amendments to it, there will be a tendency to bring in more than one subject, although we shall obviously do our best to keep within order. However, we are also coming into one of the most crucial parts of the Bill. You know, Mr. Winterton, the importance we attach to the introduction of the new test of actively seeking work in clause 7. One reason why we attach so much importance to clauses 7 and 10 is that we are worried about the way in which the test will be set and how it will affect people's prospects.

The amendments here retain the term "suitable employment" from section 20(1) of the Social Security Act 1975, and the qualification of the term is contained in section 20(4). It occurs in three paragraphs which set out the circumstances in which someone might come to be disqualified from receiving unemployment benefit.

The term occurs, first, when a situation in any suitable employment has been properly notified to a person either as vacant or about to become vacant, and he has "without good cause" refused, failed to apply for, or to accept, that situation when offered to him. Those are clear and sensible criteria, which, contrary to the impression given by some hon. Members on Second Reading, were put into law by the previous Labour Government. Therefore, I hope that we shall hear no more nonsense such as, "Does the Labour party think that everyone should be free to turn down any old job?"

An important part of section 20(1) refers to the possibility of disqualification if someone has neglected to avail themselves of a reasonable opportunity of suitable employment, or

"he has without good cause refused or failed to carry out any official recommendations given to him with a view to assisting him to find suitable employment, being recommendations which were reasonable having regard to his circumstances and to the 382 means of obtaining that employment usually adopted in the district in which he resides".

Section 20(4) of the 1975 Act provides further guidance as to the meaning of "suitable," by saying that employment is not suitable if it is a vacancy resulting from a trade dispute. Employment is a person's

"usual occupation in the district where he was last ordinarily employed"—

though with lower wages or less favourable conditions, or:

"employment in his usual occupation in any other district"—

with wages or conditions below the standards set for that district by collective agreement, or recognised by good employers.

Therefore, enshrined in the 1975 Act are a set of principles that have for 60 or 70 years been basic to our employment and social security legislation. I am not as expert in these matters as my hon. Friend the Member for Birmingham, Ladywood (Ms Short), but I believe that these principles are related to a range of other legislation that existed before this Government, such as wages council legislation and so on. It should not be possible for an unscrupulous employer to use the law to attack and lower the wages and conditions of his work force below what is fair or reasonable. The provisions of potential disqualification should apply not just to any employment but to employment that it was reasonable to expect someone to undertake.

The implication of some aspects of these elements of the legislation is that employment in a different occupation would not normally be regarded as suitable. Section 20(4) also provides that after a reasonable interval other employment would be deemed suitable, provided that the wages and conditions were not below recognised standards. That is the existing law, and it offers plenty of safeguards to protect the insurance fund from someone who willfully refuses to look for work, even when it is reasonable for them to do so and even when that work is available, which is the heart of concern about the Bill.

The clause deletes the word "suitable" and section 20(4). It preserves some of the safeguards by introducing the new section 20A. That new section provides that, as has always been the case, a person will not be disqualified for refusing to seek or accept a job vacancy resulting from a trade dispute, but only during the permitted period. However, it does not cover all the safeguards that used to be derived from the expression "suitable employment".

When I first read the Bill I was more than a little concerned by the clause, but I assumed that the permitted period would be of fairly substantial duration. Therefore, I was horrified to see in the notes on clauses that the permitted period will last a maximum of three months. That ties in with existing practice in as much as it has been generally accepted that after three months, if there is no prospect of someone getting suitable employment similar to his previous employment or reasonable employment in another occupation, he was expected to broaden his horizon. Broadening one's horizons is quite different from being subject to a whole range of sanctions.

JPEG 383 The Government set out in the notes on clauses the circumstances in which the maximum period may be considered to exist. One must not assume that the maximum permitted period will be allowed in every case. It will depend upon the length of time a person has previously been employed, his experience in that employment and the range of employment opportunities open to that individual. Only after careful consideration of those factors could the maximum period be awarded. That clearly implies that for many people the chances are high that the permitted period will be rather lower than three months.

A person might be expected to take a job in his usual occupation even during the permitted period which is in his own district on less favourable conditions, other than wages, than he was accustomed to or in another district on conditions, including wages below the locally agreed or recognised standard. The most important change is that whatever may be pressed on the person during the permitted period—however short it may be—after the permitted period, the wages can no longer be taken into account in deciding whether someone should be expected to accept a particular job. The Government are reluctant to write an explicit safeguard into the Bill.

The amendments would preserve the safeguards that have existed almost unaltered in tenor since 1911. A change as substantial, if not more so, as those under clause 7 is being made.

The use of the word "suitable" is an illustration of the way in which we fear the provision will work. Already, the Government's tightening up of the practical operation of the existing law is leading to treatment of individuals and judgments of whether they are really looking for work that we find difficult to accept, and even Conservative Members might find them a little alarming. The Government suggest that the legislation will lift the restrictions or increase the flexibility—I forget the exact words.

The term "suitable employment" has been widely used in employment legislation since about 1911. That term has bearing on the skills and training required of a job, and directs itself to the job on which judgment must be made. Although "without good cause" qualifies whether it is reasonable to accept or refuse a job, it is not a judgment of the job, skills or training required and whether those match the individual. It is a judgment of the person and whether he or she had good reason for making a decision. It lacks all the qualifications about wages and conditions for suitable employment. The decision will be a judgment by an adjudication officer, and so will be infinitely more subject to prejudice and value judgment. I suspect that the Government will say that that value judgment will be excercised sympathetically and sensitively. I hope that it will, but "without good cause" exists in present legislation and we have various examples of its use. It implies judgment of the person.

The word "suitable" in effect removes all reference to wages and conditions as does the removal of subsection 20(4). It was suggested on Second Reading, that it is necessary to remove those longstanding safeguards because otherwise people will tend to seek unrealistically high wages and conditions and, merely by dint of saying that opportunities available are unsuitable, can continue to draw benefit, rather than take up work. Fair levels of pay are the subject of the next debate, so I shall not say too much about that now. However, as that is behind this part of the clause, it is right to refer to the fact that the Government's principal argument in introducing the Bill, and especially clauses 7, 9 and 10, has been that a survey of the London labour market showed that a tiny minority of people were not looking for work as actively as the Government thought that they should. That same survey cuts the ground from under clause 9, because it shows that the long-term unemployed do not have unrealistically high wage expectations. Their hopes and expectations of the wages that they could command were within the average paid by London employers.

There is no justification in the source material on which the Government have chosen to rely for assuming that there is a need to remove from the legislation all the existing protection of wages and conditions. That is even less true, as we might expect, in the case of men with families. They were even more prepared than the vast majority of unemployed people to accept lower wages and worse conditions because they were worried about their families and anxious to get back into work if humanly possible.

Something else worries me about the attitudes enshrined in the clause. Ministers have argued that there is work available. They do not say that such work is suitable because, if that were the case, the clause would not need to be drafted in this way. Ministers argue that the only reason that unemployed people are not in work is because they are not running round desperately doing everything possible to obtain work. Potentially, this part of the Bill is extremely damaging to many unemployed people. I am not 386 referring to the potential harassment that will arise from the provisions of clauses 7 and 10. Sadly, to take a job doing work different from that in which one was previously engaged could considerably damage one's chances of getting back into the original type of work. It may do even more damage than seeking a return to the original occupation while unemployed. That is a difficult judgment to make.

General experience and anecdotal evidence suggest that someone who has been made redundant from a managerial position, for example, will find it difficult to get a similar position again, certainly at the speed which this part of the Bill requires. Unfortunately, such people may find it even more difficult to regain a similar occupation if, in the meantime, they have been forced by the legislation to take another occupation that pays less than they previously earned, and where the demands of the job are below their expectations. That should not be so.

That brings us back those parts of the survey on which the Government depend that they have chosen to ignore. The only part to which they have paid the slightest attention is that suggesting that some unemployed people may not be busting a gut to look for work that they believe is not even there. The Government have ignored the section that suggests that in looking for work, unemployed people are not pricing themselves out of the market by their requirements on wages and conditions. The Government have also ignored the sections suggesting that employers are not simply reluctant to look for new workers among the ranks of the unemployed, but are unaware that that is where such workers may be found.

I have discussed the matter with many people in similar circumstances. It does not matter what respect I or other members of the Committee may have for a particular occupation into which someone may be forced by the legislation. What matters is how future employers may judge it, and future employers seem to be extremely ignorant and prejudiced. One of their strongest prejudices is that, if someone is pushed into a low-grade job, that is justified and there is no point in considering them for more difficult or testing work.

If the Bill is passed unamended, applications to companies of any size, whether for manual or clerical work for which the applicant may be eminently qualified and which may benefit the community and the applicant, will never reach the employer or personnel manager. One can readily envisage an employer sifting through a heap of applications for a job of standing or importance. Applications that say, "I was a senior manager in company X, but my section closed down and I have now been unemployed for X number of months", will go down badly, as employers tend automatically to think that there must be something wrong with such people, otherwise they would not be on the scrapheap.
