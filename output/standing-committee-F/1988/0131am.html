clause Clause 7
title UNEMPLOYMENT BENEFIT: REQUIREMENT TO SEEK EMPLOYMENT ACTIVELY

Amendment proposed [26 January], No. 27, in page 4, line 37, after "7", to insert the words—"(1AA) Within three months of the passing of this Act, the Secretary of State shall make and lay before Parliament a report on the operation of the 'genuinely seeking work' condition of entitlement to unemployment insurance benefit between 1924 and 1930."—[Mr. Flynn.]

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

Mr. Paul Flynn (Newport, West): We welcome you back, Mr. Winterton, and offer you our sympathy that you were deprived of the undiluted joy of attending this Committee. We look forward to sharing that pleasure with you in the coming weeks—perhaps months.

I shall conclude my remarks on the amendment fairly speedily. Our intention is to try to persuade the Government to accept the clear lesson of history and the lesson from the 1920s to 1930 of the turmoil, confusion, unhappiness, humiliation and futility of a similar clause in that unhappy and bewildering decade.

The Government have not tried to deny that they are repeating history. The Conservative party briefing document says, on question 14:

"Is this a new idea?"

The answer is:

"No, similar provisions applied up to 1933."

The Minister disagreed that this was an alien concept. We are putting it to the Government that it is alien to our history because the assumption is made that all the unemployed are guilty and are shirkers, unless they prove that they are not. The onus is placed on the unemployed person to do that.

We welcome the Government's repeating some of the features of the Unemployment Insurance (No. 2) Act because when that Act came into force, although one part of it might well be seen as a retrograde step, its major features were progressive and beneficial. We recommend the Minister to emulate the reforms Col. 160 that took place then. In that Act, the Government undertook to provide benefit that was unlimited in duration, free of any means test and yet did not depend on the prior payment of more than a nominal number of contributions. The rise in rates of benefit in real terms was 155 per cent. over the rates paid four years earlier in 1920. If the Minister will make a similar proposal to add an additional clause to the Bill, we should accept that with our normal magnanimity.

The parallel with the 1930s is a clear one. I have some of the report of the debate that took place in 1927, when it was considered by a Committee of the whole House. At that time, it took 27 hours to consider clause 5, as it was then, in more than seven days of debate. While I do not intend to quote from it at great length, I shall quote just two brief comments which illustrate how intimately analagous their thinking and their words at the time were to the debate that we are having now and shall have in the next few days.

Mr. Betterton said:

"Our point is, that a genuinely unemployed man ought not to be hampered by a condition, whether in the Bill or 1924 or any other Measure, which has been so interpreted as to refuse benefits to genuinely unemployed men. Cases have been reported over and over again in which a man has produced written evidence from a foreman to whom he has applied for work, and even then committees have decided that those men have not been genuinely seeking work. We have plenty of evidence to show that the condition has been interpreted in that way, and I think it is the duty of this Committee so to arrange matters that genuinely unemployed working men shall not be refused benefits simply at the whim of a committee which will not accept the evidence of a foreman that a man has been seeking work. After all, it is the right of an unemployed man to have benefits unless somebody can prove that he has not tried to get work."—[Official Report, 28 November 1987; Vol 211, c. 78.]

Mr. Greenwood said:

"We say that the people who are genuinely out of work ought to receive benefit, and that the onus of proof in such cases ought to rest upon the Ministry, who have it in their power to deprive them of benefit. There is only one real test, and that is to offer a man suitable employment. That is the real test. We would agree that if a man refused to accept suitable employment he ought no longer to be eligible for benefit."—[Official Report. 28 November 1927, Vol. 211, c. 78–93.]

Those attitudes closely parallel our views.

The task then came down to making an accurate assessment of the applicant's state of mind, a purely subjective decision. Our task here—similarly impenetrable—is to try to assess the state of mind of the Government. We have few clues from what we have heard so far. We know that their judgments are distorted by apocryphal stories, as they Were in the past. It was not the two Jaguars outside the council house but the legendary figure of a lady in a limousine, dressed in a black fur coat, who turned up to claim benefit from every office in the country. That story spread like measles—like an epidemic —and everyone believed the myth at the time.

For the past 10 years, the Government have been distraught, tormented by the portentous nightmare of hordes of unemployed stealing the bread from out of the mouths of deprived millionaires. It is persistent, permanent quest for an unholy Grail, continued throughout the ages. The book from which we have drawn most of our history, by Mr. Alan Image Col. 161 Deakin, is significantly entitled "In Search of the Scrounger". It is a detailed account of the permanence of that quest, and of how it is continued. Once that idea is established, almost always based on apocryphal stories, it sticks in the mind. There was a serious examination, far more impressive than the anecdotal evidence that we have heard, and that investigation showed that the numbers of people who were workshy and cheating the system are very few, and they would not be caught by the clause.

The Government tell us that the unemployed are scroungers, because the Evening Standard told them so. They know it, because a man on the Clapham omnibus told them so, and they know that the unemployed are getting free holidays abroad, free furniture, televisions, winter clothes, because they saw that on television too. A Yiddish proverb is, "For example is no proof. We should like to see the Government for once making an objective scientific assessment of those who are cheating the system, rather than what we see happening now, when the Government's mind has been lobotomised by fable and ignorance, and cut off from reality by wish-fulfilling fantasies, and misinformed by a process of defective reasoning—or am I being too kind?

The answers given so far allow us to make that subjective judgment of the state of the Government's mind, not so much from what they say now as from what they have said before. Perhaps we shall hear something more informed. The Government do not say that the clause is good sense or fair, or that it is essential because the national insurance fund is strapped for money or because of a rising tide of unemployment. They say that it is popular, and that it fits in with the grain of public opinion. We take popularity to be the Government's supreme goal, because popularity means votes, and votes mean survival. They care about the unemployed, but many more times over for their own survival and political skins.

The amendment should be looked at against history. The folly of the clause glares back at us. The lesson of history is that a national coalition and every Government since then have renounced this folly; it is large and obvious. All Governments since then have stayed on the path of seeking common sense, fairness and balance. The Government are returning to the error like a drug addict returning to his needle. By the clause they actively seek popularity, for themselves, but humiliation and resentment for others.

The principle at the heart of the clause is that the State rightly accepts a duty to provide benefit for the unemployed under an insurance scheme; if their unemployment is longer than the insurance period, to provide income support for those without other means; and to provide advice, guidance and encouragement for the unemployed. While it accepts the responsibility, as far as is compatible with broader economic aims, to create an environment of enterprise and job creation, the State is entitled in return to expect individuals to take the trouble actively to seek work.
