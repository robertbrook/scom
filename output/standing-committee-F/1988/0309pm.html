I remind the Committee that the title of the schedule is "Minor and Consequential Amendments". The amendments we considered earlier today were minor; they did not involve large amounts of money but they would have made a great deal of difference, particularly those relating to the attendance allowance. The compensation we sought from the Government was much less than was offered in compensation to chicken farmers—much less, perhaps than the briefing errors that are now handed to the Prime Minister.

A principle is a stake on amendment No. 140. It will cost the Government some money. We ought to make sure that we do not take a carer's home away when the person he or she is looking after goes into long-term residential care and does not return home. Such carers may lose the home they have lived in for a long time. The value of a person's former home counts as part of his or her capital when calculating income support. After the great gaffe during the introduction of the social security changes last year the Government eventually agreed to raise the capital limit. No one with capital over £6,000 is entitled to income support, so the value of their home is usually enough to cancel any entitlement. Conservative Members argued quite forcefully in the Chamber that the limit ought to be higher, but they lost.

A constituent of mine reached retirement age, having taken out a retirement pension 10 years earlier. When he retired he decided to cash in his pension, which took him over the £6,000 limit. He tried to sort the matter out at the DSS but was told that he was not entitled to income support as he had too much capital wrapped up in his home. The officers told him to take his wife on holiday and spend some of his money. Unfortunately, they did not know that his wife was bedridden, that he was caring for her and that they had been unable to go on holidary for years. In those circumstances, it is ludricrous that the limit should make life more difficult for people.

Difficulties can arise when people go into residential care. They may be regarded as temporarily absent from their home for a period of usually not more than 1082 12 months, during which time the value is disregarded. Once the 12 months period is past and the absence from the home becomes permanent, its value is taken into account. There are only two exceptions to that rule: first, where the premises are occupied by a relative who is over 60 or incapacitated, and, secondly, where they are occupied by the claimant's wife or husband.

Our amendment would add a third exemption. If the home were occupied either wholly or in part by a person who had looked after the claimant, that home would not be taken into account. If the value of the home is to be measured against income support, the carer may have to move out so that the money on the home can be realised to pay for a residential place of care. That is unacceptable. It is unreasonable that people who have cared for disabled, sick or elderly people for many years but who do not happen to be over 60 should be compelled to leave what has become their permanent home. It is also unreasonable that an elderly carer who is not a relative of the claimant should be forced to move out.

We should provide a third exemption to ensure that carers of people who have moved to a hospice to die do not lose their homes. We should amend the Bill here an now. The amendment should be accepted. It would not cause the Government economic problems. The Minister has often said that we should wait for more information, such as the OPCS survey. I wonder whether digging for more information on the social picture is the real issue.

When we dealt with the provisions relating to the availability for work test, we had the impression that the Department of Employment was the driving force behind the Bill. But the later stages, minor amendments to schedule 6, are Treasury driven. I hope that the Minister will stand up to the Treasury and demand that minor but basic adjustments to social security are made. They will not cost the Government massive amounts of money but they will make a real difference to the people who are most in need.

When moving this amendment during the final innings of the Committee, I feel as though I am coming in to bat at number eight against two demonic slow bowlers on the Conservative Benches. Amendment No. 316 deals with young people. It is promoted partly by the Child Poverty Action Group. Young people aged 19 completing their basic education—GCSEs, A-levels or vocational courses—at school or colleges of further education lost entitlement of benefit in April 1988 when supplementary benefit was replaced by income support. The amendment would allow 19-year-olds to complete their education under broadly the same conditions as those that applied under supplementary benefit.

Young people my constituency have lost out as a result of the new regulations. They can no longer 1086 receive any benefit with which to complete full-time basic standards of education which are a passport to higher education. It is essential that everyone should be able to complete their basic education.

Under the supplementary benefit scheme it was assumed that most young people completed their basic education before they reached the age of 19. However, it was later recognised that some young people may require an extra year. That is quite common, for reasons such as illness, bereavement and family problems, or difficulties over learning a new language if the student has recently arrived in Britain, or simply because of late academic development. The regulations were altered so that supplementary benefit could be paid to 19-year-olds to give them the opportunity to their complete secondary education. They were often able to claim benefit. The qualifying rule was that the student in question should be 19 and should have spent the previous two years continuously engaged in full-time non-advanced education. The law was, therefore, beneficial.

Changes to the regulations in April 1987 meant that some young people at school or college, living independently, were entitled to supplementary benefit in their own right after reaching the age of 19. They were young people who were orphans, with no one else acting in the place of parents; estranged from their parents; not being supported by their parents, because their parents were sick, in prison, or unable to enter the country; parents, who had a child living with them; disabled, and therefore had a reduced chance of finding a job. Those are important categories. Orphans, with no one acting for them as parents, who wished to complete their secondary education had to be able to live independently. They had to leave the children's home where they were living.

It is tragic that a growing number of children in Britain are forced to leave home as young as 15 or 16 because of horrendous family disputes. We discussed that issue a few weeks ago when my hon. Friend the Member for Derby, South (Mrs. Beckett) moved a series of amendments to help them. Those children are not supported by their parents, because they are unemployed, sick or in prison. The number of people who will not be supported by their parents in future might grow when every adult over the age of 18 living with their parents has to pay the poll tax. The head of the household will be severally liable for the collection of that tax, even if there is a reduced rate for people who are unemployed or studying. The number of 19-year olds who are entitled is not enormous, but it is important.

Before 1987 those young people were awarded "extra-statutory" payments so that they could finish their courses. That provision was in force from April 1989 onwards. In its report to the Social Security Advisory Committee the then Department of Health and Social Security justified the change of entitlement under the law:

"the extension of benefit rights to some young people whose parents are unable to maintain them in non-advanced education is a recognition that the State should in these cases step in to enable a young person to make the decision to continue in education, rather than opt for unemployment."

The main thrust of our argument in support of the amendment is the then Department of Health and JPEG 1087 Social Security's statement, made only four years ago. I hope that the Minister will support us.

In April 1988 the introduction of income support reversed the position for 19-year-olds on their own. The changes made it impossible for parents to claim benefit for a 19-year-old dependant who is still at school or college. In other words, only three years after the DHSS said that it supported young people being able to continue their education as a choice rather than having to seek work or unemployment benefit, the Social Security Act 1988 reversed that provision.

Under the income support regulations, claimants with dependants at school or college can obtain benefit for them until they are 19. From their 19th birthday, benefit will not be paid. For a family on income support, that means a loss, from 10 April 1989, of £27.40 a week from their basic benefit. That loss will be at the expense of other members of the family, or the youngster must give up his or her education.

In a family where a 19-year-old wishes to continue his or her full-time education and there are younger brothers or sisters who are also at school, tension will be caused by the Government's change in the regulations. The young person will no longer receive benefit to enable him to continue his education, he will thus become a complete burden on the family. If the young person gave up his education and sought benefit as an unemployed person actively seeking work, he could get benefit. If he found a job, he would have an income. It is wholly unfair that that relatively small group of young people should be penalised and prevented from continuing their further education.

I want to deal with the case of young people living independently; that is increasingly common. They will be in an even worse position. Under the income support regulations they are not entitled to any benefit once they reach the age of 19. If they are at school or college full-time, the Government have suggested that they should seek grants from the local education authority. The Child Poverty Action Group says:

That is important. A young woman in my constituency who is just over 19 wishes to complete her A-levels at a further education college in London. She is separated from her parents, with whom she has a difficult and bad relationship. It is in her interests to live apart from them. She has a flat of her own. She ought to receive housing benefit and I hope that she eventually she will, but she is not entitled to any benefit for the purpose of study. She does not qualify for income support. Moreover, she cannot get a grant from her local education authority because its discretionary grants budget has been cut year after year. It is very difficult to obtain a discretionary award. A lot of persuasion and letter writing to the LEA is required. Unfortunately, competition takes place between mature students who legitimately wish to take a course of study and 19-year-olds who are stuck in this unfortunate trap.

If the student in my constituency is successful in obtaining help from the local education authority, she 1088 may be able to complete her A-levels, but she has now lost so much time and benefit that she has had to borrow a great deal of money from friends and relatives.
