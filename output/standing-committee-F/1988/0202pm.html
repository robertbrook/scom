Mrs. Margaret Beckett
(Derby, South)
: Before the Committee rose for those fleeting moments that allow us to tear around like idiots trying to catch up with all the work that we are not doing while we are in here, we discussed the number of weeks for which a person may be liable to lose benefit. Despite the Minister's assurances, which we have shown to be flawed, we had demonstrated that benefit could be lost for many weeks.

We also drew attention to the fact that, no matter what the Minister may say about the guidance that will be given, and the discussions that will take place with the person who will otherwise lose benefit, those assurances are not written into the Bill. I referred to the danger of relying on guidelines and quoted the example of immigration guidelines which appear not to bear any relation to any ordinary and rational understanding of the words written on the paper.

When I listened to the Minister telling us how the provisions would operate, how quite junior officials, who will be given enormous discretion, will sensitively carry out their duties because of their experience and because of the training and guidance given by the Department of Employment, I was strongly reminded of examples that cast doubt on the Minister's confidence. In November, the case of a man denied payment for his board and lodgings in October came to my attention. We queried it and found that he had been denied the payment because inquiries made by officers of the Department discovered that, although the man was living as a lodger, the terms of his landlady's lease did not allow her to have a lodger. Therefore, the officials declined to pay him benefit as a lodger.

I know of no provision in the law, in guidelines or in advice given by any section of the social security law, which empowers officials to inquire into the circumstances of landlords who are not themselves benefit claimants. Even if such powers exist, such information has no bearing on an individual's entitlement to benefit. It may cast doubt on the wisdom of the landlord or landlady having such a claimant living in their accommodation, but that is another matter altogether.

The circumstances under which the man lodged were of no concern whatever to the Department, and JPEG 283 had nothing to do with his claim for benefit. The Department finally agreed in January and after three months of being illegally refused benefit on the basis of inquiries that were not the business of the Department, benefit was finally restored. Even that one example shows why we should be sceptical about the Minister's sweeping assurances.

I am also reminded of the experience, over years, of the law relating to deductions or deprivation of benefit on the grounds that someone is said to have voluntarily made himself unemployed. A year ago, the social policy inspectorate surveyed the implementation of that rule. It discovered, and reported to the Government, that not only were many wrong decisions being made, but that even wrong decisions overturned on appeal were often not reported to the claimant. Even when back payment of benefit should have been made, in many cases it was not. We had a long-running dispute with the Department about how far back in its files it should be prepared to go to restore to people benefit wrongly denied. There were defects in the decision-making and appeals procedures.

For many decades, benefit could be lost for only six weeks. That could be a substantial loss, but the six weeks was the maximum period. Since then, for no good reason, other than that the judgment changed, the Department has doubled that period, so that benefit may be lost for 13 weeks. Last year, that was doubled again to 26 weeks. When the Department doubled the period to 13 weeks, that was the maximum. It was not the period for which benefit had to be withdrawn, but the maximum period for which it was allowed to be withdrawn. Now that the period is six months, that is the maximum. However, in the early days of the doubling of the period, the vast majority of officers deciding to disqualify someone from receiving benefit disqualified for the full period, as they had when the period was six weeks. Officials took no account of the difference between disqualification for six weeks, and disqualification for nearly 6 months.

Precedent does not encourage me to regard discretion by DSS officers in the same way as the Minister. I am least disposed to regard favourably the Minister's view of the sympathetic and kindly way in which DSS officers invariably exercise their judgment by an episode in my family's experience. During the war, my father's health was so damaged that he became a chronic invalid. Although he worked until I was two or three, he was mostly unemployed. After some retraining, he obtained a clerical job, but his health was such that he lost that job quite soon. Forever after he was unemployed. I feel strongly about the way that the Bill will operate because I remember vividly how much my father resented his plight, a resentment that lasted all his life.

After losing the job that he held briefly, my father had to sign on again for benefit. An officer in the Department of Employment made wide inquiries into our family circumstances. My mother was a teacher in full-time work and there was no question of my family being dependent on the state, although we had every right to be. Nevertheless, the 284 Department official suggested that my eldest sister, who had just decided to stay on at school to go to university, should not be allowed to do so and should leave to take up work to help support my father because she had reached the school-leaving age. Neither my father nor my family were financially solely dependent on the state.

The Minister may talk about the sympathetic and courteous treatment that one can expect to receive automatically and invariably from Department of Employment officials, but that does not strike a warm cord in my heart because of that official. His comments did not relate to the law and had no bearing on anything except that he thought that the children of unemployed people should not go to university. He had every right to hold that view, but he had no right to seek to implement it through his role in the administration of benefit.

The Minister may say—he would have every right to say—that that was a long time ago. However, as we have said before, the views of the public and, in my experience, some officers in the Department of Employment—although I would not care to suggest that they are all like that—have not substantially changed. Indeed, Conservative Members have prayed that in aid of in support of the legislation. They say that that is what the public demand and the public do not like to think that the unemployed are having a happy time. A number of stories from my childhood and my family confirm that in spades—yes, indeed. Even when they are looking at someone who is grey faced, sunken cheeked and visibly a sick man, members of the public do not like to think that unemployed people might be enjoying their unemployment in any way, shape or form and are inclined to express their view.
