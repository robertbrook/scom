This amendment touches on an important issue that I hope will commend itself to hon. Members on both sides. Massive changes were made in the social security system by the Social Security Act 1986, but carried into effect in April 1988. The amendment is aimed at one of the changes that is the most invidious in its effect and the most difficult to defend. Often people become severly ill and require a level of care that qualifies them for attendance allowance. A full-time carer may then be able to receive an invalid care allowance. There is a six month waiting period, during which time the medical conditions have to be satisfied. A person has to receive substantial care before that allowance is paid. I presume that the original intention was to ensure that the allowance could not be paid simply to anyone who had a period of severe illness that was of quite short duration.

When the changes took effect in April 1988, people who were severely ill lost some of the protection that they had previously enjoyed under the law. Although they could not receive an attendance allowance until after six months, people on low income who became severely ill and were likely to satisfy the conditions for attendance allowance could apply for, and might be awarded, an allowance under the supplementary benefit scheme during that waiting period. That completely disappeared with the new income support schemes. Moreover, if they were on a low income and had few savings, they might apply for a range of additional payments, such as for laundry bills or extra help, depending on their circumstances. They were not easily available but were available in circumstances of great need to people who were terminally ill.

Col. 1048 Officers in various local DSS offices have told me that they would bend the rules to make sure that all possible support was given to low-income families if a member of the family was suffering from a serious or terminal illness. Now they simply cannot give any assistance at all, except to offer a crisis loan. We can all imagine the feelings of such people who are told that they are entitled only to a loan that will have to be repaid when they have to cope with the bereavement and the effects of a long period of caring before the death.

Of late, we have been told that in those circumstances people can apply for a community care grant but, as with a crisis loan, a community care grant is subject to the cash-limited budget. Most local offices are grossly underspending their budgets. It is likely that money will be available. Even then, people cannot be considered for a community care grant if they have £500 in savings. A couple of years ago, when the system was changed one could just about pay for the cost of a funeral with savings of £500. That is now impossible. The average cost of a funeral is well above that. When people are terminally ill, the Government are offering them a choice between being able to retain enough money to meet their funeral costs and receiving the help and support that might enable them to manage more easily during their illness.
