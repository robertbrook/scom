UNEMPLOYMENT BENEFIT: REQUIREMENT TO SEEK EMPLOYMENT ACTIVELY

The Minister for Social Security (Mr. Nicholas Scott)
: I hope that my reply to the matters raised in this brief debate will not be entirely unhelpful.

I begin by registering what is essentially a negative point. Unemployment benefit is designed precisely to provide short-term income for those who have lost wages because they are out of work and who seek a return to work. I start with the principle that benefit should not be a means of subsidising study, training or voluntary work. Therefore, it is difficult to accept an argument for exempting the groups mentioned in the amendments from the requirements which will apply to everyone else.

The hon. Member for Derby, South (Mrs. Beckett) rightly stated that a person is deemed available for work on any day on which he is engaged in providing a service, so long as it is reasonable for him—subject to 24 hours' notice—to take up a job, attend an interview for a job or an interview with the emploment service. "Providing a service" has been interpreted as covering voluntary work as well as caring for a child or a sick or elderly person. That takes us one step further. It is a reasonable concession in relation to the availability rule, but it does not mean that voluntary workers should be exempted completely from the requirement to actively seek work. If such a concept were accepted, it would mean that unemployed voluntary workers were not really seeking a return to full employment and therefore—to revert to my original principle—unemployment 200 benefit would not be appropriate to their circumstances. However, I accept that the majority of voluntary workers undertake useful work which, in some cases, may even enhance their chances for future paid work.

Before I conclude, I should say that it is commendable that the unemployed should avail themselves of training and education opportunities and perhaps gain qualifications which may help them back to work. An unemployed claimant can receive benefit while he is studying, training or providing a voluntary service—provided that he remains available for work. To qualify at present, he needs to show that his commitment to his voluntary work—except in the 24-hour concession—or to education or training does not prevent him from attending job interviews or taking up immediately any suitable job offer.

The hon. Member for Birmingham, Ladywood (Ms Short) spoke about the 21-hour rule. I know that I am on dangerous territory, Mr. Winterton, because that rule applies not to unemployment benefit but to income support. We may have an opportunity to discuss that later. But, we have carried through the rule from supplementary benefit into income support, except for the point which the hon. Lady properly made about 16 and 17-year-olds. Therefore, people on income support can still study under the 21-hour rule. However, I see that you are getting restless, Mr. Winterton, so I shall not pursue that subject now.

Mr. Michie
: The test on "actively seeking employment" would apply only if the claimant had been notified of any suitable job vacancy by the jobcentre in the previous seven days. If the system is to be fair, the burden of proof should fall not just on the claimant but on the jobcentre and the adjudication officer as well. If an officer feels that there is not enough proof that a claimant has been actively seeking work, he has the power and responsibility to take away the meagre benefit from the claimant and his family, he should produce evidence and send the claimant on a job vacancy search, knowing that there is a vacancy. The onus should not just be on the claimant to prove that he has been all over the place looking for but failing to find a job. There should be an onus on the adjudication officer also. For the claimant to have to do that every week in which he is claiming benefit is most unfair and can be costly.

Telephone calls are expensive, and I am not sure how one can prove that one has picked up a phone every day or every week to ask companies whether they have a vacancy. Travelling around looking for jobs that are not there costs money.

We do not believe that the test is fair, because it is incapable of being administered in a fair and proper manner that will benefit the claimant. That is why we want some form of safeguard, taking the burden 208 of proof away from the claimant and putting it back on to the adjudication officer.

It is costly to travel around looking for jobs that are few and far between, and the benefits that accrue to claimants are hardly generous.
