: Before we broke for lunch, I was arguing that the Government should accept the Council of Europe's decency threshold as a basis for a statutory minimum wage policy, so that we know what level of pay people should not be asked to accept. Without that bottom limit, it is plain that the Bill will mean that employers can offer extremely low wages and that people will have to accept them or their social security will be cut off.

The Minister spoke about the average wage as if everything depended on that. The average wage is £254 a week. By holding on to the average, the Government intend to create the impression that those at the top are held in close relation to those at the bottom, and have not come too far adrift from those at the bottom. I wish to emphasise the fact that those at the top are moving further ahead. The evidence exists in recent press articles, such as:

"Burton chief regains title of highest paid executive."

I need not rehearse the list of highest paid directors—we are well used to it. The people at the top of the league table are moving ahead, but not the people at the bottom of the league table—the deciles of earnings well below the average wage and even below half the average wage.

Earlier, the Minister implied that because the economy is doing well and the average figure has 420 increased, everyone's earnings must move up with that average. If the Minister checks with the Department of Employment he will find out that that is not the case. Some wages are held level with no increases for inflation, which means that in real terms, they are reduced. Some wages are actually reduced in cash terms. I refer the Minister to an article in the Industrial Relations Review and Report on 29 January, entitled "Low Pay Increases in the Deregulated Bus Sector". It reported that the majority of pay settlements were at the lower end of the 3 per cent. to 8 per cent. range. In some instances, new employees were taken on at lower rates than existing employees for the same work. In other words, wage rates were being cut.

The Low Pay Unit's "The Poor Decade, Wage Inequalities in the 1980s" published in January 1988, spelt out that while the average earnings of the highest paid fifth of male workers have increased by 140 per cent. since 1979, the average earnings of the lowest paid fifth increased by only 98 per cent. The report states:

"for the first time since the 1930s we are witnessing actual cuts in the average earnings of the poorest workers in such sectors as hotels and catering and footwear."

The hon. Member for Stockport (Mr. Favell) told us earlier that he worked in the hotel industry in his youth. Wage rates in the hotel industry are falling. People probably earn less now relatively in that industry than the hon. Gentleman did when he worked in it. The report refers to the poorest fifth of male workers in licensed non-residential establishments such as snack bars, cafes and those selling fast food.

I remind the Committee that Lord Young said that Britain's economy was doing well and that it was useless for the Opposition to claim that we should return to manufacturing industry because the key to economic growth was the service sector. He remarked that Britain's unemployment would be totally eliminated if more people were prepared to eat out. Obviously, Lord Young was passionately committed to the sector of snack bars, cafes and fast food.

That is the sector in which the wages of people in part-time or temporary work are not rising with average wage rates, but are falling. The poorest fifth of male workers in licensed non-residential establishments earned on average £1.50 a week less in April 1987 than in April 1986.

How can the Minister believe that the whole block of earnings is rising when some people's earnings are falling? There is a fracture in the economic league of the earnings table, which means that people in the bottom bands are doing significantly less well and becoming increasingly poorer.

The report from the Low Pay Unit said:

"The increase in the numbers of low paid appears to contradict ministerial assertions that economic growth is bringing with it improved living standards for the population as a whole. The reality is that rewards are becoming increasingly unequally divided."

That is still the case today.
