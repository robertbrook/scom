: The amendment seeks to change the law so that social fund grants are made available for work-related expenses such as tools, clothing, travel to work or to allow people to take up new work.

Under the social fund, grants or loans are not available for work-related expenses. That is written on tablets of stone in direction numbers 12, 23 and 29 under the Social Security Act 1986, so there is no discretion allowed at local level. The directions are legal requirements, as are most parts of the social fund manual, and many people are prevented from looking for or taking up work because they cannot afford to buy work clothes or tools or pay for travel. Again it is one of what the Government have described as the rigidities of the system. It is one of the disincentives, and it will become an increasing problem.

Under the old supplementary benefit single payments scale, claimants had a legal right to grants for work-related expenses. Regulation 23 of the supplementary benefit single payment regulations provided that

"a single payment should be made in respect of any specified item without which a member of the assessment unit would be unable to either take up employment which had been offered to him or to continue in employment which he had has taken up during the 14 days immediately preceding the making of his claim for a single payment."

It is a very sensible rule. The items for which the single payment could be made were basic tools, working clothes, footwear, driving licence, fees for medical examinations required by prospective employers and any other item which cost less than £35.

There is probably a growing need for that payment as employers become less sensitive to the need for safety than they were and more permissive in their general neglect of many areas of work in which protective clothing is necessary. Where basic equipment is crucial for work, money should be available for it.

Under the actively seeking work provisions, it will be crucial that claimants are given financial help to at least fulfil the condition of actively seeking work. At present, claimants cannot obtain any help from the social security system towards the cost of travel to seek work and travel during the first weeks of work before the first pay day, the cost of clothes to go to interviews and to work, or the cost of tools necessary to take up that work.

If the amendment is accepted, people will be able to apply for social fund community care grants, but because of the discretionary nature of the social fund, there will be no guarantee that payments will automatically be made. It will be a question of proving the case and proving the necessity of the grant. The payment will be very much dependent on those paragons, those omnipotent people, the social fund officers, who are JPEG 661 the repositary of all wisdom on such matters. The buck stops with them.

The Department of Social Security guidance on priorities and the state of the local social fund budgets will also be factors It is therefore essential that the social fund guidance manual is amended to give high priority to all unemployed claimants requesting social fund gants for work-related expenses.

Amendment No. 134 addresses the problem of people starting work who may have to wait—they all wait for some time, but some have to wait up to a month—for their first pay day. At present, the only help available is a crisis loan from the social fund. Under the amendment, the payments from the social fund to meet living expenses during that period would not be recoverable, otherwise they would be paid as grants not as loans. That is particularly important, in that someone taking up a job is then faced with the further disincentive of building up a debt which may take many weeks or months to repay. It must act as a disincentive to someone to take up such a job.

There are other possible ways of dealing with the problem. A solution would be to pay income support and recover it by deductions from earnings, as is already done when a striker receives income support after returning to work. Compared with the present system of crisis loans, that has the advantage of not being subject to the social fund's cash limits. Apart from inconvenience to employers, this would mean disclosing to them that a person has been in receipt of income support. A system of non-recoverable payments therefore seems preferable.
