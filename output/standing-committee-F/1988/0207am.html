The clause changes the regulations about the repaying of unemployment benefit after a period. The present rule is that once unemployment entitlement has expired after the 52-week period, a person must be unemployed for 13 weeks to requalify. The requalification period does not have to be consecutive—the 13 weeks can be spread over a longer period. Clause 8 states that the 13 weeks must be within the 26 weeks preceding the new claim.

We tabled amendments Nos. 76 and 77 because of the change in the notes on clauses, which states that the clause is intended to:

"establish a closer link between working and the payment of benefit."

 That profoundly changes the existing arrangements. We wish to consider the logic behind that change.

316 Amendments Nos. 77 and 78 deal with the problems of self-employed people who now are not covered by unemployment insurance. It has always been assumed that a person who becomes unemployed after a period of self employment should not be able to claim unemployment benefit. Clause 8 transforms that assumption. The existing rule—that the 13 weeks which requalify an unemployed person for benefit can be spread over an unlimited period—is a contribution test, rather than a test of attachment to the labour force. The amendments are built on that distinction.

Until 1975, a person requalified for benefit by paying 13 weeks' contributions. It was not a matter of work experience. The abolition of the flat rate national insurance contribution for employees in 1975 made it necessary to base the test on hours worked rather than contributions paid. The underlying assumption is that someone who works for 16 hours will have earnings above the lower earnings limit and will therefore pay contributions for that week. As a contributions test, it was logical that the rule should require 13 weeks' work as an employee because only employees' national insurance contributions carry entitlement to unemployment benefit. Clause 8 converts the test from the contributions test to one of recent member of a labout force.

Why should people who have proved themselves to be active members of the labour force by working for themselves for three months or more not be allowed to requalify for benefit in the same way as people who have worked as employees for the same period? There is a case for extending employment insurance to self-employed people generally, but that is not the real point of the amendment. The amendment is aimed at the person who, after a year or more without a job, sets up in a one-man or one-woman business and fails to make a go of it and becomes unemployed again a few months later. There is a case like that in my constituency: the person set out on what seemed a promising and worthwhile enterprise but it did not work out. Part of the Government's philosophy or mythology is to encourage self-employment and new enterprises like this in the belief that protozoan self-employed people will one day turn into another ICI. The amendment is germane to that.

The measure will not apply to people who have always been self-employed. There is a strong argument for excluding them. To qualify for benefit under the provision, people will have to have worked as employees for long enough to qualify for unemployment benefit and to be unemployed and actively seeking work for long enough which is at least a year, to exhaust their benefit entitlement. Why should someone who has managed to work for 13 weeks on a self-employed basis not be allowed to requalify for benefit in precisely the same way as if he had found work as an employee and lost it again after 13 weeks?

The Minister may argue that the amendments would in some cases enable a person to claim unemployment benefit after several years of self-employment, but that would be prevented if the JPEG 317 principle in the amendment is accepted and a new amendment is tabled on Report to provide for a maximum period of self-employment after which entitlement to benefit would cease.

Amendment No. 82, which follows in our usual impeccable and logical course, would extend the definition of employed earner. Under section 2 of the Social Security Act 1975 an employed earner is defined as

"a person who is gainfully employed in Great Britain either under contract of service, or in an office (including an elective office)"—

 that presumably includes hon. Members—

"with emoluments chargeable to income tax under Schedule E".

 Now that, under clause 8, the requalification test is to become a test of recent attachment to the labour force, it no longer makes sense to discriminate against people who have done their 13 weeks outside Great Britain. We do not want the Government to be accused of being xenophobic or Little Englanders on this issue. If one accepts what we are saying in amendments Nos. 76 and 77, there is no justification for discriminating in that way. Amendment No. 82 would remove that meaningless discrimination.

The principle that the Government want to establish is that unemployment benefit claimants have a recent work record, if we understand the clause and the notes on clauses correctly. Provided that they can show that the work that they have been doing is of a king that would have been classified as employed earners' employment in Britain—that is, they were working for a wage or salary—it would not matter where it was done.
