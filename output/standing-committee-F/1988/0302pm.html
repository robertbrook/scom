Clause 18
RECOVERY OF SUMS EQUIVALENT TO BENEFIT FROM COMPENSATION PAYMENTS IN RESPECT OF ACCIDENTS, INJURIES AND DISEASES

Question again proposed.

Mr. Archy Kirkwood
(Roxburgh and Berwickshire)
: I was being admonished on all sides this morning for previous lapses in attendance, to the extent that I was unable to attend your excellent reception at 11 o'clock Mr. Winterton, for the British war widows and associates. I should have slipped down and been happier with you there for half an hour, had I not been getting a certain amount of stick from both sides of the Committee. I look to you to protect me in my isolation.

As the debate ended this morning, I was adding my voice to the criticism made by the hon. Member for Derby, South (Mrs. Beckett) about the discretionary powers that the DSS inspectors will have to gain access to compensation papers in trying to adjudicate and define the circumstances relating to claims under the clause. I hope that the Minister will recognise the concern over that, and be able to reassure us that the outline procedures that have been issued will be carefully controlled.

Committee Members should be aware that the extensive range of benefits encompassed by the clause will mean that people who suffer severe accident injuries will be potentially just as affected as victims subject to more minor and trivial injuries.

The Bill could have given the Department discretion to waive or reduce the repayable amounts of benefit out of settlements. I see no provision to introduce an element of discretion, and I cannot imagine why the Government do not introduce it, as it would mitigate some of the worst and most anomalous cases that might arise from the implementation of the clause. It is still not too late for Ministers to consider going down that route to mitigate the effects of the clause, if they are insisting on legislating on the principle.

The clause does not authorise the Department to reduce the clawback if the settlement or compensation award has been arrived at on a compromised basis. Lawyers who have more experience of the matter than I do feel that because the attempt in the clause to ignore the fact that 95 per cent, of the accident compensation claims are settled on a basis of compromise produces difficulties.

904 There could be legal difficulties, where, for example, someone has slipped or tripped and thus an element of contributory negligence was involved on the part of the victim. Secondly, there could be medical difficulties, where someone has sustained a bad back injury, but where he or she has had pre-existing degenerative changes or symptoms, or has had a history of back trouble.

A compromise is inevitably struck in such cases, and the damages to be paid are arrived at on that basis. The proposals create a great deal of uncertainty and unfairness in compromise settlements.

It is true that many cases are ultimately settled by a compromise, to avoid a tedious High Court battle which is bad for the plaintiff and the lawyers themselves. Nevertheless, in the compromises that are reached all the factors that apply or could apply to the compensation that is receivable by the plaintiff are taken into account by the lawyers. Obviously, that sometimes leads to a plaintiff not getting as much compensation as he would have liked or as his circumstances would objectively dictate. Those compromises often work the other way. The defendants settle at an amount which is not as great as it would have been, had the proceedings gone to trial, but the risk element plays on them as much as it plays on the plaintiff and his advisors. These matters are fully taken into account by lawyers. I do not believe that it is inevitable that that will be prejudiced by the clause.

Mr. Kirkwood
: That is certainly not my experience. The number of cases where settlements are made in excess of what could be expected if the matter went to court are few—it is almost a hypothetical position.

Clause 18 will not enable the agents who are acting on behalf of an accident victim to get proper account taken of the fact that a settlement has been made by an honourable compromise between the two parties. The Department of Social Security benefit will still have to be repaid in full and the full amount of benefit may sometimes exceed the amount of compensation offered.

My next point was also touched upon by the hon. Member for Derby, South. Many more cases will go to court where the victim is faced with the prospect of obtaining no compensation because DSS repayment under the clause will wipe out his or her damages. People who under the present system would settle may now have no option but to proceed to trial. I would take that position myself as an agent if I were in any doubt about a case. That will inevitably place additional pressure on the already over-burdened courts.

The clause will also create uncertainty. It states that the benefits to be deducted are those

"paid or likely to be paid".

 When trying to negotiate a settlement of a case, how JPEG 905 will a legal adviser to an injured person be able to say what benefits are "likely to be paid"? The Bill gives no guidance about that. How would a dispute about the amount of benefit which is to be repaid be resolved? Paragraph 15 of schedule 2 sets out procedures whereby the Secretary of State may review the certificate of total benefits if the victim disagrees with that but the victim may also appeal to a medical appeal tribunal.

It is easy to foresee circumstances in which an accident case which has been settled without going before a judge for determination of the damages and without a court hearing at which expert medical witnesses and others give their evidence on the victim's injuries might have to go before a medical appeal tribunal for a decision on those same matters. As the Minister knows, the medical appeal tribunal would be likely to consider the matter without the benefit of expert medical evidence such as would have been given at a court trial and without an experienced judge to decide between the merits of the medical cases put before him. That is another major example of how the clause is flawed.

How can the victim be satisfied on all occasions that a certificate of benefit has been calculated correctly? Many of these settlements are not made until the parties arive at the door of the courts. Uncertainty about how the certificate of benefit has been calculated will cause delay and doubt about the precise amount of damages that the injured person will receive after deduction of the benefits. Under the clause, such cases will inevitably proceed to trial whereas under the present system an acceptable settlement might be reached between the parties.

It has been raised before in the debate but the Confederation of British Industry has pointed out that both employers and employees contribute to the national insurance fund which pays for most of the benefits paid to those who, for example are injured at work. As the Committee knows only too well, the state contributes very little to the fund—it is currently some 5 per cent—and from April 1989 it will contribute nothing. The current arrangement is that the injured person keeps a proportion of the benefits to which he or she is entitled and the damages award paid by the employers' insurer is proprtionately reduced in line with the benefit paid. That system acknowledges and permits a compromise which rightly reflects the fact that both employer and employee have paid contributions to the national insurance fund. It is obviously inequitable to require employers to insure themselves twice—first, throught the employer's liability premiums that they are required to pay and, secondly, through national insurance contributions. Similarly, it is unfair to reduce the damages awards made to employees and accident victims as they have paid for their social security benefits through their national insurance contributions. That is a fundamental flaw in the clause.

The CBI has pointed out another major flaw, which is that the clause presumes that a compensation payment fully meets the injured worker's financial needs in respect of past, present and future losses. In my experience, that is far from the case. It also presumes that an employee who receives a payment in damages 906 as well as some social security benefits receives "double compensation". All the evidence that was available to me as a solicitor showed that damages awards do not fully meet the needs of accident victims, particularly if the award is reduced to take account of contributory negligence. The clause takes no account of contributory negligence and I make no apologies for returning to that point. The Government have suggested that to take account of contributory negligence would provide scope for manipulation between those representing victims and those insuring the defendants in the accident claim. It is surely unreasonable to exclude that factor when it is taken into account in up to half the settlements and awards to victims.

Who will benefit from the clause if it is passed unamended? The injured will certainly not benefit. They will probably receive less compensation or possibly no compensation at all and their claim may well take longer and become more difficult to settle. The insurers will not benefit. They will have to pay more compensation, but not necessarily to the injured person. In addition, they will be faced with considerably more administration and bureaucracy.

The employers will not benefit. They will have to pay higher insurance premiums, which will undoubtedly be passed on to their customers. The CBI is oposed to the proposals and has estimated that insurance premiums will increase by 16 per cent, costing employers alone at least another £38 million a year. Total insurance premiums may increase by as much as £60 million a year as car owners will also have to pay higher premiums.

Why should an accident victim be penalised in this way? I do not understand the Government's logic. The clause will not improve the present system which is effective, simple, well understood and has been used for the past 40 years.

The Minister for Social Security (Mr. Nicholas Scott)
: At this time of year, the most I can do in response to my hon. Friend the Member for Dorset, South (Mr. Bruce) is ensure that his remarks are drawn to the attention of my right hon. Friend the Chancellor when he comes to discuss those matters.

Despite some of the comments on the clause, it arises out of a long-standing debate about the relationship between social security benefits and compensation when they are paid for the same injury. It is now well understood that, under the present rules, some benefits are offset for an award of compensation by 50 per cent under the Law Reform (Personal Injuries) Act 1948 for five years from injury, irrespective of when settlement is made. Under case law, other benefits may be offset by 50 per cent or 100 per cent over the five-year period or longer.

The courts have regularly ruled that the victim should not receive "double compensation". I have been accused of using that term, but it springs from the judgment of courts in recent years. I did not invent it. The result is increasingly that compensators—usually insurance companies—do not have to meet the full liability for injury because the victim has already had social security benefits.

Our primary aim is to shift the burden for providing compensation back fully on to those responsible, while in most cases not affecting the amount of compensation that the victim receives. We are not taking away benefit entitlement or eligibility but simply removing any remaining double compensation from the compensation payment when it is ultimately made.

The clause is long overdue. I understand why many worthy and substantial organisations still do not welcome an upheaval, but the Public Accounts Committee JPEG 909 and the National Audit Office have said that we should act. We are acting in a sensible and forward-looking manner.

The hon. Member for Derby, South (Mrs. Beckett) quoted a case showing the malign effects of the clause upon victims. She quoted a cleaner who was blinded and received damages of £103,680. Under the present rules, the Industrial Injuries Advisory Council tells us that £20,000 would have been deducted; the compensator would have paid only £83,480, instead of £103,680. However, in that case and others, the council assumed that we would take into account future benefit entitlement as recoupment under the clause. That is not so. Had the case been dealt with under the new arrangements, far from recouping the £37,400 which the council suggested, we should have recouped £9,500—less than half of what was offset under the previous arrangements. That illustrates my point that in the 80 per cent of claims that are settled within two and a half years victims will be no worse off; they may be better off than they are under the existing povisions. I have hitherto refrained from suggesting that some would gain, but some would and that is a clear example. I am glad that the hon. Lady prompted me to discover the facts of that case.

The hon. Lady quoted the Parry v Cleaver decision in the House of Lords on recovering like from like and invited us to separate benefits into those connected with special damages and those for general damages. I listened with interest to the earlier debate and to the point that she made this morning but, despite its superficial attractions, I still believe that that would be an unnecessary and unworkable way forward.

I accept that there is much confusion. That is illustrated by the fact that the Industrial Injuries Advisory Council reached so totally wrong a judgment. In those circumstances, we should stick to die principles that underlies die clause—to shift the burden on to the backs of the compensators. Although some victims may get less, others may get more. Overall, that will be sensible.

The hon. Lady and the hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire (Mr. Kirk wood) referred to the powers of inspectors. It is right that the Committee should always be concerned about giving increased powers to Government inspectors. We have to be careful about the rights of citizens, particularly in relation to their private affairs. I hope that I can reassure die hon. Lady and die hon. Gentleman. The point is covered in schedule 2, and there may be further opportunities to discuss it. Some papers have been sent to say that

"inspectors powers will be widened to include insurance companies and compensation papers."

 I most emphasise that, that is a fall-back provision. There are no penalties for non-compliance, except an abosolute requirement to pay the Secretary of State.

Let us suppose that we find that a payment has been made but we have no record of a certificate being issued or paid. Without some power to check how much was paid, die Secretary of State would not be able to demand payment from die compensator—even if some sort of deduction had been made from the 910 victim's payment. It is only a fall-back measure because compensators are usually large, reputable insurers or the major employers. No difficulties should arise with that. Back in 1981, the Industrial Injuries Advisory Council actually urged the Government to do what the clause aims to do. At that point, the Government thought that it was unnecessary because the insurance companies could be trusted to operate in an acceptable way. I am sure that in practice they can. I accept that fully. The provision is no more than a fall-back safeguard.

There is no question of inspectors looking at the private files of solicitors or expecting to see other than a document which shows how much has been paid—and even that would be only as a last resort if the matter cannot be resolved in the normal way by corresponsdence.

The hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire need not fear the tread, on the stairs of his local practice, of an inspector who will require him to produce private documents.
