Social Security Bill

Clause 18
RECOVERY OF SUMS EQUIVALENT TO BENEFIT FROM COMPENSATION PAYMENTS IN RESPECT OF ACCIDENTS, INJURIES AND DISEASES

10.30 am

Mr. Paul Flynn
(Newport, West)
: I beg to move amendment No. 263, in page 13, line 24, at end insert

"less any amount by which the compensation payment would have been greater but for the contributory negligence of the victim."

 With this amendment, we deal with contributory negligence—probably the most fundamental flaw of the clause. This complication seems to have been disregarded. If the social security system or damages for personal injury alone could be relied on to provide full financial compensation, there would be no objection to setting off one against the other. Would that life were that simple, and that the victim would still be left with full compensation. However, unfortunately that is not the case. The only circumstances in which full compensation is, or ought to be, paid by the person responsible for the injury is where the victim was in no way to blame. In all other cases, compensation is reduced in proportion to the degree of blame attaching to the victim. Those are not uncommon cases. Even when the victim was not to blame, a system of horse trading comes into operation. As the Minister reminded us, most cases are settled out of court. The risk that the court might find contributory negligence is one factor that is taken into account in the negotiations—although it may not be precisely spelt out—to decide the acceptable settlement.

Some studies reinforce the view that many settlements take into account contributory negligence and the possibility that it might have been a factor, had the case gone to court. The authors of a study of injury victims undertaken by the centre for socio-legal studies at Oxford quoted in last year's report by the Industrial Injuries Advisory Council found that:

"the full amount which a court would be likely to award, if 'full' liability were established against the defendant, is heavily discounted in an out-of-court settlement in order to take account of all the uncertainties facing the plaintiff."

 Both the Pearson report and the Oxford study found that contributory negligence was taken into account in up to half of all settlements and awards.

On the other hand, social security benefits are fundamentally different from compensation awards, and are paid on the assumption of a no-fault basis. The underlining assumption is that, because nobody would deliberately bring about their own injury, negligence is an insurable risk. That has always been the basis of 864 payments of social security benefits. However, those benefits seldom—probably never—provide full compensation, since the basis on which they are calculated is that of meeting the needs, rather than of compensating for losses. Even on that basis, they are inadequate. They certainly do not cover the full range of injuries, loss of amenity and other headings taken into account when compensation is assessed.

Therefore, the only way in which a victim can be fully compensated—to the extent that money can compensate for the injuries suffered—is by a combination of benefits that meet his needs, and compensation payments that accounts for all the other headings. That system—setting off all the benefits against compensation payments—removes any possibility of full compensation. The unfairness of the proposal can be seen if we have a look at three hypothetical cases, in each of which the deductible benefits amount to half the amount that would be received as a compensation payment if there was no contributory negligence.

10.35 am

In the first case, no contributory negligence is involved and the victim receives full compensation, but has to repay all his or her benefits. In the second case, if there is a 50 per cent. deduction for contributory negligence, the victim receives 50 per cent. compensation, all of which must be paid to the DSS in repayment for benefits received. In the third case, the victim was solely to blame and does not have to repay any benefits.

We are creating a curious world based on the strange morality that the innocent should be punished, and the guilty should be rewarded. In creating new legislation, and trying to remove an injustice, the Government are falling into an old trap of creating a different injustice. Punishing the innocent is not unknown. Last night, after the debate on Welsh affairs, Welsh Members of Parliament and their guests were moved to sing a well-known Welsh hymn which is addressed "To Everyone Who is Faithful"—"I bob un sy'n Ffyddlon". The second verse begins, "Llnoedd Duw a Satan", which means "The forces of God and Satan". That was inspired by the forces of representing a lower value of good that were arranged in the Chamber. It is a peculiarly Herodic idea of morality to reward the guilty and punish the innocent.

Those three examples show that the more innocent the victims, the less help they will get from the social security system. The Confederation of British Industry and everyone who commented on the unfortunate proposals, opposed them. The Confederation of British Industry briefed honourable Members on Second Reading. It said that victims

"will lose all specified benefits if they are injured by the fault of another driver or their employer.…By contrast, those who suffer injury from their own fault or from no-one's fault will continue to be able to receive their full benefits."

 The Association of British Insurers also produced a striking example. As with widows, the Government have created traps for themselves. By defective thought processes, and because of malicious forces in their party, they have produced a system of social JPEG 865 security so harsh and punitive that it is becoming unenforceable.

I have quoted at some length my embarrassment theory of political action, which produces payments such as blush money. We shall see other examples of that if the clause comes into effect. The welcome about-turn on widows' benefit in the past 24 hours shows that although the Government thought that their actions were reasonable, their obvious unfairness was so unpopular, and enraged the public to such an extent, that the Government have been forced into an humiliating climbdown.

Yesterday, cases from Cornwall received some brief publicity. Families with severely disabled children have been asked to repay attendance allowances paid out between 1979 and 1985 because of a technicality and a misunderstanding. The defence that the families would use—that they used due diligence in making claims—was abolished in 1987.

10.40 am

The retrospective use of that power is widely seen as grossly unjust, and was portrayed as such by the media in the south-west last night. If the Bill is to proceed, the Government will be in similar position.

Whenever they seem to be floundering in a sea of chaos of their own creation, we try to offer them a lifeline.

A vivid example of the anomaly that would be created was illustrated by the Association of British Insurers in its Second Reading brief, which said:

"Someone who is entirely responsible for his own injury (for example, by driving negligently or through drug or alcohol abuse)"—

 a common experience—

"will be entitled to retain his full benefit and not make any refund to the DSS. Where somebody's injury was caused partly or entirely by another person, the injured person will be required to refund his benefit to the DSS in full."

 The Bill will allow a drunken driver responsible for his own injuries to retain his benefit in full while other people injured as a result of his drunkeness must refund all their benefits to the DSS—the innocent punished, the guilty rewarded. That is not an accidental effect of the formula chosen by the Government for calculating the amount of benefits to be repaid. However the calculation is made, it is inevitable that those who receive compensation will be affected, while those who are wholly to blame for their injuries will not.

A similar criticism could be made of the present system, under which 50 per cent. of certain benefits are deducted in calculating the damages awarded. Under this system, however, the deduction is made before taking account of the degree of contributory negligence. Thus, if only 50 per cent. of damages are awarded, the deduction for social security benefits is automatically halved. Under the Govt's proposals, that would no longer happen. Not only would all of the benefits be deducted, but there would be no proportionate reduction where the damages award was less than 100 per cent.

866 The amendment would ensure that the victim is allowed to keep as much of his benefits as is necessary to replace the compensation lost because of the contributory negligence factor. Where there is no contributory negligence, all of the benifits would be recovered. If, for example, the damages awarded were reduced by £10,000 because of contributory negligence, the first £10,000 of benefit would be protected and only benefits above that amount would be repayable. Deductions would be made only where there could otherwise be an element of double compensation. That is the heart of the injustice that the Government are trying to avoid.

We hope that the amendment will be seen to avoid the problem of double compensation, which is the Government's main inspiration for the clause. The amendment alone will not resolve the difficulties that we have identified and further amendments will be needed to lay down rules for calculating the amount of benefits to be protected where the degree of contributory negligence cannot be ascertained from the court order. That is an uncertain area because the precise element of contributory negligence that is calculated is rarely taken into account or spelt out.

Once the Bill is enacted, we may find that it is necessary to identify the components of any compensation payment far more precisely. We hope that the Minister will acknowledge that the Government are creating a fresh anomaly, which amendment No. 263 would help to correct. We hope that he will accept the amendment, and perhaps go along with us in suggesting further constructive amendments to remove the anomaly.
