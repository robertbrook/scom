Col. 44 One of the main points arising on amendment No. 9, which will possibly arise elsewhere, is the Government's increasing tendency to give themselves powers to make regulations that can be widely drawn, and that fail to come under the scrutiny of this House as do matters that are written into primary legislation. The primary intention of the clause is to enable the Department to compute an earnings factor where correct information has not been supplied by the employer. Unless we can do this, individuals will not receive the benefit to which they are entitled. At the same time, we are taking the opportunity to put right, as the hon. Amendment No. 7 seeks to make the computation of earnings factors more precise. As the hon. Amendment No. 9 narrows the scope of the clause to individuals

"with two or more concurrent employments."

It would restrict the scope of what we intend to people with two jobs. It is possible for people who are doing two jobs at the same time and who contribute at the lower rate to have, on paper, a higher earnings factor than a person with one employment but the same total earnings. By tabling this amendment, the hon. Lady recognised that discrepancy and wished to deal with it. The clause as drafted, however, does deal with it. Our purpose was to go wider; the hon. Lady narrows that purpose—I think unintentionally. We wanted to ensure that any unusual work or pay circumstances do not lead to individuals having higher earnings factors than people with one employment or one payment but the same total earnings.

I am thinking of a person who is paid on a four-weekly basis, but who receives payments for overtime on a different cycle—perhaps mid-monthly. It cannot be equitable for individuals in such circumstances to be able to achieve a higher earnings factor than an individual who has the same total earnings, but from one employment, or who receives his overtime along with his monthly pay. We wish to tackle this wider opportunity for unfairness between individuals.

Although we agree with the purpose of the hon. Lady's amendment, it would make it impossible for us to achieve this wider fairness. I hope that, on reflection, the hon. Lady will not press it; if she does, I shall ask my hon. Friends to reject it.

Amendment No. 10 would have the effect of perpetuating errors after they had been discovered. The hon. Lady is aware that from April 1987 employers have been required to keep a record of earnings on which their employees have paid national insurance contributions. The information is needed in order to calculate a person's entitlement to contributory benefits—sickness and unemployment benefit, the basic state retirement pension and Col. 48 additional pension. A small number of employers have either failed to record the information or they have got it wrong. The intention of the clause is to enable the Department to compute an earnings factor, where correct information has not been supplied by the employer, which is as close to the employee's actual earnings as is mathematically possible.

The effect of the hon. Lady's amendment would be to allow an employee whose employer had incorrectly recorded his earnings at too high a level—for instance, earnings above the upper earnings limit on which contributions had not been paid by the employee—to have a higher earnings factor and thus a higher benefit level than an employee who had the same level of earnings which was correctly recorded by his employer.

I want to assure the Committee that we keep our records in such a way that we can identify whether the earnings factor has been computed in this way. If, as a result of incorrect recording by an employer, an individual's benefit is overpaid, we shall not seek to recover any of that benefit. We shall, however, be able to change the earnings factor—up or down, as appropriate, and as accurately as new information allows—if new information comes to light. This seems to be the fairest way between individuals. I hope that the hon. Lady and other members of the Committee will agree.

Amendment No. 4 would remove from section 26(1) of the Social Security Act 1986 the offence of persistently refusing or neglecting to maintain oneself. It is remarkable that such an offence exists.

Section 26 provides that a person who

"persistently refuses or neglects to maintain himself or any person whom he is liable to maintain"
