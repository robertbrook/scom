Clause 1
REPAYMENT OF CONTRIBUTIONS WHERE EARNINGS BECOME REPAYABLE

Mrs. Beckett
: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 7, after "provisions)", insert

"after paragraph 5 there shall be inserted—

'5A. Regulations shall provide for the repayment of such proportion of any primary Class 1 or Class 2 contributions paid in 1988-89 and subsequent years as would otherwise be used to meet the current cost to the National Insurance Fund of contributions paid under section 3(1 Kb) or section 7 of the Social Security Act 1986.';

and."

 The clause provides for the repayment of national insurance contributions in certain circumstances. The amendment provides for the repayment to either employed or self-employed contributors of as much of thier contributions as has been used to finance the 2 per cent, bribe payable to those who choose to opt out of an occupational scheme or the state earnings-related pension scheme and take out a personal pension.

The amendment is valuable, not only for the opportunity that it affords the Committee to look, JPEG 5 however briefly, at the issue, but for the light that it casts on the Government's principles and policies with regard to the treatment of the national insurance contributions and funds. It also casts a little light, which will be valuable to all members of the Committee, on how much weight one should give to, and with what care one should scrutinise, the assurances given by Ministers.

Not everyone may have studied the matter in the closest possible detail. Therefore, I shall remind the Committee of the background to the 2 per cent, bribe. In 1984 or thereabouts, the then Secretary of State for Health and Social Security, as his contribution to the privatisation programme and to the new wave of social change that was sweeping the country—we are told by the Prime Minister that there is no such thing as society, but pursue social engineerng—came up with the idea of personal portable pensions. He described them as "a pension of their own", as if, in some obscure way, a pension provided in any other way was not a pension of someone's own. The consultation document on this idea met with a profound lack of enthusiasm, not only in the pensions industry but more widely, because it made it clear that a personal portable pension was likely to be such a rotten buy that no one in his or her right mind would contract out of the state scheme or an occupational scheme to secure his or her retirement income in this way.

To make his proposal attractive the right hon. Gentleman had to undermine the basis of the existing pension schemes. Initially, Government proposed to abolish the state earnings-related pension scheme, which was a much better bet and better value for money than anything that could be provided by an individual, for reasons that may be obvious to the Committee and to which I shall come in a moment. That proposal provoked a furore because by chance—no doubt by mischance in the eyes of the right hon. Gentleman—both the Prime Minister and the previous Secretary of State had given specific assurances, in the preceding general election campaign, that no such move would be made. Consequently the right hon. Gentleman had to retreat from his initial proposal to abolish SERPS. Therefore, he settled for what seemed to him like the best bet in the circumstances, and substantially weakened not only SERPS itself but the standards that have to be met in occupational pension schemes because those standards are set by the baseline of what is provided in the state scheme.

The Government's figures show that the Secretary of State's proposals, which were pushed through in the Social Security Act 1986, halved the value of the state earnings-related pension scheme. That is why we are in the position in which we are today. It is possible to argue that a personal pension is of some value to those under 40 or under 45, as the case may be, only because it is compared with the new halved-in-value SERPS, not because personal pensions are a good idea or a good buy.

Personal pensions are potentially a disastrous investment for two reasons. First, they are personal pensions. Whereas in SERPS or an occupational scheme, a person spreads the risk of providing for 6 his income in retirement across a wide group, in a personal pension he undertakes the whole risk and the sole risk. One of the most important features of the Government scheme that is being promoted through the 2 per cent, bribe is that not only does the individual take the whole risk himself but the extent of that risk is made explicit because there is no underpinning, no guarantee. At least, with SERPS and an occupational scheme, there is some underpinning, some guarantee, some baseline below which a person is not allowed to fall, or the scheme is unacceptable to the Occupational Pensions Board or the community. With a personal pensions scheme, there is a flaw—the Government refuse to write in any kind of undertaking or guarantee. The individual who takes out such a pension bears the entire risk without any back-up from the community.

The second reason why it is so dangerous is that personal pensions—like occupational schemes, which are now acceptable but which were not before the Secretary of State's changes in 1986—are basically money purchase schemes. They rely entirely on the performance of investment. A person may be fortunate with the investment that he makes, either through an individual programme or a unit-trust scheme. His investments may perform well and may be performing well when he retires. However, if that is not the case, that person could have a completely inadequate income on retirement. What we find objectionable in the 2 per cent, bribe and seek to address through the amendment, is that the Government are offering the bribe, which they call an incentive or which Tory Members might prefer to call a subsidy, paid for by taxpayer's money to encourage people to enter a pension scheme that might be completely unsatisfactory and may not even provide them with security in retirement.

We also find the bribe objectionable because it involves the whole national insurance scheme. All contributors to the national insurance scheme will pay for this bribe, despite the fact that, by definition, not all national insurance contributors are eligible to benefit from it, even though we would argue that they would be unwise to take advantage of the opportunity offered. Only a minority of contributors to the scheme could benefit from it. It is not available to members of occupational schemes that were already contracted out in 1986, nor to the self-employed, nor to a person for any period during which he is not in paid employment. That excludes the unemployed, young people on youth training schemes and those caring for the elderly, the disabled or children. Only a small proportion of those who are paying for this bribe can benefit from it, if "benefit" can result from a move that persuades people into something that is not in their best interests.

Secondly, apart from the fact that the whole scheme is funding this bribe, there is the question of the level at which the bribe is being funded. The Committee considered the Social Security Act 1986, of which a few of us are survivors, was given misleading information when the proposal was put forward. It was estimated in 1986 that the cost of the 2 per cent. JPEG 7 bribe would be £60 million, which the then Secretary of State described as "not particularly costly".. Hon. Members may recall, when we debate other amendments that may cost £500,000, £1.5 million or some such sum, and that the Government say are wildly extravagant and cannot be afforded, that the then Secretary of State described a bribe of £60 million as "not particularly costly", and suggested that it would be the equivalent of between 500,000 or 1 million additional people contracting out, depending on which date one takes the figure that the Government gave.

More recent figures from the Government Actuary estimate that £310 million of "incentives", as he is instructed to call them, but which we prefer to call more accurately "bribes", will be paid in 1989–90, £80 million relating to contributions from 1987–88 and £230 million to 1988–89. That figure is the minimum cost of paying the bribe, because it consists only of the moneys paid out, and does not take account of income lost to the fund because of people contracting out and the loss of the rebate on their contributions. In 1988–89, the real cost to the fund resulting from lost income and the 2 per cent, bribe, is £430 million, and for 1989–90, we estimate that the loss to the fund will be some £600 million.

The former Secretary of State may have described £60 million as "not particularly costly", but he, and all members of the Committee, will concede that £600 million is a substantial sum. On average, the bribe will cost every contributor to the national insurance fund £30. The bribe will allow the state to use its money—and the taxpayers' money because until the Bill is enacted, there is a Treasury supplement—to pay £600 million and introduce a scheme in which only a small minority of contributors to the fund can "participate"—that is a better word than "benefit". If they do so, they may soon regret it—indeed, we may all regret it with them. Opposition Members find the Government's encouragement of the scheme imprudent. Although the Government may issue consultation documents and say that if people make investments that do not prosper, it is their problem and they will have to bear the burden of having an inadequate income in retirement, the chances are high that the Government of the day, of whatever political shade or colour, will have to pick up the pieces—especially for those who have a totally inadequate retirement income— because this Government gave people an incentive to go into the scheme, telling them that it could offer them a potential advantage. For all those reasons, the Government should seriously consider the amendment.

As we go through the Bill, the Government will tell us that all manner of savings, some minor and others less so, should be made because the taxpayer must be protected from excessive spending. However, I notice that the Government's press release states that the savings will be £100 million, although the figures in the financial memorandum total about £130 million—about one third more. That pales into insignificance beside the money that is being lost to the national insurance fund from this proposal, which the Government are pursuing on 8 ideological grounds alone, to give people the impression that they will do better for themselves as individuals than by pooling with the community as a whole the risk of maintaining themselves in retirement.

Mrs. Beckett
: Clause 3 is a different kettle of fish from the clauses that we have already debated in that it deals with what most Members of the Committee regard as the rather obscure matter of earnings factors. I hope that it will not be the case with every clause, but I am reminded of some of the debates that we had in 1985. I recall that it was in March that year, while we were debating the Government's main plans for state earnings-related pensions and occupational pension schemes, that the Chancellor of the Exchequer came up with one of his clever wheezes, which was not wholly unwelcome at the time. Although the Government were increasing the general burden of national insurance contributions, there was some mitigation for low-paid workers and gradual phasing-in of the increase of scales for the initial payment of such contributions was introduced.

Unfortunately, it became clear through the usual channels—not the Whips Office, in this case, but other channels—that the Chancellor had not, as is consistent with the principles of Budget secrecy, communicated his clever wheeze to anyone at the Department of Social Security. The DSS Ministers were in the process of introducing very complex pensions legislation which relied on the simple calculation of earnings factors. Then the Chancellor threw a spanner in the works and thoroughly complicated the whole system, so that not only would the paying of contributions be affected, but the calculations of the earnings factors on which those contributions were based. Rumblings were heard—indeed, they reached the ears of Opposition Members—that the DSS found the idea unwelcome and did not know how it was going to cope with implementing it. As has become apparent, it has taken four years for the Department to find a way of dealing with the consequences of the Chancellor's clever little wheeze.

We have tabled the amendment because we are not wholly satisfied that the DSS has yet succeeded in adapting the Chancellor's proposal to the social security legislation in a way which will not potentially disadvantage claimants. We may be doing the Government an injustice, but on this occasion we assume that that is not their intention. We fear JPEG 39 that they may inadvertently, for once, disadvantage people when it comes to the earnings factor on which pensions are calculated.

Amendment No. 7 offers an alternative form of wording for the clause as it stands. Any member of the Committee who has had to handle national insurance and taxation matters for an employee will be familiar with the parallel process relating to earnings factors. The intention is that benefit entitlement should be ascertainable from tables or rules drawn up by the Secretary of State. That process was, and remains, relatively simple from the point of view of employees paying class 1 contributions where that is a single percentage of earnings, so a given amount of contributions can be assumed to correspond to a given amount of earnings. Since the Chancellor had his clever idea in 1985, there have been different contribution rates on different brackets of earnings. Although that may be desirable in itself, it makes it impossible to derive earnings factors as simply as it used to be. That has resulted in numerous amendments to section 13 of the 1975 Act and regulations of considerable complexity which the Committee will be amazed to learn are among the many defective regulations made from time to time by the Department. We believe that the idea of clause 3 is to validate retrospectively that legislation.

As a result of the clause, the Act would be amended so that for any tax year beginning on or after 6 April 1987, the earnings factor will be dealt with as if it is for a sum

"equal or approximating to the amount of those earnings".

 That is an imprecise term. Let us not forget that this is not simply a technical, arcane matter. In the previous debate, the minister praised the Government for the way in which they fund pensions. He knows that we disagree with that to some extent but it is only right that people should have the correct amount of pension for the contributions that they have made—that is what earnings factors are all about. The proposal is that the wording should be "approximating to" those earnings, and just in case the Department still has not got it right—we shall deal with this in a later amendment—subsection 5(a) gives the Secretary of State power to modify what is being done by regulations.

We believe that the correspondence of those two proposals means that there is not even a guarantee that earnings factors will approximately correspond to the amount of earnings on which contributions have been paid. If the earnings factors do not corrrespond even approximately, there is a danger that someone could lose all entitlement to what are known as short-term benefits such as unemployment and sickness benefit and might also lose part of his pension entitlement.

Now, because of the complexities that have been introduced, it is more important for people to be able to rely on the Secretary of State's tables giving the right answer. The phrase "in general" is simply not good enough.

Amendment No. 8 seeks to impose a slightly more definite requirement on the Secretary of State to find a method to calculate earnings factors that does not involve an unacceptable risk of depriving people of the benefits that their contributions should have earned. Even if the previous amendment were not accepted and the phrase "in general" remained in the Bill, the effect of this amendment would be that earnings factors would have to be at least equal to the earnings on which contributions were paid. We are seeking to establish some kind of underpinning or guarantee that people will have at least the earnings factor for which they have contributed.

The Government could pick out one amendment and say, "That one is acceptable although others are not." Under amendment No. 8 the words "in general" would remain in the Bill and it would still allow the Secretary of State power to modify by regulations. However, if the discussion of this package of amendments led the Government to consider the phrasing of the clause, that would be a good thing.

Amendment No. 9 deals with the power to make regulations that impose a limit on a person's earnings factor for any year. We are always wary of giving to Governments open-ended powers that could be used either in the way in which the Government say that they are intended to be used or in quite another way. The progress of this Government over the past 10 years has given us cause for alarm—alarm that at one time would have been shared by both sides of the Committee. I find it not the least disturbing of the developments over the past 10 years that Conservative Members these days are apparently quite happy to give their Government—or any future Government—unlimited powers to make up later legislation as they go along and even make it up by regulations that are not legal under the Act in question.
