Mr. Lloyd
: Paragraph 7 of the schedule and amendment No. 221 give statutory backing to a procedure endorsed by the social security commissioners whereby an appeal to a social security appeal tribunal against an adjudication officer's decision lapses if the adjudication officer can give the claimant all that he could have got, had his appeal succeeded.

The procedure not only benefits the claimant by giving him a favourable decision quickly and without the need to wait for a tribunal hearing to be arranged, but it is also administratively quicker and cheaper for the adjudication officer to review his decision.

It is true that it may be a subjective judgment for the adjudication officer to decide what the claimant would have been awarded by the tribunal, but this is not currently a problem in practice. When the adjudication officer is unsure, he simply does not revise the case but allows the appeal to proceed in the normal way. As a further safeguard, the claimant's appeal rights are unaffected. He can appeal against both the adjudication officer's original decision and his revised decision.

The Government amendment makes it clear that the appeal lapses only when all the material grounds of the appeal are satisfied by the adjudication officer's revised decision. I hope that the Committee will accept the amendment.

JPEG 831 Amendment No. 177 would require that, when an adjudication officer has favourably reviewed an appealed decision reducing the period for which a claimant has been disqualified for receiving unemployment benefit, his review decision would take effect, but the appeal would still proceed to the appeal tribunal. However, the tribunal would not then be able to increase the period of disqualification.

The amendment restricts the powers of the social security appeal tribunals, but appears to forget the Social Security Commissions, to whom the adjudication officer could appeal, on a point of law, if he disagreed with the tribunal's decision. Moreover, it presents technical problems as it allows the adjudication officer's review decision to take effect, thus cancelling or replacing the original decision, but still allows the appeal against that original decision to go forward to the tribunal.

The amendment is unnecessary. Our proposal in paragraph 7 of schedule 1 is that appeals to appeal tribunals should lapse when the adjudication officer has reviewed his original decision, but only when he can do so entirely favourably to the claimant. As hon. Members will know, we are clarifying paragraph 7 with a Government amendment. But our intention is that, unless the adjudication officer can give the claimant all that he could have got on appeal, his review decision will be of no effect and the appeal will proceed to the tribunal in the normal way. In cases of disqualification for unemployment benefit, the adjudication officer would effectively be prevented from reviewing his original decision unless he decided not to disqualify at all. Any residual period of disqualification would simply not constitute a decision favourable to the claimant.

In view of that, I hope that the Opposition will not press the amendment.

I wish to quote from a letter from a firm of solicitors that specialises in such cases. It states:

"No provision is made in the Bill to deduct benefits from the claim that they most relate to. For example, if statutory sick pay is to be deducted then it should only be deducted from loss of earnings, not in respect of the Bill as presently drafted would result in the erosion of damages … the purpose of damages is an attempt by the law to put the plaintiff in the position that he/she was prior to the accident. This can only be done by financial compensation. The effect of this Bill would be to erode this principle. This has never been done before. In the case of Parry v. Cleaver"—

I cannot remember how far back that case was, but it is fundamental and has set the case law for many years. In that case—

"it was established that you should only take away 'like from like'."

I have referred to the criticism made by the Industrial Injuries Advisory Council of some of the recommendations of the Pearson commission. That commission took the view that the reduction of benefit should be regarded as taking like from like.

The more recent decision in Hodgson v. Trapp on whether attendance allowance should be taken away was that it should be subtracted from the claim for care but not from more general claims. It appears that the courts—certainly in Parry v. Cleaver in 1969 and Hodgson v. Trapp, one in the House of Lords and the other in the Court of Appeal—have decided that that is how deduction of benefits should be treated.

The TUC has made the same point. I quote:

"Despite all the allegations of double compensation, little attention has been given to the fact that a damages award includes elements relating to different types of losses and extra expenses, and that different State benefits also serve different purposes which broadly correlate to the different heads of damages. Even if the allegation of double compensation is accepted"—

As I said on an earlier amendment, the TUC does not accept the allegation—

"it would clearly be illogical and unjust to deduct the value of a benefit provided to meet one loss or need from a part of a damages award intended as compensation for a different loss or need."

The Law Society has also suggested that the clause should be amended so that the recoveries from benefits should be limited to that head of damage for which benefit was paid.

A fundamental objection to the idea of recovery of benefit from compensation payments is that even if the principle of like from like is accepted, it is difficult to apply it consistently. We shall raise that issue on later amendments. We are considering cases where judgment is given in the High Court, where a transcript of the decision is available and where a decision about the calculation of damages can be given on a judicial basis. It is then possible to identify the precise basis of the compensation payment. As the Minister argued in resisting our previous amendment, that might not be so easy in other courts.

We recognise that there may be difficulties in some circumstances in matching the head of damages award and the payment of benefit. It is not enough for the 854 Government to argue—irrespective of the merits of the case and of the justice of their proposals—that they should act in the easiest way and the one that gets the maximum return for them without considering how the action will be viewed.

The amendments are a partial and, no doubt, imperfect attempt to incorporate the principles of like from like deductions into the clause. They seek to provide that where different heads of damages can be identified, any benefit which corresponds to a particular head should be recoverable only out of that head of damages.

We believe that that will confirm a principle that is now fairly widely applied. It is a principle that should be enshrined in legislation. The Government should not act in the way most convenient to them. We will then have to try to reconcile cases where that will not be an easy judgment to make. We may deal with that on later amendments.

The Government almost invariably prefer that course of action to writing details into primary legislation, and in these circumstances we feel that it is justified.
