Mrs. Beckett
: Welcome back to the Committee, Mr. Winterton; I kept trying to call Mr. McWilliam by your name on Tuesday afternoon.

We are moving to that part of the Bill which has serious implications. We can now discuss how the provisions relating to "actively seeking employment", which we discussed on clause 7, affect the provision of a basic level of income support. An unemployed person who is claiming income support will in future have to show not only that he is available for work but that he is actively seeking employment in exactly the same way as someone who is claiming unemployment benefit. There is one crucial difference: income support is a benefit of last resort. It is a benefit for people who have no other source of income. It might cause a person problems and distress to suspend his entitlement to unemployment benefit but he might retain a residual right to income support, although not if another member of the family is in full-time work. To remove his entitlement to income support, as the clause might do, could result in total destitution not only for the claimant but for his family.

Amendment No. 112 tries to protect families from this threat be exempting from the "actively seeking 540 employment" test, for income support purposes only, a claimant with a child. We have defined a child, according to the 1986 Act, as a person under 16. That does not mean that someone might not lose his entitlement to unemployment benefit under clause 7, but while there is a child in the household who needs sustenance and support he would not lose his right to income support.

We are considering this clause alongside the test of availability for work. Families can lose entitlement to income support at present if it is decided that the claimant is not available for work. Under the old supplementary benefit regulations which operated from 1980 to 1988, if it was decided that someone might not be available for employment, urgent needs payments could be made, subject to the 40 per cent. voluntary unemployment deduction, although only if there were no other means of preventing serious damage to the health or safety of the claimant or any member of his family.

That limited safeguard was omitted from the original version of the income support regulations which were tabled under the 1986 Act. However, the Government either gave way at the last minute or realised that they had been in error. One of the many changes that were made a few days before they came into force was the insertion of regulation 8(3) in the Income Support (General) Regulations 1987. That regulation made income support payable to someone who failed the availability test if he or a member of his family would otherwise suffer hardship. As with the old supplementary benefit system, the payments were subject to the 40 per cent. deduction.

The Minister has suggested that the provisions will apply in the same way to those who are not actively seeking employment. In our debate on clause 7 on 2 February, he said:

"Of course, the hardship provisions would continue to exist. That is, income support regulations would include a power to allow claimants to receive a reduced rate of income support for weeks when the new test is not satisfied if the claimant was able to satisfy the adjudication officer that he or a member of his family would suffer hardship. That is the current practice with availability and we propose to carry it forward with the 'actively seeking work' test."

But when my hon. Friend the Member for Birkenhead (Mr. Field) asked how long it would take for such hardship payments to come through, the Minister showed rather less confidence that the Department of Social Security had worked out the details. He said:

"The decision could be made on the same day, I think. If the benefit is to be withdrawn, the facility would be given for the claimant to discuss his income support entitlement and the decision could be made that day."

I emphasise the word "could". The Minister continued:

"I hope that that would be so in the overwhelming majority of cases." [ Official Report, Standing Committee F, 2 February 1989; c. 268.]

I can well understand why the Minister used such phrases as "I think", "I hope" and "the facility would be given". As all hon. Members recognise—although some are more ready than others to admit it—real life in the Departments of Employment and Social Security is not like that. To get income support restored on the grounds of hardship is much more JPEG 541 likely to take weeks rather than days, let alone the same day on which unemployment benefit entitlement has been questioned. If it is restored within days, it will probably happen only because the claimant practically lives in the Department of Social Security office until someone gets round to making a decision on his case.

Despite the Minister's observations, most people whose benefit is cut off will not receive hardship payments. That is not because there is no hardship. Sheer common sense suggests that very few unemployed people can survive without any income for more than a few days—or even for just a few days—and not suffer hardship. But inevitably DSS staff regard the hardship provision as something to be used only rarely, in the most exceptional cases.

Few claimants are likely to know about hardship payments unless they seek advice, which they are unlikely to do in the initial stages. It is possible that many DSS and unemployment benefit officials will be unaware of the hardship payment. Even if they are aware of it, they are not likely to mention it, unless they are specifically asked. One of the minor changes made in the Social Security Act 1986 was to remove from the staff, for the first time since 1930, the whole duty to inform people of their rights and ensure that they claimed all the benefits to which they were entitled. The onus is now on the claimant rather than the official.

We do not believe that amendment No. 122 would open the flood gates to claims from thousands of feckless parents. To qualify for income support at the normal minimum rate, people would still have to pass the availability test. The amendment would protect parents who are still available for work but who, in a particular week, are suspected of not having taken adequate steps to satisfy the "actively seeking employment" test.

It is important to emphasise that the suspicion that someone is not actively seeking employment will be enough to lead to the suspension of benefit. We already know that the Government are not prepared to allow benefit to continue—we explored that point in our debates on previous clauses and amendments—until the suspicion has been investigated and until a formal decision has been made by the adjudication officer.

In most cases where the question of non-availability arises, the adjudication officer eventually decides in the claimant's favour. In availability cases, benefit can continue, pending the adjudication officer's decision, in actively seeking employment cases, families will have no protection, except the hardship provision of regulation 8(3).

The Government have emphasised that they are introducing the actively seeking employment test because they believe that the availability test alone provides insufficient pressure. They have said that it is too passive a test and does not require enough of the claimant. So people could pass the availability test, but still have their benefit entitlement called into question under the actively seeking employment test. It would be wrong and dangerous if families had 542 to face the risk of losing all income under that second, more harsh and stringent test.

Amendment No. 113 is similar to amendment No. 112, but it refers not to families with children but to claimants who are disabled, or who have a disabled or sick husband, wife or child. We wish to probe the Government's intentions and to offer them a range of alternatives about possible concessions, all of which we should welcome.

Amendment No. 113 does not define the terms "sick" and "disabled", but it provides for those terms to be defined in regulations, which the Government usually tell us they prefer.

The actively seeking employment test poses the most serious threat to that category of claimant. Single persons in good health may be able to manage for a short time with no income at all, although even then the physical and moral risks are extremely serious, and it is not unknown for people in those circumstances to commit suicide. The risks are infinitely greater for people who are not in good health.

On clause 7, we emphasised the fact that many of those who have been ruled fit for light work, in order to save money on sickness and disability benefits, will find it most difficult to meet the actively seeking employment test. A severely disabled man or woman who insists on signing on and looking for work rather than claiming invalidity benefit will be at risk. Many disabled people fiercely cherish their independence and passionately want to do all that they can to be independent. Nevertheless, a person who, perhaps against advice and his or her own best interests, persists in looking for work and signing on may not be able to keep up indefinitely the weekly grind of sending off job applications to satisfy the actively seeking employment test. The Minister told us that the test will be administered sensitively, but that is not stated in the Bill: that is just the Minister's fervently expressed hope. So we must consider what is stated in the Bill and how peope in such circumstances may be affected by the law as well as by the practice of the local DSS office.

A woman with a seriously ill husband who is genuinely looking for work, but who is too preoccupied in any given week or fortnight with the needs and difficulties of her husband's illness to make the weekly quota of job applications could also be affected. There is a danger that in a family with a disabled child, with perhaps both parents looking for work and sharing the responsibility of caring for the child, neither parent may satisfy an official, particularly a young and relatively inexperienced one, that he or she is sufficiently actively seeking employment. Those examples are hypothetical now, but they will become real, once the test is in operation.

Amendment No. 113 would not exempt those people from the requirement to be available for work—a test which has been sufficient for many years and which would remain—but they would be protected form the risk of the arbitrary withdrawal of their only source of income on the suspicion of not actively seeking employment. We have emphasised that that suspicion could lead to the JPEG 543 suspension of benefit at a minimum for a period of four weeks.

If accepted, amendment No. 115 would mean that section 20(3)(d)(i) would read:

"A person in Great Britain is entitled to income support… (d) except in such circumstances as may be prescribed,


(i) he is available for, and actively seeking employment or hardship will result unless income support is paid to him."

Regulation 8(3) of the Income Support (General) Regulations already provides for the payment of income support to a claimant who fails the availability test

"for any period when the adjudication officer is satisfied that, unless income support is paid, the claimant or a member of his family (if any) will suffer hardship."

The Minister has said that the same hardship provision will apply to people who fail the actively seeking employment test. The amendment would write that into the Bill, instead of leaving it to regulations.

There are two reasons for suggesting that. First, it is much too important a matter to leave to regulations. Questions of survival, quite literally, may hang on whether and in what conditions income support is payable to someone who either fails, or is simply suspected of failing, the test of actively seeking employment. There are grave doubts both inside and outside the Committee about whether the test is capable of being administered in a way that does not involve a serious risk of injustice in large numbers of cases. In that situation we believe that it should be clearly stated in the Bill that benefit will not be withheld where hardship will result.

The second reason for wanting to write the provision into the Bill is that one cannot be absolutely certain that the regulations will, by the time that they are printed, reflect the advice and the assurances that the Minister has given to the Committee. That is not suggesting that the Minister did not give those assurances in good faith. Presumably the first version of the notes on clauses was issued in good faith, but it was speedily followed by a second version—clearly because a different decision had been made. We want to guard against the possibility of a different decision being made between the Bill leaving Committee and the regulations being made. The Government most unwisely failed to accept our suggestion that the regulations should be amendable, so we should have to accept or reject the regulations en bloc. Therefore, the purpose of amendment No. 115 is to remove any temptation to change the policy on hardship payments.

Amendment No. 129 is still grouped with the other amendments. However, we have decided that it would not achieve what we intend, and with your permission, Mr. Winterton, we do not propose to move it. I mention that merely to clarify the position.

The Minister for Social Security (Mr. Nicholas Scott)
: This group of amendments has a clear and understandable purpose from the point of view of the Opposition, which is substantially to change the main thrust of the provisions to ensure that in order to be entitled to benefit, whether unemployment benefit income support, people should be actively seeking work. There is a clear difference of view, which will lead me in due course to advise my hon. Friends to reject the amendments. However, I shall deal with the points raised by the hon. Member for Derby, South (Mrs. Beckett) before I deal with the substance of the amendments.

The hon. Lady had some fun with the gentle caveats that I entered about my assertion that hardship payments would normally be made on the day that they were applied for. It is almost invariably the case that if application is made under the hardship provisions, payment is made on that day. However, even though I have been responsible for social security matters for a short time, I am conscious of the fact that there can be exceptions. I would not want to give an undertaking, which could be shown to be false, that in 100 per cent. of all such cases the payment would be made on the day, but it would be exceptional if such payments were not made on the same day as the application was made.

The hon. Member for Derby, South asserted that judgments about whether someone is actively seeking work will be taken whimsically by employment officers. We discussed that fully on an earlier clause. I explained at great length that procedures, advice, counselling and ultimately the warnings that may have to be given to claimants will ensure that no one will decide on a mere whim that a claimant is not actively seeking work. Claimants will have every opportunity to show that they are so doing before there is any question of their being disqualified from benefit.

Amendment No. 112 concerns families in which at least one member is a child. It is extraordinary that the Opposition should disagree with the whole concept of actively seeking work, yet they have not tabled an amendment specifically on that point.

The suggestion that a single person, perhaps with a partner but no child, should be encouraged actively to seek work but that someone with family responsibilities should not is remarkable. Those are precisely the people who need counselling and advice. They need the possibility of being denied benefit to encourage them to seek work. They should be alive to their real responsibilities and should be encouraged in every way to maintain a positive attitude to returning to employment so that they can be responsible for the welfare of their families.

Amendment No. 113 is the second leg of the concept expressed in amendment No. 112. It says that someone should be exempt from the actively seeking work condition if a member of his family is sick or disabled. Income support legislation already provides that claimants can receive benefit without having to be available for work if they are looking after a member of the family who is ill, or if they are caring for person, whether that person is a member of the family or not. Those exemptions are in schedule 1 JPEG 545 to the 1987 income support regulations. The new regulations should ensure that people are exempted from the actively seeking work condition if they belong to one of the categories listed in the 1987 regulations. They include lone parents, or people who are incapable of work or who are receiving invalid care allowance, or people who are temporarily looking after someone in the family who is ill. The amendment goes too wide and is unnecessary.

Amendment No. 115 is another variation on the theme of exempting people from the actively seeking work condition. It suggests that someone would not have to seek work actively if hardship would result if he or she did not receive income support. Under the amendment, someone would not have to try to find work but could just say, "Sorry, I've got to have money in order to save me from hardship, so there's no point in my seeking work". The main duty of a person in such circumstances, however, should be to get himself or herself back to the position of being able to earn a living.

Clearly, hardship in the event of non-entitlement is a real issue. However, there is already an availability condition, and an identical provision will be made for people who fail the actively seeking work test. That is an appropriate way of dealing with the problem.

I am not completely sure, from what the hon. Lady said, whether the amendment would allow a full rather than a reduced rate of benefit to be paid. I believe, and successive Governments have believed, that to pay a reduced rate of benefit reflects the correct balance between the interests of taxpayers, who are entitled to see benefit paid in accordance with rules that place a sensible obligation on claimants, and those of the claimants themselves. It would be wrong to pay someone who did not satisfy the normal conditions of benefit the same rate as someone who did.

Amendment No. 114 allows provision to be made for circumstances in which a person is not to be treated for income support as sick or disabled. Again I refer back to schedule 1 of the 1987 regulations. I have said that we intend to exempt from the need to seek work actively the categories of people in that list. Paragraph 5 of the list refers to people who are incapable of work and paragraphs 6 and 7 refer to certain disabled people. Of course we understand that many disabled people are incapable of work. In those circumstances, they would be exempt under paragraph 5 and the relevant regulations, which will reflect that in due course.

The interests of those groups are already properly safeguarded and I see no need for separate regulations to cater for them, as suggested by the amendment. I must therefore ask my hon. Friends and the Committee as a whole, if I can persuade Opposition Members, to reject all the amendments.

I am not being satanic or devious in saying that that means that amendment No. 120 will fall, because there will not be a line 37. However, as there must be a possibility that the Committee will not take my advice, I should like to say a word or two about amendment No. 120 because the Committee will have to come to a decision in due course.

The purpose of that amendment is to provide express power in primary legislation for work-related expenses to be taken into account by the adjudication authorities in determining whether, for income support purposes, a claimant has "good cause" for refusing to accept or seek an opportunity of employment. The power to make regulations in relation to "good cause" matters for income support purposes only is removed by amendment No. 119, so the amendment is not appropriate.

The amendment is, in any case, unnecessary, because the regulations which will deal with good cause in the context of unemployment benefit—and its consequences—will not provide an exhaustive list of relevant matters. Power will be there for the adjudication authorities to consider a range of issues. Some of them may be mentioned, but they will be able to take other matters into account. They will certainly take account of necessary work-related expenses where they believe that that is appropriate. It is important that the Committee recognises that there are various kinds of working expenses such as fares to and from work, child-minding expenses, tools and working clothes. In considering whether to suggest a particular vacancy to any claimant, employment office staff would have regard, as they do now, to the costs the claimant would necessarily incur. They would not suggest to them that they should go after a job where the associated expenses 576 would absorb an unduly high proportion of the earnings.

Opposition Members might seek to press me to say what I regard as an unduly high proportion of earnings. It would not be possible to prescribe to in regulations what that might be, whether as a level of money or as a percentage of the wage that was being offered.

Since the regulations on "good cause" matters under clause 9 would, we intend, allow the adjudication authorities to take account of necessary work expenses, a specific power to do that is not needed. We shall consider whether we need to list those work expenses in the regulations—we do not need it in primary legislation—and it might be helpful if they were specifically mentioned. I do not give an absolute undertaking, but it might be a useful step for us to include them in the regulations.
