Mrs. Margaret Beckett (Derby, South): I beg to move amendment No. 111, in page 6, line 6, at end insert—

"(1A) After subsection (1) of that section there shall be inserted the following subsection—

'(1A) For the purpose of determining the length of the period for which a person is to be disqualified under subsection (1) above for receiving unemployment benefit, the adjudication officer or other authority making the determination shall have regard to all the circumstances of the case and shall not determine that the person is to be disqualified for 26 weeks unless there are exceptional circumstances'.".

The purpose of the amendment is to make it clear beyond any doubt that the maximum period of disqualification in cases of so-called voluntary unemployment should be imposed only in exceptional circumstances. Of course, we do not mean to imply that we approve of the power to disqualify for as long as 26 weeks. We opposed the extension of the period to 26 weeks, and we opposed the previous extension of the period to 13 weeks. However, the amendment draws attention to the fact that the 26-week period is a maximum.

If someone has voluntarily left a job without a just cause, unreasonably refused suitable employment, or has in some other way come within the disqualification provisions in section 20 of the Social Security Act 1975, the adjudication officer is obliged to disqualify that person for some period. Depending on the circumstances, the law provides that the period can be as long as 26 weeks or as short as a day. Between those limits, as a tribunal of commissioners stated in its decision in 1974, adjudication officers and appeal tribunals have complete discretion as to the period to be imposed. In a more recent decision, CU 32/86, another commissioner explained both the history and the present state of the law:

"It was held by a Commissioner in decision R(U) 17/54, applying the insurance princple, that normally the maximum period of six weeks' disqualification"—

as it then was—

"should be imposed and that a heavy burden lay on a person to establish that a lesser period was appropriate. Consequently, it became almost automatic for insurance officers to impose a maximum period of disqualification. In R(U) 8/74 a Tribunal of Commissioners decided that this was an incorrect approach and that each case should be regarded as one in which a 'sensible discretion' fell to be exercised. If cases that have come before the Commissioner since then are any guide I should infer from what I have seen of them that this latter decision has had practically no effect on the practice of insurance (now adjudication) officers Col. 462 imposing disqualification. They seem still to impose the maximum period in practically every case. When there is an appeal they draw the attention of the appeal tribunal in their submission to what was said in R(U) 8/74, but quite frequently … the tribunal … affirm the period of disqualification without its appearing that they have given any consideration to that aspect of the case."

The commissioner goes on to say:

"I am emphasising the point now because of the changes made by the Social Security Act 1986 … First… the maximum period of disqualification has been increased to thirteen weeks by section 43(2) of the Act. And this was presumably done by Parliament in the knowledge of what had been decided in R(U) 8/74. It is thus essential that adjudication officers should abandon their old habit of virtually automatically imposing the maximum."

That was said in 1986, which was before the Government doubled the maximum period again to 26 weeks. The Commissioner went on to say that appeals to the Commissioners would in future be limited to points of law and that appeal tribunals were likely to be held to have erred in law if they did not show in their decisions that they had consciously exercised discretion in imposing disqualification, stating the facts that they had taken into account when doing so.

Since the maximum period of disqualification was first increased to 13 weeks, a survey of some 500 disqualification cases was carried out by the Department of Health and Social Security at the end of 1986. 1 believe that that is the survey to which the Minister has referred. Its findings were disturbing, showing that the law was still being flouted and that the maximum disqualification was still being imposed indiscriminately. According to a report on the survey placed in the Library in March 1988, 75 per cent. of disqualifications were for the full 13 weeks, as it then was. The attached table showed that 80 per cent. were disqualified for more than 12 weeks and only 10 per cent. were disqualified for six weeks or less. The report said:

"There is no indication that either the reason for leaving or the time taken to decide a case has any bearing on the length of disqualification imposed."

Among the main findings selected by the authors was the fact that in a high proportion of cases, no detailed reason for leaving was given beyond voluntary leaving for misconduct. It is clear that even if adjudication officers had wanted to exercise their discretion they did not have the information with which to do so.

This is a serious matter. I clearly recall a case in my constituency where a young woman was forced to leave her employment as a result of a prolonged dispute with her immediate superior. She had no dispute with the firm as a whole and ultimately obtained excellent references from her overall employer but there was a conflict of personality between her and her immediate boss. One of the reasons why she came to me was that he refused to deal with the question of whether, when she had voluntarily made herself unemployed, there was any misconduct. He refused to discuss the matter with the Department knowing perfectly well that while he refused to answer any questions and cast any light on the reasons why she left, the chances were high that she would remain disqualified from benefit and suffer a penalty. Once the facts of the case were resolved she did not suffer that penalty but he was Image Col. 463 able to use this procedure to punish someone whom he had already driven out of her employment.

In April 1988 the maximum period was increased by the Social Security Act 1988 to 36 weeks. The Minister has stated that no sustained information is available on the proportion of cases in which the maximum was still being imposed. The Minister told us that the effect of the increase in the maximum of 26 weeks is being monitored and that the results might be available next year. It was suggested that the chief adjudication officer had examined a small sample of cases and that that showed that the maximum period of disqualification was now imposed in about 50 per cent. of cases. We concede that 50 per cent. of cases is better than 75 per cent. or 80 per cent. However, bearing in mind that the maximum period is now six months it is horrifying that adjudication officers, despite clear guidance from commissioners about their legal obligation to exercise discretion in every case, are still imposing the maximum disqualification in 50 per cent. of cases.

I would not wish to suggest that adjudication officers should take all the blame for this. It is far from clear what considerations they are expected to take into account in exercising their discretion. Much depends on whether one believes that this part of the law is there to punish people for refusing to take a job or for leaving a job—which we consider they have a perfect right to do—or to protect the insurance fund from unnecessary claims. If it is there for the former reason, the Government would presumably regard with some indifference a punishment as long as six months as they chose to extend the period so that it could be that long.

If, as we believe it should be and as it was envisaged, the law is there to protect the insurance fund from unnecessary claims, obviously the questions to which adjudication officers should apply their minds are how easily the person could have prevented his unemployment and how quickly he can reasonably expect to find another job. Even if one believes that it is a penalty, the circumstances in which disqualification is imposed are themselves infinitely varied and so, too, is the degree of blame that is attached to the individual involved.

There are few other areas of law where the norm is the maximum penalty. Maximum penalties are normally reserved for the most serious cases. That is the principle that we seek to embody in the amendment. A message should be conveyed to adjudication officers and appeal tribunals that it is no longer necessary merely to rely on what the commissioner thinks Parliament intended. Parliament should tell them that they have been entrusted with a discretionary power of great importance, which affects a person's basic right to a minimum income.

In deciding whether someone should lose that right, and for how long, all the circumstances of the case must be considered. If it is decided that disqualification is inevitable, the appropriate duration of that disqualification must be considered—it can be from one day to 26 weeks Col. 464 The maximum period of disqualification should be imposed only in exceptional circumstances. That is what Parliament has always intended, but that has not happened in practice. We hope that our amendment will persuade the Government to bring practice into line with the intention of the law. That should help adjudication officers and appeal tribunals.

The National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux expressed its continuing concern that the cases it sees, and suggested that the maximum period of disqualification is almost always automatically imposed. It has discussed that with adjudication officers with a view to trying to get them to exercise their discretion, and it appears that adjudication officers have received no clear guidance on the criteria that they should take into consideration when deciding to impose fewer than the 26 weeks of disqualification.

A tribunal member has written to us:

"Section 20 disqualifications are a nightmare. Parliament has given us an unfettered discretion to decide for how long a claimant should be disqualified, but without offering guidance on how we are to exercise our discretion. We are not a court of law, punishing people for the offence which the law does not in fact recognise. Our function is simply to balance the interests of claimants against those of the fund and its contributors. Some of us feel that the old limit of six weeks' disqualification was quite sufficient, and in many cases more than sufficient, for that purpose. But we are now told we can disqualify for 26 weeks. Does that mean we should normally disqualify for 26 weeks? Does it mean that, in a case where in the past we would have disqualified for 6 weeks, we should now disqualify for 26 weeks? Or does it mean that there is a minority of cases in which Parliament has decided that the interests of the fund require a longer disqualification, and that in the normal run of cases we should continue to disqualify for six weeks of less? Obviously different tribunals would take a different line, making the whole thing a lottery. From everybody's point of view, Parliament's intentions on this subject need to be clarified."

That is the purpose of the amendment, and I hope that in the light of its clarification of the law, the Minister will feel able to accept it.
