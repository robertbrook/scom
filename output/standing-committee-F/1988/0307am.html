Mrs. Marion Roe
(Broxbourne)
: I am sure that the provisions in clause 19 will be welcomed by millions of working women throughout the country, for it brings to an end discrimination in employment-related benefit schemes. Benefit schemes that discriminate against women as a result of their family or marital status are not only unfair to women, but represent an obstacle to the smooth operation of the labour market. At a time when the number of young people in the labour market is contracting and when employers will increasingly have to consider employing older people, especially married women, our economy simply cannot afford to place obstacles in the way of the employment of women. All such barriers are certainly a disincentive to women to seek work and an impediment to the creation of prosperity and jobs.

I am delighted that the clause will ensure that women who take maternity leave will not lose benefit entitlement as a result. Many women who take maternity leave are immensely irked to discover that their period of absence is treated, for the purposes of calculating pensions, as if they had taken a long holiday. I assure the Committee that bearing and rearing children is certainly not a long holiday.

However, while we are considering pensions and the equality of treatment between men and women, there arises the question of the equalisation of the pension age in the state scheme. Those of us who believe in giving women a fair deal in matters of employment and social security legislation recognise that the argument can, of course, work both ways. Women should understand that the principle of equality may require them to retire later than at present rather than men retiring earlier. I hope that my hon. Friend the Minister will reassure the Committee that the Government proposals on the matter will be carefully worked through. Mr. Lloyd
: Before dealing with that point, I shall say a few words on the general principles that lie behind the Government's attitude to statutory requirements on occupational pension schemes as it relates to this amendment and a number of other that we shall be debating.

In general, the Government do not feel that it is right to lay down requirements on schemes, as they are set up voluntarily by employers. It is for them to consult with their employees and determine what should be in the scheme, the rules, and who is eligible to join the scheme. Therefore, in drawing up the rules of the scheme, the employer will no doubt take account of the needs of the employees, his problems in recruiting, retaining and rewarding staff, and the cost to the company of implementing the scheme.

In many schemes the employers have an obligation to make up any deficit in the scheme's funds where the liabilities exceed its assets. We do not consider that it would be in anyone's interest for the Bill to implement the exception in terms which the European Court would be likely subsequently to hold to be beyond what is permitted by the directive. What does the exception permit? First, it permits differences in pension age

"for the purposes of granting old-age or Retirement Pensions".

 This means that schemes may provide for different ages at which pensions come into payment to men and to women. Secondly, it permits

"the possible implications for other benefits"—

 that is benefits to which the directive and schedule 3 apply—

"of differences in pension age for retirement benefits".

 Therefore, if a scheme member receives, for example, benefits under the scheme for ill health and those benefits are reduced or replaced from pension age by a 966 retirement pension, the ill health benefits may reduce or cease at for instance, 65 for men or 60 for women if those are the ages at which the scheme retirement pension becomes payable.

Because the exception applies for the purposes only of granting pensions, it does not extend to other areas, even though the particular issue is directly linked to the age at which pensions come into payment. First, it does not apply to the right to be, or become, a member of a scheme.
