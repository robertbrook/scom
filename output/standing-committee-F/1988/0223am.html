Mr. Battle
: I remind the Committee that we are talking about housing benefit, and the forms of payments and deductions.

The amendments deal with uprating and deductions—non-dependant deductions. When the amendment was first tabled, it was suggested that it would increase the one parent premium in the housing benefit scheme this April in line with inflation, from £8.60 to £9.20 a week. If I were tabling the amendment today, I would be tempted to go for a higher figure, as this figure is only in line with present inflation. We are having difficulty in keeping up with the Government's attempts to lower inflation. 1 understand that it is not only the Committee's difficulty; recently there were difficulties on the Floor of the House over the issue, so I make no apology. Therefore, we shall stick to our figure for the purposes of the amendment.

In the pre-1988 housing benefit scheme, the needs allowance for single parents was the same at that for couples with children, which reflected the view that their needs were similar. Under the new scheme, difficulties occurred when housing benefit and income support were aligned with what the Government called applicable amounts. After the Fowler review the Green Paper on "Reform of Social Security" stated that for lone parents

"alignment would require particularly careful attention"—

Since there was no similar special provision for their needs in the supplementary benefit scheme. It was suggested that the proposed family premium and lone parent premium in the income support scheme would

"help to meet these concerns".

When we pressed for further detail on how that would work in practice, the Government's White Paper of December 1985 included illustrative rates of benefit for 706 the lone parent premium, which turned out to be only £3.45 per week. The White Paper went on to acknowledge that

"the Government additionally proposed that one parent benefit should be disregarded in the calculation of housing benefit."

In fact that did not happen. Instead, a higher lone parent premium of £8.60 was introduced for housing benefit, compared with a £3.70 lone parent premium in the income support scheme. The difference between those figures is £4.90, which is precisely the rate of one parent benefit.

Therefore, for a family which receives one parent benefit of £4.90 a week, it makes no significant difference whether the £4.90 is disregarded in calculating housing benefit, or whether an extra £4.90 is added to the lone parent premium. Either way, the gap between the family's needs and its income is widened by the figure, and its benefit entitlement is increased accordingly.

However, in relation to the Government's overriding objective of cutting the cost of benefits, it makes a difference. To disregard the one parent benefit means that every time the benefit is uprated the housing benefit disregard will also increase. When the £4.90 is added to the lone parent premium for housing benefit purposes, it enables the Government to argue that the addition is only a transitional measure, and that, like other transitional payments, it does not need to be uprated annually. They can argue that it can be allowed to be eroded by inflation, or—as with child benefit—frozen and eventually phased out altogether. There has been a shift away from the principle and original intentions of the Green Paper and from the clear commitment in the White Paper, which gave the illustrative rates, towards transition. Is that what the Government intended? That is what has happened in practice. People have lost out.

The adviser to the Association of Metropolitan Authorities commented:

"An important side issue is the alleged 'transitional' nature of the differential. The AM A has asked the DSS for chapter and verse as to when it was first stated that this provision was transitional. We suspect the introduction of a new concept of "retrospective transitionality'."

That is a heavy phrase to handle at this time in the morning but, to put it in plainer English, it seems that if the Government decide to make a cut they suggest that this is not a permanent and proper arrangement but simply a temporary measure. What is even worse, they do so after the event. People will lose out as a result.

There is a good case for a higher lone parent premium in the housing benefit scheme. Those who would benefit from it would be lone parents whose income is above income support level because they have part-time work and are likely to have higher work expenses than married couples, particularly child care expenses. As no allowance is made for those expenses in calculating their housing benefit, the lone parent premium would go some way towards to filling that gap. In practice, it should remain at least at the present level in real terms.

Amendment No. 151 deals with the deductions for non-dependants that are currently made under the JPEG 707 housing benefit income support schemes. Some Conservative Members did not know that people do not get 100 per cent, assistance with their rent and rates. We are therefore having to spell it out at length that people do not receive it, because last year the Government introduced a rule that everyone must pay a minimum 20 per cent, contribution to their rates. At most they get 80 per cent, assistance. Interestingly, deductions for non-dependants are not made under the mortgage interest tax relief scheme. People who are helped with their housing costs in the form of mortgage interest tax relief are not penalised, whereas others, who are dependent on housing benefit, are.

Deductions for non-dependants are made in the case of grown-up children who still live at home and elderly relatives who live in the claimant's household. Those deductions have been increased in real terms in recent years and have become a substantial burden on the claimant's family. Our amendment would remove them from the housing benefit scheme. From April 1989, a top rate of £9.15 a week from non-dependants will be levied in respect of rent and mortgage interest payments from income support and £3.35 in respect of rates.

The key issue is whether the deductions made from the parents' benefit, or the children's benefit in the case of an elderly relative, based on assumptions about the contributions made by those non-dependants, are fair and realistic. The Social Security Advisory Committee thinks that they are not. When the deductions were increased in April 1984 the SSAC commented:

"We think it is entirely right that non-dependants living in a beneficiary's household should be expected to make a contribution to housing costs. We are concerned, however, that these increases have gone far beyond what is fair and realistic and that there will be circumstances in which it is not reasonable to expect the non-dependant to pay a contribution of as much as £8.20."

The broad issue here is whether it is right that the Government should penalise parents, regardless of whether they receive contributions from their children.

Parents have no legal means of collecting such money from their sons, daughters or other relatives; it depends on goodwill and family spirit. We should not superimpose on that goodwill and family spirit a financial burden which will mean that people begin to regard their non-dependants as a burden on the family.

People will be pushed out of their homes. Earlier, the Government said that it was better for people to live at home than to look for hard-to-find and hard-to-pay-for accommodation.

In practice, the April 1989 increase in non-dependant deductions is about 12 per cent., which is much higher than the figure referred to when the Social Security Advisory Committee made its remark about the figure being unfair and unrealistic. The Government have tried to justify that figure by arguing that it represents the housing costs component of the retail price index. There is no logic in that, because it is the non-dependant's likely ability to pay which is at stake. That figure is just a poor disguise for even more cuts in housing benefit. It is a pound-pinching exercise at the margins of social security and will have adverse effects. 708 If deductions for non-dependants are to remain, a more relevant uprating index should reflect movements in the income of non-dependants. Non-dependants on low incomes should be able to claim housing benefit. However, we want to deal with the question of whether any deductions should be imposed.

Deductions can cause considerable hardship, either to the non-dependant in trying to meet them or to the claimant when housing benefit is not paid. The deductions can sour relationships and cause families to split up. I recommend Committee members to read a booklet published a few years ago called "Families rent apart." It is a study of young people's contributions to their parents' housing costs. I shall not take up the Committee's time by going through all the cases set out in that booklet, but they are not simply one-off cases, they are real problems. The booklet was based on research carried out by the Child Poverty Action Group and Youth Aid in 1984 when the deductions for non-dependants were lower in real terms than they are now.

One parent wrote:

"As my two boys are on a low wage. I could not possibly ask them for any more money. I am scared they may leave home and that's the last thing I want to happen."

Another parent would not ask for the £8.20 because

"this would cause friction and drive them away from home to become one of the numerous homeless persons."

A pensioner wrote:

"I was informed if my son was not at home I would have no rent to pay … I do think it is all wrong to have to pay for your sons and daughters to live at home after bringing them up. What do I do. make him get married?"

People should not be priced out of their homes.

Earlier, we discussed low pay and the Minister expressed incredulity at the figures that we mentioned. After a radio programme on wages councils, a woman came to me and said that her son had been offered such a low wage by a supermarket that she did not believe that that was the rate offered by the supermarket. She thought that he was simply conning her because he did not want to make the full contribution to the household costs.

If the Government assume that the non-dependant's contribution will be made at the high rate that they propose, they will cause real hardship to people on low incomes. It is also bad housing policy. If financial pressure forces young people to move out of the family home, it will encourage inefficient use of the housing stock. In Leeds, the pressure for two and three-bedroom houses is far greater than for one-bedroom houses.

People then move into one-bedroom flats and create an under-use of three and four-bedroom houses, so there is an escalation of pressure within the housing sector.

This provision is bad housing policy and represents a debt engineered by the Government. It will be difficult to administer and will simply add to the already increasing rent and rates arrears. Families will squabble over the rent and rates that they have to pay.

JPEG 709 For those reasons, and in the interests of a clearer simplified system—for which the Government often appeal—it would be sounder policy and elementary justice if non-dependant deductions were brought into line with the mortgage interest tax relief scheme and abolished. That has happened in the case of people who are helped with their housing costs through the mortgage interest tax relief scheme. I hope that the Government will not say that that concerns a different Department—the Treasury. I see a real distinction between those who get interest relief on their mortgages and the increased pressure that is being put on non-dependant contributions.

I hope that the Minister will accept the two amendments; they do not involve a major change in policy. I am sorry that I have taken up time by going through the arithmetic in detail; some Conservative Members of the Committee feel that we must be careful with our arithmetic. I urge the Minister to respond with care and to take some of the pressure off families who experience real difficulties in this area.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. Peter Lloyd)
: Amendment Number 151, which the hon. Member for Leeds, West (Mr. Battle) has just discussed, would increase the amount of the housing benefit lone parent premium by 60p a week.

We reviewed the value of the lone parent premium at the same time as the other income-related benefits, but decided not to increase it from this April. The lone parent premium was set at a higher level in housing benefit from income support last April.

The hon. Gentleman asked why we did not retain the intention originally expressed in the White Paper and disregard one parent benefit rather than give extra cash by means of the housing benefit lone parent premium. We increased the premium because we realised that many lone parents, such as widows, on housing benefit did not receive one parent benefit. We increased the premium to ensure that all lone parents would benefit.

In April 1988, when we introduced the new structure of income-related benefits, we took a deliberate decision to align as many of the rules as possible. This change was widely welcomed because it led to a simpler and more intelligible system and removed the worst effects of the poverty trap.

I doubt whether the hon. Gentleman would disagree with such an objective. That meant that lone parents are now assessed as single people—as they are in income support and were in supplementary benefit— not as couples, as they were in the old housing benefit scheme. We recognised, however, that this would give rise to a transitional problem, which is why we set the premium higher. We subsequently made transitional protection available as well.

710 We shall continue to review, as my right hon. Friend is statutorily required to do, the value of all the premiums. However, for the April 1989 uprating we took a deliberate decision to make a step further towards alignment. I believe that that is right because alignment is an important part of the new structure.

I should emphasise several points about that decision. First, it does not affect the poorest people. Those on income support will have seen an increase in their premium, and they will continue to receive maximum housing benefit— protecting them against rent rises if they are tenants. Secondly, those affected by the freeze will not have seen a drop in their income in real terms. Thirdly, and importantly, all lone parents on benefit will have gained significantly from the extra cash we put into the children's rates, not only in income support but also in family credit and housing benefit.

That was the right way to provide extra resources for families on lower incomes. Lone parents are also entitled to the higher earnings disregard. I am convinced that that was, and is, the right policy, and I urge my hon. Friends to resist the amendment tabled by the hon. Member for Leeds, West (Mr. Battle).— [Interruption.] He is not an hon. Friend, friendly though he is, because Opposition Members are Gentlemen— except, of course those who are Ladies. That reminds me of the strictures of the hon. Member for Derby, South (Mrs. Beckett) at our previous sitting about the need to give local authorities adequate notice of changes. In all probability—or in all certainty, if the Committee accepted the change—it would have be introduced retrospectively following Royal Assent—a costly and difficult exercise which I do not think local authorities would thank us for. There are ways of improving benefits for lone parents. We demonstrated one of those ways in the last uprating, and future improvements should be carried out along those lines rather than in the way that the hon. Gentleman suggests.

Amendment No. 152 would mean maximum housing benefit in all cases would be 100 per cent, of eligible rent and 80 per cent, of eligible rates. No regard would be had in any housing benefit assessment to contributions towards the household's expenses made by non-dependants—people such as grown-up sons and daughters and boarders living in the claimant's household. The hon. Member for Leeds, West will not be surprised to learn that the Government do not support the amendment.

We have always believed that where a claimant has another person living in the household, such as a grown-up son or daughter, it is righ to assume that he or she is contributing towards the household expenses. Within housing benefits, therefore, flat rate non-dependant deductions are made which vary according to the age and financial circumstances of the non-dependant.

The arrangements introduced last April constitute a fairer and simpler structure than the previous one. In his remarks, the hon. Gentleman did not recognise the changes that have been made. The local authorities should find it easier to administer and we have removed many of the anomalies of the old system. There is a higher and lower rate of deduction for rent as JPEG 711 before—but the higher is applied to those in full-time work. Previously it was applied to almost everyone not on supplementary benefit. I think that that was what the hon. Gentleman was thinking of. That removed the disincentive to take a part-time job, or to go on a training course.

To protect the position of low-paid earners we introduced for the first time an income threshold. Where the non-dependant is in low paid work—earning below £49.20—the claimant is subject to the lower rate of rent deduction. The higher rate of deduction for rents applies only in respect of boarders who are paying a charge fixed by the claimant for services provided and in respect of people earning more than the threshold figure. Where the non-dependant is a 16 or 17-year-old or on a YTS course, no deduction is made, no matter what the young person's income or employment status; and no deduction is made where the claimant or his partner is registered blind or receiving attendance allowance.

When community charge is introduced, the non-dependants' deductions for rates will be obsolete because everyone over 18 will be liable for making their own contribution towards local services.

I agree with the hon. Member for Leeds, West that it is not right to price children out of their parents' home, but children should make a contribution. I also agree with him that an £8.20 deduction for those on low pay is too much. That is why we have set for those on low pay receiving under £49.20, a figure of £3.45 for rent and £3 for rates. It is wholly reasonable that such deductions should, be made, and with an income of at least £49.20 I should think that most children would want, in return for staying in their parents' home, to contribute rather more than the deductions. Rather than causing the parents to drive the children out of the home, it is in the interests of both to stay there—financially, as well as emotionally and for mutual support.
