Amendment 99, which is the most ambitious of the amendments, seeks to put no limit of any kind on the period during which an unemployed person can continue, without any effect on his benefit title, to search for a job in the occupation that he has been used to and at a wage that he has been accustomed to. Mr. Scott

The existing legislation allows benefit claimants a "reasonable interval" in which to look for their own type of work, but thereafter they are expected to look beyond those horizons. The guidance given under existing law to adjudication officers is that about three months on benefit is the longest period which it is reasonable to allow without expecting the claimant to recognise the need to extend his job search. In the immediate aftermath of losing his job, for whatever reason, it seems reasonable to enable him to restrict his horizons in terms of both salary and type of job to that to which he has been accustomed. That is becoming particularly true in a society in which jobs come and go and in which the economy is growing and speeding up all the time.

JPEG 505 In the regulations that will result from the clause, we propose to establish a position very similar to that which exists already under case law. In the present situation where old types of work are disappearing and new ones are coming on stream, it would be unfair to claimants who are prepared to be flexible and look for new opportunities, to give others an unlimited time to insist on their former type of work and level of pay.

Amendments Nos. 68 and 105 are not so wide-ranging or ambitious as amendment No. 99. Amendment No. 68 would, however, mean that there was no power to set out in regulations the longest "permitted period". Amendment No. 105 would mean that everyone, no matter what his experience or the state of the labour market, could draw benefit for at least six months if he could not find work in his usual occupation at his usual pay.

A I have said, when we design the regulations, our intention is to fix the maximum length of the "permitted period" at 13 weeks, and in effect establish in regulations what tends to happen in practice now. Briefly, the effect of current legislation is that whereas at first an unemployed person will not be disqualified for receiving unemployment benefit if he refuses to accept work other than employment in his usual occupation at the rate of pay that he is used to, after the lapse of a "reasonable interval", employment will not be unsuitable for him simply because it is not in his usual occupation. There are other conditions attached, but the basic message is that after that "reasonable interval" an unemployed person is expected to broaden his horizons and that will be made clear in regulations under the clause.

The measures are designed not to penalise the unemployed but to encourage them to be more realistic about their labour market potential. In those circumstances I think that we are right and the amendments are wrong because, as I said, in their very different ways they would take a much more relaxed attitude towards encouraging and persuading people to seek other job opportunities at levels of pay that they have not been used to, on which to support themselves and their families and provide for their old age.
