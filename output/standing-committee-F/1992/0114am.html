Clause 20
RIGHT TO MATERNITY LEAVE AND RIGHT TO RETURN TO WORK

Ms. Quin
: I beg to move amendment No. 106, in page 38, line 2, at end insert—

"(2) Subject to subsections (3) and (4), where an employee has been continuously employed for two years by the beginning of the eleventh week before the expected date of childbirth, her maternity leave period shall continue from its commencement for the period of 40 weeks or such earlier date as the employee notifies to the employer in writing.".

The amendment needs to be considered in the overall context of the amendments tabled by the Opposition. The amendment is part of a comprehensive approach to the issue of maternity provision for working women. When allied to the other amendments, amendment No. 106 represents an important step towards a more generous and more easily understandable system than the current one, or the one likely to emerge from the Bill if the Government do not make further changes.

The two main motivations behind the amendment are, first, to reduce the complexity of the current system and, secondly, to ensure that whatever new provisions are introduced by the Bill, there is no reduction in the existing maternity rights enjoyed by some employees. I hope that the Government share those motivations. At our Tuesday 380 sitting, the Minister spoke about the ways in which certain contractual rights could override some of the Bill's provisions. As I have only just had time to examine the report of the proceedings of that sitting, I am not sure exactly what he said, but I hope that he covered some of the concerns that we have expressed both in this amendment and those that follow it.

About 60 per cent of women enjoy what is known as a "right to return" for up to 29 weeks after confinement. Those women meet the qualifying conditions of two years full-time or five years part-time services. Taken with other amendments, amendment No. 106 would replace the so-called right to return by two definitions of maternity leave. Ideally, one would be of 18 weeks—although the Government still seem to want to stick to their 14 weeks—for women with less than two years service. The other would be a right to return of 40 weeks for women with two years full-time and five years part-time service. Our other amendments have to be considered in line with that general approach.

We believe that the force of the amendment and others that we have tabled would simplify the requirements of schedule 2 to the Bill. Were our amendment to be accepted in total, schedule 2 would be rendered unnecessary. The Minister should take that into account. We have been convinced by the arguments of the Maternity Alliance, the Equal Opportunities Commission and others that the current system is over-complicated. The Maternity Alliance has stated that

"The attempt to fuse the minimum provisions in the EC directive with the current maternity schemes has been ill-thought out and will produce an excessively complicated system, especially if the provisions for maternity pay are taken into account."

It is true, as again the Maternity Alliance and other organisations have pointed out, that even industrial tribunals have criticised the complexity of the legislation. One judgment stated that

"These statutory provisions are of inordinate complexity exceeding the worst excesses of a taxing statute; we find that especially regrettable bearing in mind that they are regulating the everyday rights of ordinary employers and employees. We feel no confidence that, even with the assistance of detailed arguments from skilled advocates, we have now correctly understood them: it is difficult to see how an ordinary employer or employee is expected to do so."

The problem of the complexity of the legislation was referred to by my hon. Friends during Tuesday's debate. That argument is particularly valid in connection with this amendment, which would simplify the existing arrangements. It would provide a satisfactory translation of the European directive.

We suspect that everyone welcomes the extension of maternity leave to all women, though sometimes we doubt that when we hear comments made by Conservative Members. I hope that no one wants to increase the complexity of the system. All working women would get the 14 weeks maternity leave, regardless of length of service or hours worked, and their contractual rights would be maintained for those 14 weeks. Those women who have qualified for the existing right of maternity leave of up to 11 weeks before the birth and 29 weeks after the birth—about 60 per cent of pregnant women—would maintain contractual rights only for the first 14 weeks of absence. After that, contractual rights would be suspended and a woman would have to negotiate contractual terms with her employer.

JPEG 381 The problem is that some employers allow women to retain their contractual rights during the whole of their absence from work and others do not.

On Tuesday, I discussed with the Under-Secretary of State how far contractual rights would override the existing provisions and how many women would benefit. What proportion of working women are covered by contractual rights which would, in effect, be better than the Bill's provisions? The Minister implied that many women would be covered. Our understanding, from studies such as the McRae study on maternity benefits which was published in 1990 and is, therefore, fairly up-to-date, is that only 14 per cent of women would be covered.

I cannot imagine that the situation would be dramatically different from contractual rights relating to other rights that an employee might enjoy. Will the Minister respond to that point, because it concerns us? The Bill's provisions, might mean that a woman's contractual rights would operate for only 14 weeks and that, for example, she might have to give up her company car half way through maternity leave. I cannot believe that that is the Government's intention.

Another problem is that of the different notification requirements for those who qualify for 14 weeks maternity leave and for those who qualify for the right to return for up to 29 weeks after the birth. In both circumstances, a woman must inform her employer that she is pregnant at least 21 days before leaving work. However, the return date arrangements are different. If she qualifies for 14 weeks leave, she is not required to give notice of her return date, whereas those who qualify for up to 40 weeks leave are required to state that they intend to return and to give notice of the date of their return at least 21 days in advance.

One way to simplify that complicated situation would be to harmonise the rules applying to women eligible for 14 or 18 weeks maternity leave with those that apply to the right to return to work. The amendment would abolish the right to return and replace it with a right to maternity leave for all women, some of whom would be entitled to 14 weeks leave, others to 40 weeks. All contractual rights would continue for both periods of leave, a point that I hope the Minister will address in his reply. Identical notification procedures would be required in both cases. The whole system would be easier to understand and administer, which would be advantageous to both employers and women.

I have already referred in detail to the view of the Maternity Alliance. Its view is endorsed by other organisations, such as the National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux and the Equal Opportunities Commission. As the Minister knows, those organisations receive a great number of inquiries about the complexities of the current rules. I do not know how many meetings the Minister has had with those organisations specifically to discuss those complexities, but it would help if the Minister could tell the Committee what discussions he has had with those organisations in an attempt to simplify and improve the rules.

I should also be grateful if the Minister could give the Committee some information about the number of women who enjoy contractual rights so that we can understand the implications of the Bill on both women 382 who enjoy those rights and those who do not. We are especially worried about women who do not enjoy contractual rights, and upon whose terms and conditions of employment the Bill may have a detrimental effect.

Those are the main points that I wanted to make in relation to the amendment, but I should just like to quote from the Equal Opportunities Commission's formal response to the Bill, which says:

"One important area of opportunity offered by the Bill is that of ensuring consistency with other legislation and, so, ensuring greater simplicity in employment protection legislation for the benefit of employers and employees alike. Many of the Bill's provisions represent piecemeal additions to various existing provisions, so making employment protection legislation more complex."

I believe that many organisations, which are concerned about the welfare of working women, support simplification of the current legislation, as has been proposed. The Opposition share that opinion and I look forward to hearing the Minister's response.

Mr. Richard Burden
(Birmingham, Northfield)
: Opposition Members have tried to impress on the Government two principal areas in which maternity provision in this country is far less effective than it could be. One of those, which we discussed at length on Tuesday, is the level of maternity provision. I shall not labour that point today. The other question is the complexity of maternity provision.

When one considers the various aspects of maternity regulations, it is hardly surprising that many women employees and their employers are confused about their meaning and application. The specific issue of maternity pay is not what we are discussing in the amendment, but it is relevant when set against the backcloth of the complexity of the regulations.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Wallasey (Ms. Eagle) said, qualification requirements are rigorous—two years full-time, five years part-time—to ensure a complicated formulation of maternity pay of six weeks on 90 per cent of salary and a further 12 weeks on statutory 384 maternity pay. If a woman does not qualify under those requirements and is above the national insurance threshold, she can receive statutory maternity pay, but that has implications for the first set of women regarding their right to return. If a pregnant woman does not qualify under either of those requirements and she is below the national insurance threshold, she could receive money through the maternity allowance scheme from the Department of Social Security.

Our view is that the plethora of regulations is inadequate in terms of the level of support that is provided. There is no doubt that they are also highly complex and difficult to understand. That applies to pay as well as to the issue of the absence of the woman from work and to how that is described in the European directive on the rights of pregnant workers. The Bill contains the concept of maternity leave. That is a good concept.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Gateshead, East (Ms. Quin) said, the maternity leave issue does not arise under the existing provisions. The question relates to the right to return. That is another complicated issue. Under the present law, there are rigorous qualification requirements governing the right to return. It is hardly surprising that there are so many problems with the application of the legislation. Those problems are likely to be aggravated, not improved, by some of the examples of employers' attitudes that Opposition members of the Committee gave at Tuesday's sitting We heard of irresponsible employers, who did not allow women to attend antenatal classes, who did not want pregnant women to work beyond the 11th week before the expected birth, or did not want women to come back to work after the birth, even when, under existing legislation, they had a right to return. A minority of Conservative members of the Committee on Tuesday expressed the view that the act of employing women was risky, because they may go off and get pregnant.

If we accept that such attitudes exist among certain irresponsible employers and then stir in highly complex regulations, under which all kinds of different documents have to be referred to and a detailed understanding of the law is required to be able to make sense of them, it is hardly surprising that there are problems with implementing the maternity scheme.

The irony of the Bill is that while it improves maternity leave provision, the danger is that, as currently worded, it could add yet another layer of complication to the application of the right to be absent from work because of maternity. That complication is unnecessary. The current provisions could be unified quite easily with the new ones to be introduced by the Bill and lead to an overall provision that is not only more reasonable but easier to understand.

On the issue of contractual rights, which has been mentioned by my hon. Friend the Member for Gateshead, East, both the Bill and the EC directive provide that, for 14 weeks, a woman will have the right to maintain and accrue contractual rights—a measure that I welcome. The Committee accepts the logic of that provision. It seems crazy, therefore, not to accept that logic beyond 14 weeks. The employment implications are that it could lead to women, and employees in general, resenting what appears to be an illogical rule. It could also add to the administrative problems faced by the employer, who JPEG 385 would have to find out about an employee's entitlement at a particular time. Responsible employers might face fewer problems because they may have a well-staffed, well-financed and helpful personnel department, which will help them find a way through the morass of regulations. They may have personnel policies which acknowledge the silliness of allowing rights to accrue for 14 weeks and suddenly stopping them after 14 weeks.

However, irresponsible employers, or those who do not have a well-staffed personnel department, will have many problems with the legislation as currently worded. They might be well meaning but, because they do not have resources to interpret complicated regulations, they will have difficulty applying them as reasonably as they might wish to do. Others may be unsympathetic to the principle of maternity leave and, therefore, might try to undermine rights, whenever possible.

Ridiculous situations could arise. For example, a woman executive on maternity leave would be able to retain her right to a company car for the first 14 weeks but would have to return it after that. That makes no sense. The accrual of rights for the first 14 weeks, which both sides of the Committee agree with, means that the employer would have to make alternative arrangements. The employer would not gain by suddenly chopping the accrual of rights after 14 weeks. It makes sense, for both the employer and the woman, that the provisions are unified to ensure that contractual rights accrue throughout the period of leave.
