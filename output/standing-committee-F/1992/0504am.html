However, I hope that the hon. Member for Hendon, South (Mr. Marshall) is not challenging the high quality and variety of British potatoes. By any objective measurement, our potato industry is a success story.

Certain aspects of the potato crop make it harder to manage than other commodities. Management regimes are in place for most products. We have a hugely costly EC milk regime and a cereals regime. There is support buying and removal from the market of a range of horticultural products. We also have a sugar regime. Those regimes are hugely costly to the British and European taxpayer.

The crucial characteristic of the potato crop is the extent to which the yield fluctuates from year to year according to climate. The tonnage of potatoes that will be produced in this country next year cannot be reasonably predicted.

In addition, there is a relatively inelastic demand for potatoes. If there were a glut of potatoes, and the price was at rock bottom, it would not result in increased potato sales. It is hard to say this, but most consumers buying potatoes in, for example, the supermarket are not much influenced by whether there is 2p or 3p on the pound. There can be huge fluctuations in supply from the fixed acreage that is planted each year and minimal fluctuation in the demand for potatoes. There is potential for tremendous instability in the price and management of the market.

It is only reasonable to try to manage the market. The market is managed for other products. The Potato Marketing Board has been in existence for decades to manage the potato market, and it has done that relatively well. It tries to influence the number of potatoes planted each year. If there were no intervention in the market, there would be a danger that a disastrous year might be followed by a big cut in the acreage planted. Similarly, following a profitable year, there would be a danger of a big increase in the acreage planted.

As hon. Members are aware, a target acreage is agreed by the joint committee and implemented under the quota system. It is important to recognise that the effect of the quota system is not to hold down the hectares of potatoes planted. That is not its purpose and that is not what it does. The quota system attempts to stabilise the hectarage planted at a figure that is decided not by producers but by the joint committee, on which retailers, processors and consumers are represented.

Without a quota system, in a disastrous potato year there would be a tendency for a producer to slash his planting.

That possibility is inhibited by the quota system, which would reduce his quota for ever; he would have cut his potato planting not only for the following year, but for subsequent years. It is false to suggest that the quota system operates only to hold down plantings. It exists to stabilise plantings at a level agreed by the joint committee.

One cannot just move in and out of the potato industry—at least, one should not. If we get rid of the Potato Marketing Board our standards might be driven down to those of other countries. The industry has high investment, high productivity and is of a high quality. Much planning JPEG 179 goes into the seed and there is a need for labour to be available at the appropriate time. It makes sense to retain the type of industry that we have.

It is not possible to intervene and take potatoes out of the market for a few years, as we can with cereals. The potato crop, unlike other commodities, cannot be stored indefinitely. Because of those essential characteristics of the potato crop, it is perfectly correct to manage the market. Producer co-operatives are managed in some countries on the continent. The Government's argument is that elsewhere the market is not managed and there is no agreed planting. Those co-operatives have area-agreed acreages to plant and decisions are taken not by a joint committee but by producers alone.

Our arrangements have worked well and the changes that the Government introduced in 1989, which take effect in 1990, were positive. It would be a scandal if, after the Potato Marketing Board faithfully carried out the Government's requirements, the Government were to contemplate seriously winding up the board, even if the EC had no compunction to do so.

The industry and members of the Committee know that the underlying reason is that the Government are not like the Conservative Government of the 1950s. This Government are hardline, right wing and doctrinaire and they reveal their policies by how they apply them to the management of our agricultural system.
