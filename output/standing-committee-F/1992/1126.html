Mr. Sam Galbraith (Strathkelvin and Bearsden): I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 2, line 18, leave out 'suspects' and insert

'provides reasonable grounds for suspecting'.

The Chairman: With this, we may take amendment No. 3, in page 2, leave out lines 20 and 21 and insert—

'(b) that the scrutineer considers that the suspicion of the member or candidate is founded on reasonable grounds.'.

The amendments do not attack in any way the principle behind the clause. Clause 1 marries sections 24 and 49 of the Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992. Section 24 deals with the register, its setting up and some of the statutory duties imposed on it. Section 49 deals with the scrutineer and pulls together the relationship and the statutory rights and duties involved in that relationship.

We do not want to challenge that principle. That is not the purpose of the amendments. We think, however, that the balance in the clause is not correct. We aim to redress that imbalance by the amendments.

All trade union legislation that has been developed over the past decade has imposed increasingly strict controls on trade unions. We fear that, as regards individuals, those controls and duties have not been properly balanced in the clause. Although it is right that individuals should have been given increased rights, they are not, at the same time, having duties imposed upon them.

The new subsection (3A), with which our first amendment deals, states that the scrutineer must inspect the register if he is approached by a candidate or a trade union member who suspects that the register is not accurate and up-to-date. Our contention is that suspicion alone is too wide a context, given the increasing duties that have been placed on trade unions. That contention is enhanced by the fact that the person who raises the suspicion is shrouded in anonymity. I do not object to that; it is correct that a person should be able to raise a suspicion without fear of recrimination, doubt, blacklisting or intimidation. It is right that that person's anonymity should be protected in the Bill. However, the rights given to the individual mean that he must bear some of the responsibility. That is why we consider that suspicion alone is not a sufficient reason for the register to be inspected. Paragraph (b) of the new subsection (3A) states as a condition

"that the scrutineer does not consider that the suspicion of the member or candidate is ill-founded."

That is not enough. What is "ill-founded"? How easy is it to deal with? What does it mean? What is its balance and strength in law?

The problem is highlighted and compounded by the fact that a member of a trade union already has recourse to action, if he considers that there is a problem with the register. Section 25(1) of the 1992 Act states that

"A member of a trade union who claims that the union has failed to comply with any of the requirements of section 24…may apply to the Certification Officer for a declaration to that effect."

Section 24(1) sets up the statutory duty for the register and ensures that its entries are accurate and kept up-to-date. The Bill correctly imposes further duties on a trade union, but too wide a right is given to an individual without imposing any duties upon him.

Col. 6 I am not sure what "ill-founded" means in law, but I am not a lawyer. If a member of a trade union told the scrutineer that he thought that the record was wrong, the scrutineer could prove that the suspicion was ill-founded only if the member had claimed that the record was wrong because there were several members with two heads. Short of that, how can the scrutineer reject any complaint from any individual? If, an individual approaches the scrutineer and says, "I suspect that the register is incorrect", is the scrutineer obliged to examine the register or to ask the trade union to do so? How can the scrutineer reject a claim as ill-founded? I suggest to the Minister that, short of my ridiculous example, rejection is impossible.

It would be far better to impose a duty on the scrutineer. Amendment No. 3 does that. It is an important principle of trade union legislation that the individual has rights. We accept that an individual is always at a disadvantage against big business, big battalions or big unions. Therefore, we should like the onus to be put on the scrutineer to consider that the suspicion of the member or candidate is founded on reasonable grounds. In that way, any raising of a vexatious or silly suspicion will not set in motion a big investigation, unnecessary procedures and huge cost. That is the substance of our amendments.

The principle of the clause is correct, the principle behind having a register is correct and the principle of giving rights to individuals is correct, but certain duties must go with those rights. Therefore we want to ensure that there are at least reasonable grounds for that suspicion having been raised.
