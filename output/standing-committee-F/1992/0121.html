It was recognised that Governments and Community institutions would have social obligations to the people of Europe and to work forces. Against that background, on the basis of the treaty, a directive was issued, as long ago as 1979, to protect the rights and entitlements of employees whose jobs were transferred from one organisation to another. The basis of that directive was that employees were not pawns or chattels but people with rights, whose pay, conditions and collective agreements should be sustained and honoured by their new bosses.

Between 1979 and 1981, the Conservative Government attempted, as usual, to water down and/or evade the provisions of the directive. Eventually, they were forced to seem to comply with the directive, and they passed the Transfer of Undertakings (Protection of Employment) Regulations which were supposed to meet the terms of the directive. Clause 26 aims to amend those regulations.

Introducing the regulations, the Minister, who later became Lord Waddington, said:

The Attorney-General (Sir Nicholas Lyell)
: I am grateful for the Committee's invitation to explain the meaning and effect of clause 26, which introduces amendments to the Transfer of Undertakings (Protection of Employment) Regulations 1981.

It is always a pleasure to listen to the hon. Member for Holborn and St. Pancras (Mr. Dobson) and I am pleased to see that he is in no less good form than when I see him across the Dispatch Box in the Chamber. If I recognise a little hyperbole and over-enthusiasm, the hon. Gentleman is in familiar form, which we welcome.

JPEG 505 The hon. Gentleman asked several questions. From the way in which he phrased his last few questions, he seems to know that I am here to answer some of them, but not others. I am here in my capacity as a Law Officer to help and advise the Committee on the meaning and effect of clause 26.

The law on employment rights on the transfer of undertakings is sometimes considered to be more complex than it is. I suspect—my suspicions are confirmed by some of the hon. Gentleman's comments—that the clause and the amendments it makes are believed to have more effect in changing the law than is in fact the case, but I shall come to that aspect later. The interpretation of the law in this area depends to a great extent on case law, both in domestic tribunals and in the European Court of Justice. Those who examine the case law will know that it derives from cases which are by no means confined to the United Kingdom and that most of the leading cases come from other member states.

The interpretation of the key question of whether there has been a transfer of an undertaking has been well settled for some years. I shall refer later to the leading case of Spijkers in 1986 [1986] ECR 1119. It may be helpful if I first say something about the background.

The acquired rights directive was adopted by the Community under article 100 of the treaty of Rome in 1977. The transfer regulations were made in 1981 to implement the directive. Their scope was expressed to be limited to transfers of undertakings in the nature of a commercial venture. This was not intended to restrict the regulations to the private sector or to commercial undertakings in a narrow sense. Those words were included to reflect the view then taken on the scope of article 100 of the treaty. The European Commission has challenged those words as too restrictive and the Government have accepted that it is appropriate that they should be removed from the regulations. That change of wording is thus designed to render our regulations more clearly in conformity with Community law—I emphasise the words "more clearly in conformity" because that is one of the aspects that is required of European law, even though the law applies in substance, in any event.

Throughout the period since 1981, the regulations have, in fact, often been applied in the public sector in circumstances where the operation being transferred could be described as an undertaking, or part of an undertaking, which is in the nature of a commercial venture. It is important to note that the undertaking does not actually have to be a commercial venture, or to operate commercially, or to make a profit before transfer, although it will usually be the intention that it should do so after transfer. The Committee will recognise that it is now often considered, for example, that self-contained public sector operations are capable of being transferred to a private sector body as a going concern and run commercially.

It is not, therefore, the case that the amendment in subsection (2), which removes the restriction to undertakings

"in the nature of a commercial venture",

has the effect of extending the regulations to the public sector. They have always covered the public sector. The extending effect of the amendment is very much more limited. In practice, it extends the scope to undertakings 506 that are not run on commercial lines—as, for example, some charities.

Similarly, the amendment does not "extend" the regulations to contracting out or to competitive tendering. The regulations already apply to such activities if they involve a transfer of an undertaking—I pick my words carefully—or part of an undertaking. It is clear from the case law that under both the directive and the regulations this means the transfer of an economic entity which is capable of operating as a going concern and which retains its identity. The contracting out of a service is not a transfer of an undertaking unless it involves enough of the elements of the original operation, such as premises, staff, good will or customer base, to constitute the transfer of a going concern.

Those criteria, whether they apply to a commercial business providing products or to a service, are not affected by the amendment in subsection (2).

It would be helpful if I spell out the criteria for the benefit of the Committee by referring to one of the leading cases—the Spijkers case. Lawyers and others who examine the law will discover that what I am about to quote will be found in many cases. It is common for European Court judgments to pick up and reiterate principles by, very sensibly, using the same words.

If one is trying to decide whether there is a transfer for the purpose of the directive or the regulations, one can take guidance from the Spijkers case—case 24 1985 with judgment on 18 March 1986. I quote from the middle of paragraph 11:

"It follows that the decisive criterion for establishing whether there is a transfer for the purposes of the directive is whether the business in question retains its identity.


Consequently, a transfer of an undertaking, business or part of a business does not occur merely because its assets are disposed of."


"Indeed, it is necessary to consider, in a case such as the present, whether the business was disposed of as a going concern, as would be indicated, inter alia, by the fact that its operation was actually continued or resumed by the new employer, with the same or similar activities.


In order to determine whether those conditions are met, it is necessary to consider all the facts characterizing the transaction in question, including the type of undertaking or business, whether or not the business's tangible assets, such as buildings and movable property, are transferred, the value of its intangible assets at the time of the transfer, whether or not the majority of its employees are taken over by the new employer, whether or not its customers are transferred and the degree of similarity between the activities carried on before and after the transfer and the period, if any, for which those activities were suspended. It should be noted, however, that all those circumstances are merely single factors in the overall assessment which must be made and cannot therefore be considered in isolation."
