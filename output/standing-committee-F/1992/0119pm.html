Clause 25 was introduced in response to European Community criticisms of how the United Kingdom implemented one aspect of the EC equal treatment directive—one of the most important equal opportunity Col. 472 directives introduced by the EC. We have always been strongly in favour of that directive. The notes on clauses explain that clause 25

"provides individuals with a right to challenge in an industrial tribunal the validity of terms of (a) a collective agreement; (b) a rule made by an employer for application to some or all of his workforce, or to any person seeking to be employed by him; (c) or a rule made by an organisation, authority or body for application to its members or prospective members; which may be applied to them where the terms contravene the principle of equal treatment."

The notes on clauses also say that

"The challenge is to take the form of a request for a declaration that the term is void."

As the notes on clauses make clear, clause 25 was drawn up to meet a specific concern expressed by the European Commission over one aspect of United Kingdom remedies for a breach of the principle of equal treatment established in the equal treatment directive. The problem is that there is no clear mechanism to give individuals effective redress under the part of the equal treatment directive referred to in clause 25. It is difficult to avoid saying to the Government, somewhat gleefully, "We told you so." That defect in the United Kingdom's implementation of the directive was clear six and a half years ago, on the introduction of the Sex Discrimination Act 1986.

Our amendments are necessary because of the extent of existing discriminatory terms in collective agreements. The TUC has published a helpful guide for its members and member unions entitled "Equality in Action—a TUC Guide to Removing Sex Bias from Collective Agreements". "Direct sex discrimination takes place when a woman is treated less favourably than a man simply because of her sex."

It cites the example of a policy

"not to employ women with children which it not applied to men with children; or refusing to interview any women for heavy work on the assumption that all women are incapable of doing the job."

It describes indirect sex discrimination as when

"an unjustifiable requirement is applied equally to both sexes but has a disproportionately adverse impact on one sex."

The TUC cites the examples of

"applying an unjustifiable age barrier; promoting according to seniority; or making job applicants lift a heavy weight as part of the testing procedure, when this is not necessary for the job—fewer women than men would be able to meet all those requirements".

Action must be taken whereby direct and indirect discrimination in collective agreements can be properly raised, not only by individuals, but by groups of affected people.
