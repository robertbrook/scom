Mr. McLoughlin: New clause 8 will correct a small anomaly and goes a small way towards the proposals sought by new clause 11 that has been tabled by the hon. Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden (Mr. Galbraith). I shall explain why I consider that new clause 8 is more preferable than the hon. Gentleman's new clause.

New clause 8 will rectify an anomaly in compensation for employees who have been unfairly dismissed and whose employers fail to comply with industrial tribunal orders to reinstate or re-engage them. The anomaly means that in a small number of cases there may be a financial incentive for employers to disregard the order made by the tribunal.

When an industrial tribunal decides that an employee has been unfairly dismissed, it may order that the employee be reinstated, or re-engaged to do a similar job. In doing that, it will specify an amount payable by the employer in respect of benefits and arrears of pay that would normally have been due to the employee in the period between his dismissal and his reinstatement or re-engagement, had he not been dismissed. In addition, a re-engagement order will also specify the remuneration for the new job.

The amount payable by the employer is not subject to any limits and if the employee were on a high salary, it could be considerable.

If the employer re-employs the individual, but otherwise only partly complies with the order, the industrial tribunal may make an award of compensation having regard to the loss sustained by the complainant as a result of the failure to comply fully with the order. If the employer does not comply with an order, the tribunal may make a basic and compensatory award as in ordinary cases of unfair dismissal, plus an additional award, unless the employer can satisfy the tribunal that it was not practicable to comply with the order.

All the awards made in circumstances when the employer does not comply fully or at all with a tribunal order are subject to a monetary unit. As a result, in a few cases the employer could end up paying less than if he or she had fully complied with the original order.

That situation, although it arises infrequently, is clearly unsatisfactory, providing, as it does, an unintended incentive for employers to flout the authority of the industrial tribunals. As hon. Members who follow the law reports will recall, it has attracted criticism in the courts in the case of O'Laoire v. Jackel, where the then Master of the Rolls drew attention to it. The amendment rectifies the anomaly by giving the industrial tribunal new powers to exceed the normal compensation limits in such eases, so that the total compensation awarded, excluding the basic award, can equal the arrears of the pay element of the original award with which the employer has refused to comply.

The clause will go some of the way towards meeting the points that the hon. Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden also wants to be covered. It will deal with a minority of cases, and the Bill gives us the opportunity to implement such an important change. I am sure that it will be welcomed by the Committee.

Mr. Sam Galbraith (Strathkelvin and Bearsden): Opposition Members welcome the slight changes that the new clause will make. It does not, however, deal with the Image Col. 733 basic problem that would be met by new clause 11. The Minister said that new clause 8 will deal with a small anomaly that does not occur very often. The problem is that when an industrial tribunal has ordered reinstatement, no statutory onus is placed on the employer to comply with the order—the employer can ignore it. Many reasons can be given under the Act for not enforcing a section 69 order. If an employer wants rid of someone, he can get rid of them, attend an unfair dismissal hearing, accept that the dismissal was unfair, receive and order to reinstate which he does not follow, and then pay compensation. Some employers are quite prepared to do that.

The Minister said that the new clause addressed a "small anomaly". I do not know the figures, but it is not a small anomaly to lose a job unfairly and not to get it back. Financial compensation is no substitute for regaining a job. One case is that of Peter Hutton, who was a technician for the Royal Mail in Plymouth until he was sacked for insubordination in December 1991. The case came before an industrial appeal tribunal, which found that his manager had engineered the crisis that led to his dismissal and that he had been unfairly dismissed. Furthermore, it found that spurious allegations had been made against him. It ordered his re-engagement in a suitable job with the Post Office elsewhere in the south-west of England or south Wales. The tribunal found:

"He is very highly qualified … Almost anything in the motor trade would be within his capabilities and in the Post Office there is a considerable fleet of motor vehicles."

He was also awarded £6,700 for loss of eanings.

However, the Royal Mail said that it was "unable to comply"—unable to employ him—and he received a compensatory award of £12,000. That is barely a year's wages, and just not good enough. People who are unfairly dismissed need to be reinstated. I know that under section 69 there are various reasons for that not to occur, but that is taken into consideration by the industrial tribunal before it makes its decision. If it orders reinstatement, that order ought to be complied with.

One sanction regarding which we seek to ensure compliance is an increase in the amount of compensation. That is the purpose of our new clause, which would increase the relevant periods from 26 to 52 weeks and from 52 to 78 weeks. That financial constraint on the employer would make it more likely that he or she would accept the individual back. The problem with industrial tribunals—good as they are—is that they have no power to enforce reinstatement. That is what new clause 11 seeks to provide. " . After section 251 of the 1992 Act there shall be inserted the following section—

251A.—(1) ACAS may, in any case in which it thinks appropriate to do so, but subject to any directions under subsection (2) below, charge a fee for exercising a Col. 734 function in relation to any person.

  3. A direction under subsection (2) above shall specify whether fees are to be charged in respect of the exercise of any specified function—
    1. at the full economic cost level, or
    2. at a level less than the full economic cost but not less than a specified proportion or percentage of the full economic cost.
  4. Where a direction requires fees to be charged at the full economic cost level ACAS shall fix the fee for the case at an amount estimated to be sufficient to cover the administrative costs of ACAS of exercising the function including an appropriate sum in respect of general staff costs and overheads.
  5. Where a direction requires fees to be charged at a level less than the full economic cost ACAS shall fix the fee for the case at such amount, not being less than the proportion or percentage of the full economic cost specified under subsection (4)(b) above, as it thinks appropriate.
  6. No liability to pay a fee charged under this section shall arise on the part of any person, in a case where that person had made no request for the function to be exercised, unless ACAS has notified that person that a fee may or will be charged.
  7. For the purposes of this section—
    1. a function is exercised 'in relation to' a person who avails himself of the benefit of its exercise, whether or not he requested its exercise and whether the function is such as to be exercisable in relation to particular persons only or in relation to persons generally; and
    2. where a function is exercised in relation to two or more persons the fee chargeable for its exercise shall be apportioned among them as ACAS thinks appropriate.'.".—[Mr. Michael Forsyth.]

The Minister of State, Department of Employment (Mr. Michael Forsyth): I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

New clause 9 gives effect to the provisions of charging for the services of the Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service. With clause 32, it implements our reforms to the statutory framework of ACAS. Our legislative changes modify and update the existing framework set out in the 1992 Act, but do not overturn it. They will enable ACAS to continue to make an effective contribution to the future improvement of industrial relations.

The clause has two main elements. Subsection (1) will enable—I stress that word—ACAS to charge for its services. The Committee will recall that our amendments to clause 32 remove the prohibition against charging for advisory work, but without a power to charge and a clear framework for doing so there could still be obstacles and uncertainties. Subsection (1) remedies that deficiency by giving ACAS the express power to charge a fee

"In any case in which it thinks it appropriate to do so".

The second, at subsection (2), gives the Secretary of State for Employment a reserve power to require ACAS to charge for a specified service. Subsections (3) to (8) set Image Col. 735 out how that power would be exercised. The Secretary of State can require a fee to be charged at the full economic cost of the service in question, or at a specified proportion of the full economic cost.

The power to enable ACAS to charge allows it to offset the costs of certain services, if it so chooses. The power does not apply to conciliation in tribunal cases. ACAS has made it clear that it would be likely to take advantage of this provision to charge in certain cases—for some publications and events, for example. This year, ACAS will cost over £22 million. The taxpayer meets that cost and we would be failing in our duty to the taxpayer if we did not put in place mechanisms to allow at least some of those costs to be recovered from those who benefit from the service in question.

We have no current plans to use the reserve power to require ACAS to charge for a service, but in the light of the heavy demand for its services, continuing need to bear down on all aspects of public expenditure, and ACAS's independence from ministerial control, we believe that it is right to take such a power as a reserve measure.

The new clause reflects a pragmatic approach to the issue of charging. We recognise that charging can be a sensitive issue for ACAS and may not be appropriate for all its services. But it is clearly right for ACAS to charge in circumstances where it is sensible to do so. The new clause will give clear powers to enable this to happen and I commend it to the Committee.
