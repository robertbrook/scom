Mr. Paddy Tipping

: Before lunch, the Minister was expressing his enthusiasm for a free market in potatoes, which he believes is in the national interest. Some of us are sceptical that a free market in potatoes exists or that it will exist in future. The point that my hon. Friend the Member for Workington (Mr. Campbell-Savours) pursued with the Minister was the degree to which subsidies exist for potatoes in the European market. It is difficult to obtain coherent and accurate information on that. My hon. Friend referred to telephone calls to his relatives in Italy. It is clear that substantial subsidies exist in France to help potato growers. Social security payments are phased by Departments and priority loans are given. In a raft of measures 700 million francs are available for early growers, 40 million francs for maincrop growers at harvest and 20 million francs for maincrop growers at late harvest. The position is the same in Spain, Portugal and Italy.

In Italy there is substantial intervention in the market by way of subsidies. It is difficult to get to the bottom of the 282 matter, but it is clear that the Government intervention agency—the AIMA—subsidises growers, co-ops, producer organisations and processors. There is no free market, and some of us believe that even if a European lightweight regime could be introduced, the subsidies would continue. I am aware of the strength of the agricultural lobby in this country and abroad and it is common sense that producers will not give up those subsidies easily.

We were pressing the Minister to tell us what work he knows of in the Commission on the scale of subsidies in the European Community. That information would help to put the debate in context. Producers in this country do not want subsidies to exist in future. They want stability in the market because of the need for high investment and improvements in efficiency. I believe that rather than having subsidies—concealed subsidies in some cases—the best way forward would be to have a quota system.

The potato marketing scheme in the past 30 years has been a participatory arrangement whereby producers, processors, consumers and other interests have come together to plan for the future. Many of us accept the need to plan for the future and want stability in the market, and we feel that the desire to get rid of the potato marketing scheme now is premature and a leap in the dark.

The guaranteed price ensured that, if they did not make a profit, the producers at least had a reasonable amount of income. They still lost money: I am told that there was a shortfall of about 30 per cent. between what they received from the guaranteed price and the actual cost of production. However, the guaranteed price at least provided a base income for the producers.

We must not run away with the idea that the guaranteed price system is a form of subsidy. The Committee is aware that in the main it is a levy demanded from producers. A small proportion currently comes from the Government, but that is to be withdrawn. The scheme is self-supporting, and the Minister has offered no truly cogent arguments to explain why the potato marketing scheme and the guaranteed price system should be wound up.
