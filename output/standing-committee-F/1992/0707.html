Mr. Rowlands
: In previous sittings, as the Committee will be aware, we have drawn attention to the spiralling costs of the scheme. The amendment and new clause deal with what we consider to be the accompanying fiscal incontinence of the operational costs. I give notice to the Minister, in the gentlemanly spirit in which the Committee has conducted its business, that we hope that he will be able in this debate to provide factual information about operational costs because this is the last time that the Committee will be able to debate this matter.

In earlier debates, we have discovered the capital costs through cost revelation by correspondence—meetings and debates have been followed by correspondence with the Minister. I hope that he will be able to provide the true operational costs—he should surely have anticipated that demand from the amendments and the nature and tenor of my speeches.

The operational cost that we have been using is based on two pieces of information. First, appendix Al-3 table 3 in the January 1990 economic appraisal, which gave the operational costs—revenue in one column, costs in the other—in 1989–90 prices. I hope that the Minister will tell us how those costs have been revised. Secondly, the money resolution and Mr. Nicholas Bennett's explanation of it in the Official Report of 25 November 1991. c. 729. We need the Minister to give us some information before we can decide how far we should press the amendment and new clause.

I remind the Committee of the fiscal incontinence associated with the operational costs. The estimate for the operational deficit in the original private Bill was £400.000. By the time of the economic appraisal in January 1990. that figure had risen to £1.35 million—a trebling of the original estimate. Finally, in the money 146 resolution debate in November 1991, Mr. Nicholas Bennett admitted what, indeed, was in the financial memorandum of the public Bill—that the operational deficit of the scheme would be £1.6 million. It is important to find out how the costs will be covered and whether they can be offset, particularly along the lines proposed in the amendments.

Clause 13 gives the development corporation power to impose charges, which are to be the substantial offsetting costs of the annual running of the scheme. I have found only one assessment, but my homework and my, usually diligent, burrowing into such matters may have been deficient. The only estimate that I have seen of the amount that would be collected from the charges envisaged in clause 13 is in the appendix to the economic appraisal, to which I referred earlier.

The appraisal was made in January 1990 on mid-1989 to 1990 prices. We were told, for example, that lock usage fees would collect £145,000, marina berths £170,000, mooring fees £75,000, boatyard rental £10,000 and car parking £40,000. An intriguing thing called floating entertainments was to collect £30,000, and other miscellaneous revenue—I know not what—would come to £10,000. That came to a total of £480,000.

In other words, it was envisaged in January 1990, using 1989–90 prices, that the charges raised under clause 13, with certain additions, would collect about £480,000. The problem is that the same appendix gives, not the capital costs, but the annual operating costs of the barrage, as £1.8 million. That includes £190,000 to be spent on water quality, £260.000 on operation and maintenance of equipment, £250,000 on sewer diversion pumping, £115,000 on harbour management, £510,000 on dredging and £95,000 on capital replacement. There is a gap in the figures in respect of replenishment water, because that figure was prepared only with respect to the mini-barrage. One query with which the Minister should certainly deal relates to the sum of £85,000 for the Wentlooge lagoon. That has been dropped from the Bill, so we do not know what figure should be included for a possible future scheme for alternative feeding grounds.

The figures continue with £65,000 for groundwater monitoring, £75,000 on water quality monitoring and £70,000 for fish monitoring—that, I hope, means fish passes to make it possible for salmon and sewin to come up and down the River Taff. Finally, nothing is more indicative of the utter sloppiness of the financial assessments of the scheme from the beginning than the entry which as if the writers had run out of ideas for expenditure headings, gives other miscellaneous costs of£115,000. I should like to know before the end of the debate what those other miscellaneous costs cover. The operating costs are estimated in the appendix with great precision, up to the point where the other miscellaneous costs are thrown in. What portmanteau of costs or activities is included in the figure of £115,000 a year?

The sum total is what Mr. Micawber would call unhappiness. in that by deducting the costs from the revenue one ends up broke—with net operating costs of £1.835 million at January 1990 prices. How is the deficit to be covered?

I and. I think, other hon. Members raised these matters during the money resolution debate—the most appropriate occasion for raising them—when we heard JPEG 147 the latest account of the operational deficit. Mr. Nicholas Bennett, said at column 729 that the net deficit would not be £1.35 million, as was predicted in January 1990, but £1.6 million. I assume that that figure included the £85,000 for Wentlooge, so perhaps that sum should be deducted from the total now. Mr. Bennett told the House not how the deficit would be covered, but how it might be covered, he said:

"we are looking to the corporation to carry out a number of activities that will help to recoup part of that sum. The net operating costs of the inland lake will be met by the development corporation during its lifetime, as will maintenance and capital replacement costs."—

The costs of the deficit are to be met by the development corporation. We would like to know how. In particular, will the Minister tell us simply and directly whether he can give us a categoric assurance that any contribution by the Welsh Office to the annual budget of the development corporation will not include a penny to cover the operational deficit? The development corporation already receives a significant annual grant from the Welsh Office to cover the variety of costs associated with the barrage, and will continue to receive that grant year after year. Can the Minister assure us that the operational deficit costs will be met entirely by the development corporation's own revenues and commercial activity? That is what I inferred from Mr. Bennett's reply to the House—to me in particular—in the debate on 25 November 1991 when he said:

"the corporation is considering several options to provide revenue to cover the operating costs on a longer-term basis."—[ Official Report. 25 November 1991. Vol. 199. c. 729.]

From that statement, we can assume that nobody but the development corporation will cover the operating cost deficit which, in November 1991, was identified as £1.6 million.

I want to tie down in statutory form further obligations on the development corporation to finance and cover the deficit. That is an appropriate step to take, given the history and rising costs of the scheme.

Amendment. No. 133 states:

"The Development Corporation may impose charges on nonresidential properties in the bay area, with a view to recovering the operating costs of the barrage;"

I have been moderate in my demands and have not said "will impose" but "may impose". Given the escalating costs of the scheme, there is a powerful case for such a provision.

New clause 4 proposes that

"The Development Corporation shall establish a Trust Fund into which assets and capital growth income may be transferred, and which shall provide an income to offset the operating costs of the barrage."

These arc practical suggestions for covering the operational deficit of the scheme which has grown since the original private Bill.

10.45 am

I shall argue the merits of both devices and explain why I am sure that the Minister will have to agree with me. We are told that the purpose of the barrage is to enhance the value of the area and capital property values and that if we do not have a barrage or have only a mini-barrage there will be a decline in property values. 148 We are told that the barrage will result in betterment—I think that is the term—of the properties.

If the main beneficiaries of this public scheme are to be major commercial developers because the capital and rental value of their property will rocket—I have profound doubts about that—and if the barrage costs the development corporation an operational deficit of about £1.6 million at current prices, those nonresidential property owners should bear some of that cost.

In new clause 4 we suggest that assets be hypothecated to cover the operational costs of the barrage—an eminently sensible proposal. If the barrage results in the general betterment of capital values and a rise in rental charges, the development corporation will benefit as much as the private commercial developer, but a part of the cost should be earmarked or hypothecated to cover the operational deficit.

Both my suggestions are moderate, but they come from the best possible source. I cannot claim their parentage, however much I should like to. I should be happy to say that they came from Rowlands' imaginative Merthyr mind. Sadly, they came out of Bennett and Pembroke, which should at least attract the full-hearted support of Conservative Members, although I may lose the support of one or two of my hon. Friends. They are the full-blown ideas of the former Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Wales.

Mr. Bennett explained how the operational deficit might be covered:

"they include, first, levelling charges on residential and nonresidential property in the bay area".—[ Official Report, 25 November 1991; Vol. 199, c. 729]

I have resisted the temptation to support charges on residential property even though we are told that the capital value of the residential properties will be enhanced because they look out over lagoons and not terrible mud flats. I have resisted that temptation because I am a moderate man; Mr. Bennett was a bit of an extremist.

I have borrowed his suggestion—also made on 25 November—that there should be charges on nonresidential property to help to cover the operational costs of the bay. Amendment No. 133 comes straight from the Welsh Office mouth, so I hope that Conservative Members will support it.

Mr. Bennett went on to refer to other means by which to cover the operating costs, and said:

"secondly, placing property owned by the corporation into a managed portfolio designed to provide an annual lease or rent income; thirdly, the transfer of a cash endowment from the corporation into a managed fund designed to provide the necessary annual income and capital growth; and lastly, the possibility of creating a trust fund into which assets and capital growth income can be transferred from the corporation and which will provide annual income. That last option is currently being discussed with the corporation."—[ Official Report, 25 November 1991; Vol. 199, c. 729–730.]

The basis of new clause 4 is founded on an option that was brought to our attention during the money resolution debate, and one that was being discussed with the corporation by the Welsh Office in November 1991.

Not only are we entitled to be worried about the escalating capital and operational costs of the scheme, but it is beholden on us to ensure that those costs are JPEG 149 covered in a statutory fashion along the lines set out in the new clause. The promise of further options and discussions of the sort described in the money resolution debate is not sufficient to assure members of the Committee that we shall not be signing, alongside the open capital cheque, an open cheque for deficits, too.

I wish to establish a statutory basis on which to offset an operating deficit that started at £400,000, but which has now risen to about £1.6 million. Unless we well and truly establish a proper basis on which to ensure that the costs are covered in a manner that does not draw from public funds, we would not be fulfilling our duty as scrutineers of expenditure in line with the tradition on which parliamentary time is based.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Gwilym Jones)
: I shall deal first with the amendment and the new clause and then the questions posed by the hon. Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney (Mr. Rowlands). Amendment No. 133 refers to the recovery of the costs of operating the barrage. My immediate response is that the Government will want the development corporation to take the opportunity, when it can to offset and recover those operating costs. Under the Bill, power is given to the corporation to levy charges on boats using the locks or mooring in the bay. In turn, the corporation will be offsetting its costs in the agreements that it makes with developers about sites surrounding the bay. It will be seeking to make the best deals that it can and it does not need new legal powers or, indeed, any encouragement, to do so.

As for new clause 4, the corporation will have a limited life. When it has fulfilled its functions, it will be wound up and suitable arrangements will have to be made for the continuing exercise of its responsibilities. There are already legislative powers for its responsibilities to be transferred by agreement to a local authority. The corporation has considered setting up a fund. It would not need new legal powers to do so. However, it would be premature at this stage to require the setting up of a fund. My right hon. Friend the Secretary of State will want to take decisions on the disposal of the corporation's assets and liabilities at an appropriate time.

: I draw the Committee's attention to subsection (3) and the conundrum that all docklands regeneration schemes must confront: the introduction of a new community on to the old bedrock community.
