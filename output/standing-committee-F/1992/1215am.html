Mr. Dobson
: The amendments relate to clause 14, which requires all ballots on industrial action to be full postal ballots. They are probing amendments, which suggest alternatives. Whatever the merits of compulsory full postal ballots for union executive elections or for mergers, for example, the Opposition do not believe that the same arguments apply to ballots on industrial disputes. More of that anon.

The amendments apply to ballots to decide whether to go ahead with industrial action that involves relatively few people. One can argue for compulsory postal ballots in union elections, which involve a large membership at several sites between whom there might be inconsistency, but the majority of ballots for industrial action involve 50 or fewer workers. The case has not been made for a full postal ballot in the case of, for example, 43 workers 196 in a small engineering works. In a ballot involving fewer than 50 workers on one site, it would be more convenient, expeditious and fair to have a secret ballot at the workplace. That would probably also result in a better turnout. That is what has happened to date.

Most workers in most circumstances support the idea that the higher the turnout the better the election. Indeed, hon. Members who are elected to Parliament in a general election or by-election crow if the turnout is high. I am especially aware of that, because my constituency, which contains a great many students and nurses, has one of the lowest turnouts in Britain, although the turnout among those who reside permanently in my constituency is not low.

Mr. Dobson
: The amendments concern the principle of whether unions should have no legal alternative other than to conduct full postal ballots over industrial disputes.

The Government seem to be obsessed with postal ballots for trade unions, but they are not too obsessed with them at general elections. Full postal ballots may be justified for the election of senior union officers and union executives, or on the issue of trade union mergers, because those can involve vast numbers of people on various sites. A requirement of a fair election is that all electors are treated equally. It could be argued that there might be an element of inequality of treatment in such circumstances if there were not a full postal ballot.

It has to be recognised, however, that most full postal ballots have a lower turnout than other methods. To some extent, the level of turnout either endorses or undermines the authority of those who receive that authority from the ballot. I had the privilege of attending a conference in the autumn to celebrate 200 years of representative Government in Quebec, at which the former United Nations Secretary-General, looking south, questioned whether a country which did not manage a 50 per cent turnout in its presidential election could call itself a democracy. I do not want to raise that issue, and I do not entirely accept that view, but the basis of democracy is that the voters give the candidates authority by voting for them. The candidates do not have authority without those votes. If, at any level, the majority of the voters give a person or a decision authority, that authority is given, no matter what the turnout—unless there is a threshold. The Conservative party set a threshold in the referenda for Scotland and Wales in earlier Parliaments. In general, however, the greater the support, the greater the authority for that person or that issue.

Member for Derbyshire, West (Mr. McLoughlin), the turnout in my constituency and in other inner-city constituencies tends to be low because there are many voters who are not in the area all the time. For example, nurses or students who are on the electoral register might be far away by the time that there is an election. In those constituencies, the turnout of the permanent voters is as high as, or even higher than in most other constituencies.

We are debating not union elections or union mergers but strike ballots. Most strike ballots involve a small number of people. The vast majority of strike ballots carried out by the former Electrical, Electronic, Telecommunciations and Plumbing Union in the past few years involved fewer than 40 individuals. In larger ballots, the problem of turnout is one of the major arguments against using postal ballots. However, there are other arguments. In the Green Paper which preceded the Bill, the Conservatives acknowledged that a threshold of 50 was reasonable. The Green Paper which preceded the Employment Act 1988 concluded that

"postal balloting could be unsuitable for ballots on industrial action where the issue may be of immediate concern to a relatively small proportion of a union's membership and speed of decision making is essential."

In response to the most recent Green Paper the Institute of Personnel Management said:

"Workplace ballots on industrial action normally have a high participation rate."

The Association of British Chambers of Commerce said:

"a difficulty with postal ballots is the length of time they take in the context of strike action actually being called."

The Government's 1991 code of practice on industrial action ballots made it clear that secret workplace ballots are particularly suitable where

"speed of obtaining the voters' response is of the essence."

The Engineering Employers' Federation stated

"our experience is that provided they are properly conducted, secret workplace ballots are not subject to harassment and have additional beneficial features. EEFs experience is that workplace secret ballots have a significantly higher level of return than postal ballots. Postal ballots take at a minimum several days, often longer, whilst in many cases the momentum of negotiations will benefit from a quick decision as is available from workplace ballots.


The EEF believe, for the reason stated above, that properly conducted, secret workplace ballots can be the most effective form of balloting."

None of those organisations could be described as an adjunct of the Labour party; they are usually on the other side of the negotiating table from trade unions. Their day-to-day practical knowledge—not perhaps illuminated by the outpourings of the Adam Smith Institute—is that properly conducted workplace ballots work reasonably well. I have given a good range of views from people with experience.

The Conservative party's justification for postal ballots is fear of intimidation and breakdown of ballot secrecy, but there is little evidence of that. The Commissioner for the Rights of Trade Union Members—CROTUM—produced four annual reports which gave details of 959 inquiries, 137 formal applications for assistance and eight queries on strike ballots. Of those eight queries, assistance was refused in three cases due to lack of prima facie evidence of anything being wrong, the applicant withdrew in one case, the High Court rejected one case, two complaints fell because the industrial action complained 200 of did not take place, and one case succeeded in the High Court. Of the 1,000 complaints made to CROTUM in four years, only one on strike ballots was successful.

The Government have emphasised the presence of CROTUM and have said that many people in the trade unions are worried about this, that or the other. There has been no lack of publicity about the ability to go CROTUM to complain and to obtain support and help but despite all the publicity, only eight queries were submitted on strike ballots, one of which turned out to be right. Just one query has gone through the machinery laid down by the Conservatives for dealing with complaints.

On the basis of that query, the law is to be changed again. That seems to me to be an insubstantial evidential base, as lawyers might say, for proceeding with a substantial change in the law.

Many Conservative Members are concerned not with improper influence over ballots but with wholly proper influence over ballots. There is no reason why people who work with each other should not influence each other. Workers get together to strengthen their relationship to their employer. Generally the individual employee is weak in relation to the employer. People take collective action and adopt a collective approach because they face the power of a limited liability company, which is a collective organisation. Limited liability enables people to get together and risk only a little of their savings. The collective approach of the trade unions is a collective response to the collective power of the company.

Individuals are right to try to influence the votes of their colleagues in the workplace. It is clear from any study of electoral performance or response in this country that the views of people living in an area influence the voting behaviour of others in that area. My hon. Friend the Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden (Mr. Galbraith) is not present at the moment, but I am sure that he would agree that, because of the influence of the Labour party in Scotland, more middle-class people in Scotland vote Labour than in south-east England. People are affected by the general ambience in which they live and cast their votes accordingly. Perhaps the Minister of State has some other explanation for the Scottish example, apart from the eminent good sense of the Scots. In elections, people can be perfectly legitimately influenced in the way in which they vote.

The Conservatives are also motivated by their need to make industrial action more and more difficult to be carried out within the law. Few people want to have many strikes. Most people want to get on with their job. The loss to the economy, which results from strikes, can, however, be exaggerated. Since 1979, in the worst year of Conservative Government, 24 million working days were lost through strikes. In the worst year of the previous Labour Government, 29 million working days were lost in one year through strike action. This week, as a result of unemployment, 20 million working days will be lost through unemployment. That figure compares with 29 million days in one year, as a result of industrial disputes. Those figures need to be looked at in perspective.

In spirit, if not the letter, the proposals would seem to break International Labour Organisation conventions. As the Government do not seem to give a damn about the letter, I suppose that that does not matter, anyway.

Under International Labour Organisation conventions, free trade unions are supposed to have full JPEG 201 freedom to operate. That does not mean that compulsory postal ballots are contrary to ILO conventions, but for the Government knowingly to impose a method of balloting which knowingly results in a lower turnout could be interpreted as undermining the trade unions' full freedom to operate as free trade unions. We would argue that the Government are trying, by statute, to produce a lower turnout because they know that the odds are that there will be a lower turnout in most cases.

Books about how to organise free elections have shown, from time immemorial—or at least since people have had the vote, which is far from time immemorial and, usually, Tories have opposed people having the vote—that those who try to reduce the turnout have usually been trying to work some fiddle to their advantage. We are faced with gerrymander by statute. Governor Gerry did nothing unlawful; he carried out a lawful fiddle in the United States.

Provided that ballots are properly conducted, there is merit in holding them at the workplace. They provide for the expeditious conduct of industrial relations and produce a higher turnout. I think that I have demonstrated beyond any challenge from CROTUM's record that there is no evidence of widespread manipulation, or fiddling of ballots at the workplace.

The ballot at the workplace requires the use of the method by which we were all elected. Employees are given the ballot paper to which they are entitled, they mark that paper in private, they fold it up and place it in a ballot box, without anyone else knowing what they have done. That system was good enough to elect all of us, so to claim that it is unsatisfactory for an industrial action ballot is absurd.

People around the world have died—and would die—to be able to elect representatives as we are elected and to be able to vote as trade union members do in workplace ballots. There is nothing improper about a workplace ballot. In the past, there were a few workplace ballots that were not properly conducted, but these days they are properly conducted and are not invalidated by snide and and unsubstantiated attacks upon them. In the absence of a litany of proved cases of intimidation or lack of secrecy, the Government cannot justify the legislation. They cannot prove the need for postal ballots when all they can drum up is one case in four years from CROTUM and two or three snotty headlines in The Sun.

We do not believe that secret ballots need to be fully postal. We believe that the option of a postal ballot or of a partially-postal ballot should be available for members who are sick or unable to attend, or who do not have a normal workplace because they move around the country. Those circumstances are coped with. That practice should continue, but the requirement for a fully postal ballot is not sound when it comes to the conduct of industrial relations. It will lead to a lower and less representative turnout. That is why we do not support the Government's proposals.

From time to time, Ministers have said that, in matters of industrial relations, they are farsighted and claim to be able to see further ahead than those involved in the day-to-day practice of negotiations at the workplace.
