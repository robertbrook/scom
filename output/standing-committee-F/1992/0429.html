 JPEG 155 We wish to add

"having regard to the interests of producers and purchasers of milk".

 Surely that is needed.

There is much concern that over the next 12 months the milk marketing board may conduct its affairs in a way that is not helpful to processors and others who trade with it.

I return to a debate on amendment No. 85 at the beginning of our proceedings, when we dealt with these matters in the context of varying vesting days—the different vesting days of a reorganisation scheme introduced in England and Wales and Scotland.  There is set out an explanation by way of a simplified example of trade in milk for the pasteurised drinking milk market, which covers more than 50 per cent, of all United Kingdom milk. It shows the effect of distortions in the market and why it is so important to take fully into account the interests of processors prior to vesting day. The federation says:

"The present Schemes … require the Board to sell all milk for the drinking milk market at the same price irrespective of the nature or location of the buyers. The Scottish Board in particular is known to have aspirations to develop its market in the North and Midlands of England (it has already managed to establish a trading link with Lancashire Dairies in Manchester even though the price is no keener than that offered by the MMB in England). The English MMB would be unable to retaliate without reducing the price it receives from all of its milk sold for the liquid market."

 The amendment seeks to deal with the distortions that might occur under certain conditions. The federation adds:

"Clearly, so long as milk producers … are tied into the English MMB the Scottish Co-operative could only meet the requirements of any new trade in England by drawing milk out of lower value manufacturing outlets in Scotland to the detriment of these organisations. It should be noted here that this is already happening to the extent that milk is going to Lancashire Dairies, although the scale is small.

The longer term aspiration of the Scottish Co-operative would be to recruit additional farmers, most obviously in the North of England, to provide adequate milk for its increased market requirements, but there is little doubt that it would be prepared to exploit market advantage in the short term without having adequate milk supplies available to it because of the relative attractiveness of the liquid milk market."

 That is an example of how the market might be distorted. The amendment seeks to build into the considerations of Ministers who decide these matters a commitment to ensure that the interests of producers and purchasers of milk are reasonably taken into account.
