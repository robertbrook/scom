Mr. Dobson
: In general, the Opposition support wholeheartedly the principle in clause 7 that unions 70 should be obliged to inform their members in detail of the unions financial position and the salaries of the president, the general secretary and members of the executive. There is no good reason why a union should keep that information secret. Indeed, the rules of most trade unions require them, in one way or another, to inform their members of the state of the union's finances, and many unions declare the salaries of the major officials. It is reasonable for the law to require all unions to meet the best practice of other unions.

We accept that the information cannot be kept secret. The amendments outline how that information could best be made available to union members at a not disproportionate cost to the union or its members and with the least bureaucracy. It might not be the Government's intention, but as drafted the clause is onerous and potentially very expensive, although one or two unions inform their members in the way set out by the Government.

Amendment No. 9 requires the union to inform its members of the financial state of the union and the salaries paid, but we hope that it will enable the union to do that in a way that is less onerous, less bureaucratic and less expensive. We hope that the wording of the amendment will commend it to the Minister—and, indeed, if I may refer to them, to his official advisers. It requires the union to inform its members either by sending them a statement, as the Government originally suggested, or by its usual practice of notifying members of matters of general interest—that is, via the union journal or circulars. If, as we agreed, that method of dishing out information is satisfactory in clause 5, it should satisfactorily meet the requirements of clause 7.I hope that the Government will accept it.

Amendment No. 10 makes it clear that when the union informs its members in the union journal or circulars, it should state also that full, or fuller, details of the statement of accounts are available to union members either free or at reasonable cost, by which I mean that members should, perhaps, be expected to pay the postage for receiving that information.

The Opposition hope that that achieves the Government's objectives, which, for the most part, we share. The amendment requires unions to publish and publicise that information. It will not be possible to keep it secret. The amendment would enable members to have full information regarding matters, which, we believe, is the Government's objective. One or two minor consequential changes may have to be made to other parts of this or subsequent clauses to bring them into line with our suggestions. As ever, I plead guilty to the possibility of imperfect drafting in the amendment, but I suggest that there are also slight imperfections in the drafting of the Bill. The amendment proposes the use of the union's usual channels for communication with its members. If I may stray from the amendment for one moment, the wording of paragraph (b) in amendment No. 9 is similar to the wording of amendment No. 24.1 hope that the Government will accept the amendment, or will accept that something similar would fit the Bill, literally.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Employment (Mr. Patrick McLoughlin)
: I welcome the speech made by the hon. Member for Holbom and St. Pancras (Mr. Dobson).

Amendment No. 9 would allow a union to satisfy the requirements of new section 32A by taking the steps which are its normal practice,

"When matters of general interest to all its members need to be brought to their attention."

Having listened to the arguments, I can tell the Committee that the Government are willing to consider the suggestion further, especially as the Opposition appear to have accepted the key principle that union members should have a right to relevant information. There are clear examples in the past where that has not happened—where it has proved difficult for union members to obtain what others now accept as a right to knowledge.

Arrangements for the provision of financial and other information to union members, on a regular basis, need to reflect the particular circumstances of the proposal. Therefore, they should not necessarily be precisely the same as those which apply to the provision of information about scrutiny reports on union elections and political fund ballots.

It is important that union members should be able to have the information to be provided under new section 32A without undue delay, and within a certain period after the information is available to the union.

I appreciate that amendment No. 10 is designed to provide a necessary addition to protect members whose union chooses not to provide individual written statements to members as a matter of course. In such circumstances, I certainly agree that it is vital to give individual members a right to request, and obtain, an individual statement.

I accept that the terms of the amendment have drawn upon the legislation on the provision of the scrutineer's report on union election and political fund ballots. As I have already said, the arrangements established by the clause need to reflect the particular circumstances and needs which they address. As they stand, the provisions proposed by amendment No. 10 present a number of problems, none of which, I hope, will prove insuperable.

The hon. Member for Holborn and St. Pancras said that he did not expect us to accept his amendments. I shall explain why we cannot accept the amendments. First, they impose no duty on a union to supply a copy of the statement, on request, to a member. To satisfy the requirement of the amendment, a union would only have to indicate that it would supply the information, but would face no sanction if it failed to do so. I realise that that was not the intention.

Secondly, the amendment would allow a union to charge a member for indivual provision of the statement. The hon. Gentleman referred to that, but it is not acceptable—it is not part of the arrangements in new section 32A. We do not believe that a charge would be justified. Unlike a scrutineer's report, the financial statement is likely to be produced by the union itself, and is unlikely to be a lengthy document. We believe that it should be available to members free of charge.

Thirdly, the amendment fails to place a union under any particular obligation in terms of the time in which it 72 would have to provide material to any member who requested information.

I am sure that those matters can be resolved when the Bill returns to the Floor of the House. I can assure the hon. Gentleman that we shall endeavour to address some of his concerns.

I am sure that my hon. Friends will join me in congratulating the hon. Gentleman on demonstrating so clearly that the law on trade union activities needs to be considered on its own merits, as we have done, and not by reference to wholly unwarranted analogies with company law. The amendments remove the requirement that trade unions should send to each member a copy of the statement. Companies have to send to each shareholder a copy of the annual report or summary.

In recognition of the Opposition's welcome conversion and of their arguments, we are prepared to table Government amendments to tackle the basic concerns which prompted the amendments. Therefore, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will withdraw the amendments. I assure him that changes will be made on Report.

Amendment No. 23 is a genuine probing amendment which we have introduced because we are not sure what the Government are trying to provide for or against in clause 7(3), which states:

"The requirement imposed by this section is not satisfied if the statement specifies anything inconsistent with the contents of the return."

It is not clear what potential for wickedness has been anticipated by those who drafted the Bill. If we are in favour of an information statement, it should not misrepresent the financial return that was made to the certification officer. I think that everyone would agree that, if we are in favour of the information being circulated, we must be in favour of it being accurate, not misrepresentative. However, we are not entirely clear about the aims of subsection(3).

We are concerned about the problems that could arise from that permanent spectre at the feast in all debates on trade union law—the dreaded vexatious, or potentially, vexatious litigant. Our understanding of the Bill as drafted, is that there is potential for the vexatious litigant to turn into a vexatious complainant to the certification officer when there is inconsistency between the statement and the return.

Our probing amendment aims to provide a waiver or saver whereby the statement must be "substantially" inconsistent with the return before there are grounds for complaint. Our provision would reduce the absolute aspect of the subsection.

The other problem we have with the subsection is that an inconsistency would amount to a criminal act. Most Conservative members of the Committee—perhaps not those in the legal profession—would agree that criminal proceedings should be commenced only if there are substantial reasons for doing so. As regards the stipulations of subsection(3), criminal proceedings should be commenced only if there is a substantial inconsistency. The concept of inconsistency as a criminal offence is fairly 74 novel in law. The Minister and I shall be bound to be found guilty of inconsistency before the hour is out. We need to be careful, therefore, before we make inconsistency a criminal offence.

There is no saver or reliever provision. If, therefore, "substantially" is not included, in comparison with company law it might be safer to provide a saver clause or subsection so that if a court were to find that a person who had introduced an inconsistency had acted reasonably and honestly, that person might be excused from penalties.

Without such a saver subsection in the clause and with the penalty clauses which follow it, there is all the more reason why any transgression on the grounds of inconsistency needs to be substantial, otherwise the might of the law may rain down on some hapless person who has circulated something only marginally inconsistent with the formal return.

Before any of my hon. Friends speak on this matter, we are interested to know what wickedness the Government think that they have spotted and are attempting to anticipate. I will not sit down until the Minister has his briefing.
