Mr. Frank Dobson (Holborn and St. Pancras): I beg to move amendment No. 8, in page 4, line 48, at end insert

"save that normal practices and assistance given to union officials and executive members in pursuance of their functions shall not be considered campaigning assistance".

The Chairman: With this we may take amendment No. 11 in page 5, line 12, after "any", insert "direct".

Mr. Dobson: Our understanding is that clause 3 provides that if a union gives campaigning help to one candidate, it will give help to all. There should be no favouritism. That provision is a slight variation on the slogan "one for all" but does not mean "all for one".

We accept the principle that union elections should be fair and that the general resources of a trade union should not be used to serve some candidates but not others. We face the problem of how to enshrine that principle in law without making the situation worse rather than better.

The main difficulty is that trade unions do not exist just to conduct elections to their executives: they exist for a host of other purposes and functions which have to be carried out continuously, including during an election. The unions are not concerned just with industrial disputes but with the day-to-day business of representing members, conducting negotiations, carrying out joint consultations, agreeing new working arrangements, making approaches with employers to the Government or to European institutions and being involved in training development. In addition, a trade union is a voluntary organisation with the continuous function of carrying out the process of two-way communication with its membership. That function involves visits by elected and appointed officials and meetings of the lay membership, officials and, sometimes, elected officials. It involves the production and distribution of journals and leaflets and the use of press offices and public relations. It is a continuous process.

In most union elections, most, if not all, the candidates will also be involved in that day-to-day process. The problem of enshrining in law the Government's aims is exacerbated when incumbents are involved in union elections. Those people are not necessarily incumbents of the posts that are being elected but of other elected posts, or paid officials of the union who are not subject to election. They may be running against people who are not incumbents of posts, that are subject to re-election, who are not incumbents of already-elected posts, or who are not paid officials of the union.

Col. 36 Having considered the problem carefully and having consulted with various sections of the trade union movement, we believe that it would be hard to tell whether a union official or an elected union representative would be going about ordinary business or election business.

I have not consulted the general secretary of what was the Amalgamated Engineering Union but I shall use his regular visits to the Nissan plant in County Durham as an example. His visits are partly because of the single-union agreement between Nissan and the AEU. Sometimes he goes by train and takes a short car journey; sometimes he goes all the way by car. During the election for the post of general secretary he might visit Nissan. How would we distinguish between the facilities of the union rightly being made available to the general secretary of the AEU for his regular visits to Nissan, which benefit the membership and the employer, and his visit to that part of the country in order to further his election or re-election? He might be attending a branch meeting, or holding meetings with other parts of the union, or he might just be carrying out his normal visit to Nissan to meet representatives of the work force and the management.

Visits made by Ministers during general elections or by-elections parallel those visits made by people running for trade union office. A careful study of the movements of Ministers during by-elections would show that they had pressing Government business in the constituency holding the by-election, in an adjacent constituency, or in a constituency nearby—.It would be exceedingly convenient for Ministers, going about their paid Government business in their paid-for-by-the-taxpayer car, to make a sneaking visit to the constituency holding the by-election. Nobody jibs at that.

There are other aspects to be considered. Unions, other than through meetings, generally communicate with all their members by circular. It might be thought that if a circular were to be sent relating directly to an election, it should be sent by all the candidates on the one out, all out principle. But what would the union do about a business-as-usual circular which might be sent out by an incumbent as part of his day-to-day union business? Would that be affected by the provision? We fear that it might and that it could lead to a good deal of litigation.

If the union journal, which is circulated to union members, were to have an article by one candidate, it should, according to the one in, all in principle, have articles by the others. That is clear, but there are practical, day-to-day problems. There may be a regular or, in some cases, impenetrable article in every issue of the journal signed by the president or the general secretary of the union, or by an incumbent in a different post. That regular communication with members could be lost. Would that ordinary business, which is not strictly related to the election, be caught by the provision?

In addition, the union journal usually includes considerable coverage of the newsworthy activities of members of the union, and the incumbents are more likely to do something newsworthy because power is already vested in them. As the Opposition know, that can be a disadvantage. For example, the BBC applies a test in order to decide whether an item is sufficiently newsworthy to deserve coverage on the radio or in a television news bulletin. That test is greatly to the advantage of Ministers rather than to their opponents. Ministers pass the test Image Col. 37 more often because, by announcing something, or deciding to do something, a Minister can be said to be doing something, which is more newsworthy than criticising something. Similarly, the union journal will tend to give greater coverage to the incumbents, who are doing something, than to the outside runner, who is likely, at the most, to be saying something.

Furthermore, the trade union press office, quite rightly, and with the agreement of the membership, is at the disposal of the incumbents. It carries out functions related to the day-to-day business of the union and publicises the union's activities. Therefore, during a union election involving one of the incumbents, the press office will try to get the name of that incumbent on the radio, on television and into the newspapers. That must be an advantage, but is it an improper advantage that will be caught by clause 3?

Candidates in an election may produce leaflets. However, according to one interpretation of clause 3, if any candidate were to produce a leaflet it would be the union's duty to investigate whether union resources had been used in its production. That is a far-reaching obligation. Alternatively, it might be said that the union should investigate only if a complaint were made that union resources had been used. And so it goes on.

Invitations to branch meetings are another method of propagating the interests of candidates in an election. One might suppose that that was all right, as long as all the other candidates were also invited. But what should happen if one of the candidates is the nominee of a particular branch and, for geographical or trade reasons, enjoys the wholehearted support of that branch? Would, for example, a printers branch in Glasgow, motivated by a strong craft commitment to printing and a strong commitment to non-Scottish candidates, be obliged to invite all the other non-print, non-Scottish candidates to attend its branch meeting? And if it did not, would that union fall foul of the requirement to deal fairly with all the candidates? In other words, does the requirement for fairness to candidates apply right through from national to regional or branch level? If so, who can expect anyone to monitor all those sources of potential trouble and failure to comply with the law in a widely geographically-dispersed voluntary organisation?

Let us consider another example: that the general secretary of the Amalgamated Engineering Union visits the Nissan plant in County Durham, where there is a single-union agreement, and the management allows him to address all of, or a substantial proportion of, the work force. If that is done in the normal course of union activities, it is all well and good. If it is done in the course of an election in which the general secretary may be standing or supporting another candidate, will the union insist that the management of Nissan welcomes on to its premises someone who might be regarded as a loony trouble-maker because he happens to be standing for a union post? That aspect must be considered.
