CARDIFF BAY BARRAGE BILL

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Gwilym Jones)
: I beg to move,

That, during proceedings on the Cardiff Bay Barrage Bill, the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock.

I hope that we shall give the Bill the consideration that it warrants, that we shall proceed amicably and that we shall not need to extend our sittings.

Mr. Paul Murphy
(Torfaen)
: I welcome you, Mr. Winterton, to an exclusively Welsh Standing Committee. I am sure that you will enjoy our deliberations. You are, of course, aware not just of the history of the Bill and its passage through the House, but of the fact that Opposition Members regard the setting up of this Standing Committee as an error. We believe that Standing Order No. 86 would have given all hon. Members representing Wales the opportunity to discuss the matters with which we shall be dealing. I am only sorry that that has not been the case.

I am quite convinced, however, that hon. Members from Wales—and, indeed, from England—will consider fully the matters we are to discuss. I agree with what the Minister in his comments about the sittings motion. However, I should like to make a few points which I am sure that the Minister will take on board in our various sittings. This is the first occasion on which a Standing Committee has dealt with the details of the Cardiff Bay Barrage Bill. Select Committees have dealt with the Bill extensively, but this is the first occasion on which Members of Parliament have been given the opportunity to examine the Bill line by line. The major difference between what occurred in March before the general election—when the Standing Committee met for only half an hour and had to be abandoned because of disputes over Standing Order No. 86—and now is that at that time the Government had a majority of 100 and they now have a majority of 21. The difference between those two figures must mean that the arrogance demonstrated by the previous Conservative Government—not just to this Bill but to various others—cannot continue. The Government must make various concessions and compromises and listen to what the Opposition have to say. I hope that, in the sittings ahead, the Government will not just mechanically say, "No" to anything that the Opposition proposes.

The Bill has aroused enormous controversy. There are those who, quite rightly from their point of view, believe that the establishment of a Cardiff bay barrage would benefit that city, and south Wales in general. They believe that the barrage would bring economic development and, without it, great doubts could be cast over the attractiveness of development.

There are others, who are equally genuine, who believe that the building of the barrage would present many problems. It is only in Standing Committee that we can consider all those different points of view.

There will, I am sure, be lively exchanges between the Minister and the Opposition on why the cost has increased, from about £80 million to £112 million, which is an increase well above the rate of inflation. Mr. Murphy
: I hope that sufficient time will be set aside by the Committee to consider the costs. I know that that matter will be dealt with in some detail later on today.

Another sitting should be devoted to a debate on the water quality of the bay, which concerns many environmentalists and many people in Cardiff. Another sitting should be set aside to enable the Committee to deal with the ground water levels in Cardiff. We should be able to discuss in considerable detail the fears of the residents of Cardiff about the problem of ground water levels and the possibilities of flooding.

Both South Glamorgan county council and Cardiff city council have a considerable interest in what goes on in Cardiff bay. Members on how that should take place, but there is no disagreement that the general area of Cardiff bay should be developed. That is why it is so important that the Standing Committee should consider the Bill in detail. We will look, line by line, at the points that affect not only the capital city of Wales, but the entire Principality.

I shall not support it unless we get clear-cut assurances from the Minister about information that is required to make the Committee's work effective. We all get our parliamentary kicks in one way or another. This Committee is odd, if not unique, in post-1940 parliamentary activity. The Committee is considering a Bill that started as a private Bill and was converted by the Government into a public Bill, for which there are no known precedents.

I have been through the Journal Office, the Public Bill Office and the Private Bill Office and I have not found a precedent for a Bill being treated in this way since 1940. The Committee is making history and some procedural issues flow from that. That is why I asked a question during the debate on setting up the Committee and why we felt so strongly about the character, nature and composition of the Committee. Even at this late stage, I hope to appeal to the nicer side of the Under-Secretary, who has not been in office long enough to become a ministerial cynic, and suggest that he considers varying the Committee's composition as quickly as possible.

The Under-Secretary refused to give all Members representing Wales the opportunity to serve on the Committee on curious grounds. He referred to a suggestion made by my right hon. Friend the Member for Swansea West (Mr. Williams) for a larger Committee of some 80 Members or more. He said:

"I am sure that the right hon. Gentleman is correct, but I doubt whether there is any precedent for doing it."—[ Official Report, 9 June 1992; Vol. 209, c. 272.]

The original Standing Orders that established the principle of Standing Committees such as this, intended Standing Committees to be large because the House was rightly jealous of transferring power from the Committee of the whole House to a smaller Committee. The Standing Orders envisaged Committees of 80 or more members.

We were not asking for something odd or strange. The strangeness has been the gradual whittling down of the size of Committees. We have a stronge case—and I appeal to the Under-Secretary to think again—for suggesting, in the light of the unique character of the Committee's work, that it should be larger. Even if he is not willing to reconsider the size of the Committee, will he consider sympathetically an alternative proposition? The Standing Committees on Statutory Instruments &c. and the European Standing Committees have a helpful and sensible procedural arrangement. Hon. Members who are not members of those Committees can attend them and speak, but not vote.

Members with particular interests in the Bill be allowed to speak. That would be a reasonable procedural compromise and I hope that the Under-Secretary will consider this helpful suggestion. There are hon. Members who are not members of the Committee but who have a special interest in the Bill with its curious and unprecedented history.
