Clause 21
DISMISSAL RIGHTS

Ms. Joyce Quin
(Gateshead, East)
: I beg to move amendment No. 112, in page 39, line 20, at end insert

"or former pregnancy or pregnancy related sickness".

The amendment is designed to learn from the Government whether dismissal because of pregnancy also includes cases in which a person is dismissed because of pregnancy-related sickness or miscarriage. The Bill is unclear on that issue, which we feel needs clarification. It might mean that an employee who is protected by having served for more than two years full-time or for more than five years part-time is protected from dismissal, due to pregnancy-related sickness. Unless that is clarified in the Bill, women without the qualifying period for protection may not be protected from dismissal because of pregnancy-related sickness or miscarriage.

We have been supplied with evidence that there is inadequate protection for women who are dismissed because of pregnancy-related sickness, although the incidence of such cases seems to be fairly widespread. We read and believe information that has been sent to us—we have no reason to disbelieve it—by many of the caring services, advice agencies and citizens advice bureaux.

On several occasions we have quoted cases from the National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux reports "Not in Labour", which deals specifically with pregnancy and employment problems, and "Hard Labour", which deals more generally with the lack of employment protection and some of the abuses of employees which, unfortunately, seem to be all too prevalent in our country.

The amendment is simple. It simply adds the words:

"or former pregnancy or pregnancy-related sickness".

I hope, therefore, that the Government will feel able to accept it. I also hope that they will be swayed by the weight of the available evidence. I do not want to go through the case studies in great detail but I shall refer to one or two of the findings of the citizens advice bureaux which relate specifically to the amendment.

The report "Not in Labour" found that some women are dismissed because of pregnancy-related sickness and makes the point that it is worth considering whether a fellow employee, male or female, who took time off for sickness would have been dismissed in similar circumstances. Many hon. Members, certainly my hon. 412 Friends, would claim that there is much evidence to show that other employees who were sick and not pregnant would have been treated differently. For example, an East Anglian citizen advice bureau commented:

"We are particularly concerned about women who lose their job because they are pregnant/just had a baby, for example a case of a woman who had some time off because she was suffering from sickness in early pregnancy".

A citizens advice bureau in Kent reported that a client, employed full-time as a credit controller, was made redundant while she was off sick due to pregnancy, and that a new recruit to the firm took over her job.

Since the report, CABs have received further evidence of clients being dismissed due to pregnancy-related sickness. A CAB in Somerset reports that a client was dismissed without warning during a period of sickness connected with her pregnancy, and a CAB in Essex reports that a client, who was employed for 10 months and was 11 weeks pregnant, took one day off because of pregnancy, was threatened with dismissal and told to "either stay and do the work or leave"—a harsh reaction by the employer to someone who took only one day off.

A CAB in south Wales has a client who had worked for the same firm for nearly two years and so almost came within the qualifying period. When she got pregnant she became sick and was dismissed without notice. The CAB comments that

"the client has no recourse for unfair dismissal"—

as she fell foul of the two-year rule—and that a

"Sex Discrimination case is not very likely."

All these examples make us hope that either the Government will accept the amendment or give an assurance that will remove our doubts. We are worried that the concerns of many seem not to be dealt with in the Bill. Advice services are concerned about that omission. The problem relates to pregnancy-related sickness and to women who are dismissed following a miscarriage. If the Government do not make a concession I hope that they will at least recognise the nature and scale of the problems and will tell us how they propose to deal with them in the Bill.

: I cannot advise the Committee to accept the amendment because it would provide an extension to the Bill's protection which would be unlimited by time and unwarranted. I firmly agree that employers should be flexible and, wherever practicable, be prepared to consider adapting their working practices to accommodate the needs of working women. However I cannot believe that Opposition Members would wish to impose such an unjustifiable burden on employers as the amendment could imply.

The Bill provides strong protection against dismissal for any reason connected with pregnancy, having had a baby or having taken statutory maternity leave. I do not understand how hon. Members in their reference to "former pregnancy" can possibly argue, for example that women—for women, and not men would have the enhanced protection—should be able to take any time off to look after their children, or for any other reason connected with the fact that they had children, and that there would be no requirement for them to act reasonably.

That would be the effect of the amendment although I am sure that that is not what the hon. Member for JPEG 413 Gateshead, East (Ms. Quin) suggested. I accept her point that it is a probing amendment and not one which the Opposition expect the Government to accept.

The amendment would also protect all pregnancy-related sickness absence, regardless of circumstances. Anyone who is dismissed on grounds of sickness can already complain to an industrial tribunal if they qualify, and the dismissal would be unfair if the employer had acted unreasonably. However, it may be fair for an employer to dismiss someone, if the employer had acted reasonably. The employee may have taken too much time off, may not have followed the contractual notification requirements, or may simply have been unable to continue in the job. There is no good reason for giving unlimited protection to people who happen to have one type of illness, which is related to pregnancy, and not another which is not.

The amendment goes far beyond what the European Court of Justice has said is reasonable. It held that it was for member states to decide the period of maternity leave during which women should be protected from dismissal. At the end of that time, they should be in the same position as any other employee. The amendment would be unworkable. It would constitute the greatest possible incentive for employers to avoid recruiting women, which would have serious implications. I hope that the hon. Lady will withdraw the amendment.

Ms. Quin
: I beg to move amendment No. 115, in page 39, line 48, at end insert—

"(2) For the purposes of paragraph (a) above an employee who has been dismissed by her employer at any time while she is pregnant shall, unless the contrary is proved, be presumed to have been so dismissed by reason that she is pregnant.


(3) For the purposes of paragraph (b) above an employee who has been dismissed by her employer after childbirth shall, unless the contrary is proved, be presumed to have been so dismissed by reason of her having given birth to a child.".

The amendment is one which we strongly support. We feel that the burden of proof should be on the employer if a women has been dismissed because of pregnancy. Currently, if a woman has worked more than two years full time and five years part time, her employer has to prove that the reason for dismissal was not pregnancy or childbirth. Clause 21 provides that a woman dismissed because of pregnancy, or during her maternity leave period during which she has given birth, would be able to claim that she has been unfairly dismissed without satisfying the usual length of service and hours of work requirements.

Women who currently meet the service and hours of work qualifying conditions to claim unfair dismissal do not have to prove that the reason for dismissal was pregnancy. It does not seem fair that the new provisions are subject to different rules. It must be made clear that the burden of proof should be on the employer to show the reason for dismissal.

The amendment is important because the experience of advisers—for example, solicitors working in law centres—is that it is often difficult to prove that an employer's real reason for dismissal of an employee was pregnancy. The employer may give other reasons—perhaps alleged misconduct, incompetence or even creeping incompetence—and it must then be proved that the employer was lying. It is possible to do so by inviting a tribunal to draw inferences from the evidence, but that requires expert preparation and representation. The point has been made to us strongly that due to the lack of legal aid and the under-resourcing of advice centres and similar organisations, such expert preparation and representation is in short supply.

The difficulty of proving the real reason for dismissal means that women who currently qualify for unfair dismissal protection will enjoy more protection under the 416 Bill against unfair dismissal on the grounds of childbirth or pregnancy than women who do not currently qualify for unfair dismissal protection. The amendment would remedy that defect by placing the second group in the same position as the first group concerning the burden of proof.

Clause 21(4) requires an employer to give to the employee in writing the reasons for dismissal if she is dismissed at any time during pregnancy or during maternity leave after childbirth. Surely the employer ought to be obliged to show that the reasons given are correct and not related to pregnancy. The amendment would require that to be done. The amendment would not shift the burden of proof to the employer when a woman is dismissed after maternity leave.

A European Community directive was proposed several years ago to extend the burden of proof to all equal opportunity directives, but that is not the thrust of the amendment. We are not trying to reopen the debate on the burden of proof in all equal opportunities legislation, however much some of us would like to do so. The amendment relates specifically to unfair dismissal. None the less, we are aware that the United Kingdom was critical of the European Community's efforts to introduce a directive on the burden of proof. The House of Lords Select Committee on European Communities said that it was ironic that a directive that was in some respects trying to introduce United Kingdom practice to the European Community was opposed only by the United Kingdom. That may not be germane to the amendment, but it may make the Government unfairly resistant to the amendment, which sits very well with the current provisions covering women who have served the qualifying period for employment protection.

If the Government do not accept the amendment, we are concerned that the United Kingdom may not fully implement the maternity rights directive, as agreed in October 1992. There is a danger that the protection given by the Bill will not be effective in practice, by comparison with normal unfair dismissal law which places the burden of proof on the employer. The Bill will create two classes of women: those who are protected by the burden of proof and those who are not. That seems to be contrary to the European directive.

Has the Minister had any discussions with the European Commission about that issue? Will he obtain its legal advice on the point before the Bill is enacted? It would be better to clarify the issue in advance and to avoid the chance of litigation, or of the Government being arraigned for not implementing the directive correctly. If no burden of proof on the employer provision is included in the Bill for women who are not protected by the two-year and five-year rule, questions may be raised in the European Parliament about whether the Government are in breach of the European directive. The amendment is not a technical point only. It is important if women are to be able to exercise the rights that they are given by the clause. We believe that the amendment would make then-case against an employer who is bent on dismissing them unfairly far stronger. If only for that reason, I hope that the government will accept the amendment.

The consequences of not having an amendment to achieve clarification and to put the onus on the employer to prove that he had not dismissed a pregnant woman simply because of her condition, would result in ambiguous signals being sent and in practical difficulties being encountered in enforcing the rights of a dismissed woman. Mr. John Hutton
(Barrow and Furness)
: The background to clause 21 is a matter of historical record. The clause is the result of several cases brought before the European Court of Justice, which has clearly established that the dismissal of a pregnant woman constitutes an illegal act of discrimination under the relevant directive. My hon. Friends and I warmly welcome the fact that the Government have now attempted to enshrine that principle in British law. I accept that it is the Government's intention to make bomb-proof and watertight the principle that women who are dismissed on the grounds of pregnancy will always receive compensation at an industrial tribunal. I hope that that is common ground between us. If I am right in thinking that that is the Government's intention, there is at least a reasonable suspicion that it will not be achieved.

Members on both sides of the Committee with experience of litigation in industrial tribunals will have dealt with cases in which it is relatively evident that the woman has been dismissed or discriminated against on grounds of pregnancy, but in which, because of the burden of proof required in an industrial tribunal, it is not possible to determine the issue. The burden of proof in industrial tribunals is the balance of probabilities. The employer has to establish only the possibility that some other reason was operative at the time of the dismissal. That is normally sufficient to suggest that, in such a case, the woman was not dismissed on grounds of pregnancy.

If the clause stands unamended, and the burden of proof unaltered I am not sure that the Government will be in compliance with the decision of the European Court JPEG 421 of Justice. As with the matter discussed this morning—although it feels as if it were this afternoon—we may have to return to the matter and consider further amendments because the Government have not got it quite right. It might be a better use of our time if, when the Government introduce Bills to comply with European Court of Justice decisions, they were to proceed on the precautionary principle of ensuring as far as possible that the Bill complies with those decisions. The clause is an example of the Government doing the least that they think that they must in order to comply. That is a dangerous method of drafting legislation, because it is probably only a matter of time before that minimalist approach leads to legislation being overturned by the European Court of Justice. We may be running that risk here.

If members of the Committee are concerned that the amendment proposes to change the normal burden of proof in unfair dismissal cases, I should inform them that it is not unusual for the burden of proof to be altered by unfair dismissal legislation to advance a public policy objective. For example, an employer must disprove that the employee was dismissed for one of five unfair reasons. The public policy objective in that case is to confine dismissals to five legitimate areas. There is a strong public policy argument for changing the burden of proof in pregnancy dismissals, if we are to comply fully with the terms of European case law. The reversal of the burden of proof proposed by the amendment is not unusual. It happens in many other areas of law, including other areas of unfair dismissal law.

Clause 21(4) will introduce into new section 60 of the consolidation Act a mandatory responsibility on an employer to furnish a dismissed employee with a statement of the reasons for her dismissal, even if she has not requested such a statement. That is a rather unusual legal obligation, in the absence of a request from the dismissed employee. What is the point of that? We all know that no employer who takes competent legal advice will fill out a statement that says, "I dismissed this woman because she was pregnant." In my experience, most employers are aware that any such statement would be considered to prove, if not unfair dismissal, certainly discrimination. Would it not be easier if, instead of making that mandatory responsibility incumbent on employers, we made it clear that pregnancy dismissals would automatically be regarded as unfair unless an employer was able to prove conclusively that pregnancy was not the real reason for dismissal?

If we consider the law on pregnancy dismissals under section 60 of the 1978 Act, our amendment, which would change the burden of proof in such cases, is not dissimilar from some important case decisions, particularly that of Brown v. Stockton borough council. In that case, the employers tried to show that the real reason for a woman's dismissal was redundancy, but it was clear that it was a pregnancy dismissal.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Birmingham, Northfield (Mr. Burden) made clear, such cases are not supported by legal aid, so if the burden of proof remains on the female applicant, as it would normally be, that will impose an expensive obligation on her. Invariably, such cases will involve the production of evidence to an industrial tribunal on the point of establishing the reason for dismissal.

422 Conservative Members may say that that is fair enough because it is the normal legal process and it should apply in such cases.
