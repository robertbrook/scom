Trade Union Reform and Employment

Rights Bill

Clause 12
RIGHT NOT TO SUFFER DEDUCTION OF UNAUTHORISED OR EXCESSIVE SUBSCRIPTIONS

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 38, in page 26, line 17, after "time", insert "renew or".—[ Mr. Dobson.]

4.30 pm

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Employment (Mr. Patrick McLoughlin)
: I think that I will oppose the amendment. It is meaningless and serves no practical purpose. As no right to pay trade union subscriptions through the check-off exists, it is a nonsense to remind people of a right to renew their consent. The amendment would have no practical effect.

Mr. Frank Dobson
(Holborn and St. Pancras)
: The Government's proposal, like all their brilliant efforts, is intended only to cause trouble for trade unions. We believe that the insertion of the words, "or renew" offers some benefit to unions—

The Chairman
: Order. I should inform the hon. Gentleman that the words are "renew or", not "or renew".

Mr. Dobson
: It would be easier if the amendment were properly printed on the amendment paper.

As I said earlier, if the Government think it reasonable to remind a trade union member of his right to withdraw from the scheme, they should also remind him of his right to renew. Otherwise, there will be a ratchet in the system. That is the litmus test. If the Government are concerned about individuals making choices, there is no reason why the employer's notification should remind employees of their right to withdraw, but not of their right to renew. Both rights will have equal status in law, even if the clause is agreed to. It would be unreasonable to confine notification to the right to withdraw. Furthermore, there has been no suggestion that rules will be laid down to govern how notification of the right to withdraw will be given. It could be worded in such a way that people will be encouraged to withdraw, or threatened in such a way as to suggest that withdrawal would be a good idea.

If the Government do not accept the amendment, it will show that the purpose of the clause has nothing to do with individual choice, but everything to do with fouling up the finances of trade unions—a further stage that the Secretary of State could not bring herself to announce in 170 the House, but distributed by parliamentary answer. That is all part and parcel of what we are discussing.

We are faced with a party in Government who received secret funds from Greek fascists, but are doing their best to ensure that the Labour party does not receive funds. The principal supplier of funds for the Labour party—the trade unions—will find it increasingly difficult both to provide finance for a political fund and to carry out their normal duties, which are accepted in the European Community and have hitherto been accepted in this country.

It might interest members of the Committee to know that, in relation to the rights of trade unionists and of trade unions—the two cannot be separated—in every other country in Europe and within the European Community institutions it is pretty automatic for trade unions to be valued, encouraged and consulted. As good an indication of that as any—I discovered this information through a parliamentary answer—is that the TUC general secretary has met the Secretary of State once in his British capacity, but—if I read the answer correctly—four or five times in his European role on the European TUC and the other European institutions that are part of the consultation process in the European Community

Much will hang on the Government's response to the amendment. If they do not respond favourably, I think that we can assume that we are faced with a deliberate wrecking clause—and we are often accused of tabling wrecking amendments.

He is doing his best to damage—

By discussing clause 12 and previous clauses, we have been trying to engage Conservative Members in a debate about the practical effects of this detailed legislation, which is, after all, about a voluntary organisation freely joined in what is supposed to be a democracy. The clause deals with how membership money should be collected. The agreement between a trade union and a member is voluntary and that member knows about it when he joins. The employer approves of the agreement and helps to administer it. I strongly believe that the law has little role in regulating such private agreements unless fraud or some other criminal offence is involved.

Criminal offences or fraud—in this case, taking money from people's wages without permission—are covered by existing law. One of our main contentions this morning was that no practical purpose would be served by creating another body of law that would interfere, in a minuscule and prescriptive way, in issues over which the law should have no effective right. If a law is to be respected in a democratic society, it has to be seen to be fair and, in my view, it must apply equally across different groups.
