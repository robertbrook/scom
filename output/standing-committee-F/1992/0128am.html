Mr. Dobson
: The purpose of three of the four amendments is to delay the abolition of wages councils until an inquiry into the possible effects of abolition has been conducted and its results reported to Parliament and accepted by Parliament. If the Government insist on abolishing wages councils without ascertaining the likely effects, they should at least have to report annually to Parliament on what the effects have been. The fourth amendment would require them to so so. It is difficult to believe that the Minister will oppose any of the amendments, especially amendment No. 223. It is now eight years since 1984, when the motto was that ignorance is strength, although its cogency has lasted longer than that.

It is necessary to put a few facts on the record. Wage councils set minimum wages for 2,590,000 people—almost 2,600,000 people. Abolition of wages 572 councils will attack the standard of living and is likely to diminish the standard of living of 2,600,000 of our fellow citizens and their families. That seems to be a second front which the Department of Employment is opening up against working people. Not content with throwing just short of 3 million people on the dole—that is only the official figure—the Government are now determined to undermine the living standards of more than 2.5 million people who are still in work. The number of unemployed and those who are covered by wages councils orders totals 5.5 million people. That figure is equal to one quarter of the number of employees in this country. The number of people whom the Government have either thrown on the dole or whose wages they propose to reduce is equivalent to a quarter of all employees in Britain. Wages councils represent approximately one in eight of the work force.

No one from the news media is present to report our debate. It is extraordinary that television, radio and most newspapers cannot find time to report what is happening to the wages of 2.5 million people but that they can devote a lot of time to reporting daft rubbish about scandals—or non-scandals. The scandal is that these matters should be their priority. That is why so many people think that something should be done about the news media.

Of the one in eight employees who work in industries covered by wages councils, more than half of them work in retailing and 40 per cent. are employed in hotels and catering. Even with the help of wages councils, it must be stressed that those people are not particularly well paid. Their pay ranges from the princely, or unprincely, sum of £2.59 an hour to £3.10 an hour—scarcely anyone's ransom. However, it is important to point out that that rate is not the maximum that is set but the minimum. Employers can pay more than the minimum if they want to. There is no restraint on employers who want to increase the pay of employees in industries covered by wages councils above the recommended level.

One of the Government's justifications for abolishing wages councils is that about two thirds of people employed in wages council industries are already paid more than that minimum rate. Like many things for which the Government have claimed credit, that was always the case. In the debate in the House in 1908 about the setting up of wages councils, Winston Churchill acknowledged that most employers were paying more than the rate that the wages councils were likely to set. As there is no restriction on bonus payments or additional payments, it is clear that the only possible purpose for abolishing wages council minimum rates must be to enable people to pay less than those rates. We believe that all the evidence confirms our view that the effect of abolishing wages councils will be to reduce wages.

I want to give examples that support our case. The wages council rate for shopworkers in London is £3.06 an hour. For technical reasons, some chemist shops are excluded from that category. Research by the Low Pay Unit in London shows that shop assistant jobs in chemists are being advertised at about £2.50 an hour—50p or more lower than the rate for non-chemist shops next door, covered by wages councils.

Wages councils used to cover young people under 21. That cover was taken away by the Wages Act 1986. Since then, and more than once, the Greater Manchester low pay unit has conducted surveys into what has happened to the pay of young people in Greater Manchester. Its latest survey was conducted in 1992.
