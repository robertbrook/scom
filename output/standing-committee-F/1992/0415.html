Agriculture Bill [Lords]

10.30 am

Motion made, and Question proposed,

That during the proceedings on the Agriculture Bill [Lords] the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock.— [Mr. Kirkhope.]

Mr. D. N. Campbell-Savours
(Workington)
: I shall say only a few words because I do not intend to drag out proceedings on the sitting motion. Those words should, perhaps, take the form of an appeal to members of the Committee.

I have been in the House of Commons for 13 years—I suppose I am relatively junior—and, in my early years, I was a member of several Standing Committees. Unfortunately, those Committees were characterised by contributions from Opposition Members only. The Bill is about how markets might operate and I appeal to Government Members to participate fully in our debates. Opposition Members have come to Committee in a constructive frame of mind and we believe that the contributions of Government Members are relevant to the debate.

I hope that Ministers will adopt an open mind to the proceedings because intensive lobbying has taken place outside the House of Commons by many and various organisations that feel strongly about these matters. Most of that lobbying has been about how markets will work once the proposed changes have been implemented. Members of the Committee have wide experience of how markets work. I hope that this Committee will not be like Committees considering the Finance Bill, in which Government Members write their correspondence and do not attend to the detail of the debate. I do not say that with disrespect. I understand that Governments have timetables and that, during consideration of the Maastricht treaty, Bills have been delayed and government timetables have fallen by the way. However, we must have a free debate in Committee. Government Members may be convinced by amendments that are tabled. In particular, some amendments have been tabled by the hon. Member for Gloucestershire, West (Mr. Marland) that relate to the capping of Milk Marque in certain circumstances. They will raise an extremely important issue and, prior to that debate, it is difficult to know precisely where we all stand. I hope that we shall have an engaging debate on those amendments.

I should like to add that we feel as if we are also rans. The Bill is now in Committee but, to all intents and purposes, it has almost been implemented outside the House. It is as if we are attending a theatre when the show is over, the cast and audience have gone home and, in fact, the show is already on the regional stage. The activities of Mr. Christopher Haskins and the formidable Mr. Andrew Dare, in their Punch and Judy shows, show that the Bill has advanced far beyond the stage that it should have done. We should have considered the Bill long before now. I believe 4 that it was the Government's original intention that we should have considered it during November or December 1992. Had Parliament had the opportunity of considering the matter earlier, that may have influenced to some extent the debate that is taking place in the farming communities and in the industries that are directly concerned.

Nevertheless, I hope that farmers follow the proceedings of the Bill in great detail. During our proceedings amendments may be accepted that could transform the debate and transform the judgments that farmers are having to make now as to whether they enter into direct contracts with processors or join Milk Marque.

Mr. Paul Tyler
(North Cornwall)
: First, Mr. Hill, may I say how delighted I am to see you in the Chair? Having spent some time in recent weeks, with other members of the Committee, wandering around the hedgerows of England and the Cornish banks, I am delighted to see you here. I am sure that your wise control of our deliberations will be of great benefit to the Committee.

I share some of the concerns expressed by the hon. Member for Workington (Mr. Campbell-Savours). I think, however, that another point should be raised on the motion. Although we come to the Bill comparatively late in the season because of its consideration in another place, we have perhaps the unique responsibility to ensure in the House of Commons that when powers are given to Ministers there are completely proper controls over the execution of those powers. As worded, the Bill includes a number of enabling powers that the Committee has a specific responsibility to check or scrutinise with utmost care to ensure that they will be used only when appropriate.

The Bill is most important and its provisions will come to the industry at an extremely important time. The entire way in which the industry markets its products is up for grabs. There will not be another opportunity for us to prevent the Minister from participating in the changes in an inappropriate way or at an inappropriate time.

I was about to say that the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food is allowed a major Bill only once in a blue moon or cheese, but the situation is not quite as bad as that. However, it is allowed a Bill only every few years. Although we come to the Bill late in the year, the Committee has to pace itself so that we can ensure that the Bill will stand for eight or 10 years without need of further amendment. In such circumstances, each enabling power will have to be examined with extreme care. When Parliament has a role in bringing into operation new powers that the Minister may seek to exercise, we as parliamentarians, must ensure that we consider those powers carefully. I have in mind the notorious clauses 4 and 13, which relates to the running and constitution of the new board. In addition, the Bill loosely provides for an annual report to be introduced when considered "appropriate". Those are matters for detailed consideration later, but it is only right that we should put down a marker at this stage that such issues will be subject to probing amendments and constructive contributions to our proceedings. I am sure that hon. Members on both sides of the Committee will want to contribute to the debate.

Last but not least, clause 22 provides wide powers to end the potato marketing scheme if

"it is necessary or expedient".

It has been put to me that representatives of the milk marketing board have said that they might be able to provide for an earlier date for the revocation of the scheme, which might be as early as 1 April 1994, when other schemes are being revoked. Will the Minister confirm that that is the position?

Representations have been made to us by the Scottish Dairy Trade Federation, which believes that a delay in revoking the Scottish scheme might lead to distortions in the market place. We know that milk crosses the border between Scotland and England. An article in The Scottish Farmer on 6 March 1993 stated:

"The Scottish Milk Marketing Board is supplying liquid milk on a trial basis to a dairy in the North of England, it was revealed this week. The milk, believed to be from farms in the Dumfries area, is being supplied to Manchester based Lancashire Dairies."

It goes on to say:

"The movement of milk from Scotland to England has been rare in recent years because of the shortage of manufacturing milk North of the Border. Milk is currently flowing into Scotland at the rate of two 6 million litres a week, with Express Dairies at Lockerbie taking about 60 million litres annually and the Néstle's Carnation plant at Dumfries taking 50 million litres."

A two-way flow of milk is clearly taking place for whatever reason.

If one scheme were revoked after another, the problem of distortion of supply and, perhaps, even of milk pricing might undermine processing plants in England or Scotland. It is difficult to deduce the result. Ministers should be pressed on whether they think that a distortion of trade would be likely. Such a distortion would not be in the interests of processors or anyone else in the milk market.

It has also been put to me by representatives of the industry in Scotland that the Bill could be interpreted as enabling the Scottish milk marketing board to bring forward its date for revocation to before 1 April 1994. That might distort the milk trade across the border and the allocation and price policies of the relevant boards. Therefore, I hope that the Minister will say what he thinks might happen if it were not possible for the English and Welsh milk marketing board to bring forward its date for revocation.

JPEG 7 There must then be a three month cooling-off period to allow for a challenge to the reorganisation scheme—in other words, to allow for the possibility of judicial review. As far as I can tell, the relationship to date with the National Farmers Union and the milk marketing boards is such that the National Farmers Union appears to be generally supportive of the milk marketing boards' proposals.

The Committee is entitled to know why Ministers are prepared to allow only a milk marketing board to submit a scheme for a successor body. I am told that the Government argued that the milk marketing boards have that sole right because of the complexity of reorganisation and that only they would be able to ensure that a successor scheme was successful in providing milk. I am told also that Ministers have argued that they might have had to preside over several schemes which were clearly nonsense and which would waste their time.

The reality is that anyone who has been reading the farming press recently, in particular Farming News and Farmers Weekly, will have noticed that many people sent in letters expressing some support for Milk Marque. However, those people also recognised that alternative schemes for reorganisation could have been submitted. Some went so far as to suggest that England and Wales could be split up and that the proposed five regions for England and Wales might want to submit individual schemes to be taken into account by Ministers.

One possible advantage of breaking up the milk marketing boards in that way is that we would not be confronted with the problem of Dairy Crest. A more fragmented successor regime would, to some extent, have met the difficulties that have arisen in Europe with vertical integration. The argument that has taken place in Scotland with the OFT is not one that Sir Leon Brittan felt necessary to address. He was prepared to waive through the principle of a form of vertical integration in Scotland because he saw the Scottish milk marketing board and its proposed successor body as small players in the national market and in the international market.

If fragmentation of the successor body could have been a route to secure the survival of vertical integration internally in England and Wales, surely Ministers should have addressed the matter by allowing it to be placed on the agenda. The milk marketing boards will not submit it as part of their proposals for a successor body. As the Bill is framed, there can be no basis on which such a submission could be made and considered by Ministers. The milk marketing boards have nailed themselves firmly to the principle of a single successor body in England and Wales which will be Milk Marque.
