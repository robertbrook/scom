Mr. D. N. Campbell-Savours
(Workington)
: I was dealing with amendment No. 33 at the end of our previous sitting. I want not to drag out the amendment but to draw attention to the milk marketing board's original reaction to the proposal of the Department of Trade and Industry. It said that:

"The Bill is based on the premise that it is for the boards to propose how their assets should be dealt with under a reorganisation scheme. A scheme of reorganisation can only be approved if it meets stringent requirements laid down in the Bill and if it in other respects also has the Minister's approval, as stated in the Bill. Multiple inconsistent proposals would create chaos and would put producers' assets into weak co-operatives easily dominated by powerful buyers. No legislation or Government could possibly entertain proposals for the reorganisation of the Milk Marketing Board other than from the Milk Marketing Board itself, which has legal responsibility for the custody and prudent management of its assets."

It goes on to say that it is unreasonable to adopt the Dairy Trade Federation approach.

Such is the view of the milk marketing board but not necessarily of many of the producers who comprise the board's membership. The reality is that a number of organisations and individuals have suggested in the farming press that Ministers should at least be required to consider alternative schemes for reorganisation. The amendment would provide the opportunity for persons and organisations other than the milk marketing board to propose a subsequent or successor organisation.

I should also turn the Committee's attention to the views that have been expressed to me directly by the DTF as they are very important. Organisations that are represented through the DTF buy, in effect, £2.5 billion of products for processing every year. It is very big business indeed. One would have thought that an organisation of that scale and with that sort of market in one product only—a market that is larger than the economy of many advanced countries—at least would have had the opportunity to propose schemes itself or certainly to feel assured that those who might not agree with the milk marketing boards should have the right to propose schemes for consideration by Ministers.

In France and, indeed, in other countries in the European Community, there is a tradition of smaller co-operatives being vertically integrated while retaining their assets. In this country, we have lost that opportunity because of Ministers' insistence on accepting schemes only from milk marketing boards, which want only to protect their assets when the successor authorities that they propose are set up.

The amendment is a serious one. I do not know whether it meets what is legally required by lawyers. However, Ministers reacted rapidly to a proposal that we made previously on timing and I understand that they tabled amendments to deal with another issue that we raised.
