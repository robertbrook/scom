Mr. Miller
: On the face of it, this is a small amendment and I shall explain its importance not only to our deliberations but to the national economic interest and scientific research development. David Naish, president of the National Farmers Union, speaking about set aside on 21 January 1993, said:

"I have never believed it was a long-term solution to over production. I do think it will last until the end of the current Common Agricultural Policy reform period in 1995, and probably beyond,".

 He continued:

"the pressures will grow on us as an industry to find alternative uses for the land such as using it for biofuel production, creating access, and improving the environment."

 We must define industrial crops. They are crops from which it is possible to manufacture biofuels—liquid esters from edible oils, typically oil-seed rape in Europe, soya in the USA and canola oil in Canada. They are genuine diesel substitutes. Petrol substitutes tend to be ethanol derivatives, including ethyl-tertiary-butyl-ether, which is used in high-octane fuels.

In Europe, rape methyl ester—RME—is likely to be the main source of biofuels and is produced from oil-seed rape by crushing, followed by a chemical esterification, separation and cleaning process. The process is relatively straightforward. One hectare, yielding between 2.2 and 3.3 tonnes of rape seed, would produce between 0.7 to 1.1 tonnes of RME. An important byproduct is glycerine, which is very useful in industry.

248 Recent comment in the scientific, industrial and farming press has been interesting. In December 1992, Lloyd's List referred to a study by the Scottish agricultural college, which found that biofuel can be obtained at prices comparable with taxed fuel, which is about 34p per litre. That competes with mineral-derived diesel, which is bought by large bus companies. The article explained that trials were being conducted in Austria. Indeed, the Department of Transport is playing an active role in similar work that is being carried out at Reading university.

Cereals, sugar beet and other crops provide all sorts of possibilities as alternatives to petrol. Following a chance remark made the other day by my hon. Friend the Member for Workington (Mr. Campbell-Savours), I became interested in the abundance of willow in my garden. On reading the technical literature, I was surprised to learn that it is being considered as a possible fuel. Bioethanol can be produced by breaking down lignocellulose in wood and straw and by using enzymes followed by a fermentation process. I am not thinking of my interest in my garden when I say that the research is worth undertaking.

A detailed article by David Richardson in the Financial Times in July 1992 referred to such research. I recommend that members of the Committee read that and a report published in December by the parliamentary Office of Science and Technology, which sets out interesting ideas on using biofuels for transport. The EC believes that up to 5 per cent. of our motor fuel needs could be obtained from biofuels. According to rough calculations, that would require an area of land two to five times larger than that which is set aside. The crops would have to provide raw materials for biofuel production and that would raise controversial issues which would need to be considered.

The fiscal regime for the industry is crucial. It provides a challenge to the Government, who have yet to produce a coherent energy strategy. I accept that the Ministers present today cannot be blamed for the absence of such a strategy, but they can stamp their authority on the issue and develop the debate by accepting amendment No. 60.

Although the concept of the Department of Trade and Industry co-operating with another Department may appear novel to the empire builders in Parliament, it is, nevertheless, critical to our argument. The clause states that the Government's strategy must include

"the organisation, promotion, encouragement, development, co-ordination or facilitation of marketing in Great Britain or elsewhere of"

 in this case, biofuels. It would make much sense to add the words in our amendment, "and industrial crops". That would not immediately facilitate a way forward, but it would create an environment in which important research could be undertaken.

The debate on the environmental impact is extremely complex, but the use of biofuels in a small-niche market, where the advantages of greater biodegradability can be exploited—for example, in inland waterways or urban areas—presents us with some interesting benefits. The issue must be dealt with carefully as there are important counter considerations. I want to study in detail the research report from Aberdeen university, which has recently been referred to in the media. The report relates to the prevalence of asthma in rape production areas.
