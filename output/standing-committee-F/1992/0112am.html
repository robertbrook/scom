The amendment is designed to create a more comprehensive but, at the same time, greatly simplified and more easily understood system of employment protection in terms of maternity provision for women employees.

This part of the Bill has been rightly criticised as a lost opportunity to overhaul some of the complexities of the current arrangements governing maternity provisions and to make the provisions more generous, understandable and accessible. The issue is how effective antenatal care can be promoted to ensure that women have access to it.

The present legal position is ambiguous. We want to clarify it in relation to access to antenatal care and what antenatal care and antenatal classes should include. There is a great difference in tone between the way in which the European Community directive on the matter is worded and the way in which our present legislation is worded. Article 9 of the EC directive states:

"Member States shall take the necessary measures to ensure that pregnant workers within the meaning of Article 2(a) are entitled to, in accordance with national legislation and/or practice, time off, without loss of pay, in order to attend antenatal examinations, if such examinations have to take place within working hours."

We have there a clear and positive statement that women should have the right to attend antenatal classes. The difference between that and our legislation is that in Britain the legislation is the other way round. The 286 intention is to allow access to antenatal classes. According to the present wording, an employer's refusal to allow a, woman to attend antenatal classes should not be unreasonable. The emphasis is entirely negative, not positive, as in the EC directive.

The amendment is important. It would tie in with the principle of the EC directive and would show employers that it is good practice positively to allow women to have access to such facilities. Ample evidence exists to show why that is necessary. We have received evidence from the Equal Opportunities Commission. In its written submission to the House of Lords' Select Committee on the European Communities it stated:

"All pregnant workers in the UK have the right to paid time off work for antenatal care … This right has not been well publicised however, and the Equal Opportunities Commission is aware of cases where neither the employee nor the employer is aware of this right".

My hon. Friend the Member for Gateshead, East (Ms. Quin) gave examples of that, and the citizens advice bureaux went to some lengths to describe the real problems with the existing law in ensuring that the right to antenatal care is a real, not a theoretical one. I was horrified, but not surprised at the example from my own area of the west midlands in the evidence submitted by the citizens advice bureau. A woman tried to have antenatal care during working time. She had been employed for 25 months. However, this was refused, not because something desperately important was happening at work at the time of the antenatal class, but because it was not company policy. That was sufficient to allow the employer to refuse to allow that person time off to attend antenatal classes. Antenatal care during working time in Britain may be available from good employers, who may not only be specialists in employment law but have some sympathy with it and some understanding of the importance of antenatal care. If an employer is not sympathetic, however, the emphasis of the present law of this country does not provide people with a practical right to that care.

It may be said that if the law states that an employer may not unreasonably refuse access to antenatal care, the employee has the right to go to an industrial tribunal. That is true theoretically, but there is a practical problem. Hon. Members will agree that attendance at antenatal classes is necessary before the baby is born. Under present legislation, an employer may refuse access. If that refusal is unreasonable, the woman may complain to an industrial tribunal, but it may be a year or more before the case is heard. If the woman misses the antenatal classes, access to an industrial tribunal may be of limited use. Similarly, her ability to obtain child care to attend antenatal classes a year or two later may be difficult.

If we are serious about wanting to provide access to antenatal classes, employees must have positive rights to attend such classes when they need them and not merely the right not to be unreasonably refused, coupled with access to an industrial tribunal when it is too late.

Employees may have a theoretical right to go to an industrial tribunal, but as my hon. Friend the Member for Gateshead, East said, there are problems. A company such as the one in the west midlands, which considers it reasonable simply to say that it is not company policy to allow women to attend antenatal classes, is unlikely to be liberal with employees who take it to an industrial tribunal. Intimidation, pressure and fear of losing their JPEG 287 jobs if they make a fuss are problems for employees. Unless we take the opportunity in the Bill to deal with those problems, we shall be guilty of providing a theoretical right, worded in a convoluted way, without giving a practical right to employees. I hope that there is no difference in principle between members of the Committee and that the amendment will be accepted.
