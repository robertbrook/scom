Clause 11
RIGHT NOT TO BE EXCLUDED OR EXPELLED FROM UNION

MS. Joyce Quin
(Gateshead, East)
: I beg to move amendment No. 39, in page 22, line 18, at end insert:

"or where the union does not have representation at his place of work due to a single union agreement being in force,".

The Chairman
: With this, it will be convenient to take amendment No. 48, in page 22, line 26, at end insert:
  • "(e) under an agreement with another trade union or trade unions the union of which he is a member has agreed not to accept into membership workers of his occupational description at his workplace.
  • (f) the union of which he is a member is not a party to the collective agreement with his employer covering workers falling within his occupational description, or
  • (g) the individual is or has been a member of another trade union recognised by his employer for the purposes of collective bargaining for workers falling within his occupational description.".

    • Ms. Quin
    : Both amendments refer to single union agreements and amendment No. 48 to the importance of a system akin to the present system of Bridlington principles. They are important matters on which hon. Members on both sides of the Committee will wish to express their views.

    Amendment No. 39 is the first of a series of amendments to clause 11, some of which we hope that the Government will look upon with favour, although others are far less likely to be acceptable, given the scope and intention of clause 11. None the less, the Government should at least be sympathetic to amendment No. 39, because it refers to the practice of single union agreements, which the Government have welcomed on many occasions. Certainly those which have been negotiated have worked well, especially in some successful firms. The Opposition are concerned that, although the Bill does not outlaw single union agreements, it will make their operation much more difficult and may create instability and uncertainty in workplaces in which, till now, there has been a good relationship between the recognised union and the management.

    It is not only the Opposition who are worried about this aspect of the Bill. In the briefing sent to all hon. Members on the Second Reading on 17 November, the Confederation of British Industry said that it had a practical concern about clause 11:

    "As regards trade union membership rights"

    "the CBI has some concern that this measure may adversely affect the existing and welcome move towards single union agreements and single table bargaining arrangements."

The one that I know best is that which operates in Nissan in Sunderland—or, more specifically, in Washington, Tyne and Wear. Such a firm would certainly not wish a single union agreement to be undermined, as could happen under the clause. Firms like Nissan have been among the few factors that have brought hope in a gloomy economic situation. The Government always say that they want Britain to continue to be a significant recipient of overseas investment, particularly from Japan. But they may find that many Japanese firms are, at the very least, doubtful about the effects of clause 11. There may be more worries, as they have come to accept the single union agreement.

    : I often agree with Jacques Delors's views; he was a former colleague at the European Parliament. On this occasion, however, I do not agree with him.     The Ernst and Young survey asked questions of a large number of United Kingdom companies which see a question mark over the future of inward investment. For that reason the Government should take the matter seriously. We certainly should not do anything to make inward investment more difficult, especially at present.

    As for Nissan-Sunderland, it is unlikely, given the general atmosphere there, that the single union agreement would be under threat. They might be tempted under clause 11 JPEG 103 to seek to join another union. Although the Government will no doubt say at several points during this morning's debate that joining a union does not involve recognition and representation, they would be unrealistic if they thought that workers who joined a union would not be interested in that union standing up for them in the workplace and seeking to represent them formally.

    It is unwise of the Government to take a chance in this clause and create difficulties by forcing out the single union agreements of which they were previously in favour. The Minister dodged the issue when asked specifically during the Second Reading debate what the implications of the clause were for single union agreements. I hope that he will respond more directly today and accept that our concerns, and those of the other organisations that I have cited, arise from a belief that we should not change something that is working well, especially if it would cause instability or threaten industrial harmony in some workplaces.

    Amendment No. 48 deals with the core of the Bridlington principles, which clause 11 is designed to do away with. Our amendment would reintroduce certain valuable aspects of the Bridlington principles and would allow unions to retain spheres of influence and activity which, if properly applied, would help, not hinder, good industrial relations. No one is saying that the principles are set in stone for all time but we are saying that they have lasted a long time and, on the whole, have served to arbitrate successfully in many industrial situations which, if there had been a free-for-all in union membership and recruitment, would not have been the case.

    Can the Minister tell us whether he feels that there is a great campaign among companies to scrap the Bridlington principles? Our impression is that that is certainly not so. Our contacts with representatives of companies and company organisations show that while there may be some reservations, there is no general campaign or crusade for the changes that are introduced in the clause.

    I remind the Minister that according to the Trades Union Congress introduction to its "Disputes Principles and Procedures" the principles were drawn up because trade unionists recognised that where more than one union was capable of representing a grade of workers it was necessary to prevent the indiscriminate proliferation of unions, if stable and rational trade union structures and collective bargaining machinery were to be developed. Their existence has also prevented the instability that would occur if breakaway unions were formed, or if groups of workers were able to move on a whim from one union to another.

    I refer the Minister to the reaction of the Institute of Personnel Management, whose concern was particularly strong about the dangers contained in clause 11. It says:

    "The proposal to sweep away the Bridlington rules on union membership has not been given much support by industry managements. Though imperfect, this arrangement contributes to stable industrial relations in most organisations.


    There has been no pressure from our members for any proposal to support the disbanding of the Bridlington rules. Whilst fully supportive of individual rights the removal of these procedures could be a destabilising factor.


    Highly satisfactory changes in the 1980's including the establishment of 'single union sites' or recognition and brownfield 'single table' bargaining should not be placed in jeopardy by this proposal.

    The end of Bridlington may encourage shifts of membership between unions for short term gain to the detriment of settled patterns of collective bargaining."

    Our amendment No. 48 is composed of three separate parts, one of which is designed to incorporate the best of the Bridlington principles. Subsection (f) deals specifically with the danger of workers switching unions, perhaps through political motivation or disaffection of one kind or another and undermining a collective agreement which is working satisfactorily. Underlying our concern is our strong belief in the freedom to belong to a union, but when one belongs to such an association one needs a sense of responsibility and democracy. One has to respect the rules which have been decided by the majority of people in an organisation. Only when people feel that their views are consistently not being taken into account is there a problem. The Government will create problems through their proposals, rather than solve them.

    Subsection (g) of amendment No. 48 prevents union swapping where two unions are recognised and the member wants to swap between them—again because of our concern about the instability that such an arrangement can cause for collective bargaining and industrial relations in a work force.

    Finally, the Government will no doubt make great play of their belief in the freedom of the individual. They will say that that is something in which they strongly believe, too, but that lies uneasily with a Government who forbade people to join unions at Government communications headquarters, Cheltenham. Many of their activities have undermined trade union freedom rather than increased it.

    My hon. They have handed over to the TUC the authority to arbitrate in any disagreements which they may have with other unions, in the interests of good, stable industrial relations. It is a sensible agreement and a good way of doing things which has lasted since its inauguration in 1939 with no great trouble. There has been no great outrage in the country at the way in which JPEG 105 the Bridlington principles have worked, nor has there been any pressure from employees or employers to abolish the Bridlington principles. Why have the Government decided almost out of the blue that this agreement, which has worked extremely well, is unacceptable?

    I shall talk later about the freedom of choice which the Government have mentioned as a reason for introducing this restriction, but we must consider, going back to the model of industrial relations which we hope to foster, that stability is an important issue.

    The setting up and operation of trade unions on an industry-wide basis in Germany is a model that we may wish to follow and we must not forget that that model was created in part with the help of the British TUC after the war, taking account of its experiences and wanting to set up a structure which it thought would be most conducive to good industrial relations. We ignore the experience of trade unionists and trade union officials in this important area at our peril; it should not be underestimated. Stability is important.

    I have spent my time working in and for COHSE, as Conservative Members know, and in the health service we have never had a closed shop. We have always had a multiplicity of unions that we could join. In some instances, often at branch level, there are niggling problems and some individuals will want to take groups of workers from one union to another for some pettifogging little problem that has arisen. Sometimes it is as simple as a personality clash. Full-time officials in the trade union movement will be happy to tell us that they spend some of their time arbitrating in personality clashes at branch level. We must not underestimate the destabilising effect that that sort of local event can have.

    Another paradox, which I believe is embodied in the Government's proposal to get rid of an inter-union disputes procedure, relates to the orderly conduct of industrial relations. In other areas of their trade union legislation the Government seems to assume that trade union leaders and trade union officials, be they paid or unpaid, should take personal responsibility and liability in law for the conduct of their members in branches. At the same time, however, they want this almost military discipline, where they appear to be treating union members as if they were some branch of the army that can be ordered about, for good disciplinary reasons, so that they do not go on strike unofficially, leaving the union open to legal action in tort, or whatever.

    This discipline can also be exerted by the Government if the individual member of a union can run around and join whatever union he or she wishes and cannot be refused membership of a particular union, unless in very particular circumstances. The Government do not seem to believe that there ought to be a reciprocal responsibility for individuals who, after all, since we no longer have the closed shop, have made a voluntary decision to join a trade union, which is a voluntary organisation. Therefore one would expect that they would at least have some commitment towards abiding by its rules, which includes disciplinary rules.

    The effect of the abolition of inter-union disputes procedures is anarchic. We are used to the Conservative party being into radical revolution, of an almost Maoist kind after the last 13 years, but they seem now to be a duality, so I should like the Minister to give us some views 106 on the destabilising effects of these proposals, because we are very worried about them.

    There is, and always has been, as any left-wing or even radical left-wing member of a trade union that sells newspapers outside the tube station will tell you, a conspiracy by trade union full-timers to exert discipline, and usually very sensible discipline, on the members of that union to abide by collective agreements. They may not have got all that they wanted in the negotiations, but perhaps it is the best that can be achieved. If this crucial means of disputes resolution is lost, it will risk unleashing forces which full-time officials will not be able to control, simply because individual groups of trade union members, be they politically motivated or otherwise, will be able to take their ball elsewhere and possibly destroy entire agreements as they go.

    There is also the question as to what on earth a general union will do. There are proposals that would allow trade unions to prevent individuals from joining them. There are some very unsavoury individuals who tout themselves round wanting to join trade unions. A few members of the Government have done that. I believe that individual trade union branches and other trade unions ought to have the choice as to whether to accept them. The choice provided for in the legislation as it is drafted is much too narrow.

    By doing this, the Government are unleasing forces which will be anarchic and disruptive and which will fracture good industrial relations. They risk doing a lot of damage and making much harder the job of full-time trade union officials, which is to represent the collective view and to take a majority decision, although everybody may not agree with it. It also risks good industrial relations.

    It is all very well for the Conservative party to believe that there is no such thing as society, but there is such a thing as a collective decision. When one joins a trade union, of course one has individual rights, but there is also a collective decision to which one has to respond. When a collective decision is made people decide to make a particular point, or to support a particular policy. In the Conservative party, perhaps, as well as in the Labour party, not everyone will agree with it, but everyone has to go along with it because a democratic decision has been taken. We can campaign to change it. [ Interruption.] We could debate whether it is democratically decided in the Conservative party. It is democratic centralism in the Conservative party and perhaps democracy, from the ground upwards in the Labour party. If one joins an organisation, one has a duty to accept its decisions, even if one disagrees with them and is in the minority.

    Another issue raised by the clause that we ignore at our peril is that a trade union cannot stop a member or a group of members joining it other than for special reasons. That is an intolerable interference in a voluntary organisation's internal affairs. Imagine if that were to apply to the MCC. If I decided that I did not want to be a member of Wallasey cricket club, or that I wanted to join the MCC and carry on my disruptive ways there, I am sure that the MCC would be the first to blackball me before I reached the door, partly because I am the wrong sex.

    The clause provides that any person can join any union that they see fit, so long as it has something vaguely to do with their job. The general unions, which have a wide JPEG 107 range of membership in all kinds of professions and jobs, would find it even more difficult than the sectoral unions such as COHSE to prevent individuals from joining. COHSE, for example, will expel people who are racist or who have been found guilty of sexist conduct, such as sexual harassment. Its rules make provision for getting rid of such members, and that is as it should be.

    Although a member who wants to join a union can be debarred because of previous conduct, the Bill does not make it clear whether that means previous conduct in another union. Will the Minister clear that point up? We must accept that there are people who go round causing trouble. Some of us have them in our surgeries occasionally. There are always troublemakers in any organisation. Individual organisations must be able to decide whether they want to admit a person to membership. Clause 11 makes it sound as if they have to do so, whether they want to or not, which is intolerable.

    Another aspect is that if groups of trade unionists decide to move to another union, the second union may not want to service them as members. It costs money, time and effort to represent a group of workers properly. It is not always economic for a trade union to provide proper representation for a group of workers in a different area of work? That is why spheres of influence agreements emerged in the first place. It is a more efficient and effective way of representing a work force. What is the good of being a member of the Ceramic and Allied Trades Union if one works in a textile workshop in London? Certainly the Ceramic and Allied Trades Union full-time officials would not want to spend time and carry out the necessary research work that would be involved in negotiating on behalf of such a member. Therefore, there is no point in it getting involved. Again, the clause ignores the reality of industrial relations.

    The Government say that trade union members should be able to choose. That is a red herring, because it posits that a group of general unions will have to represent everybody who comes to them, which is nonsense. That is why individual unions have grown up: they have particular expertise. COHSE has expertise in professional nursing issues, which the General, Municipal, Boilermakers and Allied Trades Union perhaps would not have. Trade unions have developed expertise in certain areas. That is why they are better at representing those sections of the work force. They do not want small break-away groups from other unions to join them. It is too much trouble, they come from areas of work where they do not have expertise and it will not be conducive to good industrial relations. So why are the Government legislating to compel them to accept that kind of membership?
