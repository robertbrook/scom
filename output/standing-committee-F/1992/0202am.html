Clause 29
CONSTITUTION OF INDUSTRIAL TRIBUNALS

Mr. Sam Galbraith
(Strathkelvin and Bearsden)
: I beg to move amendment No. 237, in page 44, line 9, leave out "shall" and insert "may".

The purpose of the clause is to allow industrial tribunal chairs to sit alone, albeit in fairly selective cases, which include the rights on an employer's insolvency, Wages Act 1986 claims, and breach of contract claims under the extended jurisdiction of industrial tribunals in clause 31, which I think we shall support. They also include those concerning application for interim relief when a complaint has been made that the employee was dismissed on trade union grounds and, most important, those cases in which both parties supposedly agree to the tribunal chair sitting alone.

The Government have said that part of the reason for the clause is the delay in claims being heard by industrial tribunals. I think that the reason that they gave is probably true—for a change. The delays are getting out of hand. The number of cases to be heard by industrial tribunals has increased greatly, as has the time that individuals must wait before the hearing takes place. In 1989–90, there were 32,913 cases; in 1990–91, that number increased to 35,926 cases. The delays will worsen because of the increased jurisdiction relating to trade union discipline under the Bill.

The time that cases have to wait to be heard has risen significantly with regard to Wages Act and redundancy payment applications. It rose in 1991 by 28 per cent. and 31 per cent. respectively. I understand that the 1992 figures will continue to increase. The waiting time for applications to be heard varies in certain regions. In the London north region, only 25 per cent. of its cases are heard within 12 weeks, while in Leeds 50 per cent. of cases 650 are heard within 12 weeks. However, no region had more than 50 per cent. of its case load heard within 12 weeks.

Part of the problem stems from the Government's parsimonious attitude towards the funding of industrial tribunals. They asked full-time chairmen to become part-time chairmen and they cut the money available to them. That policy was based on the incredible assumption that fewer cases would come before industrial tribunals. Clearly, that did not happen, and in 1991 the then Secretary of State for Employment increased the industrial tribunal budget by £500,000.

However, the Government were still faced with the question as to whether tribunal chairs should sit alone. The matter was considered by the Prime Minister's efficiency unit, which concluded that savings might accrue from tribunal chairs sitting alone. The idea was not only to deal speedily with cases but to save money, which was the wrong basis for considering the matter. However, the unit cautioned against any hasty decision to implement the proposal. Part of its argument stated:

"The tripartite nature of industrial Tribunals (and the EAT)"—

the employment appeal tribunal—

"is central to their existence, and partly explains the extent of public confidence in them. Furthermore, Tribunal hearings can be tense, and a Chairman sitting alone might feel physically vulnerable. Some people do nonetheless argue that some cases could be decided by a full-time Chairman sitting alone. There are differing views amongst those supporting this change. Some argued that there were certain jurisdictions and types of case where this would be appropriate, for example Wages Act and other common law issues, uncontested hearings, and so on. Others argued that since each case differed, the decision would need to be made for each case by a full-time Chairman, or perhaps a Regional Chairman. Such a major change would require public consultation".

That came from the Prime Minister's efficiency unit, but I also cite a case heard by an employment appeal tribunal—East Berkshire health authority v. Matadeen—because it relates to the issue of a chairman sitting alone. The reason for a meeting between the chairman and representatives of an employee and an employer is that the cases which they hear are not solely concerned with matters of pure legality. In other words, it is the job of two members of the tribunal to know about the everyday running of a business from both sides, to be aware of its inherent stresses and strains and to take those factors into consideration. They do not deal merely with arcane arguments about legal niceties, as was made clear by the chairman of that employment appeal tribunal, who stated:

"Parliament has constituted an Appeal Tribunal on which laymen skilled and experienced in industrial relations and practices are in a majority and sit not as assessors but as full members. The presiding High Court judge can be, and on occasions has been, out-voted on a decision. The function of the industrial members is not only to give advice to the judge on numerous matters and aspects of the industrial relations, but to bring their experience and industrial judgment to the application of the law and to the decision to be reached on appeal. If the EAT is required to examine each appeal in the context of the Temple or Chancery Lane, then the industrial members can see little point in their sitting. As they understand the position, it is because the strictest application of the law must at times be tempered that Parliament ordained that they should be full members of this court."

That neatly states the case for having industrial members of the tribunal. People who take their cases to industrial tribunals have confidence in them because they act quite speedily, are relatively cheap and are far better than small claims courts. The average sitting of an industrial tribunal is four hours, while a small claims court JPEG 651 sits for days. The tribunal has members with expertise in both legal matters and industrial relations.

For those reasons, we want to ensure that industrial tribunals always sit not only with a chairman but with two industrial members, who can give their aid and advice.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Employment (Mr. Patrick McLoughlin)
: As the hon. Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden (Mr. Gailbraith) said, the clause provides extra flexibility to ensure that cases are dealt with as quickly and efficiently as possible. The hon. Gentleman accepts that there has been a huge increase in the number of cases brought to tribunals. I am sure that all members of the Committee are concerned about that.

Industrial tribunals perform a highly valuable role in resolving disputes between individual employees and their employers. The case load is now at an unprecedented level and has virtually doubled over the past few years. Inevitably that has meant that hearings have been delayed longer than usual or than is desirable in a system that was intended to provide an inexpensive and speedy remedy in employment disputes.

At present, industrial tribunal chairmen may sit alone to determine some procedural matters and certain applications for interim relief. Apart from that, a tribunal can hear a case only if it comprises a legally qualified chairman and two lay members, or one lay member if the parties agree.

The clause provides that chairmen shall sit alone when hearing the proceedings listed in new subsection (2C) unless, having taken account of the matters in new subsection (2E), they decide that a three-person tribunal should hear the case, in the light of the considerations set out in the new subsection (2E).

The amendment appears to change the emphasis so that the chariman would have discretion not to sit alone, even when it was abundantly clear that that would be appropriate. In the cases listed in new subsection (2C) there is often little role for the practical experience of employee relations that lay members can provide. A case may turn wholly in a legal matter, for example, with the facts already clear and agreed by the parties. In such cases it may be unnecessary and wasteful for lay members to sit alongside the tribunal chairman. It may cause additional delay in arranging a date for the hearing when all the tribunal members, and the parties and witnesses, can be present.

However, there is flexibility even in the specified cases. The chairman can decide at any time to sit with lay members if, having taken account of various matters set out in new subsection (2E), he or she decides to do so. That means that if a chairman orginally decides that a case should be heard sitting alone, he can change his mind later if the circumstances make that appropriate.

I say to the hon. Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden that that is an important safeguard. It is an important safeguard also for the parties appearing before the chairman because they have the right of appeal to go beyond the tribunal if they believe that the chairman has not considered the matter correctly.

In many cases, lay members of industrial tribunals make an important contribution because of their practical 652 knowledge of employee relations in the workplace, in particular about workplace practices and what most employers and employees would consider reasonable behaviour in particular situations.

I would not want the Committee to gain the impression that we expect large numbers of cases to be heard under the new procedures. The experience of lay members is valued and will continue to contribute, wherever it can be useful. The vast majority of cases—in particular those concerning unfair dismissal and those raising discrimination issues—will be unaffected by the new provisions. However, the proposed discretion will permit a more efficient use of resources in the minority of cases to which it will apply, and will help to reduce delays at industrial tribunals, as we all want.

We believe that it will normally be more appropriate for a chairman alone to deal with the proceedings set out in the clause. As I have explained, even in those cases there will be discretion for chairmen to decide that a case should be heard by a tribunal of three, either from the outset or as the case develops. Our proposals contain important safeguards and I hope that the hon. Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden will withdraw the amendment.

Equally, I understand the conflict in any new system of law between the need for speed, informality and fairness and the need for certainty. One of the great benefits of the industrial tribunal system is that it has forced employers to develop systems and codes that reflect good practice. In the early days of the legislation, employers feared the randomness of tribunal decisions. Companies could not make decisions and be certain of the consequences. They did not know whether the decisions would be found to be fair or unfair and what damages might be awarded.

It was inevitable that, after a relatively short time, the areas of law that were most open to interpretation began to be interpreted. Furthermore the law, which is now relatively mature, is also inherently complex, because there is a series of interpretative judgments that enable everyone familiar with the law to be able to predict with reasonable certainty the outcome of particular cases. I accept that that makes it relatively difficult for the individual to turn up, unaided and unadvised, and have his complaint dealt with informally. That was what the tribunals were originally set up to do and that is traditional in law.

I remember learning that equity was introduced precisely to remedy the artificiality and complexity of common law and that it was designed as a series of legal, decision-making processes that would introduce informality. Gradually it became even more complex than the common law that it was designed to supplement. There is a danger that industrial relations law could go down a similar route.

The loose-leaf books that purport to describe industrial relations law today are bewilderingly complex. That is why the role of the chairman of an industrial tribunal is so important. While the tribunal appears superficially to be complex, when one is involved in its proceedings on a day-to-day basis one finds that the overwhelming majority of cases go down fairly well-defined routes. Many of these great issues oflaw are reasonably well determined. People ought to know—most good employers and large companies with personnel departments certainly know—the consequences of their actions.

When someone is dismissed by an employer, two distinct but overlapping remedies are open to him. The first is the traditional, age-old remedy of common law for breach of contract. That right has been and will be pursued when appropriate, through the ordinary courts until the Bill is enacted. Because it is a remedy under common law, it can be resolved through common law. The parties can reach an agreement, sign up that agreement between themselves and express it to be a full, final settlement of any dispute between them. That agreement, save in the most unusual circumstances, is then completed by law. A court will not look through that agreement and try to reopen the issue.

When unfair dismissal rights were introduced, the situation became a little more complex. The course open, which runs through the fabric of statutory rights, was the basic provision that individuals could not contract out of their statutory rights. That meant that if a man had been dismissed, had taken advice, reached agreement with his employer, and both were happy with the terms of the settlement, they could sign up that settlement as far as common law rights were concerned but, as far as statutory rights were concerned, what they signed was barely worth the paper it was written on, because it would have been deemed to have been contracting out of the statutory rights that has been provided.

For most of the time that I practiced, there was a simple, practical solution. Having reached agreement or, even, before reaching an agreement—certainly long before anybody thought of putting in an application to an industrial tribunal—parties would call in a conciliation officer from ACAS, who would look at the deal. If he was happy that the deal was fair, he would sign it all up on a form that, at that time, was called COT 3. That would work similarly to the old common law receipt in full and final settlement of the claim. That piece of paper was a settlement under the statutory rights protection, rather than something trying to exclude it. The industrial tribunal would accept that COT 3 receipt as being a binding settlement of the issues. Employers and employees could happily sign an agreement and the cash could be paid in the knowledge that that would be an end to the matter.

In recent times, for reasons about which I am not clear—I assume that they are resource-based reasons but that may not be so—ACAS has prevented conciliation officers from providing the service.
