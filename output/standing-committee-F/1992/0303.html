Clause 1
THE INDEPENDENT PRESS AUTHORITY

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Soley
: It may help the Committee if I spend a few moments on this important clause. I wish to draw the Committee's attention to some of the ideas underlying the Bill without again going over the Second Reading debate. The Committee will be relieved to know that I am seeking to avoid doing that. However, it would be helpful if the Secretary of State responded to a number of points. I am grateful to him for attending the Committee, as we all acknowledge that it deals with an important area of debate in British public life.

Clause 1 will set up a new statutory body—the independent press authority. The schedule describes how it will be set up, and an amendment has been tabled, but not yet been chosen. I shall work on the amendment between now and next Wednesday so that we can consider it, because suggestions have been made that the authority should be set up at arm's length from the Secretary of State. That is what the amendment will achieve if I am able to frame it within the terms of order.

Members of the Committee will see that the clause covers newspapers and periodicals. That has always JPEG 5 been the intended definition: there is no change on that.

I do not want to repeat what I and other hon. Members said on Second Reading, but I wish to say a little about the issue of accuracy. There has been discussion of whether accuracy is the key factor or whether the Bill should introduce a privacy law. I have settled on the concept of accuracy because that is an important way of raising standards in the press. Hon. Members should know that the vast majority of complaints are made about accuracy in the press. The old saying that one cannot believe what one reads in the papers—even the date on the top of the page may be suspect—is bad news for journalism and for a free press.

If we can raise standards of accuracy, editors, sub-editors and journalists will check their facts, and in doing so they will attain a higher standard of journalism. One of the points that emerged from the evidence given to the committee that I set up as a voluntary body, on which hon. Members served, was the view that standards were higher in the electronic media because journalists and editors had to make checks more often. Broadcasting legislation requires journalists in the electronic media to report accurately and impartially. I shall refer again to that point later.

The key to the majority of complaints is accuracy; 66 per cent, of the complaints made to the Press Complaints Commission were about that. Most people who write to me do so to complain about accuracy. It is important to note that many of the cases that were discussed by my committee began as stories that contained a significant inaccuracy which then led to abuses by the press.

I have circulated to members of the Committee a report of the proceedings of my informal committee and if they consider it they will see reference to the case of Linda Townley, Princess Anne's maid. That case began with what was a crucial inaccuracy, in as much as she was alleged to have stolen letters from Princess Anne. That allegation, which was not corrected quickly, led to greater intrusion by the press into her private life to try to discover the location and content of the letters. The press obviously hoped for additional stories.

The clause would provide a power to correct, which I shall discuss because it was little debated on Second Reading and it is important. Attempting to fine newspapers was discussed by Sir David Calcutt in his report and by others concerned with press intrusion and behaviour. I am not opposed to fining newspapers, but it would be a dangerous course of action to take. It has been said that the Select Committee on National Heritage, like Calcutt, may recommend fining newspapers for any breach of the code. That would make good investigative journalists look over their shoulders at every clause in the code—it may contain 40 or 50—and at libel. I have always been worried about such an approach. It would be a gift to the Robert Maxwells of this world, making it more difficult to expose such people.

6 The beauty of making the press put things right is that all that it would have to do is make a correction. That would not only raise standards, but make the press put things right in the papers. Restraints on good investigative journalism would not be imposed. That lies at the heart of the clause. The Bill intends to secure a free press and promote professional and ethical standards.

There has been argument about the question of accuracy. I want to repeat certain core arguments. When I began my campaign almost a year ago after being selected in the ballot in April or May, a lot of editors said, "You cannot adjudicate on accuracy." That was an incredible argument to use. It was not credible because accuracy is dealt with by article 1 of the Press Complaints Commission code, which shows how seriously the matter is taken.

Article 1 refers to newspapers having a duty to correct factual inaccuracies. The argument is not whether errors can be corrected—the press accepts that that can be done, despite what was said initially. The central, essential argument is about who adjudicates. Should there be self-adjudication, with newspapers judging themselves through a body dominated by press people? Or should an independent body adjudicate? It is central to my argument that bodies that are set up to adjudicate themselves never gain the confidence of the public or of complainants. There is always a feeling that vested interests are at work.

It has also been argued that the correction of inaccuracies should not be worried about and that their occasional printing is part and parcel of having a free press. Such errors have been described as being par for the course and something that we live with. There are fundamental arguments against that. As members of the Committee are aware, people suffer when gross inaccuracies are printed. The Linda Townley case and many others which were recorded at my informal hearing are examples of people's lives being severely damaged by inaccurate press reporting.

I ask members of the Committee to bear it in mind throughout our debates that there is a citizen's right to expect accuracy in news reporting, which is an important general principle. A free press should inform a democracy. If the press deliberately or accidently distorts the facts in the course of reporting factual information, it is poisoning democracy. Accurate news reporting is the oxygen of democracy—poisoning that oxygen poisons the democracy.

It is important that people have a right to expect accuracy. I see it as a basic right, but it can also be seen as a customer's right. I have made the point several times that someone who buys a packet of tea and discovers that it contains sawdust has access to all sorts of complaints procedures. Yet although accurate news reporting is much more important than a packet of tea, people are expected to put up with inaccuracies.

I have referred to the case—and shall do so again from time to time—of Mr. and Mrs. McKeever from JPEG 7 Northern Ireland. They read a story in the press saying that their 10-year-old son who was suffering from a particular form of muscular dystrophy would have a cure within one year. Their hopes were raised dramatically, but the story was inaccurate. Evidence of that case was given to my committee. It was a classic example of the citizen's right to accuracy. People should not have to expect to put up with that sort of inaccurate news reporting.

It is argued that the press gets its facts but prints corrections if necessary. The evidence against the press is overwhelming. Many people complain about the press to the Press Complaints Commission. In addition, opinion polls show that a very large percentage of the public do not believe what they read in the press. That does the press no good at all. One problem is that people are more inclined to believe the electronic media than the printed word. That too is bad news for press freedom. I do not think that the press gets its facts rights. It could help itself a great deal if it was much more willing to print corrections and respond positively to complaints.

The clause implies that the Press Complaints Commission has not worked. I know that I have the Secretary of State on my side; he has conceded that the Press Complaints Commission, as constituted and operating, has not worked and is not working. When I imported hon. Members from all parties to cross-examine witnesses, there was significant evidence to suggest that people felt that the commission could not work. In her evidence, Baroness Hollis, who was a member of the former Press Commission, gave reasons for suggesting that the new PCC could not work in its present format. Under pressure from editors, the Press Complaints Commission watered down the proposals made under the former Press Commission to give editors greater power in determining what was acceptable behaviour in press reporting.

The general feeling is that the Press Complaints Commission has failed. The Bill is so far the only measure available to put an alternative body in place. The Secretary of State is reluctant to go down the statutory road. I understand that and, in an ideal world, it would not be necessary. However, what do we do if we do not set up a body as proposed in clause 1? We either follow the recommendations of Sir David Calcutt, which I believe bear down too heavily on press freedom, or we find a middle way. There has been talk of a press ombudsman and an ability to fine, but I have already pointed out the dangers of those suggestions. If there is no middle way, why not opt for an independent statutory body? After all, it has worked extremely well for the electronic media. The BBC, and ITV—indeed, British radio and television in general—are very highly regarded around the world. They are regulated by a committee set up by the Home Secretary, No one accuses the BBC and ITV of being gagged or controlled by the Government. There have been occasional worries and complaints, but overall the BBC and ITV are regarded as providing a higher standard of journalism, which is more than capable of 8 carrying out investigations into the affairs of the Government and the Opposition.

The case for an independent press authority is strong. It should be established by statute because the alternatives tend to fall down. The blunderbuss approach of fining newspapers heavily, or introducing a new civil tort for privacy, or restricting photography and bugging—none of these is satisfactory. I am sympathetic in principle, but we must bear in mind the erosion of press freedom in recent years—I shall say more about that when we discuss amendment No. 1. An independent press authority with status is a better approach.

Let me cite some examples, taken from recent press coverage, to demonstrate not only that the press has not reformed, as it keeps claiming that it has, but that the press Complaints Commission has seriously failed. My first example is the sad case of Jamie Bulger. The headline in The Sun was:

"Boy 12 Is Held For Jamie Murder."

That refers not to one of the two boys who were charged with murder, but to a possible witness. The strange thing is that The Sun got the facts right in the story and quoted the police as saying:

"'We are nowhere near that. We have not got the murderer in these cells at this stage.' "

That is clear in the story, but the headline is:

"Boy 12 Is Held For Jamie Murder."

The story reads:

"A 100-strong mob gathered outside the home in Snowdrop Street, Kirkdale, Merseyside, as the youngster was led away, covered by a blanket … Neighbours of the arrested 12-year-old said his family moved into the £85-a-week home, run by the DSS, less than two months ago."

One neighbour was quoted as saying:

"'I thought they were gipsies. The father used to cook soup on a stove in the back yard and the kids ran around shouting for more. They were a real low life family.' "

So a boy is being held as a witness in a desperately serious case that has aroused enormous passions, and The Sun is saying, in effect—I do not accuse it of intending to—that we should not wait to see whether he is guilty, but lynch him now. The slant to the story is almost guaranteed to raise public feeling. It is no wonder that the family were not able to return to their home, even though the boy involved was never accused of murder. There cannot be a better example of why accuracy is important.

This story appeared some weeks after Mr. Kelvin MacKenzie, the editor of The Sun, told the Select Committee on National Heritage that his paper would now tell the truth and that every story would be 101 per cent. true.

Mr. Robin Corbett
(Birmingham, Erdington)
: Just for the day!

Mr. Soley
: Yes. If he had said 1 per cent, true, I might have accused him of exaggeration. I want now to cite two legal cases, because my involvement goes back JPEG 9 over many years to my anxiety about the press calling people pro-IRA or anti-police just because they maintained that the Birmingham Six and the Guildford Four, for example, were innocent. We know now that those groups of people were innocent and that our criminal justice system had gone badly off the rails. The Government must deal with the problems of how press inaccuracy distorts judicial proceedings.

This brings me to the case of the Taylor sisters, who were convicted of the murder of the woman whom the alleged lover of one of the sisters had married. The headline in The Sun—the story appeared in other papers also—read:

"The 'Killer' Mistress Who Was At Lover's Wedding."

There follows photograph and subheading:

"Hatred made her stab bride Alison 54 times, court told."

The wording of the story is technically accurate. It reports what the court was told. However, the presentation of the story forces the conclusion that the sisters were the killers. The story reads:

"It was bride Alison Shaughnessy's day of joy. But standing among her wedding guests was her bridegroom's secret lover who was to murder her, the Old Bailey heard yesterday … Eleven months after the wedding Michelle used a knife five inches long and an inch wide to kill her love rival in her home in Battersea, South West London."

Mr. Michael Carttiss
(Great Yarmouth)

Mr. Soley
: I am coming to that. The trial concluded on 24 July 1992 and the sisters were convicted and sentenced to imprisonment.

Their solicitors wrote to me:

"On our clients' instructions we lodged Notices of Appeal and before the Single Judge, The Honourable Mrs. Justice Bracewell, leave to appeal was granted"—

this is the import part—

"on the basis that the coverage by the press of our clients' trial was of such a nature that they may not have received a fair trial. Indeed, the Learned Judge indicated that she was concerned over the volume of press reporting which she was shown and the quality of it…


Whilst we expected a great deal of publicity we did not perhaps anticipate the volume and torrent of publicity which appeared to be overwhelming. The first complaint we have to make on behalf of our clients is that whilst the Prosecution was opening its case and their witnesses giving their evidence, massive publicity attended these events but when it came to the turn of the defence equal prominence in the majority of papers was not given to the defence case.


We take the view, and this will be argued in the Court of Appeal in due course, that the type of headlines employed and not always based on evidence heard in Court could be misleading particularly for a juryman who might not be able to distinguish between what he had heard in Court or what he had read in his paper or seen on television."

The hon. Member for Great Yarmouth (Mr. Carttiss) asked a valid question: what to do about that? The solicitor suggests that one possibility is to adopt the Scottish system, in which reporting restrictions are 10 much tighter. Although I do not rule out the Scottish solution, I think that it would be better to have a code of conduct that is respected. The problem is that the Press Complaints Commission does not regard itself as having a role in such a case. It does not believe that it has the power, the duty or the need to say that such reporting is distorting the criminal justice system and risking the possibility of wrongful conviction. When I have finished citing my examples, I shall expand on what I think has gone wrong with the Press Complaints Commission.

The third case is also relatively recent and involves the tragic suicide of the wife of Sir Allan Green, the former Director of Public Prosecutions who resigned after approaching a prostitute in the King's Cross area. A series of photographs appeared in the papers the day after her body had been found. They showed the prostitute approaching the wife, following her to her front door, and looking at the camera. Several other photographs show the prostitute approaching the wife. They were tragic examples of what goes wrong.

I obtained information about what happened from a newspaper. Initially, the prostitute's pimp approached a newspaper asking for money for information about the prostitute. One newspaper said that it refused to pay. I can only guess at what happened next. Given that the photographs were taken, I suspect that one of two things occurred. The pimp either approached a freelance photographer or a freelance photographer approached the pimp. In my judgment, it is more likely to have been the latter.

The prostitute was taken to the house to await the wife's arrival, at which time she approached her, the underlying story being that she was to say sorry. Photographs were then taken—to the great distress of the wife who refused to talk to the prostitute. I do not believe that money was not paid for that. Whoever organised the encounter, newspapers would have paid the freelance photographer. I am almost certain that part of the money went to the pimp; it probably did not go to the prostitute.

To the great distress of her family and friends, the photographs were used after the wife committed suicide. The article was published, to no obvious story or free-press advantage. After all, the wife was not involved in the incident over which Sir Allan resigned. It was nothing to do with her, besides the fact that she was his wife. The article, however, appeared on the front page. My objection is that the Press Complaints Commission again did nothing, although it is supposed to be the public watchdog.

My fourth example comes from my constituency. The story was published under the headline, "You Bitch" and was about the so-called "Home Alone" mother who went to Spain and left her child in the flat. Hon. Members may have seen the photographs in the paper of the crying mother being brought back from Spain. Incidentally, she was brought back by a newspaper. The article stated: JPEG 11

"But the brassy blonde shrugged off any ideas of returning to the 11-year-old daughter she callously left in London."

Such remarks are the equivalent of stoning. I am not justifying the mother's action; it is beside the point. If we want to encourage people to start stoning others, let us at least do it openly. One does not need to be a psychiatrist or a social worker to realise that both the mother and the child had problems.

The local authorities took out an injunction in respect of the photographs. Pictures of the child were shown on television. Given that the incident happened in my constituency, I began to wonder how those pictures were obtained, when an injunction had been taken out. The Evening Standard published photographs just before the injunction was granted. Completely unjustifiably, it used the names of the child and the mother in its article, thus identifying them. It also used pictures that were obtained either from the girl's school or from an agency, given that the mother was an actress. The girl's school was a private school that taught music and dancing. It offered photographs—or, at least, photographs were obtained from it. That is unacceptable. The other tabloids went to the judge who had issued the injunction and argued that it was unfair to place them under a restriction when the Evening Standard had published the material. The judge said that it seemed unfair and allowed the papers to use the material. He accorded only limited rights, as he forbade them to approach the child. By that time either the head teacher or another person, through the school, had allowed the video of the child in school to be used.

One story quoted a teacher as saying that the child had bruises—I understand the teacher denied saying that. Physical abuse was implied but it was not known whether that was true. It was part of a whipped-up story.

People sometimes ask me why I am trying to set up an independent press authority, as the clause requires, when the Press Complaints Commission already exists. The cases that I have mentioned show that the press is not cleaning up its act and that in each instance, when an obvious case arose for intervention by a public watchdog—as the PCC is supposed to be—it did not even bark, let alone bite. In none of those cases did it say, "Such behaviour is unacceptable." In the Jamie case, for example, it did not say, "This is inaccurate." Apparently we are to wait until an individual makes a complaint. I understand that a complaint about the Jamie case is being made from Liverpool. Imagine what would happen if, because the commission often will not take third party complaints, a friend or relative of the people whom I have mentioned had to make the complaint. Is it fair to place people in that position?

A pro-active body of the kind that clause 1 would set up, is vital to tackle the problem. A crucial point, which I make partly in answer to the hon. Member for Great Yarmouth, is that the independent press authority must be able to take the initiative and declare that reporting, such as that used in the Taylor case for 12 example, is likely to prevent a fair trial. If a body stated that in the public domain, the matter would be debated in that domain. That would put pressure on the press to behave more responsibly. The independent press authority is vital.

Another case that I mention in passing was that of Ann Sole, whose fiance was murdered in Florida. Inaccurate quotations were used and privacy was invaded during the funeral after the body was flown back to Britain. In the case of the Tornado pilot, Flight Lieutenant Nicholl, quotations were invented for his parents on his release from an Iraqi jail. Such cases all show that the Press Complaints Commission is not working. I have spoken at length to save time later in the Committee. My argument is that the commission has not worked and cannot work. An independent body is needed. The heart of the Bill is the independent press authority and its vital joint functions of raising standards and protecting press freedom.

Several people have written to me suggesting the setting up of a support group for people who receive press treatment of the kind that I have described. We are discussing doing so, but I should like to think that that would not be necessary. I hope that the Committee is minded to support the measure. It would be helpful if the Secretary of State would tell us his thoughts about the way in which the Bill should proceed.

Without the structure set out in clause 1, we will not crack the difficult problem of press responsibility and freedom. That is why I have spent some time on it. It is a vital part of the Bill and it is vital that it should be part of any future Government legislation. Without it, we will not be able to deal with the case that I have described. They are not easily dealt with by legislation on privacy or photography. They are best dealt with by a code drawn up by an independent body that has status and support.

The Secretary of State for National Heritage (Mr. Peter Brooke)
: The hon. Member for Hammersmith (Mr. Soley), who opened the clause stand part debate, asked me a specific question and, obviously, I want to respond to it. It may be helpful if I make some remarks on behalf of the Government so that the debate is not prematurely terminated. At the outset, I thank the hon. Member for Hammersmith for his kind remarks about my attendance in Committee.

Clause 1 would give the proposed independent press authority a general remit to seek accuracy in the presentation of news by newspapers and periodicals, and to secure free dissemination of news and information in the public interest, and the promotion of professional and ethical standards.

The Government do not believe that it would be appropriate to establish a body with such a remit. Our attitude to press self-regulation should by now be well known to hon. Members. I set it out at length in the statement that I made to the House on 14 January, giving the Government's initial response to Sir David Calcutt's report, "Review of Press Self-Regulation". I said then that, although the Government accepted Sir David's overall conclusion that the Press Complaints Commission, as currently constituted, was not an effective regulator of the press, we had yet to be persuaded that a statutory complaints tribunal on the lines that he had recommended was necessary or would be appropriate, and that we would be extremely reluctant to go down that route. That remains our view, but let me make it clear that our minds are not closed on this issue. That is why I set out our timetable on 14 January. We have not ruled out the possibility of statutory regulation, nor have we yet ruled out the possibility of a strengthened form of press self-regulation. We shall listen to the debates in this Committee with great interest because they form part of the timetable.

We also look forward to receiving the report by the National Heritage Select Committee on privacy and media intrusion, and to hearing the final views of the press and the Press Complaints Commission on the reforms that they intend to introduce in response to the 12 specific criticisms Sir David Calcutt made of the PCC in his review. For my part, I hope that genuine and far-reaching reforms can be introduced in current self-regulation.
