Clause 1.
—(ADDITIONAL POWERS FOR NATIONAL COAL BOARD.)

Mr. F. V. Corfield
: I beg to move, in page 1, line 7, after '1934', to insert:

'(except as provided in paragraph ( b) of this subsection)'.

I think it would be for the convenience of the Committee if we bracketed with this my following Amendment, to which the first Amendment is merely a paving Amendment, in page 1, line 27, at end insert:

Provided that the Minister shall not give his consent under section 9 of the Continental Shelf Act 1964 to the supply of such gas or petroleum for any of the purposes mentioned in subsection (4) of that section to any company in which the Board has a financial interest unless he is satisfied that the price to be paid is the best price obtainable in the open market and that other companies engaged in similar operations have been given an opportunity to tender for the supply of such gas.

The purpose of this Amendment is fairly obvious. It is first of all to ensure that, whatever the National Coal Board's activities may be in searching for gas and producing it in a crude state, when it comes to sell it it does not use its powers to confer an unfair advantage on either itself or any of its subsidiaries. The Parliamentary Secretary will remember that during the debate on Second Reading we did quote a number of subsidiary companies either wholly or partly owned by the Coal Board which appeared to have in themselves far wider powers than the Coal Board itself was granted under the original Act of 1946.

One of the problems which arise here is in part the refusal of the Government even to attempt to justify their proposition that there will be no unfair competition as a result of this Bill. The Parliamentary Secretary on Second Reading accused us 8 of seeing lions under the bed but one has only to walk along this Corridor where in three successive Rooms one has the Steel Bill being discussed, a vast nationalisation Measure with ramifications far wider than the steel industry, in the next Room the Industrial Reorganisation Corporation Bill with the same sort of result, and here this Bill. This is only part, therefore, of a pattern although the Government have constantly dismissed criticisms that this inevitably introduces an element of unfair competition into any of the private enterprise industries in which these nationalised boards enter.

One has only to quote the very favourable position of the Coal Board in regard to its borrowing powers, one which, it is obvious, can be passed on to subsidiaries, and the Coal Board is in fact doing so. The Coal Board has been able to borrow and then to lend to these organisations at a higher rate. This is something which, at any rate in the present squeeze, is of enormous advantage. Many of these companies, particularly the smaller ones, would probably have great difficulty in raising capital at even very high rates, but the Coal Board is able to step in, borrowing at a favourable rate, and then lending to them.

I think the secondary problem here is the real interpretation of Section 9 of the Continental Shelf Act, 1964. This Bill starts off in Clause 1 with a proposition that anything in the Bill is subject to the Continental Shelf Act, and I put this matter to the Minister on Second Reading and I am bound to say that I did not think the answer I got was very satisfactory. What is clear, I think, is that under Section 9, if the Coal Board or any other undertaking produces gas for use as a fuel, it has to offer it first to the area Gas Board concerned and it is only after the area gas board has had the chance to take that supply at a reasonable price that the Minister is likely to give his consent under Section 9 to its supply outside the gas industry.

The important aspects are in Section 9(3) and (4) which make it clear that totally different considerations arise where it is proposed to supply the gas for a purpose other than fuel. Subsection (3) says:

"The Minister of Power shall not give his consent under this section to the supply of JPEG 9 gas at any premises unless satisfied ( a) that the supply is for industrial purposes and that the Area Board in whose area the premises are situated has been given an opportunity of purchasing the gas at a reasonable price".

This follows the lines of the Petroleum (Production) Act, 1934. Subsection (3) goes on:

"or ( b) that the supply is for such purposes as are mentioned in subsection (4) of this section "—

now these are the important words—"

but shall give his consent under this section to the supply or use of any gas if satisfied that it is for the purposes mentioned in that subsection."

That is to say, in subsection (4), and when we turn to subsection (4) we read:

"The said purposes are industrial purposes which do not consist of or include the use of the gas as a fuel except in so far as the gas is used to provide heat or other energy required ( a) for a process in which the gas is used otherwise than as a fuel; or ( b) where such a process is one of a series, for any further process in the same series, not being a process in which a bulk product is converted into manufactured articles".

Now I must admit that the interpretation of that paragraph is not immediately obvious, but as I read the subsection as a whole it appears that if the Coal Board, for example, is intent on supplying this gas for a purely industrial, non-fuel purpose, for petroleum chemicals, although it may have to obtain the formal consent of the Minister, he has no discretion in the matter. The word "shall" is quite specific. In my view the Minister was quite wrong in his reply to me during the Second Reading debate when he said that this was something over which he had control and that he would not in fact give that consent. If I am right it fallows that should the Coal Board be disposed to supply its subsidiaries, Staveley Chemicals, for example, it is free to do this on terms much more favourable than the terms on which other chemical companies might be able to obtain a supply in the open market. The purpose of this Amendment is to prevent that.

While the Parliamentary Secretary is replying to the principle—and I hope he will not tell us there are lions under the bed—I hope he will also refer to the important legal aspect, namely, what is the meaning of Section 9(3) and (4) of the Continental Shelf Act. It seems to 10 me that this opens wide the door to an extension of the Coal Board's powers.

The second aspect which I hope the Parliamentary Secretary will dwell upon is the extent to which Section 1 of the Coal Industry Nationalisation Act, 1946, produces an extension of the powers under this Bill which are not apparent on the face of the Bill. Section 1 of that Act defines the powers of the National Coal Board and subsection (1) sets out that

"There shall be a National Coal Board which shall, on and after the primary vesting date, be charged with the duties of"—

and these are set out and they are all duties, broadly speaking, connected with the getting and making available of coal—

"working and getting the coal in Great Britain, to the exclusion (save as in this Act provided) of any other person".

This, of course, gives the Board a monopoly position. Then subsection (1, b) says:

"Securing the efficient development of the coal mining industry; and ( c) making supplies of coal available, of such qualities and sizes, in such quantities and at such prices, as may seem to them best calculated to further the public interest in all respects".

Whether the Board is achieving that is a matter on which we are probably divided; and if it is not, whether it ought to come into these other spheres is a matter on which I think this side of the Committee would differ with the other side.

The important problem is to what extent the very wide subsidiary powers in Section 1(2) of the Coal Industry Nationalisation Act extend the powers of the Coal Board in connection with any natural gas exploration or exploitation following from this Bill.

Subsection (2) states:

"The functions of the National Coal Board … shall include the carrying on of all such activities as it may appear to the Board to be requisite, advantageous or convenient for them to carry on for or in connection with the discharge of their duties under the preceding subsection, and in particular, but without prejudice to the generality of this Section, ( a) searching and boring for coal in Great Britain".

Then there are ( b) and ( c), but the important one is ( d) which states:

"producing or manufacturing any goods or utilities which are of a kind required by the JPEG 11 Board for or in connection with the working and getting of coal or any other of their activities, or which can advantageously be produced or manufactured by the Board by reason of their having materials or facilities for the production or manufacture thereof in connection with the working and getting of coal or any other of their activities, and supplying and selling goods or utilities so produced or manufactured".

When I first studied this matter it seemed to me that "or any other of their activities" must include, after this Bill gets the Royal Assent, activities connected with natural gas or petroleum, and if one looks back nearer the beginning of the subsection where it says

"without prejudice to the generality of this section"

I think it gives a good deal more force to that argument.

On second thoughts I confess I felt that probably the very opening words of the subsection which refer to

"such activities as it may appear to the Board to be requisite, advantageous or convenient for them to carry on for or in connection with the discharge of their duties"

under subsection (1), might confine that, but what I think is disturbing is that a number of leading industrial firms in the chemical industry tell me their legal advisers to take the view which I originally drew.

I think the Government should tell us firmly what is the intention here, and come along on Report with Amendments to make that abundantly clear, and not leave the matter to vague assurances from Ministers which may or may not be based on a correct interpretation of the law as it stands.

Member raised the question of the duties of the Coal Board and how far its powers under the Coal Industry Nationalisation Act, 1946, would enable it to range far and wide in the exploitation of natural gas. Investment undertaken by Staveley Chemicals is related to the main activities of the Board. Further, Staveley Chemicals is not a subsidiary of the Coal Board as the Coal Board has only a 45 per cent. interest in it.

This is a complex matter, and we have been so conscientious in our drafting that we have been caught out in limiting the powers and have had to table a technical Amendment. We have deliberately not made possible a wider diversification of JPEG 13 the Coal Board's activities and therefore we cannot accept the restrictions which this Amendment would place on it.
