Draft Functions of Traffic Wardens

(Amendment) Order 1993

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Charles Wardle)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Functions of Traffic Wardens (Amendment) Order 1993.

It is good to see you, Mr. Hill, on this spring afternoon. I intend to be brief in my opening remarks.

The order amends the Functions of Traffic Wardens Order 1970 to give new powers to traffic wardens, as foreshadowed in the Road Traffic Act 1991. The order will allow wardens to be of greater help to the police in the enforcement of parking offences, thereby releasing police time for Functions of Traffic wardens other duties. It applies to all police forces in England and Wales, but is especially relevant to the Metropolitan police. [Hon. Members: "Hear, hear."] I am grateful for the appreciation.

I shall explain in straightforward language the changes that the order will make. The order extends the powers and functions of traffic wardens in four ways. The first is a minor amendment; the other three are more significant changes. First, article 2 adds subparagraph (f) to article 3(2) of the 1970 order and relates to the interim disposal of vehicles under section 99 of the Road Regulation Act 1984. This is a small amendment to ensure that traffic wardens as well as constables can deal with delivery to the local authority of abandoned vehicles removed under the regulations made under section 99. As the explanatory note states, the Removal and Disposal of Vehicles (Amendment) Regulations 1993, which come into force on 1 June, empower a traffic warden to authorise the removal of vehicles that are in breach of parking regulations. That is an order made by my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Transport.

Secondly, by article 2(g), the order gives traffic wardens the power to authorise the wheel clamping of illegally parked vehicles on public roads. Clamping on public roads is authorised only for a designated area in central London. At present, only a police officer can authorise clamping; as a consequence, police time is tied up supervising that part of parking enforcement. This arrangement is not the best use of police resources. Traffic wardens who deal with parking control day by day are capable of making proper decisions about when it is appropriate to have a vehicle clamped. Police officers are guided by force instructions in this and the same guidance will apply to wardens. I stress that the order relates only to clamping on public roads; it has nothing to do with private land.

Thirdly, article 4 will allow traffic wardens to issue fixed penalty notices for three additional parking offences, two of which are endorsable. They are causing an unnecessary obstruction, parking within the limits of a pedestrian 4 crossing and leaving a vehicle in a dangerous position. The latter two are endorsable. At present, if a warden detects any of those offences, he or she has to rely on a police officer to deal with it. If a warden comes across a vehicle that has been left within the limits of a pedestrian crossing, he cannot do anything about it; he must find a police officer and ask him to help. The offences are, nevertheless, parking offences with which wardens are familiar. The endorsable offences are more serious than other parking offences; they carry a maximum penalty of a £1,000 fine on conviction by a court and three obligatory penalty points. Under the fixed penalty process, the penalty is £40 and three penalty points, if the owner of the vehicle admits guilt and pays up on the fixed penalty system rather than contesting the matter in court. The offence of causing an unnecessary obstruction carries a maximum penalty on conviction of a £1,000 fine. When dealt with under the fixed penalty system, the charge is £30 in London and £20 elsewhere.

Fourthly, under article 3, a traffic warden's power to require an offender to produce his or her driving licence will be extended. Traffic wardens will not have a general power to do so, but will be able to require the production only in defined circumstances. The additional circumstances will be when dealing with the two endorsable parking offences that I have mentioned. The power is already available to them when discharging custody functions at a car pound. Therefore, if someone turns up at the car pound, the traffic warden who is there is entitled to ask to see that person's licence. In the other two situations that I described, the warden may now make the same request.

The new functions proposed in the order provide chief officers of police in England and Wales with the means to increase the role of their traffic warden forces in day to day parking enforcement. Police officers will be able to focus their time on duties of greater priority. However, it is not a change that we make lightly. We recognise that it is important that the new duties to be carried out by wardens are discharged in a proper way and that the training of traffic wardens in the new duties is essential for the handover. We shall stress the importance of training to chief officers of police in the advice that we issue to them about the new order. I believe that the new arrangements have a significant contribution to make to the police service. Therefore, I commend the order to the Committee.

A couple of years ago, he left his flat and found his car missing. He reported the loss to the police and made arrangements to travel to his constituency by other means. The third set of offences is the least controversial. The first item in that set concerns unnecessary obstruction caused by a vehicle but, as the Minister noted, the other two are more serious—parking within the limits of a pedestrian crossing and leaving a vehicle in a dangerous position. It is sensible that traffic wardens should be able to tackle those infringements as that will help to deal with them more quickly than if wardens have to involve a police officer. My one reservation is that those offences seem to be committed increasingly often. In my home town of Penarth, the regularity with which the parking restrictions are cavalierly disregarded makes the traffic warden's job very necessary. The offences show a lack of consideration and cause inconvenience, and the latter two particularly put elderly and disabled people and children in special danger.

6 A change that allows traffic wardens to deal with those infringements more speedily is welcome. However, there is a danger that the message will be sent out that the offences are less serious—that they will be put down the tariff—because they have become the responsibility of traffic wardens. I hope that the Minister will assure the Committee that they are still considered to be very serious offences which should be discouraged, and that the order is intended to strengthen the law, not to minimise the seriousness of the offences. Moreover, I take it that traffic wardens' training and their responsibility to the chief constable or commissioner—referred to by the Minister—would mean that their evidence would be as effective as that of police officers, and would be regarded by the courts as such.

I should be grateful if the Minister would also clarify the position on the production of documents. He referred to the production of a licence. I wonder whether that was meant to be an example of how a traffic warden might exercise such powers. Presumably not only the licence, but insurance and other relevant documents could be required by a traffic warden, provided that the legislation were amended.

On first reading, the legislation seems to offer some common-sense developments in existing practice. We seek an assurance that the legislation will not diminish the importance of combating the lack of consideration and the danger to the public involved in some offences, that the accountability of traffic wardens, through the relevant local senior police officer and ultimately through the chief constable or the commissioner, will continue and that the training given to traffic wardens will be commensurate with their powers and the extension of their responsibilities.

There are fears—encouraged by the comments of some of the Minister's colleagues—that there will be some sort of salami-slicing of police force responsibilities. It makes sense for functions that do not necessarily require the expertise, training and experience of a police officer to be undertaken by traffic wardens, or in certain circumstances by civilians within the police service who have appropriate training. The Opposition and the public in general are worried that the logical extension of that may be to take functions away from the police service. It is important to maintain the integrity of the service, while it is also appropriate that the right sort of person with the right sort of training should undertake the less onerous responsibilities within the police force. I agree with the Minister that that should occur wherever possible to free police officers for the more serious and onerous duties for which they have received training and of which they have gained experience.

If one takes away responsibilities and diffuses them outside the direct responsibility of the police force, there is a danger of the service losing its integrity in the local area and of its losing the public's respect. Over the years, the function of traffic wardens has developed and is now well understood. It is understood that traffic wardens work within the police service, and that they should be respected and have authority. Systems in towns have developed. For example, an excellent system has developed recently in Cardiff whereby money raised through the local authority's approach to parking restrictions provides additional money to employ traffic wardens, who receive respect. There is a beneficial circle as opposed to the vicious circle that often occurs in other areas. I hope that the Minister JPEG 7 will be able to reassure us about the Government's long-term intentions on those matters, which are the backdrop against which the regulations need to be seen.
