Broad Economic Policy Guidelines

10.30 am

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Miss Melanie Johnson)
: The purpose of the broad economic policy guidelines is to ensure that member states' economic policies are consistent with the goals of the treaty of Rome. That includes non-inflationary economic growth respecting the environment, high employment and social protection and a raised standard of living and quality of life.

In accordance with article 99(2) of the treaty, the European Commission has adopted a recommendation on the broad economic policy guidelines—BEGs—for the member states and the European Union, for adoption at the Economic and Finance Council on 19 June 2000. The guidelines set out the Commission's views on priorities for the coming year and will, once adopted by ECOFIN in the margins of the Feira European Council, provide the reference text for the Commission and the Council to review economic developments in member states and the EU generally.

The debate to discuss the draft recommendations, in which I am more than happy to participate, was requested by the European Scrutiny Committee. I would be most grateful if hon. Members noted carefully that the draft represents the Commission's work only. The draft guidelines have subsequently been discussed and revised by member states as part of the drafting process. I welcome the hon. Member for West Dorset (Mr. Letwin) to his seat and say how much I regret that the late finish last night has—apparently—proved too much for him this morning.

The UK has been keen to develop the role of the BEGs, which are the key economic policy co-ordination tools of the EU. The 2000 guidelines do a good job of reflecting our main priorities, in particular following up the Lisbon Council and promoting the wider economic reform agenda. It is essential that the BEGs reflect fully the importance of structural reform in tackling successfully the challenges of globalisation and competitiveness, promoting employment and social inclusion and improving the performance of the European economies. We have also emphasised the importance of making the objectives sharper, setting milestones for the implementation of reforms, benchmarking good practice and developing structural reform indicators as a priority, and for putting those objectives into practice through policy.

The background to this year's BEGs is an environment in which, as a result of efforts to complete the internal market, previously over-regulated industries have been liberalised and exposed to 2 competitive forces. The Commission notes the successful introduction of the euro and the establishment of a comprehensive framework for economic policy co-ordination. The draft guidelines also note that an increasingly strong economic recovery has taken hold across the EU owing to cyclical factors, supportive macro-economic policies and an improved structural policy environment.

The 2000 BEGs are centred on, and give operational content to, the conclusions on economic subjects of the March 2000 Lisbon Special Economic Council. They are designed to focus on the medium and long-term implications of structural policies and on reforms aimed at promoting economic growth potential, employment and social cohesion, as well as on the transition toward a knowledge-based economy.

In view of that focus, the draft guidelines develop four key challenges for European policy: to restore full employment, to promote the transition to a knowledge-based economy, to prepare for population ageing and to improve social cohesion. To achieve those aims, the Commission has proposed specific recommendations under the following broad headings.

The first aim is to ensure growth and stability-oriented macro-economic policies to position the economy on a sustained, higher, non-inflationary growth and employment path. The second is to maintain sound public finances to allow the full working of automatic stabilisers. The third is to improve the quality of public finances to restrain expenditure and lower the tax burden, and to improve their sustainability for the medium and long term. The fourth is to promote appropriate wage developments by allowing wage-cost developments in member states to reflect differences in economic and employment situations.

The fifth aim is to foster a knowledge-based society by reforming product capital and labour markets to promote essential conditions for innovation and technology diffusion. The sixth is to ensure efficient product markets to reinforce the impulse given to structural reform by the Cardiff process, especially in the markets for services. The seventh is to promote efficient capital markets to improve the allocation of capital and reduce its cost. The eighth is to invigorate labour markets to tackle the problems of employment and fully grasp the benefits of the knowledge-based economy. The final aim is to enhance sustainable development by promoting environmentally friendly technologies and exploiting the efficiency gains of market-based instruments.

The Commission produced a report—for the first time—on the implementation of the 1999 BEGs. According to its recommendation, the guidelines for 2000 have drawn significantly on the findings of the implementation report. The Government believe that that is a very important step in consolidating progress made to date, and it should help to embed the notion of economic reform in Europe in the minds of all concerned. Not only does that help in the overall formulation, presentation and relevance of the guidelines, which allow people to see the results of JPEG 3 reform and the changes that are being made, it helps to provide a firmer grounding for the development of the following year's guidelines. For that reason, we strongly support the decision to draw on the findings of the implementation report when developing guidelines for the coming year.

The Commission's overall assessment is that the implementation of the 1999 guidelines is "encouraging", although it notes that the degree of implementation has varied across member states. It considers that those member states that have pursued a comprehensive reform strategy in line with broad economic guidelines have achieved the best economic performances. Furthermore, last year was the first time that the broad economic guidelines included country-specific recommendations for each member state. They implicitly acknowledge the fact that different states face different and sometimes unique challenges.

The Government share the Commission's decision to emphasise the key objectives of sustainable non-inflationary growth and high levels of employment. Our policies for achieving those are consistent with BEGs. We need sound macro-economic policies that are based on well managed public finances and low inflation; economic reform, including improvements in the workings of goods and services markets; modernisation of the labour market through improved training and by introducing better incentives for the low paid to work; better regulation; and promotion of entrepreneurship.

The specific, albeit non-binding, recommendations that apply to the United Kingdom are also consistent with the Government's approach to economic policy. They emphasise the need to secure sound public finances and improve the workings of product, capital and labour markets. The general direction of the recommendations is in line with the Government's stated policy objectives and the measures taken since coming into office. Approving the motion will allow the UK to play a constructive role in encouraging economic reform, which builds on the results of the Lisbon Special European Council, and to participate fully in the important process of economic policy co-ordination as provided for in articles 99 and 104 of the treaty. I look forward to the debate.
