Draft Education (No. 2) (Northern Ireland) Order 1996

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Michael Ancram)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Education (No. 2) (Norhern Ireland) Order 1996.

The order, which was laid before the House on 3 December 1996, is basically an enabling order. Part II provides for the introduction to Northern Ireland of a grant scheme for the education of children below compulsory school age. Royal Assent has been given to corresponding legislation for England, Wales and Scotland, and that will come into effect in April 1997. Unfortunately, plans to introduce the scheme in Northern Ireland in September have had to be postponed to maintain and protect mainstream school and other education priorities. The Government remain committed to the scheme and it is important that legislative provision should be made for parents and children in Northern Ireland to participate in the scheme as soon as the necessary financial resources can be made available.

The order's provisions are enabling powers under which the administrative and inspection arrangements for the scheme can be established in due course. I will outline the main purpose of those powers briefly.

Under article 3, the Department of Education for Northern Ireland will be able to make grants for preschool education. Eligibility for vouchers under the scheme will be determined by the date of the child's fourth birthday. The amount and frequency of the grant payment will be determined by the Department in line with the national level of grant for those entitled to a voucher for a full year. The order will also enable the Department to specify standards that providers must meet, including on accommodation, staffing ratios, staff qualifications and curriculum. Providers will be inspected by the education and training inspectorate to ensure that they are suitable for registration under the scheme.

The order will also allow the Department to delegate arrangements for the issue and redemption of vouchers to the Voucher Company, which will be employed by the UK Department for Education and Employment for this purpose. The Department of Health and Social Services in Northern Ireland will be able to provide social security information to enable eligible children to be identified and their families contacted about participation. These arrangements are 4 necessary to ensure that the benefits of the national scheme will be available to the children of Northern Ireland when the necessary resources can be secured.

Part III consists of provisions relating to the admission of children to grant-aided schools in Northern Ireland. That is basically a revision of existing provisions in part IV of the Education Reform (Northern Ireland) Order 1989. It contains no underlying change in open enrolment policy.

The main purpose of the new provisions is to rectify ambiguities in existing open enrolment legislation and thus to clarify and strengthen existing law. The new provisions will clarify the parental preferences constrained by the physical capacity of schools and make it clear that schools should, other than in specified circumstances, admit pupils up to the limit of their enrolment numbers. They will also prohibit schools from using the order of preference expressed by parents as part of their admissions criteria. That will be fairer to parents and will complement the new transport arrangements introduced for 1997–98, which limit transport assistance to pupils who are unable to gain a place in a suitable school within statutory walking distance of their home.

Mr. Roy Beggs:
(East Antrim)
: How frequently is there a revision of the enrolment numbers, particularly in primary schools, to take account of population growth in particular areas and of the fact that overgenerous provision appears to be made in some areas, enabling children in various parts of the Province, with rather low scores in the transfer procedure, to obtain grammar school places? That gives an impression of discrimination in certain areas.

Mr. Ancram
: I shall be corrected if I am wrong, but usually the enrolment and capacity figures are examined annually, though they rarely change, for obvious reasons. Schools tend to be tied, to a great extent, by their physical capacity, and the enrolment figures for each year are worked out on the basis of a school's total capacity. As to grammar school places, the fact that there are not too many of those is very much a part of the selective system. The point of selection is to try to ensure that a limited number of places is available for those children who achieve a particular level in the transfer tests. Therefore, we scrutinise closely any suggestions for more grammar school places. Where they are justified—a strong case has been made in Londonderry in the past few years, for instance—we allow for expansion. We also consider numbers at the request of schools, which is sensible.

Article 23 provides a revision of article 52 of the 1986 order, which deals with transport assistance. The proposals would enable the Department of Education to take the lead in determining the transport arrangements that are considered necessary to facilitate pupils' attendance at grant-aided schools and that of relevant students at institutes of further education. Such transport would continue to be provided free of charge for those pupils and students eligible for transport assistance.

Mr. Tony Worthington

Mr. Ancram
: The amendment will not create the saving. To a great extent, savings are being achieved already, through changes that did not require statutory provision. I shall discuss those. We estimate those changes to have produced savings of about £3 million a year. The provision that I have been describing allows charges to be made for children who are over compulsory school age. It is an enabling provision and the amount saved will depend on the level and extent of charges, which it does not specify.

The amendment enables the boards, in accordance with arrangements approved by the Department of Education, to provide transport assistance for those pupils at grant-aided schools and relevant students attending institutions of further education for whom transport is not considered necessary—that will be the test. In those circumstances, the new article provides for boards to charge for any transport that is provided and for charges to be remitted in certain circumstances. The possible need to introduce such a charge arises because of an escalation in transport costs, to which the hon. Member for Clydebank and Milngavie (Mr. Worthington), referred. They have increased from about £23 million in the early 1990s to a figure approaching £38 million at present. Those figures are considerable, as I am sure the hon. Member for East Antrim (Mr. Beggs) will understand, in the context of the savings that must be made. We calculate that if the present trend continues, charges could increase to £40 million by 1998–99. Such expense diverts badly needed finance from the classroom.

I have already taken action to contain expenditure, but it may be necessary to do more, which is the reason for the enabling provision. It will give us the option of introducing charges for transport in certain circumstances, should it be judged necessary for protecting classroom provision.

Article 23 makes minor consequential amendments to schedule 13 of the 1986 order and articles 130 and 137 of the 1989 order. Those amendments do not make any substantive change to the existing legislation.

Article 25 introduces some technical amendments to education legislation to remove some impediments to one type of contract, which may be appropriate under the private finance initiative. Previous powers were drafted at a time when such contracts were not envisaged, and some adjustments are necessary.

The PFI may apply in a variety of ways in the education sector, but one particular possibility is being explored. It is the provision of school accommodation under a contract whereby the private sector would design, build, finance, operate and maintain the school premises and provide a variety of associated services. In theory, that could include all aspects except teaching.

The existing powers for voluntary maintained schools need to be adjusted because they prescribe that the education and library boards must provide some of those services for those schools. They also prescribe separate funding mechanisms for recurrent and capital grant for voluntary maintained and voluntary grammar schools which would be difficult to apply in respect of a private finance initiative contract. No changes are needed in existing powers to facilitate PFI contracts for controlled schools or further education colleges.

I must stress that the powers will come into operation only if and when a contract is agreed between the relevant contracting authority and the individual school and is approved by the Department. That will arise only if the contract is affordable and JPEG 7 represents good value for money and if it incorporates the important principles of risk transfer that are central to the private finance initiative.

The contract might embrace one, several or a larger number of schools, and the provision on the alteration of school premises could include projects that address a specific aspect of a school's need, such as a heating system. The definition introduced in the proposed article is not intended to be restrictive.

It is not a question of the Government wondering, "Do we like you?" It is for parents to decide that they want to use the system. That is a perfectly right and proper way of extending choice and it will certainly increase provision for pre-school education for four-year-olds and improve the quality of pre-school places, because of the inspection systems that will be set up. It will extend parental choice. Judging by what is happening in England now, the signs are that all those objectives will be realised.

The hon. Member for Clydebank and Milngavie asked me about the cost of not going ahead with the scheme. We have negotiated an opt-in clause to the national contract. Opt-in will occur only when the Government decide to go ahead with the scheme and that is not due to happen at present, so no costs have been or are to be incurred.

I am delighted that the hon. Gentleman maintains his opposition to the order, because that shows that, like me, he is confident that the Government will still be Conservative after the general election and will be applying the scheme in Northern Ireland when resources permit.
