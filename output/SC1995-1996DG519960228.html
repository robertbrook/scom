Central Manchester Development Corporation (Area and Constitution) Order 1996

4.38 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Sir Paul Beresford)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Central Manchester Development Corporation (Area and Constitution) Order 1996.

The order is required to wind up the Central Manchester development corporation. It revokes the 1988 order, which designated the urban development area and established the development corporation. The order will take effect in two stages: the part of the order that designated the urban development area is to be revoked on 1 April 1996, and the part that established the corporation is to be revoked on 1 July 1996. That will allow hon. Members who wish to visit the area to do so. Under this two-stage process, the corporation will cease to exercise operational functions after 31 March 1996; it is therefore appropriate for the urban development area status to be removed on that date. The corporation will have three months in which to produce its final accounts and report, and to wind up its affairs. It will finally be dissolved on 1 July.

Two other orders have been laid before the House. One transfers the CMDC's planning functions to the appropriate local authorities on 25 March 1996; the other transfers to the Secretary of State for the Environment those of the corporation's property rights and liabilities that have not been disposed of by 26 March. The orders are subject to the negative resolution procedure. A dissolution order is also required, but it is not subject to parliamentary procedure.

I should like to list the corporation's considerable achievements during the past eight years. It is precisely because the corporation has achieved so much, and set the stage for continuing regeneration, that we are able to wind it up.

The corporation was set up in June 1988 with a remit to revitalise, and to bring back private investment to, an area that was once the powerhouse of Manchester but which had become run-down and neglected. It is an area of 187 hectares south of the city centre, running from the canal basins behind Piccadilly station in the east to Castlefield and the River Irwell in the west. In 1988, that area had many underused and obsolescent buildings, derelict sites and neglected streets and waterways. It was not attracting market interest commensurate with its location or its potential.

Attempts to regenerate the central part of the city, such as the Phoenix study undertaken by a group of local business men, had failed to turn things around. However, the advent of the development corporation brought together public and private sector interests to 4 boost the fortunes of this great city. Two of the local business men involved in the Phoenix initiative, Raymond Gerrard and the late Ken Alford, were invited on to the board of the corporation.

Furthermore, from the beginning, the corporation established a close working relationship with the city council—a partnership which has grown stronger in the years since. Three council members have been on the board since 1988. They are Graham Stringer, Labour leader of the city council, who last year said:

"this development corporation is in danger of giving quangos a good name",

Jack Flanagan, who is also chairman of Manchester airport, and Joyce Hill, the then leader of the Conservative group. The effectiveness of the corporation has been all the greater for the chairmanship of James Grigor, who spent much of his career as an industrial chemist in Trafford Park, and the deputy chairmanship of Derek Boothman, who has brought financial expertise to the board.

The corporation produced an initial strategy document, called "Opportunities", which set out its vision for the area and attracted early interest and early investment. After extensive public consultation, a development strategy was published in 1990, identifying the need to tackle environmental decay, to boost tourism and jobs and to bring housing back to the city centre.

The corporation has succeeded on all those fronts. Anyone who has seen the area recently, as I have, cannot fail to be impressed by the transformation. New housing has been built where there was none before. There are large new commercial and leisure developments, as well as many successful conversions of previously neglected buildings. Canals have been restored and high-quality landscaping introduced, and that has inspired confidence in investors. Tourism has been boosted by the development of new facilities and by the general uplift in the area.

By the end of March, 35 hectares of previously derelict or unused land will have been reclaimed, more than 144,000 sq m of new floor space will have been developed, and nearly 2,600 homes will have been built. Furthermore, more than 5,000 new jobs will have been created, and private sector investment of £376 million will have been levered in. For every £1 of public money invested by the corporation, the private sector will have invested nearly £4. That is an impressive achievement.

A splendid example of the effective partnerships that the corporation has formed with local authorities and with the private sector is the new international concert hall, the Bridgewater hall, which is due to open this autumn. It was conceived and financed in partnership with the city council, together with substantial backing from the European regional development fund. It is the centrepiece for the regeneration of an entire sector of the city wtih new offices, hotels and homes.

An important part of the corporation's work has been to bring people and vitality back to the city centre, by encouraging developers to create new housing schemes; many of them are in old, restored buildings. In 1988, the population of the urban development area was just 250. Now, nearly 4,000 people live in the area. Some 1,000 new homes are in the Whitworth "village", which was JPEG 5 once a run-down area, but is now a vibrant community with support services and popular bars, pubs and restaurants.

The work that the corporation has done to restore the city's canals and to improve the urban environment has proved an important catalyst and has been important in all senses, but especially for housing developments. Two outstanding developments are at Castle quay, which was created from a former cotton warehouse, and, close to it, the new Slate wharf development of around 100 waterside flats. The corporation has also helped to meet the growing demand for accommodation for students. It has contributed £4 million in city grant to create the Dunlop "village", which houses more than 1,000 students. The housing market in central Manchester has proved to be one of the most buoyant in Britain, with nearly 98 per cent. of completed homes already occupied. Demand for new housing sites in the city centre continues apace, and developers are now confident of success without further need for grant support. That is a measure of the corporation's success.

The corporation has succeeded in capitalising on the rich legacy of old buildings and historic canals in its area. It recognised from the outset the importance of improving the environment in order to unlock the regeneration opportunities and leisure potential, and to stimulate private sector investment. To that end, the corporation has spent some £17 million on environmental improvements, such as clearing canals, restoring towpaths and putting in new landscaping. Much of that work has been in the Castlefield area, which has been transformed into a major leisure and tourist attraction based around the historic canal network. Attractions include the Granada studios tour—for which my daughter, a fan of some of their productions, has booked—the museum of science and industry; a new youth hostel which has already attracted 8,500 visitors, over half of whom are from abroad; an outdoor events arena and a visitors' centre. The environmental improvement works undertaken by the corporation have helped to restore confidence among private sector investors and have greatly improved the overall image of the area.

The corporation has also succeeded in stimulating new jobs and businesses. Many underused buildings have been converted into quality offices and hotels, of varying sizes, and large new prestigious offices have been built. The corporation has attracted expanding companies from within and outside Manchester. Since it started its work, more than 98,000 sq m of offices have opened or are under way.

Community programmes have been another important part of the corporation's business. It has, for example, contributed to schemes to upgrade job skills, to help to make people more litter conscious, and to provide facilities for disabled youngsters. It has been concerned to work with people of all ages and from all walks of life, and to help strengthen the management of voluntary bodies.

There is no doubt that the CMDC has been a success. It has restored the confidence of private sector investors, stimulated new jobs and homes and provided a cleaner, safer environment. The influence and successes of the corporation have been instrumental in the formulation 6 of wider strategies for the continued urban renaissance of the city, and in the development of initiatives such as city pride. Under that initiative, the Government invited the local authorities in Manchester, Birmingham and London to bring together public and private sector interests to develop a strategy for their cities over the next 10 years. The corporation has played a key role in producing Manchester's prospectus, which will have wide-ranging benefits for the whole community. The corporation has also helped the city's successful bid to host the Commonwealth games.

The people of Manchester can be especially proud of the corporation's achievements. Many parts of central Manchester that in 1988 were neglected and decaying are today making a vital contribution to a city that has found a new confidence and a new vision for its future. That done, it is right to wind up the corporation, and that is why the order is necessary. I commend it to the Committee.

Members who have spoken and who all know the area accept that the development corporation has done a huge job under difficult circumstances. The hon. Member for Stretford pointed out that it was action by all parties that explains that success. To some degree that backs the point made by my hon. Friend the Member for Bury, South, but not necessarily for this area.

The difference in approach between the hon. Member for Leicester, East and myself builds on the success of development corporations such as Central Manchester. Manchester council has been building on that as well.

I have been asked about the future. The future must involve partnership and bringing in the private sector, following on and utilising the expertise that has developed. Indeed, the expertise from these development corporations and similar organisations has moved into the private sector, into local government and into other projects.

I should like to give a few pointers about Manchester's future. There is the Hulme city challenge into which £37.5 million has gone; £75 million has gone towards the Olympics; another £18 million has gone to Moss Side and Hulme SRB. We have received some applications for SRB relating to city pride. This development corporation is a fine example of the approach which the Government made in the early days. It has been extremely successful and it is one that we have built on.

Today we have given some accolades. Considerable work has been undertaken by the members and the staff of the Central Manchester development corporation over the last eight years. It has been an enormous effort which has been enormously successful. It has brought in partners who have been a key part of it. But the time has now come to wind up the corporation after an extremely successful period, and I therefore commend the order to the Committee.

Question put and agreed to.
