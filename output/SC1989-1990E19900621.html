Mr. Lilley
:I am sure that the hon. Gentleman realises that the clause introduces a new basis for tax relief for expenditure on preparing and making good landfill sites for waste disposal. For restoration payments, the full amount of the payment will be allowed in the year in which it is made. Relief for expenditure on preparing a site will be spread over the lifetime of a site in proportion to the site capacity used up in any year.

We are introducing this relief to restore what seems to have been the status quo in practice and to clarify and regularise the position, following the decision of the Court of Appeal in the case of Rolfe v. Wimpey Waste Management Ltd. The purpose is not to alter the treatment of what has become referred to in the Committee as airspace, which is a separate issue for which we did not believe we should give a separate relief. However, we thought it appropriate to give relief for restoration and preparation payments. We believe that has the general support of the Committee on environmental grounds. Where such payments are required under the terms of licence, they should be an allowable expense for relief.

Some Government amendments, some of which we have already discussed and others that we are now discussing or are about to discuss, are intended to clarify this matter, because the licensing arrangements will alter slightly. Furthermore, before the new regime of licences, there was some ambiguity about the period in which the relief, as against the licence, was available.

Several amendments tabled by the Opposition and by my hon. Friends were in tune with the Government's recognition of the need to improve the clause. I hope that our amendments meet the spirit of theirs. I think that Government amendments Nos. 166,168 and JPEG 361 241 render unnecessary amendment No. 78, which was tabled by my hon. Clause 70
FUTURES AND OPTIONS: EXEMPTIONS

Mr. Nicholas Brown
(Newcastle upon Tyne, East)

(2A) The following section shall be inserted after s842 of the Taxes Act 1988—

"842A (1) An investment trust shall be exempt from tax under Case 1 Schedule D in respect of income if the income is derived from transactions relating to futures contracts or options contracts.


(2) For the purposes of subsection (1) above a contract is not prevented from being a futures contract or an options contract by the fact that any party is or may be entitled to receive or liable to make, or intitled to receive and liable to make, only a payment of a sum (as apposed to a transfer of assets other than money) in full settlement of all obligations.".'.

I am happy to move the amendment tabled by the hon. Member for Eastwood (Mr. Stewart) because I support it. When the Committee discussed clause 47, my hon. Friend the Member for Islington, South and Finsbury (Mr. Smith) said that the Opposition would support it. The amendment is unexceptional—it simply seeks to ensure that investment trust companies are covered by clause 70. How far does the Financial Secretary believe that pension funds should go in trading in futures and options? It is acceptable that such funds hedge their portfolios to achieve a balance, but some pension funds go further, and trade aggressively.

Mr. Lilley
:The underlying clause allows pension funds to receive appropriate tax treatment so that they are no longer precluded from using futures and options more widely. That will be beneficial. Pension funds are restricted, by regulatory means, in the way that they invest pensioners' money. That is a more appropriate method of ensuring that they are prudently operated than the tax system. It is desirable that a number of institutions deal in futures and options so that the market is broad and deep and remains in this country. No one wants any institution to abuse its freedom, but that is a matter for the regulatory, rather than the fiscal, authorities.

Amendment No. 13, moved in a generous bipartisan gesture by the hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, East (Mr. Brown), would exclude profits from transactions made in futures and options by investment trusts from any charge to tax as trading income. I fear that it would go further than is intended by either my hon. Friend the Member for Eastwood (Mr. Stewart) or the hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, East. The amendment reflects unease over existing involvements by investment trusts in shares and securities, but it is not confined to them. Companies which are generaly accepted as dealers in futures and options but which have no investments in shares and securities would not be prevented from trying to adopt investment trust status to secure exemption from tax on dealing income from derivatives. Such companies would not resemble the normal conception of investment trust, but they could secure that status merely by having a small income from shares and securities. Their dealing in futures and options would be ignored. No regulatory constraints exist to stop that happening. Investment trusts are not subject to restrictions similar to those faced by authorised unit trusts on the use of futures and options.

Although I recognise that the Opposition and my hon. Friend the Member for Eastwood want investment trusts to be free, I explained in an earlier response that we believe that their freedom under the existing arrangements is sufficient to enable them to be involved in futures and options. Therefore, the amendment is unnecessary.

Mr. Lilley
:As the hon. Gentleman recognised, the amendments seek to disapply, for the purposes of group income, rules which clause 74 disapplies for the purpose of capital gains.

There are important anti-avoidance provisions to counter attempts to exploit the "group income" rules by means of artificial arrangements to gain an objectionable tax advantage. Under these "group income" rules, dividends and other distributions can pass between a subsidiary and its parent without advance corporation tax having to be accounted for. These anti-avoidance provisions are necessary to prevent the loss of what might be a very large amount of tax. They are designed to ensure that the benefit of the group income rules is not available where, in commercial terms, two companies are not, or are no longer, in group relationship, but in legal terms they are both group members.

It is possible to relax the equivalent provisions for capital gains in certain ways, as proposed in clause 74, because there are additional rules which impose a capital gains charge when a company actually leaves a group. There is no equivalent "degrouping" charge for group income and, given the different nature of a disposal of a capital asset and the way income arises, it JPEG 369 would not be possible to have such an equivalent measure. In those circumstances, it would not be right to do what the amendment suggests.

Mr. Ryder
:The new determination procedure is designed as part of the mechanism for dealing with groups of companies under pay and file, by allowing the profits and losses available for group relief to be agreed across the group. The amendment would extend the determination procedure so that it was used for a quite different purpose. It would require the inspector to agree formally with the company various figures relating to amounts carried forward for corporation tax purposes to subsequent accounting periods.

There is an important question of balance to be considered between the interests of the taxpayer and 380 the costs of administering the tax system for both the taxpayer and the Revenue. The new procedures are designed to simplify and streamline the administration of the tax system. Determining trading losses and other amounts available for relief makes it simpler and more straightforward to agree the tax liabilities of companies across a group for the same accounting period.

The amendment would require various amounts which were carried forward to be agreed in the same way. There are no corresponding overall benefits to be gained in this way. The amendment would not simplify or streamline the agreement of the tax liabilities for these companies. Instead, it would add a further layer of administration before the liabilities were agreed which would increase the costs of administration for the taxpayer and the Revenue.

I recognise that there are advantages for taxpayers where amounts to be carried forward can be agreed, as that gives the taxpayer greater certainty over his tax affairs. But there can also be considerable disadvantages to both the taxpayer and the Revenue where figures cannot be agreed between two groups. In some cases, the Revenue and the taxpayer would be involved in complex and costly negotiations and possibly litigation.

In various circumstances, the amounts carried forward can be changed by events in later years and would then have to be renegotiated. In many cases, the amounts carried forward will never become material for tax purposes, so the additional burden on the Revenue and the taxpayer of determining those amounts would be wasted.

On balance, I believe that the administrative costs that would result from requiring amounts which are carried forward to be formally agreed outweigh the limited benefits for some taxpayers. It is better to continue as in the past, by allowing amounts to be agreed informally where appropriate, leaving amounts in other circumstances, particularly amounts which cannot be agreed, to be hammered out if and when they become material for tax purposes.

This has been a difficult judgment to make. I can see the virtue of the argument of the hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, East (Mr Brown), but for the reasons that I have given, I cannot, alas, accept the amendment.

It is not easy to find in the Taxes Act 1988 the appropriate mechanism that would achieve that result. If my hon. Friend the Economic Secretary believes that the amendment is not needed, will he point to the existing statutory provision that has the same effect as the amendment?

Mr. Nicholas Brown
:We fear that the clause could have substantial implications for some taxpayers—not least, that they could end up having to pay the same tax twice. Under the current law, if tax is eventually found to be due for an accounting period but—usually for good reasons—was inadequately assessed, or assessed and postponed, interest on that tax is charged only from six months after the due and payable date. Under pay and file, it may be charged from the due and payable date. The purpose of our amendment is to perserve the existing system—that is, to deal with our second point as well as with our fear that some people could end up paying tax twice. Will the Economic Secretary respond to both concerns?

Mr. Ryder
:If I may, I shall respond first to amendment No. 216, moved by my hon. Friend the Member for Basingtoke (Mr. Hunter). As he said assessments will be made when they are needed under pay and file. The inspector is required to make an assessment once the profits have been agreed. In most cases, an assessment will not be made until then, but assessments will be made earlier when it is necessary to do so—for example, to allow either party to take an appeal to the commissioners or to counter delay by the taxpayer. Where the profits had not been agreed within six years, which is the usual time limit for making an assessment, a protective assessment would generally be made. The amendment would provide that, if a protective assessment were not made, corporation tax paid by the company should be repaid to it.

The amendment is unnecessary as that process would usually follow automatically when an assessment was not made. In some circumstances, the Revenue can make an assessment, outside the six-year time limit, when tax has been lost through fraud or neglect by the company. In those cases, it would be wrong to repay corporation tax that had been paid by the Company before the extended time limit assessment had been made and the figures agreed. For that reason, I should be grateful if my hon. Friend would not press his amendment.

The hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, East (Mr. Brown) tabled amendment No. 239. Pay and file 384 does away with the need for assessment before corporation tax becomes due and payable. Under pay and file, corporation tax will become due and payable nine months after the end of the accounting period. A company is required to send its tax returns to the inspector within 12 months after the end of the accounting period. The tax return for pay and file will include the company's own computation of how much tax it should pay and this tax should be paid already. When it has not been paid already, the clause allows the Revenue to collect the amount that the company shows that it should pay.

The amendment would displace that rule when an assessment has been made on a company. Again, that is unnecessary, as that is already the effect of the proposed rule. The new procedure works by treating the amount shown in the return as being due under a deemed assessment for the period. When an actual assessment is made, that automatically displaces the deemed assessment and the usual rules for collecting tax under the assessment then apply.

The amendment would be largely ineffective because it is restricted—I am sure that that restriction is inadvertent—to assessments made before the tax becomes due and payable, before nine months after the end of the accounting period. Assessments will rarely be made before that date under pay and file. When they are, the usual rules for collecting tax under assessment, rather than the new rule about collecting the tax shown as due by the return, will apply.

The hon. Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, East asked whether people could pay twice. When tax is paid already, there will be no additional liability under the assessment.
