Draft Firearms Acts (Amendment) Regulations 1992

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State For The Home Department (Mr. Charles Wardle)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Firearms Acts (Amendment) Regulations 1992.

At the outset, Mr. Cook, I welcome you in your new role. Today is St. Hubert's day. St. Hubert is the patron saint of shooters, and it may be of interest to some members of the Committee that he was also an 8th century Bishop of Maastricht.

The draft regulations will implement requirements of the EC directive on the control of the acquisition and possession of weapons. They will do no more than give effect to our Community obligations and are, I hope the Committee will agree, entirely uncontroversial. I shall go through each of the provisions for the Committee's benefit.

The directive is intended to compensate for the removal of systematic border checks when the single market comes into effect in January 1993. It sets minimum standards which all Community states must achieve in their national firearms controls, but states may have stricter controls than the minimum.

Successful negotiations by the United Kingdom achieved major improvements in the directive's content. As a result, no substantial changes need to be made to our current domestic firearms controls. Most important of all, I stress that there will be no relaxation of the stringent controls which Parliament has seen fit to impose on the use of firearms in this country.

On the whole, our firearms controls are stricter than the directive's minimum requirements. But there are a few instances in which that is not the case, so we need to make some minor adjustments to our legislation.

The most notable change is the extension of our existing category of prohibited weapons and ammunition to include those items which the directive says must be banned in all Community states. Draft regulation 3 raises into our prohibited category firearms disguised as other objects; explosive military missiles and their launchers; armour-piercing and incendiary ammunition for military use, and expanding pistol ammunition. Missiles for the newly prohibited types of ammunition will also become prohibited.

I know that the measures have caused some anxiety within the shooting community, but the directive requires us to prohibit those items and we must abide by it.

The good news is that we have made special provision for those who have a serious interest in collecting the newly prohibited firearms or ammunition. They may have a special condition added to their firearm or shotgun certificate which will allow them to keep the newly prohibited items, provided that they do so only as part of 4 a collection. The new condition will be prescribed by rules amending the Firearms Rules 1989 which my right hon. and learned Friend the Secretary of State for the Home Department intends to make shortly.

We shall be running a scheme for owners of disguised firearms who are not collectors and do not wish to sell their firearms. If they held such firearms legally on 16 July, the day after the regulations were laid, and if they surrender them to the police before 1 January 1993, the date on which the regulations will come into force if approved, they will be able to apply for an ex gratia payment. Payments will be based on the market value of the firearms concerned immediately before the draft regulations were laid before Parliament. I stress that that concerns disguised firearms, for example those disguised as an umbrella or a walking stick.

We have also made special provision for those who use expanding pistol ammunition and I know that that is of interest to shooters. Such ammunition is widely used in this country for target shooting, vermin control and other legitimate purposes. The directive recognises that by allowing such ammunition to be used by suitably authorised persons for hunting and target shooting. Hunting must be understood here in its European sense of any shooting of wild animals, game or birds, and not in the limited way in which we would normally interpret it.

Firearm certificate holders will be able to buy and use expanding pistol ammunition and like missiles for the purposes authorised by the directive. The authorised purposes are sporting purposes, shooting vermin, estate husbandry, slaughtering purposes, competitive shooting inlcuding target shooting, and target shooting outside competition. That should reassure shooters who have expressed concern about that.

Those who wish to take advantage of the exemption will simply need to have a special condition added to their firearm certificates. Similarly, registered firearms dealers will be able to supply such ammunition and missiles to exempted certificate holders by having a special condition added to their certificate or registration.

Draft regulation 4 implements the directive's requirement that no one under the age of 18 should be allowed to acquire or possess firearms except for hunting or target shooting. Again, hunting is meant in the broader European sense.

We propose to give effect to that requirement by providing that certificate holders under 18 may use their firearms and shotguns only for the purposes authorised by the directive, which I have outlined.

Draft regulation 5 introduces the new European firearms pass. From 1 January next year British residents will need a European firearms pass to take their firearms to another Community state; for example, on a shooting trip. Firearm and shotgun certificate holders will be automatically entitled to a pass in respect of the firearms covered by their certificates and the pass will be issued free by their local chief police officer; Following a United Kingdom initiative, all Community states have agreed a common format for the pass, which should make life easier for both travelling shooters and the enforcement agencies.

Draft regulation 5 also introduces a second new document called an article 7 authority. British residents will need an article 7 authority to purchase or acquire on the JPEG 5 continent certain firearms for which prior permission is required. Again, article 7 authorities will be issued free by local chief officers of police.

In the same way that British residents will need a European firearms pass to take firearms abroad, so we must ensure that EC visitors bringing firearms here have a European firearms pass issued by another Community state.

At present, all overseas visitors who wish to bring firearms to this country must apply for a British visitor's permit. We intend to retain that system. Although the directive sets minimum standards, domestic firearms controls in other Community states will continue to vary widely. As long as that remains the case, we think it right that we should continue to ensure the EC visitor to this country fulfil our rigorous public safety requirement by applying for a British visitor's permit before they may be permitted to bring firearms here.

Draft regulation 7 therefore, requires a prospective visitor from the Community either to produce his European firearms pass or to show that he is exempt from the need to have one when applying for a visitor's permit.

Draft regulation 6 provides that the holder of a visitor's shotgun permit who is a resident of another Community state cannot purchase or acquire in Great Britain a shotgun with a magazine to take home with him unless he has a licence issued by the Department of Trade and Industry authorising him to remove the gun from Great Britain.

Draft regulation 8 requires a resident of another Community state to have the equivalent of an article 7 authority issued by his own domestic authorities to buy firearms here. Again, as a result of a United Kingdom initiative, the 12 Community states have agreed on a common format for the article 7 authority. That will make it easier for a registered firearms dealer to recognise an article 7 authority issued by another Community state when a resident of that state seeks to buy firearms from him here.

Draft regulation 9 implements the directive's requirement that a resident of one Community state who purchases or acquires certain firearms in another Community state should inform his own domestic authorities of the purchase or acquisition. Draft regulation 9 requires a British resident to inform his local chief officer of police of such a purchase or acquisition.

The directive requires the Community state to set up an information exchange network to inform each other about transfers of firearms between their respective territories. Draft regulation 10 ensures that we will not be prevented by confidentiality obligations from supplying such information to other Community states.

From that brief summary I hope that it will be clear that the draft regulations do no more than give effect to our Community obligations. We have consulted widely on our plans to implement the directive and I am glad to be able to say that both the police and the shooting community have welcomed the way in which we have approached the matter. I ask the Committee to support the motion.

A recent article in Everywoman magazine suggested that the Home Office has given in to pressure not to be too stringent and, indeed, to relax its approach to firearms regulation which was strongly supported on both sides of the House following various incidents, not least the horrific shooting in Hungerford.

The explanatory memorandum makes considerable reference to exemptions, an issue on which the Committee needs to be satisfied. Paragraph 4 says:

"collectors have had to be specifically exempted, where appropriate, from the requirements of the Regulations."

How will the Government ensure that those relaxations are not abused or misused? In relation to customs and police controls, how will the Government ensure that the regulations—or relaxations—are not used in the wrong way? In other words, how will this work in practice? It is not enough to see the code words of the regulations, and I shall come later to an example in which the wording does not seem to give us much assistance.

The explanatory memorandum notes on regulation 3 refer to the fact that the prohibition of incendiary ammunition does not catch tracer ammunition. Will the Minister explain the implication of that? The notes on that regulation also state that collectors will be

"able to continue to collect such items by having a special condition added to their firearm or shotgun certificates. Collectors of missiles for such ammunition will need to apply for a firearm certificate with the special condition on it."

Again, the intention is clear, but how will this work in practice? How can we be sure that these quite complex regulations cannot be breached in a damaging way?

On the exemptions for ammunition, paragraph 17 of the notes says:

"this exemption does not allow firearms dealers to trade generally in prohibited expanding ammunition and like missiles without the Secretary of State's authority but is restricted to allowing them to supply such ammunition and like missiles to exempted firearm certificate holders and to other firearms dealers who have this special condition and therefore are able to take advantage of this exemption."

The intention is clear, but how will the system be policed? As it is the Secretary of State's decision, will it be dealt with by the Government, or by local police forces? How will the system operate when the decision is made by the Secretary of State but, as I suspect, local chiefs of police are obliged to ensure proper enforcement? What will be the practical situation for police and customs officers?

JPEG 7 I have a particular difficulty with regulation 4. As the Minister explained in his introduction, the intention is clear, but the content is not. The notes on the regulations are a useful starting point because they say what the regulations are meant to say rather than what they seem to say to the casual, non-legal reader. Paragraph 19 says that regulation 4

"Amends the criteria for grant of a firearm certificate in section 27 of the 1968 Act to provide that a person under eighteen is to be capable of having a good reason for possessing, purchasing or acquiring a firearm or ammunition only if he has no intention of using them for a purpose other than the authorised purposes."

How does one determine that a young person making an application has intentions as pure as the driven snow?

Paragraph 20 deals with the corresponding amendment to section 28 of the 1968 Act. It says:

"(which preserves the existing burden of proof on the chief officer of police to be satisfied that the applicant does not have a good reason before he can refuse a certificate) in respect of an application by a person under the age of 18 a shotgun certificate."

There is good reason for us to probe the Minister about that and seek satisfaction about what is being done. How will the procedure work in practice and how will it resolve the problem? The problem was illustrated recently when a chief police officer declared himself outraged that he had no choice but to issue a shotgun certificate to a young person aged 10. That must cause anxiety. The public's reaction is that if the law requires that, it must be barking mad. I would not accuse the Minister of such insanity. I am sure that he wants regulations that will work in practice. Will he satisfy the Committee about the Government's intention and about how the system will work in practice?

Regulation 4(1) states:

"(1A) Where a person under the age of eighteen is entitled, as the holder of a certificate under this Act, to have a firearm in his possession, it is an offence for that person to use that firearm for a purpose not authorised by the European weapons directive."

I understand that—a person who has obtained a firearm for one purpose must not use it for an illegal purpose.

Regulation 4(2) deals with special provision about firearm certificates and states:

"For the purposes of subsection (1) above a person under the age of eighteen shall be capable of having a good reason for having a firearm or ammunition in his possession, or for purchasing or acquiring it,"—

and this is where I place the emphasis—

"only if he has no intention of using the firearm or ammunition, at any time before he attains the age of eighteen, for a purpose not authorised by the European weapons directive."

Setting aside the fact that that seems to imply that it would be all right if such a person intends to use the firearm for those purposes after reaching the age of 18, which can hardly have been the draftsman's intention, the provision refers only to intention.

Regulation 4(3) refers to special provision about shotgun certificates and states:

"A person under the age of eighteen shall be regarded for the purposes of paragraph (b) of subsection (1A) above as not having a good reason for possessing, purchasing or acquiring a shot gun if it is his intention to use the shot gun, at any time before he attains the age of eighteen, for a purpose not authorised by the European weapons directive."

The problem is that if the person is under 18 and intends to use the shotgun for an illegal purpose when he is over 18, that will be all right. That cannot be the intention of those who drafted the directive. It also means that it will be virtually impossible to refuse an application. To be refused, a young person under 18 would have to knock on the door 8 of the chief constable of his area and say, "Please let me have a gun, I want to break the law with it." That cannot be the Minister's intention, but the Minister will have to satisfy us on precisely how the regulations will work.

If, under existing law, a chief constable has had to express outrage because his hands are tied over an application, the regulations will make it worse.
