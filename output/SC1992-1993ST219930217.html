Lanarkshire (Motherwell) Enterprise Zones Designation Order 1993

10.30 am

Mr. Tom Clarke
(Monklands, West)
: I beg to move,


That the Committee has considered the Lanarkshire (Motherwell) Enterprise Zones Designation Order 1993 (S.I. 1993, No. 24).

The Chairman:
With the agreement of the Committee, we shall also consider the other orders before us, namely, the Lanarkshire (Hamilton) Enterprise Zones Designation Order 1993 (S.I. 1993, No. 23) and the Lanarkshire (Monklands) Enterprise Zones Designation Order 1993 (S.I. 1993, No. 25).

Mr. Clarke
: This sitting is very much a constituency occasion. It is fair to say that the Minister, who has shown a great interest in Lanarkshire, will not be surprised by the presence of a number of my hon. Friends. My hon. Friend the Member for Motherwell, South (Dr. Bray) is greatly involved in these matters, particularly those arising from the closure of Ravenscraig. The order covers parts of the constituency of my hon. Friend the Member for Hamilton (Mr. Robertson). My hon. Friend the Member for Clydesdale (Mr. Hood) might make a case for parts of Clydesdale being included in an enterprise zone. My hon. Friend the Member for Motherwell, North (Dr. Reid) has shown a great interest in these matters following the closure of Ravenscraig. My hon. Friend the Member for East Kilbride (Mr. Ingram) has a particular interest in the issue of new towns in his constituency. I am sure that he appreciates that some of the problems of the new towns are not shared by other parts of Lanarkshire, such as my own.

In my constituency there are enormous areas of dereliction—the steel industry is virtually non-existent and there is high unemployment. The people of Lanarkshire are arguing for the special recognition of the industrial situation arising from rapid changes in Lanarkshire—not least the decline of the steel industry and the closure of Ravenscraig. The Minister has been exceptionally courteous, certainly to the district councils of Monklands and Strathkelvin in my constituency. I am sure that that will be the tone of his contribution to today's debate.

We are meeting in this Committee because of the serious problems facing the economy in Lanarkshire. We have witnessed devastation in Lanarkshire, not simply because of the closure of Ravenscraig and the fact that it was closed in a calculated way following privatisation, but because of the other job losses in industry after industry, the cuts in the training budget, and because the enthusiasm of the people of Lanarkshire to regenerate industry and to play their part in the manufacturing economy is not reflected in Government policy. Although we welcome the orders as far as they go, they do not match the enormous problem that Lanarkshire faces.

On 7 January 1992 British Steel announced its intention to close Ravenscraig by September 1992. The Minister 4 will recall that on the same day the Government announced that they would approach the European Community to obtain enterprise zone status for certain areas. The orders are a consequence of that.

My hon. Friends will speak from their deep knowledge of their constituencies. I shall simply make some general observations and perhaps refer briefly to my constituency of Monklands and give other hon. Members the opportunity to make their points. Although we welcome the orders as far as they go, we appreciate that the debate on enterprise zones and their contribution to the economy will continue. We insist that, if there is to be a strategy for enterprise zones to deal with our problems, it should be concerned with new jobs. I am not convinced that that is always the case.

The hon. Member for Tayside, North (Mr. Walker) has views on the recent switch of a sizeable insurance company from Perth to Dundee where it had all the benefits of an enterprise zone. That is splendid for Dundee and I support everything that my hon. Friends who represent that city want to see established in their constituencies. However, in common with what was described in the west of Scotland as the Radio Clyde debate on Clydebank, we must seriously consider whether enterprise zones lead to new jobs or merely take jobs from other parts of Scotland whose need for employment may be just as great as that of the areas in which they are located.

I look forward to hearing what the Minister has to say about the technical proposals in the orders that we are considering and whether they reflect the determination of the Government and all agencies concerned with enterprise zones to improve conditions. The agencies include Scottish Enterprise, the Lanarkshire development agency and local councils in Strathclyde region. I am interested in hearing whether the consultations that I assumed were held with Strathclyde region meant that the proposals would not inhibit its own industrial promotion activities, including an option to borrow if that was necessary to promote the job opportunities and skills of the people of Lanarkshire and its ability to respond to a modern Europe.

Unemployment creates desperation in Lanarkshire and the levels are appalling in Hamilton, Monklands and Motherwell. Most alarming is the rise in male unemployment in those areas. My hon. Friend the Member for Hamilton may wish to comment on conditions in his constituency but I understand that its unemployment rate increased from 13.6 per cent., 4,077 people, to 14.8 per cent., 4,443 people, between December 1991 and December 1992.

In my constituency of Monklands unemployment increased from 14.7 per cent., 4,321 people, to 15.6 per cent., 4,606 people. In Motherwell, the rate increased from 14.7 percent, 5,843 people, to 16.4 per cent., 6,496 people.

I am citing figures but we all know that they represent people, their families and the devastation that unemployment brings to the quality of life in our communities. The sad backdrop to the orders is that male unemployment in Lanarkshire increased by more than 30 per cent, between December 1989 and December 1992, according to figures from Strathclyde region.

In accepting the orders, I hope that the Minister will not assume that the Opposition believes that they go JPEG 5 far enough to deal with the problems of our economy, infrastructure, industrial investment and so on. The proposals obviously take account of conditions in Monklands, where a number of steel jobs were lost in Coatbridge. There was the closure of Gartcosh, cutbacks in the Imperial steel works in Airdrie and a spin-off in the rest of Lanarkshire from Clydesdale to Ravenscraig.

I accept that the setting up in December 1990 of the Lanarkshire working group was welcomed. Its report, published in June 1991, was certainly welcomed by Opposition Members. One of its most significant proposals was the creation of

"selected strategic and industrial development sites in North Lanarkshire as Enterprise Zones."

The orders result from that strongly held view.

What is needed is a wide-ranging, effective and co-ordinated strategy for the regeneration of Lanarkshire. I should be doing less than full service to the people of Lanarkshire if I had not said that. The enterprise zone will not of itself, wherever it may be placed, be a panacea for existing economic problems—clearly, more initiatives will be required—but it will go some way to alleviating existing problems. For those reasons, I welcome it.

Will the Minister tell us specifically the benefits he believes will be derived from each order? The interesting Library paper explains in detail the benefits of enterprise zones. They are exempted from rates on industrial and commercial property and from development land tax. The zones operate under a system in which there is 100 per cent, allowance for corporation and income tax purposes for capital expenditure on industrial and commercial buildings. The siting of enterprise zones means a simplification of planning procedures and speedier administration of planning controls and a reduction to a minimum of Government requests for statistical information. Employers are exempt from industrial training levies and from requirements to supply information to industrial training boards.

We have a strong view on training and we need more of it. We need a commitment to training in Lanarkshire to make the most of the industrial and economic opportunities that might exist. A further benefit of enterprise zone designation is that applications for customs facilities will be processed as a matter of priority and some criteria will be relaxed. Will the Minister tell us how those principles will apply specifically to the orders?

Although we welcome the orders as far as they go, we recall that, following the closure of Ravenscraig, the Prime Minister said that he would not forget Lanarkshire. We want a full scale commitment to a strategy for job creation in Lanarkshire. We welcome the work carried out by most of the agencies that are involved. We want a reiteration of the commitment to the Eurofreight terminal in Mossend, in the constituency of my hon. Friend the Member for Motherwell, North. That is crucial to our infrastructure. We have had numerous and helpful discussions with the Minister about that.

There is a need for debate in a constituency sense about the orders. We say that unashamedly because of our commitments to our constituencies. The reason we prayed against the orders was that we wanted to put our views on record. The Minister will also have the opportunity to tell the people of Lanarkshire how he sees the position and how the Government intend to respond to the appalling problems that we face.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Allan Stewart)
: May I tell the hon. Member for Monklands, West (Mr. Clarke) that I welcome the opportunity to debate the Lanarkshire enterprise zone designation orders. I accept entirely the basis on which the debate is taking place. Opposition Members have prayed against the orders not because they do not welcome them but because of their wish to debate the implications of the orders and how they relate to the problems to which the hon. Gentleman rightly referred.

I wish first to make one or two general observations. I shall respond later to the more detailed constituency issues that will no doubt be raised during the debate.

As the hon. Member for Monklands, West said, the specific measures can be traced back to December 1990, when my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State announced the establishment of the Lanarkshire working group to consider the problems in Lanarkshire caused in particular by restructuring in the steel industry. That group was quickly established with representatives of all the key players in the Lanarkshire economy. It worked speedily and efficiently in producing findings and recommendations for action. It identified the need for new and quickly available industrial sites as a priority for the regeneration of Lanarkshire.

Hard on the heels of the establishment of the working group, the Lanarkshire development agency came into operation as part of the Scottish Enterprise network. I hope that everyone agrees that the local enterprise company has been a major driving force in the regeneration process for Lanarkshire. It now approaches the end of its second year of operation. It has an excellent track record as the Government's agent for economic, environmental and skills development in Lanarkshire.

I do not propose to spend time ennumerating all its achievements. It has been supported by Scottish Enterprise National and by its local-authority, private-sector and other partners. It has achieved a great deal in those two years for Lanarkshire and for Scotland.

The hon. Member for Monklands, West asked how the orders related to Strathclyde regional council's development activities. I assure the hon. Gentleman that Strathclyde is happy that the enterprise zone does not cut across its industrial development activities. The council was a member of the working party and fully supported the creation of an enterprise zone. The regional council has been fully involved in the selection of sites and of the members of the management group that will oversee the development of the zone.

That is one example of the cooperative approach of everyone trying to work together in tackling Lanarkshire's problems and exploiting its opportunities. It is important that our real and proper perception of people working positively together on what are widely admitted to be serious problems, is widely shared. Outsiders will not invest in losers; they will invest only in people who believe that they will and can succeed.

The Government have invested a great deal in Lanarkshire, not only in financial resources, although it is undeniable that we have made substantial additional resources available through Scottish Enterprise and the LDA via local authorities and the Scottish Office. The JPEG 7 additional cost to the Exchequer of the enterprise zone orders, will exceed £50 million. We are confident that that investment will be repaid. We are also investing a great deal of energy and optimism in Lanarkshire's future, an investment to which the hon. Gentleman kindly referred.

We are acutely alive to the problems that unemployment has brought to Lanarkshire. I do not deny that key tasks need to be tackled and that a great deal more must be done.

The hon. Gentleman referred to the need for a general strategy. We hope that the Lanarkshire regeneration strategy, which involves the local authorities, will be publicly available in two months or so. The document will be available for hon. Members to study.

We have laid the foundations on which the new Lanarkshire economy can be built. The enterprise zone is one of the building blocks and will be a major contributor to the regeneration process.

The hon. Gentleman asked about the likely implications of the enterprise zone. If it is successful, as we have every reason to believe it will be, it will create 17,000 gross jobs and more than 8,300 net additional jobs for the people of Lanarkshire and their families. I mentioned the sum of £50 million from the Exchequer, which is being invested in various ways in the zone. We expect that to lever £250 million worm of private sector investment. Those are substantial sums.

I hope that that background to the orders has been helpful to the Committee. I look forward to hearing the comments that Opposition Members want to make as constituency Members, and I hope to have an opportunity to respond later in the debate.

Dr. Jeremy Bray
(Motherwell, South)
: All my right hon. and hon. Friends welcome the enterprise zone proposals but, as the Minister said, they can be only a part of a wider strategy that must deal with the future of all the people of Lanarkshire.

It is appropriate that students from Braidhurst high school in my constituency are in the Room listening to our debate. Lanarkshire high schools have the secret of the future in their hearts.

Tax incentives will be especially important in the enterprise zone. I have little doubt that development will take place there. The site preparations are long overdue. Those developments should not divert attention from the complementary developments needed to make a success of the re-industrialisation of Lanarkshire.

As the Minister said, perhaps half the jobs created in the enterprise zone will result from the diversion of jobs that would have been created elsewhere. Because of the location of most of the enterprise zone sites along the easily accessible motorway routes to the entire central belt, many of the jobs, whether new or diverted, will be filled by people living outwith the area—from Glasgow and Edinburgh. I do not begrudge them that, but it is mistaken to think of the enterprise zone as being for the benefit merely of Lanarkshire. It will provide benefits for the central belt over a much wider range.

There is some danger, therefore, that the intention of providing jobs for Lanarkshire fulfilling its industrial 8 potential following the loss of the steel industry will be dissipated. It is unfortunate that not all members of the Committee have maps showing the proposed enterprise zones. The red patches on the map indicate the sites scheduled. They are located along the main motorway and ring the principal steel site areas, which are left stranded in the town centres, principally of Motherwell and Wishaw.

The steel sites, particularly the Ravenscraig, Lanarkshire and Clydesdale works sites, are large urban sites. They were ringed by what were once steelworkers' houses and they were served for industrial purposes by railways, not roads.

Ravenscraig—the latest of the steelworks which operated for only one generation—was manned from the start mainly by workers who were not steelworkers by trade and who came from a wider area in Lanarkshire—many were from mining and other industries which experienced a decline in employment in the 1950s and 1960s. The inner, urban core in the steel sites seems to have been forgotten by the planners who scheduled the enterprise zone sites. The danger is that we might create a ghetto—an inner ring of unemployment—around blighted town centres that are remote in terms of public access and remote from new jobs in public transport terms.

A housewife looking for a half shift who lives in Paisley or Gowkthrapple will simply find it impractical to get to the new industrial enterprise zones sites along the motorway unless she has a car. The people who do not have cars need the jobs. There is a danger of a serious social problem developing in areas that already have high levels of unemployment.

The planners argued that to reclaim the steel sites would take time and cost a lot of money and that it would be quicker to develop on green field sites. However, the development could have gone ahead just as quickly on some of the brown field sites. The Lanarkshire work site—which is in the middle of Motherwell and has good road access—has been available for development for the past 10 years but nothing has been done. It has been in the ownership of first the Scottish development agency, then Scottish Enterprise and now the Lanarkshire development agency. Still nothing has been done.

Consequently the industrial land market is in danger of being over-subscribed by subsidised land from within the enterprise zones while the inner urban decay will be allowed to continue. It is especially unfortunate because the main railway line from Glasgow to Euston—now with main line trains to Edinburgh and down the east coast—passes alongside the Lanarkshire and Ravenscraig sites. That offers obvious scope for major redevelopment similar to the development of Bristol Parkway station and many other parkway stations which are being developed around the big cities in the south. There has been no such thinking in Scotland.

I am asking for the simultaneous—not the delayed—tackling of the urban problems and the redevelopment of the steel sites. The hon. Member who represents a constituency in Teeside, in which area I used to have a constituency, well knows the problems of urban decay and the necessity of tackling them. The Scottish Office seems to have lost sight of those problems.

The emerging attitude of Strathclyde region, Scottish Enterprise and other parts of the Scottish Office seems to JPEG 9 be that with the enterprise zone, the Eurofreight terminal and the Strathclyde business park, Lanarkshire will have had its share of development. That is understandable in the light of needs elsewhere in Scotland which are pressing and urgent.

I am confident that, according to the criterion of what will benefit Scotland as a whole most, the problems of urban decay and the reclamation of the steel sites have to be tackled. There is a strong case for industrial redevelopment. It is necessary to make the best use of what are inevitably limited resources and inevitably limited and time-constrained political good will.

Although the Minister said that there would be, there is no plan or strategy for the redevelopment of the steel sites. So the options tend to close. I shall use the important example of road access to the steel sites and to Ravenscraig, Clydesdale and the heart of Motherwell. The road access must match motorway standard, or at least the dual carriageway standard of the A725, which has been so successful in attracting development in recent years.

The Ove Arup feasibility study, which was commissioned by the Lanarkshire development agency in consultation with other authorities, spelt out several options for the future road development in Lanarkshire. The cheapest link between Carfin and Holytown from Ravenscraig to the motorway, to the north, is already pre-empted with Strathclyde regional council plan putting in only a two-lane single carriageway in next year's capital programme.

The railway bridge is out to contract now. That is going ahead in advance of a strategic decision about what the road plans should be. A possible preferable link to that specific route is one that goes through the Clydesdale steel site up to the M8. However, that would go slap bang through the middle of the Eurofreight terminal, which has been pre-empted for other purposes. How the planners will get round that problem I do not know.

The third possible link and a necessary complement to whichever of the other two routes is chosen is the link to the M8 to the south. That would complete the fast link concept between the M8 and the M74. It would make it much easier for traffic coming in from the constituency of my hon. Friend the Member for Clydesdale. The traffic coming up from the south could spread itself round the central zone if there were a fast link from the south up to Edinburgh or Glasgow through either part of Motherwell. That road is, however, being restricted by local housing developments, not only by current developments, but by those that are proposed under the enterprise zone plans. The Excelsior Park site alongside the railway blocks a road access route to the main steel sites.

As a sign of the political attitudes developing in Scotland, Strathclyde regional council, as the strategic planning authority, is using an obsolete structure plan to call in and block useful supporting developments such as the Cleland estate proposal for school playing fields and good quality housing adjacent to the Ravenscraig site.

That planning proposal will go on appeal before the Secretary of State, so I do not expect the Minister to tell us the outcome. That is a clear example of the political reaction from the rest of Scotland in that it considers that Lanarkshire has had all that is due to it. Such an argument is not in the interests of Scotland but, given the 10 considerable planning confusion that exists, such a reaction is understandable.

Further and higher education and research and technology transfer have made an important contribution to re-development in Lanarkshire. They have not yet begun to be integrated with the industrial development proposals. There is already the prospect of the health board, with its influence over the training of nurses and the College of Nursing, forming links with one Scottish university and Bell college in Hamilton forming links with another. As a result, there will be confusion and dissipation of effort.

The difficulties are caused by classic planning confusion between authorities with lack of strategy that is in danger of causing wasteful and conflicting plans and developments and frustrating overall intentions. Such confusions were analysed and ways in which to avoid them were developed after the war, when serious strategic planning was first adopted. Under the baleful influence of the short-termism of Tory Scottish Ministers, those lessons seem to have been forgotten.

The proliferating agencies and the divorce between funding and statutory powers, so beloved of Scottish Tory Ministers, is at the root of the muddle. They talk about co-ordination but the reason they do so is that they have created so many different authorities that fall over each other to develop proposals, put them forward and implement them without taking account the overall strategic direction.

The Minister referred to the Lanarkshire working group as a success. It was chaired by Scottish Office civil servants. Only officials sat on it. It had no public accountability. When he came to details, the Minister could not sustain his case. The orders are a collection of proposals of the past 30 years put together by officials who wanted to get their pet schemes through. There is no strategy. The strategic plans that I have talked about cannot be found in the plan.

There should be fuller, franker, earlier consultation. A strategic planning brief is quickly needed to relate the physical, social and economic aspects. It must break out of development stereotypes. There is scope for a much wider contribution to be made to planning Lanarkshire's development, perhaps by international competition— why not?—on the working environment of the future, with particular reference to Lanarkshire and the steel sites, seeing the enterprise zone in its total environment. We could find industrial sponsors with local interests, including British Telecom, Mercury, British Rail, British Steel and Motorala, which I believe, would be enthusiastic about the idea of promoting a well-publicised international competition.

There is no room for the closed-shop attitudes that too often rule the narrow professional cliques that assume responsibility for those matters. Why should the best of British architecture been found only in Paris, Hong Kong or Tokyo? Why not in the industrially redeveloped heart of Britain? I hope that the Minister will today and in the future show greater efficiency and effectiveness and strategic sense than he has so far in Lanarkshire's redevelopment.

As the Member of Parliament for that constituency, I claim that it should be given equal consideration.

Members have received this week from a constituent of mine, Mr. Alex Brown, who is chief executive of Midscot training services—a very worthy, largely engineering training institution that deals with the private sector.
