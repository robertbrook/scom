Clause 1
THE REGISTER OF SOCIAL LANDLORDS

Mr. Nick Raynsford
(Greenwich)
: I beg to move amendment No. 3, in page 1, line 10, after "(1)", insert

"After having on the first occasion established criteria for registration in accordance with section 3(1) below,".

I welcome you, Mr. McWilliam, to the Chair. I also extend my welcome to Mrs. Winterton, who will assist you by chairing the Committee in your absence. An interesting parallel can be seen with the Standing Committee that considered the Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993. On that occasion, the hon. Member for Macclesfield (Mr. Winterton) chaired the Committee and you, Mr. McWilliam assisted him. You are now in the Chair and the hon. Member for Congleton (Mrs. Winterton) will assist you. I am delighted to see that the post has been kept in the family.

The Committee will deal with a long and complex Bill and we shall debate a wide range of issues. The Opposition do not think that the Bill provides an adequate response to the many housing needs that the country faces and we shall highlight the many problems and housing needs that are not being tackled properly. In particular, we shall highlight those provisions in the Bill that are retrograde and damaging. They include the measures on homelessness.

4 We shall aim to amend the Bill when it fails to deal with problems adequately and when there is a need to clarify or strengthen it. I suspect that many amendments will be tabled. The Bill shows signs of its rushed arrival in the House and, on Second Reading, the Minister revealed that at one stage it was heading towards having 380 clauses. Surgery was necessary to reduce it to its present not insignificant size of 180 clauses. Therefore, we know that last-minute pruning has taken place.

Many loose ends have not been well tied up and we anticipate the need for many amendments to rectify drafting problems. Indeed, the Government have already tabled a huge wadge of amendments and, even more remarkably for the first day in Committee, a huge wadge of Government amendments have been withdrawn before they have even been considered. We shall need to be eagle-eyed when we consider the detail.

Part I of the Bill deals with the social rented sector. Opposition Members believe that the prime concern is the inadequate output of rented housing to meet the needs of all members of the community. The level of output of social housing has fallen in recent years to its lowest level since the end of the second world war. The figures published yesterday by the Minister's Department show the appalling collapse in the provision of housing in general and particularly in the provision of social housing in 1995. Total housing starts in the private sector and by housing associations and local authorities were down by 15 per cent. on 1994. Only 33,000 new housing association homes and a derisory 1,200 council homes were started last year. Fewer than 35,000 new homes were started in the whole of Great Britain.

Those figures demonstrate a wholly inadequate response to social housing needs. Anyone who has followed the issues over the years will have seen an appalling decline in the output of new housing for rent from housing associations and local authorities. We have reached the point where needs are simply not being met.

The Opposition will articulate the need to expand the supply of social rented housing. That is best done through a range of different providers. Local authorities should once again be allowed to build homes for renting. Housing associations have an important role and social landlords—and non-profit-making organisations—who wish to provide accommodation to meet social needs should play an important role, too.

We shall not oppose the principle behind chapter I of the Bill, which establishes a register of social landlords that is wider than the existing Housing Corporation register, which is made up of housing associations only. However, some important points must be stressed. We strongly oppose the Government's original design to include profit-making organisations. Such a measure would have confused entirely the distinct roles of the profit-making private sector and of social housing providers, which should be non-profit-making organisations. We recognise and shall encourage the role of the private sector and I will discuss that later, but it has a distinct and separate role from that of social housing providers.

We are glad that the Government, albeit at the last minute, have recognised the error of their ways and JPEG 5 withdrawn the proposal for profit-making companies to be eligible to be included in the register of social landlords. That proposal appeared in the White Paper published last summer. However, we understand that the Government may try to introduce another Bill in due course and that they may wish to return to that proposal. I would not wager my shirt on such a Bill reaching the statute book and I doubt whether many members from either side of the Committee would place a large bet on legislation going through at the tail end of this year if there has not been a general election by then.

The provision in the Bill is rightly limited to non-profit-making organisations. Its Explanatory and Financial Memorandum states:

"non-profit-making landlords may register with the Housing corporation".

We shall consider the precise definition of a non-profit-making landlord in more detail when we reach the relevant clauses. We fear that the Government may define the term in such a way that it does not meet the normal test that most people would have for non-profit-making organisations.

Clause 1 deals with the register of social landlords. Clause 1(1) requires the corporation to

"maintain a register of social landlords".

Before the Housing Corporation takes that step, we must know what the register's purpose is. The aim of the amendment—I am sorry that I have taken a little time to reach this point in my remarks, but I hope that a modest digression is permissable, as it is the beginning of the sitting and we have not delayed the proceedings with lengthy speeches on sittings motions, as has happened in the past—is to ensure proper consideration of the need for social housing before the register is compiled. Clearly, there is no point in establishing a register in a vacuum. It would be laughable to produce a register and enable anyone, on the strength of a rough idea of entitlement, to be included. An initial appraisal should be carried out, embracing the need for social housing and examining the role of existing and potential providers of social housing, before the Housing Corporation reaches the stage of compiling its register. That would be a necessary and sensible start to the process. We should expect the Government to explain how they expect the process to be carried out.

The amendment is self-explanatory. It would require the corporation, at the beginning of the process, to establish

"criteria for registration in accordance with section 3(1)".

We shall in due course move amendments to clause 3(1), which sets out the criteria, but the principle of establishing criteria for registration at the outset is obvious.

I hope that the Minister will find the amendment acceptable. It is tabled in a positive spirit of trying to improve the Bill. Elements of the Bill are objectionable and we will strongly oppose them, including the measures on homelessness, but we broadly agree with other features, and we shall try to improve them by amendment. Our amendments will be constructive. Amendment No. 3 sets the tone in that respect. We hope that Conservative Members will not blindly vote against 6 them merely because the Opposition have tabled them. I say that for two reasons. First, some Conservative Members expressed concerns on Second Reading about aspects of the Bill, such as the licensing of houses in multiple occupation. There may be common ground between the two sides of the Committee on the need to toughen the Bill with respect to those matters. We shall certainly try to make progress along those lines.

Secondly, I advise Conservative Members that, before long, there will be a general election, after which those of them who remain in the House may be on the Opposition Benches. I should hate to think that they could spend many years—the rest of their parliamentary lives—seeing amendments rejected purely because of the side of the House on which they sit. It would be in their interest to adopt our spirit and seek constructive progress through the amendment of a Bill that, in many instances, needs significant changes and amendments.
