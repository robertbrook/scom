National Health Service (Determination of Districts) (No. 4) Order 1992

10.30 am

Mr. Ian McCartney
(Makerfield)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the National Health Service (Determination of Districts) (No. 4) Order 1992 (S.I. 1992. No. 2751).

The Chairman
: With this, if the Committee so wishes, we shall debate at the same time the other order before us namely, the National Health Service (District Health Authorities) (No. 4) Order 1992 (S.I. 1992, No. 2752).

Mr. McCartney
: Before I make my contribution, on behalf of my hon. Friends I wish you, Sir James, the staff of the Committee, the civil servants and even Conservative Members a 1993 that you and they will remember with joy. This gives me the chance to renew my acquaintance with the hon. Member for Harrogate (Mr. Banks), with whom I spent some delightful weeks this summer in Australia on parliamentary business. I hope that he enjoys the next 90 minutes as much as we enjoyed those few weeks together.

I shall set the Minister five tests on the orders as they are controversial and objections from across the political spectrum in Yorkshire have been raised to them. All seven district councils—of which only York is controlled by Labour; the rest are controlled, one way or another, by the Conservatives—community health councils and community groups have expressed serious doubts, reservations or outright opposition to the Government's proposals. They are anxious about how the Government are stealthily reorganising district and regional health authority boundaries without coherent discussions on the consequences for the numbers of health authorities that will be left, for the value of the reorganisation, for the delivery of local services, or for the involvement of local communities in the development and output of those services.

The Minister must fulfil five tasks to make clear why the orders were introduced. Can he produce evidence of increased efficiency and of what that will mean for the volume of contracts that the new authority will issue to its provider units? If the Government maintain that the changes will improve dramatically the efficiency of delivery of services, the Minister should be able to show the Committee what that will mean in practice for north Yorkshire. In the establishment of the new amalgamated super district, 4 Department officials responsible for the initial discussions made clear that they could prove to the local community the improvement that would result in the delivery of services. What are the Government's priorities in judging the performance of the new health authority?

What steps is the Minister taking to increase local involvement? He may be able to argue that an amalgamation is necessary because of the constraints of the market and of the need to ensure that there is an organisation large enough to negotiate properly the number, value and quality of contracts. However, he must also give a firm commitment that he has not moved away from the position in the White Paper which referred to devolving decision making to a local basis for delivery of service and for involving the locality in the creation of local services for local needs.

What is the Minister doing about development of decision making? How will that affect the quality of local community involvement?

That is most important. In much of the evidence that I have seen from community groups about this proposal, the objections relate to the Government's failure to maintain that commitment of local accountability and local involvement of community health councils. Later, I shall give evidence of the large curtailment of local involvement in the decision-making processes under this proposal.

The proposal brings us back to the 1960s and the early 1980s when there were giant bureaucracies in the national health service under Governments of whatever political complexion. Area health authorities were abandoned with general political support on both sides of the House. It was recognised that those area health authorities were remote and out of touch with local opinion. They did not involve the local communities in the delivery or planning of services. In the 1970s a reorganisation took place under the then Heath Government and it was a disaster. In the 1980s, after consultation and a national agreement, this Government took steps to get rid of area health authorities and to introduce more locally accountable health authorities.

We are now going back on that decision. What has happened in north Yorkshire is probably the best example of what went wrong in the 1960s, 1970s and the early 1980s. In north Yorkshire, district health authorities failed to deliver either the level of service or local community involvement as needed.

The proposal mirrors the arrangements that were discredited at that time. This is not a new revolutionary approach to delivery of health care in north Yorkshire. The proposal would take north Yorkshire back to the time of those discredited arrangements in the 1970s and 1980s. The Government must provide evidence for why that should be so. Why should the district health authorities be persuaded that those past experiences will not be revisited?

JPEG 5 I understand the pressures on Government and those on district health authorities to amalgamate as they lose their operational responsibilities which are divided between the purchaser and provider. In many areas nothing more than a rump is left. In that rump, little or nothing will be done about the planning of services. Only a few people will be left to deal with contractual obligations. However, that is not the case in respect of the size of the districts in north Yorkshire. There is no evidence of a lack of viability in the current arrangements. That is clear from the consultations that have taken place. I have seen no evidence presented to the Government from across the spectrum of those involved in health care—family health services authorities, district health authorities, local authorities, social services, general practitioners whether fund-holders or not—for the patchwork of boundary reorganisation that is proposed for north Yorkshire.

Therefore, it is incumbent on the Minister to say what evidence has been submitted to him. What overwhelming evidence has he had from the local community that suggests the need for these changes?

Surely the truth is that in north Yorkshire, as in the north west, East Anglia, the south-west, Trent and the northern region, the Government have been forced, because of the market mechanism, to make horrid changes in respect of health authorities and to create new, super arrangements. They hope that, by introducing such measures, the large institutions and organisations can cope better with the market and the internal negotiations that take place.

That has nothing to do with patients' involvement or local community involvement in the delivery of services. It is a million miles from the commitments given in the White Paper about the need to maintain local accountability. "Working for Patients" states that it was to be devolutionary, not revolutionary.

However, a revolutionary change is taking place in region after region with no real public debate, no national strategy from the Government about the result and no word from the NHS management executive about the intended result of the reforms as regards numbers, size, or the setting of priorities in decision-making about NHS service delivery.

The proposals for north Yorkshire are unacceptable and not simply because of the local community's gut reaction. There are sound economic reasons for not proceeding with the proposals. There are also sound management reasons and sound reasons related to the delivery of service.

Early in the discussions about the proposal a statement was issued and carried in the Health Service Journal of 18 April 1991. It was made by the then project leader, Roger Brown, who was, I assume, either an official of a regional health authority or of the Department of Health official. It stated:

"The main aim of the project was to consult with local people. GPs. voluntary groups, the family health service authorities, to pin down gaps in services and to develop ideas for improvements."

6 Whether or not one agrees with changes in the health service, on the face of it, that was an excellent approach to the development of services in the long term and the involvement of those who are delivering services or who are involved in the promotion and creation of services.

What has happened since that commitment was given in that press statement from the Department? Consultation has been, to say the least, brief. The views of those who have been consulted have been virtually ignored. If the view expressed by the civil servant in the press release is to be taken at face value, the response to the proposal was overwhelmingly hostile in relation to the geographical nature of the changes, accountability at local level, central administration, cost-effectiveness, purchasing arrangements and their effect on local purchasing and local participation.

I should like to give the Committee a flavour of the objections that have been placed before the Minister, and which one must assume, given the decisions that have been made about the statutory instrument, he either ignored completely or overruled because of some other overwhelming evidence that has not yet reached the public domain although we hope that it will reach it this morning. On geographical boundaries, for example, Airedale, Harrogate and Richmondshire stated that:

"Geographical distance makes practical unity impossible" in terms of affinity, natural links, response to need, use of service and building up new links to improve services. That is quite a damning indictment from those local authorities, which are all Conservative-controlled.

On accountability there are four major objections, from local authorities, community health councils, community groups and GPs. For example, Harrogate and Richmond said:

"How is this reform in keeping with the main purpose of health service reforms, to develop decision-making and responsibility to local people?—purchasing effectively is being removed from local involvement and local control.

That is completely against the commitment given by the Department in the White Paper.

"Proposals for 'local involvement' are not met by establishing local offices of employees—it does not meet the need for the Chair and Authority having close links with local people and organisations."

That was the view of Richmondshire district council while Airedale community health council and Ryedale district council, which is not labour controlled, stated:

"Uniformity and cost-effectiveness are principal themes of the Consultative Document, not patient views. Inadequate account of how these will influence decision-making."

Harrogate said that a result of the proposals would be

"Inadequate account of health education, infection control etc at present done locally".

That is another damning indictment by an organisation responsible for the co-ordination of such activities. The position is the same regarding the proposed centralisation of administrative decisions. JPEG 7 Northallerton district community health council said:

"A GP with a list of 7.000 is considered to be a viable purchasing unit: therefore what is the need for a larger body in North Yorks?".

That is to say, if it is viable for a general practitioner to have the right to purchase health care in the market, why is there a need for such huge amalgamations which will take away from districts the right to be involved in purchasing health care in their community? That makes no sense—there is no logic in the proposal.

Richmondshire district council said:

"The assumption that this county-wide authority would emerge regardless has resulted in DHA staffs being run down through the "option" of joining Trusts or for the North Yorks Health Project, therefore appearing inadequate".

That is an accusation that the Government are deliberately undermining the morale of those working in the health service in this area by giving the impression that the amalgamations are necessary and inevitable.

The most damning objections to the proposals have come from those in community groups, community health councils and local authorities attached to community health councils. There is to be a proposal to reduce dramatically the number of persons involved in community health councils across north Yorkshire. My hon. Friend the Member for York (Mr. Bayley) and other Opposition Members will be giving details of the numbers involved.

The Government have indicated that there will be local committees to consider arrangements and encourage contact with the local community. Those committees will have no statutory rights—like the community health councils. There is nothing to say how they will be funded—like the CHCs. The proposals are totally inadequate: they will mean the loss of local statutory rights to be involved in the development of services and to consider how they are delivered and managed, the loss of access to the complaints procedure through the community health councils and the loss of access to public involvement through the community health councils.

A great deal of local knowledge will be lost from those who are involved in community health councils, the overwhelming majority of whom I imagine are involved not because of party political considerations, but because they are connected with local community groups helping people with health problems. Their experience and knowledge of the health service is being cast aside because of bureaucratic changes. The health service will lose experienced people who are committed to it and who work regularly and free of charge in the interests of patients and potential patients.

We want the Government to say that, whatever else they will do, they will seriously reconsider the position and ensure that when the proposed changes 8 have been introduced, there will be a review. The Government should make a commitment to allow community health councils, as they currently exist, to continue having the same statutory rights and funding. Most importantly, local communities and the health service should be able to continue to draw on the experience and knowledge of that core of people involved in community health councils in north Yorkshire.

At the very least, the Minister should give us that commitment, which would signal to those in north Yorkshire that the Government are still interested in what local people have to say about the delivery and management of services.

Mr. Sackville
: That is not entirely the main emphasis given in some newpapers. Either way, I fear that what was said displayed an unwillingness to accept or understand the nature of the reforms which separate the purchaser and provider. That means that the health authorities' role is entirely different from what it was a few years ago. They are not service providers or hospital managers. They now purchase health care so the analogy of health authorities as they were some years ago is inappropriate.

Therefore, as health authorities have a new, reduced role and do not employ a large number of people to manage hospitals, there is obvious common sense in examining whether it is practical to continue to have nearly 200 health authorities. Yorkshire, as would be expected, has been ahead of the game and it has taken the reforms upward in exemplary fashion, showing the rest of Great Britain the way and how the reforms should work. A considerable reduction in the number of health authorities is likely. Including the amalgamations contained in the orders, I expect a fall from 170 to around half that number although I cannot be sure because decisions, based on all sorts of criteria, will be taken locally.

JPEG 9 I was asked to justify the principle behind the amalgamation of health authorities. First, most health authorities are too small to be viable and do not have sufficient flexibility and purchasing power. Therefore, it makes sense to amalgamate to ensure that they can deal better with and take an overview of an area's health needs. There are a number of special considerations relevant to the two amalgamations that we are discussing and I shall deal with them later.

The question of joint boundaries with other agencies, particularly with family health services authorities and with the social services—in effect, the local authorities—is also important. They will increasingly have to work together over joint purchasing and community care. The amalgamations which the orders will bring about will make the health authorities coterminous with the FHSAs and with local social services. That is satisfactory. There will be economies of scale which will allow savings to be made on administration. There is little point in duplicating roles in several health authorities when they could be better fulfilled by one. I expect considerable administrative savings over time, particularly in north Yorkshire.

The reforms have had the important result of breaking through artificial barriers. Distances are measured more in terms of travelling time than mileage because of our improved roads and general communications.
