9.15 pm

The crucial point here is that as each year goes by the Minister will make up the shortfall, or some of it, for the period and pay it out. There is the question of when, which I shall come to shortly. But it is not clear to me from part III that all of the shortfall will be made up. We shall be seeking confirmation on that point.

The Minister will do the paying and he will not fund. That is the change from funding to pay-as-you-go. The Minister's view is that basically the only change proposed is from funding to pay-as-you-so, and the Minister picks up the tab, whatever method is used.

In addition to the eminently sensible and practical points raised by my hon. Friend the Member for Newcastle upon Tyne, Central (Mr. Cowans), who has a long experience of railway pensions and has been drawing on that experience to the advantage of the Committee in a way that I cannot, a further question is this. Why have the Government introduced what can be described as this deferred payment scheme? That is what it is, except that with luck the payments will not be behindhand but current, and not accumulated prior to being paid out.

We have been told that the Public Accounts Committee thought that a bad thing, that the degree of funding contem- JPEG 1901 plated for British Rail pensions was as large and as big a proportion of various things such as, no doubt, the public sector borrowing requirement, and so on. But I suspect that to some extent the views of the Public Accounts Committee may have reflected the slightly jaundiced view of Conservative Members about public sector pensions in the first place. It may have felt that spending money on public sector pensions was not altogether a good thing, however it was done, but particularly if funded, because of the large sums involved.

Pay-as-you-go could be described in economic terms as expansionary. To that extent it would be welcome, because any expansion of the economy would be welcome. It also has the advantage for the Government of being cheaper in the short run. I emphasise "in the short run". There is no immediate need for large sums for funding. One does not need to make any provision now for future inflation. Conversely, one must continue providing in the future funds that have not been saved up for in the past. In that sense it is advantageous for the Government, particularly with their present obsession with reductions in public expenditure.

Any reduction of public expenditure in this sphere now carries with it in the inevitable consequence that there will be an increase in public expenditure later. The sums which are not being accumulated now will have to be paid out directly, without saving, in the future. I regard this as all of a piece, in that sense, with other aspects of the Bill—in particular the selling off of the NFC.

Selling off public assets is similar to stopping funding. Oddly enough it is wildly at variance with what I am sure is the generally prudent approach adopted by the Prime Minister's much-talked-about grocer father. I cannot believe that he would behave in such a way. I imagine that he would agree with very secure funding, certainly not in flogging off all the assets to get a short-term gain for one year.

Therefore, I have doubts about the general economic approach. It is not based on a prudent approach to economic matters or to pensions. It seems to be a typical Treasury short-term approach—that is, to save a few bob now and when problems arise in the future to deal with them as best one can.

1902 The Minister commended the non-funding—that is, the pay-as-you-go—approach on the ground that it was his Department's contribution to savings in public expenditure. I should be grateful to know whether he and the Department are receiving full credit for that. There are substantial savings from such funding. If he is being given all the credit, it seems to me that he was not going to receive any money at all for transport expenditure from any other source and that the funding of pensions can be envisaged as the bulk of public expenditure on transport for the next year or so.

My other question concerns something that the Minister raised on Second Reading, when he told the House:

"I might say that the board has also argued that the funding payments provided under the existing funding orders are not enough to fund the deficiencies fully."—[ Official Report, 27 November 1979; Vol. 974, c.1131.]

If I understand that aright, it amounts to an unfunded deficiency. I should like to know whether the Minister's proposals, not for funding but for pay-as-you-go, will meet the whole of the difference between the funded part of the schemes and the totality that is paid out, including the under-funding from the 1974 and 1978 arrangements that the board spoke about. That is something which the pensioners are entitled to know.

Subsection (4) states:

"Payments under subsection (1) shall be made,"—

we now come to a phrase about which I am not too keen—

"so far as practicable, not later than the day on which the pensions, increases and expenses to which they relate fall to be paid or incurred."

It seems to me that if the pensions scheme is under an obligation to pay out money to pensioners by a particular date, there is absolutely no reason why the Minister should not be under a similar obligation. It would be most unfortunate if, as a result of this let-out—the words "so far as practicable"—the Minister's pay-as-you-go make-up were delayed in any way so as to cause problems either for the pension schemes or, more importantly, for the pensioners themselves. Therefore, I seek an explanation as to why that phrase needs to be included in the legislation.

Clearly, there is general concern about pensioners' security. But there is another JPEG 1903 detailed matter that I should like to raise at this stage which in a sense is the nub of part III. Certain issues of principle are involved. I am not attempting to score points, but I am not clear from this clause or any other, or from any explanatory memoranda, whether the pay-as-you-go proposals as formulated in the clauses that we shall consider could work to the pensioners' disadvantage, in that surpluses accrued by funding one scheme may be taken into consideration towards the Minister's total contribution to make up the shortfall on the schemes in general.

The usual position with funded pension schemes is that if there is an excess of money above what the actuaries regard as necessary, both to pay current pensions and to meet future obligations, the trustees can consider inproving benefits to existing and future pensioners. As I read the Bill, I am not at all clear whether surpluses accrued on one or two schemes—and there are a large number of small schemes involved—may be taken away, to the disadvantage of pensioner members, and used to reduce what might be described as the Minister's liability to contribute to the total. I am concerned about that clawback aspect, particularly when one or two schemes may be in surplus.

Another point occurs to me on examining the provisions of clause 43 and others. The matter is difficult because some parts of clause 43 do not make any sense unless one reads subsequent clauses. Consequently, it is rather difficult for me even to explain some of the problems that have occurred to me.

However, something which so far as I can see has been raised in some of the meetings that the Minister has had with pension scheme representatives is whether, in the face of the possibility of a dubious or arguable actuarial assessment of the shortfall on the funded schemes, the current schemes which are self-funding may be endangered. A scheme's actuaries might see a shortfall which could be made up only by not paying in sufficient funds to the currently-running element of the scheme that is self-funding, or possibly by—this is what could bother pensioners—reducing pensions from the current funded schemes to make up for losses against which the trustees felt they had to secure themselves as a result of the actuarial assessments.

We must recognise that there may be 1904 severe problems for trustees in such circumstances. If they find that there is a shortfall in one sphere and the Minister's actuaries are adamant that they will not make that up above a certain point, they are faced with a difficult decision concerning their other responsibilities. They cannot ignore their obligation to pay close attention to a shortfall in one aspect of the scheme. They may therefore feel obliged to pay less into the current funding, making damaging changes to the pensions now being paid, or to make reductions in the pension arrangements for future pensioners. The funds could get into serious difficulty if, as I suspect, that is a possibility. However, I am not absolutely sure that it is a possibility. We shall wish to explore that further in due course.

The Minister is introducing a fundamental change on top of a previous fun- 1908 damental change. The present pension system really dates back to 1974. The funding of the obligations is embodied in the 1974 Act. We have heard about the Public Accounts Committee's attitude as to whether it is necessary to fund the pension schemes of nationalised concerns if one assumes that the Government will always step in to meet the obligations.

However, it is interesting to note that the Labour Government in 1974 were of the opinion that the view that one did not have to fund the pension obligations of nationalised industries was leading to increasing financial difficulties for British Rail. It was envisaged that in coming years more and more of the moneys given to British Rail for operating the industry would have to be devoted to meeting pension requirements. That was why a major reorganisation of the financing took place in 1974. The Labour Government recognised that there had been a build-up of liabilities and that a vast unfunded liability was facing British Rail as a nationalised industry. Governments have gained since then by not having had to contribute to a pension fund over the intervening period.

The Public Accounts Committee and other Committees of this House held the view, which has also been expressed in legislation, that nationalised industries should have commercial objectives, should compete with other commercial firms and should face commercial obligations. In those circumstances the last Administration took the view that the best way was to fund their pension systems, as opposed to those of civil servants, who work for an organisation that will remain. I think that we can presume that Governments will still be here even if businesses disappear. There will always be civil servants. One can understand that point. One can see the reason for funding the pay-as-you-go principle.

That conclusion which was accepted at the time, is now to be overturned. The present system, by which the liabilities of the pension funds were to be assessed and then financed, is now to be changed. The assessment at that time was £1,000 million—a tremendous amount of money. I believe that the latest estimate, made in 1979, was that the liabilities stood at about £925 million. That is an awful lot of money.

When we realise that, we get some indication of the attraction of the proposed scheme for the present Govern- JPEG 1909 ment. That represents another great difference of attitude towards public expenditure between the Government and the Opposition.

It is true that under the present scheme the Labour Government would have applied the same principle from 1986. Under the Bill, support payments will be made by the Government only as and when the pensions to which they correspond are paid to the pensioners concerned. The Minister is, therefore, placed under an obligation to meet the emerging costs corresponding to the historic obligations which cannot be met from the assets of the pension schemes concerned

After the enactment of the Bill an actuarial assessment will be made of each scheme in respect of which there are historic obligations. From this assessment will be calculated the unfunded proportion appropriate to that scheme—the proportion which the value of the liabilities of the scheme, corresponding to the historic obligations less the value of the associated assets of the scheme, bears to the value of the liabilities, The Minister will 1918 then be required to pay over to the scheme each year sums equal to the unfunded proportion of the scheme's total outgoing on pensions corresponding to the historic obligation.

I want to meet that concern as far as possible. It will be difficult to do so by having a regular review, as suggested by Mr. Kentridge and his colleagues.
