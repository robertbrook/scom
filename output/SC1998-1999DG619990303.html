I wish to make a substantive statement to the Committee because the regulations and the work done on them by Ministers' officials, employers' organisations, trade unions and others involved in the process cannot be overestimated.

I am proud that we have come almost to the end of a road set out on nearly 80 years ago, when first the Labour party tried to divide the House on the then King's Speech to introduce a national minimum wage. The Government gave an election promise that they would introduce a national minimum wage. We are now on the brink of delivering that promise.

Hardly anyone in Britain, except Conservative Members, opposes the principle of the minimum wage or the way in which we are introducing it. 6 The minimum wage has been good for employment and will continue to be so. I will make the business case for the minimum wage and set out in spades why that is good also. I was going to say that all Committee members have welcomed the minimum wage. However, as the hon. Gentleman has made it clear that that is not the case, I think that I will scrub such a remark.

Business regards the minimum wage as making good business sense. Low wages do not make sense, as they lead to a vicious circle of low morale, low performance and low productivity. That damages the capacity of small and medium companies in the local marketplace to receive or retain contracts for goods and services because they are competing not on the basis of quality, but by undercutting.

A prime example is a contract cleaning company in my area, which pays its workers well: they receive more than £3.60 an hour. The company trains its workers, gives them qualifications and allows them time off to go to college. However, it must compete with an employer who is paying £1.10 an hour, and so loses contracts.

That anti-competitive practice is additional to matters of social justice raised by the minimum wage. In a flexible economy, employers must not be allowed to achieve advantages over their competitors in the marketplace through a cycle of continuing low pay. That is why an overwhelming number of employers now support the national minimum wage.

Poor morale is a real cost: estimates from the Confederation of British Industry indicate that absenteeism costs British businesses up to £25 billion a year. That is 10 times more than the estimated impact of the minimum wage on the wage bill, which is £2.4 billion. Wages mean that resources are going into the economy—especially local economies—and that money is spent on local goods and services.

The £2.4 billion figure is not a loss to the British economy, but represents an extension or gain to it. With that come goods and services, especially from small and medium companies, which work almost exclusively in their own local areas. Such companies exist against a background of lack of purchasing power for low-paid workers and people who are on benefit as a result of the failure of Conservative policies.

Staff turnover and recruitment are a real cost and a real burden in the low-pay sector. The London School of Economics estimated recruitment costs in 1997 and 1998 at £150 to £700 per vacancy. Some low-paying companies have employee turnover rates of 130 per cent. per year. That is equivalent to the whole work force disappearing every year, along with a third of the new work force. As a result, training and recruitment are continuous. That is a huge burden, which is caused mainly by the low morale and commitment to companies that results from low pay.

Low pay is a massive burden on employers. The minimum wage will help to lift that burden and give employers a better opportunity to make a return on their investment in their staff. It will also help to improve the quality of goods and services supplied to customers.

Committee members should not lose sight of the significant, long-awaited benefits that a large number of low-paid workers will receive for the first time. Such JPEG 7 workers include pieceworkers, agency workers, cleaners, caterers and security guards. We heard many stories of outrageously low rates of pay in those sectors when we debated what was to become the National Minimum Wage Act 1998. The regulations are designed to deal with that problem.

The minimum wage is part of the broader picture for ending low pay and attacking poverty. It forms an integral part of our overall strategy to promote work incentives and make work pay.

Workers will benefit in April from the minimum wage. Many will benefit again in October, when the working families tax credit comes in. It will guarantee that every working family will have an income for full time work of at least £190 a week. The tax credit will include a new child care credit up to £150 a week for families with two or more children. There will also be a generous disabled person's tax credit which will provide a guaranteed minimum income of at least £150 a week for a single disabled person who moves from benefit to full time work earning the minimum wage.

Those are significant steps to end in-work poverty and I cannot for the life of me understand why there is so much opposition to the proposals from Conservative Members.

JPEG 13 We decided not to proceed with a requirement for employers to provide a statement of the minimum wage on every pay slip. My Department is including in the guidance a model poster, which I hope that employers will want to display at the workplace. That seems a sensible approach and I believe that good employers will adopt it.

It is also important to ensure that workers are aware of their rights and employers of their obligations. We recognise that the Government have a role in that process and the national minimum wage provisions require us to publicise the minimum wage—an issue referred to by the hon. Member for Guildford. We are spending around £5 million to ensure that people know about the national minimum wage, who can receive it and when it commences.

We have decided to require employers not to keep specific records but to keep sufficient records instead. It will be left to the employer to judge what counts as sufficient in the first instance. We considered the issue carefully, because some businesses told us that they would welcome knowing precisely what records should be kept, while others told us that they wanted a light touch. As a result, the guidance will contain advice on the sort of records that might be regarded as sufficient.

That is a sensible starting-point. It should not reduce protection for workers, because they will still have a right of access to records if they believe that they do not receive the minimum wage. In the event of a dispute, it will still be for the employer to prove that he or she has paid the minimum wage, rather than for the worker to prove that he or she has not received it. It will still be an offence not to keep records or to keep or produce false records.

In providing for annualised hours and term-time workers, we recognised that special rules should apply for workers who receive an annual salary in regular payments but who work irregular hours at different times of the year—for example, workers who work only during school terms but are paid a regular amount through the year. Representatives of employers and workers confirmed the need for such rules, which we have devised for the regulations. The effect is to treat annual working hours as though they were evened out across the year, reflecting the evening-out of pay received. As a result, it should not be necessary for employers or workers to restructure existing arrangements. Both the Confederation of British Industry and the Trades Union Congress have welcomed that approach.

I hope that I have shown that the Government have taken pains to listen to responses, and have adjusted the regulations when appropriate, without reducing protection for workers or affecting the integrity of the minimum wage. As a result, I am confident that the regulations will achieve our main aim—the elimination of unfairly low pay—with minimum knock-on effects and disruption to existing arrangements. The cost, as estimated in the regulatory impact assessment, is confined purely to the impact on the paybill, at around 0.6 per cent. of the total United Kingdom paybill.

14 I hope that I have said enough about setting the rate of the minimum wage, which will be £3.60 for those aged 22 and over; £3.00 for those aged 18 to 21; and £3.20 for those aged 22 and over who are undertaking accredited training in the first six months of a new job. In that respect, the regulations also define what counts as accredited training.

The regulations also define what counts as pay the definition is broadly in line with the Low Pay Commission's recommendation. 28 The hon. Gentleman also raised issues about accredited training under regulation 13; the regulation is important because it clarifies the rate for those who are on the new deal. It does not cover those who are not on the new deal because we do not want to encourage training that is poor or ineffective or which is a means of getting round the payment of the minimum wage. We are trying achieve a balance between increasing the quality and effectiveness of training and encouraging employers to take people on and train them to high standards and the provision of the national minimum wage.

The hon. Gentleman also raised issues that should properly have been debated under previous regulations; nevertheless, I shall respond to him. Elements of pay that could also be excluded are overtime and shift premiums, allowances that are not consolidated into basic pay, the provision of tools, a uniform or other equipment needed for doing the job. That is fair and effective.

It is also essential to define what hours count when the minimum wage is payable, a matter to which the hon. Gentleman referred. In general, the times when a worker is at work and required to be at work will count, as well as time taken travelling or training during working hours. That is only fair. Employees in such circumstances are under the employer's control and direction, and what they are doing related to their employment. They will therefore be covered by the minimum wage. That is important for employees on, for example, zero hour contracts without such definitions. Such exploitation could otherwise continue. It is generally accepted that the way in which we define the hours that count for the national minimum wage is reasonable and sensible, and has the support of the business community, as has the way of calculating a worker's hourly rate of pay.

The issues that the hon. Gentleman raises are important, but because we shall provide quality information and guidance for employers, any anxieties about those matters will quickly evaporate. Our work with employers is designed to help them and give them advice, as are the regulations.

In working with employers, we have a responsibility to ensure that the training that is provided is quality training. One of the side-effects of the introduction of the minimum wage and the regulatory regime relating to trainees will be to give a big boost to quality training and effective training as they will clarify what constitutes training and define quality. A significant amount of work involving employers' organisations and the Department for Education and Employment has gone into the regulations that deal with training to ensure, for the first time in the United Kingdom, clarity in defining quality of training. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will—I am sure that employers will—find the regulations that deal with training helpful in driving up the quality of training and defining who can receive the minimum wage during training.
