Sheffield Development Corporation (Area and Constitution) Order 1996

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Sir Paul Beresford)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Sheffield Development Corporation (Area and Constitution) Order 1996.

The order is required to wind up the Sheffield development corporation. It revokes the 1988 order which designated the urban development area and established the development corporation. It will take effect in two stages. First, it will remove urban development area status on 1 April and, secondly, will revoke on 1 July that part of the 1988 order which established the development corporation.

Under that two-stage process, the corporation will cease to exercise any operational functions after 31 March 1997. It will then have three months in which to prepare its final accounts, to report and to wind up its affairs. On 1 July, it will finally be dissolved.

Two other orders have been laid before the House in respect of the wind-up. One transfers the Sheffield development corporation's planning functions to Sheffield city council on 20 March 1997; the other transfers to the Secretary of State for the Environment those of the corporation's property rights or liabilities that have not been disposed of by 21 March. The orders are subject to the negative resolution procedure.

Sheffield development corporation—the SDC—is the fourth urban development corporation in England to wind up. We intend to wind up the remaining eight next year. Urban development corporations—UDCs—were always intended to be short-life bodies, charged with the specific task of bringing economic regeneration, new life and new hope to some of our most severely run-down urban areas. It is precisely because the SDC and the other UDCs have been so successful in meeting their remit that we are able to wind them up.

In 1988, the lower Don valley, once considered to be the powerhouse of the world, with a long history of great steel-producing firms—

Mr. Richard Caborn
(Sheffield, Central)
: Excellent.

Sir Paul Beresford
: In 1988, however, it was a very sad place. I am waiting for someone to say, "Hear, hear".

In 1988, there were vast areas of dereliction and decaying, unused buildings, a legacy of manufacturing industries going through hard times with little confidence of any good coming out the other side. 4 Some steel companies had steered their way through a major restructuring in the industry and were beginning to succeed in the new competitive economic climate. Numerous small companies were just holding on, hoping for something better round the corner. Although there were signs of action—the Meadowhall project was one of note—in reality, the task was too large and too deep-seated for the existing agencies to deal with in any acceptable time scale. Time was important. The city's reputation in the country was poor and confidence in its future was low.

Therefore, the Government established the development corporation with a single mission: to regenerate the lower Don valley. Surprisingly—especially in retrospect—not everybody welcomed that move. Some critics claimed that, with the same resources, the city council could have achieved the same results. The corporation had a single-minded approach which focused entirely on regeneration without any competing demands and priorities. It drew on private-sector expertise and built up a regeneration team of considerable expertise. I hope that, after nine years, even those critics think differently. For example, the current leader of Sheffield city council has publicly stated that the transformation in the lower Don valley could not have been achieved without an organisation such as the corporation.

It is worth spending a few moments to consider why the SDC gains such praise from so many quarters. I shall touch on a few statistics: when the SDC was set up, 350 hectares out of a total of 800 were derelict and most were held in small parcels. By assembling decent-sized development areas and reclaiming land from past contamination, 239 hectares have been brought back into full use.

The corporation followed a professional marketing strategy based on meeting customer needs, aimed at improving the image of the valley and of Sheffield as a whole. As a result, it has succeeded in attracting over £680 million private sector investment. This represents £7 of private money for every £1 of public money invested.

Major infrastructure projects have literally forced new life into the area. The £30 million Don valley link spine road has opened up land for development and provided what had previously been lacking—fast links to the city centre and importantly to the motorway network. This has made land alongside the Don valley link particularly attractive to inward investors. Nearly 500,000 sq m of new commercial floor space has been built. From all this 18,000 jobs have been created during the corporation's lifetime.

I am aware that this jobs figure includes 7,000 jobs in the Meadowhall regional shopping complex, which was planned and started before SDC came along. But we must recognise that it is due to the SDC that thousands of jobs have and will come on board during and after its lifetime as a direct result of its entrepreneurial activities and that 30 million visitors each year are attracted to Meadowhall, many of whom would not otherwise have come to Sheffield. A good number will visit other places in the city and use public transport, all helping the local economy. This was once JPEG 5 an area in terminal decline. It has now been brought into the mainstream of a mixed economy. It has a wide variety of employers in manufacturing, distribution, industry, offices and even food production. There is therefore a much wider choice of employment, including part-time employment; yet industry and manufacturing remain important. For example, Sheffield produces more steel now than it did in 1943 at its peak.

The SDC's achievements sound impressive enough. I can assure members of the Committee that they are even more impressive when one has visited Sheffield, as I have. Some hon. Members live in the area and know it well. I have seen the difference that the corporation's hard work has made over the past few years.

The area looks and feels so different, but it has not been an easy ride. The corporation would be the first to acknowledge that there have been setbacks and difficulties in both projects and in establishing working relationships within the city. But overwhelmingly, this is a story of effort rewarded and I congratulate Hugh Sykes, SDC's chairman, his fellow board members and all the corporation's hard working officials who had both the vision and the determination to make things happen.

Two of SDC's projects illustrate how this tenacity has paid off. The first, is the regeneration of Sheffield's historic canal basin, now called Victoria Quays. This area close to the city centre had been derelict for 20 years, unnoticed by most people in Sheffield. The corporation worked in partnership with a number of developers, on refurbishing listed warehouses—while retaining their historical and architectural interest—and on new build schemes which would retain the character of the setting. This has produced a waterside development which the city can be proud of. One of the country's major law firms has moved to the city setting up its northern headquarters on the quays and a further 20,000 sq ft is available for letting; a pub and restaurant—satisfactorily as I can say from my visit—is already open and there is more to come; a four-star hotel will open in the summer and the Halifax has chosen the quays for the location of one of its new businesses. The area also offers a splendid setting for exhibitions and public events. The first and most noticeable was a boating carnival held last summer, attracting more than 60,000 visitors.

The second example where SDC's determination has paid off is its success in delivering a city airport for Sheffield. For over 50 years, Sheffield has been talking and speculating about an airport, but the corporation saw the opportunity and was determined that the city should have one. After several difficult years, SDC eventually struck a successful deal with a private developer and the corporation has had the pleasure of seeing the very first plane land on the runway at Tinsley Park. Construction is still under way and oil drums have had to be placed there to prevent enthusiastic amateurs landing illegally. The airport will be fully open later this year. Local businesses will soon benefit from the venture enabling them to deal more quickly and competitively with Europe and other UK centres.

6 The corporation's impact is not restricted to the lower Don valley. It has consistently worked to improve the image of Sheffield as a whole. It has taken a leading role in developing local partnerships such as the Sheffield city liasion group and has spearheaded work on a strategy for the city centre. It has helped to lift the awareness of companies looking for new locations that Sheffield might indeed be the right place to consider. This only rarely happened before 1988. It has restored the confidence of developers in the city, especially by its approach to a swift turnround of planning applications. I hope that the city council will pick up and learn from that when the planning functions move back to the authority.

The SDC has helped transform Sheffield from a city which was unsuccessful in the first two rounds of city challenge to one where partnership is now at the forefront. This has brought success in each of the three rounds of the single regeneration budget challenge fund, totalling £91 million. It has also secured the largest allocation in Yorkshire and Humberside for capital challenge adding just short of £11 million to the city's funds.

Prior to the SDC's designation, the immediate future for the lower Don valley—and perhaps for Sheffield itself—did not look good. Now that the corporation has put in place all the essential ingredients for continuing regeneration, the lower Don valley is well placed for continued growth and prosperity. For those projects which have not been fully completed in the lifetime of the Corporation, it has been at great pains to put suitable succession arrangements in place. The city council, English Partnerships and British Waterways are all playing a key role. The SDC has developed partnership into a new way of doing business in the city. By working together, the partners in Sheffield have already seen greater benefits than could have been achieved separately.

"After listing many of SDC's achievements, all these developments have helped create a new Sheffield with a more modern and diversified economy and we must thank SDC for their contribution to reinventing the City".

Nice phrase, but not mine.

It was the testimonial of Sheffield's Labour Lord Mayor who, until recently, was the deputy leader of the council's Labour group.

The Corporation's task of securing sustainable regeneration of the lower Don valley has been completed. It is right therefore that the corporation should be wound up while we congratulate it on all that it has done. That is why I commend the order to the Committee.

If regeneration has been successful in the lower Don valley—and I believe it has, substantially, on balance—it has also been successful in the whole of Sheffield because the development corporation has contributed to a general move in the 10 city to regenerate and improve itself, in concert and partnership between the development corporation, the city council and other private and public bodies in the city. The chamber of commerce, the Cutlers' Company, our two universities and many other organisations have all played very important and substantive roles in that process.

I part company slightly with Ministers when they seem to imply that partnership was something which the Government invented in the 1990s or which came about when development corporations were established. We know differently in Sheffield and even the hon. Member for Hallam will probably recognise that working in partnership has started in the mid-1980s before the development corporation came along. The private and public sectors in Sheffield were already working together. Many Labour councils up and down the country were working in partnership with their private sectors at a time when central Government did not want to recognise the importance of local government in that process.

The trust that was being built up at that time between the various sectors in our city was a good foundation for what came about and the resources that the development corporation had at its disposal. It has to be seen as part of that wider picture—regeneration of not only the lower Don Valley, but the city as a whole.

It has already been acknowledged that within the valley, even before the development corporation came into being, there were signs of regeneration taking place. The hon. Member for Hallam referred to his role in persuading the then Secretary of State to establish a development corporation for the city. He implied that all that happened was that the Government gave their blessing and asked the chamber of commerce and the Cutlers' Company if they wanted a development corporation and it was then established. Member for Hallam were brought together to enable more constructive redevelopment to take place—the city council, on behalf of the people of Sheffield, would act in a pragmatic way, accept the creation of the development corporation and do its best to work with it.

At its inception, the SDC established the first partnership agreement in the country between a development corporation and a council. The greatest test of what the development corporation has achieved concerns image. In the mid-1980s, I was not Member of Parliament for Attercliffe.
