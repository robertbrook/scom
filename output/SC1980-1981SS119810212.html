We are now plunging into quite a short clause, a technically interesting but not necessarily in itself highly controversial clause, which deals with the housing support grant. However, the most innocent of apparently technical changes can have behind the mask quite far-reaching effects, and I make no bones about the fact 454 that I am not entirely clear about all the implications of the clause. The amendment gives an opportunity for the Minister to explain rather more fully how these changes will operate before we come to the clause stand part debate, which it may shorten as a result.

To understand the point of the amendment, which deals with the new section 1(4A) that the Bill inserts in the Housing (Financial Provisions) (Scotland) Act 1978, one has to have at least a rough idea of how the housing support grant is calculated. I am sure that I shall be on common ground with the Minister in saying that I understand that at its simplest level it is a subtraction sum. What happens is that one takes the aggregate of eligible expenditures of housing authorities in Scotland and subtracts from that the aggregate relevant income of the, I think, 56 housing authorities in the nation. Once the aggregate is arrived at, the difference between the expenditure and income figure is the housing support grant. Then, obviously, for each individual authority a further individual sum is done on exactly the same basis to get the apportionment of the total which has been arrived at from the aggregate calculation.

That is simple and straightforward simplification of a very complicated process, in which there are many anxious—indeed, I sometimes think tortured—debates about the various factors taken into account and the various weightings to be ascribed to those factors. The eligible expenditure is basically loan charges plus management and maintenance charges, with some weightings for high-rise dwellings and sparse population. The relevant income is the assumed rent income, the implications of which we shall come to in the debate on the clause stand part, and the rate fund contribution.

The new subsection (4A) on the face of it is a perfectly sensible suggestion, because, as I understand it, the present law is that one has to aggregate in the first stage of the calculation all the eligible expenditure and relevant income of every one of the housing authorities in Scotland—the 53 district authorities and the three all-purpose authorities that make up the total. Subsection (4A) says that the Secretary of State at his discretion may omit from that aggregate calculation any local authority figures if he estimates that in the case of an individual authority the amount of the relevant income is likely to exceed the amount of the eligible expenditure.

In other words, if there is a surplus—or a notional surplus, because these are often notional figures—what subsection (4A)( a) says is that then it is not necessary to aggregate those figures in the Scottish total. Whether that is so we shall come to in the debate on clause stand part, but on the face of it that is actually an advantage to the remaining local authorities, which I hasten to concede to the Under-Secretary. Clearly, if there is an inverse balance on an individual local authority and it is included in the calculation, it will close the gap between expenditure and income and one would imagine would normally reduce the amount of housing support giant that is available

"he determines, under section 2 of this Act, that no proportion of the aggregate amount of the housing support grants is to be apportioned to that authority".

Mr. Rifkind
: This is an important and complicated matter. I shall confine my remarks specifically to the amendment. The hon Gentleman has asked a reasonable question, to which I shall try to respond.

The clause, including paragraph ( b), is a matter on which there has been full consultation with the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities, which understands the reasons for it and has not raised any objections to it. I think that the hon Gentleman will understand why when I explain.

As the hon Gentleman has correctly pointed out, paragraph ( b) envisages the possibility of a situation in which, although the expenditure of an authority might be greater than its relevant income, it might not be entitled to any housing support grant. This is a possibility under the existing system, not because of any whim or discretion of the Secretary of State, but because of the distribution formula that applies for housing support grant. Although successive Governments have tried to get the distribution formula as near 100 per cent, reliable as possible, that has never been entirely possible, for technical reasons.

There are two reasons why it is possible for an authority not to receive housing support grant although its estimated expenditure may be marginally or significantly greater than its relevant income. The first is what is known as the distribution factor, which has meant that, over recent years, the percentage of grant which a local authority has received has been equivalent to 85 per cent, of its assessed need. This has been meant to be an incentive to authorities with particular problems to keep down their costs.

456 We proposed to the convention this year that that distribution percentage should be abolished because the advantages which it was supposed to provide are of great doubt in any event. We felt that the time had come when it could be phased out, and this would have helped to deal with the particular problem which the hon Gentleman has raised.

The convention was at first sympathetic to that idea, but subsequently raised the point that it feared that an immediate abolition of this distribution percentage could create transitional difficulties for a small number of authorities which might be significantly affected by the consequential loss of income. We accepted the convention's advice and agreed with it that for next year the distribution percentage, instead of being 85 per cent., should be 90 per cent., and for the following year 95 per cent., and then gradually be phased out altogether. So, within a very short time, one factor which can lead to a local authority with expenditure greater than its relevant income not having housing support grant will disappear. It would have disappeared this year if the convention had agreed, but, for problems of transition, the convention was unhappy about that.

I shall try to be brief, because I do not want to lay myself open to criticism from hon Gentlemen for making excessively long speeches.

The amendment concerns the powers of investigation of the Commissioner for Local Administration, usually known as the local Ombudsman. I start by paying a tribute, in which all members of the Committee will share, to the work done by the local Ombudsman and his officials, which has been very useful to Members of Parliament, the public and local authorities.

Under the 1975 Act, the Commissioner is only empowered to investigate actions since 1975—that is, actions taken by new authorities but not by their predecessor authorities. I accept the general case for limiting the Commissioner's powers of investigation to the period' after 1975. Otherwise, we should be in danger of passing retrospective legislation, and in practical terms it is clearly difficult for a new authority to take responsibility for the actions of its predecessors.

The amendment is designed to deal with a particular administrative problem—where an alleged 488 maladministration before 1975 has become apparent after 1975. Lest hon Members should think that this a purely theoretical point, may I mention the case of a constituent who bought land from a predecessor authority before 1975 and built a house on that land. He subsequently discovered a mine shaft at the back of the house. I need not labour the resulting difficulties and problems. If he could have proved from the records, as a result of investigation by the Ombudsman, that the predecessor authority had known about the mine shaft but had not informed him of it, that would have been enormously helpful to him. I do not prejudge the case as to whether the authority was right or wrong.

The amendment would not open up the whole general area of action before 1975 to investigation by the Commissioner, because there are a number of limitations in the 1975 Act. It would simply give the Commissioner the discretion, in a few exceptional cases, to take up complaints about maladministration before 1975 which had become obvious only after 1975. It is a very limited amendment, but I think that it would be extremely helpful to a small number of people who have genuine problems and complaints which cannot be investigated under the present legislation.

Mr. Rifkind
: My hon Friend's amendment suggests that the local government Ombudsman should be able to look at matters as they affect local people and at the alleged maladministration that took place before 16 May 1975. I must tell him, however, that it would create major problems, not simply in terms of principle but in terms of practicality if such a change were to be made.

The date of 16 May 1975 is not merely an arbitrary date that was fixed but is, as I am sure my hon Friend recognises, the date on which the new local authorities came into operation. Therefore, apart from all the other problems that might arise from now allowing cases that arose earlier than that to be investigated, we would have the very practical problem that, if the Commissioner were to investigate such a case and to find in favour of the complainant, the local authority that would have been responsible for the maladministration had ceased to exist, is not able to be approached to rectify the problem. Therefore, there would be difficulty for successor authorities in dealing with the matter.

I am not suggesting that that by itself is absolutely conclusive to the argument. I use it as an indication of the difficult complexities that would arise. It is not simply a question of what one would do if the complaint were upheld. There is also the problem of the actual investigation of the complaint.
