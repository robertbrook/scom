Clause 14
INCREASED RATES ON 30TH NOVEMBER 1994

Ms Dawn Primarolo
(Bristol, South)
: I beg to move amendment No. 67, in page 11, line 31, leave out "£135" and insert "£133".

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to take the following amendments:

No. 68, in page 11, line 33, leave out "£135" and insert "£133".

No. 69, in page 11, line 36, leave out "£135" and insert "£133".

Ms Primarolo
: The amendment reduces vehicle excise duty from the Government's proposed amount of £135 to £133. In settling on that lower figure we took the retail prices index from November 1993 to November 1994, which stood at 2.6 per cent., and felt that that was the maximum reasonable increase in vehicle excise duty on cars. Bearing in mind the comments of the hon. Member for Blackpool, South (Mr. Hawkins) this morning and his reluctance to vote for tax increases because of his manifesto commitments, I hope that we can persuade him to vote with us this afternoon—that is if that commitment was worth the paper it was written on.

On 16 March 1993 vehicle excise duty was £110. On 17 March 1993 it was increased to £125. On 1 December 1993 it was increased to £130, and on 30 November 1994 the Chancellor proposed to increase it to £135. In just over 18 months vehicle excise duty has thus been increased by 22.7 per cent. Over the same period, the RPI has risen by only 4.3 per cent. If vehicle excise duty had been uprated by inflation only, its current rate would be £115.

At the same time as increasing vehicle excise duty, the Government have pursued a policy of increasing the duty on petrol. The duty on a litre of leaded petrol has risen by 9.8p in the three Budgets since 1993. If petrol duty had been increased and uprated solely by inflation, it would have risen by only 2p over that same period.

These increases represent the Government's one-club response to the Rio environmental targets to which they signed up. The Government constantly justify those increases in terms of that commitment, but to many people, particularly those in rural areas, they represent a severe blow to their living standards. In the absence of a decent public transport system in rural areas, it is hardly surprising that people are forced to rely on their cars and 70 so have to absorb the additional charges. If the Government were really serious about meeting environmental targets and using vehicle excise duty as a mechanism for that, they should perhaps consider varying vehicle excise duty according to the size of a car's engine. There is a strong argument that that would be fairer, as the poorest car owners will typically own smaller cars. They would then be more likely to be persuaded to pursue other issues relating to car pollution.

The increases in petrol duty and vehicle excise duty are another example of the Government's hidden tax increases; they are part of a policy of increasing indirect taxes. For instance, since 1979 there has been a massive increase in the percentage of tax in the price of petrol. In 1979, it was about 45 per cent. of the retail price of a gallon of petrol; by January 1995, it was 75 per cent.

Through our amendments, we hope to open up debate and to persuade the Government that they must consider pursuing policies that are environmentally friendly and designed not simply to increase Government revenue but to tackle the real environmental issues. Indeed, a few weeks ago, the Prime Minister's own advisers publicly challenged him and the Government to introduce green taxes. They made radical proposals about how to shift the emphasis in our taxation system, to become more environmentally friendly while providing the necessary revenue. I freely admit that we, too, do not have the answer to this series of questions. The report was challenging and far reaching. If it were decided to take such action, there would be a long and sometimes difficult process of transition. But road tax is a good starting point. We are attempting today to nudge—nicely—the Government in the direction of finally tackling the relationship between vehicle excise duty, duty on petrol and the use of the car instead of car ownership, as the beginning of recognising that we need radically to shift our taxes if we are all to put into practice what we so often repeat and some people preach. We need a taxation system that protects our environment as well as our finances and Treasury income.

I urge members of the Committee to vote for the amendments as a sign of good faith from this Finance Bill Committee that all of us, regardless of political party, intend to begin that difficult but vital transition from our present taxation system to a more environmentally friendly system.

The Paymaster General (Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory)
: The amendments would limit the increase in vehicle excise duty for cars, taxis and trailers. I should perhaps take the collective memory of the Committee a little further back than the point at which the hon. Member for Bristol, South (Ms Primarolo) began.

For many years it has been the practice to increase VED in multiples of £10 or £5. We have rounded the increase up to £5, although the Government's successful counter-inflation policy, if used as a strict criterion, would have delivered a smaller poundage increase.

Over the years, the Government have restrained the rate of VED to below the rate of inflation. Between 1985 and 1992—a period of seven years—there was no increase in VED. Since then it has increased either by £10 or £5 a year, and it is a £5 increase that we are proposing.

The hon. Lady mentioned the Rio environmental convention, suggesting that we had a one-club approach JPEG 71 to meeting our targets. I should point out that we were, as I recall, the first country to publish a comprehensive plan to meet the Rio targets. There is a tendency elsewhere in the world to sign up to things without having any idea of how to deliver. That is a sort of declaratory approach to the environment. We followed up our pledge to stabilise CO
2
emissions with a comprehensive plan. We are not relying on the so-called one-club approach of simply increasing fuel or VED rates.

Transport is only one sector involving carbon dioxide and other emissions. Although not the largest sector, transport emissions are increasing fastest. It is important that we find a way of limiting that, and a powerful incentive is to increase taxation. Fuel taxation is particularly effective because it encourages people to drive less, to seek other modes of transport and to drive more economically. It also influences people's choice of vehicle.

VED taxation also has a part to play. Some who wish us to abolish VED altogether and put the whole burden on fuel overlook the essential job of maintaining a comprehensive record of vehicle ownerhip. Even if VED were not used to raise tax, we should need to find a way to keep that record up to date, not only to combat car crime but because the record is important to recalling vehicles for safety checks and is a cheap, easy way to check that MOT certificates and insurance certificates are valid. The VED system is an important part of our system of motoring taxation and it is right that we should increase it from time to time.

That answers the hon. Lady's points, but if other hon. Members wish to contribute to debate I shall answer at the end.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory

Cars with smaller engines do not necessarily cause less pollution than those with larger ones. Old, small cars are among the worst polluters on the roads, whereas a modern car with a catalytic converter, even it if has a larger cubic capacity, can be more environmentally benign.

The Liberal Democrat amendment was a blunt instrument with which to achieve the same result as increasing the price of fuel, which encourages the motorist to economise in various ways, not only by buying smaller cars but by driving them more slowly and using them rather less.

Clause 20
CO-OWNERS ETC. OF BUILDINGS AND LAND

Ms Primarolo
: I beg to move amendment No. 72, in page 18, line 4, at end add JPEG 83

"provided that no such statutory instrument shall come into force until it has been approved by resolution of the Commons House of Parliament".

The clause deals with a new section of the Value Added Tax Act 1994 and applies to any case in which two or more persons together may make a supply of any interest in, right over or licence to occupy land.

The persons concerned, the grantors, are treated as a single person who is distinct from each of the grantors individually and is separately registered for VAT.

The grantors are jointly and severally liable for the property and the VAT obligations. Any notice for VAT purposes to the property owner may be served on any grantor.

Where a grantor ceases to be a grantor he or she remains liable for the property for the purposes of VAT in respect of any VAT returns period starting before the change is notified to the Customs. People become liable for an entire period.

We tabled an amendment, which was not selected, to deal with the problem. I hope that the Minister will examine the matter again because it seems unfair that people are still liable when they have no interest.

The amendment provides for a mechanism to respond to yet another requirement for a statutory instrument, and for a proper assessment of the likely impact of the proposal. There should be a resolution before Parliament on the changes after a report has been made and before we are required to discuss and agree the detail of the statutory instrument. We are attempting to obtain information before we are required to make a decision. That is a reasonable request.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory
: By way of introduction, it would be helpful if I sketched the purpose of the clause. It is designed to clarify the position of co-owners of property who make taxable supplies of that property.

At present where there are co-owners—they are often trustees—they are registered for VAT purposes as partnerships, but it has been put to the Government that they are not usually partnerships in the legal sense and that many of those involved resent being treated as partners because there may be significant direct tax disadvantages in being so considered.

We are responding to representations and removing that necessary status; instead, they will be registered as a single taxable person without the quasi-legal tag of partnership. That is a responsible way of responding to a legitimate anxiety. I am surprised that the hon. Lady should query it.

In relation to the substance of the amendment, it is not uncommon for the effective date of a measure to be determined by statutory instrument after the measure is passed by the House. That happened to last year's Finance Bill: A number of measures concerning the variation of penalties and the restriction of set-off of revenue debts were brought into force on a day determined by statutory instrument. Again, that is for the convenience of the tax paying public. There are occasions when businesses or those advising them, or indeed Customs and Excise or the Inland Revenue, need further time to prepare for new legislation. They might have to print some additional stationery and amend a computer system.

The Chairman

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory
: Clause 21 is intended to extend the exclusion of set-off to VAT credits arising after a business has traded out of insolvency. Those will no longer be set off against any remaining pre-insolvency debt. The clause also provides clarification that the exclusion from set-off during and after insolvency applies only to the setting off of those credits against pre-insolvency debts. This is a measure to help businesses that are trading out of insolvency, which the existing law does not do. Therefore, it should not be resisted by the Committee, but welcomed.

There is a Scottish dimension to this because Scottish insolvencies were fully covered in the existing provision, but, for technical reasons, it was not possible fully to include the reworded Scottish provisions in the published clause. This is one of the effects of having a separate legal system in Scotland—a fact of which I heartily approve, but it does mean that, from time to time, separate provisions or amendments have to be made to cater for a separate legal system. I encourage the Committee to accept the amendment.
