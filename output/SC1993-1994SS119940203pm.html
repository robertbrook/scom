"economy, efficiency and effectiveness in the use of resources." Until now, there has been little discussion about the costs of local government reorganisation. We hear about the back-to-basic issue, but today's debate is about getting back to real basics. The Government should be telling Scots about the true costs of reorganisation and about the basic issue of spending taxpayers' money wisely.

COSLA's figure show that the costs of this expensive fiasco could be as much as £720 million. More significantly, it states that there will be no savings. That should be contrasted with the financial memorandum which refers to 44 significant annual savings and transitional cost of only £196 million, which the Government hope will be partly offset by the first year of reorganisation in April 1996.

The debate is about making a judgment about whom we believe. We do not accept the Government's figures, which I shall explain in a moment. We acknowledge that it is a complex issue that deals with the future and with matters that are difficult to quantify. The scale of the difference means that there is a huge credibility gap in anything that the Government say about costs.

Let us consider when the mess was created. In October 1992, the then Secretary of State for Scotland said: JPEG 45

"In all of this we must pay heed to the issue of cost. Irrespective of the structure chosen …. I regard it as essential that the new system should be able to deliver services to the taxpayer at less cost."

That is not what the COSLA document says. Even if we give or take a few hundred million pounds, the reorganisation will cost taxpayers, families and the business community dear.

The statement that I have quoted was vacuous. Matters went from bad to worse. The Government commissioned the famous Touche Ross report, which represents £50,000 of wasted public expenditure, simply to tell the Government what they wanted to know. A case of putting rubbish in, in the shape of a brief, and taking rubbish out, in the shape of a document. I have no qualms about saying that. If, when the Opposition are in the Scottish Office, we spend £50,000 of taxpayers' money, we shall ensure that we get value for money. We shall not believe irresponsibly. Touche Ross decided to help the Government. Its report gave figures for the four options to be published. It said that the cost for the 24 option, the nearest to the one selected, would be £226 million over five years. Later, the report gave a figure of savings of £120 million, virtually for ever. It may all be the stuff of fairy stories, or horror stories, but how can such nonsense be accepted as a positive response to a £50,000 investment by the Government?

The saga went from bad to worse. In July 1993, in the document "Shaping the Future—The New Councils", the Secretary of State began to unravel the farce. The document mentions the implications of the Touche Ross report, stating:

"While these estimates … will no doubt feature in an ongoing debate on costs, there is now widespread acceptance of the fact that a single-tier structure of local government has the potential to yield significant saving in terms of costs."

Let us not worry about the semantics of the word "potential". The Secretary of State said that there was a consensus that a single-tier structure would save costs. But there is no consensus. It is just another vacuous statement that ignores reality. The Secretary of State continued:

"The Government's reasons for undertaking this reform are, of course, more wide-ranging than the single issue of costs."

We know what those other considerations beyond costs are. The Secretary of State went on:

"These savings will arise because fewer managers will be needed than in the present structure."

That is the one reason the document gives for saving £200 million. Can anyone believe such nonsense? I do not see anyone on the Government Benches getting animated about the Touche Ross report. Perhaps Conservative Members want to forget about it. I sincerely hope that no public finance will be wasted simply to get local authorities into this sort of tangle.

Finally, there are the financial effects of the Bill as the memorandum outlines them. Not content with suggesting that the cost will be £196 million—with either £22 million or £66 million recouped each year—a suggested saving of £1 billion—the Secretary of State tells us that we shall begin to save on 6 April 1996. That is the date the councils take over. How can anyone claim that £50 million will be saved in the first year of reorganisation?

Throughout, the Government's willing allies, the consultants, have given them what they want to hear, but now all that mythology has been blown away by the report from the COSLA.
