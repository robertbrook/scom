RATES AND RATING IN NORTHERN IRELAND

10.30 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for Northern Ireland (Mr. Tom Pendry): I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the matter of rates and rating in Northern Ireland.

I welcome the opportunity for this debate, because it has become clear to me that the question of the regional rate in Northern Ireland, and particularly how it is arrived at, is one which is a cause of concern both to ratepayers and to those who represent them. It is for this reason indeed, that I issued the report of the working party setting out the problems of assessing the regional rate and asking for h. Ms.' comments, for those of other elected representatives and indeed of anyone interested. This meeting of the Committee gives me the valuable opportunity of hearing h. Ms.' responses.

H. Ms. will know that the rating system currently applying in Northern Ireland, and, so far as the regional rate is concerned peculiar to Northern Ireland, arose from the decision in 1972 by the Government of Northern Ireland to reorganise local government along the lines recommended by the Review Body on Local Government, generally known as the Macrory Report. This reorganisation was endorsed by the Stormont Parliament when it passed the Local Government Act in 1972 transferring many major services from the former local authorities to central Government Departments and setting up 26 district councils to carry out the remaining functions. The district councils, as h. Ms. will be aware, strike their own rates to finance their own services, and the Department of Finance makes the regional rate for all Northern Ireland to raise part of the cost of services now carried out by the central Government Departments.

It is, of course, this latter regional rate with which I think the Committee is most concerned. It is, however, not like a normal local authority rate, which is Col. 2 struck when, in broad terms, a local authority has decided on its policies, seen what grants it will receive, what money it will have in hand and therefore what it needs to raise by way of rates and what poundage needs to be struck. With such a normal local authority rate, therefore, a ratepayer can know what services his local authority is providing for the money he pays in rates, since these services are quite clearly defined and separate from any others provided by other bodies or central government. But the regional rate is not like that. It is related not to services which can be clearly isolated but to those which are part and parcel of wider government services.

It seems rather a pity that the regional rate ever became known as a rate, when it is much more nearly a tax. Indeed the Minister who introduced the Rates (Northern Ireland) Order in the House of Commons in October 1972 stated that the Northern Ireland ratepayer would pay his regional rate into the Northern Ireland Exchequer like any other tax, and would thereby have a burden of rates equitable by comparison with his counterpart in the rest of the United Kingdom. He went on to say that it would become impossible beyond 1973–74 to calculate the regional rate by reference to what services had been transferred to central government from the former local authorities.

Furthermore, on 4 April 1974 in the Northern Ireland Assembly the Head of the Department of Finance spelled out in great detail why the regional rate could not be related to specific services and stressed that it was a contribution towards the cost of regional services. I am sure that it is clear to hon. Members, therefore, that the current system of rating in Northern Ireland, and in particular the concept of the regional rate, is not a burden imposed by direct rule but was devised and endorsed by both the Stormont Parliament and the Assembly.

Of course, many find it difficult to accept that the regional rate cannot be assessed on the basis of precise expenditure in the way of the normal local authority rate. They ask why we could not have continued to cost the services as they were originally carried out by the former local authorities. We could not have carried on like this since it would have been unrealistic to assume that those services, as they were in 1973, would remain the same either in content Image Col. 3 or expenditure or in the degree of central government grant support obtaining.

10.35 a.m.

Any rate based on such an idea of freezing the situation as in 1973 would be purely speculative. I feel that we would not have been any better able to show the Northern Ireland ratepayer that he was getting a fair deal nor the United Kingdom taxpayer that the Northern Ireland citizen was bearing a fair share of rates and taxes.

Another idea put forward by some is to select services similar to those taken over from local authorities into central Government Departments and to fix a rate based on the expenditure on these. The problem here is not just one of isolating or costing services but of deciding what portion of the cost of such services should fall on the ratepayer and what on the taxpayer who would meet the balance of the cost.

This leads me to an important point which I feel I must stress. The Macrory report recommended that in financing the reorganisation of local government in 1973 there should be no net transfer from the ratepayer to the taxpayer, and the Government have firmly taken this stand since. In addition, this means that the Northern Ireland ratepayer should not pay less than his counterpart in the rest of the United Kingdom. I think that we all accept that this is a fair and reasonable approach.

One has, of course, to acknowledge that if the Northern Ireland ratepayer and taxpayer is paying the same as his Great Britain counterpart he should receive the same services. Whilst there can be no absolute comparison of services—they vary, indeed, from area to area within the United Kingdom—it is Government policy to see that the Northern Ireland services are comparable with the rest of the United Kingdom.

I think everyone realises the desirability of showing the ratepayer that he is getting a fair deal. Indeed, the Government have clearly shown their awareness of this problem by setting up the working party to look into the fairest way of assessing the regional rate. In the report of the working party, various methods are examined. The Government are ready to examine others which seem to have merit, and I should like to have such comments from interested parties by the end of May this year. As the report was published on 5 February this Col. 4 year, that gives a rather longer period of consultation than normal.

I wish to make it abundantly clear that the Government have not made up their mind on these issues, and I shall take on board, and give proper consideration to, all the points put to me. I look forward in particular to hearing today the considered views of members of this Committee on this complicated and admittedly controversial topic, for I regard today's debate as an essential part of the consultation following on the publication of the report of the working party.

10.57 a.m.

Mr. J. Enoch Powell: I think that this is the first appearance of the Under-Secretary of State in the Northern Ireland Committee, and it would therefore, I hope, be thought appropriate to welcome him on his debut. He is already well known in the Province, and short or long as his future tenure of office may prove to be in that office, all members of the Committee will wish him success.

We are engaged this morning on an activity which, at first sight surprising, in reality forms the greater part of the business of politics, namely, that we set ourselves a problem which is inherently insoluble and then debate at length upon various non-solutions to it, and from time to time quarrel over them. The whole business has a Gulliverian flavour which, perhaps, is not inappropriate to a Committee whose business is tinctured with the affairs of the island for ever associated with Jonathan Swift.

It all starts very easily with a rate. A rate, in its primordial simplicity, can be easily defined. It has three characteristics which it is useful to bear in mind as we enter the labyrinth from which, even with the assistance of the working party, we have not succeeded in finding the Ariadne's thread by which to emerge.

These three characteristics are as follows. First, the levying of a rate is a sign of a responsibility taken by the body which levies it for the expenditure which it is to meet. It is an expression of the coherence of democratic representation and the power of taxation which lies at the root of our constitution. Secondly, it is a local tax, those who pay it and the scale upon which they pay it being locally anchored, so that the income tapped by this tax is unambiguously related to the area in which the relevant services are administered and applied. Thirdly, unlike taxation, which is not necessarily precisely related to particular expenditure, a rate, by its nature, starts from the fixing of the total sum to be raised for the meeting of specific expenditure and then is a way of allocating it over all the ratepayers.

These are the three characteristics: democratic responsibility, local anchorage in the relevant area and relation to a specific outlay which is distributed fairly or unfairly over a known group of people— namely, the ratepayers of that district. That is how it all started. That classic Image Col. 11 picture is not seriously altered by the existence and the growth of Exchequer subventions.

11.00 a.m.

For there still remains, unless those subventions become quite exorbitant, a sufficient degree of local responsibility and initiative in deciding upon the items of expenditure, and, one may add—an important point to be borne in mind— policing the efficiency and probity with which money is locally spent upon those services.

When we get into trouble, and when we get into the trouble which is engaging the Committee this morning, is when we transfer services from local democratic control to the arms of central government, whether central government nationally or central government provincially. That is how we entered the labyrinth. We did not, as the Under-Secretary correctly reminded the Committee, get into this as a spin-off from direct rule. We were already deep in it over the knees when it was decided at the beginning of this decade to dissolve the upper tier of local government in Northern Ireland and merge its functions in those of the provincial Government and Parliament.

As soon as this happens an insoluble conundrum is created. The expenditure is decided upon not by the representatives of the ratepayers but by the representatives of the taxpayers and since 1973, with a brief intermission, by the representatives of the taxpayers of the United Kingdom here and elsewhere in this building assembled. In that sense, the Under-Secretary was quite right in saying that a rate is by that transfer transmuted into a tax.

But this unfortunately does not go all the way, for the decision taken was not that these services in future should be borne by general taxation. It was not a transfer of a subject from local government to central government; it was a removal from one set of democratic representatives to the control of another set of elected representatives. Consequently, since local government continued in the rest of the United Kingdom, there was the contradiction whereby in the rest of the Kingdom ratepayers, as ratepayers and through a rate, would continue to bear their share of the cost of the corresponding services.

It would be absurd and indefensible if the pattern of taxation in Northern Ireland Col. 12 became different from that in the rest of the Kingdom. It was in an endeavour to maintain the pattern of taxation and the place of this tax called a rate in that total pattern that the notional regional rate was invented at the beginning of the 1970s and has existed ever since.

Perhaps I should have said earlier that a rate levied upon the value—annual, current or capital—of property in a given area is and has been almost from the start under continual attack. No sign of the novice in this field is more eminent than the delight and self-confidence with which he applies himself to discovering an alternative to rates. But over the decades these attempts, joyfully entered into, have invariably ended with the sorrowful conclusion that no alternative could be found.

I believe—this is germane to the point— after some third of a century devoted to, amongst other things, the contemplation of this question, that if we are to have local government at all, and therefore if we are to have locally identified taxation, there is no substitute for rating of property values as we know it, or almost as we know it.

11.05 a.m.

This is fair enough provided that not too large a proportion of the total tax burden is borne in this particular, rather regressive, form. It is something that we have to live with—we are not living with it in Northern Ireland at the moment though we should like to be—as the price which most of us believe we ought to pay for having representative-elected local government.

I return to the proposition that it would be intolerable and a breach of the principle of parity in taxation which the people of Northern Ireland insist upon within the United Kingdom, if this transfer had caused an alteration in the pattern of taxation in the Province, as compared with the pattern of the burden in the rest of the United Kingdom. There had to be the simulacrum of a rate in Northern Ireland. But how was it to be assessed? In the total expenditure, grant and what would otherwise be rate borne were inextricably combined. The decisions were to be taken by those who, although not conscious of having ratepayers breathing down their necks, were conscious of having the resources of the whole United Kingdom— as far as they could be extracted from the Treasury—behind them. There was no means of localising, which is the nature of Image Col. 13 a rate, the varying burden and the varying incidence of the services in the different parts of the Province.

A compromise was hit upon, the compromise of comparability. Find what the people of Northern Ireland would be paying by way of rates if they were not the people of Northern Ireland but were the people of Humberside and other parts of Yorkshire and it is a rough measure of fairness to construct a system which levies from them, in lieu of rates, the sort of sum which they would be paying as rates in those hypothetical circumstances.

I do not need to dilate upon the difficulties of any such comparison, such as the fact that the sources of decision which govern this expenditure, as well as the forms of administration which police it, are decidedly different in Humberside from Northern Ireland. But that is not the only example of many disparities which render the comparison inherently unsatisfactory.

Nor, as a matter of fact—this is important to be understood—has this approach been carried through. Here I should like to thank the Minister for his courtesy in placing in the hands of the members of the Committee and of Northern Ireland Ms. in particular, this excellent collection of documents bearing upon the rates in Northern Ireland. My attention was arrested by annex A to paper No. 1, headed

"Striking of Rates since Reorgonisation of Local Govt".

An extraordinary story it is. In 1974–75 this new course was entered upon in the belief that the difference which had to be bridged between the comparable areas in England and the current rate in Northern Ireland was 10 per cent. That figure turned out afterwards to have been wildly wrong, wildly low, and the successive paragraphs of that annex record that never has Northern Ireland caught up with the estimate, always belated, of what rates would have been if Northern Ireland had been in Yorkshire.

Instead of that the original figure, as adjusted in 1975–76, has been taken as an arbitrary base and the figure levied in lieu of rates in Northern Ireland has been scaled up year by year in the same percentage as the increase—

—in rates payable in England and Wales. I hope that that is correct, but if there were Col. 14 some marginal inaccuracy in the description it conveys, I am sure correctly, the fact that even if at the start there was some—I will not misuse the word "theological" but will say instead "hypothetical"—basis of comparability to provide the starting point, that ground was never stood upon and we have progressively departed from it as the years have gone by.

What is happening now is that each year there is levied in Northern Ireland in lieu of rates and upon a rateable basis a sum which represents a percentage of X, a percentage annually adjusted in relation to a comparable upward movement in England and Wales.

This is where we come to the report of the working party. It was generous of the h. M. for Abingdon (Mr. Neave) to pay tribute to Professor Lawrence for his work in this connection, but in fact Professor Lawrence was occupied on other things and the fruits of his labours were garnered in the order that we were debating when the h. It did not come to a conclusion, but the chairman gained the general impression—I hope that I am not being too unfair in that description—at the end of it all that there was nothing better than the system on which we were working, of an annual adjustment of X in line with the rise of rates in England and Wales.

I must say that I agree with the chairman, for it will be an arbitrary figure anyhow. At least there is a non-arbitrary element in the annual pari passu adjustment with the burdens on ratepayers in England and Wales and one cannot tinker with something which is inherently arbitrary to make it less arbitrary.

Incidentally, perhaps our deliberations would have been more mature this morning had we been able to start upon them at an earlier date. I think that we should put on record a faint complaint that the report was signed on 18 August, so the Under-Secretary kindly informed me in a parliamentary answer, because a date does not appear in the printed document, and it was not available until February. One wonders what had been going on in the meantime. It seems a long period for proof correction of a document as brief as this.

However, now that we have it and now that the conundrum is displayed once again by the working party report, it is incumbent upon us to express our judgment. Image Col. 15 Accordingly I say that, having got ourselves into this insoluble difficulty by the transfer of services from local government to nonlocal government, there is no advantage to be gained in trying to find some different but equally arbitrary basis for the levying of sums in lieu of rates.

The people of Northern Ireland would be well advised to take a look at the percentage of the total rate-attributable expenditure, as shown in paper 4 in the dossier, which they are bearing by way of rates. For 1977–78 the rates yield was £93.3 million against a total relevant expenditure of approximately £420 million. In other words, well under 25 per cent ofthe relevant expenditure is, in Northern Ireland, being borne in the form of the rate equivalent.

11.15 a.m.

Before anyone rushes off and tells it in Gath that the people of Northern Ireland are getting away lightly because they are paying a lower proportion of the relevant expenditure by way of rates than in the rest of the Kingdom, we should remind ourselves straight away, not merely that the total of £420 million is precisely the total that the Under-Secretary explained could not be accurately ascertained, but, much more seriously, that it is a total of expenditure not decided upon in the same way as those expenditures are decided upon and controlled in England and Wales, and is, rather, the fruit of Government decision. Maybe we would support most of their decisions, but it would not be true to say that the ratepayer as a ratepayer in Northern Ireland is the gainer by a true disparity of the nature of that 25 per cent ratio that I have mentioned.

So we have a conundrum, and like all conundrums it is insoluble and we are duty bound to explain to those whom we represent what is its nature, why it is insoluble and that, while we have to live with it, they are not by and large being unfairly dealt with. We have to live with it for as long as the services, which in the rest of the Kingdom are administered by local authorities, are not administered by local authorities in Northern Ireland. That is a precise and accurate statement. The restoration of a post-Macrory Stormont would leave us with almost exactly the same conundrum. It would still be impossible for the rate burden to be justified to the ratepayer as it has to be in the rest of Col. 16 the Kingdom and the figure assessed by way of rates would still be wholly arbitrary. Maybe it would be a political decision, but it would still be an arbitrary decision.

It is only by the restoration to the people of Northern Ireland of the right which the people in the rest of the United Kingdom enjoy unconsciously, and take for granted, namely the services towards which they pay rates being administered by their democratically elected local bodies, that we shall solve the conundrum ofthe rating system of Northern Ireland. We would then solve a great deal more too, but we are talking, as you reminded us Mr. Wells, about the rates. One was gratified to observe that the h. M. for Abingdon both grasped and stated this principle so clearly and firmly.

This is an unsatisfactory conclusion to arrive at in the short term, but in the long term the implications are unmistakable. Parity we claim. Parity, as the Minister has reminded us, we have in taxation and are intended to have in services. But there is another parity which cannot be omitted and that is parity in form of government. Until we have parity in form of government, which, assuredly, sooner or later, this House will see is a necessity for and the right of Northern Ireland, we shall continue to flounder into the morass from which there emerged the working party.

The system we have forced on us would not be entertained by a Westminster Government for any other region in the United Kingdom, so why should we tolerate it for Northern Ireland? We had civil right marches for one man one vote many years ago, but I believe that this system, based on what councillors elsewhere in the United Kingdom determine, is utterly repugnant to what this Parliament and British justice are supposed to stand for.

Perhaps it is time that the people of Northern Ireland rid themselves of their complacency, decided to act and made sure that this Westminster Parliament and Government, or their successors, realise that they will not be complacent much longer about colonial direct rule with such consequences as the present rating system.

It seems that what is unacceptable to any other region in the United Kingdom Col. 34 can be maintained in Northern Ireland, despite the fact that Ulster ratepayers have a punishing amount to pay. There is no element of democratic control over the regional rate. Macrory envisaged that the Stormont Parliament would provide the element of democratic control which ensured that ratepayers' money was sensibly and carefully used to provide value for money. This could be guaranteed only by the keeping of proper accounts of the costs of the regional services in the same way as district councils must show how money is spent.

Public accountability by district councils means that the ratepayers, if displeased with the manner of dispersal of their money, can kick out the district councillors at the next local government elections.
