Public Expenditure Plans for Northern Ireland for 1997–98/1999–2000

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Sir John Wheeler)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of the public expenditure plans for Northern Ireland for 1997–98/1999–2000.

The Secretary of State announced his public expenditure survey allocations on 10 December. The allocations reflected the outcome of ministerial considerations of all the pressures facing the Northern Ireland block. The overall total for the block was set in the national survey, which once again confirmed the Government's commitment to maintaining tight but realistic targets for public spending. At the national level, spending as a percentage of gross domestic product is set to meet the Government's target of 40 per cent. or below in 1997–98. Northern Ireland shares in the benefits of prudent fiscal policy and must also play its part in the control of public spending.

The Northern Ireland economy has been performing well in recent years. Northern Ireland's GDP per head in 1995 relative to the United Kingdom average was at its highest for two decades. Over the past five years manufacturing output has grown at more than double the rate for the United Kingdom as a whole. Unemployment continues to fall and is now at its lowest for 16 years. Record employment has been achieved. The Province's economy is therefore in good shape and I am confident that, if we had peace and stability, it would continue to improve strongly.

In spite of the economic improvements, the Government continue to recognise fully Northern Ireland's particular needs and circumstances. Total public spending per head continues to be substantially higher than for the rest of the United Kingdom.

Turning to the allocations to programmes made in December, I reiterate the Secretary of State's comment of the time that we were faced with difficult decisions. The law and order budget, which had been a source of what was called the peace dividend in the previous two surveys, once again required substantial additional resources following the Provisional IRA's abandonment of its ceasefire, the terrible disturbances of the summer and the general increase in terrorist activity.

Even during the PIRA ceasefire, there were continuing terrorist punishment attacks. In the light of those inescapable pressures, the Secretary of State had no alternative but to restore law and order as the top spending priority. Almost £120 million had to be provided to the police and for compensation over the 2 survey period. Those resources could, of course, have been used elsewhere by our economic and social programmes.

The Government also attach great importance to economic growth in the Province and additional resources were given to the Industrial Development Board to help to fund projects promising new jobs. Self-sustaining jobs are vital to Northern Ireland's future prosperity and are also an effective means of targeting social need. It would have been short-sighted not to have taken advantage of the solid inward investment opportunities on offer.

The health service is also an important spending priority. We already spend £1.6 billion a year on health and personal social services, but the Government recognise the pressures that the health service is facing. Additional resources were therefore provided to health in the 1996 survey, allowing for real growth, as has also been the case in previous years. The importance of the new Causeway hospital was also recognised and the Secretary of State decided that that project, costing £47 million in total, should proceed.

The education of Northern Ireland's children and young people is also important to the Province's future prosperity. While protecting education where at all possible, the Secretary of State decided that the classroom should receive priority in the 1996 survey.

The key priorities of the police, compensation, industrial development, health and schools had to be accommodated from within the control total set for Northern Ireland. Ministers were faced with difficult choices on which programmes should be reduced to release the necessary resources, and housing, training and employment schemes and urban regeneration were identified as the main sources of reductions. Those programmes have received substantial allocations of resources in the past and much has been achieved as a result. Even after the cuts that have to be made they will continue to receive substantial resources, which will be targeted as appropriate on the most needy.

I hope that my introductory remarks provide a helpful context to last year's public expenditure decisions. The survey was tight for everyone, not least because of the constraints imposed on us by security and compensation considerations. We had to cover key pressures and in doing so release resources from elsewhere. I have identified the main programmes which were affected and I would of course be happy to hear of other programmes which might have been targeted instead. I look forward to what I know will be an encouraging, interesting and positive debate.
