The Health Service In Northern Ireland

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Michael Ancram)
: I beg to move.


That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of the Health Service in Northern Ireland.

To leave as much time as possible for hon. Members to take part in the debate. I do not intend to speak for long. The Government welcome the debate on the matter has been chosen for today, I shall briefly set a scene that I hope may inform the debate, which I look forward to hearing.

As hon. Members know, my noble Friend Lord Arran, who had responsibility for health and social services in Northern Ireland since April 1992, has moved to other duties and has been succeeded by my noble Friend Lady Denton. I take this opportunity to pay tribute to him for his dedication and commitment not only to health and personal social services but also to agriculture during his time in Northern Ireland. I am sure that all members of the Committee wish him well in his new duties. Equally, I am delighted that Lady Denton is joining the Northern Ireland team. She is the first lady to do so and I know that she will bring with her an enthusiasm and a fresh perspective to the many issues both in health and personal social services and in agriculture which, rightly, are of great concern to the people of Northern Ireland.

The provision of integrated health and personal social services in Northern Ireland is the envy of the rest of the United Kingdom and beyond, and its dedicated staff have a record of achievement that is second to none. Any public service, however excellent, can always be improved and must change to meet the changing needs and expectations of the people it serves. Health and social services are no exception to that rule. In Northern Ireland, as in the rest of the United Kingdom, in the past few years there has been what is undoubtedly the greatest period of change since the inception of the national health service and the welfare state. Against that background it is perhaps timely to review the achievements, to take stock of the changes, and, as far as possible, to look to the future.

I shall start by considering some of the achievements in hospital services. The average number of patients treated in each acute hospital in Northern Ireland over the past five years has increased by 20 per cent. The number of people who receive high-quality treatment on a day basis increased over the same period by more than 50 per cent. The total number of cardiac surgery operations increased from less than 700 in 1988 to an expected 1,100 in this and subsequent years. The impact of those statistics on hospital waiting lists has been dramatic. Between April 1991 and April 1993, waiting lists for ordinary admissions to hospital decreased by 2,300 to a little more than 24,000, and the number of those waiting for two years or more decreased by almost 4,000, or 80 per cent, to a little more than 1,000. On the health promotion front, also, there have been achievements over the past 10 years. The perinatal 4 mortality rate has more than halved, and, since the launch of the "Change of Heart" coronary prevention programme in 1986, the number of deaths from coronary heart disease has fallen by almost 25 per cent. I am sure that we all welcome that.

In the past few years there have been a number of important reforms in the health service in Northern Ireland and in the rest of the United Kingdom. The fundamental principle underlying the reforms package is the purchaser-provider split, whereby health and social services boards retain responsibility for assessing the health and social care needs of their resident populations and for purchasing services to meet those needs from a range of providers, including newly-formed trusts. That has permitted the formation of an internal market, which allows for a degree of healthy competition between hospitals and other provider units, and has already led to improvements in the quality of care delivered to patients and clients, not least through the greater involvement of doctors, nurses, social workers and other professionals in the decision-making process.

The creation of health and social services trusts is of 'particular significance. The management freedoms which come with trust status are designed to devolve decisionmaking to those responsible for delivering services, offering them the opportunity to be imaginative and responsive to local needs.

Significant progress has already been made. By April 1994, more than 50 per cent, of expenditure on health and social services in Northern Ireland will be accounted for by trusts, which means that the majority of the population will by then benefit from greater choice and higher quality care within a value-for-money framework. Consideration is already being given to another round of trust applications which will further enhance the quality of services.

Nevertheless, the improvement of services through contracting is heavily dependent upon developing the purchasing process to the point where it can effectively drive service changes. Before moving to his new Department, my noble Friend Lord Arran instigated a review of purchasing functions and structures throughout Northern Ireland. The review group is scheduled to report to my noble Friend Lady Denton by the end of May, and I look forward keenly to seeing the outcome of their deliberations.

One of the most significant aspects of purchaser development has been the introduction of GP fundholding. This scheme began operating in Northern Ireland in April 1993, two years later than in the rest of the United Kingdom. From that date, 20 general medical practices began to refer their patients for a range of services under contracts—which they had negotiated—and to pay for those services from their own fund. Fundholding is at the leading edge of the health and social care reforms, and there is evidence that fundholders are improving the service to their patients.

From April 1994, a further 24 practices are likely to join the scheme, by which time 26 per cent, of the population of Northern Ireland will belong to fundholding practices. Such coverage was reached in England only in the third year of the scheme's operation. That is a clear indication of the great interest of Northern Ireland GPs in the scheme, and, over the next two or three years, I would expect more than half the population to be covered by fundholding practices.

JPEG 5 Important changes have also been taking place in the delivery of community care. Last April, major changes were introduced in the structure and organisation of these services in Northern Ireland, at the heart of which is the aim to sustain people in their homes or in other community settings as far as possible and to meet their needs through rigorous individual assessment and care management. We are determined through that process to enable dependent and elderly people to remain longer in their homes, to reduce the number of people in psychiatric hospitals and largely to eliminate the need for people to be admitted to hospital because of learning difficulties, hon. Members will agree that these are important and worthy aims.

The community care reforms will not be without cost, and it is indicative of the Government's commitment to them that more than £100 million of additional resources is being made available to boards for the provision of community care services. While I am on the subject of money, I am glad to that my right hon. and learned Friend the Secretary of State was recently able to announce a real-terms increase in funding for health and personal social services, including the family practitioner services, by allocating £1,423 billion for 1994–95. That means that for yet another year we have been able to honour our manifesto commitment to provide a year-on-year real increase in resources. Services of the standard that are available in Northern Ireland do not come cheaply; indeed, they will cost next year £863 for each man, woman and child, but that is a bill that the Government are glad to pay on behalf of the community.

I hope—although perhaps it is a vain hope in any political forum—that members of the Committee will agree that what I have said adds up to a positive picture of the state of health and personal social services in Northern Ireland. The services there are as good as those anywhere in the world and Northern Ireland can be proud of them. I hope that hon. Members will join me in acknowledging and applauding the enormous contribution made by those who work in the health service to its continuing standards of excellence.
