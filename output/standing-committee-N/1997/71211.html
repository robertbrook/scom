Public Expenditure in Northern Ireland 1998–99

10.30 am

The Chairman
: Before I call the Minister to move the motion on public expenditure in Northern Ireland, I remind the Committee that the debate can continue until 1 o'clock. I cannot impose a time limit on speakers, but I urge hon. Members to be as brief as possible, otherwise some hon. Members who wish to speak will not be able to do so.

Reverend Martin Smyth
(Belfast, South)
: On a point of order, Mr. Atkinson. You said that the debate would go on until 1 o'clock. However, does not the debate continue until 12.30 pm when we have an Adjournment debate?

The Chairman
: No. This debate goes on until 1 o'clock. There will be a half-hour Adjournment debate.

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Paul Murphy)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the matter of Public Expenditure in Northern Ireland in 1998–99.

 A copy of my speech and details of the relevant allocations have been placed in the Library. I take heed of your admonition about time, Mr. Atkinson; I shall be as brief as possible. The hon. Member for Belfast, South (Reverend Martin Smyth) will miss part of his lunch break as a consequence of the 1 o'clock Adjournment debate.

It is significant, interesting and almost historic that this is the first meeting of the Northern Ireland Grand Committee at which public expenditure allocations will be announced. I look forward to listening to what my hon. Friends and Opposition Members consider to be right for Northern Ireland spending plans.

There is no public expenditure survey this year. In its place, the Government have initiated a comprehensive spending review. The review is examining each spending programme and considering whether it contributes to the Government's objectives, whether it is the most effective way of achieving those objectives and whether there is scope for improving efficiency and effectiveness. That will ensure that future spending plans fully reflect the Government's priorities.

The spending review will be completed next year, and the allocations to Departments for 1999–2000 and subsequent years will be determined in light of the outcome of the review. This is the first time since direct rule that a Government has launched such an extensive consultation exercise on spending priorities. It has been done on an unprecedented scale and has involved 4 all the political parties in Northern Ireland, local authorities, businesses, trade unions and the voluntary sector. It has been helpful to me to read and to hear a wide range of views on public spending and priorities.

The Government are sticking to the totals that were set in the inherited spending plans for this year and for next, subject to the changes announced in the July Budget, and particularly the deployment of the proceeds from the windfall levy. The plans that I shall announce this morning for 1998–99 are consistent with that commitment; they represent reallocations within a Northern Ireland public expenditure total of £8·4 billion, excluding cyclical social security benefits.

The plans must, of course, be seen against the background of developments in the Northern Ireland economy, which has been performing well. A record number of people are in jobs and unemployment is continuing its downward trend. The current level is similar to that of 1981, and manufacturing output continues to outperform the rest of the United Kingdom. Like all members of the Committee, I want those trends to continue.

Mr. Norman A. Godman
(Greenock and Inverclyde)
: The comprehensive review is extremely welcome. Will my hon. Friend confirm that there will be a Northern Ireland version of the social exclusion unit?

Mr. Murphy
: Yes. We are liasing on the matter with the relevant Whitehall Departments. I shall write to my hon. Friend later about the details.

In the July Budget the Government provided substantial extra resources for Northern Ireland, for welfare to work, the health service, education and housing. Those Budget allocations have been well received in the Province and amount in total to £9 million in 1997–98 and £117 million in the coming financial year. In addition, the Government announced a further £5 million for health this year to deal with winter pressures. Of course, Northern Ireland will also benefit from measures announced in the Chancellor's pre-Budget statement, with additional resources for child care and assistance of up to £50 per household to pensioners during the winter months.

Unemployment is a huge waste of our economic potential and a major cause of poverty. A priority of the Government is to tackle the root causes of unemployment and provide new opportunities for work. Our welfare-to-work initiative will help us to do this. The new deal for the unemployed is aimed at getting the young and long-term unemployed into jobs and to help them to stay in employment. It aims to increase the long-term employability of the unemployed by improving their self respect, their skills, their experience and their motivation. In 1998–99, some £36 million of the £140 million allocated to Northern Ireland over the life of this Parliament will be spent on assisting the unemployed.

Next year we shall provide £1.8 million to introduce a new programme of assistance for lone parents, which will offer help and advice on jobs, training and child care to lone parents who wish to find work. In JPEG 5 addition, some £32 million currently allocated to the Training and Employment Agency's temporary employment and training programmes will continue to offer opportunities for the long-term unemployed. The activities of action for community employment, community work programme and Enterprise Ulster will be aligned with the new deal. My hon. Friend the Parliamentary Under-Secretary will set out the plans in greater detail in a separate announcement very shortly. Overall, in 1998–99, we will provide about 16,000 places for the young and long-term unemployed, compared with 7,400 in 1997–98.

Targeting social need continues to be an important underlying theme relevant to many public expenditure allocations and we shall try to ensure that programme spending is targeted on those areas and on people in greatest social need. The allocations for welfare to work will make a significant contribution to the objectives of targeting social need. The Government are also considering their response to the comments on targeting social need raised earlier by the Standing Advisory Commission on Human Rights.

I shall now refer in more detail to the allocations to particular programmes, beginning with education. In the July Budget Northern Ireland received £27.6 million for schools next year. As well as ensuring that schools have the resources that they need to cover teachers' pay and other costs, the allocations will be used to enhance pre-school provision, to reduce class sizes in primary one, and on action to raise school standards. Thus we are making a start on our manifesto commitments to increase education spending as we cut the costs of social and economic failure over the five years of this Parliament; to extend nursery education; and to reduce class sizes in the early years of primary education. In addition, the new deal for schools announced in the Budget will provide £8 million next year and in each of the following three years for improvement of schools' infrastructure—the need for which has been the subject of comment by many Northern Ireland Members while I have been in this job.

From 1 April 1998, the Northern Ireland further education colleges will become incorporated bodies. On a like for like basis, further education will have a real-term increase in funding of 1.65 per cent., to ensure that the colleges have the best possible start in their new status, and hence make a strong contribution to achieving educational standards. I can also confirm that Northern Ireland universities will benefit from the changes in student support expenditure that were announced by my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Education and Employment.

Mr. Murphy
: I take my hon. Friend's point about the difficulties that we encounter when we consider the juxtaposition of PFI with the ordinary way in which we spend money in Northern Ireland.

Northern Ireland has an integrated health and personal social services programme, which, I am happy to announce, will benefit from a substantial real terms increase next year. Again, I am sure that that will be widely welcomed.

The £31 million for health and personal social services, announced in the Budget, will be used primarily to develop front line services; in particular, to address waiting lists, for community care, for additional investment in cancer services and for implementing the Children Order in Northern Ireland. In addition, I am announcing a further £2 million for health and safety work in the health sector.

The allocations will ensure that the health and personal social services budget will increase in real terms, in line with our manifesto commitment, by 1.9 per cent. over the 1997–98 plans. Some £3·1 billion, or 56 per cent., of the Northern Ireland public expenditure block, excluding social security benefits, will be spent on health and education. That is a significant investment in those key services. It reflects the priorities of the Government. We were elected, among other reasons, because of our commitment to improve our health and education services. Those services are priorities in Northern Ireland as well as in Great Britain.

Law and order has been a substantial programme for many years, for obvious reasons. It is right that we should keep under constant review the resources that are required to deal effectively with terrorism and civil disorder. Despite the ceasefires, it is still too early to assess any potential future peace dividend from the law and order programme.

We will examine the appropriate level of resources for future years as part of the spending review. However, the civil disorder and criminal damage caused earlier this year have placed a regrettable claim on resources that could and should have been spent on other programmes in Northern Ireland.

In total, an additional £14·5 million has had to be allocated to the law and order programme. We are faced with a bill of £6 million next year for criminal damage from civil disorder and from the bomb at Markethill on 16 September. The block will also have to provide £5 million to meet an increase in criminal injury claims. I am sure that all hon. Members would agree that that money would be better spent on improving our schools and hospitals and the employment prospects of people in Northern Ireland.

Other additions to the law and order programme are £1·5 million to meet the costs of the Parades Commission, established in March 1997. We are also 8 providing £2 million to the Prison Service to meet contractual commitments on a new building programme at Her Majesty's prison at Maghaberry.

Spending on the Police Authority grant will be contained within the baseline of £658 million already established for 1998–99. A pressure of £7 million to fund the RUC overtime at 8,500 average daily hours will be met from the introduction of efficiency targets that my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State has set for the Police Authority for the first time.

The level of law and order expenditure will be crucial in determining the budgets for health, education, roads, industrial development and other services in the years beyond 1998–99. That adds even greater urgency to the search for political agreement and lasting peace. The Prime Minister, the Secretary of State and I will make every effort to pursue those objectives, which are so vital to every man, woman and child living in the Province.

I am greatly encouraged by the continuing interest of businesses in investing in Northern Ireland. We need more good quality projects to create lasting jobs in Northern Ireland to help sustain improvements in the economy. I am announcing a further £10 million for industrial development next year, mainly to meet pressures for new investment. The additional resources, which will bring the total for industrial development next year, to £115 million, will allow the Industrial Development Board to build on its successes and to continue to provide effective support towards developing the local economy.

We have had to find reductions in other programmes to meet the pressures in health, education, and law and order. The housing programme will need to bear some £17 million of those reductions; that is necessary to balance the books, although the amount of public support available next year will still be about £230 million. That includes the £13 million allocation to Northern Ireland from the capital receipts initiative, which will help to sustain the social housing programme. The transfer to housing associations of the function of building new social housing will help to mitigate the effects of the reductions, and when the Northern Ireland Housing Executive's own resources are taken into account, the total expenditure on housing will be just over £600 million.

In determining the allocation of resources to the Housing Executive, rental income is always taken into account. Rents will increase from the beginning of April 1998 by an average of about £1.60 a week. We have also had to make adjustments to the roads maintenance programme.

Accessibility of either hospital should not be a major problem, especially as they are regional hospitals. Patients from all over Northern Ireland travel every day to one or other of the hospitals to benefit from their wide range of regional facilities. Our priority must be the good of the patient, and one way to protect that is to ensure that the volume of work undertaken in a top-quality hospital that is a regional resource is great enough, and that there is enough specialisation, to allow skills to develop. When I was considering the issue, the unanimity of the Royal and City hospitals about the need for one unit was striking. There was no disagreement about that.
