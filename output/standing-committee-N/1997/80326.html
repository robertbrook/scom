The Chairman: Before I call the Minister to move the motion, I remind members of the Committee that our debate can continue until 1 pm. 10.31 am

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Adam Ingram): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the matter of the Northern Ireland economy.

I shall try to be brief, but that may be slightly difficult I have many of pages of notes on many matters that members of the Committee will wish to discuss.

I am pleased to open our debate. It is important to have a substantial discussion on the Northern Ireland economy, which is vital to the future of the Province. In many ways, and in spite of the difficulties, the Northern Ireland economy is a success story. Since the early 1990s, Northern Ireland has out-performed the economy of Great Britain, primarily because of external factors rather than self-generated indigenous growth. The growth that has occurred in recent years can be built on with fairly focused strategies and the right framework. In that context, I shall set out the Government's aims and our views of the way ahead.

To give a topical example, the Government's commitment to the Northern Ireland economy is illustrated by the personal interest that the Chancellor of the Exchequer has taken. He has been examining ways to underpin the political talks. Obviously, I do not want to discuss the Belfast talks, but hon. Members will recognise that they are at their most crucial phase. I was therefore pleased to announce a few weeks ago that my right hon. Friend is exploring options with political parties and putting together a package of measures to boost the local economy, help the drive towards a settlement, and put something in place post-settlement.

The Chancellor's initiative, as it has become known, is a great opportunity for local political parties to help to shape the package's contents. I have already set the ball rolling. I have completed a round of meetings with all the local parties, which have suggested to me the area that they think should be tackled. My view, which we may discuss later, is that those parties have much more work to do to clarify their thinking—that was one of my reasons for talking to them—and they should consider what they are seeking to achieve in such a one-off initiative.

Col. 2 The economy has a significant part to play in the peace process—hence the Chancellor's involvement. I again place on record my gratitude to the business community in Northern Ireland for its public support for the political talks.

Will the process inevitably lead to an increase in the overall block grant for Northern Ireland, or will there be redistribution within a block?

I shall talk to the local parties and explain that the Chancellor has the freedom to do what he believes is right and appropriate but must take account of the impact of his actions, not just on regional economies in the United Kingdom but within the EU framework. If there is a reduction in the number of people employed in security, there will be savings in the longer term, but there will also be significant initial costs. Those who talk of peace dividends have been mis-selling the reality. That was part of the problem previously because it was assumed that a false economy could be created on the back of peace. We must face the reality that costs will accrue from the process and we must examine ways in which to fund those costs. There will be longer term benefits to the whole United Kingdom economy from not investing in excess of £600 million in policing alone. If Northern Ireland is equated with Kent in terms of size, the saving would probably be £400 million, but it is difficult to predict with any certainty when we shall reach that point.

As with everything else, the present Government have had to deal with the lack of planning and decision-making by the previous Government.

I want to make some progress by dealing with the national overview while spelling out the Government's vision for the Northern Ireland economy and how that fits in the broad framework for national growth and renewal. The Government have been putting in place a set of consistent policies to improve the underlying rate of growth and employment by creating economic stability, based on low inflation and low Government borrowing, and to modernise the welfare state, which will help people out of welfare and into work. The Government will announce further thoughts on that later today. The policies will also help to develop a tax system that is fair, and is seen to be fair.

The Chancellor reaffirmed in the Budget those national priorities, which are good news for Northern Ireland. The lower corporation tax rates will benefit the Northern Col. 8 Ireland economy. The 20 per cent. rate will be especially competitive given that Northern Ireland has, in the main, a small-firm economy.

The Government have also put in place improved incentives to work. Higher thresholds for employees' national insurance contributions will be welcomed, I am sure, by the low paid and the unemployed. Similarly, the working families tax credit will guarantee an income to families on low pay, which should make work more attractive than simply drawing benefit.

The Chancellor also allocated an extra £20 million to Northern Ireland to spend on key services in the coming year; £13 million was allocated to the health service; more than £6 million was allocated to schools; and £1.3 million was allocated for rural transport.
