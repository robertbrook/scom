1. Mr. Barnes: If she will make a statement on abortion law in Northern Ireland.(24144)

Col. 2

Mr. Worthington: As I have said, we have no agenda to extend the Abortion Act 1967 to Northern Ireland. As my hon. Friend will know, such measures have traditionally been framed in private Member's Bills and regarded not as party issues but as matters for individual consciences. "That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of priorities in education in Northern Ireland."

Welcome to the Chair, Mr. Jones. I am sure that your Welsh charm will work even with the hon. Members who are assembled here.

I welcome the debate for the opportunity that it provides to outline some of the things that we have been doing and many of those that we shall do in the coming months and years. The Government recognise that learning is the key to the future that that lifelong learning is essential to economic prosperity. Education is at the heart of our policy agenda. We are strongly committed to promoting higher standards of achievement and maximising opportunities for learning.

We have already begun substantially to fulfil the manifesto commitment that education spending will rise over the five years of this Parliament. Since I assumed responsibility for education in Northern Ireland I have been repeatedly impressed by many features of the education system, particularly the high degree of commitment by teachers generally to providing the best education possible. That teaching force is envied by many. However, a great deal more can be done to raise education standards in Northern Ireland. We need to pay more attention, not to England, as has been traditional in Northern Ireland, but to European and world attainment standards. We should model ourselves on the best. We have much to build on—all the ingredients for the best education system in Northern Ireland. We should set our sights no lower than that.

Mr. Worthington: A great deal can be said about that. One of the failings of the British education system is that it has taken so long to establish a credible system of vocational qualifications. That has been neglected, within a framework—stronger in Northern Ireland than anywhere else—that has stressed academic attainment, and undersold the further education sector. Mr. Worthington: I ask my hon. Friend to be patient. I cannot deal with all matters at the beginning of my speech. I shall tackle that point later. However, my next point is relevant to my hon. Friend's question.

Next month, I shall announce a school improvement programme that will challenge and support, in equal measure, all schools to help them raise their standards. The programme will focus on the most fundamental building blocks of success in any school. Discipline creates and maintains an orderly working environment in which every child can learn and every teacher can teach effectively. Literacy and numeracy are the most basic skills that open the door to all other learning. Mr. Worthington: Yes.

I shall start at the beginning of education, and outline our pre-school education plans. If we are to raise standards significantly, the key starting point is the development of good quality pre-school education. In Northern Ireland, there has been underprovision. Matters have been complicated by the fact that most children start school at the age of four in Northern Ireland rather than five, the age at which children start school in the rest of the United Kingdom. A costed, planned expansion programme for pre-school education starts this year.

Image Col. 13 That expansion will be targeted on those children who will benefit most, especially children from socially disadvantaged circumstances and the oldest pre-school four-year-olds. It is essential to use resources where they are most needed.

We have devoted a lot of time to considering that. Again, that emphasises the point of my hon. Friend the Minister for Greenock and Inverclyde. I am committed to achieving commonality of funding for schools. An education system for 1.6 million people has seven ways of funding schools. Part of it involves the need to consider how we can better target social needs funds at the most needy schools. We need to hold those schools to account.
