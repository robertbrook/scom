Draft Appropriation (Northern Ireland) Order 1998

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Paul Murphy)
: Thank you Mr. Atkinson. After that exciting introduction, I beg to move.

That the Committee has considered the draft Appropriation (Northern Ireland) Order 1998.

Details of the sums sought in relation to the draft order are given in the estimates booklets and the statement of sums required on account, which have been placed in the Library of the House and are available from the Vote Office.

It is significant that this is the first meeting of the Northern Ireland Grand Committee at which a Northern Ireland appropriation order will be debated. I look forward to hearing what hon. Members have to say in relation to the draft order. I shall please them by saying that I intend to make my initial remarks as brief as possible. I was reading last night that Mr. Gladstone made Budget speeches that lasted for at least four or five hours. That is certainly not my intention this morning.

The draft order has two purposes. The first is to authorise expenditure of £148 million in the spring supplementary estimates for 1997–98—the current financial year. That will bring the total estimates provision for Northern Ireland departmental services to just under £7,000 million for this financial year. The second purpose of the order is to authorise the vote on account of just over £3,000 million for the next financial year, 1998–99. That will enable the services of Northern Ireland Departments to continue until the main estimates for next year are introduced later this year. I remind the Committee that the draft order does not cover expenditure by the Northern Ireland Office on law and order and other services.

2 Some of the votes seek token increases only, because new pressures on spending have been offset by savings elsewhere in the vote. I shall refer only to the main areas in which supplementary provision is sought, so that hon. Members have the maximum time for debate.

A net increase of more than £17 million is sought for agriculture. That includes £5.4 million to control outbreaks of animals diseases, such as salmonella, Newcastle disease and, of course, BSE. Within that vote, £5 million has also been made available to bring forward, to the 1997–98 financial year, the payment of about 1,250 capital grants claims, relating to the sub-programme for agriculture and rural development. Those payments would otherwise have been held over for payment in the next financial year.

Token increases of £1,000 for the Department of Economic Development are sought in votes 2 and 3. In vote 2, an addition of about £500,000 is for the Northern Ireland Tourist Board, to assist the development of tourism in Northern Ireland.

Tourism plays an important role in economic development in Northern Ireland, where it is estimated that spending by holiday visitors and domestic holiday makers accounts for approximately 2 per cent. of Northern Ireland's gross domestic product, and sustains approximately 12,500 jobs. This and other increases are off set by reduced requirements and increased receipts elsewhere in the vote.

A token increase of £1,000 is also sought by the Training and Employment Agency. An increase of £3.6 million is sought for the jobskills programme to meet increased demand, and for the training centre network for payment of training allowances. Also in vote 3 an additional £1.5 million is sought for the Government's new welfare-to-work initiative. That is a major plank of Government policy, which will fund preliminary work, including demonstration models, before the Province-wide introduction of the new deal in April. At present, approximately 3,000 18 to 24-year-olds participate in agency employment and training programmes. From 6 April, the agency will provide for approximately 10,000 young people, on a new deal option financed through either the new deal allocation of £140 million or transfers from existing provision.

All members of the Committee believe that unemployment is a huge waste of our talents and economic potential and a major cause of poverty. A Government priority is tackling, the root causes of unemployment and providing new opportunities for work. Welfare to work is a major and innovative initiative, which is targeted directly at tackling unemployment. It is aimed at getting the young and long-term unemployed into jobs, and helping them to stay in employment. That will be achieved through increasing their long-term employability by improving their self-respect, skills, experience and motivation.

Main increases in the Department of the Environment include £4.1 million for roads and maintenance, £1.6 million for the operation of ferry services, £2 million for compensation payments to Northern Ireland Railways and nearly £2 million to meet the cost of concessionary bus and rail fares for the elderly, disabled people and children of school age.

JPEG 3 In a previous sitting of the Northern Ireland Grand Committee, we discussed next year's estimates. Unfortunately, we had to deal with a reduction in the estimates for housing in Northern Ireland. I am glad to say that in the supplementary estimates, we have found a net increase of £17.2 million for the rest of the current financial year. Some £7.5 million is for assistance to the voluntary housing movement, and includes a transfer of funds from the Northern Ireland Housing Executive to housing associations to enable an additional 400 new dwellings to be started in 1997–98.

It is important to recognise that the housing success story continues. Preliminary results of the 1996 house conditions survey show a significant reduction in unfitness levels. An additional £10 million, including £3.7 million from the capital receipts initiative, is being provided to the Northern Ireland Housing Executive.

In vote 4, which covers environmental and other services, a net increase of some £6.2 million is sought. Some £7 million is for regeneration-related matters and £8.4 million represents carrying forward running cost and capital underspends from previous years.

4 An increase of £2.8 million under the Government's new deal initiative is also sought for schools and institutions of further education.

Turning to the Department of Health and Social Services, a net increase of some £48 million is required in vote 1. That is for expenditure on hospital, community health and personal social services, health and social services trusts, family health services and certain other services.

On vote 4, a net increase of £11 million is required. In addition to capital and administration pressures within the Department, that includes some £700,000 in respect of administering the welfare-to-work initiatives.

Vote 6 covers social security centrally administered by the Department of Health and Social Services. A net increase of some £2.4 million is sought. That is required to reimburse the social fund for expenditure on cold weather payments in 1996–97, winter-fuel payments to pensioners in 1997–98 and increased payments into the Northern Ireland national insurance fund in respect of statutory sick and statutory maternity payments. Those increased requirements are offset by savings elsewhere in the vote.

Hon. Members will want to debate the real Northern Ireland. Schools, hospitals, jobs and training matter deeply to the people of Northern Ireland, and I look forward to debating those important matters.

We are debating £150 million bering rightly spent on additional services, yet over the past few days, as members of the Committee will remember all too vividly, there have been bombings and the public purse will, rightly, have to pay millions of pounds in compensation. It is worth considering that that money could have been spent on hospitals, schools, roads and jobs. That thought must be at the back of all our minds.

The Grand Committee's work will continue but in time, the administration of the services to which I have referred may be the work of an assembly for Northern Ireland. I commend the order to the Committee.
