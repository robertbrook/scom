title Disabled People (Access)

1. Rev. Martin Smyth (Belfast South): What plans she has to amend Article 103 of the Planning (Northern Ireland) Order 1991 in respect of access for disabled people. (47186)

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Northern Ireland (Mr. Tony Worthington): There are no proposals to amend article 103 of the Planning (Northern Ireland) Order 1991, but I may be able to provide more information when I have heard the supplementary question.

Rev. Martin Smyth: I am puzzled that there are no arrangements to amend, when there is a proposal before the House. Does the Minister accept that insufficient consideration is being given to the need for open access for people with disabilities as distinct from those who drive invalid cars? Government policy is to provide open access for people with disabilities, but the proposal will restrict such people from part of Belfast.

Mr. Worthington: There are no proposals to change the order, but a consultation document has been issued. The problem arises throughout the United Kingdom. When it is necessary to provide exemptions for some vehicles in pedestrianised areas, there are complaints about abuse of those areas. The Department is not prohibiting access for disabled people to Belfast city centre; it has simply issued notice of its intention to maintain pedestrian priority and to reduce the number of vehicles using the main shopping area. We are introducing special arrangements to provide six parking places for holders of orange badges in the central pedestrian area and approximately 70 parking bays for disabled people on the periphery of the central pedestrian priority area. There has been consultation with organisations for disabled people, and I am sure that the hon. Gentleman accepts that we need a balance between people's general desire for pedestrianised areas with access for some categories of vehicles and the difficulty that arises when there are 15,000 holders of orange badges in the Belfast area.

Col. 2

Rev. Martin Smyth: The Minister will accept that the 15,000 holders of orange badges do not go to the city centre at the same time. I accept the concept of limiting the use of the orange badge, but those with certain disabilities will be unable to go to Belfast city centre. Will the Minister keep the matter under review because the disability lobby has contacted me with its concerns?

Mr. Worthington: Of course we understand those concerns, and I urge organisations for the disabled to return to the Department for discussions. The consultation paper has been issued and I hope that the Department's invitation will be accepted.

Gentleman an estimate of the number of prisoners who seem to have been convicted of qualifying offences—that is, persons who were sentenced to five years or more, or for life, for a scheduled offence that was committed before 10 April and not scheduled out by the Attorney-General. I cannot comment on whether an individual prisoner will qualify for release, or on when a prisoner could be released, under the legislation. As I have said, no prisoner will automatically qualify for release. Only prisoners serving sentences in Northern Ireland will be eligible to apply. Prisoners sentenced in England and Wales who are transferred to serve sentences in Northern Ireland could, of course, apply to the commissioners who would decide whether those prisoners satisfied the legislation's conditions. These matters have to be dealt with sensitively, which is why the commission has been established to deal with individual cases.

Mr. William Ross: My hon. Friend the Member for West Tyrone (Mr. Thompson) asked about the number of prisoners in Northern Ireland that are to be released, but prisoners can transfer from Great Britain to Northern Ireland. Mr. Murphy: I cannot give the hon. Gentleman the precise figures, but I shall write to him.

Mr. Murphy: I cannot agree. The hon. Gentleman knows from the Bill's passage through the House—it is now in another place—that the release of prisoners depends on the decisions of the sentencing commission. The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Paul Murphy): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Appropriation (No. 2) (Northern Ireland) Order 1998.

Details of the sums are given in the estimates booklet and the statement of excess, which I arranged for members of the Committee to receive in advance of today's sitting. The order covers, in total, almost £7,000 million of expenditure—an increase of 6 per cent. on 1997–98. The Committee needs no reminding that estimates from the Northern Ireland Office, covering law and order services and other services dealt with by the Northern Ireland Office, are not covered by the order. It covers expenditure totalling £7,000 million, which, after February of next year or thereabouts, will be the direct responsibility of the Northern Ireland Assembly and its Executive.

Yesterday, at the inaugural meeting in Belfast of the Northern Ireland Assembly, the First Minister and Deputy First Minister were appointed. It will fall to them and their colleagues and the Assembly as a whole, in February, March and beyond of next year, to deal with all the matters to which members of the Committee will undoubtedly refer this morning: education, health, transport, agriculture, roads, the environment, housing and local government. All those matters, which are currently dealt with by the six Northern Ireland Departments, will become matters for the Assembly. The Committee will be aware of the momentous change that that will bring in the years ahead if, as we all hope and pray, the Assembly is successful.

As the House of Commons will deal with the block grant, it will still have an important say in how the money is distributed. Similar arrangements will apply to Northern Ireland, Wales and Scotland in that respect. There should be—and I am sure that there will be—a good working relationship between the House of Commons and the Northern Ireland Assembly with regard to the distribution of these funds. Seven thousand million pounds is a great deal of money for a population of 1.6 million. However, it is necessary money, not only because of the problems of the past 30 years but because—as in my country of Wales—of the decline of staple industries, such as shipbuilding in Northern Ireland or coal and steel in my part of the world. Places like Northern Ireland therefore need and deserve such public expenditure.

I shall refer only briefly to the sums of money in the booklet. Members of the Committee, especially Northern Ireland Members, must be given the opportunity to discuss in the House and in this Committee matters affecting them, their constituents and people living in Northern Ireland in general. The Minister with responsibilities for health and education, the Under-Secretary of State for Northern Ireland, the hon. Member for Clydebank and Milngavie (Mr. Worthington), who will wind up the debate, has specific responsibilities for much of the expenditure to which I refer and to which other members of the Committee will refer in their speeches.

Col. 10 The Committee should note that £17 million will fund EU and national agricultural support measures that apply throughout the country. Members representing agricultural constituencies will be especially interested in some other measures. Fourteen million pounds will provide support for farming in special areas by means of headage payments on hill cattle and sheep. Funding of £130 million will be provided for on-going regional services and support measures, and that will include £63 million for agriculture and agricultural products industries. Farm support, enhancement of the countryside, fisheries and forestry will receive funding of £23 million. Twenty-two million pounds will be provided for central administration and information technology. Funding will also be provided for the Rivers Agency, some of which will match European Union funding.

The money allocated to the Department of Economic Development—especially the £160 million for the Industrial Development Board—will provide opportunities for economic and industries development in Northern Ireland. Those opportunities will inevitably be enhanced by the advent of peace and—as a consequence of the Assembly—political stability. When the Assembly is up and running, I hope that whichever Department is responsible for economic development will have the capacity to increase the good work of past years in encouraging industrial investment. It does not matter whether that is inward investment or investment from Europe, the United States or elsewhere. What matters is that the work force in Northern Ireland—particularly its younger members who are highly trained in information technology—are ready to take advantage of the opportunities ahead.

People often talk of the Celtic tiger and of the way in which the Republic of Ireland has developed in the past decade, which has been partly due to its membership of the European Union. That development also occurred because that country had the sense to ensure that its young people were well trained in skills and technology. The opportunities ahead for Northern Ireland will depend not only on the way in which it governs itself but on a sound educational system on which much can be built, and to which my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary will undoubtedly refer when he winds up the debate.

A sum of £210 million is sought for the Training and Employment Agency; £62 million will fund nearly 15,000 training places under the job skills training programme. There will be £23 million to assist companies in improving their competitiveness by developing the skills of their work forces. Under the Government's welfare-to-work initiative, £40 million will be used to provide employment and training for young people in Northern Ireland as part of the new deal. From November, £42 million will be provided to assist the long-term unemployed, establishing 30,000 places for them.

The Northern Ireland tourist board will receive about £14 million. That is an interesting and significant point, because as it increasingly becomes evident that there is peace in Northern Ireland, people will want to see the great treasures there. That will provide great opportunities for tourism; indeed, the Republic of Ireland has exploited such opportunities extensively. The tourist industry that has been developed in the south of Ireland could also be Image Col. 11 extended to Northern Ireland. Tourism currently accounts for 6 per cent. of GDP in the republic; it is 2 per cent. in Northern Ireland. IF we can exploit the potential for tourism in the north, and attain the same percentage of gross domestic product as in the south, we could create 8,000 extra jobs. The opportunity is there.

We have referred to the environment in questions; and £174 million has bee allocated for roads, transport and ports, of which £20 million will be for road passenger services. The Northern Ireland Housing Executive and the voluntary housing movement will receive £267 million. The total resources for housing in Northern Ireland next year will be just over £600 million. An amount of £194 million will be allocated for the provision of water and sewerage services, of which £87 million is for capital spending and £104 million is for operation and maintenance. Hon. members—especially those from Northern Ireland—will be aware that much remains to be done in relation to the water and sewerage infrastructure. That will be a high priority for the new Assembly and for those hon. Members representing Northern Ireland who will be involved with the estimates. It will be a major task to ensure that the infrastructure is in place to achieve economic development.

A host of environmental and other services will receive £190 million; that is about £5 million more than last year. The Northern Ireland Fire Authority will receive £44 million. Almost £1,500 million will be provided for education in next year's budget. Taking into account the sale of the student loan debt, spending will increase by just over 3 per cent.

My hon. Friend the Under-Secretary will probably refer to several of the matters relating to education that he has considered during the past year. There are some good stories about education in Northern Ireland: the development of Springvale; the fact that the Department and the boards now work together on school improvement plans; the fact that 20 per cent. of children will begin pre-school education this year; the restoration fo cuts in the library budget; the fact that further education colleges now stand on their own and that there has been a tremendous new investment in computers.

Rev. Martin Smyth (Belfast, South): I did not catch the exact capital figures. Will the Minister repeat them? Do the figures refer only to public finance or do they include the amount that it is hoped will be provided from private finance initiatives?

Mr. Murphy: The bulk of the expenditure is public. There will be an announcement soon about private-public partnerships in education. I know that the hon. member for Belfast, South is interested in that matter. When that announcement is made, I hope that we will be able to draw on public funds and on those private companies that are interested in providing decent education opportunities for people in Northern Ireland.

The estimates allow for £1,600 million for hospital, community health and personal social services, health and social services trusts and family health services: that is an increase of about 2.3 per cent. on last year's final net Col. 12 provision. That takes place in the year of the 50th anniversary of the national health service, the introduction of which was as welcome in Northern Ireland as it was in Britain.

There is still much to be done in the health service in Northern Ireland, as there is in the rest of the country, but the priority that my Government places upon that will be reflected by the priority that is placed on it by the new Assembly. With regard to the health service, I do not believe that there is any difference in the priorities of the elected politicians who took their seats in Belfast yesterday and those of hon. members in the Committee.

Mr. Jeffrey Donaldson (Lagan Valley): The Minister will be aware that today the Prime Minister is to announce funding for a modernisation programme for the national health service. Will the Minister ensure that Northern Ireland gets its fair share of the moneys that are made available for that? As he knows, Northern Ireland takes great pride in its health service. However, there is always a need as there is in the rest of the country to update and modernise the resources that are available.

Mr. Murphy: The answer to the Question is yes. It is important that any modernisation plans are spread in such a way that all parts of our country, including Northern Ireland, will benefit. When the comprehensive spending review announcement is made later this month, I hope that good reference will be made to the health service in this, its 50th anniversary year.

Finally, I turn to the funding of the new Northern Ireland Assembly, whereby £9 million is sought for set-up and operational costs. Assemblies have to be paid for—democracy has to be paid for—and we have therefore to include in the appropriation costs the costs of setting up the Assembly that met yesterday in Belfast. When it is up and running, after February or March 1999, it will have to pay for its own running, like its Welsh and Scottish counterparts. However, that is a small price to pay for political stability and a lasting peace in Northern Ireland. No one will begrudge Northern Ireland its fair share.

I have made only a short speech so as to ensure that Committee members, especially those from Northern Ireland, have an opportunity to look at the figures, comment on them and, I hope, approve them at the end of the debate.
