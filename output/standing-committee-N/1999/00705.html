Arms Dumps Inspections

10.30 am

1.

Mr. John M. Taylor
(Solihull)
: If he will make a statement about arms dumps inspections in the context of the decommissioning provisions of the Belfast agreement. [128185]

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Adam Ingram)
: We welcome the inspection of IRA arms dumps by Mr. Ramaphosa and Mr. Ahtisaari. We believe that this step signals the sort of process of moving from talking about the arms issue to actually getting it under way. I hope and expect that the IRA will honour that undertaking. The re-establishing of contact between the IRA and the Independent International Commission on Decommissioning is another sign of helpful progress. The recent IRA statement included for the first time a clear and unequivocal commitment to put arms "completely and verifiably" beyond use.

Mr. Taylor
: I welcome the Minister's reply, but as confidence and openness are essential ingredients in the fulfilment of the Belfast agreement, are we for ever to expect the inspections to be a sort of mysterious medieval sacrament, visible only to a high priesthood?

Mr. Ingram
: In one sense, that question was couched in obscure and opaque language. As I said, it is obviously a matter of making positive progress towards that which we all seek to achieve. I am sure that the hon. Gentleman hopes that the expectation that I expressed in my first response—that we shall move on from where we are now, with what was called the confidence-building measure, to the full undertakings that we believe to be consistent with the Belfast agreement—is fulfilled by those organisations, which can play an important part in achieving that objective.

Mr. Andrew Robathan
(Blaby)
: The Minister and I have discussed this matter in the past and we take slightly different views on it. Given the recent court case in the United States in which people were convicted for buying arms during the IRA ceasefire 2 and in which it was verified that the arms went to the IRA, although it seems strange that the IRA should want to buy more arms during a ceasefire, what tangible evidence does the Minister have for hoping—I use his word—that the IRA will honour its undertakings?

Mr. Ingram
: We have always made it clear that it is not a simple and straightforward process. Regarding the court case raised by the hon. Gentleman in the opening part of his question, we have said that all such matters are kept under scrutiny and examination. He will know that the previous Secretary of State made a determination in advance of the court's final decision of whether that information was in the public domain. That decision was tested in a court of law and the Secretary of State's finding was supported.

Clearly, we must at all times consider the process in which we are engaged, keeping alert to the possibility of failure but always building for success. It requires the involvement not only of the Government and of those who have influence over those in possession of weapons, on the republican side and the so-called loyalist side, but of all parties in Northern Ireland to move forward progressively—first to establish the peace that we all hope for and, secondly, to keep the pressure on those who have illegally held weapons.

Devolution in Northern Ireland

11.3 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Northern Ireland (Mr. George Howarth)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of devolution in Northern Ireland.

I should like to begin by thanking the Committee for the opportunity to discuss the important subject of devolution in Northern Ireland. The story of the road to devolution, as played out on the political stage, is familiar to us all. Thirty years of armed conflict, spurred on by diversity and different aspirations for the political and constitutional future of Northern Ireland, have left an indelible mark. Since May 1997, we have striven with the Northern Ireland political parties and the Irish Government to devise a new political settlement that will be for all the people of Northern Ireland, whatever their religion, creed or background. The outcome of those endeavours was, of course, the Good Friday agreement, which provides for the first time an agreed approach to fundamental issues.

As the Committee will know, the Good Friday agreement set out arrangements for the establishment of a devolved power-sharing Assembly, together with an Executive, and for closer cross-border co-operation between north and south and between east and west. It made provision for policing, criminal justice, security, equality and prisoners, which were some of the issues that were discussed in oral questions earlier.

Crucially, the agreement established the principle of consent—the right of the people of Northern Ireland to self-determination. It acknowledged the legitimacy of British sovereignty in Northern Ireland for as long as the majority of its people wish it to be so. At the same time, it recognised the right of a substantial section of the people of Northern Ireland legitimately to pursue the role of a united Ireland—so long as that goal was pursued peacefully and democratically. In that way, it was possible to incorporate the aspirations and political beliefs of nationalists and Unionists into one agreement. Without that, I believe that lasting progress would have been impossible.

Since the referendum on 2 May 1998, we have been working hard to give practical and tangible expression to the provisions and principles contained in that historic agreement. Much has been achieved in a short time. Good progress has been made on implementing the agreement's provisions. The reviews of the criminal justice system and policing have both reported. The Police (Northern Ireland) Bill is making steady progress through the House, and we expect next year to introduce legislation arising from the recommendations of the criminal justice review. We proceeded quickly to implement the rights and equality provisions of the agreement.

The level of threat is now such that troop numbers are at their lowest for 30 years. Of course, as we made clear in answers to oral questions, the security 12 arrangements are always made under the best possible advice and they reflect the current level of threat. However, although our early progress was very encouraging, the Committee will agree that we still have some way to go before we can move forward on the agreement's provisions for the establishment of devolved government and for decommissioning, both of which are fundamental to the overall settlement.

Multi-party talks in Belfast, Dublin, Hillsborough and London have helped to move the process on, and the right hon. Member for Upper Bann (Mr. Trimble) was central to all of those discussions. He will confirm that it was on occasions a halting and frustrating process. I was not the only one to have wondered whether the vision of the Good Friday agreement could be turned into reality. However, between July and November 1999, Senator George Mitchell spent a considerable time—we must acknowledge that it was done at great personal sacrifice—to guide all sides to an agreed conclusion.

The parties and the two Governments agreed that the power-sharing Executive and related devolved political institutions would quickly be established in return for tangible progress on IRA decommissioning. That was to happen within weeks of devolution. On 2 December 1999, following the signing of the Northern Ireland Act 1998 (Appointed Day) Order 1999, devolution was established in Northern Ireland for the first time in 30 years. It was a momentous occasion. There were not necessarily joyful celebrations in the street, but there was a mood that willed the arrangements to succeed.

The Assembly and the Executive Committee quickly got down to business and new Ministers approached their new jobs with energy, ability, skill and commitment. Another route by which devolved issues can be raised in the House is in considering funding arrangements. Until 1972, the funding arrangements for Stormont were very much ad hoc and had developed a long way from the basic legislation contained in the Government of Ireland Act 1920. There is now some consistency in funding for the devolved regions because, for all of them, funding is based on the Barnett formula. That formula evolved in 1979 and recognised different levels of expenditure in Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland based on need. It also provided that when expenditure is increased for England and Wales, an increase based on a per capita percentage of the population in the devolved area should be introduced for comparable programmes. The significance of that is that it provides for increases for comparable programmes, and if block grants are determined by reference to the comparability of programmes, that will enable people in the House to question those programmes. An important point underlying that is the parity principle, which was one of the main principles on which we operated during the Stormont years. The parity principle is simple; people in Northern Ireland, like people in Wales and Scotland, pay the same taxes as people in the rest of the United Kingdom. Consequently, they are entitled to the same quality of public service. The parity principle applies not only to benefits, but to quality of service. People who pay the same taxes as every other citizen in the United Kingdom are entitled to the same service quality, or at least one that does not differ significantly. It may cost more to deliver a service in a dispersed rural area such as the highlands and islands of Scotland or parts of Northern Ireland than in more densely populated areas. However, that is beside the point, as people who pay the same taxes are entitled to the same quality of service.

The question is what is needed to deliver the service. Whether it is achieved through finance measures or more general policies, there should be greater opportunity to consider these matters in the House. Mentioning the Barnett formula brings to mind one of the discoveries that I made about it after devolution. Before devolution, I had believed that the formula was a good thing from the point of view of the devolved Administrations, as it guaranteed significant levels of funding. However, I discovered after devolution that it does not necessarily provide such a guarantee. In fact, it can operate retrogressively in respect of the devolved institutions.

I shall give two examples of how significant increases in expenditure for programmes in England and Wales have had a deflationary effect on available expenditure. First, in the past financial year, spending 26 on health in Northern Ireland was 8.6 per cent. above the national average. As a result of the health spending increases that were announced by the Chancellor in his Budget statement, additional sums were made available in Northern Ireland. However, they were calculated on a per capita basis rather than on a needs basis, although the original Barnett formula was based on needs. After those increases were made, the amount that was available for spending on health in Northern Ireland was only 4.8 per cent. above the national average. The steepness of the increases meant that per capita spending dropped from 8.6 per cent. to 4.8 per cent. above the national average. Of course, people in Northern Ireland will expect the same increase in service, which will be difficult to cost when there is not the same percentage increase in the funds that are available.

My second example is education. When funds are increased in England and Wales, Northern Ireland receives an increase that is based on per capita figures. However, we have a higher percentage of people in education than England and Wales. Off the top of my head, I think that 16 per cent. of the population in England and Wales are in primary and secondary education, while 20 per cent. of the Northern Ireland population are in such education. The consequence of our per capita increase is that for every £1 per pupil of extra spending in England and Wales, Northern Ireland receives 76p, although we are expected to fund the same increases in service provision.

Those are clear disadvantages to a formula that we thought to be useful, but which has serious deflationary effects. We can live with that problem in the short term, but in the long and even the medium term, it will cause serious problems in Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland. I know that it is the habit of some English Members to speak about Barnett with a completely different agenda in mind. They may not appreciate the extent to which there are problems in the operation of Barnett in the devolved regions and the consequent need for a proper needs-related assessment of expenditure.

I return to the basic principle of parity. For paying the same taxes, everybody in the United Kingdom is entitled to the same quality of service. The question is how to assess the cost of providing that service in the different regions. Those issues are of a different type from the matters that have engaged the Committee in the past, but it will increasingly have to consider them. The extent to which it is able to do so will be the true mark of the progress that we are making.

Although its status has sometimes been shaky, we cannot fail to recognise the real benefits that the reduction of terrorist activity has brought to every part of Northern Ireland, notwithstanding the current difficulties.

It is fair to say that progress on decommissioning has not happened as quickly as we would have liked, and that progress on the implementation of the strand 1 provisions in the Belfast agreement—the constitutional settlement—has consequently stalled on a number of occasions. Perhaps the most serious of those problems was the suspension of the new institution. After the justifiable euphoria of Good Friday 1998, it was hugely disappointing and frustrating that progress could not be made more quickly on implementing the two key aspects of the Belfast agreement—decommissioning and devolution. It was clear that those issues had to be tackled head on and that significant movement was needed on decommissioning if devolution was to be restored on a lasting basis.

We now have assurances that the IRA is committed to pursuing its political aims by exclusively peaceful means. The statement that it issued on 6 May gave precisely the sort of commitments that Unionists have been seeking for a long time. It stated its commitment to non-violence and undertook to initiate a process to put its arms completely and verifiably beyond use. The hon. Gentleman will know that that terminology is recognised by the Decommissioning Act as constituting actual decommissioning. As a gesture of good faith, the IRA undertook in addition to carry out an early confidence-building measure. For the first time ever, it agreed to make its arms caches available to independent inspection, which was an unprecedented move.

The Committee will be aware that the IRA's commitment on that last point has now been honoured. On 26 June, the Independent International Decommissioning Commission published a report by the inspectors, Cyril Ramaphosa and President Ahtisaari, confirming that they had inspected a number of arms dumps that contained a large number of weapons. They confirmed that the weapons were secure and that they could not be accessed without detection. They said that they would inspect the dumps regularly.

Decommissioning is back on track. There is every reason to be cautiously optimistic about the prospects for progress, yet some people seek to undermine those achievements. There have been objections that the confidence-building measure is not actual decommissioning. I agree—it is not. The Government have never maintained that the confidence-building measure is a substitute for decommissioning and neither, to be fair, has the IRA. Decommissioning remains our ultimate goal.

If we impose time scales within the overall time frame, they could have the opposite effect to that intended.

I do not understand how recent events and developments could possibly mean that the suggestion that we should plan for failure is well founded. That is not the case. Of course we must maintain the momentum, but we also have to give time for the new political settlement to establish itself and for the positive aspects of devolution and the continuing implementation of the agreement to have an effect. Politics will be shown to work. I am cautiously confident that progress will continue to be made.

We should be encouraged by the fact that the threat has declined in the past few years. The processes that are in hand, the agreements that are in place and the process that is under way give me every confidence that that trend will continue.
