1.

Mr. Jeffrey Donaldson
(Lagan Valley)

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Adam Ingram)
: Responsibility for reducing waiting lists lies with Down-Lisburn trust and the eastern health and social services board. In the past year, the trust increased staffing by one whole-time equivalent occupational therapist. The board is conducting a root and branch review of the occupational therapy service throughout the area to inform future commissioning decisions.

Mr. Donaldson
: I thank the Minister for that reply. However, people in the Down-Lisburn trust area are having to wait for up to a year for occupational therapy assessments to be carried out. State of Northern Ireland Agriculture

3.2 pm

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Adam Ingram)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the matter of the state of Northern Ireland agriculture.

 [ Relevant Document: The Second Report from the Northern Ireland Affairs Committee, Session 1999–2000, on Current Problems relating to the Livestock Industry in Northern Ireland, HC 62.]

There is no disputing the fact that agriculture in Northern Ireland has gone through a difficult four years starting, of course, with the onset of the BSE crisis in 1996. Although that event marked the beginning of the downturn in the industry's fortunes, subsequent developments have also played a major role. Those include the strengthening of sterling and a weakening of international markets for many of the main agricultural commodities.

The scale and nature of the problem has been highlighted recently in the report of the Northern Ireland Affairs Committee entitled "Current Problems Relating to the Livestock Industry in Northern Ireland". I welcome the report. It provides a thoughtful examination of the issues involved and adds to the body of knowledge on a way forward for the industry.

Since 1996, the aggregate income of the industry in Northern Ireland has fallen by almost 80 per cent. in real terms, which is well ahead of the 60 per cent. reduction across the UK as a whole. Although the 1995 income level in Northern Ireland was at a historic high, the 1999 figure represented a 19-year low. It would be wrong to suggest that the situation in Northern Ireland is worse than anywhere else in the UK. The income fall in Wales has been even more severe and the industry there, which is of a similar size and structure, has an aggregate income of only half that of the industry in Northern Ireland. It would also be wrong to suggest that the Government have not recognised the difficulties or done nothing about them in Northern Ireland, Wales or anywhere else in the United Kingdom.

Since coming to power in 1997, the Government have introduced numerous measures to assist the agriculture industry in its current difficulties. Agrimonetary compensation was paid in 1997, 1998 and 1999. It will also be paid this year and next year. Substantial sums are involved that are paid for largely by the UK taxpayer. Last year, the UK agriculture industry received a great deal more than £260 million in agrimonetary compensation. This year, a further £154 million will be provided, including the £66 million announced by the Prime Minister at the agriculture summit at the end of March.

The industry in Northern Ireland is benefiting in full from the compensation packages, with total payments expected to exceed £30 million during those two years, but the Government's response to the difficulties has gone further than providing agrimonetary compensation. The particular difficulties of hill farmers have been tackled by injecting a substantial amount of cash into the less 12 favoured areas through the hill livestock compensatory scheme. Government provision for the 1999 and 2000 schemes was increased by £60 million across the UK. Northern Ireland hill farmers received £8.5 million of that in each of the two years. At last month's agriculture summit, the Prime Minister announced that a further £60 million will be made available for the 2001 scheme and that future funding would be considered in the forthcoming spending review.

Recognising the importance of the beef and dairy sectors in Northern Ireland, the Government have asked the European Commission to remove the weight limit that applies to the over-30-month scheme as soon as possible. If agreed, that will represent a welcome cash boost for both sectors.

The pig industry in Northern Ireland has also been under severe strain. The tremendous difficulties that it has faced in the past two years reflect an EU-wide imbalance between supply and demand. Although the pig market is notoriously cyclical, the latest downturn has been especially prolonged and harsh. I welcome the first signs of recovery that are evident in the marketplace. The Government's ability to provide financial assistance to the pig industry is severely curtailed by the limits imposed by EU state aid rules. There are good reasons for the rules. They are designed to prevent unfair competition and achieve a level playing field throughout Europe. Hon. Members from Northern Ireland will be aware of the fire at the pig processing factory in Ballymoney in 1998 and the difficulties that that created with the build-up of stocks of live animals on farms.
