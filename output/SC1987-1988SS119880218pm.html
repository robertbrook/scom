Mr. Nicholas Fairbairn
(Perth and Kinross)
: I am extremely disturbed by the language of the clause, and am for once indebted to the honourable Member for East Lothian (Mr. Home Robertson), for understanding the clause and to my honourable Friend the Member for Eastwood (Mr. Stewart) for pointing out its fallacies.

It will be impossible for a tenant to establish that his rent is higher or lower than any other, except by illegal or impertinent means. The phrase "significantly higher" has no significance, and the phrase:

"under similar tenancies of similar houses

seems to beg a question. In, say, Dunfermline, all the houses in a row may be similar, but some may have tenancies in the basement, in the top, or at the back. Some may be quiet, or noisy, some may have more facilities than others, some may have central heating and some double glazing. What are the characteristics of similarity?

The drafting of the clause is incomprehensible and meaningless. It is not a test by which a tenant could establish an appeal to the tribunal. I suggest that the normal phrasing of such a provision would be that, if the tenant considers that the rent payable under the tenancy is unreasonable or unfair, in all the circumstances, he may apply to the rent assessment committee. It is for that committee to adjudge the tenant's reasons for thinking that the rent is unfair and unreasonable, and whether he is right. The rent assessment committee may take all relevant factors into account, including, for example, that the tenant's house is at the end of a row, or on a roundabout; that he cannot sleep at night and lorries are parked outside 822 his house; and it is therefore quite different from the house at the other end of the row.

The only burden on the appellant should be a criterion that he has reason to believe that, in all the circumstances, the rent is unreasonable or unfair. A tribunal should operate on that sort of language of statute. I humbly commend to my honourable Friend the Under-Secretary of State that he gives an undertaking that the clause will be rephrased to form a criterion that a tenant can achieve.
