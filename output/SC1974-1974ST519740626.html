THE PRISON (AMENDMENT) RULES

10.30 a.m.

Mr. David Lane
: I beg to move.

That the Committee have considered the instrument.

The Opposition are grateful for the opportunity of a short debate on some aspects of the prison system which are covered by these amendments to the rules. We are not in any way hostile to the amendments. Indeed, the changes were largely prepared by the previous Government, but I should like to ask the hon. Lady one or two questions about them in practice.

We were anxious that, at least on this one occasion during the present rather short session, hon. Members on both sides should turn their attention to the prison service. I am sorry that there are not more of our colleagues here this morning to take advantage of this opportunity, but perhaps a few more will come before 12 o'clock which I believe is the latest time to which we may continue the debate.

Hon. Members will know that exactly two weeks ago today there was a debate in an other place—a wide-ranging general debate, which we clearly cannot have this morning—about the prison service in general. On the whole, the flavour there was encouraging, the message being that advances are being made in our whole prison system; not dramatic advances, but gradual and steady ones in the right direction, which we hope will continue.

We cannot range as widely this morning as the other place did two weeks ago, but I should like to make one or two brief observations merely as background to the issues raised in these amendments because they affect in a number of important aspects the day-to-day running of our prison system in general.

The first general point I hope I may make, and I hope that hon. Members will agree in this, is that the prison service is one of the most important front-line 24-hours a day responsibilities of Gov- 4 ernment. We rightly pay a great deal of attenetion to the problems of prisoners and their families and friends, and in the same way the public and we in this House pay a great deal of attention to the victims of prisoners and their families, but I doubt whether we give enough thought to the prison officers who have to work with these rules and administer them, and who will have to adjust their routines to these amendments, assuming that we agree to send them on their way with our blessing this morning. They have a very difficult and often dangerous job. We depend absolutely on their calibre and morale. The country is greatly in their debt, and I am glad of this chance to express our gratitude to them.

It is well known that the prison service has been under great strain and stress in terms of numbers and the burden of work in recent years. Is there any up-to-date information about the latest recruiting trends to the prision service? If there is, I am sure we shall all be interested to hear it. Secondly—and here I have in mind the over-crowded nature of some prisons which complicate such things as visiting which, as you know, Mr. Fitch, is covered by one of the amendments — we should be interested in any information which the Government can give us about their latest forecasts of the prison population and about their general plans for prison buildings. I am not suggesting that we can go into this in detail, but as far as I know—and I should be glad if the hon. Lady would at least confirm or correct my understanding—the prison population, with its implications of overcrowding, has been on a plateau of about 37,000, which is somewhat lower than was forecast. It would be excellent news if a turndown were forecast.

What worries us most—and again I have in mind the amendment about visiting, among other things—is that part of the overcrowding problem, which is relevant to visiting and general supervision problems, arises from the fact that nearly half the 37,000 population of our prisons have to live in prisons which were built before the end of the nineteenth century. I hope that the momentum of the prison building programme will be maintained as far as possible so that we can make a real impression within a few years, as JPEG 5 we were starting to do, on the problem of antiquated buildings and overcrowding.

My third and last general observation is prompted by the episode of the Price sisters, about which there is undoubtedly some continuing unease in the public mind. I do not expect any comment from the hon. Lady on the matter, but I hope that the Home Secretary will keep a free hand in the location and movement of prisoners. Nothing could be more disastrous for morale inside the prison service or for the reassurance of the general public if the idea gained currency that a Home Secretary could be shifted on any of these vital decisions by any form of blackmail or duress.

I now come to the amendments in the rules. I said at the beginning that these were largely worked out by the previous Government. We believe that they are on sound lines, and we are certain that the Committee should approve them. I have one or two points to make, and some questions to ask the hon. Lady.

The first change has to do with the religious denomination of prisoners. As I understand it, changes in the record of religious denomination are now to be in the Governor's hands. We believe that this is sensible, though the change should be made only, the rule says

"in a proper case and after due enquiry".

Is there much evidence of abuse? Bogus religious denominations could be dragged into a prisoner's record, and the hon. Lady may like to say something to the Committee about that.

Change No. 3 in the Statutory Instrument is concerned with the censorship of mail so that in future it will be discretionary and not mandatory for governors to continue the censorship of mail. As I understand it, when this change has been made in any establishment outgoing mail from prisoners to their friends or families will not be read. Incoming mail will be opened but not read, and that is clearly a necessary precaution against the possibility of money, drugs such as LSD, or other strange goods being enclosed with letters. They should clearly be intercepted before the letter gets to the prisoner, and that is how the system will work.

6 Presumably the Government intend to extend this discretionary practice gradually, establishment by establishment, on the basis of the experiment which has been going on for about two years in certain open establishments. I am sure that the Committee would appreciate a report from the hon. Lady on how far this has been extended and how much further she proposes to extend it over the next years or two. I am sure that the Committee would accept that where there are any security problems censorship must continue but, subject to that, we greatly welcome the more relaxed atmosphere that this change will bring.

There is, too, the welfare aspect of censorship. Up to now, one of the main usefulnesses of censorship has been that, from time to time, a letter has been a kind of warning to the staff, which they have not had before, of particular problems in a prisoner's life or in his home circumstances. This has enabled helpful action to be taken by the welfare services, and in many cases has led to these problems being solved, obviously for the greater ease of the prisoner. It is therefore essential that where censorship is no longer being applied there is some other way in which the welfare services can come to hear, in good time, of any developing problems of this kind. I wonder whether we may have a reassurance on that aspect from the hon. Lady.

Paragraph 4 in the Statutory Instrument deals with the frequency of visits. Here again the rules are being relaxed, and we welcome this. Clearly, visiting is, and always will be, one of the main sources of hope for prisoners. The greatest incentive of all is, of course, the possibility of parole. I doubt whether the Government want to say anything about the recent annual report of the Parole Board, but if the hon. Lady has any initial reactions to its interesting report we should be glad to hear them.

This change is already in force, and it has increased the frequency of visits to adult convicted prisoners to a minimum of one visit in every four weeks, instead of one visit in every eight weeks. This is merely giving statutory effect to the general practice in the prison service, and it is of the greatest importance that this change should work smoothly. I am JPEG 7 thinking here not only of the prisoners and what it means to them, their friends and families, but to the staff who administer the prisons.

One obvious difficulty is that the weekend, which for the families and friends of prisoners is the best visiting time, is the least suitable and least convenient time to provide extra supervision for people moving to and fro, particularly in the crowded prisons about which we were talking about. One has, of course, to bear in mind the obvious difference between, for example, busy local prisons where there are complications about visits at any time, and training prisons with more stable populations from different parts of the country where the complications are fewer. Perhaps the hon. Lady may be able to tell us something about how this will to work in practice.

I wonder whether the Minister would say something about travel warrants for prisoners' families. I understand that it has been the practice for these to be issued where necessary by the Department of Health and Social Security for visits once a month. This point was raised by Lady Sharples in her maiden speech two weeks ago in the debate in the other place, when she pressed for the more frequent issue of warrants, or at any rate a simplification of the procedure. On reading her speech I thought that there was a lot in this, and although it may be too soon to get any Government reaction this morning we should be interested to hear whether the hon. Lady has anything to say on the matter.

Paragraph 5 contains the package of changes in the rules which have to do with offences against prison discipline. Our objective—and we welcome very much that this has been carried on by the present Government—is to get greater flexibility in the discipline system. I am sure this is right, and I have only a few points to make here.

As the Committee realises, the punishment of a restricted diet of bread and water is out. In its time it certainly had some usefulness, but it is an antiquated form of punishment and, on balance, I am sure that we are better to be rid of it. The Committee will have noticed that in other respects some of the penalties are being increased; the 8 most obvious is the increase in the prison governor's powers over the stoppage of earnings or forfeiture of remission, which is raised at his discretion from 14 days to 28 days.

New Rule 53 deals with the novel idea of giving greater incentives to prisoners who are doing work and thereby earning money. It is interesting to note that the Government have chosen the method of a percentage reduction in the earnings that may be kept by a prisoner over a period. An alternative would have been a specific stoppage or fine of so many pence, or £1, or whatever it might have been.

I should have thought stopping a specific amount would have had a greater incentive effect than the percentage system which the Government have adopted. I admit that this is more flexible than the previous arrangement, but there is still an element of rigidity in reducing what can be earned by up to X per cent. Would the hon. Lady like to comment on why the Government finally decided on this method, rather than the other? How does she foresee it working out in practice?

My last comment is on the new Rule No. 54, which is about the forfeiture of remission. A prospective loss of remission will provide a new power to deal with anyone who is temporarily in prison while on remand awaiting trial or sentence, or who has been guilty of gross indiscipline or caused trouble. In the few cases where it may apply—and these can be troublesome cases—it will also prove a useful deterrent, and I hope that it will work well in practice.

I hope all the changes will work out successfully in practice, because I know how much thought has been given to them in the Home Office, particularly in the prison department. I hope the hon. Lady will confirm that there will be further reviews, at whatever intervals are necessary, on these and other matters which are so important to the smooth day-to-day running of prisons and to the feelings and way of life of prisoners and their friends and families, not forgetting the prison officers whom I mentioned earlier.

We look forward to a reduction in the prison population at the earliest date for which one can hope. One cannot allege JPEG 9 that this Government or any other Government can directly control that, but we are interested in the suggestions and the general trend of opinion in favour of fewer custodial sentences and the wider, more experimental, non-custodial treatment of prisoners. I am sure that is the direction in which we must go. We look forward in general to a continuing improvement in the administration of the prison system.

The Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Dr. Shirley Summer-skill)
: Perhaps I may reply to the hon. Member for Cambridge (Mr. Lane) now and after listening to the other speakers have a few minutes at the end to reply to any points that are raised.

I thank the hon. Gentleman for giving the Committee the opportunity of discussing the statutory instrument and the new rules. I assure him that the Government do not regard penal policy as in any way static. It is a dynamic subject, and we must constantly be able to adapt to take advantage of new experience and new ideas that are put forward. There should be total flexibility not only of attitude but in the regulations and rules. This is an example of the flexibility shown by the previous Government, and we are carrying it on.

Prisons vary enormously. There are more than 100 prison establishments of different kinds, employing nearly 20,000 people and having about 37,000 inmates. I join the hon. Gentleman in his tribute to prison officers who have the admiration of everyone in the work that they are doing. I shall let the hon. Gentleman know the recruiting trends to the prison services.

We are constantly reviewing and experimenting. The debate in the other place is being studied very carefully, and all the different suggestions made there will be studied.

10 The prison population exceeded 40,000 during part of 1970 and part of 1971. It fell gradually during 1972 and 1973, but since then the population has risen again and now stands at 36,586. That was the figure at 15th June 1974, and it is about 300 fewer than this time last year. The reduction in the population has been mainly in the adult male sentenced population serving short-term and medium-term sentences, and also in the number of young men sentenced to borstal. Over recent years there has, unfortunately, been an increase in the number of males under 17 sentenced to detention in junior detention centres. Although I say "unfortunately" this does reflect the increase in the number of places available.

The most recent public statement in the OFFICIAL REPORT concerning population forecasts was made by the hon. and learned Member for Runcorn (Mr. Carlisle) when he announced that the prison population was unlikely to exceed 42,000 by the end of the decade. Forecasts are under review all the time in the light of the movement of the prison population.

I should like to deal with certain specific points regarding the rules. I refer first to religion, which was the point raised by the hon. Gentleman. I am not aware that this is a widespread problem or one with which the prison authorities find it difficult to cope. The rule was intended to make the registration of religion flexible. For instance, there have been a number of instances where young prisoners have claimed to be Sikhs in order to be allowed to wear their hair long. At least one prisoner has claimed to be a Jew. That may have been in order to take advantage of the special holiday periods or diet, I do not know, but the rabbi, doubting that he was Jewish, felt difficulty in admitting him to religious celebrations.

A different problem is that of claims to membership of groups. Some people claim that they are Rastafarians, Black Muslims or Scientologists, so clearly there must be some flexibility in the rules about the registration of religion.

Dr. Summerskill
: Obviously, this would be done in close consultation with the prisoner involved. If he insisted that he was a Sikh and nothing could shift him from that belief, that could be considered. This provision makes for flexibility, so that what someone registered as when he goes into prison is not necessarily what he is regarded as 15 years later if he is still there.

The results of the experiment in limited censorship at certain open establishments have been encouraging. In the last two years censorship has been abolished in three open prisons, an open borstal and three open establishments for women and girls. I cannot give the hon. Gentleman a detailed reply on what the effect of the censorship is or has been. As I said, it is encouraging and, when one considers how long pr sons have been going, two or three years in the life of prisons is not all that long. But clearly this is the start of the relaxation of censorship.

Apart from welfare considerations, it is obviously necessary in the interests of justice, so as to prevent attempts to deceive the court and, which is very important, in the interests of security, that there should be censorship among the high security prisoners. The relaxation of censorship would naturally have to take these factors into consideration.

Censorship gave no guarantee that serious welfare problems would necessarily be brought to the notice of the staff, even when it was carried out. In cases where censorship has been abolished, prisoners have shown more self-reliance in tackling their own domestic problems and have in general sought advice when necessary, which is encouraging.

As regards assisted visits, the Department of Health and Social Security at present assesses and gives financial assistance to prisoners' relatives who qualify for supplementary benefit, but it is unable to do so for the additional numbers who would be involved in the several extensions of the scheme now under consideration. These extensions involve cases where there are sentences of three months or less, cases where prisons allow visits more frequently than monthly, and cases where the relatives have full-time earnings not in excess of supple- 12 mentary benefit level. In these' last cases, assistance is at present provided by the probation service.

Officials at the Home Office and the Department of Health and Social Security are studying ways of overcoming these difficulties, which mainly involve staffing. If a solution to the practical problem can be found, we have then to consider whether, in the present climate, financial provision for these improvements can be made.

I now come to new Rule 53. The concept of a fine presents practical difficulties as well as problems of equity for implementation in the prison context. The problem is to assess the amount to be specified in relation to the expected earnings of different prisoners at different times, but earnings can vary from 35p at the minimum to £1·89 or more with overtime. Also the job that a prisoner is doing varies; he does not necessarily stay at the same job week after week.

Given that the aim of this change in the rule is for the prisoner to retain an interest in the level of his earnings, it was thought simpler and more equitable to fine him an appropriate proportion of his earnings than to set a fixed sum. The effect of this will vary according to the kind of work he is doing in the period following the award.

As regards new Rule 54 about loss of remission, I have noted the comments which the hon. Gentleman made.

Dr. Summerskill
: I have a variety of questions with which to deal. I shall start by answering the hon. Member for Antrim, North (Rev. Ian Paisley). I thank him for his informed questions.

Under Rule 34(5) dealing with the denial of visits as a form of punishment, a governor may defer the right to visits of a convicted prisoner when he is undergoing an award of cellular confinement. This is a punishment. It can last as long as 56 days. Unconvicted prisoners may have as many visits as it is practicable to allow.

As regards the wearing of clothes by convicted prisoners, men must wear prison clothing but women can wear their own clothes.

Regarding denial of access to religious services, normally there is no difficulty, but for practical, including security, reasons it may sometimes be necessary to deny access to corporate worship. It is possible for a prisoner's facilities for worship to be in private. The priest, rabbi or clergyman would come to the cell. The sacraments are made readily available, there is never any question of denial of them.

Long periods during which remand prisoners are locked in cells are sometimes unavoidable, because of staff shortages, which make it impossible to have everyone wandering round adequately supervised. We should certainly like to JPEG 19 have as little unnecessary confinement as possible, and to that end possible changes in bail procedures may help.

I come to the point made by the hon. Member for Harlow (Mr. Newens).
