Mr. Brian Wilson
(Cunninghame, North)
: I beg to move amendment No. 58, in page 2, line 29, at end insert:

"(h) the duty to keep under review all matters relating to the provision, development and improvement of facilities for the enjoyment of the natural heritage and the need to secure access for the purposes of open air recreation, and to encourage, assist, or promote the implementation of any proposals with respect to the enjoyment of the natural heritage, being proposals which SNH consider to be suitable.".

After Tuesday's proceedings, I am not sure that any of our amendments will be agreed to. The most exceptionable notion in our amendment No. 4 was the idea of giving Scottish Natural Heritage an educational remit. As the Minister could not accept that, I am in trepidation about expecting him to accept anything designed to improve and strengthen the work of that new agency. However, we plough on.

Amendment No. 58 has the advantage of being widely drawn. It does not concentrate exclusively on access, although I shall refer to that. It takes a wide sweep, embodying the idea that the new agency should, in general, encourage as many as possible of our fellow citizens to take advantage of Scotland's natural heritage.

The amendment is meant to embody what I regard as the present role of the Countryside Commission for Scotland as distinct from that of the Nature Conservancy Council for Scotland. The Bill places too much emphasis on the scientific aspect of conservation and the environment, and not enough on leisure and recreation—the idea of getting people out into the countryside to enjoy it. It is a matter of emphasis. Although nothing in the Bill precludes those aims, stating them in the Bill will upgrade their significance, and responsibility for promoting them will fall on SNH.

I am encouraged to believe that the Minister might find the idea acceptable. In a previous debate, when we were talking exclusively about access, the Minister said:

"The amendment would highlight access at the expense of other aspects of SNH's purpose of facilitating enjoyment of the natrual heritage."—[ Official Report, First Scottish Standing Committee, 19 February 1991; c.24.]

That amendment was too narrowly drawn for the Minister's liking, as it concentrated on access, amendment No. 58 is broadly drawn and includes 116 the aim of encouraging other aspects connected with the enjoyment of the natural heritage. I hope that the Minister will follow the logic of his words, but I am not entirely confident.

Many people are disappointed by the limited references to recreation and public access in the consultation document and, subsequently, in the Bill. There seems to be a disproportionate emphasis on the scientific aspects of conservation, for instance, the ommission of any reference to the growing threat to access in the highlands is surprising. That problem seems to arise from the development of forestry, intensive agricultural development and various other activities that preclude the public use of the countryside. I hope that one of SNH's key objectives will be to safeguard footpaths throughout the farmed and forested lands, of the highlands and lowlands. New powers and resources are clearly needed to ensure that that network can be maintained.

I am disappointed that the consultation document and the Bill do not propose clear land acquisition and management powers for SNH to safeguard landscape and recreational resources. Obviously, it will be difficult for it to operate in an integrated way if its powers are limited to safeguarding natural sites of special scientific interest or those that are similarly defined.

The amendment can do justice to the past work of the CCS. I believe that we can promote the concept of encouraging people to go into the countryside to learn about it and enjoy its benefits to the full. I dislike the exclusivist tone that often enters such debates. I accept that some people have a particular interest in, or commitment to climbing in the Scottish mountain areas or are interested in other valuable aspects of the rural environment. However, they have a tendency to concentrate on the great threat posed by large numbers visiting the countryside.

There is an exclusiveness in that attitude that I do not like and it was summed up in the other place by Lord Grimond. I am constantly surprised by the role of the Liberal Democrats in this Committee, and those who read the proceedings of the Bill will be struck by the anti-environmental stance that is so often adopted, by Liberal Democrat spokesmen in both Houses. The rhetoric used to promote their green image is seldom matched by their speeches on the Bill. To paraphrase Lord Grimond, he believes that while it is splendid that people should visit our natural heritage and sites of natural beauty, a limit on the numbers, might have to be imposed. He drew a comparison with Notre Dame cathedral which he said had been ruined as a place of worship and he argued that that meant that the number of tourists would have to be reduced. The logic of such comments is that we do not want too many people to visit the Scottish countryside because their very presence will destroy it.

That sort of attitude seems to ignore the fact that the Scottish countryside is almost infinite in scale and variety. We live in a very big country, the majority of which is uninhabited. Those who fly over Scotland are aware of just how much of our land mass is uninhabited.

The idea of the amendment—I hope that it also applies to the Bill— is not to draw ever more people JPEG 117 into the Scottish rural equivalent of Notre Dame, but to draw them into the countryside.

The Ramblers Association has kindly given me some figures about the extent to which Scotland's population presently enjoys the countryside. Walking is one of the most popular pastimes. The 1981 Scottish leisure survey showed that frequency of participation in countryside walks is much higher in Scotland than in England. In Scotland, 77 per cent of those who walk participate at least once a month in walks of two miles or more, compared with only 62 per cent in England and Wales. Walking is one of the prime ways in which ordinary Scottish people enjoy Scotland's natural heritage.

Unfortunately, walkers in Scotland are not as well served as those who walk in England. A recent survey of walking in Scotland, which was undertaken as part of the Scottish opinion survey of 1990, showed that more than one third of all walkers in Scotland encounter obstructions or difficulties when walking, 118 which is more than in England. The Rights of Way Act 1990 will take effect in England and Wales, but there is no equivalent legislation for Scotland. There seems to be no urgency on the part of the Government to ensure that walkers in Scotland can enjoy the countryside without obstruction.

Another survey appeared in the book "This Land is Our Land" by Marion Shoard, published in 1987. It tried to compare the number of right of way networks—local footpaths and so on—available to walkers in Scotland and in England, and found that the average path density was much lower in Scotland than in England. Among the counties highlighted as the worst in England was Norfolk. It is a pity that the hon. Member for Norfolk, North-West (Mr. Bellingham) is not in the Room as I am sure that he would be interested to learn that, according to Marion Shoard's survey, Norfolk had a density of only 0.9 miles of path per square mile of land. Even that low density by English standards was considerably higher than the average Scottish density.

JPEG 125 Clause 3 lays a duty upon SNH in exercising its functions to have regard to a number of other factors that interact with the natural heritage of Scotland. The new paragraph adds to that list the duty to take such account as may be appropriate in the circumstances of the need to conserve sites and landscapes of archaeological or historical interest. I know that the hon. Member for Linlithgow (Mr. Dalyell) and my hon. and learned Friend the Member for Perth and Kinross (Sir Nicholas Fairbairn) have a keen interest in that subject.

The landscape that we see around us today is obviously the product of a close relationship between man and his environment over many thousands of years. The conservation and protection of the natural landscape and of archaeological records must therefore inevitably proceed in parallel, not least because knowledge and understanding of the one must depend upon an informed view of the other.

The Government believe it important, therefore, that SNH give due regard to the need to conserve the archaeological record in exercise of its functions; that is the reason for the amendment. It is, of course, true that scheduled monuments and listed buildings already have a statutory protection, but the archaeological record also includes many thousands of ancient monuments not scheduled, archaeological landscapes where a number of smaller archaeological sites may be grouped relatively closely and important historic sites such as battlefields, designed landscapes and gardens of major importance. Sites in that last category were usually constructed to provide a suitable setting for a great house, but in many cases the landscape created is now of more importance than the house itself, and, indeed, in some cases has outlasted the house.

A wide range of interests responded to our consultation paper "Scotland's Natural Heritage: The Way Ahead", including the Ancient Monuments Board for Scotland and the CCS. They consider that a duty of this nature should be introduced into clause 3. I believe that the amendment fully meets those representations and those made by the Committee.

At our second sitting on 21 February, I promised that the Government would table an amendment as soon as possible on sites of archaeological and historical interest. Amendment No. 62 fulfils that undertaking.
