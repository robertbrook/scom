Draft African Development Fund (Seventh Replenishment) Order 1996

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs (Dr. Liam Fox)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft African Development Fund (Seventh Replenishment) Order 1996.

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the draft International Development Association (Interim Trust Fund) Order 1996 and the draft Caribbean Development Bank (Further Payments) Order 1996.

Dr. Liam Fox
: Mr. Mills, I see that, according to the Committee list, your knighthood has come a little prematurely.

Of the three concessionary lending arms of multilateral development banks in the International Development Association provides concessional financing to the poorest developing countries, some 79 in total, with a population of more than 3 billion, where most annual incomes are less than $905 a head. We very much welcome the lead that the president of the World bank is taking in emphasising that the IDA's main objective is to improve living standards by reducing poverty, and that all its activities must be a means to that end. That is complemented by two supporting objectives: broad-based economic growth led by the private sector; and environmental sustainability. Those objectives are in line with the United Kingdom's aid programme.

The IDA's funds are replenished every three years and negotiations provide an opportunity for donors to re-examine and agree policy priorities to guide the IDA's operations. Donors reached agreement in March this year in Tokyo on the 11th replenishment—IDA-11. New donor finance, combined with the IDA's own resources, arrears and unused money from the 10th replenishment—IDA-10—should enable the IDA to provide $22 billion to poor countries over the next three years.

The United States' arrears to IDA-10 precluded the establishment of a fully burden-shared replenishment from IDA-11 for the three years from July 1996. Accordingly, it was agreed that, for the first year, an interim trust fund excluding the United States would be established, leaving the United States to deal with its arrears. For the other two years of IDA-11, a traditional burden-sharing arrangement was agreed. Subject to parliamentary approval, the total UK contribution will amount to some £477 million over the three years.

4 The purpose of today's order is to authorise a UK contribution of £177.6 million to the interim trust fund. I shall seek the House's approval for the remaining £299.4 million contribution next year.

The IDA tailors its assistance, in consultation with each borrower, to the country's development needs, its capacity to implement projects and its demonstration commitment to sound policies and good governance.

We also consider that, for economic development and reform programmes to be sustainable, the IDA should intensify its efforts to ensure that Governments seek broad participation in their design and implementation. Participation should become an integral part of the IDA's operations.

In their deputies' report donors reaffirmed that, in addition to broad-based economic growth, investments in health, population, education, water and sanitation are vital. They have welcomed the increased share of IDA lending for projects in sectors and recommended that each country assistance strategy explicitly considers the role of the IDA's lending for them. I am particularly pleased that emphasis is placed on education for girls, which has a uniquely catalytic effect on all other dimensions of development.

Together with other donors, we acknowledge the importance of the IDA's continuing effort to integrate thoroughly environmental considerations across the spectrum of IDA projects. We are urging that the quality of environmental assessments continue to improve, and be integrated systematically into project design and implementation.

The UK's view that private sector economic activity is the cornerstone of job creation and poverty reduction is now common ground. The IDA has a role in assisting borrowers to improve the environment for private economic activity through its policy-based lending and investment in training, institution strengthening and infrastructure.

The IDA's activities financed by the interim trust fund will be guided by all these objectives and will follow the IDA's policies, practices and procedures. Through our representation on the executive board, we shall continue to monitor its activities closely to ensure that the IDA meets all its objectives. The need now is to give the IDA the wherewithal to translate its policies and intentions into practice. I therefore strongly commend approval of the British share of that multilateral enterprise.

I now wish to turn to the African development fund—that part of the African Development bank which lends on concessional terms. The UK was a founder member of the ADF in 1973. In recent years there has been a delay in reaching agreement on the seventh replenishment because of concerns about the financial status and management efficiency of the African Development bank group as a whole. In particular, high arrears among borrowers and poor management efficiency control led to a general lack of confidence in the bank. The UK was among those donors who declined to contribute to the replenishment of the fund until those problems were properly addressed. We and our fellow donors are now JPEG 5 confident, however, that under its new president, Omar Kabbaj, an excellent start has been made in restructuring the bank to make it more effective. Since taking up his appointment in September 1995, Mr. Kabbaj has reduced the size of the bank's staff and introduced a reorganisation to ensure that the bank is run more efficiently and prudently. Most important, the board of governors has agreed on a more rigorous lending policy to prevent a build-up of arrears. Taken together, those actions have restored shareholder confidence.

The seventh replenishment of the ADF, agreed earlier this year, raised special drawing rights of about £1·7 billion—in contributions from donors. The proposed UK contribution amounts to £70·2 million. Those donor contributions will also be supplemented by SDR 730 million of loan repayments and cancellations which are expected to accrue to the fund over the next three years. That will give an overall amount of £2·4 billion.

As part of the replenishment agreement between the African Development bank, management and donors to the fund, policy guidelines for the fund have been established. They are broadly similar to those for the IDA, with the emphasis on poverty alleviation and environmental sustainability. The African Development bank group has also followed the lead of the World bank in adopting a system of country strategy papers for discussion at the executive board as the basis for deciding a clear lending programme tailored to the needs of each borrower. Commitment to sound economic policies, good government and effective implementation are the agreed factors in determining the bank's relationship with recipients.

Finally, the Caribbean Development bank. That has developed steadily since 1969 when the UK became one of its founding members. We maintain a very strong interest in the CDB and have agreed, subject to parliamentary approval, to provide £10·6 million to the fourth replenishment of the special development fund. That maintains the UK share at 17 per cent. The overall replenishment will be $100 million—just over £60 million.

The UK has great confidence in the CDB's very experienced president, Sir Neville Nicholls, a Barbadian, who is well supported by a staff drawn exclusively from the Caribbean region. The CDB has proved itself to be a well-managed bank and a cost-effective vehicle for resource transfers. Its financial ratios compare favourably with those of the larger development banks in terms of profitability, liquidity and quality of loan portfolio. Its net income is growing and it enjoys a high credit rating. Special development fund resources account for two thirds of the bank's resources.

Like all of the other banks, the CDB is increasing its efforts to help the poorest. In the process, it is shifting the emphasis from physical infrastructure to interventions in the social sector. Most of the CDB's borrowers are small countries that rely increasingly on the skills of their people in growing sectors such as tourism and financial services. The CDB is responding by increasing lending for human resource 6 development, environmental protection and public health. It is also providing assistance for capital market development and institution-building and supporting policy reform through increased sector lending. Inevitably, that involves a change in the way the CDB works and the type of expertise required of its own staff. The UK is helping by providing technical co-operation to alter the skills mix within the CDB to meet those new priorities.

We are pleased to continue the support of successive British Governments to those important institutions dedicated to the relief of poverty and to sustainable development. I commend all three orders to the House.
