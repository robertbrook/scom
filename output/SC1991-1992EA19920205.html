European Community Document No. 9997/91 on Milk

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. David Maclean)
: I should like to start by explaining the background to the Commission report and proposal contained in document no. 9997/91. In the United Kingdom and Ireland, whole milk has traditionally been marketed with its fat content unaltered—the fat content is as it comes from the cow.

THE CHAIRMAN'S attention having been called to the fact that three Members were not present, he suspended the proceedings; and other Members having come into the room and three Members being present, the proceedings were resumed.

Mr. Maclean
: I was explaining that in continental Europe, however, whole milk has been sold in standardised form with its fat content adjusted to a fixed level.

As the Commission's report shows, in the UK and Ireland, liquid milk consumption is a much more significant outlet for milk than in most other member states. The importance of maintaining that was a major factor when the present rules on marketing of whole milk were drawn up.

It was therefore decided that member states would permit the production and sale within their territories either of standardised or non-standardised milk, but not of both. Member states that chose to permit the production only of non-standardised milk were permitted to prohibit the import of standardised milk with a fat content lower than the average of their domestic production. The purpose of that provision was to protect the domestic industry from imports of standardised milk with which it could not compete. The guideline figure for imports is set annually by the Council on a proposal from the Commission. The UK and Ireland are the only two member states to have chosen that option. Since milk in other member states is generally standardised to 3.5 per cent or 3.6 per cent fat, below the UK average and thus guideline figure of 4 per cent, the existing rules largely protect the UK industry from any imports of whole milk from the ten other member states.

In setting the guideline figure for 1991–92, the Council charged the Commission with presenting to it a report on the application of that system and proposals for change by 1 June 1991. Those proposals emerged in November 1991. In its report, the Commission concludes that the maintenance of measures that effectively partition the Community market in whole milk is no longer justified. It does not wish, however, to impose a single type of whole milk on the entire Community. That would have a negative impact on consumption. The Commission 2 considers, quite rightly, that the consumer should be free to choose between the two products.

The Commission's proposal would therefore allow both types of milk to be produced and marketed side by side throughout the EC. In addition, it proposes an increase in the minimum permitted fat content for whole milk sold as from the cow from 3.0 per cent to 3.5 per cent to bring it in line with the minimum for standardised milk. New names for the two types of milk and retention of a separate category of milk with a high fat level, but with the minimum fat content increased from 3.8 per cent to 4 per cent, are also proposed. Finally, the proposal makes clear that standardised milk falls within the monopoly buying rights of the milk marketing boards.

That is an important proposal for the UK dairy industry. The Commission is adamant that the present restrictions on standardised milk entering the UK cannot continue. Since maintenance of those restrictions requires a proposal from the Commission to the Council each year, its view is certain to prevail. It is therefore essential that we negotiate changes to the existing rules to enable the UK industry to compete on equal terms with imports of standardised milk. The proposals are therefore welcome, as they are intended to permit the production of both standardised and non-standardised milk throughout the EC.

Concern has been expressed about the timing of the introduction of the new arrangements. The Commission originally proposed that they come into effect on 1 April 1992. However, that would have given the industry insufficient time to invest in new plant, design new packaging to conform with new rules on the naming of milk and use up old stocks of packaging. Following requests from the UK and other member states for a delay in the implementation date, the Commission has now said that it will propose delaying the introduction of the new rules until 1 January 1993. At the same time, it will propose extending the existing import restrictions until the same date. That is essential from the Government's point of view. A situation whereby the UK industry was prevented from producing standardised whole milk, but had to compete with imports, would be unacceptable. Ideally, we would have preferred a year's delay, but the proposal has the support of all other member states and is intended to give the industry sufficient time to adapt.

The proposed increase in minimum fat content of non-standardised milk is aimed at reducing the risk of fraud. Without the increase, there must be some risk that milk, which has had its fat content reduced to below 3.5 per cent could be passed off as milk with a low natural fat content. At a time when we are encouraging a reduction in total fat intake, it would be wrong for us to increase the average fat content of whole milk. But, at present, the average fat content of UK-produced whole milk straight from the cow is 4 per cent and only about 2 per cent of our milk falls below 3.5 per cent. So the overall impact of the measure is likely to be a reduction in fat consumption. The availability of standardised milk at 3.5 per cent will also give consumers who do not wish to purchase semi-skimmed milk another less JPEG 3 creamy alternative to non-standardised whole milk. The traditional product would, of course, continue to be available to those who wish to drink it.

We consider it important that, whatever new names are introduced to describe the two types of milk, they should enable the consumer easily to distinguish between them. That is probably the greatest sticking point at present. The Commission's proposal is that all milk would be described by the term "whole milk". For non-standardised milk, that term would be followed by the words "natural fat content", and for standardised milk, the term "whole milk" would be followed by the description "adjusted fat content".

That proposal was an honest attempt to meet the problem that both standardised and non-standardised milk are currently described as "whole milk" or "milk" in different parts of the Common Market. It has, however, met with opposition from member states that currently produce standardised milk. They consider that the proposed term for that milk is derogatory and would deter consumers from drinking it. Several member states argue that, under the new rules, standardised milk should be called "whole milk". However, that proposal is unacceptable to us as it would cause considerable confusion among UK consumers. At present, that remains unresolved.

We support the proposal to increase the minimum fat content of the higher fat milk category.

The Channel Islands will be able to continue producing their separate high-fat creamier milk. The practical impact of raising the minimum fat content is negligible as Channel Islands milk in the UK is already required to have at least 4 per cent fat.

The proposal relating to the monopoly buying powers of the milk marketing boards is a welcome element. Until such time as the boards are replaced by non-statutory marketing arrangements, the Government are committed to sustaining the proper and reliable operation of the milk marketing schemes within the existing legal framework.

Standardised milk could potentially become the norm for whole milk sold in the United Kingdom, in which case it could take up to 60 per cent of the market for liquid milk or 30 per cent of the total milk market. If there was doubt over whether standardised milk was within the boards' powers, the schemes could be seriously undermined. We therefore strongly support this aspect of the Commission's proposal.

Change to the existing rules on imports of whole milk into the United Kingdom is inevitable. The 4 Commission's proposal will enable the United Kingdom industry to compete on equal terms with imports of standardised milk and will give consumers added choice in the types of milk they can purchase. It is in our interest that the Council should adopt it. Problems remain, however, over the proposed names for the two types of milk. We shall continue to press for names that enable United Kingdom consumers to make an informed choice—All sensible suggestions on a postcard please. I commend the motion to the Committee.
