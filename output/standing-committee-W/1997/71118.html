Petrol Leakage 10.30 am

1.

Mr. Llwyd
: To ask the Secretary of State for Wales how many incidents of petrol leakage from underground storage tanks at garages there have been in Wales in the past two years; and if he will make a statement about the situation. [14784]

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Peter Hain)
: In the past two years, the Environment Agency recorded six incidents of water pollution from underground storage tanks at petrol filling stations in Wales, including one at Bontddu in the hon. Gentleman's constituency which, unfortunately, necessitated the evacuation of a local school and some homes.

Mr. Llwyd
: I thank the Minister for that reply. My major worry concerns the Bontddu incident, in which several agencies were involved. The Environment Agency, the Health and Safety Executive and the local council were contacted, but the Health and Safety Executive declined to act. It said that there was no perceived danger. An explosion then occurred, after which the HSE decided to become involved. A far better, more co-ordinated approach should have been taken. I regret to say that such incidents will become common occurrences over the next months and years. Will the Minister have some input into the current discussions on the draft legislation for the sale of petrol?

Mr. Hain
: I sympathise a great deal with the hon. Gentleman's view, especially in respect of the very serious incident in his constituency. I remind the Committee that on 1 September 1997, the Health and Safety Commission published proposals for a new set of regulations to deal with such problems. As the hon. Gentleman said, the situation is messy because regulations date back to 1928. They need complete modernisation and, undoubtedly, his point of view will be important in achieveing that objective.

Health Trust Funding

12.

Mr. Ieuan Wyn Jones
: To ask the Secretary of State for Wales when he last met the officials of health trusts in Wales to discuss their funding for the current financial year. [14785].

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Win Griffiths)
: I have met a number of trust chairmen. Welsh Office officials regularly meet with trusts, both individually and collectively, to discuss 2 their financial positions. Trusts are not funded by the Welsh Office—they receive their income from contracting with their main commissioners.

Mr. Jones
: The Minister will be aware of reports that at least half of the Welsh trusts will probably overshoot their budgets by the end of the financial year. He will also know that they have currently been charged by his officials with examining reconfiguration of the trusts. Does he agree that the first priority is to make sure that the trusts are properly funded? The management time involved in reconfiguration is not a good use of their money at the current time. Should not that issue be examined by the Welsh Assembly?

Mr. Griffiths
: I cannot agree with the hon. Gentleman. We are committed to reducing bureaucratic costs in the health service in Wales, and one way of doing so would be by reconfiguring the trusts. They did not grow up in the context of a logical means of delivering services throughout Wales. They were local responses to the policy of local quangos pursued by the previous Government.

We want to ensure that the trusts are reconfigured so that they deliver the best services to people locally. We admit that, in the short term, that will take time, but the overall benefit to the health service in Wales will be substantial. Between £5 million and £10 million will be saved in the operation of trusts and that will go directly into patient care.

Mr. Rowlands
: I remind my hon. Friend that the trusts are at least local trusts. Is he aware that an idea has been mooted in the Bro Taff area that the number of trusts should be reduced to two—creating large amorphous and even less accountable trusts and quangos? Why was the whole process initiated and being run by the health authorities? Why does not the Minister take charge of any reorganisation so that there is a democratic imput, rather than an input only by health authorities?

A health authority is a large administrative organisation with costs of £35 million, and with almost £2 million being paid in remuneration to the board of directors. Surely we should be considering a more democratic input rather than transferring responsibility to health authorities.

Mr. Griffiths
: I and my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Wales are in charge of the trust reconfiguration exercise. The health authorities are acting under a remit from the Welsh Office to develop more consistent proposals for trusts in Wales. As for Bro Taff, I have no details of the two-trust option referred to by my hon. Friend, but I understand that several options are being considered. I assure him that we want trusts to operate in such a way that services will be better delivered to people in Merthyr and in other parts of Wales. We also want to ensure a local accountable element in the operation of those trusts.

We must also ensure that the strategic advantages of a national ambulance service will be accompanied by strong local management, as there is much quality management in trusts such as the one in north Wales.

North Wales and the Government's Proposals for a Welsh Assembly

11 am

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Ron Davies)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of north Wales and the Government's proposals for a Welsh Assembly.

Before I start on the main thrust of my remarks, I want to make a brief comment. We are having a short debate this morning and I want to keep my remarks as brief as possible. I hope that hon. Members will understand that we are in an experimental period and we are having short debates to allow us to concentrate on specific parts of the Welsh agenda. I should be grateful if members of the Committee will let me know, either during the debate or subsequently in writing, the worth that they attach to this innovation, which allows us to have short, more focused debates.

This is the first opportunity that we have had for a Welsh Grand Committee since those momentous events of 18 September, so it gives me great pleasure to address the Committee on this occasion. The yes vote in the referendum has ushered in a new era for Welsh politics, which will be marked by greater democracy and accountability and which will bring about fair representation for all parts of Wales—north and south; east and west; rural and urban areas. For the first time, the government of Wales will be brought to the people and decisions will be taken to meet their needs and a Welsh agenda. I intend to make the Welsh Assembly as inclusive as possible—an Assembly that is answerable to all the people of Wales and involving partnerships with all areas and interests in Welsh society. I do not want to exclude any political party from the process. There are no representatives of the Conservative party here today, although they gained 20 per cent. of the vote in the general election. There will be an opportunity within the Assembly for the voice of Welsh conservativism to be heard.

JPEG 11 I believe that the voting system that we have chosen for the Assembly elections is the fairest available. In view of the fact that we spelt out the detail in the White Paper, I do not think it appropriate to change it prior to the first elections of the Assembly. I have been pressed by the Liberal Democrats on this matter, but I hope that they will understand that as the detail was in the White Paper, which was endorsed in the referendum, we should not move away from that principle. It may be that all four parties will have seats in north Wales—which is particularly important given the fact that we are debating north Wales today. The political diversity that exists throughout Wales, but especially in the north, can be recognised within the Assembly in a way that the Westminster structures do not allow.

Mr. Dafydd Wigley
(Caernarfon)
: The elections in north Wales will be based on the current European Parliament constituencies, which will disappear as there will be only one constituency with five seats for the whole of Wales. I am aware that representations have been made to the Welsh Office about the redesignation of the boundaries of those units for the purposes of elections to the Assembly. Mr. Davies
: It is certainly our intention that the existing European constituency arrangements will continue to apply for the purposes of calculating additional Members, which I believe was the right hon. Gentleman's question.

Ann Clwyd
(Cynon Valley)
: I am sorry to interrupt my right hon. Friend so early in his speech, but I must return to a Select Committee sitting just up the Corridor. He mentioned the system of elections for the Assembly. There has been some controversy over the system of proportional representation for the European Parliament elections. Can he give us any idea of what system of proportional representation will be adopted for Welsh Assembly elections?

Mr. Davies
: Of course, there is no controversy in Welsh political parties about proportional representation. The Labour party was unanimous on the matter in February and March this year, which shows how committed it is to pluralistic politics. We have considered the additional Member system and we have calculated the impact of it on elections in 1999, based on various turnouts and votes for alternative parties in recent European elections and in elections to this place. The advice that I have received, based on those calculations, is that with the additional Member system, with 20 additional seats, it is reasonably likely—although no one can forecast the strength of support for parties in 1999—that the system will be broadly proportional.

I gave a public undertaking to the Liberal Democrats prior to the general election that if the elections were to produce a system which was out of accord with our express wish of having an element of 12 proportionality, I would examine the matter. It is my firm view, however, that the system will ensure that element of proportionality.

The referendum results showed a marked difference in views between north Wales and south Wales and between east Wales and west Wales. Everyone has a theory about why different parts of Wales voted in the way that they did. What I want to stress, however, is not so much the various analyses since 18 September, but how much less divided Wales is in 1997 than it was in 1979, the year of the previous referendum.

The yes percentage in 1979 in Gwynedd, for example, was five times the yes percentage in Gwent. This year that gap narrowed to 2:1—a substantial move in favour of devolution. In the south Wales valleys, which were so hostile to devolution in 1979, there was a massive swing in the yes direction in 1997. Although it is true that there were unitary authority areas in the north, east and south which voted no in the referendum this year, I believe that, given the strength of the no vote in areas such as Monmouth, the Vale of Glamorgan and, indeed, constituencies in north Wales, the overall yes vote is a remarkable result. In all the unitary authority areas with a strong no vote, there was a high yes vote. It is a credit to the way in which political parties and other interested bodies in Wales were able to work together for the sake of creating a better democracy. I am conscious that we need to do a lot more work to bring Wales together. We must address genuine concerns and I acknowledge the fact that we must listen and continue to listen.

I want to say a little about how I see the Assembly working for north Wales in particular. I want especially to listen to the views of colleagues who represent north Wales constituencies. We undertook in the White Paper to give the Assembly regional Committees. The Bill will ensure that that commitment is delivered. One of the Committees must be an north Wales Committee—I have made that clear throughout all our discussions. It would, however, be a brave person who would dare to define the borders of north Wales in legislation. We must consult on that.
