Implications of the Budget for Wales

10.30 am

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. William Hague): I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of the implications of the Budget for Wales.

May I be the first to welcome you, Mr. Hughes, to the Chair of what is the first sitting of the Grand Committee in 1996? Many of us had hoped that there would have been other meetings in the interim and that those sittings would have made use of the new procedures that were approved by the House of Commons a year or so ago. The procedures make provision for Back Benchers to raise short debates and for there to be more Question Time sessions, but sadly, the Opposition Front Bench has not yet agreed to make regular use of such procedures.

Mr. Rhodri Morgan (Cardiff, West): Will the Secretary of State tell us what the Conservative Whips Office has said to the hon. Member for Cirencester and Tewkesbury (Mr. Clifton-Brown)? In Welsh questions recently, he seemed to be under the impression that the Welsh Grand Committee had met frequently in Wales during the past 12 months. On what was that misinformation based?

Mr. Hague: Clearly, my hon. Friend had not had contact with the Whips Office or anyone else, otherwise he would have known the position.

It has been a mystery to us why the Opposition Front Bench has not wanted to utilise the new procedures. I do not associate the hon. Member for Cardiff, West (Mr. Morgan) with being shy of asking questions, but for some reason Opposition Members are curiously shy of asking questions on a more regular basis. However, no doubt we shall resolve the problem in the end. Although it has not been an active year for the Welsh Grand Committee, it has been—more importantly—a highly active and successful year for the Welsh economy.

Mr. Ron Davies (Caerphilly): Will the Secretary of State give way?

Mr. Hague: It will be a long speech if I am regularly interrupted.

Mr. Davies: I shall be brief. As the Secretary of State saw fit to say that the Welsh Grand Committee has not met for a long time, I have some points to put to him. He will recall that he gave me an assurance in the Chamber, which I accepted in good faith, that the arrangements for the Welsh Grand Committee would continue as they had hitherto on the basis of an Col. 4 agreement between the Government and the Opposition. Will he now confirm that he is not prepared to honour that undertaking?

Will he further confirm that I have written to him on three occasions urging that the Welsh Grand Committee sits to discuss, respectively, the problem of bovine spongiform encephalopathy in Wales, the Sea Empress oil disaster in Pembrokeshire and the child abuse scandal in north Wales? On each occasion, the right hon. Gentleman frustrated any meeting of the Committee.

Mr. Hague: I remain firmly of the view that arrangements for the Grand Committee must be agreed by the usual channels. It is also my view that the procedures that have been approved by the House, which were widely welcomed by members on both sides of the Committee, should be used. Of course, the subjects for debate must be chosen evenly between Opposition and Conservative Members. In fact, the hon. Member for Caerphilly (Mr. Davies) will recall that in my last letter to him on the subject I offered him the opportunity to choose two of the next three subjects for debate between now and the end of the parliamentary session. However, because he does not want to use the procedures for Question Time and short debates, it has not been possible to agree on that. I am glad that I have had the opportunity fully to explain the position.

The success of the Welsh economy will be enhanced by the Budget that my right hon. and learned Friend the Chancellor of the Exchequer presented a little more than a week ago. The economic prospects for Wales are now the best that they have been for a generation. We have a tremendous combination of low inflation, low interest rates, booming exports and falling unemployment. The International Monetary Fund report that was published in July stated:

"The UK's economic performance is enviable".

That is different from what the IMF would have said about it 20 years ago—then it would have been enviable only to someone who was completely bankrupt. Now, the United Kingdom's economic performance is envied by countries throughout the world.

That performance is set to continue. We forecast 2.5 per cent. growth for this year and 3.5 per cent. for next year. The IMF and the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development agree that the British economy will be the fastest growing economy in Europe this year and next year. It is our fifth year of sustained growth. Our gross domestic product is now 8 per cent. above its previous peak; business investment was up by 6 per cent. in the first three quarters of this year; investment and consumption are showing sustained growth; export volumes have increased by more than 30 per cent. in three years; and the current account is at its best for nine years. That is an outstanding record and one that we want to continue.

Mr. Ted Rowlands (Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney): The right hon. Gentleman mentioned the significance of low interest rates. Can he guarantee that they will remain low?

Mr. Hague: The hon. Gentleman knows that members of the Government must not comment on or Image Col. 5 forecast the path of interest rates. They are subject to the decision of my right hon. and learned Friend the Chancellor, in consultation with the Governor of the Bank of England. During the past few years, my right hon. and learned Friend has clearly achieved a period of remarkable stability and low interest rates, which has been the reason for our incredibly successful economic record.

The Budget presented last week by my right hon. and learned Friend took no risks and did nothing to jeopardise those achievements. It outlined a variety of proposals to help small businesses. As a result, they will be even better placed in future to take advantage of the situation. The Budget will be a considerable help to small businesses in Wales. The small company rate of corporation tax comes down to 23 per cent., in line with the standard rate of income tax. That rate was 38 per cent. when the Opposition last had their grubby paws on it.

Employers national insurance contributions are to be reduced, further widening the competitive advantage that this country has over the over-regulated, overtaxed and overburdened economies of continental Europe. Business rates for small properties are being frozen, which will benefit almost 80,000 small firms in Wales. Four fifths of all business rate payers in Wales will benefit from the freeze in business rates in the coming year. The VAT registration threshold has been increased.

Those changes are on top of other measures being pursued by the Welsh Office to help small businesses in Wales. People are being helped to start up in business through training and enterprise council start-up schemes. Indeed, some 2,000 unemployed people are being helped to set up in business. The small firms training scheme, the loan guarantee scheme, the small firms merit award for research and technology—SMART awards—and support for products under research—the SPUR scheme—and the work of the new business connect network all add up to a good package of measures.

Mr. Llew Smith (Blaenau Gwent): The Secretary of State has outlined what he believes to be the wonderful things included in the Budget. Does he accept that if we go for a single currency, the Budget will not be decided by the Government of the day but by the European central bank? Does he also accept that article 107 of the treaty states quite plainly that it would be illegal for the Government—or, dare I say it, even a Welsh Assembly—to try to influence the European central bank?

Mr. Hague: It will not surprise the hon. Gentleman to learn that I do not intend to make an announcement today about single currencies. It may be a great disappointment to him. It is important to stay in the negotiations on a single currency because it will affect us whether we are in or out of it. There will come a time, of course, when we will have to decide whether we wish to go in to a single currency, and the points made by the hon. Gentleman will doubtless be weighed in the balance then.

Before the hon. Gentleman so helpfully intervened, I was saying that our help for small businesses comes on Col. 6 top of a healthy economic background. It is no surprise that the Western Mail should report last week that my right hon. and learned Friend the Chancellor was being credited with having delivered the best Budget for Welsh business for a decade.

Mr. Peter Hain (Neath): Can I test the right hon. Gentleman's shyness on the matter of a single currency? Can he envisage circumstances in which a single currency would benefit Wales?

Mr. Hague: I shall not speculate about the advantages or disadvantages of a single currency. On many public occasions, I have listed the possible advantages and disadvantages of a single currency, of all which must be taken into account when making any decision in the future.

The background to the Budget is one of rising living standards. After the Budget and after the rises in earnings over recent years, a family will be £20 a week better off than it was in 1992. That process will be further assisted by the reduction in income tax announced in the Budget. Britain has a sensible tax system with the lowest main rate of corporation tax of any European Union country. We have the lowest total business taxation of any G7 country, except Canada. We have pursued structural economic reforms—privatisation, deregulation and labour market reforms—all of which the Opposition trenchantly opposed at the time, but which are now widely praised throughout the world. They have given us a more flexible and less inflation-prone economy. Our business sector productivity has grown faster than in any other industrial economy since 1979.

The United Kingdom is attracting record levels of inward investment, much of which is coming to Wales—a subject to which I shall return in a moment. Inflation has remained below 4 per cent. for more than four years. Unemployment is falling steadily. The United Kingdom now has a higher proportion of people in work than any other major European economy.

Mr. Dafydd Wigley (Caernarfon): The right hon. Gentleman mentioned the 1p reduction in the rate of income tax. We note that there are no papers before us on the detailed effects of the Budget on Wales. Will he confirm that Welsh Office spending on housing is one of the items being slashed to fund that 1p reduction in income tax? The implication of the reduction in housing spending from £89 million to £60 million is that only 1,000 houses a year will be built, rather than the 5,000 houses that are needed. Will not the consequences of that cut be that homeless people will not be accommodated; that social workers, who are already under pressure, will be yet more overworked; and that the building sector, which needs money to build houses and sustain its activities, will be depressed yet again?

Mr. Hague: I hope to make my statement on public expenditure next week on the Floor of the House. As the Welsh Office baseline expenditure has been increased in the Budget in all three years of the public expenditure survey, the hon. Gentleman should realise that any tax reduction is not financed from the Welsh block. The Budget has increased public spending provision for Wales, so in no way has there been the trade off that the hon. Gentleman alleged.

Mr. David Hanson (Delyn): I am very interested in what the right hon. Gentleman has said. According to the Welsh Office press release that was issued with the Budget, there will be a 2.3 per cent. real terms cut in Welsh Office public expenditure by 1998–99, a 0.9 per cent. cut next year and a 0.9 per cent. cut the year after that. Does the right hon. Gentleman accept those figures, which were in his own press release? Mr. Hague: If the hon. Gentleman looks more carefully at the figures, he will see that all of the baselines for Welsh Office spending have been increased compared with the baselines at the time of last year's budget. The forecast Welsh Office expenditure for the coming years is higher for all the years of the public expenditure survey than it was forecast to be at this time last year.

Mr. Hanson: What about the figures?

Mr. Hague: I certainly accept those figures. It will be interesting to discover whether Labour Members accept them. If they think that those are not the appropriate figures for Welsh Office expenditure, they should tell us whether they think that the figures should be higher, lower or about the same.

Gentleman is sitting on the Opposition Benches, he can quiz us about our budget, but he is now answerable for his budget. Members should say whether they agree with the figures—[Interruption.] There is no point in Plaid Cymru Members making noises about that, because if they had their way there would be no calls by Wales on the United Kingdom Treasury—Wales would no longer be connected to the United Kingdom Treasury and none of that money would be available.

Mr. Alan W. Williams (Carmarthen): Following on from what my hon. Friend the Member for Caerphilly said, page 2 of the Welsh Office press release, issued on the day of the Budget, shows that the precentage change in real terms year by year is a negative figure—it is a cut. Mr. Hague: No, that is not right. The figures are not cumulative; the hon. Gentleman has added them up incorrectly. There is no 5 per cent. cut.

I return to the important subject of the Government's public spending plans, which are extremely clear—the numbers are set out plainly. We want to know whether Opposition Members agree with them. Member for Caerphilly will have to run off to the right hon. Member for Dunfermline, East (Mr. Brown) to get permission to say so. Mr. Cynog Dafis (Ceredigion and Pembroke, North): I thought that I would try the Red Book, which gives figures under the heading:

"Control Total by department in real terms"

and clearly shows that the grant to the Welsh Office will be reduced. In 1996–97, the figure is £6.7 billion in real terms; in 1997–98, it is £6.6 billion; in 1998–99, it is £6.4 billion; and in 1999–2000 it is £6.3 billion. Can those figures be interpreted in any way as a real terms increase?

Mr. Hague: What is very clear from other tables in the Red Book is that Welsh Office baselines are being increased in the Budget. The hon. Member for Caernarfon (Mr. Wigley) asked whether there was a trade-off in the Budget between tax reductions made by my right hon. and learned Friend the Chancellor and provision for the Welsh block. The answer is that there is not. In the Budget, provision for the Welsh block has been increased in every year to which the Budget applies.

The reforms of the 1980s and 1990s have created a vastly improved economic situation, in which Wales is sharing. I have said many times that my highest priority is to assist the flow of jobs into Wales. We are able to do that because we have a more flexible and competitive economy and a more diverse and well-balanced industrial structure.

The number of production businesses in Wales has increased by 50 per cent. since 1979. Indigenous companies now work alongside inward investors. Welsh steel-making is among the most efficient in Europe. Manufacturing has again become a Welsh success story. Wales is the only part of the United Kingdom where manufacturing employment has Image Col. 9 increased over the past 10 years. Manufacturing output in Wales rose by almost 5 per cent. in the year to June and by 10 per cent. over the past two years, while productivity in Wales was 7 per cent. above the UK average in 1993 and 9 per cent. above the UK average in 1994. The UK average has also been improving quickly. We must add to those successes the advantage of British non-wage labour costs: a UK employer spends £18 in non-wage labour costs—national insurance contributions and so on—for every £100 of wages, compared with £31 in Germany, £34 in Spain, £41 in France and £44 in Italy.

Those factors have made the UK an ideal business location and, within the UK, Wales is a particularly ideal business location. I have been pleased by the business surveys in recent months which show confidence among businesses in Wales at or among the highest level in the UK.

Mr. Rowlands: Will the Minister give way?

Mr. Hague: I shall in a moment, but I have already given way to the hon. Gentleman.

About 100,000 more people have found work in Wales over the past decade; unemployment is falling steadily and fell below 100,000 last month for the first time in many years. We know that more jobs are on the way. We have had a record-breaking year in terms of inward investment—so far this year, 128 projects, 13,600 jobs and £2.4 billion of new investment.

Mr. Morgan: New jobs?

Mr. Hague: Yes, new jobs; safeguard jobs are in addition to them.

Names like Halla, US Can, Newport Wafer-Fab, Ford, JCB and LG are bringing good-quality jobs to a range of industrial sectors and a wide range of benefits to our economy—not just the huge benefit of 75,000 people in Wales now working for overseas-owned companies, but the benefit of displaced imports and increased imports, reduced social payments, contributions to the Exchequer, management techniques from abroad meshing with our own and the potential for increased business for all.

Mr. Win Griffiths (Bridgend): May I check on an aspect of the right hon Gentleman's figures for employed and unemployed? Will he confirm that the increase in the overall size of the work force in Wales since 1979 is due to the huge increase in part-time jobs, mainly for women? In fact, the figures show that somewhere between 80,000 and 100,000—I cannot give the exact figure—fewer men are in full-time employment. That is disgraceful.

Mr. Hague: There is a flow of jobs of every description into Wales. Much of the rise in employment over several years has been in a different kind of employment—a kind that the hon. Gentleman should not disparage. Many women wanted to come into the work force through flexible, part-time jobs. The fact that our economy has been able to provide such jobs is a tremendous boost and adds significantly to the incomes of many households. We should not regard such employment as a second-class economy.

Mr. Rowlands: The right hon. Gentleman is making a serious point and I will ask a serious question. The non-wage costs have not disappeared; they have been transferred to the state in the form of family credit and rising unemployment benefits. Is that why we have the unique situation of public finances disconnected from the economy?

Mr. Hague: The countries with which I compared the UK have not only those non-wage costs, but a very expensive social security and welfare system. Many have a larger budget deficit than our own. I do not think that the connection that the hon. Gentleman was trying to make is valid.

Mr. Alan Williams (Swansea, West): Surely, my hon. Friend the Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney (Mr. Rowlands) is correct. Table 4A.1 of the Red Book shows that social security contributions from 1995 to 1996 and up to the next financial year have increased by £5 billion. Given that, and the fact that the right hon. Gentleman is rejoicing that he has reduced employers' contributions, does not that mean that even more than £5 billion has come from employees?

Mr. Hague: What it means is that because employment has grown, many more people are paying national insurance contributions—or having them paid for them by employers—than has been the case during the past few years. Of course, revenue grows when more people move into work. The point that I was making about international comparisons was from the standpoint of the employer who is deciding where to locate jobs. The United Kingdom has a substantial advantage; I think that the hon. Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney and I at least agree about that.

This week has brought more good news on the jobs front. On Monday, Lindale and Chambers announced 220 new jobs in mid-Wales. Today, two more significant inward investment successes have been announced—HSBC Gibbs is expanding its insurance services, creating 100 new jobs and safeguarding 250 jobs with a £1.5 million investment, and Kyushu Matsushita Electric—a company that I visited in Japan in September—is commencing the manufacture of digital cordless telephones with an £11 million investment that will create 100 jobs and safeguard another 110 jobs. Both of those are expansions of existing operations.

It is a striking feature of our inward investment success that so many companies want to reinvest in Wales because of the outstanding performance of the work force and the attractive business environment. Both of those expansions are in the Newport area, but I expect in the near future to be able to announce further projects bringing up to 2,000 new jobs in a variety of locations. Our inward investment success is set to continue.

Mr. Nick Ainger (Pembroke): Does the Secretary of State accept that for every £1 of inward investment there is £3 of exported investment? Can he explain why he thinks British companies are seeking to export investment, instead of investing in this country?

Mr. Hague: We now live in a global economy, as the hon. Gentleman may have noticed. The nature of the international economy has changed dramatically during the past 15 years. British companies have been investing in this country and elsewhere, where it is profitable to do so. It is entirely in the interests of the economy of this country that they should do that. The good news to add to the success of those British companies is that when companies from the United States and the far east want to set up an operation in Europe, they tend to turn to the United Kingdom and to invest here, not in continental Europe. Of course, an increased proportion of investment by companies right across the global economy is made in countries other than their own because there is now global competition and we have a world of much freer trade than that of 20 years ago.

Mr. Rod Richards (Clwyd, North-West): Does my right hon. Friend agree that investment by British companies abroad generates profits from which pension funds benefit?

Mr. Hague: My hon. Friend is quite right. It is welcome that British companies are finding profitable opportunities. That is helping to develop one of the other great competitive advantages for the future of the United Kingdom economy—the £600 billion that British people hold in pension funds, which is more than is held by the whole of the rest of Europe put together.

Mr. Peter Hain (Neath) rose—

Mr. Hague: I will give way to the hon. Gentleman, but then in the interests of time I must make some progress with my speech.

Mr. Hain: I am grateful to the Secretary of State for giving way. Will he confirm that, despite the rosy picture that he paints of inward investment, Britain's share of inward investment is no higher than it was under Clement Attlee's Labour Government of the 1940s?

Mr. Hague: As the whole of the rest of continental Europe was in ruins during the late 1940s, that may be understandable. It is remarkable that we are doing just as well now, in the 1990s, when continental Europe has been rebuilt. If continental Europe cannot do better than it did in the 1940s, it is doing a lot of things wrong. The hon. Gentleman's party is dedicated to copying many of the things done in the 1940s in continental Europe.

What lessons should we learn from our inward investment success? How do we take it forward? There are many lessons to learn—although the thoughts of the hon. Member for Neath (Mr. Hain) are not among them. The first lesson is that we must encourage and assist home-grown companies to take maximum advantage of our success. The Welsh Development Agency's initiative, Source Wales, is bringing many orders to Welsh companies and creating supplier parks. We must encourage companies to realise that something like the LG investment represents one of the greatest supplier opportunities in the history of Europe. I was delighted to go to Alignrite in Bridgend a few weeks ago, where Col. 12 200 jobs were announced—partly due to confidence in the industry because of the LG investment.

Mr. Elfyn Llwyd (Meirionnydd Nant Conwy): I listened carefully to the good news that the Secretary of State has imparted, but what does he intend to do about the imbalance in job creation between east and west Wales?

Mr. Hague: That is exactly the point I am making in talking about the lessons from inward investment and how to take that forward.

First, we must emphasise that the same assistance is available to both home-grown and overseas companies. There is a common misconception about that. I quoted figures in the Chamber the other day showing that in the past 10 years of regional selective assistance, £213 million went to overseas-owned companies and £215 to indigenous companies. We have given £88 million of regional selective assistance in the past five years to 660 small and medium-sized companies. However, the success of the Welsh economy is not just for inward investors—they are an important part of that success, but they are not the whole story.

Secondly, we must be alive to opportunities to spread more widely the actual incidence of inward investment—a point that was made by the hon. Member for Meirionydd Nant Conwy (Mr. Llwyd). The benefits can already be spread more widely. The people who will supply LG are located in a wide variety of places. They do not have to be in Newport; they can be in any part of Wales. But, of course, we must take every opportunity to encourage inward investors to locate nearer to the pockets of higher unemployment, such as south Pembrokeshire, the valleys and Holyhead, which stand out as having the highest unemployment in Wales. We cannot direct businesses or tell them where to go, but can enourage them through setting the Welsh Development Agency targets for investment outside the eastern M4 and A55 corridors and by asking the Development Board for Rural Wales to move half of its activity to the west, which we have done. We are trying to ensure that the WDA takes particular care to consider strategic sites in the west and the north.
