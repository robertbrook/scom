Question again proposed.

Mr. Rod Richards
(Clwyd, North-West)
: I shall confine my remarks to local government, its expenditure, priorities and the quality of services that it provides and enables to be provided.

The Opposition are critical of my right hon. Friend's proposals for total standard spending in Wales, even though he has not yet published any details. It is surely axiomatic that any organisation or body in the public sector bids for more resources than it has previously received or, indeed, expects to receive. It is self evident that, in the public sector, everyone could spend more than has been allocated. Spending commitments and priorities can always be expanded and increased with additional resources. It does not follow, however, that the quality of services would necessarily be improved. The task of local authorities and, indeed, the art of spending taxpayers' money is to ensure value for money within the set budget, not to complain and make excuses for low-quality services by alleging that central Government have not allocated sufficient resources.

My constituents, who live in the new unitary authority, Conwy county borough, are becoming disillusioned, concerned and alarmed by the new authority's policies and the prospect of deteriorating services in the Colwyn Bay area and beyond. As a local council taxpayer myself, both I and local authority-elected members share their concerns. My constituents say that they were generally content with the level and quality of services provided and enabled by the former Colwyn borough council. I reaffirm that which I have repeated many times: Colwyn borough council was an exemplary local authority. Its elected members acted responsibly in the interests of electors. Party politics were generally relegated in favour of quality, value for money and the greater good of the people of the borough. In this, they were well supported by consciencious and able officers, well lead and well motivated to enhance the quality of life in the borough.

I should like to cite one or two examples to illustrate my point. Colwyn Contracts, a thoroughly professional body, not only fulfilled its commitments to general satisfaction, but recorded a surplus of £200,000 in the last financial year. The environmental health department not only met its statutory duties satisfactorily, but pressed outside bodies, including 44 Welsh Water and the Welsh Office, to expedite its programmes to enhance the environment. I recall, in particular, the pressure on Welsh Water—no pun intended—to clean up the beaches and bathing water in the region. That programme is now well advanced.

The Chairman
: Order. Will the hon. Gentleman tell the Committee how his remarks are relevant to the implications of the Budget for Wales?

Mr. Richards
: Indeed, Mr. Hughes. While my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State can allocate resources to local authorities, the onus for the quality of services rests with the local authorities. There is a limit to my right hon. Friend's powers in relation to the spending of those resources, especially on education. I wish to draw a distinction between a well-run local authority and an authority that does not spend the resources that it has received—and those that it will receive under the Budget—on the provision of quality services and ensuring value for money.

My constituents reflect longingly on times past, but now increasingly realise that the new authority, Conwy county borough, reflects the standards, culture and ethos of the discredited Aberconwy borough council. This April, Aberconwy borough and Colwyn borough were joined together to form Conwy county borough.

Aberconwy borough council, unlike Colwyn borough council, is widely regarded as having been something of a disgrace. Indeed, my right hon. Friend's predecessor, my right hon. Friend the Member of for Wokingham (Mr. Redwood), would have capped it, had not the disaster of severe flooding in Llandudno in the summer of 1993 rescued it. Its environmental health department was almost criminally negligent. It did not fulfill its statutory duties of inspection and enforcement, and such inaction led to several incidents of food poisoning. Indeed, when those matters were drawn to my attention during my time in office, I considered asking the Secretary of State to invoke his powers to take control of environmental health matters from Aberconwy borough council.

The preserved county boundary between Clwyd and Gwynedd, which remains for the time being, continues to remind us of those circumstances.

There are already many examples of how Conwy county borough council discriminates against the people of Colwyn as a consequence of historical and cultural differences between the two sides of the River Conwy.

Mr. Richards
: The point I am making, Mr. Hughes, is that my right hon. Friend, with the best will in the world, allocates resources to local government to enable services to be provided. A moment ago, I quoted a leader of a council, who said:

"if it is a few grand over, so what?"

 If local authorities do not spend money on a value-for-money basis, and therefore waste money, my right hon. Friend must bear that in mind when he considers total JPEG 47 spending and allocation of resources to those authorities.

I shall be brief; I want to make just one or two more points. I have a proposal to put to my right hon. Friend for his consideration. Under section 56 of the Local Government Act 1972, my right hon. Friend, if he is so minded, can ask the Local Government Boundary Commission for Wales to conduct a review of the area that is currently the responsibility of Conwy county borough council, and of surrounding areas. The commission could make any of several recommendations, including constituting a new local government area—effectively creating a new unitary authority. There are many possibilities, but the option favoured by many of my constituents is a new unitary authority made up of the former Colwyn borough council plus Llandudno—the east bank of the River Conwy. The west bank, consisting of Aberconwy minus Llandudno, could be joined with Gwynedd, where many believe it would be more at home. Under section 58 of the 1972 Act, my right hon. Friend may, by order, give effect to proposals made by the commission with or without modification.

I do not wish to be prescriptive or to pre-empt any deliberation that my right hon. Friend might command; I merely ask that he considers the matter. I emphasise that primary legislation would not be required. I emphasise also that the divorce advocated by my constituents and their locally elected representatives on the ground of incompatibility has no bearing whatever on the reform of local government elsewhere in Wales, where it has been relatively harmonious and successful.

It is with serious intent that I put those matters before my right hon. Friend. Were there another way to ensure the effectiveness of the resources that he allocates through the public sector, in particular to local government, I would have explored it. My constituents feel that it is time to act.

"Following the Chancellor's Budget statement, Mr. Hague said: 'The need for a higher level of spending in Wales compared to England has once again been recognised' ".

 Those of us who represent Welsh constituencies know why that has been recognised year after year. Levels of deprivation are far higher in Wales than in England. Wages are about 10 per cent. lower in Wales than they are in England. In some areas, especially the peripheral parts—west, north-west and south-west Wales—levels are way below the Welsh, United Kingdom and European averages. The Sea Empress economic impact inquiry identified south-west Wales as having the same per capita income as parts of rural Portugal. That is the reality of parts of the Welsh economy.

Wales is deprived in terms of health and social services. Through no fault of our own, the mere fact that we are in the western part of Europe means that we have some of the most severely disadvantaged areas for agriculture. All those factors justify a per capita expenditure for Wales higher than that for England.

We want to know whether the level of public expenditure that the Secretary of State announced in the press release will answer the needs of Wales. I argue that it will not. it certainly will not meet the needs in this financial year—witness the problems in local government where there have been council tax rises in excess of 20 per cent., but local authorities have still had to cut services. People who need care in the community and those who need home helps are having to dig into their benefits to pay for those services. There are further problems in our education service. That, rather than the rosy picture that the Secretary of State painted, is the reality in Wales.

Let us examine the reality as shown in the Red Book and the Secretary of State's press release—not on the front page, but on the second page. Despite all the problems in the services that are directly funded through public expenditure, the Secretary of State proposes to cut £60 million from the budget for this financial year. He intends to cut a further £60 million 50 from public expenditure in 1997–98, £150 million in 1998–99, and £110 million in 1999–2000. Over five years, there will be a total of £380 million reduction in public expenditure in Wales for our health service, our education, our roads, and so on.

This morning, the Secretary of State and some of his hon. Friends kept asking my hon. Friend the Member for Caerphilly (Mr. Davies) what the Labour Party would do. We are debating the Government's Budget, however, so we need to ask from where that £380 million will be cut—I will gladly give way to enable the Secretary of State to explain that. Will it be taken from the health service? The Secretary of State assured us that if the Conservatives are re-elected, there will be year-on-year growth in real terms in the health budget. We can debate that. I am willing to accept, for the sake of the argument, that he can meet his promise. If he does, however, there will have to be even worse cuts in all the other services, because the health budget forms such a significant part of public expenditure.

Will the money be taken from agriculture, which has taken the hammering of BSE? Will it be taken from social services, which are under enormous pressure because so many more elderly people are living longer and needing support in the community? Will it be taken from our education service? Even the Secretary of State recognises that more needs to be spent on education, but the only way in which he can honour his promise of year-on-year growth in the health service is by cutting social services, education and every other service funded by the Welsh Office. Otherwise, the equation does not add up and is nonsensical.

If local authorities are to take that hammering, one of the problems that it will cause my—and many other—constituents is that education will not be improved in the way that those in industry, the political class and parents who want their children to have the best opportunities desire. If those cuts go ahead, the Welsh economy will be even more disadvantaged in the long term. The only way in which Wales can climb up the prosperity league table, in relation to not only England, but the rest of Europe—and it must do so, because it is languishing virtually at the bottom—is by investing in its people, especially its young people, and by having a highly educated and skilled work force. The Budget sends a clear message that those who want increased expenditure on education and training will have to wait for a different Government. The Red Book shows that in England, and presumably in Wales, there will be year-on-year cuts in education and employment. That is the reality of the Budget.

Wales also has severe infrastructure problems, nowhere more so than in my constituency and that of the hon. Member for Ceredigion and Pembroke, North (Mr. Dafis). Way back in August 1992, a report by Conran Roche Planning, funded at considerable cost by the Welsh Office, analysed infrastructure problems and suggested solutions; it reported to the task force which had the then Secretary of State as its chairman. One of the main objectives it listed in regard to infrastructure in west Wales was the identification of priority road schemes; it also suggested improvement in rail facilities and the attraction of new investment in telecommunications.

My constituency and the whole of south-west Wales have huge structural and economic problems. Whatever nice noises and promises the Secretary of State makes, the Budget has failed Wales.
