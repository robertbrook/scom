Resolved,

That the Committee do meet again this day at Four o'clock.—[Mr. John Morris.]

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that this afternoon we shall rise not later than 6 o'clock.

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. John Morris): I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the matter of the Welsh Economy.

First, I am sure that the Committee will want to welcome back the hon. Member for Barry (Sir R. Gower) after his recent illness. [Hon. Members: "Hear, hear".] We are all glad to see him looking so well.

I trust that it will be for the convenience of the Committee that the first subject for debate in this Session is the Welsh Economy. It is the subject which, after all, is the dominant one for all of us who represent Wales, and it is right, before we move to discuss particular sections of the life of Wales, that we should have our priorities right and comment on the economy as a whole.

Of course, as I have said repeatedly, we cannot divorce the economy of Wales from that of Britain. We share its economic chill winds and we share its sunshine. It is against this background that I hope that both sides of the Committee will welcome the progress made on our national current account and the fact that inflation, despite the gloomy forecast of some Opposition Members only a few months ago, is still down to around 8 per cent., and the battle against inflation is on course provided that we get sensible levels of wage settlements. Output has been rising this year well above 3 per cent., and though at a slightly lesser rate it is expected to grow again next year.

These are sound indicators, indicators upon which Britain and Wales share increased strength and confidence. I believe that Britain as a whole, and Wales in particular, have made a great deal of progress in the last four years in fighting and overcoming the effects of worldwide recession.

We have got on with the job of preparing Col. 2 for the future. Solid foundations have been laid because we decided that priority should be given to improving the infrastructure and industrial regeneration. During the life of this Government 58 miles of motorway have been completed in South Wales. We are in sight of a magnificent road right through from the Thames to very near the banks of the Tywi. This Government, because they made the road a priority, have got on with it, and everyone who cares for the economy of South Wales will view this accomplishment with pleasure and pride. The feeder roads will follow, but I believed it right to get on with the main artery first. We have already seen industry after industry—-and we shall see more—noting this important development in South Wales and deciding to come and settle here.

Then there is the A55 North Wales route. The motorways in the Chester area are to be extended to give better connections with Deeside. Thereafter, I want to see a dual carriageway from Chester to the west of Bangor.

Work is expected to start next year on two schemes, costing about £4 million—the Casino junction and the diversion east of Abergele. Planning is going ahead on the rest of the route. The A55 is my next priority in the trunk roads programme.

We have continued to encourage the diversification of Welsh industry. I wish particularly to refer to the advance factory programme. We took the decisions to build advance factories, although they are only part of the answer. We were criticised for our inability to let the factories immediately they were built. The policy of continuing to build in the face of letting difficulties was scorned by Opposition Members. We continued, and I believe that the policy has been vindicated. I have authorised the building of nearly 400 factories since I took office. The bulk of them are either built or under construction, and we are letting them. Firm lettings this year come to 86, and this includes the lettings of seven more factories being announced today. That is over 1 million sq. ft. of factory space let this year, providing opportunity for employment for some 4,500 people. There will be further lettings before the end of the year. Indeed, we currently have no fewer than 74 provisional allocations on the books. I expect the final tally for 1978 to be a "ton"—that is, 100. Indeed, I am confident that we shall be there or there- Image Col. 3 abouts—in other words, about four times the level in 1977 or 1976—a remarkable achievement.

Another indicator of the upsurge in activity is the number of applications for regional selective assistance under section 7 of the Industry Act 1972—a good innovation of the Opposition which we have built upon by marketing it in order to encourage investment. The result is truly impressive. Since April 1974 over 600 offers of assistance under section 7 totalling over £50 million have been made, to create or safeguard more than 45,000 jobs. Since the beginning of this financial year applications are 25 per cent, up on last year in terms of numbers, nearly 30 per cent, in terms of jobs, and nearly double in terms of project costs—that is, investment. Twenty-four of the 136 applications this year so far are for projects costing over £1 million apiece, and one application in four is in respect of greenfield projects.

I turn now to what is associated with section 7 assistance, namely, a scheme whereby European Investment Bank loans can be made available through my industry department. It is a scheme which provides cover to companies against fluctuations in the exchange rates.

We have already cleared seven loans totalling £2¾ million with the bank, another nine totalling over £6 million are under consideration, and a further three very large ones totalling over £10 million are being negotiated by the companies concerned directly with the EIB. The rates of interest on these loans are very favourable, and we have missed no opportunity of encouraging firms in Wales to apply for this facility.

It is against this picture of, a great deal of progress being made that nevertheless, regrettably, levels of unemployment both in the United Kingdom and on a Welsh basis remain too high.

This is a welcome drop in unemployment but, of course, still not good enough.

Since unemployment among young people must be a cause for special concern, the fall in the number of unemployed school leavers is encouraging. I remind the Committee that when we met in June this year I reported that 15,600 school leavers registered as unemployed in August 1977 and that this total fell to below 5,000 by January 1978. As I forecast at that time, the number of school leavers seeking work in August this year has been greater—we all know that the number of youngsters coming on to the labour market is increasing—yet in the three months since August 1978 the number has fallen from over 16,500 to below 5,000—a notable improvement on the position last year.

I yield to no one, either in this Committee or outside, in my concern for a decrease in the level of unemployment, but what is not always recognised is the increase in the number of people coming on to the market. We should not overlook the fact that the number of people at work in Wales this year, at June 1978, is higher than at any June in the past decade. Admittedly, unemployment has increased, but this is significantly because the supply of labour has been rising fast. Therefore, it is right to get this matter in its proper perspective.

We have some way to go, but the gap between the unemployment rate in Wales and Great Britain has narrowed over the years.

I suggest to the Committee that, given the difficulties that the industrial world in Image Col. 5 general, and Wales in particular, has experienced, this is no mean achievement.

There is, of course, more to be done, and particularly in youth unemployment. That is why I am taking such a keen personal interest in the Manpower Services Commission's new youth opportunities programme. This programme has got off to a good start. The commission is confident that it will meet the guarantee that no Easter or summer school leaver who remains unemployed by the following Easter should be without an offer of a place on the programme. Within the next week or so I shall be meeting the chairman of the MSC Committee for Wales, together with the three chairmen of the area boards responsible for the youth opportunities programme in the different parts of Wales, to take stock.

I am sure that the Committee will have welcomed the recent announcement by my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Employment that from next January the small firms employment subsidy scheme is to be extended and will be available to small firms in the non-manufacturing sector in the development areas and special development areas. To date, SFES has been paid in respect of 2,892 new jobs in Wales, and I am sure that the extension of the scheme to the non-manufacturing sector will be of particular benefit to the Principality.

We have already debated the WD A, and the only factor I wish to mention today is its industrial investment. In its industrial investment role, the WDA has now built up an investment portfolio totalling over £9 million. It is estimated that the jobs saved or created by these investments will total some 2,600. Many of these investments have been in the small firms which are so vital to the economy of Wales. In those cases the agency's intervention will have been doubly valuable, since the provision of expert advice by the agency's small business unit often goes hand in hand with the provision of a loan.

I turn now to the DBRW. As Secretary of State for Wales and a native Mid-Walian, I am proud to have brought the DBRW into being and I am encouraged by what it has done so far. I felt when I took office that it was time to stop talking about the problems of Mid-Wales—Mid-Wales was sick of mere plans—and time to do something new and positive. Well, we have, The board took over its executive responsibilities on 1st April 1977 and, as its Col. 6 first annual report shows, it has this year made the best of starts.

The board has been provided with adequate funds to meet its developing programme for the regeneration of industry and the economy of rural Wales. Expenditure during 1977–78 was about £5.7 million, and in the current year the total cash budget, including supplementary provision, is about £8.6 million. The board is confident that it will be able to use all these funds by the end of the financial year. Next year, I shall be seeking Parliament's approval for funds totalling £9.3 million.

The board inherited 36 completed or partly-built factories for which tenants had not been found when it commenced work in April 1977, but by 31st March 1978 only seven of these were still available. This was an outstanding achievement by any standard, and I am happy to say that the trend in lettings is still firmly upward. To date, the board has let 468,500 sq. ft. of factory space and, with the let factories it inherited, the board's factories now provide some 4,500 jobs, with over 350 more in the offing from factories in the pipeline. The policy of building advance factories, be it in the WDA area or in the DBRW area, has been thoroughly vindicated.

May I say how heartened I was by the massive support for the retention of the milk marketing boards. A 99 per cent, vote in favour is a rare event in this country. I turn briefly now to the Wales Tourist Board. Many jobs have been created through its scheme of assistance to the tourist industry to extend and improve its facilities. Up to the end of March, the board had passed on nearly £4 million in grants and loans to the industry since the scheme began in 1971, generating a total investment of nearly £9 million. A further £1J million is available to the board for this purpose in this finnacial year.

I am sure that anyone who travels around Wales will have noticed the steady improvement in the standard of tourist accommodation throughout the Principality, although I readily acknowledge that much still remains to be done. For this reason, I was pleased to announce last week that I would be making an extra £200,000 available to the board in 1979–80 to extend the scheme to the intermediate areas—I know of the concern expressed time after time in the Committee on this score—which means that assistance to the industry is now available throughout Wales. I hope that the North Wales coast resorts in particular will take advantage of this opportunity to modernise their facilities to provide the sort of standards which attract the higher-spending visitors, especially those from overseas.

I turn now to two sectors of our economy in Wales—Steel and coal—which will continue to need care and attention. The steel industry has faced and is still facing an unprecedented recession, a recession in demand which has affected Image Col. 9 steel makers throughout the developed world. This, of course, is of particular significance to us in Wales where the Welsh division constitutes about one-third of the British Steel Corporation. If we are to remain in this business we must be able to compete on equal terms with our competitors. This has called for unpalatable decisions. No one could be unmoved by the closure of the steel works at Ebbw Vale and Cardiff, areas with a long and proud tradition of steel-making and with a reputation second to none. But in grasping this nettle we are aware of the consequences for the communities involved and the enormous burden this places upon them. I shall say more about the additional support we are giving to these areas.

I turn now to the coal industry, which continues to play an important role in the Welsh economy, although on a much reduced scale from former years. With the industrial recession and the slow pick-up in the demand for increased energy supplies, it is inevitable that the coal industry will continue to face difficult days ahead. But the Government have already demonstrated their support for the British coal industry, and for the South Wales industry in particular with its difficult geological conditions.

Only last week, in a debate on the future of the South Wales coal industry, initiated by my hon. Friend the Member for Aberdare (Mr. Evans), my hon Friend the Under-Secretary of State for Energy announced the setting-up of a subcommittee of the coal industry tripartite group to conduct a study of the South Wales coalfield. This will cover both the difficulties currently being experienced in the coalfield and the positive prospects for the future. The Welsh Office will be involved throughout with the working of the sub-committee. I have asked my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary of State to take on this impotrant job.

We have already taken other steps to help South Wales. For example, £17 million is to be made available nationally to secure extra coal burn at power stations this winter, of which Wales gets its share. Exchequer assistance will be available to continue the earlier Government financial measures for extra burn of low-volatile coal in South Wales power stations which was first introduced in 1977. Apart from saving on expensive imports, this will mean that our valuable indigenous coal is being Col. 10 used rather than put into stock until Aberthaw "B" becomes fully operational shortly.

In 1977–78 a total of £8.3 million was provided to the NCB to assist towards the cost of stocking coke, and recently we announced that £16 million for such aid would be available, with Parliament's approval, for the current financial year. This, I might add, is part of a five-year programme, estimated at £60 million, to help National Smokeless Fuels.

The Committee can be assured that the Government will continue to keep very close watch on the problems of our coal industry. Our aim is to create jobs to replace those lost, whether in steel, coal or other industries. Let me turn very briefly from this general account to give specific examples of what is happening in some areas. I am sure hon. Members could give examples too.

In Ebbw Vale, the Government, the local authorities, the WD A, BSC and many other bodies have worked together to develop the large industrial estates at Tafarnaubach and Rassau. The WDA itself has programmed 52 advance factories since April 1977, totalling nearly half a million square feet. The measure of the importance of these programmes is that the manufacturing firms which have already opened in Blaenau Gwent since 1973 now employ over 1,400 people, and some of these projects promise more jobs. In all there are now about 1,230 manufacturing jobs in the pipeline for the area.

The agency is providing new factories on this massive Rassau site, some of which will be ready for occupation next spring. Here two factories have already been provisionally allocated, and that must augur well for the future.

All this can be achieved only by hard work and attention to every possibility that might bring new jobs to Ebbw Vale. That is what is being done. Similarly the Government have announced a programme of assistance in Cardiff following the closure of East Moors steelworks. I took the chair at a large conference in Cardiff in April. All the various bodies are cooperating to provide new sites and factories and road links to them. Plans have been announced for 20 advance factories capable of supporting some 500 jobs initially and 1,000 after three years. The Welsh Development Agency is taking the lead in providing sites for further factories and in drawing up plans for the revelop- Image Col. 11 ment of the site of the East Moors steelworks itself.

The co-operation between the county council, the WDA and BP Chemicals—together, if I may say so, with the Welsh Office acting as catalyst—is a fine example of what can be done in dealing with a difficult situation. I am confident that out of adversity there will be developed a most attractive industrial site which will undoubtedly prove to be a winner.

Perhaps the most telling evidence of all in support of regional policies generally—with all the changes that have taken place over the years—is to be found in an analysis of the migration trends. We all know about the half a million people we lost in the inter-war years, when unemployment rates of 30 per cent, were commonplace and 50 per cent, not unknown. We all know that the Second World War shelved our problems for us but did not solve them. After the war, outward migration loomed large again.

In 1951–61, there was net outward migration from Wales of about 35,000—that is, 35,000 more people moved out than moved in. In 1961–71, net outward migration was reduced to 10,000—the watershed was in the mid 1960s when we moved from a negative balance to a positive one, Image Col. 13 despite the massive loss of jobs in coal mining alone in the 1960s—an average of 5,000 jobs a year lost. The improvement was therefore evident, and this has been maintained—indeed, it has accelerated in the 1970s. In 1971–1977, there has been net inward migration of nearly 40,000—that is, 40,000 more people moved in than moved out.

To have narrowed the unemployment gap and reversed the migration trends at a time when our older basic industries have shed so much labour is truly remarkable, and an indication of how regional policies are working. Despite all the difficulties, therefore, I believe that we can report genuine progress to the Committee, and this optimism in the face of adversity is borne out by the comments in The Economist two weeks ago. Perhaps I might quote them to the Committee:

"The hangdog defensiveness that used to greet the outside inquirer into the Principality's affairs has disappeared. Partly, of course, that is because of cultural, political and purely administrative changes in the way all Wales is run. But much flows from the ending of the physical isolation of industrial South Wales. It is no longer a corner for the dirty jobs that others prefer to forget.


"First, it has become a place where a national firm can logically set up a factory or a warehouse to serve markets almost anywhere in the southern half of Britain. Second, the improvement in the internal communications of South Wales means that it can itself be regarded as a single market; no longer a series of villages but a string of reasonably well-connected communities. Third, as its publicists emphasise to the point of boredom, it is the nearest place to the South and Midlands of England where labour is in surplus. Welsh workers have earned a reputation for adaptability and for getting on with the job."

That is the quotation, and that is what I endorse. It is tributes like these from outside that really count. What The Economist said last week is being said, and will be said more and more, by those who have come from outside Wales and have experienced the quality and the worth of our people in every corner of the Principality.
