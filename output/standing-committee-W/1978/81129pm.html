THE WELSH ECONOMY

4.4 p.m.

Question again proposed.

Mr. Fred Evans
(Caerphilly)
: Listening to the hon. Member for Pembroke (Mr, Edwards) attempting—I deliberately say "attempting"—to answer the Secretary of State for Wales this morning, I might have thought that he was naive except that I know that the hon. Gentleman is anything but that. But at no point in his speech did he make a single reference to any achievement in the economy in Wales. He spoke as though he expected the Secretary of State to come here and say in a sorrowful voice "Mea culpa—I have done nothing right". The truth, as we all know, is that there have been substantial achievements in the Welsh scene during the past few years. That does not mean to say, however, that we absolve people from blame. We know the serious problems that face Wales. We are all determined to overcome them and we all expect—

The Chairman
: Order. I remind hon, Members that the reading of newspapers in the Committee is not permitted.

Mr. Evans
: We all expect the Secretary of State for Wales to play his full part in seeing that our aims are achieved and in giving the necessary leadership. When, therefore, we on the Government Benches are critical, and sometimes deeply critical, our criticism stems from the knowledge that the instrument is there if only we are able to persuade that instrument to do the job for which it was created.

The hon. Gentleman was naive also in trying to buttress a feeble argument by throwing at it masses of statistics from Treasury figures, as though everyone in the House did not know the history of mistakes, sometimes catastrophic mistakes, of that body and of the people who 54 have placed their faith in trying to interpret some of its conclusions and decisions.

The hon. Member ended his attack by quoting the article by the so-called economist-journalist Nicholas Davenport, which began with a faulty premise and then built on that premise a long list of assumptions of what could happen. This, if I remember correctly, the hon. Member for Pembroke (Mr. Edwards) described as "These are the facts". If Mr. Nicholas Davenport is so sure that these are facts, I am sure that my right hon. Friend the Chancellor of the Exchequer, with his usual generosity of spirit, would willingly move over to let Mr Davenport see what he could do about the problem.

There is, and always has been since the industrial revolution, an imbalance in the economy in Britain. That imbalance is reflected in the regions of Britain. It so happens that tragically, Wales reflects that imbalance far more deeply than do most other regions. Hon. Members who said that only with a regeneration of the total economy of the United Kingdom can we hope for brighter things in Wales were correct. That is the key factor. But we have in Wales the same imbalance. It is in this sphere that we expect the Secretary, of State for Wales to take positive action, and try to help us to create the kind of society in Wales that we all across these Benches wish for.

We have heard much about the various tragic episodes of today the increase in unemployment and the decrease of employment opportunities, was pointed out, with 80 people chasing each notified available job. For youth employment the figure is even higher and is likely to rise.

Looming on the horizon, are technological developments which, if. not carefully guided, will affect the total economy, and the economy in Wales very deeply, indeed. We are faced with developments' such as the discovery, and. now the imminent development, of the microchip, which is bound to lead to a massive displacement of labour throughout, our country.

Thus, as well as the present problems—and some of them I would agree are now pretty well endemic in the Welsh economy —we are faced with another set of problems which will overlie the old ones.That was recognised by my hon. Friend, the. Member for Aberdare (Mr. Evans), who spoke of our making ah attempt to alter the economy in some drastic ways. I "shall JPEG 55 have a little more to say about that later, because it seemed to me that his contribution did not include radical enough approaches to changing the economy in order to absorb the effects.

As so much attention was paid to these matters this morning, I shall refer to some problems that are besetting my own area at the moment and which, perhaps, are indicative of problems which are raising their heads in far too many parts of our country today. The area which I represent has the ignominious position in the 14 travel-to-work areas in Wales of all too often being in the top three or four for unemployment, and there have been two occasions on which one of the travel-to-work areas in my locality had the top unemployment figure in Great Britain— not just in Wales. That is a problem that all of us in the locality have had to live with for a very long time.

It came as a deep shock a couple of months ago, when industry after industry was collapsing, to find that with closures and threatened closures in the Rhymney valley 1,000 jobs were threatened in the space of, perhaps, a fortnight. I know that to all who are unemployed—it has been said often enough in this room—there is no percentage. For the unemployed person, it is 100 per cent. But leaving aside all the other factors of the Welsh heritage of culture and language and many other factors and looking at it only from a population point of view, I argue that there is a relativity about unemployment which means that the loss of two, three or half a dozen jobs in some parts of Wales, in rural areas, would also be looked upon as catastrophic, but in that part of Wales in Glamorgan and Gwent in South Wales where 1.8 per cent, of the population live, the density of population is such that a figure of just 2 per cent, unemployment has a tremendous impact on families and on the services around them, with, in addition, the creeping effects cutting off consumer production, reducing the productive capacity of the area and so on.

To take a classic example, there is in my constituency a coking works which produces what is probably—if one can so term it—the Rolls-Royce of cokes which is known from Scotland to Land's End. It employs over 300 men. It has always made a profit. In fact, it is the only part of the National Coal Board that every year since nationalisation has made a profit. It was 56 hived off under the Administration which the hon. Member for Pembroke supports and it began to make a loss. It has made a loss this year. It will probably make a loss next year. But, immediately, the decision was reached that this was an uneconomic works and must therefore close.

Those magnificent intellects who sit in their splendid isolation in palatial offices forgot some of the repercussions of decisions such as that. They forgot, for example, the tragic fact—these figures were given in answer to my Questions only 10 days or so ago—that this country imports 3 million tons of coking coal per annum instead of using indigenous resources. Indeed, although the figure is small, we are importing finished coke.

The pundits forgot that in the centre of a coalfield there is one colliery where, out of five day's production, this works absorbs two day's production. There is a second colliery where, because of geological, water and tipping space problems, production is down to 1,300 tons per week but that coking works absorbs 700 tons per week. There is another colliery nearby where the situation is much the same.

Therefore, the effect of closing the coking works would almost certainly be to close one colliery, to place under another colliery which could be pretty prosperous on the jeopardy list, and to leave a big question mark over the third.

In addition, we should end with all coking works centralised in the north of England, with nothing in Wales and nothing in the Midlands. One of the excuses given is the downturn in the steel market. If there is picking up of that market, it is not difficult to foresee what will happen. This country will demand coke, there will be no coke, and coke will be imported, in particular from Western Germany.

Another aspect needs to be gone into at Government level, and I suggest to the Secretary of State for Wales that he take a lead here. A brief reference was made this morning to the way in which EEC countries are massively subsidising many of their industries. We know all about the regulations against dumping, against subsidies and so on, but there are ways of circumventing these. Indeed, the old story is true that there are many people holding down highly paid jobs to discover ways of circumventing them.

This coking works had secured an export order for 10,000 tons, and a very favour- JPEG 57 able market in Detroit. It was well on the way to securing a permanent outlet, because a further sample had been asked for, the sample this time being 100,000 tons. The negotiations were nearing completion and the finality of signature on the contract was not far away, and then West Germany stepped in and undercut the offer by £4 per ton, so the contract was lost.

So far as I am aware, no Government have made a study across the board of what goes on and assessed the results. We find that in West Germany the cost of production per ton of coal is £40. The cost of production per ton of coal in Britain is £22. The investment by the British Government in the National Coal Board is about £75 million a year. The investment by the West German Government is £2,500 million per year. It is not difficult to see the pattern. There is an understanding that the mining industry will produce coal at £40 per ton, but the prices charged to people who make coke are reasonable, the people who sell coke to the steel manufacture charge reasonable prices, the manufacturing industries receive raw materials at reasonable prices, and we end by importing the cars and manufactured goods into Britain.

I am not arguing any case for protectionism. I am simply stating what one can easily see is happening. Unless we are extremely careful, the manufacturing base of Britain will be weakened to an almost non-existent level, and our industries will be little else but service industries.

That may seem a far cry from Wales, but in the past these heavy industries have been the basic industries of Wales, and coal still remains so to a large extent. Unless we take steps in Wales to make these things clear, we shall fail in our duty to the Welsh people. As someone who is not a nationalist but who is a fervent Welshman, I am sure that all hon. Members across these Benches share my feeling that we must do our best to see that our people become conscious of these movements in economics and history which can so gravely affect their future.

Let me say a word now about the heritage of the past and what we try to do about it. Because of our dependence upon a couple of heavy industries we have in the past spent much time saying that we must diversify within Wales. But at present it appears that all we have done is diversify in order to close industries once we have 58 diversified. It seems to have been purely an academic exercise. Far too many of the incoming industries, at the first breath of contrary wind, run home to their mothers—the parent companies—or fold up altogether. In my own constituency this has happened time and again. There is no enduring quality about these enterprises.

It is hoped that the Welsh Development Agency may in time see that the pattern is changed, but in the meantime we need in Wales facilities for training people to a high level in the arts of industrial management and industrial administration so that we have our own key workers. The hon. Member for Caernarvon (Mr. Wigley) referred to the refusal of a key worker from Poland and the folding up of an industry in his constituency because the key worker was not provided.

There is a massive resource of talent and intellectual power among Welsh people to provide all the management skills and all the administrative skills that are needed if we have the proper approach to the question. It is not an insoluble problem by any means. It is comparatively easy if we have the will to do so.

The last point that I make—there are many others I should like to deal with on this problem, which is such a trying one for all our constituencies—relates to the speech of my hon. Friend the Member for Aberdare when he said that the only way in which we shall reach any solution to this problem is to change the economy. One does not change an economy by sitting down and saying "I wish things would change". I have just come from the third meeting I have had with the Triang toy people in Merthyr, and only a week ago at a conference here in this building of the Heads of the Valleys standing conference we had them there, too, giving their story. It is a story of tragic misconception, of bureaucratic decisions which have thrown money away, and will throw it away again, as was rightly pointed out by the hon. Member for Caernarvon, because no study has been made right across the board of the impact of closures.

For some economists it is enough to say that something is not profit making and that is the end of the story. But when we take into account the total cost of redundancy payments, where they are applicable, the cost of social benefits and unemployment benefits, the further costs—these are only money costs I am talking about— of support for children in a family and the JPEG 59 loss in service industries, right down to the buses which take workmen to work—when we add all that up, in nearly every case that I have looked at the total of what is being lost is astronomically greater than the alleged amount saved by a closure.

On top of that, of course, one has the deep human suffering, the feeling of inadequacy, particularly the feeling that one is no longer a contributing and constructive member of society. That feeling comes from many years of conditioning to what may be the wrong attitude to work. I do not know. I do not know whether work is natural to man or whether it is part of the curse that was laid upon him when he was thrown out of the Garden of Eden "by the sweat of thy brow shalt thou labour"— and I wonder whether that is entirely a correct concept. Perhaps it is something we can alter. I rather think that we can.

We need to alter the economy—yes, certainly—but we have to break the problem down to terms of practicality, putting the emphasis where they should be. The emphasis in Wales, surely, must be first of all on anything and everything that will regenerate the life of Wales.

In addition, we must not be too squeamish, about getting rid of shibboleths of established patterns of economic behaviour We must develop a degree of ruthlessness whereby we say that, if something is standing in the way of the wellbeing of the people it is the wellbeing of the people that must triumph. That is the only way in which we shall generate the Wales we all want to see.

However, in discussing the economy of 60 Wales and the problems of our constituents, we are discussing what really concerns and worries them. I do not know whether members of the Committee have noticed, but this last year has been the peak of the economic cycle. If that is so we may now, on historical trends, look forward to a downturn, despite what the National Institute has had to say. Its track record has not been notably good if I remember rightly, at the turn of the year it was forecasting a current account surplus of £2,000 million. It seems to have fallen flat on its face in under a year on that prediction, so one has to lay off on its most recent prediction.

Clearly, we still have the problem of unemployment. Industrial production in Wales has still not got back to what it was in 1973. We have the lowest housing starts figure for 20 years in the first six months of this year and the rising interest rates can only push that figure down further. In my constituency in the city of Cardiff, we have an extremely serious situation, particularly following on the closure of the East Moors steelworks, so that we now have a most disproportionately-loaded service sector and a very low manufacturing sector. About 17 per cent, only of the jobs in the Cardiff area are now in manufacturing, and this means that youngsters leaving school who are not prepared, able or willing to work at desk jobs are finding it extremely difficult to obtain jobs suited to their hoped-for skills.

Another problem in Wales, of course, is the lack of skills which we have, particularly in what one might call intermediate work. This was referred to in the annual report of the Manpower Services Commission, which said:

"Despite the existing high level of unemployment, a problem of 'mismatch' already appears to exist in parts of Wales where some employers are reported to have difficulty in recruiting skilled labour, particularly certain engineering craftsmen.


"Particular attention will be paid to those labour shortages which threaten to inhibit the implementation of the Government's industrial strategy and identify the action to be taken by the employment and training services to alleviate the shortages".

That is putting it fairly mildly. We are desparately short of suitable skills, and I JPEG 61 hope that the Under-Secretary of State will be able to throw more light on the effort being made to repair that difficulty.

I hope that the hon. Gentleman will also find time to welcome the proposed scheme of the South Glamorgan education committee for the £4 million MSC-funded training scheme, which, it is hoped will process—if that is not too nasty a word—1,000 youngsters a year in trying to give them a taste of work and certain skills, so that when the economy begins to pick up and jobs become available they will be useful material.

The closure of East Moors has drained the economy of South Glamorgan. It was taken over £30 million out of the local economy, it has damaged the rate structure of the city, and it has damaged the docks. All that was foreseeable. What was not perhaps forseen by many people was the speed of the closure. Those in particular who did not see it were those who had taken early redundancy from East Moors with the lower redundancy pay. They now find themselves competing on the market with those who hung on until the works were finally closed and got very much bigger compensation. Those who went easily on have very much less in their pockets and bank balances, and I believe that they have been rather shabbily treated by the BSC.

I wondered whether in the advance factory figures which the Secretary of State gave this morning he included the possible letting of the 25,000 square feet of advance factory at Pentwyn in Cardiff, which I understand will probably be back on the market on Monday, the hoped-for letting having, apparently fallen through.

Again, what is happening to the proposed or possible plant of Mitsubishi at Wentloog in Cardiff? This plant, which would bring 700 jobs in precisely the sectors which we need in Cardiff, seems to be somewhere in the air. As I understand it, the Department of Industry has sent a team to Japan recently, and we would like to know the result. At the moment, I must say—although I cannot believe it to be true—the hon. Member for Nelson and Colne (Mr. Hoyle) seems to have had rather more effect in deterring investment in Cardiff than the Prime Minister has in bringing it to his own constituency.

Precisely what is happening to the Hoover investment at Merthyr? I am for from clear about what precisely is happening there, because that is a plant success 62 story and something into which all of us have been putting a lot of hope for the future.

But surely the real problem that we have been facing in Wales and Britain as a whole has been our appalling record in productivity or, in the jargon of the times, in value added for our investment. We have not suffered from low investment but because the return on the investment which we have has been appallingly low in industry after industry. That is the threat to our future and to our jobs, and not the so-called microcircuitry revolution.

I was amazed that the hon. Member for Aberdare (Mr. Evans) seems to think that to deal with the new technology one needs a fully Socialist society. It seems to me that the revolution has started up very largely in California, and that is not exactly an area which is noted for Socialism or Socialist planning. It is, indeed, one of the most prosperous and booming areas of the world, and the revolution is being taken up at high speed by those countries of Asia which operate on a capitalist free enterprise model. It is their growth rates which perhaps are threatening us and which produce the value added on their investment which we need badly to follow.

I aslo find it rather extraordinary that the hon. Member for Swansea, East (Mr. Anderson) and also the hon. Member for Carmarthen (Mr. Evans) in his way, in a different sort of speech, did not refer to the possible effects on employment in their constituencies of the measures taken by the Government against Ford. After all, if the various sanctions are to mean anything at all—we have been told that they are meant to bite and Ford tells us that they are likely to bite—presumably these will be some sort of an effect on employment in Swansea and, in turn, it may have an effect on Bernard Wardle in Caernarvon as one of Ford's suppliers.

I should like to know from the Minister what figures the Government have on the likely effect of employment in Wales as a result of the sanctions. If the Minister does not know what those figures will be, it is a disgrace that the Government could have taken these steps without understanding the results of their action.

We cannot in any way in Wales cut ourselves off from the economy in Britain. We cannot cut ourselves off—as Ministers often tell us—from the economy of the world at large. Of course, we have been living through a period of slump in the JPEG 63 industrial world—and, indeed, the non-industrial world, for it is our slump that has damaged it so severely. Yet we are given as an excuse that we have merely been swimming with the tide. But there are two considerations on this.

What has happened to inflation in this country? I remember the electorate being told in 1974 how prices had risen when we had a Conservative Government. We have now the various excuses why prices have risen under a Labour Government. The argument always seems to be rather different. But in our period in office we had a most enormous expansion—and explosion, really—in commodity prices, which rose by around 158 per cent, during the last period of Conservative Government, and prices rose by 40 per cent. Since this Government came to power, the index of commodity prices has risen by 4 per cent., yet prices have risen by 97 per cent. That is the measure of our competitiveness abroad which has so damaged us and damaged our pound and taken away jobs from Wales and all other parts of the United Kingdom.

The particular damage that the Government will always do is to misunderstand the springs of endeavour and desire of the ordinary man and woman in the street, whether they are working for themselves, or working for a small company or a large company or whether they are savers and, therefore, investors in a building society, a company or in their own house. The Government do not seem to understand that to give encouragement, reward or incentive is the only way to move forward; without that one can never get anywhere. By their tax burdens, by their centralisation of decisions, by their belief in a Socialist economy, the Goverment have damaged the Welsh people and are continuing to do so.

Several hon. Members mentioned training. Most of us realise the important contribution that training and retraining measures make towards alleviating the problems of unemployment.
