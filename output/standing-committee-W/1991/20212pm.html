4.12 pm

Question again proposed.

Mr. Keith Raffan
(Delyn)
: When we broke for lunch I was discussing the support in my consitutency among heads and teachers for the Government's reforms. I mentioned the head of a local primary school who said, in reference to LMS, "Now that I am director of education for this community, I have control and responsibility for my school's budget. I can allocate it according to the needs of my school." That view has been welcomed widely.

There are worries that the pace of reform has been carried out too hastily, with too little consultation and with inadequate consideration of the administrative burden that will fall on headmasters, especially in smaller schools in which headmasters have to teach.

In view of the Government's achievements there is a need for consolidation and consensus. Two years ago the mere utterance of those words by Conservative Members would have made the Front Bench shout, or perhaps screech, "Send him to the tower. Off with his head!;" That is what happened to my right hon. Friend the Member for Shropshire, North (Mr. Biffen) who lost his place in the Cabinet because he dared to utter the word "consolidation". I have noticed that the words "consolidation" and "consensus" are creeping back into the parliamentary lexicon, and they are occasionally uttered by the Government Front Bench. I am glad to see that even my right hon. and learned Friend the Secretary of State for Education and Science has dared to use them. Within the next few months, they may become fashionable and—who knows?—the Under-Secretary of State for Wales may use them. I am glad that he is in Committee to hear my remarks. Like other hon. Members, I have visited schools. I shall go quickly through my impressions. I agree with the hon. Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney (Mr. Rowlands) that the national curriculum is too detailed and restrictive. Even my right hon. Friend the Member for Finchley (Mrs. Thatcher) realised when she saw the huge piles of documents on different subjects that the national curriculum had taken a turn that she did not expect and that it had become too detailed. That was also the view of my right hon. Friend the Leader of the House when he held the education portfolio. There was a feeling that the 42 national curriculum had to out of hand and that there was too much detail for teachers to absorb.

There is a shortage of textbooks in various subjects, especially in Welsh. The hon. Member for Gower (Mr. Wardell) mentioned the stock in a school library; I agree with him. When I visit a school one of the first things that I ask about is the library stock. When a school has to hold back its spending, one of the first things to suffer is its library stock.

There is a problem with the number of Welsh language teachers, which my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State discussed. I shall not repeat that point. However, despite the Government's initiatives and despite the encouragement in teacher training colleges for those training to do so in Welsh, there is a shortage of teachers using the Welsh language.

I am also worried about funding. Is the funding that is available through education support grants and local education authority training grant schemes adequate? There was £7 million available for 1989–90, £10 million for 1990–91 and £14.5 million for 1991–92. I do not want to detract from the fact that the Government have increased spending but, given the extent and complexity of the problem, I wonder whether that was enough.

I am worried about standard assessment tasks, given their first year of application, and I am particularly worried about their impact on seven-year-olds. I have two reports from head teachers in my constituency, whose schools I visited. I asked them to let me know their teachers' views on standard assessment tasks. I understand that the School Examinations and Assessment Council is reviewing them.

Pilot schemes led to the Government slimming down materials and assessment arrangements, but I am not sure that enough slimming down was done. There is still a feeling that they take too long and are too stressful on pupils. There is also difficulty obtaining books for SATs.

One report on SATs from head teacher W. G. Hughes concluded:

"Considerable time spent on preliminary work and during SAT period. All done in one's own time.


Too heavy a work load on top of usual work connected with preparation of lessons.


SATs impractical in its present form. Too time consuming and not fair to rest of class. With all the will in the world impossible to concentrate on all the children in the class while the SATs were in progress.


No provision has been made to indicate a child's level when he or she commenced full-rime education, and the subsequent achievement that has taken place during the first two years of schooling.


The assessments are unfair because they do nor take into account the rate of development of children. Some children at the age of 7 might appear as low achievers or even failures and these same children could within a few months reach a far higher attainment level.

There are also comments on the good aspects of SATs:

"All practical work was well enjoyed. The Story Discussion was popular. Working on a Theme was a good plan."

The report attempted to give a balanced assessment, but the question is one of the time involved and the stress put on children, as well as the use of the word "test".

JPEG 43 I recall my 11-plus examination as a horrid experience because I was made to sit a test. The teachers' guidance handbook uses the word "activities", but all children know that they are being tested. A problem exists here. We want to move more towards an assessment and less towards the idea of an obstacle that children see ahead. We want less defining of seven-year-olds into categories, especially as they might move out of them rapidly at that age.

I am also anxious about the publication of league tables. I am aware that that is to enable parents to compare the performances of their local schools, and that is good. The Government have said, in response to points that I have raised, that they have enabled parents to make the comparison while taking account of circumstances that are unique to the school and any other factors affecting it, but how can that be done? The hon. Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney also asked that.

I give as an example a village school in my constituency. It is in a deprived area. Some 75 per cent of its pupils take school meals because their parents are receiving supplementary benefit, so the meals are free. I have visited the school and I know the teachers. Nowhere in the principality could one find a more committed bunch of people, but they are worried about the publication of the results. They feel that publication is an unfair measure of the work that they do because they are actually raising educational standards from a very low base. That in itself is an achievement, but it will not be measured by SATs.

What worries me is the potentially detrimental effect of pupil-led funding on the ability of schools to pay experienced, stable, long-serving staff.

I cite another example from my constituency. There is a small school with a low turnover of staff who have remained for many years, at the highest pay level. That is an admirable statement and we must work towards its fulfilment—not just at election time. I welcome Monday's announcement that the review body report will be met in full and will not be scaled back or phased in. I am sure that that has nothing to do with Easter.

"to help cover the cost of the award."

I have many differences with my county council regarding spending—I JPEG 45 shall not mention the castle—but it cannot have that thrust on it at this time, particularly when it has a backlog of school building maintenance. The award is essential to raise morale, retrain existing staff and increase recruitment. Teachers' pay must be increased steadily to attract high-quality recruits. We shall not be able to compete with Germany or Japan or anywhere else—I shall not mention the United States in view of Japan's comments on it—unless pupils have the best possible education and that means the highest quality of teachers. We must work hard towards that.

I agree with the review body when it says that the top priority is properly designed performance-related pay arrangements. We need targeted incentives for experienced teachers for scarce subjects—the report lists subjects for which it is difficult to find teachers—and for hard-to-staff schools. Kids must not suffer because they come from a deprived community and go to school in the middle of a deprived area of a town or of a rural area. The Government must provide incentives to counterbalance the difficulties such schools may have in finding staff.

I welcome the increased number of applications for teacher training, but I am worried about turnover: 5 per cent a year leave the profession and among new entrants the proportion is as high as one third within five years, although half of them return. We must consider job sharing, child care schemes and part-time working. I am worried about the low number of applicants for each vacancy. About a third of vacancies have only three or four applicants. In the south-east of England two thirds of vacancies have only three to four applicants. That is very bad. I should like to know what the ratio is for Wales.

I have mentioned the inspectorate and I do not want to repeat my remarks. We have only 58 inspectors who spend 65 per cent of their time going round schools and 35 per cent of their time advising. I am worried that we will lose that expertise through a registration or licence scheme. We must look at the Scottish scheme. If they can do something different from England, why cannot we?

Mr. Raffan
: I hope so. I am sure that all the county councils and LEAs will be very happy. My right hon. Friend the Minister of State wrote to me not long ago saying:

It is too demanding. Too much has been done too quickly. The pace has been too fast. My right hon. and 46 learned Friend the Secretary of State for Education and Science says that his aim now is to take forward the reform programme and maintain the momentum. We must look at consolidation and building on what we have achieved through consensus. I am worried about his further initiative, particularly in training and his plans to move training from colleges more to schools. Schools are having a hard enough job absorbing and digesting the national curriculum, SATs and LMS. He is now taking an initiative in primary education as a result of SATs. I do not disagree with that, but the inquiry was announced in December and only two months were allowed to carry it out. How can one consult in that period? How can a report, which is to be achieved by consensus and is to be as influential as Plowden was, be produced in such a short time?

I am glad that my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State is taking a different course here. There will be wider consultation on primary teaching methods in Wales and evidence will be taken from the profession in moving to a more gradual, consensual approach. That is the way to go. According to that extremely left-wing newspaper, the Financial Times,

"the priority now is to stop making endless policy on the hoof."

Wales's education provision needs to be examined fully, calmly and deliberately taking evidence from all in the field, even abroad and yes, including Scotland. We must harness and not ignore the professionalism of teachers without whose co-operation no education reforms can succeed. The Government cannot dictate teaching methods. The only way they can move forward is with the co-operation, enthusiasm and whole-hearted support of the teaching profession. At present their morale is distressingly low. We need a consensual approach to replace conflict and dissension.

The Government's responsibility is to safeguard basic education standards and opportunities. Their role is one of oversight and monitoring. They must let the reforms settle down. They must stop turning schools upside down. I quote from one of my heroes in the Conservative party, the late Sir Edward Boyle, who was and Education Minister and Vice-Chancellor of Leeds University. He once said that we do not believe in equality but—and this is where I part company so strongly with the hon. Member for Alyn and Deeside (Mr. Jones)—we do believe in equality of opportunity. All our policies and all our efforts must be geared towards that.
