Comprehensive Spending Review

10.30 am

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Paul Murphy)
: This spending review is good news for all the people of our country. There has been a sustained and sustainable improvement in public finances, which allows sustained and sustainable improvement in our public services. The Government have delivered that improvement by creating stability and sustainable public finances, and establishing clear fiscal rules over the economic cycle. Our current budget is in balance, and we are borrowing only to invest within prudent and cautious limits. We have low inflation, stable growth and year-on-year current budget surpluses. The national debt is being repaid this year and next year, and extra resources are available because of—not at the expense of—our prudence. Moreover, with the help of the new deal, unemployment is now at the lowest level for 20 years. Almost 28 million people are in work in the United Kingdom—more than ever before.

Now that we have the firm foundation of stability and a strengthened economy, we can move to the next stage in the creation of a better future. Prudent, targeted long-term investment is a social good, but in a changing and often insecure world, it is also an economic necessity. The Government have stuck to the spending envelope that was firmly established in this year's Budget. Total spending in the UK will grow by 3.25 per cent. a year in real terms. Savings on debt interest and social security have enabled the Government to free resources for priority public services. More than 75 per cent. of all increases announced in the spending review were for health, education, transport, housing, and law and order. The review's main objectives are to extend opportunity, promote responsible and secure communities, raise productivity, sustain growth, and provide a modern international role for Britain.

The spending review will not only provide cash for the Assembly but have a big impact on the quality of people's lives in Wales. In the main, the police and fire services in Wales are funded by the Home Office. By 2003–04, overall Home Office spending will be £10.6 billion—an increase of £2.4 billion on this year's plans—and in the House today, my right hon. Friend the Home Secretary will set out his targets and how that allocation will be spent. The annual police budget will rise by £1.6 billion. Some of that money will be used to boost the spending power of police forces in Wales, which will have a real impact on local communities. On the subject of policing, I welcome yesterday's figures, which show an overall reduction in crime rates across Wales as a whole.

2 There is also a good settlement for the fire service in England and Wales, which will receive an average increase of 4.5 per cent. The criminal justice system will get more money, which will fund a programme to modernise the courts system in England and Wales. Courts will be able to improve their service and facilities, and help to ensure that cases progress more efficiently. The Department of Trade and Industry will receive a significant increase in resources to enhance Britain's world-class science base, and there will be extra funding for science and key research areas.

Universities' potential as engines of growth in the knowledge economy will be expanded through extra funding to commercialise research and engage with business. There will be increasing focus on building a more competitive environment, with better regulation, modern company law and effective enforcement of competition policy. All those initiatives will affect the business landscape in Wales, and—combined with the boost provided by objective 1, which I shall discuss in a moment—present a real opportunity for genuine change in the Welsh economy.

The Government are improving the delivery of social security services to the public, so that staff and claimants of working age can focus on finding work, not on processing claims. The service for pensioners will be overhauled, so that they can make their claims over the telephone, and the legacy of fraud and error will be tackled to release further funds to where they are really needed.

The new deal will become a permanent deal. The ONE service will be extended, so that benefit recipients receive a more integrated service and claimants benefit from a personal adviser service to help them into work—which I have seen in my constituency. The new deal for over-25s will be enhanced to increase rights and responsibilities.

Of course, the real news is the outstanding settlement for Wales that my right hon. Friend the Chancellor of the Exchequer announced yesterday. In 1999–2000, the Welsh departmental expenditure limit was just over £7 billion. By 2003–04, it will rise massively to almost £10 billion. The spending review has produced an excellent outcome for us. It represents a 5.4 per cent. above inflation average annual increase in provision—the biggest ever increase in Welsh public spending. It will give an immense boost to public services in Wales and will have a real impact on the quality of life for all the people whom we represent.

Overall, the settlement will increase the size of the Welsh departmental expenditure limit by around £2 billion by 2003–04. Most changes in the Welsh budget have been linked to changes in United Kingdom departmental spending plans by the Barnett formula. That gives Wales a population-based share of changes in comparable spending in England.

An extra £1.2 billion over the next three years was announced for Wales in the Budget earlier this year—£153 million for 2001–02, £394 million for 2002–03 and £653 million for 2003–04. That increase was included in the figures that were announced yesterday, but I am pleased to confirm that the amount that Wales will receive over that time has, as a consequence of JPEG 3 increases in health spending in England, risen to £163 million for 2001–02, £458 million for 2002–03, and £728 million for 2003–04. Those figures are over and above the increases that my right hon. Friend the Chancellor announced previously. That real increase of an extra £149 million over the next three years will come to Wales because of the extra spending on health in England that was announced yesterday.

Wales will get £78 million in 2001–02, £183 million in 2002–03 and £290 million in 2003–04 as a result of extra spending in England on education. That excludes the funding that we will receive because of increases in education funding for local government.

Roads and public transport will receive significant increases as a result of the spending review, and Wales will consequently get an extra £40 million in 2001–02, £94 million in 2002–03 and £163 million in 2003–04.

The significant increases in spending in England will feed into Wales through the Barnett formula, and will help to build up the massive increase in public funding available in Wales that was announced yesterday.

As I have explained, the Assembly has the freedom to make its own spending decisions about the use of the extra resources in response to Welsh priorities, but the settlement will make a huge difference to the services that it provides. It will give the Assembly, in partnership with the United Kingdom Government, the resources to deliver better services and increase prosperity for all the people of Wales.

The total increases over next year's planned baseline are £480 million in 2001–02, £1.173 billion in 2002–03 and £1.821 billion in 2003–04. Those resources will be invested in modern public services for Wales.

Best of all, the settlement for Wales includes cover, outside Barnett, for the structural funds programmes in Wales. The Government have made good their commitment not to let Wales down over objective 1—£80 million pounds will be made available in 2001–02, £90 million in 2002–03 and £102 million in 2003–04. In addition to that, the European social fund money is being transferred to the Assembly from the Department for Education and Employment. That will give £38 million next year, £58 million in 2002–03 and £58 million in 2003–04.

Altogether, that means that additional funding of £113 million in 2001–02, £148 million in 2002–03 and £160 million in 2003–04 has been made available for the structural funds programmes in Wales. That means that the full forecast increase in structural funds receipts in Wales is covered. This is the first time ever that a settlement has gone beyond Barnett for structural funds. It is unprecedented. The money is completely extra, above and beyond the block grant. It is Barnett-plus.

4 This is a very generous settlement that recognises the special needs of Wales, with objective 1 covering around 65 per cent. of the population of Wales. Our valleys, one of which I represent in the House of Commons, west Wales and large parts of north Wales will all benefit from this funding. The settlement unlocks objective 1 funding to be used exclusively in Wales, for the first time in financial history.

I have talked to my hon. Friend the Member for Cardiff, West (Mr. Morgan) and Edwina Hart, who agree that as a result of this settlement no worthwhile objective 1 project will be held back by a lack of resources. The Government have cleared the way for objective 1 to be a success in Wales. It is now time to move forward and to concentrate on the important business of ensuring that this objective 1 structural funding is a success. We need to encourage good projects, to make the most of this once-in-a-lifetime opportunity. We can do that only by working together in partnership between the United Kingdom Government, the Assembly, and local authorities and communities in Wales. We have been given this opportunity and we must not squander it. We owe that to the people of Wales who send us to the House of Commons.

Taken together, this is a first-rate settlement for Wales. It is possible because the Government have managed the economy sensibly and prudently. Now we can reap the benefit of that and invest in the future of Wales.

Mr. Martin Caton

: I feel lucky and privileged to have secured the first Adjournment debate at a Welsh Grand Committee. However, I am sorry to say that the focus of the debate is a community that has been neither lucky nor privileged in recent years. The subject of the debate is economic regeneration in the Swansea valley, but the subject that I applied to speak to was regenerating Clydach. I intend to speak widely and to cover regeneration other than just economic regeneration, but my main focus will be the village of Clydach.

For more than a year now, whenever I have spoken about Clydach to people from outside the Swansea area, they have immediately responded with the comment, "You mean the murder village." That is not surprising and I do not blame people for being reminded of the major news story of the tragedy that put Clydach in the limelight. Whenever I see the name Dunblane or Hungerford, the tragedies there come to mind. Every right-minded person must be sickened at the monstrous crime that ended the lives of a grandmother, a mother and two small children. We are all dismayed that the crime remains unsolved more than a year after it happened. The recent arrests and releases have, inevitably, renewed media interest and, just as inevitably, increased the anxiety, fear and anger felt by many people in Clydach.

My first purpose this morning is to say that it does no good to allow that terrible tragedy to define the community of Clydach. It does a particular disservice to the victims of those horrendous murders who were part of the village and knew it as the decent, caring and responsible community that it really is and it is not helpful to the surviving family and friends who remain part of the community or who live in nearby villages and still look to Clydach as their local centre. Clydach is not a murder village. It is a fine community where an appalling crime was committed for which justice remains to be done. It is even more than that. It is part of my constituency that has taken some hard knocks in recent years and where some real problems have developed, to which I shall refer. It is also a community that has recently decided not to wait for someone to do something about those knocks, but to solve its problems. It has decided to set its own agenda for the future, and that is what I want to talk about.

Clydach lies about six miles north of Swansea city centre and has a population of about 7,500. In many ways, I suspect that its history since the early 1970s is fairly typical of similar villages and small towns in the valleys of south Wales. Until the mid-1970s, it was a thriving employment and retail centre, but as the heavy, labour-intensive industries were run down—a process that accelerated markedly after 1979—Clydach, as an economic centre and, in some ways, as a cohesive community, began to decline. Even for those who found alternative employment, the link between work and community was broken. For instance, Inco, traditionally the main employer in Clydach, whose JPEG 23 works still dominate the village, employed more than 1,500 people in the early 1970s. Now about 300 people are on the payroll. It is still a vital force in the local economy but it is no longer central to the lives of every family in Clydach.

The retail core of Clydach, the high street, has contracted, and in the main provides just a local service centre. The word "just" is not meant dismissively. The role fulfilled is important, especially because Clydach has a high proportion of elderly residents and other people with mobility or transport problems. Sadly, the local retail centre is still under threat. Businesses in the high street struggle to survive.

Parts of the village have become popular residential locations because of Clydach's closeness to the M4, Swansea and other employment opportunities. As a result, Clydach-wide statistics on economic and social factors disguise some significant and serious problems below the surface. In particular, Graigfelen ward scores very badly with respect to economic activity, income levels, sickness, mortality, proportion of single-parent households and population loss in the 20 to 59 age group. The level of car ownership per household is very low. It is one of the three most deprived wards in the City and County of Swansea. Some of its problems are mirrored elsewhere in Clydach—most notably in the Penydre area towards the other end of the village.

A couple of years back, the local hospital was mothballed. A few months ago, Lloyds TSB pulled out. A couple of weeks ago, it was announced that a family cafe that had been going for 70 years was closing down. What I am talking about in Clydach is not massive decline or the lack of any prospect of future growth; it is not the breakdown of community values or social responsibility. Rather, I am talking about the slow run-down of the village, accompanied by a debilitating loss of hope or expectation; a depressing feeling that continued decline is inevitable.

In the three years that I have been a Member of Parliament, the monthly surgeries that I have held in Clydach have worried me more than any that I have held in any part of Gower, not because of the nature of the problems, but because so often they have been accompanied by an expectation that they cannot not be solved. That is partly because of a feeling that Clydach had been more or less written off by Government at all levels. I do not think that that is true or fair, but I understand how cynicism has grown over the years.

The attitude was reinforced in the mid-1990s when the then responsible local authorities, West Glamorgan county council and Lliw Valley borough council, commissioned consultants Chapman Warren to draw up a regeneration strategy for Clydach. That was done, and a final report was produced, in October 1995, which was adopted in principle by the two councils. It followed a model set in other Lliw valley communities, where physical regeneration strategies were carried through, particularly in Pontardawe and Pontarddulais.

24 With local government reorganisation, the new City and County of Swansea undertook what was described as a public-participation exercise, including a three-day exhibition in Clydach library, articles in the local press and special meetings with the community council and Clydach chamber of trade. Members of the community read and heard fine descriptions of what might happen. They saw wonderful artists' impressions of what could happen to their village as the regeneration strategy was carried out. Unfortunately, nothing came of the exercise, apart from some improvements to a car park outside the community centre. Disappointment and disillusionment were further reinforced. To be fair, the City and County of Swansea used the Chapman Warren report as the basis of bids for challenge funding and strategic development scheme funding, but was unsuccessful.

Although the Chapman Warren approach identified correctly some of the needs, especially for the physical regeneration and improvement of the environment in Clydach, it was a top-down process with a bit of consultation tacked on. It got the general key aims right: to arrest the further decline of the town's retail core; to create a retail environment that is attractive, safe and convenient; to create opportunities for new business set-ups; to create recreational links in the study area and the strategic or evolving regional network; to remove derelict property; to improve environmental quality; to improve community support facilities at Graigfelen; and to foster partnerships between public and private sectors.

However, the detailed proposals did not reflect the community's priorities. At the end of last year, Clydach tried to do things the other way round—although, at first, not completely the other way round. A bunch of politicians—those on the community council, the county council, Members of the National Assembly and myself as Member of Parliament—who represent Clydach at all levels, from different political parties and no party, were called together by the village dentist, who is also a community councillor, to set the ball rolling just before Christmas. That meeting began a series of meetings with other individuals and organisations that might have a part to play in creating a better and more secure future for Clydach.

We agreed that we did not want to resurrect the Chapman Warren strategy or follow a similar proposal which would result in another set of bids to make the place look a bit better. We wanted to step back and say, "Do we really know what the people of Clydach want? How do they see their community developing? What are their priorities? What are their needs?" We tried to find answers to those questions while stimulating interest in local people and getting them directly involved in making that regeneration happen.

We went to see the management of Inco—or the Mond as it is still known in the area. It was understandably cautious but, after deliberation, made a commitment to financial support to the tune of several thousand pounds and to consider providing other resources in the future. We went to see the JPEG 25 chamber of trade. Some of its members were, understandably, bitter. The improvements to the high street that they expected years ago had never happened and they did not want to invest more time and effort in another doomed or futile project. However, they were persuaded to come on board and send a representative to our meetings.

We organised a meeting of voluntary sector organisations, including sports clubs, Churches, disabled groups, the civic society, school parent-teacher associations and anyone else who came to mind. We consulted young people from the secondary schools that serve Clydach. Although some people identified them as part of the problem, we knew that they were a key part of the solution. They told us what was wrong from their perspective and what would make their lives fuller and more enjoyable as youngsters and their futures in Clydach more interesting, exciting and rewarding. What emerged from the interchanges was the realisation that Clydach was still a community. Its members cared about its future and, if they believed that change could happen, they were prepared to help to achieve that.

At the same time as we were fostering that involvement, we were investigating what structure we needed to advance the objectives that we were starting to identify. We decided on the community development trust model for several reasons. First, CDTs have a proven track record in Wales and other parts of the United Kingdom. Secondly, in our discussions, we had come to realise that, to achieve and sustain regeneration in Clydach, we needed to adopt a comprehensive way forward that brought together economic regeneration, social development and environmental improvement. We discovered that dealing with those components separately was less effective than a more joined-up and holistic approach.

Thirdly, we chose a CDT because we wanted to move quickly to identify projects and put in bids to funders to make them happen. Development trusts are familiar to such bodies, straightforward to deal with and transparent in structure. Fourthly, development trusts are about doing things—delivering results—rather than talking in endless committees about the possibility of making things happen. They are also flexible beasts and, frankly, we wanted to create something that would fit in with the objective 1 set-up that is being established by the Welsh Assembly. In particular, we wanted to adopt the partnership model advocated by the Assembly and the European Union—the tripartite approach involving political representatives, the business community and the voluntary sector.

We have adopted precisely that model for the board of the trust. We hope that Clydach will be well placed in that way to participate in and to benefit from the west Wales and valleys programme in the next few years. It is exactly the sort of community that needs objective 1 support and, we suggest, the new community trust is just the sort of vehicle to act as a local partnership to achieve the economic, social and environmental objectives.

26 Clydach community trust is a three-way partnership. However, to regenerate Clydach as we want to, we will need help from other organisations and agencies working at various levels. The partnership approach must be stretched beyond the boundaries of Clydach. Members of the board and others involved with the development trust have moved quickly to establish links with players that might be able to help. We have had meetings with the Welsh Development Agency, West Wales training and enterprise council, Iechyd Morgannwg health authority and various local authority departments in the City and County of Swansea, as well as regular meetings with its leader and chief executive.

We have also opened discussions with Lloyds TSB bank, from which we are trying to get some conscience money or resource. It has moved out of the high street in Clydach and we are asking it to give us the premises as a base from which the community development trust can work. We have not yet had a response from Lloyds TSB bank, but we are keeping the pressure on. The other agencies with which we have opened negotiations have been positive and keen to play a partnership role as we move forward.

That point brings me to the most important thing about the community development trust: its practical plans for improving the village and the quality of life of the people who live there. Frankly, I thought that that task was going to be the difficult one. The more organisations and individuals became involved, the more varied the sets of ideas on the way forward would emerge. I foresaw a difficult stage at which we would have to prioritise, and that might have put noses out of joint, thereby losing the enthusiasm or even the support of parts of the community. In fact, so far, that has not really happened, although it might do when we are a little further down the road.

Remarkably, there is an amazing consensus in Clydach about the things that should be tackled first. Virtually everyone wants the high street—the little retail centre of Clydach—to be improved, believing that that would be the best early symbol of the regeneration of the village as a whole. Everyone wants to see something done about the disused swimming pool and park area beside the canal at Forge Fach in the village. Everyone wants the old Globe cinema—a large and unsightly asbestos-clad shed—pulled down, with something useful put in its place. Everyone recognises that there is very little for teenagers to do in Clydach. Everyone would like a range of training opportunities to be available in the village.

Tonight, the Clydach community trust board is meeting. I am pretty sure that it will establish three sub-groups to work with the wider community to develop three detailed projects. One will be the improvement of the physical environment of the high street. That will need investment by the council and the traders. Hopefully, a commercial improvement area will be created to help retailers to improve the appearance of their shops at more or less the same time as pavement improvements, tree planting and other street-enhancement work.

JPEG 27 Another group will tackle the redundant swimming pool site. It will draw up proposals for a community leisure centre, which could be a tremendous resource for all age groups in Clydach. We believe that the community council will be prepared to contribute the land and the cost of demolition of the old swimming pool, but we will be looking to outside funders to help with investment in the new centre. A meeting with Sportslot has been arranged. A third group will work on the old Globe cinema site. Its redevelopment will include a resource centre for the community, where training schemes and other facilities can be housed.

The projects are all ambitious but they are demonstrably what Clydach wants and needs. I firmly believe that the community of Clydach can make them happen and that they will be just the start. Far from being defined as the murder village, it might already be offering a model for regeneration to small communities throughout Wales. The model requires local politicians of different political parties to submerge their differences whenever possible and to seek common objectives. Most challengingly for us as politicians, it requires us not to claim more than our fair share of responsibility for the achievements of or in the community.

The model requires the community's ownership of community development. Other partners are welcome, but the community must be in the driving seat. The basic impetus must be bottom up. The model is built on partnership at every level and between levels and on inclusion and involvement. I know that these are early days, that it could still all go pear-shaped and that nothing might come from a lot of hard work, but I do not think that that will happen. I honestly believe that Clydach is turning round. The community development trust is one sign of that and I hope that it will be the vehicle for all sorts of initiatives in years to come.

Other things are also happening. Today—it is all happening today as far as Clydach is concerned—the area health authority is meeting to discuss options for reopening Clydach hospital. Its preferred option is a multi-use facility, including a base for the community mental health team and voluntary organisations, such as Crossroads, that service the area. The project also includes an out-patients facility and health-centre services, and will be welcomed by the community. The problem is, however, that about £1 million of capital investment is needed to bring the building up to scratch. Of course, the decision will be for the National Assembly, but the project has much better prospects after the Chancellor's announcement yesterday and the statement made by my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State this morning.

Graigfelen, the poorest part of the village, is now the base for a sure start programme as part of the battle against child poverty. Of course, the national minimum wage, the working families tax credit and other Government measures are helping that worst-off part of Clydach, as well as other deprived communities in the area. The beautiful Royal Society for the 28 Protection of Birds reserve at Cwm Clydach has just received major investment to create accessible pathways for elderly and disabled people, and other organisations have proposed improvements to the Clydach environment. Let us have no more Clydach, the murder village—rather, Clydach, the village of imagination, determination and achievement.

As I conclude my eulogy to Clydach, I am conscious that I have not pressed my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary for any specific action or actions. I invite him to visit Clydach, to meet the trust board and others involved in making the village an even finer community and to see for himself why I want Clydach to be recognised for what it really is and for what it can achieve. My only other message is to urge Government at all levels to embrace local initiatives such as the one in Clydach, which I hope my hon. Friend will come to see, to provide the support and partnership that they need and to ensure that the direction is always set by the community itself.
