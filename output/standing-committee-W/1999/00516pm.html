Mr. Richard Livsey (Brecon and Radnorshire): I intend to make a short speech because I believe that many other hon. Members wish to speak before my hon. The contribution made by the hon. Member for Ribble Valley (Mr. Evans) was breathtaking in its cheek. All that I heard from him was that Wales was crippled and strangled, that manufacturing was in terminal decline, that the farmers of Wales were just about to disappear over the horizon and so on. Gentleman making a speech on the loss of jobs in manufacturing during the 18 years when Col. 50 his party was in government. Everywhere in the developed world, manufacturing jobs are on the decline. That applies even the most prosperous and strong economies, simply because technology is making many jobs obsolescent. and, unfortunately, workers become redundant as a result. It is the duty of Governments to try to provide different opportunities or training, so that people who lose their jobs and are coming on to the job market can find opportunities in new sectors.

The truth is that employment has risen under this Government. As was pointed out this morning, wages have made a real-terms increase. On top of that, the minimum wage in Wales has helped 100,000-plus people. The working families tax credit has helped about 60,000 families and, on average, has provided an improvement of about £1,000 in their take-home income. Almost 2,000 families in my constituency have been helped. When we look at the big picture, there is no doubt that the Government have been and are being successful. There are more jobs and people are becoming more prosperous. Inflation is under control; despite the slight jump in today's figure, which relates to the abolition of MIRAS, the underlying rate remains well within the Government's targets. The ratio of debt to GDP is coming down at a time of increased public spending. The big picture is good.

We look forward to the implementation of the objective I programme to help the areas of Wales with special problems—the valley communities, or those that were not necessarily in the valleys but were part of the traditional coal and steel-making industries of Wales, as well as rural communities where, because of the decline in agricultural job opportunities, special action is needed. No fair-minded person would attribute those problems to the past two or three years. In fact, some things have begun to improve in that time. However, there has been a steady decline. This morning, the Barnett formula was mentioned and it was pointed out that, since it was devised, GDP in Wales has been reduced by 10 per cent. or more. That process continued through the 1980s and most of the 1990s. We now have an opportunity in Wales to turn that around.

Despite the big picture being good, however, we cannot turn our eyes away from the problems. We are all aware of the long-term problems. We must remember that, although the high interest rates are helping to keep inflation stable, they put pressure on manufacturing and steel making. Recent figures show marginal improvements in production in both sectors, but we cannot ignore the warnings that the balance is very fine. The Bank of England's Monetary Policy Committee has a difficult job to do in getting that balance right So far, taking the economy—and the Image Col. 51 economy of Wales—as a whole, a good job is being done.

The idea that we can manage exchange rates down is hoping for the impossible—the gold crock at the end of the rainbow. My right hon. Friend the Member for Llanelli (Mr. Davies) pointed out in his brilliant contribution this morning that he is still waiting for a reply 25 years after asking Treasury officials how to go about that. We do not know how to do it, because we cannot control the market. We know that some big players can spend billions if they think that the currency will go up or down.

Mr. Robert Walter (North Dorset): The answer to the question was given by the right hon. Member for Caernarfon. Cutting 2 per cent. off interest rates would make it possible to manage the exchange rate down.

Mr. Griffiths: I think that we all know the futility of doing that. We would enter a spiral of difficulty. A 2 per cent. cut in interest rates would mean a significant effect on taxation and there would be many complex issues to take into account. I must admit that, if I were George Soros, the price of the euro now and the well-being of virtually all the economies that are part of euroland might persuade me that there was a case for a bit of a dabble in the euro. I think that it will make a comeback. [Interruption]. Unfortunately, I do not have enough to affect the market; I only wish that I had.

Interest rates are significant and we cannot forget them. However, the hon. Members for Brecon and Radnorshire (Mr. Livsey) and for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy (Mr. Llwyd) mentioned other factors that are also beyond our control. I had in my constituency—it is now closed—a Bairdwear textile and clothing factory, most of the production of which was for Marks and Spencer. Marks and Spencer orders away from its supplier not because the factory was not providing quality goods but because it wanted to produce more cheaply abroad.

Marks and Spencer's big mistake was to get its decisions on what customers would want out of line. Speaking to several of the company's women customers, I heard complaints that it was no longer producing the items that they wanted. Marks and Spencer made the bad decisions, not the producers of the clothes that it was selling in its shops. That has been a great tragedy.

Mr. John Smith: Does my hon. Friend agree that we can counter the effect of events in Wales by informing the Welsh consumer of what such companies, especially Marks and Spencer, are doing? We should then let the consumers exercise their choice over what they buy. Jobs might return.

Mr. Griffiths: I agree with my hon. Friend. I have not walked into Marks and Spencer since it announced the closure and I shall not in future, even though I had been a loyal customer. We must put pressure on companies to make them realise that they cannot depart willy-nilly and not expect backlash from consumers.

Col. 52 In the meantime, our Government are plainly trying to find alternative employment and to provide training for new skills so that people who lose their job can find a new one. In my constituency, overall unemployment is down by 19 per cent., youth unemployment is down by 17 per cent. and long-term unemployment by 11 per cent. That does not mean that we can rest on our laurels—there is still much more to do to ensure that the Welsh economy becomes more prosperous. We aim for the goal of full employment.

I wish to consider the rural community. It was difficult to listen to the hon Member for Ribble Valley this morning and to have any feeling for the fact that BSE had arisen during the Tory years. On coming to power, we were left with a massive and difficult legacy. In stabilising the British economy, we have created problems for farmers regarding interest rates. I heard what the hon. Member for Brecon and Radnorshire said about the problems in the Welsh Development Agency regarding rural development. I hope that the Assembly will listen to that argument and act. Our farming communities must be more ready to ally with food processing and to develop cooperatives. Several cooperative ventures have failed, but the strength of agriculture in countries such as France is based on the co-operative ventures that small farmers have pushed through. We must get used to doing that because that is how the rural economy can survive.

The Government are successfully tackling the remaining problems in Wales, but we cannot rest on our laurels just because we are doing much better than the previous Government. We must do even better for the sake of the people of Wales.
