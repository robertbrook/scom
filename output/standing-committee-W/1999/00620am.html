The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Paul Murphy)

4

Mr. Wigley

However, most inquiries—I am sure that the right hon. Gentleman agrees with this—are now a matter for the Assembly.

Social Exclusion in Wales

11.1 am

The Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. Alistair Darling)
: In line with the usual conventions of the House, I arranged for copies of my statement to be sent to the principal Opposition spokesmen. Copies left my office at 9.30 am, but for some reason did not arrive until about 15 minutes ago, for which I apologise. However, I am sure that Opposition spokesmen will be able to follow what I have to say.

I shall make a short statement on what the Government are doing to tackle poverty and social exclusion throughout the country and in Wales. We are working closely with the National Assembly for Wales, and both Administrations are working together to tackle poverty and exclusion. The United Kingdom Government's strategy was set out in the report "Opportunity for All", which I published last September. It states the problems that we face and the measures we are taking to alleviate poverty and to tackle its root causes.

The National Assembly has shown its commitment to tackling social disadvantage in Wales. In January, the First Secretary published "A Better Wales", a 10-year plan to improve life for everyone in Wales. Poverty and social exclusion are endemic problems that build up over generations, but that has been especially so in the past 20 years. We want to build a country in which everyone has the opportunity to get on. A key way to combat poverty is to get people into work, because lack of work is the main cause of poverty, especially child poverty. The number of children growing up in workless households doubled between 1979 and 1997. That is why there is so much persistent poverty, especially among children. That is not just economic madness; it is morally wrong. No child should grow up without the chance to get on. We are determined to halve child poverty within 10 years and to end it within a generation.

We are already making progress; there are 900,000 more people in work now than there were in 1997—31,000 in Wales. By getting more people into work and making work pay, we are helping to break the no-pay, low-pay cycle that has blighted too many people's lives.

Mr. Nigel Evans
(Ribble Valley)
: Will the Secretary of State give way?

The Chairman

Mr. Darling
: I will answer questions later. No doubt the hon. Member for Ribble Valley (Mr. Evans) will acquaint himself with the Committee's procedures in due course.

Our strategy goes much wider because it tackles the many factors that cause social exclusion: unemployment, inadequate housing, high crime rates and the lack of access to good quality public services, such as health care and a decent education. We are systematically tackling problems faced by children, people of working age and older people, and are JPEG 13 working to close the gap between the most deprived areas and other communities. For example, 3,000 people aged 25 or over who have been unemployed for 18 months or more will benefit from the employment zones established in north-west Wales and in the heads of the valleys and Caerphilly.

It is vital that we work together, because the National Assembly has responsibility for many policies vital to tackling social exclusion. We are working in partnership with the Assembly and local agencies to build a more inclusive society. The Joint Ministerial Committee met in Edinburgh on 26 May and agreed to work with councils to encourage take-up of the benefits and services available to pensioners.

We are making significant progress towards our goal of ending child poverty in 20 years, as the UNICEF report published last week recognised.
