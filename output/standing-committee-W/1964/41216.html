OFFICIAL REPORT

10.30 a.m.

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. James Griffiths)
: May I say, Lady Megan, on behalf of every Member of the Committee, how pleased we are to see you in the Chair to preside over our deliberations?

I beg to move,


That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the matter of the functions of the Secretary of State for Wales and constitutional changes in Wales and Monmouthshire.

Sir Keith Joseph
: I associate my right hon. and hon. Friends with the welcome to you, Lady Megan, as Chairman of the Welsh Grand Committee and say how much we welcome the combination of firmness and charm we are sure to receive from you.

Before embarking on the debate, it would be appropriate if we referred, sometimes with joy and sometimes with a certain sadness, to changes in the composition of the Committee. It strikes an odd note to see the hon. Member for Cardiff, West (Mr. George Thomas) in the body of the Committee. We congratulate him on his promotion. We wish him well while he sorts out the problems of Sabbatarianism and licensing and other subjects for which he has been made responsible.

We on this side of the Committee also welcome the new Members, although in the case of the hon. Member for Swansea, West (Mr. Alan Williams), naturally my hon. Friends and I regret the loss of Hugh Rees who played such a large part in our activities before.

We would all like to say how much our minds are dominated these days by the floods in Wales and we congratulate the Secretary of State on the obvious trouble he has personally taken to see the extent 4 of the flooding. We welcome the assurance of the Minister of State in the House yesterday that the right hon. Gentleman will report to the House as soon as he knows the extent of the damage when the water has receded and what action needs to be and is being taken.

Of course, we congratulate the Secretary of State personally on his assumption of the great office he now holds. Wales has a Secretary of State who is widely respected, and we shall give him ample time this morning to explain the exact meaning of the functions for which he is now responsible.

The history of the post-war devolution of power to Wales starts with the work of my right hon. Friends when they took office in 1951. The post-war Labour Government took no steps whatsoever to devolve administrative powers to Wales. It was in 1951 that my right hon. Friends created the post of Minister for Welsh Affairs in fulfilment of their election pledge and it was not until 1954, two and a half years after the first Minister for Welsh Affairs had been appointed by the Conservatives, that the Labour Party proposed that there should be a Secretary of State. Now, thirteen years after the Tories first created the office of Minister for Welsh Affairs, the Labour Government have fulfilled their pledge of the mid-nineteen-fifties to create the office of Secretary of State.

Of course, there are a number of questions arising from this appointment. What we and, I am sure, all hon. Members want to know is what powers the right hon. Gentleman has. The Prime Minister, in announcing to the House the functions of the new office, used words which seemed to indicate that the Secretary of State does not even have all the executive responsibilities of his Tory predecessor. The Prime Minister said:

"He will now take over virtually all the executive responsibilities of the Ministry of Housing and Local Government in Wales."—[OFFICIAL REPORT, 19th November, 1964; Vol. 602. c. 623.]

Those words were repeated by the Secretary of State himself in an interview with Mr. David Rosser, reported in the Western Mail on 1st December. The right hon. Gentleman is reported as saying:

"It is true that the transfer of executive functions to the Secretary of State will cover JPEG 5 virtually the whole of the work of the Ministry of Housing and Local Government."

We would like to know from the right hon. Gentleman what is the significance of the word "virtually". For instance, we assume that he will be responsible for the housing function which has until now been within the scope of the Minister of Housing and Local Government and Minister for Welsh Affairs. No doubt the right hon. Gentleman will confirm that. We assume that he is now responsible for the Ministerial work in connection with local government, and would like to know whether that includes local government reorganisation, local government finance and rating. I hope that the right hon. Gentleman will be able to give us answers on these points.

We note that in response to questions the right hon. Gentleman has said that he is still considering the proposals that he should make in connection with Welsh local government reform and that he is contemplating the issue of a White Paper. The Committee will be aware that the previous Administration had committed themselves to the publication of a White Paper which would make proposals for the reorganisation of Welsh local authorities in terms not only of boundaries but of functions. We would be grateful if he would tell us that his White Paper will also cover both functions and boundaries.

We would like to know more about his responsibilities for housing. The Committee will be aware that the Welsh Office has in the past few years mobilised a great increase in the momentum of Welsh housing. We would like to be assured that the right hon. Gentleman will himself be responsible for maintaining this momentum and will have the power to do so.

Then there is the question of water. We would like to know whether the previous responsibility of the holder of the Ministry of Welsh Affairs in co-ordination with the Ministry of Housing and Local Government is continued in the case of water. In Opposition, the Labour Party made great play with the need for a Welsh water resources board. We would like to know what the present position is. Is the right hon. Gentleman responsible for water in Wales?

Some of us noticed the reply of the First Secretary of State in response to 6 Questions by my hon. Friend the Member for the City of Chester (Mr. Temple), who asked whether the Secretary of State for Wales would take over the powers of the Ministry of Housing and Local Government for water in Wales. The First Secretary of State replied that he thought it highly improbable that these things were being considered. We hope that the Secretary of State will tell us today what the position is regarding water.

We note that he is to be responsible for town planning functions of the Ministry of Housing and Local Government in Wales, and we have no quarrel with this, but we would like to know more about the interplay of town and country planning and economic planning. As the Committee will be aware, the late Administration had set up a survey and redevelopment section of the Welsh Office to carry out, in conjunction with the then Secretary of State for Industry, Trade and Regional Development, a plan for Wales. We note that the link between the Welsh Office and the central economic planning mechanism is no longer with a Secretary of State for Industry, Trade and Regional Development, but with the Secretary of State for Economic Affairs. This is not in itself, as far as we can see at first sight, disturbing, but we want to know whether the Welsh Office will maintain a survey and redevelopment section, that the work already done will not be wasted, that plans will be issued for Wales and the five divisions of Wales—although these are not sacred—as quickly as possible.

We also want to know the present state of play about the famous new town. We do not want to make life awkward for the right hon. Gentleman, but we know that, whereas unanimity prevails on practically everything on the other side of the Committee, there are some differences of opinion about this. The first thing the right hon. Gentleman said on accepting office was that he would have a new town for Wales. That may or may not have been welcome to all his hon. Friends. No doubt we shall hear about it today. But is this a decision he is actively pursuing at the moment? Is it in his mind that the occupants of this new or expanded town will come from congested industrial cities of England JPEG 7 or from the already depopulated areas of mid-Wales? We should like to hear something about this and the parts which he and the First Secretary of State are playing in all this.

We know that the right hon. Gentleman has been given powers over roads, both trunk and urban, in Wales. We see nothing in this to criticise in itself, but does he think that he will be able to maintain the highly favourable ratio of spending on roads by which the expenditure on roads per head of population in Wales has, over the last few years, been so very much higher than expenditure per head in the United Kingdom as a whole and England in particular?

We ask him to clarify the obscure words of the Prime Minister about a Welsh Vote. What is the position there? We are not saying that a Vote for the Welsh Office is in itself the final criterion of the effectiveness of the Office, but we think we should know as soon as possible what the position is, and we ask the Secretary of State whether he thinks that, with his new and smaller hat for Wales, he will be able to keep the disproportionate share of the total road expenditure that Wales has enjoyed until now.

We note with some surprise that, despite categorical assurances in "Signposts for the New Wales" that executive responsibility for health, agriculture and education would be transferred under a Labour Government to the new Secretary of State, the right hon. Gentleman has no such executive responsibility. We note that he has been given the honour of replying on some occasions for other Ministers whose executive responsibility in Wales remains unimpaired by the creation of his new office.

We would like some explanation, and we think that the electorate will want some, of why, after thirteen years in Opposition, when the Labour Party made categorical promises, the Government have, so early in this case, completely broken them.

We note that, whereas in Opposition, the Labour Party was always saying how vital it was that Wales should have control of her own economic sinews—that is, powers which go with the Board of Trade, the Ministry of Power, and, par- 8 ticularly in connection with railways and docks, the Ministry of Transport.

Now, in office, the Secretary of State has no more power over trade, transport—except for roads—and power than his Tory predecessors. We do not think that this is a wrong decision. We think that in rail transport, trade and power the U.K. as a whole is the economic unit, and we always said so. We would be glad to know why the Government, having maintained for thirteen years contrary opinions while in Opposition, have so quickly swallowed their own words.

It all adds up to this: the Secretary of State has a splendid new title, on which we congratulate him, but he has not as much power as his predecessor, the Minister for Welsh Affairs who was also the Minister of Housing and Local Government, because, by his own admission and that of the Prime Minister, he only has virtually all the powers of the Minister of Housing and Local Government in Wales. We note that he has none of the executive powers of the other Ministries which were promised to the electorate.

In answer to all this the Secretary of State will, no doubt, tell us that all this is offset by the fact that now for the first time the Minister for Welsh Affairs, renamed the Secretary of State for Welsh Affairs, has a seat in the Cabinet exclusively on Welsh matters. I ask the right hon. Gentleman and his hon. Friends to reflect whether this implies a larger effectiveness.

These are essentially matters of personality as well as of power. No one would deny the right hon. Gentleman his full share of personality and certainly passion for Welsh matters, and I should like to leave altogether the personal aspects. No one should underrate the importance of weight, in terms of the constant importance to other Ministers, of a Cabinet office. Until now, the Minister concerned with Wales, the Minister for Welsh Affairs as he was called, always doubled the office with that of another major State Department. On both counts, he was sure to have his views very fully considered at all stages in the formation of national policy. This is being replaced by a situation in which the Minister concerned, whatever his personality and his JPEG 9 personal qualities, does not have a Ministry which is large or whose work is essential to the functioning, in almost all their spheres, of the other Ministries.

I ask the Secretary of State to tell us whether he does not agree that there is some risk that Welsh affairs will not get the same effective voice in the Cabinet, and will not, to put it crudely, have the same bargaining power, which the Minister for Welsh Affairs had in previous Administrations. I ask him to reflect on the disproportionate share of total national expenditure on Welsh roads and tell us whether he thinks that he can keep up that record.

We all want the right hon. Gentleman to have ample time to reply to some of our questions and I am sure that my hon. Friends will have many more. I will sum up my case by these last words: Wales has a widely respected Minister with a sonorous new title, but his powers have been cut.

Mr. James Griffiths
: I thank the right hon. Gentleman the Member for Leeds, North-East (Sir K. Joseph) for his very kindly personal remarks. I look forward to many years of seeing him display his great gifts in Opposition. We all recognise his gifts and talents.

I begin by associating myself with the right hon. Gentleman and with all hon. Members in our sympathy to the people of Wales, particularly in mid and north-west Wales, who have suffered so grievously from the floods this week. I felt that it was my duty as well as my privilege to make as quick a visit as I could, though it involved travelling in a helicopter. It was very difficult to find anywhere to land, but we were able to visit Newtown. My hon. Friend the Minister of State made a statement yesterday and I promise the Committee, as he promised the House, that as soon as we have the information necessary to estimate the damage, we shall make a statement in the House.

In the meantime, let me pay tribute to all those who are working so well, to the emergency arrangements and to the courage of the people. The Committee may allow me to cite one instance which illustrates the spirit in which the people have approached—I will not say a disaster for all, but a terrible ordeal. I went to a house in Newtown where 10 the water the day before had been 6 ft. deep. Fortunately, the Severn receded very quickly, almost as quickly as it rose. The water had been baled out of the house and there, in colliers' language, was the muck. I expressed my sympathy—and back came the answer from a good workman: "There it is; it has all happened now; there is nothing to do but tidy it up." That is their spirit and I pay my tribute to them.

I want to set out as clearly as I can the functions and responsibilities which I have taken over with this office, but first I want to say a few words about the Opposition's attitude to the Labour Party programme. It is intriguing that this morning we should be told that we are not carrying out our programme, when tomorrow we shall be begged, or whatever it may be, not to do so. The Opposition's attitude is very interesting. I hope that the country will recognise its inconsistency, whatever may be ours, and will appreciate that hon. Members opposite are anxious that we should carry out some parts of our programme in full and at once and are desperately anxious that other parts should not be introduced at all.

Let me begin what I have to say by stating how I have always approached this problem. For many years, there has been a continuing debate in Wales about the relationship between Wales and the rest of the United Kingdom. There has been a wide desire, not confined to one party and not satisfied by whatever arrangements previous Administrations have made, for a fuller recognition of our nationhood and of a new status for Wales within the constitution of the United Kingdom. This feeling has been very widespread. We are an old nation for whom the imponderables mean much.

I hops that the right hon. Gentleman will understand that this is one of the things which have convinced many people that whatever the arrangements were before, and however good or bad they might be, they did not fulfil this deeply felt desire to have Wales recognised. I am sure that my right hon. Friend the Prime Minister has gone a long way to meet this desire by appointing a Secretary of State for Wales for the first time. In case there should be any misunderstanding, my title is not Secretary of State JPEG 11 for Welsh Affairs, but Secretary of State for Wales, with a seat in the Cabinet in virtue of the office. I believe that this has met with widespread approval in Wales and I am sure that, when the time comes, the electorate will express its gratitude to the Prime Minister for being the Prime Minister who went some way to meet this deeply felt desire to have Wales recognised, to have its own representative in the Cabinet to be the eyes and ears and voice of Wales. I am conscious of the honour done to me, the privilege given to me and the responsibility placed upon me for being permitted, in the twilight of my career, to be the first Secretary of State for Wales, and I can promise that I shall do my best for my country.

I want to set out the arrangements as clearly as I can and in the course of doing so I hope that I shall deal with some of the right hon. Gentleman's questions. If he catches your eye, Lady Megan, my hon. Friend the Minister of State will later take up any questions with which I do not have time to deal. I hope that the Committee will forgive me if this morning I stick rather closer to my notes than is my custom, or habit by temperament. I shall do so in order to save time.

I want first to speak about the executive responsibilities which are transferred to me as Secretary of State for Wales. I have said, and I repeat what the Prime Minister has said, that I shall be taking over virtually all the executive responsibilities within Wales which hitherto have been the responsibility of the Minister of Housing and Local Government. This is a transfer of functions of major importance to Wales, for it touches policies and administration which have a vital relationship to the work and life of the people.

As the Prime Minister announced, I shall be responsible for housing, town and country planning, organisation of local government, and new towns in Wales. This is a considerable devolution of adminstration. It is a transfer of functions from the existing Minister to the Secretary of State. When we use the term "virtual", it is because all these transferred functions will have to be embodied in a transfer of functions order. This is a matter of some complexity to which I shall refer later. In addition, I shall be taking over responsibility from 12 the Minister of Transport for roads in Wales, including trunk roads in Wales. This is a function which naturally follows, because it is intimately linked with responsibility for town and country planning.

It will be necessary, as I have said, to have a transfer of functions order to give effect to these changes of responsibility, and the work of preparing the order is now under way. It involves careful examination of existing legislation to see precisely which powers are affected by the separation of responsibilities. There are many of these. Some can be geographically separated quite easily, while in other cases it is desirable to continue joint responsibility. I am sure that with his experience the right hon. Gentleman will appreciate that this requires some care in drafting. All I can say at this stage is that the work of preparation of the transfer of functions order is proceeding as rapidly as possible. When it is laid before Parliament, the situation will be more clearly set out than I can state it this morning. I promise that no time will be lost in making these preparations. I cannot give a firm date yet, but the work is proceeding. I understand its complexity and the difficulty and the care needed in preparing it, but I am sure that those responsible will work with all due urgency to see that it can be announced as quickly as possible.

Sir K. Joseph
: Is the right hon. Gentleman to say anything about water?

Mr. Griffiths
: Yes. As the Committee knows, a new Ministry has been set up, the Ministry of Land and Natural Resources, and future responsibility for water is now being considered in the light of the setting up of the new Ministry. Consulations are taking place among my officials, those of the Ministry of Housing and Local Government and those of the Ministry of Land and Natural Resources. The Prime Minister hopes to make a statement very shortly about the responsibilities for water in the light of the decisions reached.

Those are the responsibilities to be transferred. I regard them as a very good beginning. I am very anxious in what time is left to me to make a success of this office. I am very anxious to lay the foundations. This is how our constitution works. We often start with small JPEG 13 beginnings and then the thing grows. It may be a matter of interest that I begin with more executive responsibilities than the Secretary of State for Scotland had when he began. This is exactly how the process works. We go from precedent to precedent. I am content to begin with the responsibilities which have been transferred to me and to make sure that we lay a sound foundation. For the rest of the devolution of administration, I am sure that, having made a good beginning and having laid a sound foundation, it will grow from year to year. That is my view and my hope. That is how things have happened in this country in years gone by, and that is how I think we shall continue in the years which lie ahead. Meantime, I regard it as my responsibility and duty to make a success of the administrative powers which have been given to me as Secretary of State for Wales, and to that end I shall devote myself.

11.0 a.m.

I come now to say a word about the separate Welsh Votes. In due course there will be separate Welsh Estimates to cover the expenditure on the functions which by then will have been transferred to me. I envisage that this will include three separate Votes dealing respectively with roads in Wales, with housing in Wales, and with the Welsh Office. I am not yet in a position to give the Committee an estimate of the total provision which will be necessary for this purpose in the financial year 1965–66.

As I have indicated, the actual responsibilities will be handed over as soon as the transfer of functions order takes effect. In the meantime it has been arranged, as a matter of accounting convenience, for the Ministries of Transport and Housing and Local Government to continue till the end of the present financial year to defray the charges under these Votes as if the functions had remained their responsibility. This is purely a temporary arrangement for accounting purposes. There will in due time be Welsh Estimates and a Welsh Vote. This means that for the first time we shall have a Welsh Vote, and I can see the Welsh Grand Committee conducting debates, as we have conducted debates in the House, on Supply, and I am sure 14 that my right hon. and hon. Friends will take the opportunity to air the grievances of their constituents.

I propose now to say a word about the general grant and the rate deficiency grant. The right hon. Gentleman said—and if he looks up the debates on Wales he will note that I have said it, too—that we have been anxious to find a way by which Wales' desire for a Secretary of State and for the devolution of administrative responsibility should be made possible without severing the economic and social ties which for generations we have developed with the United Kingdom. My right hon. and hon. Friends know that this has been my view all along. I am Welsh enough to recognise that we must have a Secretary of State for Wales, that Wales desires this, and has a right to it. At the same time, because of my industrial experience, I have been anxious to secure this in a way which would ensure that Wales would not be taken out of the main stream of economic and social development. I believe that as a coal miner, as a M.P., and as Secretary of State.

I have given a great deal of thought to the question of these two main grants, particularly in view of the fact, as announced by the Minister of Housing and Local Government yesterday, that in the course of the long Parliament which has just begun, we shall be considering the future of the financial arrangements with regard to local government. As my hon. Friends know, and as the right hon. Gentleman learned from his experience over rates—recently he had to provide first aid quite urgently—there is a problem here. The problem is the distribution of the burden for local government services between the central Government and local government. A radical study is being made of the problem.

The right hon. Gentleman referred to the fact that he instructed the Department in Wales to make a study of local government reorganisation. He rejected the report of the Commission, except with regard to towns. He allowed the normal procedure to take place there, but he instructed it to begin the task of examining local government in Wales from the standpoint not only of boundaries but of finance. When he says that a study of JPEG 15 local government must include all those factors, I cordially agree with him, and I am sure that my hon. Friends, too, will agree.

I have come to the conclusion that in the meantime we should continue the general grant and the rate deficiency grant as a United Kingdom exercise, if I may put it that way. It is sometimes said that Wales gets an undue share. I do not say this by way of criticism, but I think that Wales has carried an undue share of the burdens of Government policy for many years. She has carried an undue share of the consequences of Government policy for many years. A small country of 2½ million people suffers a very severe blow when, in the course of one generation, she loses more than 250,000 of her young people. We know that this country, and indeed the whole of Europe, suffered grievously because of the 1914–18 war, and lost so much of the flower of their youth, but we in Wales suffered a double blow. We lost the flower of our youth because of that war, and we suffered a similar loss from 1921 to the beginning of the Second World War. We therefore feel this problem acutely, and we have carried an undue share of the burdens of this country.

Yesterday I went and saw the Head of the Valleys Road and was privileged to open a part of it. Who can count the contribution made by those valleys to the economic strength of this country in the years gone by? Who, too, can find words to express admiration, as I do, for the way in which all the old industries and the new ones have played their part in the economic life of this country? Our Welsh people have shown a remarkable degree of adaptability and have learned new skills and new techniques, and we can face the future without difficulty.

For that reason I do not want to take any step without first having made a close study of its effect on Wales. I am very anxious that all the advantages which flow to Wales from association with the United Kingdom shall not be broken. I might add that I also take that view because of the advantages which flow to the United Kingdom from Wales. This is a two-way advantage.

That completes what I have to say about the transfer of responsibilities of 16 an executive character, and, as I have indicated, they will be set out very fully in the transfer of functions Order, when we shall see the full picture in more detail than I am able to give this morning, and I promise that that will be done as quickly as possible.

I come now to my next function, what the Prime Minister described as the oversight of the administration and activities in Wales of other Government Departments. I am glad to find that something which was started by the Labour Government between 1945 and 1951 has taken root. This will be of enormous advantage to me and to my hon. Friends in carrying out these functions of overseeing Government action and Government administration in Wales. It will help us to find out how these actions, be they legislative or administrative, are affecting the people of Wales, and at the same time will enable us to learn from their experience the problems with which they have to cope.

What we did between 1945 and 1951 was to begin the task of co-ordinating administration in Wales. We decided that there should be quarterly meetings of the heads of Departments in Wales. There are actually 16 of them. They are located in Cardiff with the exception of agriculture, which is situated in Aberystwyth. We brought them together so that they could exchange experiences and develop the habit of working together. They are collectively responsible for the whole field of Government administration and action in Wales.

This has worked very well, and I pay tribute to the fine spirit of co-operation which exists amongst the officers workings in Wales. This is an advantage, because oversight depends on some imponderables. I know that I shall have the full co-operation of my colleagues who are in charge of Departments in Wales in carrying out these functions. I know that I shall have the united co-operation of all the heads of Departments. I know, too, that I shall have the co-operation of the local authorities. My desire is to ensure that we bring together all those working in Government administration in Wales to enable them to understand one another's problems, and to cooperate with one another. This will simplify administration, and I shall keep in touch with them.

JPEG 17 One or other of us will make frequent visits to Cardiff and other parts of Wales. We shall hold periodic meetings. The practice was to hold quarterly meetings, but they are now held once every four months. We shall discuss what arrangements can be made, because I can see great advantages in holding meetings between heads of Departments at which Ministers attend so that we can keep in close touch with one another. I know that there is a tendency to under-rate this function which has been transferred to me, but I think that it will give me and my colleagues an opportunity to keep in touch with what is being done in Wales. I believe that by contact between Ministers and the heads of Departments in Wales we shall be able to help them and they will be able to help us. I approach this task not as somebody merely watching, but in the spirit that at Ministerial level, and at the Welsh level, we can carry out common endeavour to do the best for Wales.
