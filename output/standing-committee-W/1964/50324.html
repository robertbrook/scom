WELSH GRAND COMMITTEE
Wednesday, 24th March, 1965

[Lady MEGAN LLOYD GEORGE in the Chair]

MID-WALES DEVELOPMENT AND DEPOPULATION
OFFICIAL REPORT

10.30 a.m.

Motion made, and Question proposed,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the matter of Mid-Wales development and depopulation problems.— [Mr. James Griffiths.]

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. James Griffiths)
: I thought that it would be convenient to the Committee today if I opened the debate and set out the proposals which the Government now want to submit to the Committee to deal with the problem of mid-Wales. I am grateful to hon. and right hon. Members opposite for their agreement to this course and for sacrificing their constitutional right to open the debate. I am anxious to afford the fullest opportunity to hon. Members to discuss the proposals which I shall set before them, and I will not detain the Committee longer than is necessary to explain what I propose. If I stick to my notes more than is normal to me with my temperament I hope that the Committee will understand. It will shorten my speech.

There is no need for me to set out in any detail the nature and character of the problem that confronts us in mid-Wales. If it can be summed up briefly. It begins with economic decline leading to depopulation, the flight of the young in search of work and livelihood, leaving behind an ageing population. At present the proportion of those over 65 in mid-Wales is 20 per cent. above the average for England and Wales as a whole. Not only is it an ageing population. It is an impoverished population. The average income per head in mid-Wales is £132 per annum compared with £172 for the whole of Wales and Monmouthshire and £228 for England. The overall picture in mid-Wales is one of a vicious circle—(economic decline leading to depopulation, and depopulation leading to 56 still further decline and to an ever-increasing drift away from the area.

Our problem is how to break this vicious circle. By its nature and character the problem in mid-Wales is somewhat different from that which I and many of my hon. Friends were confronted with and still are confronted with in the industrial areas. There the problem was and still is in many areas that of how to bring work to the unemployed still living within the area. As I see it, the problem in mid-Wales is twofold: first to seek to bring as rapidly as possible work to those who remain in the area, and in particular to the youth, and secondly to provide a focal point or points of economic growth which would begin to turn the tide back to the area.

Here it is not only a problem of providing jobs but of providing men as well. This must be understood if we are to make, first, the right appraisal, and then apply the right solution to the problems of mid-Wales. I recognise that this is a tough job. To reverse the trend which has gone on at an accelerated rate for several generations will call for long and sustained effort and will need the cooperation of all concerned—the central Government, the local authorities, industrialists and workers. It will require the good will and co-operation of all the people, not only in mid-Wales, and not only in Wales as a whole, but also of our fellow-citizens the other side of Offa's Dyke.

Ever since I became Secretary of State for Wales my desire has been to start on this job because Deuparth gwaith yw ei ddechrau or, a job begun is two-thirds done. I want the Committee to regard what I say this morning as the beginning of what I hope the Committee will realise will be a long job. It is a job which can be done and a job which can begin now and must continue with unabated effort for many years. This is why, since I took up office and set up the Welsh Planning Board, my first remit to the Board was to consider the problems of mid-Wales and submit its report to me. I should like to express my gratitude to the members of the Board for the speed with which they have worked. They submitted their report to me on 11th February. They considered that what was needed in mid-Wales was a range of mutually supporting measures, each JPEG 57 designed to complement and supplement the others and all adding up to the beginning of a comprehensive plan to deal with all the problems.

If we are to bring new growth and new vitality to this area we must take care that we safeguard the base and make full use of its existing resources. I begin, therefore, by saying something about the base. Agriculture is still the basic industry of mid-Wales. It will be strengthened and it has been strengthened, particularly since 1947, by the proposals initiated by the Labour Government for assisting small and hill farmers. This has been continued by the late Government and it is acknowledged that this has helped agriculture in mid-Wales. Everyone realises, however, that increased efficiency may mean less employment. This is a feature with which we are becoming familiar not only in agriculture but in other industries. But efficient it must be, otherwise it will not survive. Whilst we realise that it is our duty to make it efficient we also know that it will create human and social problems.

Everyone who knows mid-Wales and other parts of our lovely country will know that in recent years forestry has made a valuable contribution not only to providing employment but to improving amenity and to making the lovely country still lovelier. Responsibility for forestry is one of the responsibilities which have been transferred to me as Secretary for State. I shall be considering future plans with the Forestry Commission and my colleagues.

Last Friday I had the privilege of visiting at Gwydyr. I visited the Forestry School and met the students. I was disturbed to find so few Welsh students there. I appeal to those who are engaged in education and to parents to realise that here is an opportunity for an interesting and adventurous career. I was impressed by the enthusiasm of the pupils there, including the Welsh pupils, and I hope that next time I go there I shall be able to meet more Welsh students. Forestry will still have a part to play, although of course there are problems and difficulties, not least that of securing land, to which I am giving my attention.

Mid-Wales has great natural beauty which makes it an ideal area in which the tourist industry can be developed. As the right hon. Member for Leeds, North- 58 East (Sir K. Joseph) knows, for he took the final steps, the development of tourism in the area began with the Welsh Council and its recommendations. The Welsh Tourist Board is at present conducting investigations and I should like to thank the parent body for seconding one of the ablest young officers to conduct the survey and make a study of the holiday potential of Wales. I look forward with great interest to the report. Tourism too has its part to play in the lovely area of mid-Wales.

Everyone is familiar, and perhaps no one more than I, with the fact that whatever mid-Wales lacks it does not lack water. I want to see the best use made of it, and not only to provide water for consumption for people and for industry. I have been impressed by the recreational opportunities which water provides. I was glad to see the Forestry Commission creating a picnic centre near the Gwydyr Forest so that people may be given the opportunity to enjoy recreational facilities there. I commend the Commission upon their imaginative plan.

I come now to the subject of a new town. I do not propose to argue this morning whether it is desirable or not. It may be that the argument in future will be whether it should be a town or a city. I propose to indicate to the Committee that the time has come to do a practical job. This is the next and appropriate stage under the machinery of new towns.

I want to tell the Committee about the steps which I am taking now. I am appointing consultants to carry out the necessary detailed study of the most suitable locality, which may, of course, be the site of an existing town. I hope to make these appointments very shortly. The consultants will make proposals to me about the location, the size, the nature and the rate of build-up of this new development.

It will also be necessary to study the practicability of securing the required movement of industry and population. I emphasise again that if this is to be done we must bring industry and men to the area. It is my desire that this work shall be carried out in the closest association with the interests and people concerned in Wales, with industrialists JPEG 59 and others, with local authorities, and indeed with industrialists and local authorities in the Midlands. I shall do this in consultation with the First Secretary and arrange for it to be done quickly.

I should like to say a word of advice to my compatriots as well as to hon. Members, and for the moment I am looking at the Liberal Members. I ask them for the sake of mid-Wales and its future not to lend themselves to a jealous campaign against the Midlands. It should be remembered that there are at least 50,000 of our compatriots in the Midlands who were compelled to leave mid-Wales to seek work. Many of them now occupy important positions in local government and elsewhere. All my colleagues from South Wales will remember, in our experience there in the last quarter of a century, how glad we were, and still are, to be able to induce industrialists from every part of the country to establish themselves in Wales.

I am convinced of the practicability of inducing industrialists to make this move, because I have had the opportunity more than once of visiting in mid-Wales existing factories built by the Development Commissioners. I was enormously interested in them. Every one I saw had been established in mid-Wales by industrialists from the other side of Offa's Dyke. I noticed one in Aberystwyth which had moved there from the Midlands. We welcome these factories, as indeed do the people of Aberystwyth.

In looking to the years ahead—and I hope that we are planning for longer than my life and perhaps that of most of us here—I believe that great conurbations will move out of the Midlands and will move westward. Indeed, this is already happening. As hon. Members know, a move is now being planned to Dawley, 25 miles from the Welsh Border. We must look to the future. The consultants will look at sites and so on and then make a study of this very important practical problem, for success depends upon the movement of men and of work.

I want to build a successful town. I want to build a live town. I do not want to build a phantom city. I want to build a real community, and these are the steps I am taking. Mr. Emlyn Hooson

Mr. Griffiths
: I have read these documents where they talk about a satellite for mid-Wales, and things of that kind. That is all. I am sure that hon. Members' constituents are very glad indeed that firms have come from the Midlands and established businesses.

I recognise at once that among the problems confronting us here—in which I am very anxious to help—a practical step is to strengthen the existing towns in the area. I have given a good deal of thought to this. I am convinced that the best way to do this at this stage is to JPEG 61 expand the work so well begun by the Development Commissioners in conjunction with the mid-Wales Development Association. I am glad to be able to say that after discussions with me and my colleagues the Development Commissioners have agreed to increase their efforts in this direction.

Up to now, they have been able to provide about 100 new jobs a year. It is their hope—and they are working to this end—to double this in future and to provide 200 jobs a year. In this work the Development Commissions will be helped, as they are now, by the Board of Trade and other Government Departments concerned. It is important that they should be given, as I am sure they will be, the closest co-operation of local authorities, and in particular of the local housing authorities. I would express my thanks to the Development Commissioners for proposing this expansion and doubling the programme. By doing this, I believe we shall reach the stage at which these developments will begin to develop a momentum of their own. That is how economic growth proceeds.

For many years it has been my privilege to be associated, sometimes officially and many times voluntarily, with promoting economic growth in the underdeveloped countries. I have always been attracted by the imaginative idea that what we have to do is to promote economic growth to the stage when they take off themselves. By developing in this way, modest as it may seem, these developments will generate their own growth, and will expand themselves.

I am convinced that by using existing organisations rather than creating new ones, and using local initiative, we shall make a real contribution to the economic development and will thus provide jobs for the young people. To quote from personal experience, I have been deeply impressed by the way in which the young people I saw in the factories in mid-Wales have shown the capacity and the ability to learn new skills and crafts. These will bring them added opportunities.

I have elsewhere paid—and I do here again this morning—tribute to the excellent work of the mid-Wales Industrial Development Association in publicising the attractions of the area, in encouraging industry to settle there, in 62 dealing with the problems—and there must be problems—of industrialists to establish themselves in new areas. I have been deeply impressed by its work. I have been very anxious to help it, and I am glad to be able to say to the Committee that to enable the Association to play an even fuller part in this work, and in my anxiety to increase the resources available to it, the Development Commissioners have indicated that they will be prepared to receive an application for substantial additional assistance to help the Association in its work. By strengthening it, and giving it even greater resources, added to the other things I have done, I believe it will continue to play a very big part and become a very good medium through which the councils and people in this area will work.

Lady Megan, what I am about to say will be of interest to you. I would like to refer to the Rural Industries Loan Fund, associated with your great father in the Act of 1909. It is appropriate that we should be using a Lloyd George Measure to help the land he loved. I have also discussed the rôle of this loan fund with the Development Commissioners. It does make a contribution, and it can help. They tell me that they recognise the need for improved loan facilities for small rural businesses in Wales. They expect shortly to be able to increase the funds available to the Rural Industries Loan Fund and to widen the scope of its work. That again will be an added contribution to the development of this area of mid-Wales.

I recognise fully that a key feature of economic development in this area, as in Wales as a whole—and indeed in any area—lies in the development of communications. I have had an opportunity to meet industrialists and local authorities, and when I have asked them what they consider to be the greatest need to promote the further economic development of every part of Wales they have all replied with one word—communications. I have asked the Welsh Planning Board to make a thorough and speedy study of the problem of communications in Wales. It is part of the study which has been made all over England. I look forward to receiving its report. I realise that in all this communications are one of the most important—indeed, perhaps the JPEG 63 most important—keys to economic development. For those reasons, I have asked the Welsh Planning Board to look at this vital problem and to report to me as soon as is practicable. When I receive its report, I shall then study it.

On all these proposals and problems for expanding the growth of mid-Wales I shall be consulting the Welsh Economic Council. I am sure I can call upon its members' combined experience, good will and knowledge to help promote this work. My hon. Friend the Minister who will preside over this will, with the Planning Board, be discussing this and other problems very urgently.

I have set out briefly the Government's proposals. I commend them to the Committee and to the people of Wales. They are a beginning. It may be a modest beginning. I think it is what is required in mid-Wales. We have had our fill of reports; we have had plenty of grandiose paper plans. I would rather be associated with a real attempt and start to halt the drift, and an endeavour to turn the tide, a combined effort to bring new life and vitality into this lovely area. I want to make this a joint endeavour of Government, local authorities and individual citizens all working together. I would echo the words used by the Mid-Wales Industrial Development Association in its excellent brochure:

"In the end the development of mid-Wales will not depend on any commission, bureau, council or association. It will depend on the outlook of the people in the area, their readiness to accept change and above all to grasp the new opportunities opening up for them. It will depend on their determination to re-invigorate rather than to preserve their way of life."

 I conclude by recalling that a quarter of a century ago it was my privilege, speaking for the second time only from a Front Bench, to open a debate in the House on the report of our late colleague and friend, Clement Davies, on the problem of T.B. in Wales. We called that the decline: We had come to accept it as a fate. That report was the turning point. We began to realise that this was not a fate, that it was a man-made problem and could be conquered by man. We look back over 25 years, and we look at Wales today. If we have not conquered it entirely, the scene has changed. The decline is no longer a fate which 64 we cannot combat. It is a disease which we can eradicate. We are eradicating it. It is now our job to look with the same spirit and the same resolve at this disease of economic decline. This, too, is man-made. This, also, can be cured by man. It is my hope that what I have announced will be the beginning of a combined effort to cure it and bring new life and vitality to this area.

We do not underestimate the work which has already gone on. In my reasonably short lifetime I have seen the small industries in mid-Wales go—tanneries, saw mills, cloth mills. Even a corn, mill half a mile from my own home is no more. The building is still there, but there is no industry in it. Therefore, we also have to balance what has been done over past years. This has not been a problem which has been neglected by the previous Government. Far from it. If one reads the excellent report of the Mid-Wales Industrial Development Association, one sees that since 1957 24 industries of various sorts have been brought in employing at the moment up to about 1,000 people, and in the next few years there will be an extra 500. There certainly has not been inaction. The Report shows action—action which we wish to see increased as the years go by.

As the right hon. Gentleman said in his speech, the tragedies of unemployment in the old days were such that the problems—perhaps he did not exactly say this, but I will—of depopulation could not receive quite the same attention as at the present time. If one is facing an essentially human tragedy such as unemployment, that represents a far greater tragedy than actual depopulation. Therefore, it is only due to the fact that over a long period of time the employment position has been considerably improved that we have been able to give our attention in the last few years to the question of depopulation.

One matter we should take note of is that in the United Kingdom the total population per square kilometre is in the region of 309 persons, which is almost the largest population density in the world. There is only one larger, and that is Holland where the figure is 351 persons per square kilometre. They are the changes in agricultural practices and the size of holdings; high employment and good wages over a period of years in most of our largest cities, particularly in the Midlands, which have drawn away our young men and women from the mid-Wales area. Young people are less tied to their families and are much more mobile than they were forty years ago.

Gentleman did not really today come out and take the political decision, which he alone can take, as to whether there is going to be a new town and where it is going to be.
