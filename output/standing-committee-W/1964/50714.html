Motion made, and Question proposed,

That the Committee do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the matter of Iron Ore Ports and Industrial Development in Wales and Monmouthshire.—[Mr. James Griffiths.]

Mr. David Gibson-Watt
: This debate is largely to be about the important problem of the iron ore ports in South Wales, a matter which closely affects the future economic prosperity of what I believe is the largest industry in Wales today.

It is not often that one must strike a discordant note in this Committee, but I would like to place on record the disappointment of my hon. Friends and myself that it has not been possible, in spite of requests, for a transport Minister to be here on behalf of the Government. I say that without disrespect to the right hon. Gentleman the Secretary of State for Wales or his hon. Friends, but it is, unless the constitutional position has been changed overnight, and unless the constitutional powers of the Secretary of State have been greatly increased—and we have not yet been told that the right hon. Gentleman is responsible for ports or transport of this sort—disappointing that a transport Minister is not here. Why is a transport Minister not available to speak in this debate? If there is a very simple and happy answer I will quickly give way so that the right hon. Gentleman may explain the position. If there is no reason, then I must emphasise and place on record the fact that is is a great pity—

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. James Griffiths)
: If the hon. Gentleman wishes me to intervene, I will. We are discussing the iron ore ports, and pre- 158 sumably we are discussing the subject on the basis of the White Paper which was published by the Government and in which they set out their decisions. That White Paper was presented jointly by my colleagues the Minister of Power and the Minister of Transport and myself as Secretary of State for Wales. Because of its special Welsh connotation and since it was a jointly presented White Paper—and since we are discussing the matter here and not on the Floor of the House—I thought it appropriate that I should take part in the debate.

Mr. Gibson-Watt
: I am grateful to the right hon. Gentleman for his contribution. I repeat again that while I do not in any way derogate his position or his obvious interest in the matter, I must emphasise that he is not responsible for ports and that it is a great pity that a transport Minister is not here.

Mr. Roderic Bowen
: Does the hon. Member not welcome the extension of the interest and responsibility of the Secretary of State?

Mr. Gibson-Watt
: That is another question which, no doubt, the hon. and learned Member for Cardigan (Mr. Bowen) will, if he is lucky enough to catch your eye, Lady Megan, develop with his usual quality.

The subject we are discussing today—the question of the location and concentration of industry in Wales, with special reference to the ports—could never have been so well advanced had it not been for the steps which were taken in past years. As I see it, South Wales lies at the point of a triangle with London and Birmingham as the other points. When a Conservative Government took over in 1951 there was not the slightest vestige of a blueprint for the communications which now exist. The Severn Bridge, in all its magnitude, the motorways, so soon to be finished, and the other developments are a lasting tribute to the fact that a Conservative Government understood the essential importance of communications to South Wales and saw not only that they were blueprinted but that they were carried ahead with such impetus.

Only a day or two ago we saw yet another section of this communications JPEG 159 triangle put out to tender; the section between Wyebridge, Monmouth, and Mitchel Troy. The contract was awarded to the Cleveland Bridge and Engineering Company and was for more than £2 million. I therefore say that the size of this transport concept linking South Wales so firmly with London and Birmingham provides a foundation for future industrial success.

The Secretary of State wrote a very good article in "The Voice of Welsh Industry." Indeed, the right hon. Gentleman is becoming a voluminous contributor to the national newspapers. He wrote another extremely good article in the Western Mail the other day. I like his style. Whether or not he likes it, in his articles he indirectly pays great tribute to what has been done in Wales over the years—and I mean over the years during which we were happy and honoured to be the Government.

In his article in "The Voice of Welsh Industry" the right hon. Gentleman paid tribute to the amount of money which the Board of Trade had brought in assistance to Wales from the start of the Local Employment Act in 1960 to the end of 1964—more than £9 million. The right hon. Gentleman pointed out that one had only to look around to see clearly that industrialists had great faith in the future of Wales. I am glad that he paid that tribute to the solid foundation made for the industrial fabric of Wales during 13 years of Conservative rule.

As I say, the road communications—planned and carried out by Conservative Administrations—prove a lasting testimony to the determination of Conservatives to bring prosperity to Wales. I only hope that the party opposite ensures that that trend is continued.

Four years ago, in the debate on 14th April, 1961, referring to the changes in employment since 1925, the present Secretary of State said:

"So we see Llanwern and we rejoice; we see Margam and we rejoice still more; we see Trostre and we rejoice still more again, and all the other industries in the valleys."—[OFFICIAL REPORT, 14th April, 1961, Vol. 638, C 727.]

Would it be too much to ask him to add today, "After 13 years of Tory rule we see South Wales and we rejoice"?

In discussing the iron ore ports today we realise that the National Ports Coun- 160 cil was asked by the present Government to make a report on them. The Council did that in January of this year. The White Paper which the Government have produced quite rightly puts forward the alternative schemes which have been considered—Milford Haven, Port Talbot, Bristol Deeps and Cardiff—with two separate schemes for each, making a total of eight. The protagonists for the various alternatives of siting, depth of water and weather have all been put forward by the steel companies themselves. Indeed, this subject was not totally absent from the election addresses of certain well known politicians.

The concluding paragraph of the White Paper does not seem very definite. It comes down in favour of the two independent schemes for Port Talbot and Bristol Deeps and says, in paragraph 48:

"After considering all the factors set out above the Government has come to the conclusion that the balance of advantage lies with a decision to proceed with the two independent terminals."

The phrase "the balance of advantage" is not very definite speech in Government parlance and the Government appear to tread delicately, like Agag. Although they seem to favour these two plans on operational and technical grounds, they do not say definitely that they intend to go ahead with them. It would appear that the matter is still open to debate.

There were two articles in The Times on 8th July which seemed to cast some doubt on the Bristol Deeps project, and a short letter in The Times this morning was headed "Another look needed". I do not intend to condemn the project in such strong terms as The Times article because, clearly, this is a matter into which much thought and research has been put. However, would the Secretary of State tell us whether the various factors which, through lack of space in the White Paper, have obviously not been able to be included—for example, the problems of the objections of the English side of the Bristol Channel—have been considered? What about the views of the Somerset people? What type of construction is envisaged? We know that the Bristol Deeps jetty will come within about 1,600 yards of the English border because of the size of the Channel and the site. What is to be the type of construction, and is it envisaged that shipping in the JPEG 161 Bristol Channel will be interfered with? One imagines all the problems of tide, climate and fog—and such things are never absent when considering shipping problems—and these problems must obviously be considered. Will the right hon. Gentleman also tell us whether, on both the Bristol Deeps and the Port Talbot schemes, it is planned that there should be some diversification of cargoes, because clearly this would lead to much more economical management?

A much discussed question is that of the size of ships. Those who brought forward their schemes to the National Ports Council were told to provide those schemes on the basis that ships should be up to 65,000 tons. It is difficult to be right, at any moment of time, on this issue. Indeed, I remember so well, only seven or eight years ago, hearing in the House of Commons certain things about atomic research which were afterwards proved to be entirely wrong. I remember people talking about what the size of oil tankers would be. They were proved afterwards to have been entirely wrong.

Would the Secretary of State give the reasons why it is thought that 65,000 tons is the right figure? Why have the Government and their advisers considered that the schemes should be planned on that figure? After all, if one considers the figures one finds that by 1970 of the ports which are at present providing us with iron ore—Sweden, South America and others throughout the world—only about one third of them will be capable of taking a 65,000 ton ship into harbour before sending the ore to us. On the other hand, and also looking to 1970—and the few years will go quickly enough—we know that certain ports, in Australia in particular—a most important area for long-term consideration—will be able to accommodate ships of over 100,000 tons. Ships of that size will operate at very low cost.

We must remember that when discussing this question of the iron ore ports there is only one thing that matters to the steel industry, and that is that iron ore should be brought to the hoppers at the back of the blast furnaces at the lowest possible price. That is something which, whatever our knowledge or inter- 162 est—and I have no personal interest to disclose—must be carefully considered.

We must also remember that there are certain new scientific industrial performances, one of which is described as pelletisation. I have not seen this going on, but in certain producing countries crude iron ore is being rendered richer and comes here with a far higher percentage of iron content than in the original ore. It is of interest that the quality has gone up over the years. In 1956 there was an average iron content of 56 per cent. and in 1964 that had risen to 59 per cent. and it is likely to grow again. This is a factor on the other side and almost questions whether we shall always be taking ships of the very largest size and whether it is not possible that this is such a complicated business that we might well need both kinds of ships.

The Government have not laid down their policy very definitely. We wish to give them plenty of elbow room. We do not underestimate the difficulty of coming to a decision of such great importance to the steel industry and to Welsh industry as a whole. There are many problems about the Bristol Channel and the future of the ports there which there is not time for me to discuss this morning. However, I hope that when the Government make their decision they will be able to give us a little more evidence to chew on.

This is a matter of such importance that it should be debated on the Floor of the House. We have been promised by the Government that it is to be debated on the Floor of the House, but we have not yet been promised that we shall have a day before the House rises for the summer Recess. I ask the Secretary of State, as I asked the Leader of the House, whether he can give us any assurance that there will be a chance to debate Welsh affairs on the Floor of the House and whether he will agree that this is a matter of such importance that he should bring further evidence to be incorporated in a major debate.

The White Paper is not very definite on a number of subjects. It does not show us what the Government's intention is. We press them to give us more evidence, and, if possible, to tell us the JPEG 163 way in which they intend to handle this extremely difficult matter.

I have opened the debate with the humility of someone with no knowledge of the steel industry, but realising, as we all do, that its future, especially in Wales, is of the highest importance. We have to get as near to the right conclusion as possible, and there are many detailed considerations to think about. Will the Government tell us what is in their minds, because we consider that there is not sufficient in the White Paper to make a proper case for what they may intend to do?

164 Important as the ore ports are, the debate is also concerned with industrial development in Wales. The livelihood of our people and the prosperity of Wales largely depend on economic development. It is, therefore, fitting that I should refer to some of the features of that development which have occurred since we have been in office. There is no harm in spending a few minutes on the position in Wales and showing how we have made progress and how things stand in the present set-up.

I say at once, as we have been reminded, that industrialists have shown great interest in Welsh industry during the past six months. Investment in factory development has increased over the same period of last year. In that respect we are making progress. I am happy to note that industrialists are taking this keen interest. During the period 1st January to the middle of June, 28 firms not previously operating in Wales agreed either to build factories or to occupy factories already in existence, and 14 of those firms have chosen locations outside the industrial area of South Wales, thus contributing to the diversification of industry in the more rural areas. Furthermore, 50 firms already manufacturing in Wales intend to build entirely new factories in which to expand or extend existing factories by a total of more than 1 million square feet. Nine others plan to expand by acquiring space mainly on Government-owned industrial estates.

These 87 cases of new industrial gain or growth which have become known promise nearly 7,000 additional jobs, 4,600 of which will be for men. This compares very favourably with the 9,000 expected from a full year's development in 1964 and is equal to the employment which was expected from all developments in the whole of 1963.

However encouraging the figures for the first half of the year appear to be, we must not lose sight of the fact that, although the insured employee population of Wales in 1964 was the highest ever, at 991,000, the figure hides the situation that since 1954 the male employee population has not grown at all. In 1954 it was 684,000 and it remained at about that figure in 1964. So we are not catching up. Developments have, there- JPEG 165 fore, done no more than maintain the opportunities of employment for men at the 1954 level.

Of course the Committee will appreciate that the Government cannot in eight months deal with the legacy of the past years but they are conscious that the number of jobs for men must be increased substantially. We went through the period of the 1930's with its stagnation and decay and we have not got over that. The problems which face us today are the problems of growth, problems resulting from automation and mechanisation. We are going into the computer age. All this is creating new problems, but they are not the problems of the 1930's.

The Committee will be aware of the Report of the Council for Wales and Monmouthshire on the employment of disabled persons. Much has already been done, and today there are fewer than 4,400 unemployed disabled people in Wales, whereas in June, 1950, there were nearly 11,000. We are making progress. Remploy, the D.R.O.s of the Ministry of Labour and the disablement advisory Committees and the employers have all done and are continuing to do their parts. Under the 3 per cent. quota arrangements, better employment prospects for all automatically mean better prospects for the disabled The further industrial developments, the new employers and the new factories, will take their 3 per cent. quotas, so that the more factories we have, the more of these disabled men will be employed, and already over the years the number of unemployed disabled workers has been reduced to 4,400.

Friend the Minister of Power has announced the short-term aid measures which the Government propose for the coal industry, and, more recently, their intentions about the capital reconstruction of the industry.
