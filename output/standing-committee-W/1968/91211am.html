Mr. George Thomas
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the Statement of Government Proposals for Reorganising Local Government in Wales.

As long ago as 31st July, 1939, the late Arthur Jenkins, father of my right hon. Friend, the Chancellor of the Exhequer, wrote in the Western Mail:

"… it is, I think, obvious that there should be an early and far-reaching re-organisation of local government areas. The old boundaries are no longer suitable; the standards of services in many of them are far below modern requirements, and the parish pump must now be superseded by institutions to designed as to be able to perform their duties with a rate liability on the inhabitants that approximates to the cost of similar services in other districts."

Twenty-nine years later, the basic structure of local government and the areas of individual local authorities are both relatively unchanged. Indeed, as far as the administrative counties and county boroughs are concerned, there have been two major changes only since they were first set up in 1888. They were the establishment of the county boroughs of Newport in 1891 and Merthyr Tydfil in 1908. I think that both sides of the Committee will agree that I should have been lacking in my duty as Secretary of State if I now failed to deal with the question of local government reform in Wales.

I see in this morning's Western Mail that the hon. Member for Hereford (Mr. Gibson-Watt) promises me "a hot time" on this question. I think that the words are those of the sub-editor. I should not have been surprised if they had been the words of his hon. Friend the Member for Barry (Mr. Gower).

This is a difficult subject. There are many vested interests which will be distured. No one can hope to provide a plan for local government reform which will please everyone. My over-riding aim is to produce a scheme that will best serve the interests of the Welsh people as a whole. I am tremendously encouraged by the friendly reception which the White Paper has received both among those directly concerned with local government in Wales and in the Welsh Press. No previous scheme of local government reorganisation has gained as wide a measure of acceptance, and this factor, although it is not an over-ruling one, must clearly be a major one for any Government.

JPEG 5 Paragraphs 12–14 of the White Paper deal with the two main alternative forms of re-organisation considered by the Working Party and also by their informal advisers and subsequently by the Government. The first was re-organisation based on all-purpose authorities—that is, a system in which there would be only one main tier of local government and the authorities in this tier would have responsibility for all local government services in their areas other than those which would be done at parish level. This would mean a tier of most-purpose authorities—perhaps about 16 of them—replacing the present county boroughs, counties and county districts and performing their functions.

The second main alternative considered by the Government was re-organisation based on a tier structure broadly like the present structure in the administrative counties, enabling those functions that can be carried out by relatively small authorities to be so carried out and those functions that need to be organised over a large area to be so organised.

These two models of re-organisation, with their variants, include all the possibilities which might be applied to the Principality. They have been considered very carefully by the Government.

Mr. Gwynfor Evans
: Have the Government considered a scheme based on a neighbourhood unit as the basic unit?

Mr. Thomas
: The hon. Member may be satisfied as I go along that such a consideration has been before the Government. We have looked at all these considerations.

The possibility was considered of applying one pattern to one part of Wales and another to the remainder—for example, going over to all-purpose or most-purpose authorities in parts of South Wales and retaining the existing system in the rest of the Principality, or vice versa.

The Government's conclusions, however, were that evolutionary reform on the basis of a tier structure was most appropriate to Wales at the present time. There were two main reasons for this. First, in rural Wales, for which my hon. Friend the Member for Brecon and Radnor (Mr. Tudor Watkins) and other hon. Members are such doughty advocates, the main county services, notably town and country 6 planning and education, would require such large areas and populations to make them effective that there could be no more than two or three authorities to discharge them. Since on the most-purpose authority basis, these authorities would discharge the district functions as well as the county functions, the reduction in the number of authorities would be very drastic indeed.

s Secondly, in South Wales, re-organisation on a one-tier basis would mean abolishing all the present district councils and bringing about the disappearance of some of the largest and most comprehensively equipped county and county borough authorities in the whole Principality; authorities which are already large enough to operate efficient services.

Naturally, whilst everyone is in favour of local government reform in general, many tend to have reservations when it comes to their own particular area. Before I turn to the main proposals of the White Paper, I hope the Committee will allow me to say a word about the effect of re-organisation on the staffs of local authorities in Wales, since understandably there is anxiety on this point.

Let me first say that the great majority of local authority employees—teachers, district nurses, midwives, and health visitors, manual workers of all kinds—nearly all those, in fact, who do not work in the actual offices of local authorities—are unlikely to be much affected by re-organisation since their work remains the same whatever the character of local government.

It would be wrong to exaggerate the extent of changes so far as office staff are concerned. There is no question of there being wholesale losses of jobs in the Principality. Obviously there are bound to be some individual problems among the staff of district councils and of the smaller county councils in the fense that some officers may have to move house or travel longer distances than they have been used to do. Some senior officers, too, will certainly have to accept that they will no longer be heads or deputy heads of their departments.

But against this, I want to stress that the creation of stronger authorities will lead to the better deployment of staff and will undoubtedly provide new career opportunities. The changes may well prove materially advantageous to some JPEG 7 and I doubt whether they will be materially disadvantageous to any.

A very important safeguard to staff will be the Welsh Staff Commission which we intend to set up by the legislation. My hope is that this Commission will be set up on an informal basis before the legislation is passed and it will certainly be at work long before the appointed date. The Commission will consider and keep under review arrangements for the recruitment and transfer of all local government employees and it will consider such staffing problems arising from the re-organisation as I may refer to it.

This body will therefore be a powerful safeguarder of the interests of the staff.

The White Paper may well be regarded as both an evolutionary and revolutionary document. It is evolutionary in that it proposes the continuance of the same kinds of local authorities as exist at present—county borough councils, county councils, district councils, parish councils, neighbourhood councils, or something very like them. It is, however, revolutionary in the drastic changes it proposes in the administrative map of Wales—the number of administrative counties will be reduced by more than 50 per cent; there will be 35 districts instead of 164.

Needless to say, the reception it has received has not been uniformly favourable. Some hon. Members will no doubt have come primed with the views of individual authorities or individual local authority associations. But it may help the Committee as a whole if I indicate the broad lines of the views which have been expressed.

8 The County Councils Association are broadly content with proposals in the White Paper, though they have reservations about the continued separate existence of the county boroughs. Their views are generally shared by county councils representing the majority of the population in Wales—Glamorgan, Monmouthshire, Breconshire, Carmarthenshire, Radnorshire. Other county councils—Pembrokeshire, Montgomeryshire, Cardiganshire—wish to remain as they are, or to become all-purpose authorities or most-purpose authorities. The division of opinion among the North Wales counties has been more complicated. I will deal with it later.

The Rural District Councils Association and the Urban District Councils Association have both accepted the principle of the continuance of a tiered system over most of Wales. Their main reservations are over the distribution of functions between the first and second tiers. They, and the Association of Municipal Corporations, but not, it should be noted, the County Councils Association, want functions to be taken from the new county councils and entrusted to the new district councils. This view is shared by many of the existing non-county borough and district councils.

The National Association of Parish Councils—of which, I believe, my hon. Friend the Member for Brecon and Radnor is Vice-President—are broadly in agreement with the White Paper as it affects their members.

The only Association fundamentally opposed to the suggestions in the White Paper are the Association of Municipal Corporations. They would prefer reorganisation on the same general basis as they have proposed to the Royal Commission on Local Government in England—an elected provincial council, for the whole of Wales, responsible for a limited number of strategic functions, with a number of local authorities exercising on a uniform basis all the other local authority functions.

The Association of Municipal Corporations suggested 15 areas which might form the bases of these new local authorities, though they made it clear that these were not necessarily the only groupings which would meet their requirements. They would have preferred, therefore, a reform on the lines of the most-purpose JPEG 9 authority model considered by the Government. There are good reasons why I do not think this is the answer in the particular circumstances of Wales.

Of the four county borough councils in Wales, Merthyr Tydfil have argued eloquently and forcefully that they should remain a county borough. I shall have more to say about them later.

I was speaking of the councils of small towns and of the way in which they serve their communities. But for these councils, the present and the future of these small urban communities would be considerably bleaker. People with problems turn to these councillors. If there are 12 or 15 councillors in a town of 2,000, everybody knows them. If someone has no councillor in the same street, he has one in the next. Here we have a splendid example of participation locally. There is nothing remote, nothing impersonal, about this kind of administration, and the people are vitally interested.

At elections to small councils people turn out in greater numbers than they do for large councils—that is true of Wales. For the larger authorities there is never a turn-out of more than 40 per cent., and it is sometimes less than 30 per cent. For the small councils it is never below 45 per cent. and it goes up to 68 per cent. Some people have been talking a lot about participation in this part of the world. Here is participation in the life of the community, and what do the Government propose to do with it? They want to get rid of it and thus weaken the structure of the local community.

The same kind of thing, although not so intensively, is true of the counties, too. Nearly every county in Wales has a sense of history. They have their own written histories. There are historical societies, publications and even county archivists in almost every county in Wales. County consciousness is now so strong that in London the strongest Welsh societies are county societies. It is weakest in the two most populous counties, the only two which the Government propose to retain.

Here, too, the sense of community and local participation will be reduced without JPEG 51 any corresponding gains to offset the losses. Nothing will be gained in this way that could not have been gained through methods of co-operation. Even in small urban or rural areas, one could co-operate by having the same clerk, for instance, for half a dozen authorities. There is no reason why this could not be done as it is done in other counties. Better councillors may not be found in this way. My constituency is 15 miles across and under the new system there will be constituencies three times that size to be served, and the distances to be travelled will be far greater. Membership of councils may be almost restricted to retired people or the professionally party-political, and this would be a great drawback.

The White Paper says that this plan is necessary because these authorities are too weak. But in what ways are three weak and poor authorities strengthened and made more wealthy by being knocked together? They have the same revenue; they have the same costs. The costs of running the schools are the same; the costs of building the roads are the same; the costs of the welfare services are the same. One does not save anything financially, but one loses much in local participation. The argument that one 52 will have abler officers is very dubious. In some cases there may be abler officers, but when a poor choice is made, the damage done by a poor official will be three times as great.

This is the old argument about uniting the states of Germany into a big country all over again. It had some advantages, but when one has a Hitler in charge of the country, obviously it has disadvantages, for he can do a lot more damage than he could do in a little country like Bavaria. This applies in all matters of local government and national government. It would be a bold man who would say that there are abler people running Great Britain than run the small countries of Scandanavia, even including Iceland. It is the worship of bigness which has vitiated the whole of the Government's thinking about local government.

Finally, I turn very briefly to the national community. If the London Government had established an elected council for Wales with planning powers and adequate finance, that would have had value.
