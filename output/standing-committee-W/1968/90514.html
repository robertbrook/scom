10.30 a.m.

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. George Thomas): I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the matter of health and welfare services in Wales.

I am sure the Committee will join with me in expressing our sorrow that the hon. Gentleman the Member for Hereford (Mr. Gibson-Watt) is still not well enough to be with us. We wish him a speedy return to good health.

The Welsh Office took a major step forward at the beginning of April when we were entrusted with responsibilities for the health and welfare services in Wales as well as with increased powers in agriculture and in tourism, and also with regard to our ancient monuments in Wales. I wish to pay tribute to the Welsh Board of Health, to the National Insurance Commissioners, who were their predecessors, and the Secretary of which was the illustrious father of our Minister of State. We are inheritors of a great legacy in the health service in Wales. The transfer of the health and welfare services gives me particular pleasure, for there is no part of the Government's activities which touches more closely the life of our people.

It is a great advantage to us that there is but one Hospital Board for the whole of Wales and only one Board of Governors for teaching hospitals. This makes administration easier and relationship between the Government and those concerned in the administration of the service much closer.

My colleagues and I have visited many hospitals in Wales in the seven weeks since we took over responsibility. I took early steps to meet the chairmen of every hospital management committee in Wales and the members of the Welsh Hospital Board, in addition to meeting the Board of Governors of the United Cardiff Hospitals. I shall shortly be meeting the Col. 4 Welsh Executive Councils' Association and I have also to meet with the local authorities concerned with personal health and welfare services.

I turn to the three main parts of the Health Service, and begin with that provided by the general medical practitioners. They are the people who generally see patients first and, in the main, manage the cases themselves. It is common knowledge that we have suffered from a shortage of general practitioners, but I am glad to say that we seem to have halted the downward trend. Four areas only are designated as being in special need of additional doctors. They are the areas of Mountain Ash Urban District Council, Pontypridd Urban District Council, Nantyglo and Blaina Urban District Council and Merthyr Tydfil Borough Council. But because of a rise in the population figures, the average number of patients per doctor has continued to rise slightly, and on 1st October, 1968, it stood at 2,231 compared with 2,229 in the previous year—an increase of two, which is neither here nor there. In any case, this figure compares very favourably with that for England.

Obviously it is important that when people visit their doctor they should be received and treated in adequate premises and that doctors shall be freed so far as possible to concentrate on the medical aspects of a consultation, leaving a receptionist to deal with routine telephoning, making of appointments and the filing of records, and for a nurse to undertake the many jobs which require her knowledge and skills in the practice.

A great deal of encouragement has been given to the formation of group practices, to health centres and to the improvement of existing practice premises. Likewise, encouragement has been given to the employment of staff to relieve general practitioners of non-medical work. Cooperation is the life essence of the Health Service. It is essential that local authorities, hospital authorities and general practitioners shall get together to work out common problems. Without this close cooperation we cannot have as efficient a Health Service as we all desire.

I should now like to refer to the general dental services in Wales. There are some 393 dentists on the list of Welsh Executive Councils. I am not satisfied with the number of dentists practising in Wales, but I would draw the Committee's atten- Image Col. 5 tion to the work of the Dental Hospital and School which forms part of the University Hospital of Wales, in Cardiff.

This hospital was opened on 1st October, 1965, and has already achieved its designated annual intake of 50 students. The first students qualified in 1968 and are now in practice. There has been, not surprisingly, a rapid increase in the number of patients seen at the Dental Hospital since it opened. In 1966, 5,150 patients were attended to. Last year, the figure reached 8,421.

I want to tell the Committee more about the health centres in Wales.

Mr. Arthur Pearson: Could my right hon. Friend indicate the proportion of dental students who are entering the teaching hospitals?

Mr. Thomas: I will do my best to see that my hon. Friend is supplied with the information in the course of the debate.

I wish to refer to the health centres. At the end of 1965 there were six health centres only throughout Wales. By April this year the figure had reached 16 with another 11 under construction or with building about to start. Many more are now in the active planning stage. The continued expansion in the provision of health centres is one of the keys to the improvement of the Health Service in Wales.

I now turn to the question of mental health. The overshadowing event in this field has been the Report on Ely Hospital which my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Social Services accepted on behalf of the Government a few days before we took over. I am not going to cover the whole of that Report, but it must be remembered that there is much dedicated service given in the Ely Hospital. During the past seven weeks, I have visited the Ely Hospital on three occasions, and I know that life has been very difficult for those who care for the 460 mentally subnormal patients in that hospital since the Report was published.

No less than one-third of all our hospital beds in Wales are taken up by mental health cases, and there is no doubt in my mind that these unfortunate people must be given a higher priority in our considerations. We should remember that many of the mentally subnormal patients spend long years in the care of the hospital. Indeed, I met a patient at Ely Col. 6 who has been there for over 20 years. These are people who cannot fight for themselves, as articulate patients in our general hospitals are usually able to do. It is all too easy for the community to put out of its thoughts the needs of this minority, but I must tell the Committee that I feel they have a high claim on my consideration. One of the marks of a civilised community is that it cares properly for those who are not fit to care for themselves.

A major problem that faces us is the fact that, although the numbers employed in our hospitals have risen substantially, staffing shortages still present difficulties. Indeed much of the troubles of hospitals such as Ely and Hensol Castle may fairly be attributed to shortage of staff.

I believe it is particularly important that local authority health services and the Welsh Hospital authorities consider together the kind and amount of services that they are providing for these people. I have a very strong conviction that there are many who spend years in the hospital when they ought to be in the community. This is a serious matter. It must be our aim to enable someone who is subnormal or mentally ill to lead as normal a life as possible, preferably within their own family circle. Where this is not possible, for family or medical reasons, our aim should be to enable the person concerned to remain in the local community by providing domiciliary support and residential accommodation. A tremendous amount remains to be done in this field by local authorities.

This leads me naturally to the question of the personal welfare services. I am glad to say that our local authorities are increasingly providing homes for those who need them because of age or infirmity. They are also providing purpose-built homes for our older folk, and they plan to provide some 8,600 places by 1976. Also provided is a wide range of services to assist the blind, the partially sighted, the deaf and the hard of hearing, and those with other handicaps. Naturally, I shall do all I can to encourage the continuous improvement and extension of these services within the limits of the finances available. Of course, there are limits, unless the community is prepared to pay more through taxation.

The last subject with which I wish to deal is the service provided by the hos- Image Col. 7 pital authorities, and I know that a number of Members of the Committee have a deep interest in the hospital service.

It is common ground that, as medical knowledge increases, specialisation increases. Diagnostic apparatus and treatment facilities become more and more complex, and hospitals, therefore, have to be larger, and serve more people from a wider area. The questions are: how large? How many people? How wide the area?

My own predisposition is against the over-large hospital. Social and medical considerations are important in assessing the size that our hospitals should be and the area that they seek to serve, and social considerations, like medical considerations, can never be out of the mind of a responsible Minister. I can assure the Committee and the people of Wales that I shall do my utmost to reach a right balance in these considerations.

Obviously, the time is overdue for a reorganisation of our hospital services. It is only now that we are having new district hospitals built, and it would be foolish to expect that with the provision of new district hospitals we shall nonetheless want to keep open every one of our older and smaller hospitals, even though they were established by great sacrificial effort to meet the need of earlier generations.

Radical change in the structure of the Health Service there must be if it is to meet the demands of the 1970s and the 1980s. I am entirely convinced that the younger generation will not be prepared to tolerate the smaller hospitals lacking expensive facilities which can be provided in the larger hospitals. I know that there will always be some people who resent change and who will resent the closure of a hospital anywhere. I take no pleasure in knowing that hospitals have to close, but I am in no doubt at all that the important question I have to bear in mind is that the hospital structure must be the most efficient we can provide for our people, taking into account such social considerations as I have already outlined.

I conclude by assuring the Committee that my Ministerial colleagues in the Welsh Office and I regard it as a privilege and a challenge to be the pioneers in Col. 8 creating a Health Service tailored to meet the special needs of Wales. We pledge ourselves to this Committee and to the people of Wales to do everything in our power to ensure that there are close and friendly ties between Ministers and those involved in our health services. We shall make ourselves readily available to those who are engaged in the noble task of caring for the sick. One of our great advantages in Wales is that we are a small country, we can have closer relationships, and we intend to ensure that the Health Service for the Welsh people reflects the spirit and the attitude of our country as a whole.
