Question again proposed.

Mr. Ioan Evans: I am very pleased to have this opportunity of making my maiden speech in this Parliament in the Welsh Grand Committee, although of course I have often listened to previous meetings of the Grand Committee when I was the Member of Parliament for Birmingham, Yardley. I am fully aware of the envious eyes that are cast at this assembly by representatives in England, because, not only do we have the opportunity of speaking on the Floor of the House on matters relating to Britain, but we also have this additional opportunity of dealing specifically with issues relating to Wales. This is, as it were, a second home.

The hon. Member for Pembroke (Mr. Edwards) agreed with his parliamentary colleague the hon. Member for Flint, West (Sir A. Meyer). They both said that they were not proud of the record of the last Government. I am sure that they will be pleased to learn that I am not proud of it either. While it can be said that the record of the last three and a half years is deplorable, I do not go along with those hon. Members when they say that this applies to all parties, because, in considering the housing progress which has been made since 1945, the record of the 1945–51 Labour Government, that of the 1964–70 Labour Government and the way that the present Government have started show that the Labour Government compare very favourably with the Tory Party.

The Secretary of State has given us a challenging speech. We have heard before of the need to tackle the prob- Col. 56 lem in housing that faces us in Wales, but today we heard a promise of action, I thought. If the programme outlined in the Secretary of State's speech is to be carried out, it will go a long way towards solving the problems that face us in Wales.

Opposition Members say that they want to keep ideology out of housing. But that cannot be done. One's ideology determines one's attitude in forming housing policy. Either we believe that houses should be built for profit, or for homes, to meet the housing needs of the people. That is where we come to the parting of the ways in our attitude to housing, compared with the Opposition.

I believe that the Government have started well in this programme. The policies outlined in the Gracious Speech, supported by the majority of Members of Parliament, should go a long way towards solving the problem. Within days of the new Government being formed on 8th March, orders were issued freezing all residential rents at their present level for the rest of 1974. The move applied to council rents and private housing whether furnished or unfurnished. Over 270,000 council house tenants in Wales will benefit from the consequences of the Government's step.

Mortgage rates have been held at 11 per cent. The houses we are building today in Britain are too expensive. Young couples are finding it increasingly difficult to set up homes. The Tory Party has spoken about the need for a property-owning democracy. But what we have created in the housing sector is a moneylenders' paradise. Since 1970 prices for both new and existing houses have risen on average by 100 per cent. They doubled in price during the previous administration's period of office. Many young people who have committed themselves to buying houses will think themselves lucky if their grandchildren own the houses they occupy now.

The hon. Member for Conway (Mr. Roberts) suggested that the choice lay between buying or renting a house. That choice does not exist for the vast majority today. Houses for rent are not being built, and people cannot afford to buy one. It has been said that 90 per cent. of the houses being built are too expensive for 90 per cent. of the population. Image Col. 57 Therefore, to suggest that building houses for owner occupation will solve the problem is not the case. The Government are to be congratulated on their recent action in preventing a further increase in mortgage rates to 13 per cent. It is reported in the Press and through the media today that funds are flowing into the building societies, which fully justifies the Government's prompt action, as that may prevent mortgage rates from rising further.

The hon. Member for Conway said that owner-occupation was very dear to the hearts of the people of Wales. Under a Conservative Government it has certainly proved very dear to their pockets. In addition to the Government's step forward, we are promised further action in the future. Tenants of furnished accommodation are to be given security of tenure. That is a big step forward. The disastrous Housing Finance Act has done much to cause difficulties to municipal tenants. Local authorities had to appeal to the Government to be given dispensation not to increase rents. But the previous Government impelled them to increase rents. Now we have a Government who are acting wisely to prevent those increases from being brought about.

Land needed for development is to be brought into public ownership. At long last in Wales we shall be able to sing, "Hen Wlad fy Nhadau" and mean it. All the proposals, I believe, will contribute towards solving some of the housing problems. Many of the houses in Wales are, however, too old and ill-equipped. That is why we should welcome the extension of the home improvement grant scheme that was to have ended on 23rd June.

The hon. Member for Bedwellty (Mr. Kinnock) suggested that a register of builders should be compiled, so that the work could be carried out, and that the list should be maintained. Numerous complaints can be made about the way in which this work is being carried on at the present time. In Wales there are too many one-man building businesses—jobbing builders—and many of them would submit a tender to rebuild the Pyramids if they were given the chance. They are taking on more than they can cope with, and that is why I welcome this extension. Col. 58 But I hope that there can be greater control to ensure that they carry out the work, and carry it out more efficiently, because there is a great deal of heartbreak in the way that the schemes are being carried out in many areas at the present time. I am sure that many hon. Members could relate stories of constituents who have accepted one tender, after which the individual who gave it has not been seen again until months later and the whole home improvement has been delayed.

Although much can be achieved by improving old homes, we need the new housing drive that the Secretary of State has spoken about. We need to expand the local authority housing programme. It was interesting to hear the Member for Flint, West say that he does not need more houses in West Flint. Let me quickly say that if his allocation can be given to us in the Cynon Valley we shall be very pleased to receive it.

I welcome the statement made by the Secretary of State that in 1974–75 an extra £14 million will be made available to local authorities in Wales to increase the housing effort and that £9 million of that amount will be earmarked for additional houses. We in the Labour Party are not anti-owner-occupier. We can take pride in the fact that we introduced the Leasehold Reform Act, and no Act benefitted owner occupiers and the people of Wales more than that one. If the growing needs for better housing are to be met, we must give the people the choice to own or rent houses. We must find methods to encourage the people who wish to own their own house, but we must also make provision for those who wish to rent their house.

I should like local authorities in Wales to build houses for owner occupation for local authority tenants, thus releasing local authority houses which could be offered to those on the waiting list. We would then be fulfilling the need of those municipal tenants—local authority tenants—who want to become owner-occupiers, and the people waiting for a home would be provided for.

I should like also to see more encouragement given to housing associations and to co-operative housing schemes. Reference was made this morning to what has happened in Scandinavia, but the housing problem there has been solved to a great Image Col. 59 extent by the formation of co-operative housing schemes. This is a form of collective ownership where people have the advantages of owner-occupation without the disadvantages of being tenants. I think this is something which we should consider in the years ahead.

I should like to see an expansion in the number of homes for the elderly, the lonely and those unable to look after themselves, by encouraging those elderly citizens who would be willing to enter the homes and look forward to the opportunity. I believe it would release existing accommodation for younger families.

I should like local authorities to get together to form building consortia to build houses for both public and private development. The Labour Party is committed to the extension of social ownership. I think this is one method by which it can be expanded, and I believe that local authorities should be brought in to a greater extent.

In considering the question of housing, I hope we shall have increasing regard to obtaining a better environment. The Cynon Valley, which I represent, is a beautiful Valley from Hirwaun down to Ynysybwl and Abercynon. But despite the unavoidable difficulties created by industrial and mining development, the local authorities have endeavoured to improve the environment in that area. But we must ask ourselves: Is the destruction of a better environment always unavoidable? In the middle of the valley we have a great Phurnacite plant which is pouring smoke into the atmosphere. The people of the valley are prepared to put up with this pollution because it means jobs, but cannot something more be done to minimise the pollution in this lovely valley?

The Coal Board now proposes to commence work on on opencast mine near Abernant, another lovely part of the valley, to provide the coal for the Phurnacite plant. I hope that the Welsh Office can help us in the Cynon Valley to obtain extra jobs in the area without our having to be threatened time and time again that it must be at the expense of the environment.

The Secretary of State has rightly said that as far as the Government are concerned our priorities are to be twofold, Col. 60 namely, housing and jobs. I agree with him, but I would add a third: let us have decent housing, let us have a good choice of jobs in Wales but let us also have an improved and continually improving environment where we can enjoy working and living.
