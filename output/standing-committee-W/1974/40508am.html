Resolved, That the Committee do meet again this day at Four o'clock.—[Mr. John Morris.]

The Chairman: Perhaps it will be convenient for me to remind the Committee at this stage that the House has permitted the Committee to rise this afternoon not later than six o'clock.

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. John Morris): I beg to move

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the matter of Housing in Wales.

My first privilege, Mr. Davies, is to congratulate you once again on taking the Chair. The second is to congratulate the two hon. Members, the Member for Conway (Mr. Roberts) and the Member for Barry (Sir R. Gower), as I understand they are hoping to catch your eye, Mr. Davies, and to speak on the Front Bench during this debate.

Wales is facing, as we all know, one of the worst housing crises since the war. This is primarily the result of the disastrously low rate of house building in 1972–73—the worst since 1946—in the public sector. That is our start line, and it is from there that we move to examine the situation as a whole. The sooner that is accepted and understood, the better. Almost all local authorities report a massive increase in their waiting list, wherever one goes in Wales. The totals are 5,000 in Cardiff, over 3,000 in Swansea and Newport and 1,500 in the Rhondda. In North Wales, 1,900 in Arfon and 1,450 in Delyn.

Wherever one goes, from discussions with local authorities, large or small, cities, valleys and in our rural counties, the same depressing pattern of growing housing queues has emerged. It does not matter whether one talks to housing authorities in Merioneth or Merthyr, pro- Col. 4 portionatley all are suffering form housing stress.

Many young couples, priced out of the home ownership market, awaiting council accommodation with little hope of getting it, are forced to live with parents in overcrowded conditions and all the social complications they involve. The growing needs of what, for a better term, I must call "front room families" are filling our constituency surgeries time after time. We know the problem; we see it, day after day. Tension resulting from such overcrowding is, I am sorry to report, turning occasionally, and far too frequently, housing problems into personal and marital problems for very many of our young married people. These are the realities of life in the constituencies that we all represent.

The private housing market hag not provided the alternative solution, as was once believed possible. Although the high level of private house building has prevented the total collapse of the housing programme in Wales, it has failed to make up the deficiency in local authority house building.

Owner occupation has been placed beyond the reach of a very large number of people—particularly the young married couples with children. We all know that since 1970 the price of the average house in Wales has doubled. Some of us will recall the very pertinent Questions which were put down month after month by my hon. Friend the Member for Swansea, East (Mr. McBride)—to whom I am sure the whole Committee will send their best wishes for a speedy recovery. He followed the course of house building prices in Wales most diligently month after month, and the point which he has been making is only too regrettably true.

That strikes home particularly hard in Wales, where we have such a strong tradition of owner occupation—the highest in Great Britain. Not too long ago, in many of the older areas, young couples could have purchased an older house for not far off £1,000 and improved it with a grant to secure a good home—it was a first rung on the ladder—but not any more; we all know that. Indeed, that kind of property is averaging £4,000 and more. The shortage of mortgage facilities and the high interest Image Col. 5 charges combined with the astronomical rise in house prices have driven many young couples out of the home market, only for them to find that the local authority is unable to provide for them. Can anyone describe the situation of such couples except that they are caught in what I term the "housing trap"—the same kind of trap as the other traps about which we have heard recently?

I should like to pose three questions to the Committee. As a good Presbyterian, I should be allowed so to do in the course of my sermon! They are questions which the Committee and the whole of Wales should ask and attempt to answer when we discuss the housing policies of the Principality.

First, why has such a situation occurred? Secondly, what can be done now to alleviate the immediate problems? Thirdly, what are the long-term lessons to be learned from the sad story of the last three years? Those are my three Calvinistic points this morning.

Mr. Cledwyn Hughes: Hear, hear.

Mr. Morris: I hear them endorsed by another good Calvinist, the right hon. Member for Anglesey (Mr. Hughes).

First, why has such a situation occurred? It is now evident that following the high building programme of the 1960s—over 100,000 houses were completed between 1964 and 1970—many local authorities concluded that the end of their problem was in sight. I am sure most of us read the perceptive article in the Western Mail on 7th January which pointed out that local authorities did net foresee the dramatic change in the market which transformed the situation, and it is also true to say that few steps were taken to assess real housing needs—a point to which I should like to return at the end of my speech.

This outlook accorded with the previous administration's philosophy in favour of private housebuilding. Additionally, the introduction of the complex and controversial Housing Finance Act absorbed the administrative energies and efforts of local authorities. We are paying the penalty of that today. At the same time, the housing cost yardstick, originally a valuable instrument of cost control, had become so inflexible—hon. Members have cases in their own con- Col. 6 stituencies—in a period of rapidly rising prices that it stifled local authority house building schemes. This is the practical experience of so many of us in our discussions with our own local authorities. There were few positive measures under way until 1973 to stir local authorities into activity. This has now been changed.

Secondly what can be done to alleviate the immediate problems? Even in the first days of this Government, a clear message has gone out to local authorities to persuade them to build again. The Government believe that the only solution to the problem of the growing waiting list is to build more homes and to build them quickly.

The council house building programme began at long last to revive in the second half of 1973. Approvals for housing authorities and new town corporations for 1973–5,265 compared with a bare 2,000 in 1972—confirm this, and I am glad to be able to tell the Committee that this upward trend has been maintained in the first four months of the current year. But they still do not meet the present need, as I shall indicate shortly.

The Government are therefore urging local authorities to bring forward all the housing schemes they have in the pipeline, and they will do everything in their power to assist in getting these schemes under way so that plans and statistics can be turned as rapidly as possible into bricks and mortar, into homes to meet the needs of our people. Extra resources have been made available to enable local authorities to make an immediate further increase in their house building programmes. Needless to say, the maximum encouragement will be given to them by my Department.

We recognise also that there have been and still are practical difficulties facing local authorities, particularly the problem of obtaining tenders from contractors in the face of the restrictions imposed by the housing cost yardstick. Further, we are embarking on a review of the cost yardstick, and we hope to make an announcement of a new system as soon as possible which will be more realistically linked to the cost of housing.

In the meantime, my Department has been instructed to act with maximum Image Col. 7 administrative speed to iron out obstacles and to obtain approvals for all schemes submitted at reasonable cost. My Department has been pursuing this aim vigorously since the Government took office, and I am glad to be able to tell the Committee that of the 5,265 dwellings already referred to in local authority tenders approved in 1973, construction work has, in fact, started on all but 70. That is an indication of the housing drive that is now taking place. I can also tell the Committee that following an urgent review by my Department it has been established that more than 2,300 dwellings are, in fact, covered by local authority schemes approved in the first four months of this year.

I assure the Committee and local authorities that no administrative problem will be allowed to hold up the crash house building programmes which we would wish local housing authorities to undertake. There will be no red tape, and I have instructed my officials and technical officers to bring every problem that may get in the way of the programme to the attention of my hon. Friend the Member for Merthyr Tydfil (Mr. Rowlands), the Under-Secretary of State. For this purpose I would hope that he is "Mr. Housing" in Wales to ensure that this crash programme really does get under way, as I have already stated.

I believe it my duty, however, to inform the Committee that even with all these efforts we shall be unable to shrug off the legacy of an appalling council house building record this year. We inherited a council house building programme which totalled at the end of 1973 no more than 5,000 properties under construction. That frankly means that we shall be lucky if council house completions this year exceed 3,500 to 4,000. This is woefully inadequate to meet the needs of all those families in the housing queue.

Mr. Roy Hughes: Will my hon. and learned Friend appreciate that this would barely meet the needs of Newport alone?

Mr. Morris: I have already referred to Newport's housing situation—3,000 on the waiting list. I am most grateful to my hon. Friend. This is the dark and appalling picture that we have inherited.

Col. 8 I wanted to make it clear, and my hon. Friend has endorsed my remarks, that this is pitifully inadequate, but it is our start line.

I regret to have to report to the Committee that if the picture as far as local authority housing is concerned is gloomy, the prospects facing the private housing sector are equally worrying. Even before the industrial crisis and the three day working week, the writing was on the wall. Starts in the private sector in the last quarter of 1973 were 25 per cent. down on the previous quarter and on the same period in 1972. The effect of the industrial crisis has been to plunge into even worse chaos an already critical situation. In the first two months of 1974 starts fell by over 30 per cent. compared with the same period in 1973.

It is clear that despite the urgent Government intervention to restrain mortgage interest rates and to provide funds for the building societies, this year will be a very bad one indeed for the private sector. We shall be fortunate if half the number of houses are completed in 1974 compared with the total last year.

The Government will do everything in their power to assist private house building in the immediate crisis. The Government have announced a series of interventions to do just that.

As an emergency measure to ensure that urgently needed houses continue to be built, to prevent unsold properties lying empty, and to save many builders from bankruptcy, the Government have informed local authorities that they will assist with the purchase by agreement of sites and homes where appropriate and helpful to meet a local need. I shall be dealing in a moment with the full financial implications of our housing policies as a whole, but I can say now that for these purchases I have agreed with my right hon. Friend the Chancellor of the Exchequer that extra resources will be available to local authorities in Wales. The basic objective will be to obtain the fullest public advantage of empty houses standing on largely unsold private sites and, by agreement, to persuade builders who have land available and who are prepared to build for the local authorities to do as a matter of urgency. Proposals involving some 1,000 Image Col. 9 dwellings have been approved or are under consideration in the Welsh Office.

This is a new field for local authorities, and I would like the message to go from this Committee to the local authorities that it is my wish to encourage them to co-operate with the building industry to ensure that houses and—what I should say are more important than houses—homes do not stand empty or even half built or sites remain undeveloped.

Mr. Tom Ellis: In view of the disastrous situation which my right hon. and learned Friend has just outlined, would he say, so far as concerns liquidity and the building firms which are in serious trouble, what the Government propose to do to ensure that these firms are able to overcome their immediate problems?

Mr. Morris: What I have now spelled out will, I think, be of enormous value to builders as regards liquidity. There are—I have had figures, and my hon. Friend is turning up some of them now —right across Wales these estates built or half-built which remain unsold. People, on the other hand, are dying for homes. If some of these houses can be got off the hands of builders, the money will be in their pockets, they will be liquid and they will be able to continue building again.

What more important step can there be than this to help the builders once again to become liquid?

Sir Raymond Gower: Would it not also be extremely helpful to these private builders on private sites if the Government could provide extra money for the local authorities to advance on mortgage at competitive rates to enable some of these people who want to buy houses to buy them on these estates which, after all, were planned for private occupation?

Mr. Morris: On the mortgage position generally, the hon. Gentleman is aware of what the Government have sought to do and the way in which the Treasury has sought to assist the building societies to ensure that money is available and that the rate does not go up. I want to refer to the question of second homes, which has aroused a great deal of interest and, indeed, burning passion in many parts of Wales. Perhaps it would assist the Committee if I were to run over the ground.

The question is frequently asked whether local authorities can acquire houses which come on the market and might be sold as holiday homes. No new powers are needed to enable them to do this. Part V of the Housing Act 1957 already authorises local authorities to acquire land or houses for the purpose of providing housing accommodation to meet the housing needs of its district. This applies whether there is any second home element or not. The cost of any statutory acquisition of housing will rank in the normal way as reckonable expenditure attracting the full rate of subsidy.

Perhaps it is right to spell that out and, as my hon. Friend the Member for Merthyr Tydfil pointed out in the debate on Monday, it is proposed to give local authorities powers to acquire compulsorily properties within a housing action area, to secure the improvement of the housing accommodation in the area as Image Col. 11 a whole, and the well being of the persons for the time being residing in the area and the proper and effective management and use of that accommodation. While this provision is not specifically directed at second homes, some housing authorities may find the powers appropriate to deal with second homes in certain circumstances.

Hon. Members will already be aware that the Government propose to withdraw all financial support from public funds towards second homes. My right hon. Friend the Chancellor of the Exchequer announced in his Budget Statement that tax relief would no longer be available on mortgages for second homes, and it is specifically provided in the Housing Bill that improvement grants shall not be paid on second homes. That perhaps completes the picture of our proposals.

I come now to the cost of our programme. As the Committee knows, we are faced with a difficult budgetary situation, and the calls on public expenditure are many. However, we have been determined to give housing an overall priority. We have, in fact, increased the public expenditure provision ceiling placed by the last administration on housing. I have agreed with my right hon. Friend the Chancellor of the Exchequer that in 1974–75 an extra £14 million will be made available to local authorities in Wales to increase their housing effort. The larger part of this sum, some £9 million will be earmarked for additional houses, whether by expansion of existing building programmes, or by purchases from private developers. Wales will be receiving its fair share of the additional resources that the Government are making available. I hope that will commend itself to the whole Committee.

I come now to home and community improvements which are so fundamental to the future solution of the Welsh housing problem. The figures speak for themselves. In the 1971 census it showed that nearly 25 per cent. of the housing stock in Wales lacked one or more of the basic amenities. We all know that within this overall figure many of our oldest communities have a much higher proportion. In most valley communities of South Wales, for example, the figures range from 30 per cent. to, in the Col. 12 Rhondda, as my hon. Friend the Member for Rhondda (Mr. Jones) would be the first to tell this Committee, 50 per cent.

I readily acknowledge that one of the very few bright spots in the last administration's housing programme—and I seek to present the facts as fairly as I can to the whole of the Committee—was the boost to house improvement given by the 75 per cent. grant. I am glad that we have been able to make an announcement extending the dealine of 23rd June for at least some of those householders who, through no fault of their own, have been unable to complete the work by the arbitrary deadline imposed under the previous legislation.

Mr. Alec Jones: I know that both sides of the Committee welcome the idea of the 75 per cent. grant, but on many occasions we were given figures of the number of grants approved. Is it possible to have the number of works completed during any period? Otherwise we may be talking about grants which were never really used.

Mr. Morris: My hon. Friend the Under-Secretary of State will check whether we can look at the problem posed by my hon. Friend and see whether we can provide an answer in the course of the debate.

I realise that this announcement will not help all. Let me be perfectly frank on that issue. I believe, however, that it will make a significant contribution to those householders who could have most reasonably expected to have completed the work by 23rd June. As my hon. Friend said in the debate on Monday, we now realise that the general 75 per cent. grant in itself has not arrested the serious deterioration of our oldest housing stock, and new and more drastic measures are required to save our oldest and best loved housing areas and communities. That is why we have retained the concept of the housing action areas, prompted by our predecessors, in the new Bill which has been introduced as a matter of urgency. We felt that there should be no time lapse between the phasing out of the existing 75 per cent. grant system and the introduction of the new system incorporated in the Bill.

My hon. Friend will say a little more about these aspects in winding up. However, I should just like to say this. One of Image Col. 13 the immediate lessons that can be drawn from the painful experience of the last two years is that house improvement is no substitute for house building. I think one of the most bogus statistical tricks that have been attempted, but which have not succeeded, by our predecessors was the totting up of grant approvals on top of house building, as if together they amounted to a massive housing programme. I am sure that in retrospect—and I say this with the utmost of charity —hon. Members opposite must be rather shame-faced about the juggling of these figures. I can tell them that such juggling provided no comfort for those waiting in the growing housing lists for the past two years. I think that is understood right across Wales.

The third question I put in opening this debate was: What are the longer-term lessons to be learned from the sad story of the past three years? Our immediate aim as outlined today must be to get a crash house building programme going, and our longer-term objectives must he to establish a properly planned housing programme for the whole of Wales. Only in this way can we really avoid the painful experiences of the past two or three years. Only in this way can we overcome the practical problems of the house construction industry. Only in this way can we ensure that we do not have a boom followed by a slump process as we have witnessed during these past two or three years.

I am therefore asking all housing authorities to survey, as a matter of urgency, their needs for the next five years, and to inform us what they feel their annual house building rate ought to be over this period. One of the greatest mistakes of the previous administration was to abandon any attempt to forecast and plan a regular programme of house building, particularly in the local authority areas.

I am as conscious as anyone of past failure to make accurate estimates of need, as also the pitfalls in establishing targets. However, what we desperately need now is to establish our medium-term requirements and housing needs—figures not plucked out of the air from suspect global statistics but from proper grass roots assessments of housing needs.

As a result of these assessments made by housing authorities we can really Col. 14 begin to rebuild the firm partnership between Government and local authorities to ensure that the consistent annual housing programmes of authorities are achieved; that land will be available, that the building resources will be organised, that central government priorities on essential services to achieve 'these programmes, such as sewerage, are established in order that together we march in step towards achieving the housing objectives we all share.

We, on this side, regret 'the speculation of the past two or three years in this area of social need and wish to establish housing, not as a purely commercial enterprise but essentially as a social service.

The measures I have outlined today, and those we intend to bring in the coming months and years of this Administration, are designed and aimed to achieve that purpose.

To my hon. Friends and me in the Welsh Office, jobs and homes in Wales are the number one priorities. Provisions of jobs in my case dominate my political life as a constituency member of Parliament. I am equally mindful of the vital importance of homes. What we will do, all of us knowing these needs in our areas, drawing on our experience, I hope, of all my colleagues in the committee, is to use what tools are available to ensure that we provide for those that so desperately need provision.

When the previous Minister of State offered to meet them to discuss the housing cost yardstick, none of the authorities took up the invitation. In our area all authorities agree that it is a lack of builders, contractors and of materials that has been our major problem.

That is why we must study each area with its own particular problem. We must decide the best way of solving the problem in each area, not on a blanket all-Wales basis, but on the basis of the needs and problems of each area. It is not enough to say that we shall build more houses. We have to analyse the situation. We must analyse carefully the reasons for the reduction for a decade in local authority house building starts. There has been a reduction in the number of local authority housing starts in Wales in every year since 1964, and in completions since 1967.

The Secretary of State's analysis was superficial, and in some aspects was simply not borne out by the facts. The danger is that if we on either side of the Committee base our policies on that kind of false analysis, namely, that we are dealing with a two-year or three-year problem that was the fault of the previous Government and not the fault of something much more deep-seated and longstanding, we shall reach the wrong decisions and the people of Wales will suffer. The whole of the right hon. and learned Gentleman's speech was based on that assumption.

Only right at the end did I find a moment for hope, when the Secretary of State suggested that we should base our policies on a grass-roots analysis of needs. If the right hon. and learned Gentleman tears up the rest of his speech and concentrates on that aspect, I believe that there is some prospect of achieving the targets that I know we all wish to achieve.
