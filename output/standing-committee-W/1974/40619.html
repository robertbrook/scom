[Mr. IFOR DAVIES in the Chair]

AGRICULTURE AND RURAL AFFAIRS IN WALES

10.30 a.m.

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. John Morris)
: I beg to move

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the Matter of Agriculture and Rural Affairs in Wales.

I am sure, Mr. Davies, that in due course you will be reminding the Committee of the need for brevity to ensure that as many as possible right hon. and hon. Members can catch your eye. That is my aim and I hope to fulfil it, and I shall start by being as brief as I can.

My first happy duty is to welcome my hon. Friend the Minister of State for Agriculture, Fisheries and Food to this Committee. As a Scotsman and an old friend of many of us, he is very welcome. I am not sure what sort of welcome a Welshman would get in a Scottish Grand Committee, but nevertheless he is welcome in our midst.

As a departmental Minister in the Department whose head is jointly responsible with me for agriculture in Wales, I know that my hon. friend will make a most valuable contribution in winding up our discussion if he catches your eye, Mr. Davies, at the appropriate time.

It is a very long time since this Grand Committee turned its attention to the state of agriculture in Wales and rural affairs generally. It is within the recollection of many hon. Members how we sought in opposition, particularly at the turn of the year, to get agriculture debated: to no avail. It was obvious then that difficulties were being stored up for the farming industry. There were clear signs of existing problems and of further troubles ahead. In these circumstances, one need not be surprised that we were not able to get a debate.

Whatever the difficulties of agriculture today and there are difficulties, and there is deep concern, particularly in the livestock sector they do not stem from the decision of the nation on 28th February 4 to turn out the previous Government and leave us to pick up the pieces and get the nation back to work. This has been recognised by representatives of the industry, who, in expressing their complaints about the existing situation, have emphasised that their origins stem from the decisions and activities, or perhaps the inactivity, of our predecessors.

When on taking office I had to decide which matters within my field of responsibility I would deal with directly myself and which I would allocate to my two ministerial colleagues, it was of course natural for me as Secretary of State to choose agriculture as one of the subjects I would handle personally. Because of the depth of my farming background, of which I am very proud, I am sure that the industry would have expected nothing else.

It goes without saying that I have a deep conviction of the importance of the industry to Wales, which Alun Mabon described as "crefft gyntaf dynol ryw". If I may translate words which are familiar to so many members of the Committee and to the Ministry of State—the first craft of mankind. Nevertheless, while it is crucial to the prosperity of a great deal of rural Wales, it cannot be the sole answer to its future. Agriculture cannot operate without vital services, and I believe that the problems of rural Wales demand a comprehensive approach. It is our duty to ensure both agricultural prosperity and balanced economic development in other respects so that there will continue to be a living and vital community in the heart of Wales.

My first job as Secretary of State was to open up the channels of communication between the Government and the industry in Wales. Very early on, in the first few weeks, I met the Welsh Council of the National Farmers' Union. In Foreign Office terms, if a diplomatic communique were issued, it would have said in those early days "a full and frank discussion took place ". It certainly was.

Soon afterwards, the leaders of the Farmers' Union of Wales called to see me at my agricultural Welsh Office at Aberystwyth, and they made their views equally clear. Since then, the Welsh Labour Group has been to see me, and I went again to Trawscoed to meet the Ministry's regional panels, consisting of JPEG 5 representatives from both sides of the industry in Wales.

The picture that they painted to me was depressing. Point after point of difficulty was discussed, although there was, as in most agricultural discussions —and I have taken part in many over a period of twenty years—an element of exaggeration and of special pleading. Nevertheless, I recognised the deep anxiety of the industry.

I contrasted this with the high expectations that we had when we entered the EEC, certainly with the "full-hearted consent" of the British agricultural industry. At that time, both in our speeches and in our publications, many of us on this side of the House gave a solemn warning to agriculture in Wales, but it was not heeded.

My sympathies go out to the industry now that it has realised, late in the day, that the streets of Brussels are not paved with gold. It is not agriculture's fault: the responsibility lies with the right hon. and hon. Members opposite to whose siren voices the industry listened and who swallowed a common agricultural policy and turned a deaf ear over many months to the industry's pleas for help to offset rising costs. That did not occur only on 28th February. Rising costs, as hon. Members will recall, were the main topic of conversation at last year's Royal Welsh Show and earlier—certainly for those who visited the show in July.

As I have said, the sharpest anxieties are in the livestock sector, particularly among beef and pig producers. Hon. Members know, however, that the sheep sector of the industry has been remarkably bouyant and I dare say prosperous. My well-informed spies tell me that "we live on the sheep ". That is their comment and criticism of the livestock sector.

The industry has moved from the guarantee system to the more complex support systems of the EEC. On taking office, although the annual review had been completed and the determinations made, we were faced with the problem of making a settlement in the EEC farm price negotiations which would meet the needs of our producers and consumers: although I know that many livestock producers were hoping for, indeed expect- 6 ing, a higher beef price, consumers had already shown that they were not prepared to pay high prices, and therefore it was important to encourage a price level that would keep beef within the limits of the housewife's purse. To have done otherwise clearly would not have been in the consumers' interest; nor would it have been—I think that this should be recognised—in the producers' long-term interests. I know the producers and the long-term problems that they face.

We have also avoided permanent intervention by taking meat off the market, a system that was designed to maintain consumer prices. At the same time, we recognised the producers' needs by increasing the subsidies which our livestock sector received in direct aids and which was not available to producers in other member States of the EEC. In Wales alone these aids will amount to about £11 million per year. I am sure this was a wise decision.

We recognise that the £10 per head increase in the calf subsidy, negotiated as part of the agreement on EEC farm prices, will not solve the immediate difficultits of all sectors of the beef industry. So, too, do we recognise the desire of producers for a greater degree of confidence in the beef sector and we all know that this part of the industry is one that demands a certain degree of confidence.

At this point I should like to ask hon. Members opposite what they would have done. The common agricultural policy has just not worked and its intervention policy in Europe in creating a beef mountain some of which may end up by being sold off at our expense to third countries.

That is why my hon. Friend the Minister of State and I, conscious as we are of these problems, knowing that we are part of the EEC, are concerned to ensure that our negotiations are conducted in such a way as to bring the maximum of benefit both to our producers and to our consumers.

It was because we were conscious of the difficulties faced by pig producers that we arranged for a special subsidy to be paid to them from 25th March. This subsidy represents a major move towards helping pig producers in their difficulties. The changes which we wish to bring about in the operation of the CAP cannot be achieved immediately, but we are nevertheless determined to bring the matter to a satisfactory conclusion.

Mr. Morris

He is pursuing those negotiations in the light of the policy upon which my hon. Friend and I fought the last election. I am confident that at the end of the day the results which he will obtain will bring satisfaction both to my hon. Friend and to myself.

rose—

Mr. Morris
: I have given way twice. I want to ensure that hon. Members on both sides can speak.

My right hon. Friend the Minister will be making a statement on these discussions and their special relevance to beef and pigs in the House this afternoon. I am sure members of the Committee will accept that it would be wrong for me to comment in advance on this.

I now turn to some of the more general problems of rural Wales. A famous report in 1954 stated that in mid-Wales JPEG 9 depopulation was a form of economic adjustment. Those words are written on the hearts of many of us who hail from the heart of mid-Wales. I am determined to take the necessary steps to ensure that this process is permanently reversed.

I have been taking a little time to ensure that the right machinery is devised to put into effect my policies. To be effective, the right policy must be acceptable. I regret very much the passing of the Rural Development Board and, because of this rejection, mid-Wales has lost a good deal of money which could have been injected into it. I want, however, to make it clear that I have no intention of resurrecting the past. Rather, want to build now for the future.

We are, of course, firmly committed to a policy of comprehensive development in rural Wales and to the creation of the machinery need to bring this about.

The last Labour Government established the Mid-Wales Development Corporation and gave it the initial task of doubling Newtown's population. That Government also designated six other growth towns within which industrial and other development is being concentrated, and this itself will bring substantial benefits to neighbouring communities.

I am, of course, only too well aware that progress in the growth towns, with the exception of Newtown, has been disappointingly slow. Factories have certainly been built. Jobs have been provided. But all too often it has proved impossible to build houses on the scale required and provide the associated facilities and services. We are, I believe, at a critical point today in rural Wales. Several of the growth towns are nearing the limit of their capacity to absorb factory developments, given their existing size. Henceforward it will be vital to ensure that labour supply grows in line with factory development. All this can be done only if there is a co-ordinated programme of industrial and housing development. Without this, the growth town policy just will not succeed.

The Committee will know that I came to office pledged to set up administrative machinery which will effectively ensure that the growth towns policy becomes a reality. In consultation with my ministerial colleagues I am considering very urgently what should be done.

10 I think, however, I should sound one note of warning today. I sounded a number of notes of warning generally in the course of my speech when we discussed Welsh affairs on the Floor of the House. It may be that in this field, as in others, the very serious economic situation that faced us as a Government when we took office just over three months ago will limit the scope for action this year. It may be that we shall not immediately be able to do all that we would like. Those are the harsh economic realities of life today. But, having said that, let me again emphasise my firm intention to create the machinery which will make possible the vigorous development of rural Wales. In the meantime, it is vital that we keep up the momentum of industrial development, to the very limit of the growth towns' capacity to absorb new employment.

In this connection, I am glad to be able to announce today two further advance factory building projects in the growth towns. A new 10,000 sq. ft. factory is to be built in Bala and a cluster of nursery units in Brecon. These developments will again be financed by the Development Commission. Coming, as they do, quickly after my announcement of new nursery units at Penrhyndeudraeth and a tenant for the vacant Blaenau Ffestiniog factory—I here pay tribute to the officers of my Department and those of the Department of Industry for the enormous work that was put in to ensure a tenant for this factory—they mark a further step forward in our efforts to inject new life into the growth towns and indeed into the areas as a whole, and as we said in our election document—that is our aim and that is our pledge—to

"implement a co-ordinated strategy that will end the running sore of rural depopulation."

Mr. Peter Thomas
: I, too, should like to welcome the Minister of State to our deliberations here in the Welsh Grand Committee. I think that he will find that we are a very courteous body and that we certainly greatly appreciate visiting Ministers giving up their time in order to help us in our deliberations.

I should like to start by congratulating the right hon. and learned Gentleman on fulfilling in part his expressed hope at the beginning of his speech that he would be JPEG 11 brief. His speech was, indeed, quite short and I therefore hope that I shall emulate his example.

The right hon. and learned Gentleman rightly talked about his association with agriculture in the past. He is by experience—having worked with a farmers' union—and by family association probably better qualified than most of us to speak on Welsh agriculture. That being so, it seems extraordinary that, despite paying lip service to the difficulties which undoubtedly exist in farming in Wales, he appeared to be totally remote from the true state of the farming industry in Wales today.

I was surprised to find that most of his speech was devoted to an apology for the Government's attitude towards the European Economic Community. There appeared to be a gesture of defiance to the farming community in Wales, who were chided for having in the past accepted that there would be benefits for them in the Community. I felt that was the wrong approach.

Eighty-five per cent. of Welsh agricultural gross output is produced in the livestock sector. A large part of that sector today finds itself in a situation which can be described without fear of exaggeration as disastrous. The right hon. Gentlemen may know that we had a Conservative conference at Llandrindod Wells on Saturday. The first debate was on agriculture, and many farmers spoke. I have rarely heard such anxieties expressed. Those anxieties were tinged with extreme bitterness. It is quite clear that the farmers in Wales are feeling very angry indeed. It seems that the right hon. and learned Gentleman has joined the rest of the Government in either failing to understand or, in his case, ignoring the magnitude of the problems the farmers and, ultimately, the consumers face.

Livestock farmers, particularly pig, beef and poultry producers, are now in a serious financial situation. Beef producers on present markets are losing between £25 and £35 on every animal marketed. Pig producers are losing £3 to £3.50 on every pig. Egg producers are losing 6p to 7p—and that is a conservative estimate for some people estimate that they are losing 10p—on every dozen eggs produced. Poultry meat producers 12 in the United Kingdom are losing about £¾million per week.

In the main, as the right hon. Gentleman knows, Welsh farms are small family farms with little capital backing to carry them through these difficulties. At the present time they are suffering a severe crisis. The result is that many are facing bankruptcy, the breeding stock is being depleted, slaughterings are taking place at a frightening rate, and eventually this will hit the consumer who, in due course, will have to pay more than ever.

The right hon. and learned Gentleman said these problems did not begin on 28th February. Of course they did not. Enormous problems were produced by the unprecedented rise in the cereal prices last year, reaching their peak last October. Because of this, pig and dairy producers faced great hardship in the later months of 1973. That is why we as a Government brought forward the Annual Price Review by a full month. That is why we negotiated the biggest award ever on milk, £145 million, which was a substantial help, and saved what would have been a very serious situation for the dairy farmers.

During that time, the end price of pigs hardened in October, November and December of 1973, but deteriorated in January. It was clear that there was a serious situation and that something had to be done about it. My right hon. Friend the Member for Grantham (Mr. Godber), who was then the Minister of Agriculture, was actively engaged in Brussels in securing a settlement that would have brought lasting relief to pig producers.

Of course the right hon. and learned Gentleman is right: the problems existed on 28th February; but they were being tackled. I admit that we left unfinished business for the incoming Government, but our complaint is that that business has remained unfinished. It is all very well for the right hon. and learned Gentleman to try to create the impression that this problem has been caused by our membership of the Community, but that is totally wrong.

The farmers know full well that there can be no doubt about what was the major factor causing today's crisis. It was the visit of the Minister of Agriculture to JPEG 13 Brussels on 21st and 23rd March. I agree that the right hon. Gentleman was facing difficulties when he went to that meeting. He was hamstrung, as indeed is the right hon. and learned Gentleman, by his own and his party's attitude towards Europe. He was also hamstrung by their policy for cheap food and he was, therefore, in difficulty. But the package deal, which he negotiated at that meeting, has now been proved beyond doubt to have been disastrous.

First, he negotiated an increase of only 6.3 per cent. in the guide price for adult cattle; that included a transitional step. The rest of Europe increased prices by 12 per cent. and Ireland by 17 per cent. That has meant increased penalisation for United Kingdom exporters of beef, and increased subsidisation, in the form of transitional compensating amounts, for importers of beef from the EEC to the United Kingdom.

How can United Kingdom produced beef compete with that? Added to that, the Minister for Agriculture dealt a serious almost a death blow, to confidence: he took away all forms of market guarantee. We took away the deficiency payments guarantee on the clear commitment and understanding, accepted by the farmers, that it would be replaced by the Common Agricultural Policy guarantee. The Minister of Agriculture has now taken that away and, for the first time since 1947, there is no guarantee price for beef. The result of the right hon. Gentleman's negotiations in Brussels is lack of confidence and the undercutting of the market. What a contrast that is between the success that we had over the previous years of building up a national beef herd!

The truth is—the right hon. and learned Members cannot blame me for entering the party political arena—that the present Government, supported, I suspect somewhat reluctantly, by the right hon. and learned Gentleman, are playing politics with the nation's food supply. "It is noticeable that, although sow and boar slaughterings dropped in the weeks following the implementation of the subsidy, they have increased since, and it can confidently be said that the breeding herd is now lower than at any previous point in the 1970s."

According to this document, beef, milk, egg and pig farmers are losing money. There is a decline in their stock 16 numbers and in the breeding herd. The document states:

This inevitably means that in a few months food prices will rise steeply, and that there will be

"an increase in food imports of the order of tens, and soon hundreds, of millions of pounds. These trends are inevitable since the deterioration has already gone far. They can only be checked, and eventually reversed, if there is immediate Government action to prevent farmers' losses."

This is what the Farmers' Union of Wales says at the end of the summary:

"Unless Government action is taken soon, 1974 will be a year of suffering for Welsh farmers. Twelve per cent. of farmers left the industry during 1973 in Wales, and present conditions can only aggravate the trend. In rural Wales it already seems inevitable that depopulation will increase and that personal incomes will fall considerably. The only certain way to prevent the further deterioration of the situation is for the Government to take action to ameliorate conditions in agriculture."

There is no doubt that action is needed to recreate the confidence that the industry so badly needs. It must be recreated if British consumers are to be protected from massive price increases in the coming months. The urgency of the situation cannot be overstated. The right hon. and learned Gentleman has spoken about his right hon. Friend's visit to Luxembourg and the statement that he will be making this afternoon. We await that statement.

I have read the Minister of Agriculture's statement to the Council of Agricultural Ministers in Luxembourg yesterday and it is clear from it that he does not have any real answer to the present problem. I repeat that the awful truth is that this minority Socialist Government are playing fast and loose with the country's food supplies to achieve short-term political popularity. But it is only for the short term. In the near future the Government's present agricultural policy will mean shortages and high prices, and it is time that they realised that there are no supplies of cheap food available anywhere in the world and that it is of major national interest for Britain to produce as much home-grown food as possible.

If the right hon. and learned Gentleman does not act quickly —and we pray that it is not already too late—rural Wales will suffer and the consumer will pay.
