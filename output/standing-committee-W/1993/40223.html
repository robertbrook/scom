Education Standards in Wales

10.30 am

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. John Redwood)
: I beg to move,

"That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of Education Standards in Wales."

I wish to pay tribute to the many fine teachers and good schools in Wales. The best are excellent and serve their pupils well. Their students do well at exams, have their minds broadened and stretched and leave well equipped to obtain a worthwhile job or to go on to further study.

I am pleased to report that, following the introduction of the national curriculum and our other reforms—not always welcomed by Opposition Members, who now welcome the progress that we are making—the average standard has been rising. Last year, it rose quite sharply. That is good news for schools, good news for Wales and cause for words of praise and encouragement. [Hon. Members: "Hear, hear."] I am glad that Opposition Members agree.

I suspect that those are not the contentious issues. Today I want to ask some other, more fundamental, questions. Why do some schools do so much better than others? Why do schools that spend least, often do better than those that spend most? What should we expect, especially of our primary schools? In short, what makes a good school in the public sector?

I do not expect opposition today to our aims. We all want better results. I hope that Opposition Members share our desire for four out of five school leavers to obtain five good GCSEs or the equivalent, and for one in two to obtain two A-levels or the equivalent. We want to strengthen vocational as well as academic studies. I expect more lively debate about what goes on in primary schools and how well pupils should do, especially in less well-off areas.

I invite the Committee to consider two contrasts. Cardiff High spends £1,809 per pupil—one of the lowest costs in Wales—and achieves the best results, with 71 per cent. obtaining five good GCSEs.

Mr. Win Griffiths
(Bridgend)
: I am not sure what the Minister's follow-up comparison is, but if it is the same one that has appeared in the newspapers it is obviously not a comparison of like with like. To ignore the social background of the pupils who go to those schools is to do an injustice to the children, the parents and the teachers.

Mr. Redwood
: I agree that we should not ignore the social background. I also agree that we should offer 2 more money, therefore more teaching, to the areas with the greatest problems, but I should like the hon. Gentleman to bear with me as I develop my argument on that point.

At Dynevor school in Swansea, which spends £2,649 per pupil—one of the highest costs in Wales—only 5 per cent. obtain five good GCSEs. Some will say that the difference between the two schools I have mentioned is written in the stars because of the point that the hon. Gentleman made about background, so I shall contrast Dynevor school with Glanafan school in Port Talbot, where 42 per cent. of pupils obtain five good GCSEs and which spends only £2,206 per pupil. Put more bluntly, a pupil at Glanafan school has an eight in 20 chance of opening the door to a good job or university place, whereas a pupil at Dynevor school has a one in 20 chance. The gap between the best and the worst is too great.

When a young soldier was asked recently on television why he was putting himself through gruelling trials to become a commando, he replied, "If you're going to do it, be the best." If a job is worth doing, it is worth doing well. Every day, every week, many people in Wales put themselves to the test in their own way—on the rugby ground, at the village show, in business, on the ice rink or at school. It is a truth that some schools do better than others. That is not a truth that some Opposition Members seem to like, but it is a truth none the less. However it is not a permanent truth; the order is neither pre-ordained nor fixed immutably. If we wish to shape the future, we should gain a good grip on the present. To live in ignorance of the results is to bury our heads in the sand.

Members cavil at the measurement of schools' performance and say that it is not fair, that it does not reveal all that we need to know about a school, or that it does not take into account the background of the children or the prosperity of the catchment area. They sell the students of Wales short by those weasel words.

How can one seriously believe that the absence of a car in the garage at home means that a child cannot add up, that the inability to go abroad on holiday means that a child cannot grasp the rudiments of geography, or that having a father out of work is an incentive to play truant and disregard the educational ladder of opportunity? I challenge the Opposition to deal with their arguments more carefully.

I accept, of course, that some families give more support and encouragement than others and that family break-up puts strains on young children. A minority of parents fail to tell their children that doing well at school matters, but politicians and social workers should not encourage that. If the parents are 4 trapped by unemployment or a lack of skill, there is all the more reason for their children to strive for something better.

We need to return to the basic truth—[ Interruption.]—of an old Welsh saying, that the best weapon is the weapon of education; gorau arf arf dysg.

If children's homework is disrupted by rows or rowdiness at home, maybe the school should stay open for an extra hour to provide a haven in which homework can be done, because it is important that every assistance be given.

Schools inspectors have discovered that, in some of the weaker schools, there are too many deputy heads and senior posts without subject responsibilities and that policy frameworks take priority over classroom teaching. In too many Welsh schools, children are not even taught for the recommended minimum number of hours a week. Why are one quarter of all the lessons in Welsh primary schools adjudged unsatisfactory by the independent inspectorate? Because they break the five rules of good teaching set out by the inspectors, based on their great experience of success in the classroom.

Mr. Win Griffiths
: Can the Minister tell us what progress is being made with training the new inspectors? What percentage of them, before training, have had no direct experience of schools, apart from going to school about 30 or 50 years ago?

Mr. Redwood
: The hon. Gentleman's memory of how inspectors work and are trained is a little hazy, but I shall ensure that the factual question is answered by my right hon. Friend the Minister of State when he replies. I do not know the percentage figure that the hon. Gentleman seeks. I can assure the hon. Gentleman and the Committee that the inspectors achieve very high standards and that they have good information about what works and does not work in the classroom. I value their reports and think that governors and other people interested in education in Wales should also value their reports. The inspectors are independent people who care about standards and they deserve the support of all men and women of good will in Wales, whatever their political views.

The inspectors believe that good lessons have five essential characteristics. First, each group of lessons should "be a positive attack on specified aims or objectives". Secondly, they should include a planned variety. Thirdly, teachers should have clear and high expectations of pupils. Fourthly, teachers should seek the active involvement of their pupils. Fifthly, there should be a substantial and influential response by teachers to pupils' work.

In other words, the inspectors are saying that teachers should know what they are doing, expect 6 high standards, keep the students alert and interested, and mark and comment in detail on pupils' work. Many fine teachers and head teachers throughout Wales are doing that already, but all of them must do it so that all schools and pupils have an equal task.

The situation is good and improving, and in some of the areas with the least satisfactory results in primary and secondary schools the percentages of pupils who attend is higher. An effort is being made to correct the problems that Opposition Members see.

Mr. Redwood

Many authorities are now achieving high levels of nursery provision, especially in Wales, and I do not think that the hon. Lady is correct to assert that Wales is at the bottom of the European pack on the matter. She has provided another example of someone giving deliberate misinformation to the Welsh people.

It is the task of head teachers and governors to encourage the best standards in teaching, to make wise appointments, to back up enthusiastic staff who are delivering and to have a quiet word or two with any who are letting the side down. [ Interruption.] Hon. Members say that that is banal and trivial, but it is important. There are some governing bodies that clearly have not been asking enough hard questions or taking enough remedial action to get the right teaching to the right pupils. I am challenging the governing bodies of that minority of schools that are not doing as well as we would like to get to the bottom of why they are not succeeding and to help to take action to correct the situation.

Mr. Ron Davies
(Caerphilly)
: I must take issue with the Secretary of State because he is now issuing a condemnation of governing bodies throughout Wales. Will he, during or at the end of the debate, publish a list of governing bodies that he feels are not achieving the standards that he is now laying down?

Mr. Redwood
: The Government have already produced a list of schools' achievements. I would like—and I hope Opposition Members would like—some of the schools on that list to do better. I am saying that we all—including the governors—have a role to play in achieving that. If governors of schools where the results are very poor are concerned, I hope that they will ask questions. This is not a banality; it is an important truth about the governors' role and responsibilities.

Mr. Davies
: Name them.

Mr. Redwood
: I have already produced a list of Welsh schools' performances. It is up to those schools' governors to decide whether there is anything that they can do to improve matters by appointing new teachers when the opportunity arises, by encouraging the head teacher to produce better standards or by investigating, with the head teacher, to see what can be changed.

Opposition Members always promote the pathetic fallacy that the only person who can do anything about schools' problems is the Secretary of State for Wales. It is the duty of local education authorities, governors, head teachers and teachers to do what they can to provide the necessary impetus. The governors' role is to praise and promote the school and to ensure that all is praiseworthy. The head teacher is the leader 8 of the school, the standard setter and the first among equals, who must lead from the front and by example. There are many fine examples of that happening in Wales. We need to ensure that it happens everywhere.

I recently visited a small village school, which had 54 pupils, near Builth. I saw good work under way in an atmosphere in which teachers wanted to teach. It was a reminder that arguments about size, structure and money are far less important than what goes on in the classroom and the commitment given by both teacher and pupil. Teaching pupils who do not want to learn is not easy. Stamping out truancy is an important first task. If a child absents himself too often, catching up is difficult, if not impossible. The thread is broken; success is unlikely. The Welsh Office is determined to help schools and LEAs clamp down on truancy. That is why I have taken a special interest in curbing truancy.

I am told that some teachers in England think that to expect seven-year-olds to read 30 basic words is an unreasonable request dreamt up by Ministers. I have a simple answer for them: nonsense. The Sunday Times ran a story about 1,400 primary schools complaining about that—

Mr. Redwood
: If the hon. Gentleman will be patient, I shall explain. I am delighted not to have been told that Welsh schools share the concern. That is a sign that teachers in Wales are aware of the need to teach reading and writing well. I am proud of the fact that they do that and dissociate themselves from crazy views.

I do not profess to know the best method or methods of teaching reading. That is a matter for schools and teachers to decide, based on their experience and training, but I know that seven-year-old Welsh children should be able to read and I believe that some structure in a child's day, with some formal instruction and correction of sloppy work, is essential to success.

I am also told that some teachers in England think that 11 to 14-year-olds should not be expected to read any author who wrote before 1900—whether on a list or an amended list. Those teachers want to deny children the brilliance of the metaphysical poets, the grandeur of Milton, the elegance of the 18th century prose writers, the intensity of the romantics, the 19th century novelists and—if that attitude were to be adopted in Wales—the great Welsh literary tradition. I am pleased to say that, according to my advisers, Welsh teachers are again pioneering a different view and see the importance of great literature to the teaching of English in schools. We cannot afford to say that it is impossible to select three groups of poems or novels from the works of such greats as Donne, Swift, Johnson, Keats, Dickens, Shelley, JPEG 9 Austen, Eliot, Wordsworth, Kipling, and Lewis Carroll. I trust that Welsh teachers appreciate the strength of Welsh tradition, which includes the Mabinogion. I know many Welsh people enjoy reading that.

There are great 20th century writers, but Eliot, Pound and Joyce are even more difficult to understand than the greats of the 17th and 19th centuries. We cannot enthuse a winning generation with the pap of mid-century social commentators and recent popular culture.

If children read badly, they will write badly. If they read and write badly, they will think badly. If young minds are not stretched by complex thoughts, densely written language and wide-ranging vocabulary, they may remain narrow for life. The hon. and learned Gentleman is welcome to intervene on the subject of great modern authors.

Some teachers say, "Don't push the child to do things he can't do." How will he ever learn? I am all for a good project, which can be enriching and encouraging. If a child is to do it, however, it helps if he can read, spell, punctuate, multiply and divide to a good standard.

Some teachers tell me that it is unrealistic to expect punctuation and that it cramps a child's imagination and style. Growing up is about disciplining the mind and the emotions.

In the 1920s and 1930s, when times were hard in the valleys, many showed a fierce stoicism. They valued their independence. Fortunately, times have improved dramatically. We do not have to experience those conditions in an age of many more jobs, housing benefit and income support. It would be a pity, though, to lose that intense spark of dignity that is our inheritance. It is what is needed to drive us to higher achievement in school and college and to keep alive the hope and promise of a better tomorrow.

That is what the Government are doing. The best of Welsh education is excellent. The average standard is rising. Stretching targets have been set and we should all work to hit them.
