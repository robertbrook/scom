HEALTH AND HEALTH CARE IN WALES

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. John Redwood): I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of health and health care in Wales.

It is a great birthday treat for me to have the opportunity to debate health in Wales this morning and local government in Wales in the Chamber this afternoon. What better birthday present could there possibly be for a Secretary of State for Wales? I am sure that hon. Members will enter into the spirit of this morning's proceedings in the traditional way.

My one regret has been to discover that a mistake has been made and Scottish water is being served in Committee. I trust that, in future, we can have good Brecon water or some other excellent brand of Welsh water. We are proud of our water in Wales and would like to drink it to loosen our tongues for this crucial debate on health care.

First the good news—the news that the Opposition will seek to bury or deny this morning. For the first time we have delivered the patient charter guarantees for better and prompt treatment. For the first time no patient is waiting more than two years to receive non-urgent treatment, and nor should he. For the first time, no hip replacement, knee replacement or cataract removal patient is waiting more than 18 months for the operation, and nor should he. All urgent treatment is provided within one month and emergency treatment is, of course, supplied on demand. All those guarantees are being fully met and most patients are treated well within those limits.

We are also beginning to see the tide turn on the increase in waiting lists and on waiting times for people who have been referred to a hospital consultant by their general practitioner. In the six months to March 1994, the out-patient list was reduced by 4,100, which is a 3 per cent. reduction. I am also pleased to announce today that the number of people waiting over three months for their appointments fell even more sharply, by nearly 10,000, which is a 15 per cent. reduction. That is a significant and most welcome trend that I wish to see continue.

Mr. Rhodri Morgan (Cardiff, West): I am grateful to the Secretary of, State for giving those figures to the Col. 2 Committee. I believe that the then Under-Secretary of State for Wales first gave an undertaking on in-patient treatment in 1988, saying that the targets would be achieved in two years, not six years. Will the Secretary of State admit that the fact that the Welsh Office—under pressure from the Opposition—is now seeking to bring in guarantees on the length of wait for first consultation shows that the figures on the length of wait for in-patient treatment are the most open to manipulation of all health service statistics? It is easy to delay a first visit to a consultant until that consultant can guarantee treatment within the maximum waiting time set out in the patients charter. Will the right hon. Gentleman therefore agree that the figures that he has announced do not represent an achievement, but an admission that the figures are widely open to manipulation?

Mr. Redwood: No, I do not agree. It is a real achievement and most welcome to those many people who have received treatment without having to wait as long as people did in the 1970s and early 1980s. I do not know what the then Under-Secretary of State for Wales said, to which the hon. Gentleman referred, but I do know that most people are extremely pleased that we have now hit the targets, and they will be even more pleased that I intend to tighten the targets further and to address the specific point raised by the hon. Gentleman.

I have already announced that, from 1 April 1995, the maximum waiting times will be guaranteed for new referrals and will be measured from the date the patient's GP first sees the patient and agrees that something needs doing. That is another extremely welcome improvement in the guarantees that we offer patients and it directly answers the point raised by the hon. Gentleman. It represents a tightening of the target and a further improvement in the standards of service. It brings the time taken to be seen as an out-patient at a hospital within the measurement of total waiting time. A separate standard of a maximum six-month wait for the first out-patient appointment will still apply. The existing guarantee for uergent treatment will, of course, be retained.

The guarantee in Wales will be the best in the United Kingdom, so let us have some support from Opposition Members for that.

Mr. Gareth Wardell (Gower): Will the Secretary of State, in addressing the issue of urgent treatment, answer the point made in "Protocol for Investment in Health Gain", which was published by the Welsh Office in 1992. Paragraph 268 of that document states:

"The Royal College of Surgeons report suggested that up to 30 per cent. of seriously injured patients who die following admission to A&E do so from potentially treatable causes."

What does the Secretary of State plan to do about that in Wales?

Mr. Redwood: The intention is clear that the ambulance should pick up the accident victim as quickly as possible, and we have set out demanding times that must be met for rapid transfer of the victim to the right place. Of course, victims' injuries are usually assessed from the moment that the ambulance crew first make contact. Once the injured person is in hospital, he or she will be given the best possible medical care.

Image Col. 3 Government policy is that people should have immediate access to the best possible medical care available. That is the intention and I trust that all hospitals will carry out that policy. I am sure that the hon. Gentleman will agree with that policy.

Our success in improving timeliness of treatment should be set beside the undoubted success in treating ever more patients. In the past year an estimated 712,200 people have been treated as in-patients and day cases and 609,800 treated as out-patients—increases of 6.6 per cent. and 4.6 per cent. respectively over the previous year. Those figures paint a picture of a growing service, which is doing ever more for the people of Wales. That sort of picture is never painted by Opposition Members.

New treatments and techniques recently introduced include minimally invasive therapy and patient-controlled analgesia. New and improved hospitals have been opened such as Gwynedd phase II, Ysbyty Glan Clwyd phase II and Brecon War Memorial, and we will soon see the benefits from the new burns and plastic surgery development at Morriston, the new cancer treatment centre at Ysbyty Glan Clwyd and the regional blood transfusion centre at Ynys-y-Plwm.

Mr. Barry Jones (Alyn and Deeside): May I raise the individual case of Miss Vicky Rimmer, who lies desperately ill in Wrexham Maelor trust hospital? She is, I think, 16 years old and a pupil at Hawarden high school. She is being well care for by the medical staff, but it is believed that she suffers from Creutzfeld Jacobs disease—the human form of the disease usually called mad cow disease.

I have informed the office of the Secretary of State that a great deal of distress was caused to the family of Vicky Rimmer—particularly to Mrs. Beryl Rimmer, who looks after her—when a press statement was made by the hospital, without any reference to the family. The consequences of that have been that the family has been upset and concern has spread throughout the community.

Will the Secretary of State look into the circumstances surrounding the press release? Will he guarantee me some form of report that I may give to Mrs. Beryl Rimmer?

Mr. Redwood: My sympathy, along with the hon. Gentleman's goes to the family and the patient concerned. It is our policy that she should get the best possible medical care, and I am delighted to hear his reassurance that that is happening. We hope that progress will be made for that patient. I am very sorry if a press release caused distress. I shall look into that and come back to the hon. Gentleman in the way that he requested. It was not my intention to cause such distress. It is usually better not to comment on individual cases but to consider them after the problem has been sorted out.

General practitioners are improving their quality of service and there has been an expansion of preventive medicine. In 1992–93, 262,319 women in Wales had cervical smear tests, 7,000 more than the previous year. The proportion of children under two years of age being immunised also increased: 96 per cent. were immunised against diphtheria, polio and tetanus, up 2 per cent.; 9 per cent. against measles, again up 2 per cent; and 88 per cent. against whooping cough, an increase of a full 4 per cent. on the previous year. Prevention is better than cure. Col. 4 The health service is not just a sickness service but is a wider health service offering people support and advice on how to sustain or improve their health.

The burden of the Opposition's tack today will be the usual one—that there is not enough money. For this year, I announced to Parliament a massive increase of £100 million, or more than 5 per cent. in the amount of money going to the health service in Wales compared with the previous year. I want all that cash to be used for the benefit of the patient. Within that sum, I have provided more than £40 million for the primary health-care services provided by GPs, general dental practitioners, opticians and pharmacists. More than £56 million has been provided to health authorities. That will secure a significant increase in the number of patients treated and in the quality of health care provided to them.

Mr. Ted Rowlands (Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney): Will the Secretary of State tell us whether he will examine the practice of fundholding? Small GP practices in communities represented by myself and others often believe that they are being discriminated against because fundholders have special access to hospitals. That will result in a two-class system of patients—those who are covered by fundholding practices and those who are not, either through GP choice or because the type of practice has the ability to become fundholding. Will he ensure that no such discrimination takes place?

Mr. Redwood: I do not want a two-tier service and that is not the intention of our policies. Smaller GP practices can get together if they believe that fundholding would greatly advantage them and their patients. We are very happy to offer advice and assistance to GPs in the constituency of the hon. Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney (Mr. Rowlands) if he wants.

Let me explain to the hon. Gentleman why it is not our intention or aim to create a two-tier service. There are high minimum standards of care for everyone in Wales, whether he or she goes to a fundholder or a GP under the more traditional arrangements. The same high minimum standards of service should be available at whichever hospital a patient may attend. But we like to make progress, we like innovation and we like to use the skills of doctors to the best effect. If there are doctors, as there clearly are, who think that they could make a better job of running their own affairs more directly, we are happy to trust them and give them that independence. Their patients are on the whole very satisfied with that approach and it has undoubtedly produced good results. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will consider that with the GPs in his area.

Mr. Allan Rogers (Rhondda): If there are such obvious advantages, will the Secretary of State tell the Committee what percentage of doctors have taken up the option of fundholding in Wales, especially in constituencies such as mine with high morbidity?

Mr. Redwood: About a third of patients in Wales are covered by GP fundholders and we anticipate that next year it will be 40 per cent., so there has been a steady increase. We are offering doctors a freedom. It is good that that many patients are covered by the improved services. But if one believes in choice, one must accept that some people want fundholding and some do not.

Mr. Jonathan Evans (Brecon and Radnor): Does my right hon. Friend share my disappointment at the resistance of Opposition Members to the very clear advantages that come to fundholding GPs? Is not that disappointment underlined by the fact that it is anticipated that by 1996 all practices in Powys will be fundholding, including two run by doctors who are prominent supporters of the Labour party?

Mr. Redwood: My hon. Friend makes a good point. The hon. Member for Rhondda (Mr. Rogers) is muddling the choices. We are allowing doctors to choose how they can best look after their patients. The proof of the pudding is in the eating, and I am sure that patients in my hon. Friend's constituency would agree that services have been improved as a result of the changes above the acceptable level that already existed.

Mr. Rogers: The Secretary of State has condemned himself from his own mouth. He confirmed that what my hon. Friend the Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney (Mr. Rowlands) said was true. If there are advantages to fundholding, there must also be disadvantages for doctors who are not fundholders who, at least, will remain at the same level. The Government are creating a two-tier system of those who have and those who do not have those advantages.

Mr. Redwood: It is clear that Opposition Members do not like choice and do not understand human nature. I believe that the majority of doctors probably could provide an even better service given the extra freedom, but I shall leave it to them to decide. Many doctors are already doing that, but it is conceivable that some would be unable to provide a better service, and that is why we are introducing an element of choice.

I should like to return to talking about the advantages of the extra £100 million.

Mr. Alex Carlile (Montgomery): Will the right hon. Gentleman give way?

Mr. Redwood: Not to the hon. Gentleman because he is extremely discourteous to me and was so again this morning on the radio.

The extra money will reduce waiting lists and serve to introduce new patients charter guarantees on total waiting times. It will also support the employment of a significant level of additional doctors and nurses and ensure improved levels of education and training for all NHS staff. The remaining £4 million has been provided to support the resettlement into the community of long-stay mental handicap patients and to provide other vulnerable members of our society with free milk and vitamin tablets. In prescribing that the funding must be used in those ways, I have helped to ensure a resolution of the problems that hospitals and surgeries have experienced in the past as a result of top slicing—deciding centrally where money will go and taking it away to be used in the Welsh Office, the Welsh Health Common Services Authority and the district health authorities, rather than allowing it to filter down to the people providing patient care. I announced some time ago a change in policy; not top slicing, but slicing the top. I should like to report to the Committee today on the progress that I have made.

Col. 6 Good housekeeping begins at home. I have, therefore, decided not to have two senior posts at the top of the health service in Wales but to amalgamate the post of director of the NHS Wales with a senior civil service position. That has now been done. The amalgamation of the NHS directorate with the Welsh Office health policy staff has enabled me to reduce the number of posts and the overhead costs by about 15 per cent.—a reduction of 30 posts. That is a clear signal to the rest of the health service in Wales that I am serious in wishing to control the administrative overhead.

Mr. Morgan: The Secretary of State knows that there is currently considerable controversy in England about whether members of the Opposition Front Bench with responsibility for health are permitted by the changes in the British constitution to meet the director of the NHS in England, Alan Langlands. The holder of the equivalent post in Wales would have been John Wyn Owen, who is now merged within the Welsh Office. Has the Secretary of State given any thought to whether members of the Opposition Front Bench with responsibility for health, such as the shadow Secretary of State and myself, would be permitted to meet Peter Gregory, who is the director of the NHS in Wales, as well as director of NHS policy in the Welsh Office?

Mr. Redwood: I should consult my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Health before responding to the hon. Gentleman's question. I shall let the hon. Gentleman know whether that would be possible under the rules that commonly apply.

Mr. Morgan: And make them up as you go along.

Mr. Redwood: The hon. Gentleman is being extremely unhelpful. I shall see whether I can be helpful to the hon. Gentleman, but I need to consult colleagues. I shall come back to him on that basis. He will know that I am normally generous in ensuring that Opposition spokesmen are properly briefed when they ask for such consultation to take place.

I wish to see health authorities reduce their headquarters staff to a maximum of 50 for so-called commissioning purposes. Some authorities are already below that figure, and I am not keen for them to increase up to it. In addition, all authorities should limit their non-commissioning staff to the absolute minimum needed to carry out statutory responsibilities. I wish firmly to put that on the record again. The policy is clean district health authorities should cut their commissioning staffs until they are around the 50-post average. They should do that in preparation for geographical amalgamation and merger with the FHSAs.

My right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Health and I hope to bring to Parliament legislative proposals to consolidate the work that we are doing on merging health authorities. In the meantime, I wish to see more joint work undertaken between DHAs and FHSAs. By 30 June, I expect to have received the consultation replies to my proposals for reducing the number of DHAs. I shall consider them carefully. Of course, I am open to persuasion, but my inclination is for a significant reduction in the present number—17 authorities—preferably to fewer than eight authorities.

Image Col. 7 We have offered for consideration a radical reduction to only three authorities, which would have many advantages. The scope for savings would be substantial. The case seems particualrly strong in respect of the two south-west Wales authorities and would reduce the number of authorities from nine to eight. There is also a good case for joint working and preparation between FHSAs and DHAs where that is not already occurring.

There have been comments about the approach in England compared with that which I have adopted in Wales. So the Committee might like to hear what my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Health said outside the House of Commons in a recent speech, in which she made it clear that we have a common interest and a common policy in keeping control of administrative costs.

She said:

"There is always the tendency for bureaucracies to multiply in size unless firmly checked…NHS Executive figures in England suggest considerable variations between Trusts in management costs … The public have a strong and legitimate interest in the size of those management costs.


The Government is undertaking a radical reduction in the layers of NHS administration. We propose to abolish the regional health authorities and allow district and family health services authorities to merge. The NHS Executive will be slimmed down. We are examining the Department of Health itself to ensure that our administation is appropriate to the task it has to discharge without becoming cumbersome.


I expect to see less bureaucracy and less paperwork at every level and more spent on patients."

The message from both of us is the same: good management is essential, but there can be too much of a good thing.

Mr. Allan Rogers: Does the Secretary of State know that I sent him a lengthy document as a contribution towards this debate? As yet, he has not answered the questions that I set out in it. We all support his desire to reduce bureacracy in the health service; will he tell us by how much the bureaucracy in the Welsh Office health division will be reduced?

Mr. Redwood: I have just done that. I said that we were reducing it by 15 per cent. or 30 posts. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will welcome that lead from the centre.

By far the largest block of staff at the centre of the health service is employed by the Welsh Health Common Services Authority. I can no longer support a system in which 1,750 people are employed in that operation at the centre. I have pleasure in announcing my proposals for the authority. For all its non-clinical functions, including purchasing, prescription pricing, estate management and supplies, I intend to implement a vigorous policy of market testing. I wish the trusts to be free to buy the facilities and services that they need.

I hope that the managers in the Common Services Authority will propose plans to take themselves into the private sector, where they can compete against others to offer the best possible quality and lowest possible price for those services that the health servive still needs. In a world in which trusts are doing more for themselves, we should not second-guess them or double-bank the activities through a central Common Services Authority.

Many trusts will find it better to deal directly with suppliers. The residual tasks to negotiate some central Col. 8 contracts to maximise the discounts that trusts enjoy does not require the present number of staff that the authority employs. The clinical activities of the authority, which are important, will become part of the mainstream NHS and the remaining activities will be market tested.

I have imposed a recruitment freeze on administrative and clerical staff in all central units and managed hospitals. It is necessary to offset the increased number of administrative staff required in the trusts. The proposals that I have announced today go further and start to make a good impact on the total number employed as the Common Service Authority reforms go through.

Since introducing a specific ban on replacement or recruitment for administrative staff in DHAs and the Common Services Authority, I have received requests to approve 20 places for staff not assisting directly with patient care. I have rejected eight. That means that natural wastage has reduced the staff by the equivalent of 61 whole-time jobs to compensate for the increase in trust staff over that period as 24 new trusts have been set up.

I am also announcing an initiative to put more clinical staff back on to the wards. As part of the slimming-down exercise in health authorities, together with efforts being made by trusts, there will be an opportunity, for example, for nurses to return to clinical work on the wards and possibly in surgeries. I want to encourage that and I suggest that employers use the clinical trading structure to allow such staff to return to clinical work, possibly on protected salaries. That would give the nurses concerned the opportunity to use their professional skills again and enable hospitals to make better use of staff overall. The same principles would apply to other professional staff. I hope that that will include nurse lecturers in nursing colleges where there are too many staff. I am not discouraging doctors or nurses from entering management—far from it. I am very much in favour of that, but I am encouraging those who wish to return to clinical work when, as a result of slimming down, there is no management job to be done. My Department will be pursuing that actively with employers.

I am continuing my initiative to reduce the amount of paperwork and bumf in the health service. We reviewed the 106 financial and statistical forms sent out to hospitals and cut them by one third, or by 35 forms. We have also made reductions in the size and complexity of many of the remainder. Much of the information previously collected centrally is no longer needed by the Welsh Office in the light of the reforms. Information on the day-to-day running of the hospitals is a matter for the hospitals and not for central Government. I need to know and to report to Parliament on the total money spent and the overall achievement of the system. The sort of forms that have been discontinued include those that collect information on fixed assets in land, buildings and equipment, details of the number of each ambulance crew sent out on specific jobs, details by speciality of the number of patients who return briefly to have dressings changed, the pathology workload in hospital laboratories and the work of opticians who visit hospitals to prescribe glasses to patients after eye operations. I am sure that the health service will welcome that clean out of unnecessary information requirements.

I am also concerned about the amount of paperwork which GPs are expected to complete. Some of it will be inescapable in the interests of properly accounting for Image Col. 9 their time and costs, but GPs are busy people whose primary focus rightly has to be on the treatment of patients. I expect health authorities to recognise that and to take whatever steps are available to relieve the administrative burden. The Welsh Office is playing a crucial part in pushing ahead with that with the joint England and Wales programme to link GPs with the FHSAs by computer. That will enable many of the authorities' needs to be satisfied, reducing the need for flows of paper and special form filling between the two. I want to send an ever clearer message that it is patient care rather than paperwork that should take priority.

The "Caring for the Future" policy which I set out a couple of months ago is an action plan for three years. The central point is that quality should be the prime consideration in all the work of the NHS in Wales. Patients are to be well informed of hospital waiting lists and where they can best obtain the surgery that they need in a timely way. The waiting time information bulletin is being revised to cover more medical procedures and will be made directly available to the public as well as to their general practitioners, who will usually advise and guide them. I shall issue an updated patients charter for Wales in July. The effectiveness of clinical care is being reviewed by the Chief Medical Officer and all units in the NHS will be kept in touch with this work through bulletins issued to doctors. In line with our commitment, the CMO will develop a plan to improve the effectiveness of clinical care and will monitor its implementation. We need doctors to ensure that medical work is truly making patients better.

The Chief Medical Officer's group will also highlight evidence of inappropriate treatment. For example, I am told that D and Cs for women under the age of 40 years of age have shown to be of limited value. In my view, politicians should not intrude into medical judgments, unless they have medical skills of their own, which I do not have. I have no intention of so intruding. I am delighted, however, that the medical profession is developing this important work to ensure that all doctors are up to date in knowing what is the best treatment.

Quality in the health service rests upon the individual actions of thousands of people. I want managers and medical team leaders to make quality the watch word in everything that they do.

Members of the Committee may wish to know a little of how we are progressing with the west Glamorgan and south Glamorgan reviews. I understand that the west Glamorgan health authorities have agreed proposals, following public consultation, on reorganising the service for the people of Neath and Port Talbot. They prefer improved primary and community health services, together with a new hospital at Baglan to replace Neath general and Port Talbot hospitals. I am glad that progress is being made in reaching local agreement. I look forward to seeing the detailed proposals. From what I have heard so far, we may now have the basis for making real progress.

Mr. Gareth Wardell: I am grateful for the Secretary of State's comments on west Glamorgan. I wish to ask one question about the west of the county to which the health authority has responded. I refer to the pay settlement this year, which the Secretary of State announced, and interrelate that with the circumstances in west Glamorgan. If it is a fair assumption that provider units will pay the review body awards by doing extra work, Col. 10 what happens to the non-acute community providers whose work load is constant or dropping and who have no waiting lists? Mr. Redwood: The Government made their general policy clear. We believe that the pay awards can be catered for out of improved efficiency which, I am pleased to say, the health service is recording. I have announced that, overall, we are increasing cash by 5 per cent.—that is, £100 million—and that for the hospital and community health services area in Wales the increase is 4.3 per cent. For general practitioners, dentists and the like, it is 9 per cent. These large increases in cash in a period of low inflation more than take care of the needs of the service and will be allied to the efficiency improvements. The targets that I set for administrative costs add to the efficiency improvements because that money, too, is available for patient care.

Mr. Wardell: I think that the Secretary of State misses my point. In the western part of the health authority in west Glamorgan, there is no additional money for community nursing. How will he adjust the payment—not for general practitioners—in the health service for the community service? I accept the Secretary of State's overall premise. However, my personal reading of the facts is that, for that specific sector, the Welsh Office has made a fundamental mistake in linking productivity and pay. In so doing, it has completely missed the fact that it is not possible to cater for that when there are no waiting lists, such as in the community sector.

Mr. Redwood: I do not share the hon. Gentleman's anxiety about that. We announced a general, fair policy across England and Wales when the pay review bodies announced their finding. Indeed, the policy was set out by my right hon. and learned Friend the Chancellor of the Exchequer in his Budget statement. We believe that the health service is managing and can manage that.

Mr. John Morris (Aberavon): When does the Secretary of State expect to make a decision on Baglan hospital? If he makes a favourable decision, as I hope he will, when would he expect work to start? In view of deep resentment in my part of the world to his original midwifery proposals, will he seriously reconsider alternatives?

Mr. Redwood: I cannot give the right hon. and learned Gentleman a firm date because I have not yet received the proposals from the health authority. However, if the process goes as smoothly as it appears to be going, I shall endeavour to turn the proposals round as quickly as possible and let the constituents of interested Members know the outcome through the usual channels as soon as lean.

Mr. Peter Hain (Neath): To endorse the point made by my right hon. and learned Friend the Member for Aberavon (Mr. Morris), may I press the Secretary of State on two points which I hope to raise later in greater detail if the opportunity arises? Mr. Redwood: I have agreed some words with my advisers ahead of the proposals being made and will now reply to the hon. Gentleman.

In south Glamorgan, the health authorities have not yet submitted their proposals to me. For their part, the council and others involved in planning must take account of the significant difficulties of delivering up-to-date health care and improving local faculties. Health professionals should also understand the ambitions and worries in the local community about Cardiff Royal Infirmary and the University Hospital of Wales.
