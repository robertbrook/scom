Mr. Peter Hain
(Neath)
: I am grateful that you have found it opportune to call me in my first Grand Committee. It reminds me of the statement made by the late Aneurin Bevan, the predecessor of my right hon. Friend the Member for Blaenau Gwent (Mr. Foot). He described how he searched for power from the lowliest constituency party ward meeting right through to when he was a Member of Parliament and Cabinet member, but he was never able to find it. I now realise that he should have been searching for it in the Welsh Grand Committee.

I am honoured to follow in Donald Coleman's footsteps. He was an extremely distinguished Member of Parliament, who not only represented Neath but was Chairman of the Welsh Grand Committee. I am sure that you will follow in his footsteps, Mr. Hughes.

The last time that I was on my feet in this Room was at about 1.30 am, in a Finance Bill debate. I was making a speech about the Conservative proposals to bail out Lloyd's names, among whom are 62 Members of Parliament, including the Secretary of State for Wales. The people of Wales are facing massive recession; unemployment is rising; businesses are closing; bankruptcy is increasing and poverty is apreading. I hope that they will not wake up one morning to find that the Government, including the Secretary of State for Wales, have bailed out Lloyd's names to the tune of hundreds of millions of pounds. That money should be spent on investing in the people of this country, rather than going to the rich and powerful who can rig the rules in their favour and put their hands in the public purse. I hope that that concern will be conveyed by the Minister of State to his right hon. Friend the Secretary of State and the rest of the Cabinet.

We heard from the Secretary of State the familiar litany claiming that the recession has not hit Wales very hard. Indeed, he painted a picture quite unrecognisable to the people who live in Wales and are experiencing the effects of the recession. If there is any substance in the claim that Wales has done less badly than the rest of the United Kingdom, it is justified on only two grounds. First, the impact of the recession has been felt principally, at least in its early period, in the service sector of the economy, and Wales has a smaller proportion of service jobs compared with the south of England where the recession has bitten even deeper. Secondly, over the past 12 years, and exceptionally in the United Kingdom, Wales has practised half-hearted Keynesism, rather than pure Thatcherism or even Majorism. Every week we await with bated breath the definition of Majorism as a further rehearsal, rather than the final performance, takes place. I trust that JPEG 105 when the Minister of State receives the definition of Majorism, he will have it translated into Welsh, although I am sure that the people of Wales will find that just as unpalatable as the English version.

What we have experienced in the past 12 years, accelerating in the past year, is recession biting deeply, notwithstanding the excuses made by the Secretary of State this morning. The number of males in employment in Wales fell by 106,000 between 1979 and 1990, and there has been a further fall this past year. In its May 1991 survey, the Confederation of British Industry reported that employment in Wales had fallen sharply over the past quarter and was expected to continue to fall over the next few months, belying the rather rosy picture painted by the Secretary of State and his Ministers.

South Wales and the coal industry are particularly affected. In Neath, in September there were an estimated 2,500 people employed in coal mining, whereas in September 1989 that figure had fallen to 600. Now, with the closure of Blaenant colliery last year, those jobs have disappeared almost completely, save in a few licensed mines. Will the Minister consider the serious question of the collapse of the coal industry and employment in the industry in south Wales during the Government's reign?

In March 1979, there were 26,400 wage earners on British Coal's colliery books in south Wales. That figure had fallen to 1,281 by 8 June 1991—an astonishing collapse. The claim that it has been offset by the growth of opencast mining does not bear scrutiny, as the number of employees on sites in Wales operated by British Coal fell from 1,940 in 1985–86 to 1,500 in 1989–90. That is a fall of 22.7 per cent., and it shows that the number of jobs available on opencast mining sites has declined while those available in deep mines have all but disappeared.

The deliberate strategy of running down the deep mine industry while encouraging opencast mines, although it has not helped employment, has been paralleled by a ferocious rise in coal imports. In 1979, United Kingdom coal imports totalled 4.375 million tonnes, but by 1990 that figure had risen to 14.783 million tonnes. In 1979, coal imported into Welsh ports totalled 1.17 million tonnes, whereas the 1990 total of 3.59 million tonnes represented a staggering increase of 207 per cent. Employment has been encouraged almost everywhere else in the country at the same time as it has been run down deliberately in the south Wales coal industry.

Neath suffered the closure of the Blaenant colliery last year, with the loss of 518 jobs. That helped to ensure that the unemployment rate in Neath jumped in the 12 months from May 1990 by 731, according to official figures from the Department of Employment, or by 891 according to the Unemployment Unit, which estimates unemployment on the pre-1982 basis. That was before the Government's 30 changes to the method of calculating—or, rather, fiddling—the figures. The official figure shows an increase in unemployment in Neath in the past year of 41.6 per cent., while the Unemployment Unit figures show a 31.7 per cent rise. Both sets of calculations are absolutely staggering and reveal the true nature of the recession that is biting deep into my constituency, just as it is biting into the areas represented by all hon. Members in south Wales, regardless of whether they are Labour or Conservative.

The Government have lost control of the economy and of the battle for full employment. That is why the Secretary of State for Employment produced a package of half-hearted measures this afternoon which, frankly, is chicken feed. It come nowhere near to measureing up to the scale of the problem that the Government are seeking—or pretending—to tackle. They are camouflaging, not solving, the problem by producing short-term gimmicks rather than long-term serious answers to the catastrophe that is gripping Wales and the United Kingdom as a whole. The recession is visible not only in direct employment terms, as revealed by the figures that I have quoted.

Mr. Hain
: I am happy to be interrupted by my hon. Friend the Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rymney (Mr. Rowlands), as he made the point far better than I could have. I hope that the Minister will discuss that point when he replies.

Let us consider the wider picture of the recession. Business failures in Wales are accelerating dramatically. 346 business failures were recorded in Wales during the first quarter of 1991. That is an increase of 51.1 per cent over 1990. Bankruptcies, of which there were 221, rose by 61.3 per cent, and liquidations rose by 35.9 per cent. That is not a picture of a happy JPEG 107 economy rosily rowing into the sunshine, as the Secretary of State wishes us to believe. It is a picture of an economy in desperate crisis. The Secretary of State should concede that.

When does the Chancellor of the Exchequer intend to cut interest rates? The people of Wales want to know because we are suffering from desperately high interest rates. When do the Government expect to negotiate the position of the pound within the European exchange rate mechanism? The artificially high pound is having a dramatic impact on the ability of businesses to survive the recession and recover from it.

The latest survey conducted by the Welsh chambers or commerce and industry shows that the recession in Wales is deepening. While there is some confidence that in 12 months businesses may have turned the corner, the realities of the moment are serious. The chief executive of the Cardiff chamber of commerce and industry, Dr. David Philips, said:

"In the five quarterly surveys with which I have been concerned since I joined Cardiff Chamber, I have never seen such a mass of minus signs when comparing the current quarter with the previous quarter. This result regrettably confirms my suspicions expressed in January that Welsh industrial performance, having held up against the recession in October last, might eventually be fully indistinguishable from the rest of the UK. That has now happened."

That was said by someone speaking not from the comfort of Westminster, but from the reality of business experience.

My hon. Friend the Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney mentioned the problem of training in Wales. Despite what the Secretary of State said this morning and the minuscule measures announce by the Secretary of State for Employment—I nearly said "Unemployment"—this afternoon, training places and provision in the Neath area and west Wales have been cut time and again in the past year. For example, West Wales training and enterprise council reports that, measured in employment training weeks, the provision of employment training for the year 1990–91 was 208,500 training weeks. In 1991–92, just 165,300 training weeks will be provided, a fall of 43,200, or 21 per cent. That is not a sign of health training provision; it is evidence of training cuts.

Similarly, I shall quote last week's statement by the director of personnel of Dyfed county council about training places. He said that there were

"not enough places for youngsters coming out of schools in the summer".

In Neath, and it is perhaps the most criminal cut of all, 38 training places for special needs trainees have been cut. Depsite the fact that the Secretary of State for Employment made a genuflection towards the need for extra special needs training in his statement in the House this afternoon, that will do nothing to meet the crisis faced by those with special needs in West Wales.

The general manager of the West Wales training and enterprise council told me in a letter that the reduction was a result of cuts in Government funding. He wrote:

"The total employment training provision in the West Wales areas has had to be reduced."

That is straight from the horse's mouth. The Minister cannot deny it when he replies. The Minister and his 108 colleague, the Secretary of State, would have painted a more credible picture of upturn out of the gloom of recession if it was not the second time around. We had a massive recession in the early 1980s. Now we are having another one which demonstrates the incredible incompetence and the abject failure of the Government's policies.

Only yesterday, the monthly figures showed a further decline in manufacturing output across the United Kingdom and in Wales. We have the biggest public sector borrowing requirement since the early 1980s, which bodes ill for any Government funding of the proper provision of training and employment that south Wales desperately needs.

The pound lost a pfennig against the deutschmark every day last week. The pound has slipped from fourth to seventh position in the ERM. That is a sign of the weakness of sterling and the failure of the Government's economic policies, which are predicated on a strong pound. The pound now stands at a 15-month low against the dollar in the midst of a massive balance of payments deficit, which will increase with any recovery from the recession.

The story is one of incredible Government failure to address the severe problems that the people of south Wales have faced over the past 12 years. We must have a complete change in policy towards investment in training and employment. Market forces must not be allowed to operate unfettered and unrestrained as though they alone can deliver the employment prospects, the industrial efficiency targets and the strong economy that Wales deserves.

We need a new partnership between Government and industry to provide a better future for the people of Wales. I am confident that that will happen after the general election under a Labour Government.

Wales needs to look forward to the next century. The people of Wales recognise increasingly that they want to be part of the new Europe of tomorrow. That includes the money that has been provided to the Welsh Development Agency. With those figures, we need more than someone to speak up for us in the Cabinet.

As we have heard from other Opposition Members and, for the first time in my memory, from Conservative Members, who unfortunately are not present today, we need a Welsh parliament. If we could coalesce around that aim, we could put forward great ideas whereby Wales joined the future with confidence rather than looking to the past and the disasters of the last Labour Government.
