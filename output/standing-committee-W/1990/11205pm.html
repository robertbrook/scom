EDUCATION AND TRAINING IN WALES

Motion made and Question proposed (this day),

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of education and training in Wales.—[ Mr. David Hunt.]

4.07 pm

Question again proposed.

Mr. Ieuan Wyn Jones
(Ynys Môn)
: This morning I was informing Welsh Office Ministers why so many good, experienced teachers are applying for early retirement. That is worrying, because many are conscientious and experienced teachers. Many who teach children under seven question the need for compulsory testing at seven and publishing the results. That will create problems and confusion in primary schools. I hope that Ministers will review that policy.

There is crisis in many classrooms. We desperately need a new approach that enhances the value of education and of teachers, provides adequate resources for books and buildings, and gives all those involved in education a decent environment in which to work.

Schools should be encouraged to develop vocational skills at an earlier age. Too often children are not given proper advice on the choice of subjects to guide them through their careers. It is the height of folly to ask a child of 13 or 14 to make subject choices without giving that child proper advice and guidance on career prospects. That is one reason why we have a mismatch of skills. Too often children leave school with high educational attainment but with little or no idea of career prospects. We should restructure our career service and enhance the value of careers teachers by giving them proper training.

The careers service in Wales is grossly underfunded and barely exists in some areas. Co-ordination between the career service, local authorities, the training agency, the training and enterprise councils, industry and trade unions must be improved. Careers officers and teachers in our schools are immensely important and should be brought into contact with younger children. That would not hinder the broad-based education that children need, but would allow them to make more effective choices. The Government say that they are about creating choice, but more advice at an earlier age would give children real choice in career prospects. It would also widen their opportunities when they move on to further education, job training or apprenticeships.

I also wish to echo some of what was said this morning about the need to provide Welsh language education throughout Wales. I am sure both the Secretary of State and the Minister of State will know about the efforts being made in West Glamorgan, South Glamorgan and Gwent, 38 and about the cross-border relationships between Powys and Dyfed. Parents' aspirations for their children should be met by all the local education authorities in Wales.

At a recent sitting of the Select Committee on Welsh Affairs, I invited the Secretary of State to consider whether it would be appropriate to transfer responsibility for training to the Welsh Office. The problem is that the Welsh Office has complete responsibility for education in Wales, but only partial responsibility for training. If a coordinated approach involving education, the careers service and training is to be a success, it makes sense that the responsibility for planning should be made at an all-Wales level. I was surprised that the hon. Member for Alyn and Deeside (Mr. Jones) was not prepared to commit the Labour party to that concept. It is not—or should not be—a party matter. The development of a Welsh identity in education and training crosses party lines.

I hope that the Secretary of State will consider the need to adopt an integrated strategy for Wales. All hon. Members will wish only the best for the TECs that are up and running in many parts of Wales, but they face a tremendous task and we should not shrink from acknowledging the difficulties that will arise when they are fully operational. One problem, especially in rural areas, will be to ensure that the industrial input into the TECs remains, in accordance with the Government's intention, at the highest possible level. The Government have striven to ensure that that is achieved, but the problem is less acute now, when TECs are in their early stages, than it will be when they have been in operation for two or three years. Will that commitment continue into the future? I doubt whether it will.

It is too exacting to ask leading industrialists in rural areas to devote time to the programme for years. I fear that the Government may be absolving themselves of responsibility for training and transferring it to industry. The problem in rural areas is that industrialists do not have the breadth of experience in training that many of their counterparts in urban areas have. Perhaps we should reconsider the matter in 18 months or two years.

Will the Minister of State deal with my intervention this morning during the Secretary of State's speech on funding for education in industry initiatives in rural areas? I have often raised the matter with the Minister of State, not only in Committee but elsewhere. He is also aware of my pleading in favour of funding for a compact scheme in Gwynedd, and he will recall my plea in the January sitting of the Committee. On that occasion the Minister said:

"I agree with much of what the hon. Member for Ynys Môn said about compacts. We are working on them, and I am sure that we shall be making an announcement in the not-too-distant future."—[ Official Report, Welsh Grand Committee, 10 January 1990; c. 64.]

He made an announcement, which we welcome, but it was disappointing because it did not include Gwynedd.

I hope that there will be another announcement shortly. As the Minister of State knows, the local education authority in Gwynedd has worked hard to build links between industry and education. Industry has been encouraged to participate in the initiatives and there are now plans for a new education-business partnership, which may attract funding.

The Minister of State, Welsh Office (Sir Wyn Roberts)
: I hope that I am about to give the hon. Gentleman the kind of assurance that he seeks. It will be for the training and enterprise councils to take forward the proposals for JPEG 39 compacts in future, funded out of the budget for youth training. I understand that in the north-west there is an intention to work closely with the local education authority to develop arrangements for business compacts and partnerships.

Mr. Jones
: I am grateful for that intervention. That information will be welcomed in Gwynedd, where there is great interest in developing such links, getting a seal of approval for existing links, and obtaining funding through the TEC. We shall have to discuss the future development of the scheme with the officials of the north-west Wales training and enterprise council.

I now refer to self-employment and unemployment in Gwynedd. As the Minister knows, there is an excellent record of business start-ups in Gwynedd. A high percentage of people there are setting up in business on their own. However, the cut in funding for employment training that was announced by the Department of Employment in April this year has led employment training managers to question the Government's commmitment to the service that they provide. The reason given for the cut was the drop in the number of long-term unemployed, and the fact that there were other initiatives in which the Government were interested.

To follow on from the point made by the right hon. Member for Llanelli (Mr. Davies), several training managers are now worried because they are not sure whether funding will be available for them to continue their initiatives over the next few years. Will the Minister clarify that?

Many people, especially in colleges of further education which are now involved in business enterprise programmes, have put to me the need for greater flexibility in such programmes. In rural areas a scheme with 10 people on it may be viable. In urban areas one can easily find 20 people to participate, but in rural areas it is difficult to get classes of more than 10 people in higher education colleges. The programme should be made more flexible so that people in rural areas will not miss the training that is necessary for them to become self-employed.

In rural areas, the enterprise allowance scheme has caused problems that affect women who want to return to employment, especially women who want to set up in business on their own. The Minister of State knows that for a person to qualify for enterprise allowance he or she has to be in receipt of benefit. The problem for many women is that they are not in receipt of benefit, and although many women have the skills necessary to start up in business on their own, they therefore do not qualify for enterprise allowance. Many women would welcome financial assistance when setting up in business. I hope that the Government can therefore amend the scheme to ensure that benefit is available for women to develop their business skills.

The Minister should consider the co-ordinated approach to training and education in Wales. For many years Welsh education authorities have been in the vanguard of education. My own county, Anglesey, began comprehensive education, and its education authority has been involved in many initiatives. We have much to contribute to education and training, and I should like to see the Welsh Office taking responsibility for that in years to come.

Some facts have hardly been mentioned in this debate and it is high time that they were. The number of young people enjoying further education has increased by 25 per cent. over the last decade—from 25,000 to 31,000.

There is also the welcome increase in the number of students to initial teacher training courses in England and Wales this autumn. That is 9·4 per cent. up on last year. I am also proud of the progress that we have made with schemes for the local management of schools. In the past, many schools have been severely penalised because historic budgeting has tended to reflect numbers and seniority of teachers rather than the needs of pupils. Local management of schools will rectify that by ensuring a fairer distribution of the available resources. All schools are now funded according to a formula and a school's budget is determined largely by the number and ages of its pupils. Many schools can now decide for themselves how best to use the money in their budget.

I am well aware of the worries that have been expressed about the LMS arrangements and I shall comment on two of them. The change of emphasis in the way that schools are to be funded has raised understandable anxieties in the teaching profession, and there has been lengthy debate about whether schools' salaries should be treated in a different way from other schools' costs.

JPEG 59 I remain convinced that, as teaching costs are by far the largest component of educational spending, it follows that the formula for a school's budget must reflect the average cost of employing teachers, with individual schools being charged the actual cost of the teachers whom they employ. We are prepared to look sympathetically at the problems of schools facing difficulties in the trasition from actual to average salaries, and the schemes that we have approved in Wales reflect that.

I remind the Committee, and especially my hon. Friend the Member for Delyn (Mr. Raffan), that the schemes have been in operation for only eight months. If serious problems emerge they can be taken into account in our review.

My second point relates to small rural schools. The hon. Member for Brecon and Radnor (Mr. Livsey) has been most vociferous in his anxiety and I understand his position. As I have already said, we are committed to pupil-led funding, because it ensures schools will be funded objectively on the basis of their task—educating children. Within the overall objectives prescribed by LMS, we must keep the position of a large number of small schools in Wales, especially those which are sutuated in our rural areas, in the forefront of our minds, Those schools need not face any greater threat from LMS than they did from previous funding methods.

There is ample scope for authorities to protect small schools through their formula. They can take account of the higher unit costs of delivering the curriculum in small schools and the disproportionately high salary costs in primary schools with fewer than 300 pupils. As before, it is for the LEAs to determine the extent to which they should continue to augment the resources of their smaller schools, so that they may continue to provide the excellent service to the local communities that they have provided in the past.

The hon. Member for Brecon and Radnor also raised the issue of surplus places in rural schools. I assure him that our main concern is with the position in the urban areas where real savings are to be made and educational gain achieved by tackling the problem.

All schools are now funded according to a formula arrived at by the local education authority. We stipulated that at least 75 per cent. of the resources allocated to schools must be related to the numbers and ages of pupils. However, each authority—I think that the hon. Member for Pontyridd (Dr. Howells) will be interested in this—was required to draw up its own formula. If that has given rise to the difficulties over funding which were referred to by the hon. Member for Pontyridd, my right hon. Friend is ready to consider proposals for authorities to amend their schemes.

We are continuing to review the implementation of local management and we are prepared to give sympathetic consideration to amendments to schemes. We also need to consider the next steps to maintain the momentum of delegation. To that end, we will be issuing later this month a draft circular to local education authorities and other interested bodies. The circular will, among other things, consult on proposals for increasing the proportion of resources available for schools to mange themselves. We will also put forward proposals for the treatment of those budget heads that result in distortion and diversity between local educatin 60 authorities. In addition, performance-related funding criteria will provide a positive incentive to TECs to meet certain specified objectives, which will indlude targets for participation by people with disabilities.

The Government are continuing to encourage close links between TECs and local voluntary organisations which, working in partnership, will be able to identify and provide for the needs of those members of the community with special needs. We are also taking steps to ensure that people with a mental handicap can lead as normal a life as possible within the community. Under the mental health strategy, the Welsh Office is providing funds to enable local authorities to improve the quality of training given to adults through innovative training schemes, and specialist employment agencies.

Sir Wyn Roberts

Spurred on by the TEC movement, employers are investing more in training than ever before. They are investing some £20 billion in Great Britain as a whole. It is not just the Department of Employment that lies behind the TECs—the idea is very much to involve the private sector and get employers to contribute. That, I am glad to say, they happily do.

The opportunity has been given to employers to demonstrate further their commitment to training through the new standard "Investors in People"; a rising proportion of young people are undertaking further education and training beyond the age of 16 as I mentioned earlier.
