Dr. Dafydd Elis Thomas
(Meirionnydd Nant Conwy)
: I think that I was referring to dripping from the countryside, according to the hon. Member for Clwyd, South-West (Mr. Jones). The reason for the dripping out of people from the agriculture industry is income levels, which have been mentioned. There was a fall in farm incomes of more that 23 per cent in 1990 compared with 1989, and of more than 18 per cent on the 1988 figure. In that period, total farming income in Wales fell by more that 41 per cent. It is relatively worse because of the higher dependence in Wales on the livestock sector. The farm business survey results show that all dairy and livestock incomes fell by 22 per cent last year to £12,000 a year. However, in the hill and upland livestock farm area—the area that I live in and have represented for 17 years—incomes fell by 37 per cent and are down to an average of just under £9,000 a year. Net interest charges of some £4,000 have to be deducted from that figure, leaving a little over £5,000—if that—for the management of investment. Those staggering figures do not allow for the difficult market conditions that farmers had to face during 1990.

We must look to the Secretary of State who is responsible for agriculture in Wales—and to his colleagues in the other agriculture departments to ensure that we have greater stability in the returns from agricultural support policies. In that context, we look to the common agricultural policy and to the points for "modulation"—to use the jargon—which have been produced by Commissioner MacSharry.

I want to emphasise the positive aspects of improving and augmenting farm incomes that could arise out of developing the CAP to give farmers further responsibility for preserving the environment, the landscape and the family farm model of agriculture. We must continue to emphasise the dual role of the farming enterprise, not only in maintaining employment and production in the countryside but in maintaining the environment. We need even more encouragement for extensification of the less intensive production techniques. We must ensure that any income aid measures within the CAP framework—the form is now being discussed—relate to factors such as the size, income and terrain of the 36 region in which the operation takes place. All those objectives, which now form part of the discussion of the MacSharry proposals, can be related to the particular needs of Welsh agriculture.

Finally, there is a need to examine coherently countryside planning policies. The response from agriculture through environmental concerns to a greening of attitudes towards the countryside has been positive. Farmers are willing to enter into a voluntary partnership with conservation and that is evident from the activity on many farms. We need to extend the partnership by making it more attractive for farmers to invest in environmental conservation.

Everyone concerned about agriculture welcomes the shift of emphasis from production-based support to other forms of support through guidance and through payments for environmental conservation consideration. The simple way to augment that would be for environmentally sensitive area-type schemes to be extended to all agricultural land, including national parks and other designated areas. Some members of the Committee were members of the Committee that introduced ESAs. The intention then was to ensure conservation in areas that were not already designated. There is a clear argument for extending that approach to national parks and to the whole countryside to ensure that environmentally friendly policies are pursued everywhere.

It can be argued that payments should be tiered so that they relate better to the income needs of small enterprises. The same can be said to apply to the hill livestock compensatory allowances, where a more tapered form of payment would ensure that they related more closely to the farm's income.

I have emphasised the agricultural position because our countryside policy can never succeed without it. Over the years emphasis has been put on reorganising agriculture and creating viable units, but that has been at a social cost. There has been a relative gain in income levels for production in a period of growth in the industry, but now that spending on the CAP has to be further reduced, the emphasis on units of size and viability needs to be shifted to maintain the support that is also relevant to environmental needs.

The Welsh Office should be well placed to deal with those changes. It should have more input into what is happening in European agricultural policy. That is my final and critical point: I should not want to make a speech that was entirely uncritical of the Government or of the present Secretary of State or his predecessor.

He talked of a market-based rural economy but went on to say that we must spend much more on intervention in that economy. I agree with that for social resasons, but it has not been the stated philosophy of the Government for the past 12 years.

The Secretary of State said that he would give £20 million extra, up to £1 million a day. That is good, but I calculate it to be a 6 per cent increase. If it is an annual amount, it is not keeping up with inflation. Is it in addition to inflation?

I approve of the rural initiative of the Welsh Development Agency, which as my hon. Friend the Member for Alyn and Deeside (Mr. Jones), the shadow Secretary of State for Wales said, was set up by a Labour Government, as were other bodies that had more money given to them.

The rural initiative is overdue, and its efforts must be targeted on the areas most in need. In my area, Colwyn and Denbigh are most in need of such help. The help outlined by the Minister will do little for the rural economy in those areas because farming is the major employer, and the knock-on effects of farming problems are wider than is suggested by the level of farm employment. Seedsmen, agricultural merchants, repair men, providers of machinery and, 38 as my hon. Friend the Member for Pontypridd (Dr. Howells) says, lorry drivers would be affected.

Unemployment in rural areas is hidden. There is a major recession in the United Kingdom, including Wales. In a recent reply, the Secretary of State was kind enough to tell mne that in January there were 11,383 people claiming unemployment benefit in Clwyd and 101,452 in Wales as a whole. Those figures do not represent the numbers of people who are out of work, which are what I sought. Instead, they are the product of the Government's method of calculating unemployment, which, as all hon. Members know, has been changed 26 times. On only one occasion did a change in the method of calculation increase the figures. Every other change reduced the figures. However, even according to the Government's measure, there are now more than 100,000 people unemployed in Wales. The 1978 total, calculated according to the old rules, was 100,000, so there has been no great success in fighting unemployment in Wales.

Rural unemployment is a different problem. The Government give the figure for the number of people claiming unemployment benefit, but many people who work in agriculture do so on a part-time, seasonal or family basis. They do not receive a full living wage, but retain the hope that they will become farmers in their own right in future. That hope is growing increasingly fainter among the farming community. Conditions in constituencies such as mine are worse than at any time in more than 10 years. Similar areas throughout the United Kingdom are beginning to suffer a decline of catastrophic proportions as a result of the continuing pressures of falling prices, crippling interest rates and high inflation. Admittedly, the weather has played a part, as always in farming, but I do not blame the Government for last year's drought.

To maintain their income they have to intensify their farming and grow more crops because price support is linked to crop production. It is as simple as that. Farmers with only a small acreage do not find that solution helpful. Intensive farming damages their farms. It can harm the natural environment, grub up hedgerows and make larger fields and increase soil erosion. The farm literally slips away. On the other hand, current methods of farm support, which are discredited by consumers and taxpayers, do not even have the effect of supporting their sort of farming. Mr. Alun Michael
(Cardiff, South and Penarth)
: My hon. Friend will run out of hands.

Mr. Jones
: I shall run out of hands as my hon. Friend says.

Farmers have to work with the pressures on them that I have described in a social structure which is disintergrating rapidly. In rural Wales, schools and shops are being closed, bus services are disappearing and even pubs are being shut down. When I had a proper job, I worked in the brewing industry. I consider that if pubs are closing there must be a genuine, unmitigated recession. If prices continue to decline, farmers will be unable to continue in agriculture and will, therefore, be unable to protect the amenity value and visual beauty of the countryside. Things have become worse since October 1990, when draft ewes were selling for £15; in 1989 they made £27 and in 1988 they were £35. In the document "Agriculture in the United Kingdom 1990" the Government's national figures for the United Kingdom show a significant decline over the past three years. Members of the Committee have made that point, but we must continue to reiterate it. In the past farmers have tended to cry wolf, but they now face grave difficulties. The present system of support tends to make the situation worse for the small farmer because it is tied to price support.

Last year, farming incomes in Wales were cut by more than £12 million. In 1989 there was a drop of £20 million. If prices continue to decline and price support is also cut, farming has little future on the hills of Wales. People who are not swelling the unemployment lists and who are hard-working sons and daughters of farmers are keeping family farms going for a pittance, but they may not be able to continue farming in future.

40 The drop in farming prices is bad enough for farmers, but they have had to pay higher prices for machinery and face huge interest rate increases, the poll tax and the uniform business rate which has made it easier to run a business in the south-east of England than a farm in south Wales.

Thanks to deregulation some areas of my constituency no longer have bus services and deregulation was supposed to have the opposite effect. The Government were warned time and again about that. All those factors make the rural situation even more dire. They do not stop there. Water connection charges have quintupled in my area thanks to the privatisation of water companies. Telephone connection charges have also risen. Post offices have been forced to close. The rural environment in Wales and elsewhere is under serious threat. If farmers are driven off the land, as is already happening, whole parishes will become wastelands.

Farmers of family farms in my constituency have told me that they feel powerless and that they have no control over the situation. They see lambs being sold in Tesco for £70 for the equivalent value of the meat, when they received perhaps £20 for the lamb. They see their bills rising remorselessly. They see cattle tied up, as it were, and equipment that they cannot use or employ people to use because they cannot afford it.

What are the Government doing? I welcome the rural economy initiative. It is probably churlish of me to say this—unlike my hon. Friend the Member for Alyn and Deeside (Mr. Jones) on the Front Bench, I can afford to be churlish—but it is not enough. Nor will it affect farming or help the agricultural structure of rural areas. There has been a small increase of 14 per cent. in hill livestock compensatory allowance payments. Conservative Members might say that that is not small, but when one compares it with the 22 per cent. by which farm incomes have fallen in the past 12 months, it does not look big. It is welcome, but not big enough. The standard 1991 price figure for HLCA payments of £142 million throughout the United Kingdom is in real terms the same as that paid in 1988 and less than that in 1987.

The CAP and how we reform it has been discussed by several hon. Members. Everyone agrees that it does not work and does not help our farmers or the consumer. The Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food for England has rightly rubbished MacSharry's proposals. It is fair to say that when the proposals were first leaked and we did not realise what the definition of a family farm or small farm was going to be, he was not wrong to start with the broad view—which many hon. Members accept—that the European Community must seek to stop giving large sums of public money to efficient producers who need no support to make a good living, and find a means of targeting subsidies on selected groups.

JPEG 41 The Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food for England has not come up with any concrete proposals to reform the CAP. He hides behind the fact that such reforms must be agreed with all the other members of the European Community. That may be so, but it seems to me that the farmers of Portugal, Spain, France and Germany do pretty well out of MacSharry's proposals. I suspect that they will continue to do so if the United Kingdom goes into Europe without firm policies and proposals.

Conservative Members have a problem because the Government's philosophy for the past 10 or 11 years has been deregulation and the removal of public intervention. The report of the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food for England on the Government's expenditure plans for 1991–92 to 1993–94—it is the only concrete evidence that the Government intend to reform the CAP—says:

"The Government believes in increasing the role of market forces, reducing support and protection".

Saying that the Government also believe in improving budgetary discipline and management is like believing in motherhood and apple pie—impossible to argue against. However, the Government are talking about market forces and the world market. Even the Minister attending the Select Committee yesterday said that the world market price was a dump price. It is only fair to say to the Secretary of State, who can pass it on to his right hon. Friend the Minister of Agricuture, Fisheries and Food, that the world market price for coal was used to close coal mines.

That could be considered a dump price by South African standards in that the South African Government were dumping coal on the world market. That did not stop the right hon. Gentlemen's Government from closing coal mines and steelworks, and I suspect that it will not stop them from closing farms.

It is all very well for the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food to say that he needs to reform the CAP in combination with his partners. That will effectively mean accepting the MacSharry proposals. I do not disagree with the starting point of those proposals, but we shall be supporting second farmers—in the sense that people have second homes—in tiny farms in Portugal and Spain to the detriment of our own farmers.

If we had proposals that defined a small farm as being less than 150 acres, we might be able to use them. But the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food would not say that. He wants to be like Pontius Pilate and wash his hands of the whole thing. I suspect that when the Government's reforms are proposed—unfortunately for our farmers, they will probably be very similar to the MacSharry proposals—the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food will wash his hands and say, "It's not my fault; it is the fault of all the farmers in Portugal and Spain." The right hon. Gentleman does not give a damn. He and his colleagues did not give a damn when coal mines and steelworks were being closed because of the world market price.

An alternative approach should be adopted, but people who believe in the free market would find it 42 distasteful. Opposition Members believe in support for agriculture, and a way of achieving that without giving most of the CAP money to those who run intervention stores or have huge farms would be to pay direct income aids to farmers who need support the most. We would pay them to keep the countryside pleasant and tidy. That would fulfil the objectives of the CAP and broaden them to include qualitative aspects of food production, including the provision of safe, wholesome food at a reasonable cost, the sensitive management of agricultural land and the maintenance of rural communities.

A green premium could be introduced so that farmers received payments for managing their farms according to agreed criteria and were encouraged to use less intensive farming methods. I am sure that my farming constituents, especially those who wish their families to have a future in farming, would welcome the opportunity to farm in such a sustainable way. They would be able to plan for the future. Young people, who keep down the Secretary of State's unemployment figures by working for next to nothing in farming and making rural areas seem as if they do not have an unemployment problem, will have something to look forward to. Farmers will be able to perform their role as producers of better quality food and as custodians of the countryside. That is the future for rural Wales.

I am glad that we have had this debate. People in rural Wales will be glad to hear that Members of Parliament are worried about their future and they can judge for themselves how best that future can be supported. I wish them well, but I expect that matters will get worse before they get better, unless there is a complete change of policy regarding the common agricultural policy.

That reflects the pressures of the recession and high interest rates on farmers as on other businesses in the rural economy and in towns. The Labour party's critique of the Government's economic policy has been proved right. My right hon. Friend the Member for Islwyn (Mr. Kinnock), the Leader of the Opposition, was right to press for a cut in interest rates. They were cut by only 0.5 per cent—too little and too late for many rural businesses. Greater cuts are needed soon.

The hon. Member for Carmarthen referred to the problem of small farms, which are difficult to define. European farms are much smaller—microfarms in comparison with those of the United Kingdom and even those of Wales. We must defend our small farms—that is the prime responsibility of agriculture Ministers in the Welsh Office. British farms are in danger of drifting yet further in character from those in Europe. Massive agribusiness leaves no room for small farms and family businesses. As my hon. Friend the Member for Carmarthen rightly underlined, Wales is disadvantaged by those factors. The Secretary of State must intervene to improve matters. My hon. Friend the Member for Glanford and Scunthorpe (Mr. Morley) referred in the House last week to the problem of the EC spending £24 billion on the CAP, the bulk of which goes on the storage and disposal of surpluses. He argued that if that JPEG 59 money was to be spent on the CAP, it should be spent, as should our taxes, on supporting small communities and environmental protection, not on the disposal of food mountains. My hon. Friend noted that many farmers in the less favoured areas and uplands do not fit the criteria of the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food for the future of farming. My hon. Friend argued that there was room for traditional small farms. I hope that the Secretary of State for Wales will concur with my hon. Friend rather than with the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food.

My hon. Friend the Member for Caerphilly and the hon. and learned Member for Montgomery were right to emphasise the importance of abattoirs in the food chain. The problem is not only slaughter, but food hygiene and public safety and their place in the farming industry. My hon. Friends the Members for South Shields (Dr. Clark) and for Caerphilly published in the autumn the Labour party's detailed proposals on such issues in a document entitled, "Slaughterhouse Secrets". My hon. Friend the Member for Caerphilly was right to criticise the Welsh Office for its failure to tackle the scandal of having only three abattoirs up to EC standards. We have had time enough to deal with the problem.

In 1974, as a new member of one of the new local authorities, I was involved in a working party that dealt with the problems of abattoirs. At virtually all of our meetings we discussed the ways in which Welsh abattoirs would have to respond to the requirements of Europe. That was 16 years ago, so my hon. Friend the Member for Caerphilly is right to castigate the Welsh Office for not doing enough to resolve the problem. The Secretary of State should bring together industry representatives in Wales as soon as possible to tackle the matter.

The hon. Member for Cardiff, North referred to appeals on planning. I am glad to have his support. I wish that the Welsh Office would show more respect for local views on appeal. The problem is not to do with the number of appeals that have been granted but with the fact that many big appeals have been granted in the past 12 years. That laxity is based on an obsession with unrestrained free market forces and has created worrying pressures on the rural areas around our towns and cities. Those pressures have not been exerted overnight, but are the product of planning consents that have led to a drought in land for development in some places. Greater respect for local authorities in the past would have ameliorated the difficulties.

The hon. Member for Cardiff, North also referred to Rhiwbina Garden Village, which was built in what was then a rural area as part of a good socialist initiative on behalf of local railwaymen. It is an excellent example of collective co-operation, and the exact opposite of the Thatcherite mode of Conservatism of the past decade.

Finally, I must mention the comments of my hon. Friend the Member for Pontypridd (Dr. Howells) and the hon. Member in short trousers—my hon. Friend the Member for Bridgend (Mr. Griffiths). They spoke about the need for strong policies on the 60 green belt and rural areas immediately around our towns. That concept must be developed positively and not simply as a defensive tactic against development. The former Countryside Commission's work was devoted to the idea of a positive, living, community-based countryside. That is what we want around our towns and in our valleys. The countryside should not be a museum but a living environment supporting living communities. I also pay tribute to the Council for the Protection of Rural Wales for its work in trying to unite protection for our rural heritage with the need to preserve its vitality.

The Secretary of State was asked for his assessment of the impact of the current recession on business and employment in Wales. He said that the Government's policy of fighting inflation was working throughout the Welsh economy. We should remember the words of my hon. Friend the Member for Alyn and Deeside, who said that farm incomes have fallen by 23 per cent. That fall has been felt most deeply in rural Wales, which depends on the livestock sector. There must be realism as well as headlines in the attempt to secure the future of all parts of rural Wales. The next Labour Government will work to that end with both realism and idealism. Such a Government will be good news for the people of Wales. To judge from what has been said by Conservative Members today, that Labour Government may well come sooner than had been thought and may even be in power before this Welsh Grand Committee meets again.

Sir Wyn Roberts
: I, too, welcome you to the Chair, Mr. Hughes, and in passing wish to pay tribute to your predecessor. I was very fond of the late hon. Member for Neath (Mr. Coleman), who always reminded me of the marvellous schoolmaster in Goldsmith's "The Deserted Village":

"A man severe he was, and stern to view;


I knew him well, and every truant knew;"—

I very often felt like one of those pupils—

"Well had the boding tremblers learn'd to trace


The day's disasters in his morning face;".

I do not think that the late Donald Coleman would take offence at that small tribute to him.

I am glad that my right hon. Friend's initiative has been so warmly welcomed by hon. Members on both sides of the Committee. I have been present at the launch of several initiatives in my time, but I do not recollect any of them being as universally welcome as this one, and I am sure that that will be the response in all parts of Wales.

It has been clear for some time that the rural economy of Wales was going through a difficult time, in spite of the considerable progress that has been made during the last decade. It is because we appreciated these difficulties that we chose rural Wales as the subject of today's debate. We have been working on this initiative for several months.

We not only wanted to show what has and is being done, to help the rural areas overcome their problems; we also wanted to show our willingness to do more. Indeed, I believe that we have. My right hon. Friend's announcement this morning of an JPEG 61 additional £20 million to be spent by the agencies and the local authorities will put new heart into the rural communities and assure them that the Government are doing their utmost to safeguard their interests.

There have been many references in this debate to the plight of agriculture. The facts about the decline in farm incomes have been rehearsed time and again. My right hon. Friend the Secretary of State said that the Government have increased the hill livestock compensatory allowance to assist farming. All in all, about £100 million a year is provided in subsidies to Welsh farmers.

However, it is a very telling fact that, as my right hon. Friend the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food said in the debate last Thursday,

"more than 80 per cent. of spending on agriculture comes from decisions made in Brussels".—[ Official Report, 14 February 1991; Vol. 185, c.1018.]

Reform of the CAP is therefore central to the future of agriculture. Some of Mr. MacSharry's ideas discriminate against the United Kingdom and must be resisted. Neither the hon. Member for Caerphilly (Mr. Davies) nor anyone else contradicted that. We are not taking the defeatist line apparently advocated by the Liberal Democrats. There must be a realistic prospect of a successful future for Welsh farming, under whatever proposals are eventually agreed upon.

I agree with the hon. Member for Caerphilly that it is becoming increasingly clear that Welsh agriculture must become more market-oriented, and the importance of Welsh Food Promotions cannot be over-stressed. We all wish Alun Evans success in his chairmanship of that body. The development of an effective strategy to promote and market Welsh food products is vital to the future success of farming in Wales.

The hon. Member for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy (Dr. Thomas) spoke about extending environmentally sensitive areas and the greening of HLCAs, which already have a green tint about them. Several hon. Members commented on the abattoir provision in Wales in respect of the EC hygiene standards due to come into effect after 1992. I do not dispute the figures quoted this morning by the Opposition Members, but they did not say that these three EC approved abattoirs already account for about 45 per cent. of the throughput in Wales. A further four abattoirs are currently taking positive steps to upgrade and we expect six more to do so before 1993. Those 13 EC abattoirs would, between them, account for about 85 per cent of the throughput and would undoubtedly have the capacity to increase throughputs above that level.

The hon. Members for Alyn and Deeside (Mr. Jones) and for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy (Dr. Thomas) spoke about housing. In recent years we have made available additional allocations and credit approvals totalling some £2.8 million to rural district councils with severe second homes problems, to enable them to buy dwellings for rent or re-sale or to purchase land for housing development.

The £3 million additional funding announced today replaces the second homes allocation of earlier 62 years. Authorities will be able to make a case for second homes purchase, but the emphasis will be on more innovative and cost-effective approaches to rural needs. Detailed terms will be available to authorities later this week. We shall soon issue planning advice on land for low-cost housing in rural areas, following our consultation last year. Last year Housing for Wales launched a rural housing initiative to assist local people, especially young people, to find suitable homes which they can afford in their own communities. That initiative is being run on a pilot basis in 31 villages across rural Wales, the majority of homes being provided on share ownership terms with a pre-emption right to return the home eventually to the relevant housing association. The capital used in each project can be recycled.

Local authorities can establish low-cost home ownership schemes which allow the home and the subsidy to be recycled for second and subsequent purchasers.

The hon. and learned Member for Montgomery (Mr. Carlile) referred to rail and road traffic in rural Wales. The Government's general view is that public transport services should be provided on a commercial basis, but we realise that in rural and other areas that is not always possible.

The infrastructure costs of rural railway lines in Wales are supported by the public sector obligation grant. Using that support, British Rail has plans to bring more modern rolling stock on to rural lines in Wales. That will include the new 158 units on services between Aberystwyth and Shrewsbury and on west Wales lines; and single car 153 units on the central Wales line.

I too regret the withdrawal of the London-Aberystwyth Cambrian coast express, but the use of 158 units and good cross platform connections into the London services at Shrewsbury should provide a high quality of service with an improved journey time overall. British Rail is reviewing inter-city and regional rail services for North Wales and will announce its plans shortly.

Over the next three years, expenditure on trunk roads of about £550 million is planned, around 60 per cent. of which will directly benefit rural areas. Recent scheme completions include the A48 Nanty-Caws to Coed Hirion and A487 Cardigan Bypass. The bypass for Llanidloes will open shortly.

Updated information on schemes in the forward programme will be included in a supplement to "Roads in Wales", which we plan to publish next week.

On north-south routes, the upgrading of the whole of the A483 or A470 routes to dual-carriageway standard cannot be justified in economic or environmental terms.

Since 1979, more than £50 million has been spent on improvements to the A483, with six schemes under construction or planned, at a cost of over £40 million; more than £100 million has been spent on improvements to the A470 and expenditure on 26 schemes in progress or planned totals a further £100 million.

Transport grant assistance is being given in the 1991–92 settlement for the A497 Abererch to JPEG 63 Llanystumdwy scheme in Gwynedd. Powys county council is working on route improvement proposals for the A44 which will be considered for transport grant in due course. Similarly, Dyfed has been invited to consider the scope for using new route improvement criteria to develop proposals for improving road communication north of Carmarthen.

Of course we all acknowledge the importance of tourism to the Welsh economy. I know that the Wales Tourist Board is working closely with the British Travel Authority and the travel trade on its overseas 64 marketing and exhibition campaign and is even more determined this year.

The WTB has stationed an officer in the United States to encourage holidays in Wales. I know that that officer has a busy programme of meetings and presentations with the travel trade in north America. I wish him success.
