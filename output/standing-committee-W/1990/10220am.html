Mr. David Hunt: I beg to move.

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of rural Wales.

The subject of today's Welsh Grand Committee is rural Wales, which I Welcome. Since my appointment as Secretary of State. I have done my best to travel Image Col. 3 extensively throughout the Principality and have spoken to many who live and work in the rural communities, as well as to others outside the rural communities who care about their future. Together with representations from hon. Members, that has given me a keen appreciation of the great natural assets that we have in rural Wales and of the achievements of the people, as well as the problems and challenges that face them in future. I shall focus my remarks on the latter.

Our long-term policy objective for rural Wales can be stated succinctly as the creation of a self-supporting market-based rural economy. In striving for that, we must acknowledge and respond to the special problems of structure and outlook that have followed many years of debilitating depopulation and to the need for diversification and for measures that also promote the valuable social, cultural and environmental features of rural society.

The main engines for the creation of a self-sustaining economy and the encouragement of enterprise are the Development Board for Rural Wales, the Welsh Development Agency and the Wales tourist board, which work closely with other public agencies, local authorities and those with an interest in rural affairs. They have been responsible for a wide range of job opportunities in rural Wales and played key roles in bolstering the strength of individual communities. I shall deal with some of their activities shortly.

There are substantial efforts and resources currently being devoted to the development of rural Wales. Taking into account the activities of the various agencies, and the contribution towards training, housing provision and the roads programme, the Government's overall commitment is already running at over £300 million per annum. However, the time has come for the Government to take a special initiative to establish with the people of Wales how we can improve on targeting this substantial sum of taxpayers money.

We should not rest there, however. The time is right for fresh initiatives to give impetus to the work of all those involved with the future prosperity of rural Wales. I therefore announce today additional resources of £20 million, bringing spending in rural Wales to £ 1 million per day.

First, I am arranging for a £15 million increase in current provision for rural programmes: an increase in the WDA's funding for local projects, which will increase from £20 million to £25 million the agency's annual budget devoted to rural matters; a new £1.1 million increase in funding for local authority projects by the Welsh Office; projects of regional and national importance support totalling £1.2 million for leisure and sports centre developments at Rhayader and Llandrindod Wells; an increase from £l million to £3 million in the reserve put aside for assisting districts on rural housing matters; and an increase of £6 million by Tai Cymru in its support for housing in rural Wales in 1991–92.

Secondly, I have decided that a further £5 million should be set aside for the support of projects put forward by local authorities which are likely to be of most benefit to the development of rural communities. I shall invite all the relevant authorities to make applications under these arrangements within two Col. 4 months from today's announcement. I intend to impose no restriction on the size and nature of the projects submitted but I hope that the views of local communities will be fully reflected in the choice of projects put forward and that, where appropriate, the authorities will work closely with the relevant agencies, including the DBRW, the WDA and the WTB in drawing up their proposals.

Thirdly, the rapidly changing circumstances of the rural economy; increasing resources devoted to rural issues both by the Government and various agencies; and the emergence of new institutions, notably the training and enterprise councils and the newly-formed Countryside Council for Wales, require a fresh look at the strategies being followed by the various players and the way in which these interact. I shall therefore embark on a series of consultations with all interested parties to seek their views on the right way forward for rural Wales in the 1990s. In the light of those consultations, I propose to publish my objectives in the early summer.
