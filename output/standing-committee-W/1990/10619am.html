The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. David Hunt)
: I welcome the opportunity that the subject provides because the Welsh economy now has a resilience and a strength that Opposition Members would have given their eye teeth to have when they were in Government.

After recent difficulties, business in the Principality is beginning to show signs of renewed optimism. I noted in a press report only last week that the Institute of Directors in Wales had published a business survey showing that 10 per cent more company's directors had expressed optimism about future prospects than was the case six months ago. There has been a decline of 25 per cent in those who say that they are now less optimistic about their prospects. Of the companies that responded to the survey, 40 per cent said that they expect their total employment to increase over the next year. The average expectation of employment was for an increase of 2.1 per cent over that period.

That renewed optimism, as reported by the Institute of Directors, builds on the survey that the Confederation of British Industry conducted in April, which carried the same message. However, Opposition 66 Members should not believe that those facts give rise to Government complacency about the present difficulties. Equally, it is not an occasion to publicise remarks that Opposition Members may make that seek to stress, for political reasons, that the picture is one of doom and gloom. Clearly that is not the position in Wales—[ Interruption.] I know that Opposition Members do not like listening to a series of bull points about the Welsh economy, but having excited the Committee's expectation, I shall outline them anyway. Long-term unemployment, for example, has fallen from 78,000 to under 26,000 since January 1986, a reduction of 67 per cent. That is much greater than the fall for the UK. There are now almost 100,000 more people in employment that there were before the 1983 general election. There are almost 200,000 more civilians in employment. Encouragingly, a large part of that growth was in self-employment, which has increased by about 50 per cent since December 1979.

A recent paper on employment trends in the British engineering industry, produced by the Engineering Industry Training Board, shows that employment in the Welsh engineering sector has increased by almost 14.5 per cent since 1984. It is the only region in the United Kingdom to have experienced growth in that sector over that period. Employment in the Welsh electronics industry has increased by almost 90 per cent since 1978, compared with a fall in Great Britain of around 11 per cent. Employment of professional engineers, scientists and technologists has trebled over that period. The employment of professional engineers, scientists and technologists in the Welsh motor vehicles sector increased 548 per cent; in 1978, there were 44 such senior personnel and there are now 285. In the remainder of the country, there has been a decline of 1 per cent in employment in that sector. The people of Wales know that they have high quality work force and they are prepared to get on with the job.
