Farm and Conservation Grant (Variation) Scheme 1996 (S.I. 1996, No. 230)

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Gwilym Jones)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Farm and Conservation Grant (Variation) Scheme 1996 (S.I. 1996, No. 230).

May I express my pleasure, Mr. Hicks, at seeing you in the Chair so immaculately turned out? It is St. David's day on Friday, and you are wearing a daffodil—that wonderful emblem of Wales. I envy other members of the Committee who may .have attended today's gathering in the Cholmondeley Room for St. David's day. Ministerial duties in Mold meant that I did not have the opportunity to be there, so I admit to having a naked lapel. However, St. David's day is not until Friday.

I welcome the opportunity to present this short and, I trust, uncontroversial statutory instrument to the Committee. The Farm and Conservation Grant (Variation) Scheme 1996 was laid before Parliament on 9 February 1996, and it was considered, and passed, by the Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments on 13 February. The purpose of the scheme is to extend those elements of the farm and conservation grant scheme 1989 that remain extant for a further two years. It will enable capital grants for environmental and conservation measures to continue to be made available to farmers in Wales, until either 18 February 1998 or until such earlier time as alternative arrangements can be put in place to absorb remaining activities within other environmental and management schemes. I shall touch on that aspect later.

It may be helpful if I first explain the background to the farm and conservation grant scheme, which was launched on 20 February 1989, using powers contained in sections 28 and 29 of the Agriculture Act 1970. Those powers restricted the operation of the 1989 scheme to a period of seven years, unless extended by further schemes made under the same powers.

Various elements of the 1989 scheme have been withdrawn over the intervening years. What remains is a valuable combination of features that will continue to attract grant aid. They range from the provision, replacement or improvement of shelter belts for shading stock, the enclosure of grazed woodland to encourage regrowth by excluding livestock and the renovation of traditional boundaries such as hedges and stone walls. They will cover grants for the provision of stiles, gates and way markings for public access and opportunities for the control of bracken and heather. Finally, they will include grants for the repair and renovation of traditional agricultural buildings, using materials that are traditional to the locality.

The measure provides for grants at rates varying between 15 per cent. to 40 per cent., depending upon 4 whether activity takes place on land designated as either outside or inside a less favoured area.

The consequences of extending the farm and conservation grant scheme in Wales would allow other schemes that in part use the same legislation to continue until other arrangements may be made. Those include capital grants for fencing under the water fringe, coastal belt and broadleaved woodland elements of the habitat scheme and the countryside access scheme. Similarly, provisions for bracken control and fencing can be applied under the moorland scheme. In addition, the Countryside Council for Wales uses the legislation for the hedgerow renovation scheme when that operates outside the three pilot Tir Cymen areas.

The Welsh Office will continue to examine all opportunities to achieve simplification in the range of schemes on offer, and to ensure that they are well focused and, wherever possible, integrated. As part of the process, the Welsh Office will, in the course of 1996, review the overall provision of various schemes so as to ensure their cohesive delivery and value for money. That will include the integration of remaining grants available under the farm and conservation grant scheme with other suitable mechanisms.

The scheme has a knock-on effect on rural employment in terms of contract work and other employment opportunities associated with undertaking these measures. Similarly, the rural landscape is rendered more attractive by investment on traditional buildings. Positive environmental management also benefits the tourism industry. The farming industry in Wales has welcomed the scheme's continuation.

There are no public expenditure implications associated with the order. Resources to pay grants exist already within the overall provision for the farm and conservation grant scheme. No additional bids are therefore necessary. I commend the scheme to the Committee.

4.34 pm

Mr. Rhodri Morgan
(Cardiff, West)
: I add my welcome to you, Mr. Hicks, for being selected from the Chairmen's Panel to chair this debate. I congratulate you also on your choice of lapel wear for the day. It matches mine, which tends to roll over because of the inadequacies of its pin. I trust that you will draw that to my attention, if necessary.

As a fellow Celt, Mr. Hicks, it is appropriate that you are chairing the Committee. I am sure that you agree that it would be as well if we got rid of the meaningless Oxbridge claptrap of referring to each other as hon. Members. Perhaps we should just say "me 'andsome" whenever we want to address another member of the Committee. However, that might prevent you from being selected again, so I will not take it too far.

Farmers in Wales want to know why the Welsh Office waited until the previous scheme had virtually expired before renewing it. That has had a considerable effect on the implementation of the old scheme, because people were not assured that there would be an extended scheme. I hope that I can persuade the Minister to get back on his feet after I have spoken—and after others have sought to catch your eye, Mr. Hicks—and explain whether the Welsh Office feels a JPEG 5 sense of shame about having left farmers and its civil servants dangling during the past six months.

The Welsh Office made verbal promises to the farming unions and other organisations that the scheme would be continued, but as the new scheme did not appear, farmers thought that they should rush to make applications. They were not always well thought out, and divisional officers of the Welsh Office Agriculture Department said, "This may not be the greatest scheme that we have ever heard of, but we do not want to be unfair to the farmers." They were not absolutely certain that there would be a follow-on scheme, so they made decisions on the applications rather than saying to the farmers, "Wait until you know for certain that there will be a follow-on scheme for a further two years, then you can submit a well-thought-out application."

Having well-thought-out schemes is at the heart of good conservation of the countryside, which involves the use of traditional materials in agricultural buildings such as old barns and byres, and hedging and walling, shelter belts, enclosing grazed woodlands, controlling bracken expansion and so on. It is essential that those schemes are well thought out in conservation and agricultural terms. Rushed schemes are not a good idea.

The major criticism is that renewing the scheme took an inordinate length of time. We all agree about the merits of the schemes and that by 1998, there will be a need for a proper quinquennial review of the Tir Cymen and the environmentally sensitive area legislation. The rates of grant in less favoured areas, sensitive areas and Tir Cymen will have to be co-ordinated properly. In taking an inordinate length of time to reassure people that there would be a follow-up scheme, the Minister and his Department can accurately be described as having let down the farmers of Wales, who were interested in playing their part. That put the civil servants and farmers under unnecessary pressure. They were informally advised that there would be a follow-up scheme, but that promise needed to be turned into reality several months ago. It is highly unsatisfactory that farmers and civil servants were forced to rush applications and their consideration.

It is sad, but perhaps inevitable, that in this case Wales is one year behind England—that is not a general comment on Wales and England. It happens that the countryside stewardship scheme came in five years ago and the Tir Cymen scheme was introduced four years ago. Obviously, farmers in Wales tend to look across the border and ask, "If we lived in England, would the scheme have been renewed so that we could make our applications after due consideration and with plenty of time to seek advice from civil servants in WODE?" The answer is yes, because the countryside stewardship scheme, although its rules are different, has been renewed. It is only today—late in the day—that this scheme is being extended.

Another puzzle that I hope the Minister will solve is whether, as farmers suspect, the reason for the delay is that the Welsh Office was not sure whether it should try comprehensively to review the whole issue of conservation-related grants on farms by bringing together ESA, LFA and Tir Cymen schemes or simply extend what we have now and perform that major review in two years. Their inability to decide whether to 6 have a comprehensive review or an extension meant that the extension was left until late in the day, which was unfair and incompetent. I do not want to be too hard on the Minister or the Welsh Office Agriculture Department. However, I have tried to convey accurately to the Minister the feelings of fanners in Wales, especially those who want to protect and proficiently farm the countryside. They also want to ensure that the most attractive aspect of the countryside is presented to townspeople, including my constituents, who enjoy playing their part in the countryside. Farmers also want to play their part by restoring hedges, banks and old stone barns, and byres. To do so, however, they expect a reasonable amount of notice from the Government.

My hon. Friend the Member for Pembroke (Mr. Ainger) asked me to send his apologies to the Committee. He had hoped to be here this afternoon, but, obviously, problems associated with the Sea Empress incident kept him away. He wanted to discuss that feature of Pembrokeshire that is a hybrid of a hedge and a bank, and which is known—logically enough—as the hedgebank. Although my hon. Friend is unable to attend the Committee, he wanted to ensure, on behalf of his constituents, that Pembrokeshire's hedgebanks are incorporated into the scheme.

4.41 pm

Mr. Paul Tyler
(North Cornwall)
: I do not want to disappoint our Celtic cousins, but I suspect that the reason that you, Mr. Hicks, and I are wearing daffodils is because we support the relevant charity and because we want to advertise the fact that Cornish daffodils are already in full bloom and that other parts of the country, sadly, are way behind us in climatic terms.

We are, of course, aware of the concerns of our friends in the Principality. As was said earlier, the proposed extension of the scheme mainly concerns Wales. We should recognise, however, that the extension should be viewed in the context of an overall approach to conservation on farms, and that it is therefore relevant to efforts to assist farmers in other parts of the United Kingdom.

Many of us were struck by the fact that in the White Paper on rural policy—which might more accurately be described as a Green Paper—mention was made of such schemes. No doubt a Welsh version of the White Paper will appear in due course. The White Paper states:

"It is probable therefore that there will be a continuing and perhaps increasing need for incentive schemes with specific environmental objectives. We would like to see part of the savings from the reductions in production-oriented support made available for this purpose."

Sadly, however, those schemes give no such encouragement. A diminishing proportion of the total agricultural budget is used for conservation objectives on farms. Those of us who are concerned about the way that agricultural support systems will develop in future are worried about the way that the overall structure of the support system is developing. It seems that neither the White Paper's promise nor the interests of the taxpaying public are being met. However, the proposed extension of the scheme is a modest improvement on the situation that appeared to be developing. As the hon. Member for Cardiff, West (Mr. Morgan) said, the extension has been introduced at the last minute. It is JPEG 7 none the less welcome. A reprieve is always welcome, although it has caused problems for Welsh farmers who did not know what would happen next.

Anxieties have been expressed about the limitations that will result. For example, fanners who have already claimed a grant for effluent disposal facilities, which are extremely important in the dairy and the beef sectors, are worried whether their claims will be disallowed immediately: I believe that they will be. There seems to be a good case for granting a reprieve to those farmers—important environmental consequences would follow.

In the longer term, the abolition of grant aid for effluent disposal is an important matter in Wales and in other parts of the country. Members who represent farming areas know that the tendency has been that while grant was available, those who already had money in the bank were able to make progress with regard to the development of waste water facilities, but those who had no financial support to fall back on were denied any help from grant aid. That issue will have to be revisited.

In the meantime, the powerful advocacy for the scheme's value and for the way in which it is to be varied should be applied this side of Offa's Dyke. I am sure that all hon. Members agree that the Minister made an excellent case for the scheme, but, while we endorse and support his rationale, those of us who represent constituencies in other parts of the United Kingdom must ask, "Why not us?" We all support the concept of subsidiarity for different parts of the United Kingdom, but the onus should be on Ministers to explain why the Welsh are entitled to special treatment, when the same logic is not to be applied elsewhere. I hope that the Minister will indicate that he is prepared to raise that issue with his colleagues in the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food.

While there is to be a transition to the countryside stewardship scheme in other parts of the United Kingdom in due course, we have yet to hear whether the criteria and qualifications are to be as convenient and favourable to farmers as those under the farm and conservation grant scheme. It would appear that greater complexity and more onerous and restrictive conditions are to be the order of the day. That might discourage farmers from undertaking maintenance work on important and valuable conservation features.

All in all, you and I, Mr. Hicks, might be forgiven for having the green eyes of jealousy when we look at what is to continue across the border—albeit for only two years—on the ground that we are to be denied a simple, cost-effective scheme. Even if the Minister cannot give explicit assurances in respect of other parts of the country, I hope that he will convey the views of members of the Committee to his colleagues in MAFF.
