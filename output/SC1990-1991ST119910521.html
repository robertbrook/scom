Draft Broadcasting (Independent Productions) Order 1991

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Peter Lloyd)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Broadcasting (Independent Productions) Order 1991.

May I welcome you to the Chair, Sir Anthony, and, for the convenience of the Committee, give a short introduction to the order? The Committee will recall that the Broadcasting Act 1990 wrote into the law earlier voluntary agreements by the ITV and BBC networks to take at least 25 per cent. of the qualifying programmes that they broadcast from independent producers by the end of 1992. The order defines qualifying programmes as those that the broadcaster commissions or makes alone and those that have been made in collaboration with others, so long as the broadcaster involved has contributed at least 25 per cent. of the cost.

The order makes it clear that qualifying programmes do not include advertising, repeats, new coverage, party political broadcasts, Open university and separate items of less than two minutes' duration. The order also defined independent producers as those programme makers in which no single domestic broadcasting company has more than a 15 per cent. shareholding, and which itself has no shareholding above 15 per cent. in any domestic broadcaster.

That is the nub of the order. I have no doubt that members of the Committee will have some observations to make.

10.31 am

Mr. Robin Corbett
(Birmingham, Erdington)
: May I join the Minister in welcoming you to our proceedings, Sir Anthony? The last time I spoke in the House, I provoked the Chancellor of the Exchequer into saying that, in recession, rising unemployment is a price well worth paying to lower inflation. I hope that nothing that I say this morning will provoke the Minister into making such a callous remark.

The delay in bringing the order is all to the good, because the context in which we discuss it is now clearer, following the blind auction bids for the new Channel 3 licences. Independent producers and their productions have a vital part to play in the development of Channel 3, Channel 5 and the BBC, as was set out in the Broadcasting Act 1990, which fathered this order. That Act provides that not less than—those latter three words are important-25 per cent. of total programme time allocated to qualifying programmes shall be from independent producers.

4 In a lunatic system that time may reveal has invited over-bidding, which will put a later squeeze on what is seen on the screen in terms of real choice, diversity and quality, some bidders will want not to make programmes but to provide the service that they plan to provide solely as publishers on the Channel 4 model. In other words, they will not make programmes, but will commission them from independent companies. That is new, deliberate and has been made possible by the 1990 Act. I believe that the Government have thought too little about the consequences of that on training, regional commitment and the development of in-house talents and skills at all levels, which has been the basis of the present commercial network.

Those companies which hold the present commercial television licences have axed hundereds of jobs, closed studios and put the permanent staff who remian on the short-term contracts. It is never-never land. Employees never know from one day or one series to the next whether they will have a job to go to the next morning. Mr. Will Wyatt, the managing director of BBC Network Television warned:

"The 25 per cent. independent production quota, coupled with Funding for the Future targets, puts a double squeeze on us to achieve the necessary savings.


"We are closing down studios and premises and cutting programme budgets, but we cannot achieve our targets without reducing the number of jobs."

When the previous Prime Minister got a bee in her bonnet that television was rampant with restrictive practices and old Spanish customs based on the former Fleet street model, the Monopolies and Mergers Commission could not please her. It found no such behaviour, but found instead an anxious willingness on behalf of people within the television industry and their unions to be as efficient as possible in the production of quality programmes across the range of tastes and interests.

As has happened elsewhere, what has generated sackings and closures is cash, and the need to save it to fund the sealed bids, which are the basis of awarding the new Channel 3 licences, and also the need to use more independent material. However, the Minister and the Committee should know that there are dangers—I put it no higher than that at present—in what is proposed in the order in relation to qualifying programmes and defining independent producers.

When the two major political parties were persuaded of the value of a 25 per cent. floor for independent productions, it was generally considered that times must change and that there must be room in both the commercial and BBC networks for new talent and ideas. Few people quibble at that. However, it has not worked out like that. Independent producers have been used to squeeze the cost of programming on both networks and, as a result, cheaper programmes have, in the main, been farmed out to independent producers.

I shall not linger over the tales of "sweetheart" deals, especially at the BBC, where staff producers were tempted to cut the knot with a nice little commission in their pockets if they became independent. I shall pass over the heads of various programme sectors who claim the lone right to JPEG 5 decide which independent company shall receive a commission and which shall not, and which companies are not allowed to put in bids or tenders. Why should they have that right? They dictate the price. They have a take-it-or-leave-it attitude as too many independent producers are chasing too little work while the main buyers have their eyes on Channel 3 licences. From October, when the Channel 3 licences are known, that may change. Among others, bank managers know that 700-plus independent production companies in and around London, let alone in the regions, will not survive unless such a practice changes.

I draw the Committee's attention to some other factors. Although independent commissions rose from 1,172 hours in 1989 to 1,301 hours in 1990, the average cost per hour fell from 149,000 to 124,000. The publication Television Week for 5 to 9 May states that the cost of local programmes remains static at about £32,000 an hour. It adds:

"Media analysts claim the fall is due to the disproportionate share of low-cost, high-volume programmes with low profit margins being commissioned from independents."

Moreover, according to Jonathan Davies, managing director of Knowledge Research,

"The bulk of commissions are quizzes and low-cost entertainment. Eight companies produce 65 per cent. of independent programme hours, mainly producing music and game shows … The average cost of an independent programme is around £78,000 an hour but ITV's programmes cannot average less than £100,000."

Therefore, if programme costs are being reduced in the independent sector, why are they not falling with in-house productions? Independent companies have overheads, too. It could be argued that, as they have fewer commissions spread over those overheads, as a proportion the overheads may be the same or even higher than those of commissioning companies. We must consider the discrepancy in cost that does not help the independents, which must invest many hours and much money in the origination and development of ideas for programmes before they can be submitted.

Margaret Heffernan, director the Independent Programme Producers Association summed up the issue when she said:

"We do have a fear, shared by the Office of Fair Trading, that some broadcasters will respond to the 25 per cent. quota by issuing junk commissions, mostly cheap game shows. OFT calls for the 25 per cent. quota to apply across the whole range of output and that means high-class dramas as well. If any broadcaster thinks that cheap programmes are the answer to the quota, they are wrong."

I refer now to the small fine-print provisions, the definition of an independent, and the cross-shareholdings made possible between broadcasters and independents. IPPA complains, rightly, about what it regards as a failure to "trust-bust". The order contains no controls on aggregated shareholdings by coalitions of broadcasters or production companies.

I appreciate that, as the Minister will probably tell me, broadcasters will not directly affect the Channel 3 independent commissions, as those will be arranged by a central commissioning executive under guidance of agreed network policy. However, nothing in the order will prevent the development of dangerous 6 cartels. For example, seven Channel 3 licence holders could each take a 14.2 per cent. stake in five or six independent production companies, each specialising in a particular type of programme. Most independents have a major programme strand. The production companies would be controlled entirely by a group of broadcasters who collectively would control their suppliers and a full range of programming.

The regional structure of Channel 3 network makes such collusion inevitable. If that were to happen—the order contains no anti-trust mechanism to prevent it—real competition to supply programmes to Channel 3 would vanish. The industry would be denied the vigorous competition that the Government say that they want to encourage between broadcasters to get the best programmes at the best price.

That is not the end of the matter. Those five or six independents would still qualify to bid for a slice of producing the 25 per cent. of independent quota of the BBC, Channel 4 and Channel 5. That would put them in a very powerful position to limit access for independent productions to a few powerful players.

The Government have told us that one of the main reasons for the Broadcasting Act 1990 was to break up the monopoly in programme supply. As a result of the order, that ambition will be in ruins. The order will also allow four independent producers each to own less than 15 per cent. of a Channel 3 licence holder—thereby effectively controlling it—and still bid to supply the 25 per cent. reserved for independents, regional programming from that licence, and networking programming for Channel 3. Again, they could try to obtain chunks of the reserved independent productions on BBC and Channel 4. If that were to happen—there is nothing in the order to prevent it—it would fix the market in such a way that it would not be much different from the present setup.

I suspect that the Minister will tell me that if that or anything like it were to happen, the broadcasters and independents concerned would be clobbered by the restrictive trade practices legislation. Given that the order does nothing to prevent that from happening, I do not know how that could be the case. However, if that is what the Minister tells me, I will accept it. If that is not the case, and if such dangerous developments take place between and among broadcasters and independents, will he undertake to introduce an order to sort out the matter?

The Government have moved a long and risky way since they accepted the voluntary agreement defining an independent as one who had no shareholding by a broadcaster. That was cleaner, and left no room for doubt. The order runs higher risks, which were avoidable. There could have been less of a threat to real competition among truly independent companies. IPPA comments:

"It would be nothing short of catastrophic if all the positive benefits brought about by the 25 per cent. initiative over the past 4 years were to be undone at a stroke by the creation of a small number of new production cartels whose structure would be more complex, more slippery and more pernicious than the old ITV/BBC duopoly."

JPEG 7 I do not know whether the Minister is sufficiently concerned to withdraw the order to ponder the issues.

No Opposition Member would criticise him if he did so. [ Laughter.] In these changed times, one never knows, but if he does not, he should understand the real risk that he is taking with an industry in turmoil, faced with less cash for programmes and mounting competition from satellite and cable services.

I refer to two other matters. I wonder why the 25 per cent. quota is based solely on the status of the programme producer rather than on the cost of production which would have been fairer. As I have shown, the cheaper, low-cost, high-volume programmes have been farmed out. It would be fairer if the order specified the 25 per cent. in terms of each broadcaster's annual programme budget across the whole range of qualifying programmes. The present system may well inflate in-house production budgets and reduce overall turnover and value in the independent sector. That may be another reason why the Minister wants to abandon the order before its starts to breathe.

The other hole in the order is that it makes no reference to the status of non-EC production companies as regards the 25 per cent. quota. The general agreement on tariffs and trade may prevent barring non-EC producers, but the existing distinctions between co-productions—which are acceptable towards the quota—acquistiions, pre-sales, output deals and commissions are fast-changing and disappearing. The IPPA fears that the lack of clarification about production costs could enable United Kingdom broadcasters to fulfil their quota by massaging acquisition contracts to make them look like independent commissions.

The danger in that is that the independent quota could be used as an incentive for broadcasters looking for cheap programming to import yet more American and Australian programmes, which would diminish investment in our industry and the possibility of exports, and be culturally damaging to an industry with a good record for quality.

At the end of the day, neither the order nor the 1990 Act is solely about broadcasters and the executives, managers, accountants and advertising salespeople who, as Robert Harris observed in last week's issue of The Sunday Times, will be about the only people with safe jobs in the present set-up. He forecasted:

"The closer you get to a camera, studio or cutting room, the more risky your employment will be."

The order is also about viewers who have a right to rising standards of quality in terms of real choice, range and diversity. The latest Independent Television Commission "Attitudes to Television" survey, like last year's, shows great demand for more of what I described as scatch-your-head programmes—nature and wildlife, plays and drama, drama documentaries, science and technology and adult programmes—and for fewer quizzes, game shows and soap operas. The order will do little or nothing to serve thier proper interests or the Government's proclaimed aspirations.

8 I believe that there are four gaping holes in the order. First, there are no controls over the creation of a dominating cartel of Channel 3 broadcasters controlling most independent commissions. Secondly, there are no controls over a cosy cartel of major independent production companies carving up the 25 per cent. independent Channel 3 quota between them and still being free to bid for space on Channel 4, Channel 5 and the BBC.

Thirdly, there is no 25 per cent. cash share of programme spending to the independents. They may get the hours, but not the money to make the programmes that matter. Fourthly, there is no protection for either viewers or independents from broadcasters making non-EC acquisitions look like independent productions, although they originate in the United States or Australia. In turn, that means less opportunity for our domestic talents, less investment, less for export and less real choice for viewers.

I say again to the Minister that his best course would be to withdraw the order and to allow a period of proper consultation, so that he can better try to get it more nearly right.

Secondly, the independent schedule that will be devised by the central scheduler will operate as the licensees wish. Separate companies have an interest in competing and not in making cartel arrangements, but let us suppose they did so. The scheduler will be obliged to ensure that there is equal access to the network. If there were improper associations, it would be impossible to keep them secret. Moreover, if there were any suggestions that such associations existed, that would trigger an investigation by the OFT, which has a clear mandate under the Act to ensure that competition rules are thoroughly observed.

Gentleman himself gave part of the answer to that rather vexed question when he suggested that there might be some problems with GATT. The ITC, itself as part of its quality threshold, requires that at least 65 per cent. of that which is broadcast by the Channel 3 licensees should be made and commissioned by them.

It is possible, I suppose, that a licensee will consider that it makes sense to commission a programme from the United States. For example, if a United States production company can produce what ITV needs, at a price and of a kind that the licensee considers will fulfil his obligations under the agreement—bearing in mind the quality threshold—that contractor should be free to do so because that will no doubt be in the interests of his viewers. However, it would not be possible, if that were at the back of his mind, for a large proportion of programmes shown on independent television to be acquired products that were made for other markets, whether in Europe or the United States. Part of the arrangements clearly laid down, by the ITC is that 65 per cent. of programming must be originally commissioned material.

I believe that those safeguards meet the hon. Gentleman's anxieties. The changes made by the 1990 Act are fundamental and radical. The hon. Member for Bradford, South also asked about the requirement that no broadcaster can insist 16 on the use of its own production facilities when commissioning a work from an independent.
