VETERINARY SURGERY (EPIDURAL ANAESTHESIA) ORDER 1992

10.30 am

Mr. Ron Davies
(Caerphilly)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Veterinary Surgery (Epidural Anaesthesia) Order (S.I., 1992, No. 696.)

May I first of all welcome the Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food to the Committee. I had a brief opportunity on the Floor of the House to congratulate him on his recent appointment. I am sure that as the years unfold, he will spend many interesting hours in Committee dealing with orders relating to his Ministry. If he continues in the same spirit as he started, he will find that the hours are not too long and can be enjoyable.

Without delaying our proceedings, I should also like to welcome new Members of Parliament to the Committee. I hope that they will enter into the spirit of our debates. I hope also that they will not be too partisan, but hon. Members should have the opportunity to present their views so that we can have informed and lively debate. I am especially glad that I am joined on this occasion by three of my hon. Friends, all of whom are new Members of Parliament and have a special interest in animal welfare. I hope that we shall be able to raise the profile of animal welfare during this Parliament.

I think that we have a Government Whip among us—

Mr. Robert G. Hughes
(Harrow, West)
: Yes, I think that I am a Whip.

Mr. Davies
: Ah, but does the Government Chief Whip think that the hon. Member for Harrow, West (Mr. Hughes) is a Government Whip? As with policemen, it appears that Government Whips are getting younger every day; but that may be a reflection of my own failed aspirations in that regard.

I wish to reassure—[ Interruption.]—Of course, I am happy to take interventions, but they will delay our proceedings, something that I do not wish to do unnecessarily.

I was about to reassure the Government Whip that I have no intention at present of pressing for a Division, but that depends on the Minister. If he is his usual affable, courteous and helpful self, I am sure that we can bring our proceedings to a conclusion without a Division.

I wish to explain why we are praying against the order. It would allow a person who is not a qualified veterinarian to administer an epidural anaesthetic. That operation can be difficult and can cause injury to an animal receiving that anaesthetic. For example, complications can arise if the injection is not made in the right place. It can also lead to paralysis and spinal damage.

4 We must ensure that adequate safeguards are built into the proper procedures. We should also seriously consider the wider issues involved in the process of embryo transfer. I understand, Mr. Knox, that we are dealing with a very specific order and that it would be inappropriate to have too wide-ranging a debate. However, we must take very seriously the fact that administration of an anaesthetic is being taken out of the hands of veterinarians. It will be cheaper and easier to administer, and that will give a green light to people working in genetic research and its commercial application. It will therefore lead to an increase in embryo transfers as well as epidural anaesthetics administered by non-veterinarians.

Embryo transfer, as it is currently conducted, has two purposes. It is an essential part of genetic research, which no Labour Member wants to inhibit because it will allow scientists to improve the genetic material of livestock, and it might also improve the health and welfare of animals.

However, embryo transfer is not only carried out for the benefit of animals; it also has commercial applications. It could improve the quality of breeds by allowing animals of lower quality to give birth to animals of higher quality. That can involve either greater milk production for dairy cattle, or a greater rate of growth or better confirmation for beef cattle. We should be aware of those problems. If we give a green light to the industry to develop new techniques by loosening controls we should ensure that animals are properly safeguarded.

I am not alone in my anxieties about that matter. Francis Anthony, who is vice-president of the British Veterinary Association, asked of embryo transfer and the administration of anaesthesia:

"Why is it that we subject an animal to a procedure in order to make it breed how we want it to breed rather than in the way it is naturally capable of breeding"?

He continued:

"The issue of embryo transfer has not been properly debated by Parliament and the technology appears to be outpacing effective legislative control."

A couple of years ago, The Veterinary Record, which is the journal of the BVA, said:

"To a large extent the pace of the debate on embryo transfer is being set by developments in the technology and pressure for its commercial application. If the veterinary profession does not make a stand on the welfare aspects who will"?

The veterinary profession has made its anxieties known.

There are considerable welfare problems. The process of embryo transfer means that donors of eggs or embryos will be subject to hormone treatment to make them superovulate. That would allow 10, 15 or 20 eggs or fertilised embryos to be collected from an animal.

The recipient—the animal into which the fertilised embryo is implanted—is often inappropriate. That happens most strikingly when a low-quality cross-bred cow is implanted with the fertilised embryo of a beef breed, which is much heavier. To be anthropomorphic for a moment, the Minister could represent the beef breed and my hon. Friend the Member for Nuneaton (Mr. Olner) a slim-line version of the human breed. Clearly, there is a great genetic difference between the two. It would be quite inappropriate if the forebears of my hon. Friend were forcibly implanted with genetic material from the Minister and I am not sure whether the genetic make-up of the people of Nuneaton would be improved. My point is that an unnecessary stress is put on the animal that receives the fertilised embryo.

JPEG 5 There has been a substantial increase in the use of Caesarean sections, which lead to multiple births, which are otherwise rare in cattle. The animal's body is violated to withdraw and to implant embryos. The animal is subject to anaesthesia during that process, which shows that it is a difficult and painful procedure. Animals are not treated in those ways for their benefit. There is no health or welfare benefit to the animal; the procedures are solely for human profit.

There is nothing wrong with that, and we do not wish to stand in the way of progress if there is genuine benefit to the economy, but we have to decide whether the animal's suffering is justified by the economic benefit. Given that both products of the British herd—milk and beef—are in surplus, we must ask ourselves whether we want widespread commercial application of the process and where that would lead us. I believe that it would lead to increasing surpluses.

That point was recognised by Francis Anthony, who said:

"Our members are seeing an increasing number of bad calvings and are having to carry out more caesarians. It is a real welfare problem and we are having to pick up the bits."

We should note that important last point, and the implications as stated by the veterinary profession.

Will the Minister arrange an opportunity in Parliament—not necessarily immediately or in pursuance of the order—for a proper debate on the whole question of the use of this technology, and especially the practice of embryo transfer among cattle? Will he also ensure that a proper code of practice is laid down? The current rules are 10 years old; they need to be updated, and clear and specific. As well as having a debate for our own sakes, we must give clear guidelines to the public, the practitioners and the researchers on how we should proceed.

The preamble to the order refers to

"consultation in accordance with the said section … with the Council of the Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons".

Will the Minister tell us about the progress of those consultations? I understand that the royal college had doubts about whether the order should be laid before Parliament. We know that the British Veterinary Association is worried about it. All hon. Members will have received briefings from the Royal Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals. Compassion in World Farming, a highly respected organisation which has also sent us briefings, has made known its concern, and there is a dispute among members of the royal college.

The college gave an equivocal briefing to at least two Members of Parliament who are members of the council of the college, stating:

"Epidural anaesthesia is an invasion of a body cavity and should therefore be regarded as an act of veterinary surgery that can only be carried out by a veterinary surgeon on the Register of the RCVS."

The Minister was obliged to consult the college. Were its views put to his Ministry, and if so, will he explain how he reconciles with those views his failure to accept the advice, not of the RSPCA or welfare groups, but of the members of the college whom he is required by statute to consult? This is most important.

The Veterinary Record of 17 November 1990 mentioned the position of the royal college. Its leader article quotes the views of the college as follows:

"On the specific question of the administration of epidurals, the working party recommended that the preferred option was for epidural anaesthesia to be administered by a veterinary surgeon".

6 That is a clear and unequivocal statement of the views of the working party. I must ask the Minister to reconcile the conflict between that view and his failure to accept advice.

We need to explore the matter for two reasons: first, because of the welfare problem and the need to ensure that a properly qualified veterinarian is present to make the necessary assessment of the welfare implications and, secondly, because if something goes wrong or the anaesthesia is administered incorrectly, it may lead to collapse of the animal or paralysis. If a qualified veterinary surgeon is not present, how can we ensure that the animal will receive proper veterinary care? I must press the Minister on that.

The Minister may say that the Government have taken account of that because the order states in paragraph 3(4)(b) that an unqualified person, if he has been trained, can administer the anaesthetic

"under the authority of a team veterinarian".

That is a major loophole. What does "under the authority" mean? If I were a team veterinarian, could I say to a colleague, "Yes, that is a suitable donor, you have the stuff, you have been trained, so off you go and do it"? Does the phrase mean that I must be present on the same premises or available at the end of a telephone? Does it mean that I must be available at all or even in the country? Will the Minister clarify the meaning of "under the authority"? Does it mean under the direct supervision or can the authority be passed by verbal authorisation?

Those anxieties are felt by those of us who have the highest concern for animal welfare and by the veterinary profession, and also by the practitioners, those involved in the research and the commercial application of that research. It could be argued that they have a vested interest and I accept that.

I shall quote from a letter in The Veterinary Record of 28 March 1992 from a representative of Coulthard Genetics, which is involved in research. The right hon. Member for Selby (Mr. Alison) is not here, but a couple of Yorkshire Members are present and may be interested to know that the representative is from Selby in north Yorkshire. He is a veterinarian and stated:

"Administration of epidural anaesthesia, flushing of the uterus and implantation of embryos all involve entering a body cavity which is, and should remain, the sole province of the veterinary surgeon. Many of the techniques involved are not only acts of veterinary surgery, but require specialised training and much practice.


Damage can be, and is, caused to the animal if careful manipulation and aseptic techniques are not adopted. Having been involved in the training of intelligent veterinary surgeons and lay personnel, I have seen first hand the problems which can occur through lack of proper training. I have also seen, in other countries especially, the disregard for animal welfare which can occur in these procedures and the poor results obtained where operators have not had the essential veterinary training, experience or a correct code of practice with regard to animal welfare."

He continued:

"In my opinion, a veterinary surgeon well trained in the various techniques, ie, the team leader, needs to be present, not just responsible, when any of these activities are being undertaken.

There it is. I have presented to the Minister what I believe is a wide case. Genetic manipulation is a major development of bio-engineering and biotechnology. We do not wish to stand in the way of that development because there may be necessary benefits, but we must ensure that the welfare of animals who are subject to the procedures are safeguarded.

JPEG 7 I have described anxieties and a range of opinion from responsible welfare organisations, the veterinary profession and practitioners in the field, as well as of Members of Parliament—we all receive representations from people who are concerned about animal welfare. I have made a general case and asked a couple of specific questions. I hope that the Minister will assure the Committee that we shall not today loosen requirements that may lead to widespread abuse of animals which we use for our own purposes.

Mr. Soames
: I am grateful to my hon. Friend. The order covers nothing more or less than epidural anaesthesia. We shall be able to return to those matters if the hon. Member for Caerphilly secures the debate that he wants.

In preparing the regulations, it became apparent that the welfare implications of the procedures could be significant if animals started to strain and became restless while the manipulations were being carried out. Animals vary greatly in their reaction to the manipulations involved and it is just not possible to predict the degree of reaction or which animals may be affected. Once the procedure has begun it is too late to remedy the situation.

That difficulty can be overcome by using epidural anaesthetic. This is done by injecting an anaesthetic solution into the epidural cavity at the base of the animal's spine at a point much lower on the spine than is used in human epidurals. The veterinary advice on this matter is clear: the risk of causing unnecessary pain and suffering in the course of embryo collection or transfer is high enough to justify making epidural anaesthesia mandatory in all cases.

I hope that that will set at rest the minds of all members of the Committee who raised the matter of animal welfare. The requirement is driven entirely by consideration for animal welfare. The requirement does not represent an additional burden on businesses or a change in practices: epidural anaesthesia is already commonly used by veterinarians engaged in embryo transfer.

Community legislation allows technicians working under the responsibility of a veterinary surgeon to collect embryos and it is logical to permit the same person to carry out the entire procedure, including administration of the epidural prior to the collection. However, under the Veterinary Surgeons Act 1966, epidural anaesthesia, as the hon. Member for Caerphilly rightly said, was classed as an act of veterinary surgery which could be performed only by a veterinary surgeon. The Government took the advice of the veterinary profession and the industry as to whether lay persons could or should be suitably trained to perform JPEG 15 epidural anaesthesia. It was the view of the council of the Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons—and I am grateful to my hon. Friend the Member for Hereford for setting that out so clearly—that suitably trained and competent lay persons should be allowed to perform epidural anaesthesia. The council voted in favour of the order—no member of the council voted against it.

The Government therefore concluded, having consulted extensively, that the best solution, in order to implement the directive and at the same time ensure that the welfare of donor and recipient animals was protected, was to make an order under section 19(4) of the Veterinary Surgeons Act 1966 allowing trained lay persons to perform epidural anaesthesia in the course of embryo transfer.

Hon. Members should be aware that Parliament has entrusted the enforcement of ethical aspects of the Veterinary Surgeons Act 1966 to the Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons. The question of whether lay persons should be allowed to perform a task hitherto reserved to veterinary surgeons is basically a matter of ethics, and the decision properly belonged with the royal college. I understand that some members of the veterinary profession believe that epidural anaesthesia should be performed only by a veterinary surgeon. The royal college took account of those views, but—I stress this point again—came to the conclusion that with proper training and veterinary control, it was acceptable to allow lay persons to perform epidural anaesthesia.

I presume that objections to that arrangement are on the grounds of animal welfare. I assure the committee that the order contains specific safeguards to ensure that the welfare of the animals is not prejudiced. Lay persons must follow an approved course in the theory and technique of epidural anaesthesia at a university veterinary school. The hon. Member for Nuneaton raised an important point about training and skills in that regard.

Cambridge university veterinary school has agreed to organise a suitable course, which will be scrutinised by the Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons and my Department. The hon. Member for Caerphilly and my hon. Friend the Member for Norfolk, North-West asked about a code of practice. The royal college and the British Veterinary Association will draw up a code of practice in support of the embryo transfer regulations. If we have a further debate, those matters can be more fully discussed.

Another important safeguard included in the order is that lay persons administering epidural anaesthesia will be under veterinary control. The hon. Member for Caerphilly referred to that. They will be required to work within an approved embryo collection or transfer team, on the instructions and under the authority of the veterinarian responsible for the team. "Under the authority" is a key 16 phrase and I understand the hon. Gentleman's anxiety about its meaning. The team veterinarian will be bound by the rules of the guide to professional conduct issued by the Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons. The guide specifies clearly the responsibilities of veterinary surgeons and we have no reason to believe that it is not satisfactory, although in drawing up the codes we shall ensure that they are enshrined in the proper canons.

Lay persons will be able to summon veterinary help in the case of an emergency and if the team veterinarian is not to be present when the epidural is given, he must have made prior arrangements with another veterinary surgeon to ensure that veterinary assistance is available without delay.

I have listened carefully to the well-informed and coherent arguments of hon. Members, but remain satisfied that suitably trained technicians, working within an approved embryo transfer team and under the instructions and authority of a veterinary surgeon, should be allowed to perform epidural anaesthesia in the course of embryo transfer.

The point at issue remains whether lay persons can be suitably trained to perform epidural anaesthesia or whether the technique is complicated enough or presents such risks to the animal that it should be restricted to veterinary surgeons. The council of the Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons has made the professional judgement that lay persons can be suitably trained. The members of the council were unanimous in that view and I see no reason to challenge it. In making their decision they specified the safeguards regarding training and veterinary control which must be included in the order. I am also satisfied that those safeguards will ensure that the welfare of animals is not prejudiced by allowing lay persons to perform epidural anaesthesia in the course of embryo transfer.

I conclude that the Government took the right decision, with the full support of the Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons in making the Veterinary Surgery (Epidural Anaesthesia) Order 1992 and that the Opposition have no grounds on which to seek its annulment.
