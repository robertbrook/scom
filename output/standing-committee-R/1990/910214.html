CENSUS (CONFIDENTIALITY) BILL [LORDS]

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Health (Mr. Stephen Dorrell)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee recommends that the Census (Confidentiality) Bill [ Lords] ought to be read a Second time.

My first duty is to welcome you back, Mr. Hughes, after a period in which you were not on the Chairmen's Panel. I am very pleased to be sitting on a Committee under your chairmanship.

The Bill represents the final preparations for the 1991 census, which will take place in April. It is important to stress the importance of the census process. It provides us with our only complete record of the makeup of those who live in this country, and it is an essential basis for planning a range of services, especially in the public sector, although information is widely used in the private sector, too.

Mr. Andrew F. Bennett
(Denton and Reddish)
: Will the Minister explain why no mention was made of the necessity for the Bill when the census orders were laid down 13 or 14 months ago? Why is it being rushed through at this late stage? I note from this year's report on statistical information in the Department's booklet that it is anticipated that the Bill will be on the statute book early this year.

Mr. Dorrell
: I would never try to anticipate the decisions of the House. However, it is important that the protection provided by the Bill should be in place before the census begins. It should apply not only to England, Wales and Scotland, but to Northern Ireland under clause 2. The Bill has been introduced because we reviewed the confidentiality assurances under existing law and concluded that further steps had to be taken.

The census is an important process, and the Bill will provide all the safeguards that are necessary to ensure that the information that the public give as part of the census process will be treated confidentially, as they are entitled to expect. It is common ground that, whenever personal information is provided to any agency, the person giving it is entitled to expect that it will be treated confidentially. There are extensive provisions in common law and in other statutes to provide that those who gather information are under an obligation to observe confidentiality.

The census is different from any other information gathering about the population because it is the only time when people are required compulsorily to give information which then becomes the subject of a duty of confidentiality. In recognition of the compulsory nature of the gathering process, the census has always been the subject of particular and specific statutory guarantees on the confidentiality of the information. Historically, such guarantees have rested on two provisions—section 8(2) of the Census Act 1920 and section 2 of the Official Secrets Act 1911.

4 The Committee will know that section 2 of the Official Secrets Act has now been repealed. The review to which I referred revealed that that repeal left two gaps in the confidentiality provisions that the Bill is designed to plug. The first gap arises because section 8(2) of the Census Act 1920 applies only to staff employed in the taking of a census and not to other staff who are employed by the census offices.

The second gap arises because the relevant section of the Census Act 1920 protects only information in the census, and does not protect information given as a result of census tests or census related surveys. The Bill is the result of our recognition that the repeal of section 2 of the Official Secrets Act leave those two specific gaps in the statutory confidentiality guarantees provided by the 1920 Act. We recognise also that, in the special circumstances of a compulsory gathering of information from every resident of the United Kingdom, those gaps must be filled and adequate statutory guarantees must be given.

Clause 1(2) specifies the people who may legitimately have access to census information. They are the Registrars-General, people under their control and people who supply services to either Registrar-General. Clause 1(6) will ensure that all people who are working or have worked for the Registrars-General are included in the provision. Clause 1(3) preserves an existing provision and will catch people who pass on information acquired as a result of unlawful disclosure.

Defences are provided in clause 1(4) for people who reasonably and genuinely believe that information that they have disclosed does not contravene the 1920 Act. Clause 1(5) deals with penalties. Those provisions are designed specifically to deal with the gaps in the definition of the people to whom the statutory guarantee of confidentiality applies.

The Bill also contains a definition of census information. It extends the term to cover information collected in census-related surveys and in the census tests that the Registrars-General carry out in preparation for a census proper. That is designed to fill the second gap. That definition under clause 1(7) refers to information acquired in the course of the discharge of functions under sections 2 and 4 of the Census Act 1920. Under section 2, the Registrars-General conduct their census tests and take the census. Under section 4, information is collected in surveys to help the Registrars-General to report on the census results. We will be following previous practice by holding a voluntary sample survey after the 1991 census to check the accuracy of the census. We plan to collect census information from members of households who are away from home over the census period, but who agree to fill in the form when they return home.

Census information is further defined to include information collected in follow-up surveys that are carried out under section 5 of the Census Act 1920 and are directed to samples of people or households selected by reference to the answers given in the census returns. We have taken the opportunity to focus the criminal sanctions contained in the Bill on the only information that really needs that protection—that is, personal information. That is defined as

"information which relates to an identifiable person or household".

Section 8(2) of the Census Act 1920 protects any information obtained in the course of taking a census. That would, for example, currently cover, at least technically, a JPEG 5 member of the census office who told a member of the public how many census forms were to be printed. We have therefore focused the criminal sanction on information about which the statutory guarantee of confidentiality matters—that is, information of a personal nature, which the person who fills in the census return has a legitimate right to expect to be treated as confidential.

Several hon. Members have expressed concern about the inclusion in the Bill of the concept of lawful authority of disclosure. An employee of the census office may have lawful authority to pass confidential information to someone else. That is nothing new. The provision is in the original statute—indeed, it is hard to imagine how any census could ever be collected if it were not. The most important occasion on which advantage is taken of that provision is when one census office employee passes confidential information to another employee. Without the concept of lawful authority it would be impossible for an enumerator to pass confidential information to the person responsible for the area in which he collected it.

The lawful authority provision is essential to allow information to pass within the census offices. It is needed also so that census offices can pass formerly confidential information to the Public Record Office at the end of the 100-year protection period. Further, it is needed to allow the enforcement of the compulsory nature of the census. If an individual does not adequately complete the census form, information that would otherwise be confidential may have to be revealed in court.

Mr. Dorrell

The publication of aggregated census information on a postcode basis is specifically bound by the provisions of the confidentiality guarantee. It is part of the output of the census office as a result of the aggregation process. The publication of returns on a postcode basis is covered by the confidentiality provisions, and is not one of the exemptions for which lawful authority would be necessary.

Mr. Dorrell
: I must disagree with the hon. Gentleman that the publication of small area statistics of that nature can lead to the reliable identification of a citizen's confidential census information. The hon. Gentleman referred to the blurring process, which is designed specifically to obscure personal information. He will be aware also of two other provisions.

First, small-area statistics are released only if the population and number of households in an area exceed specific thresholds. There must be a minimum number of 6 households in an area before small-area statistics can be released. Secondly, standard census tables are designed for areas below local authority level, so that instances in which a count of the number of people with a particular combination produces very small numbers are kept to an absolute minimum. The Registrar-General has defined that as defence in depth, and it will act against the danger identified by the hon. Gentleman. It is a danger concerned more with the publication of census output than with guarantees to the citizen who fills in a census return.

(Preston)

There would be resistance to publishing it unless it was clearly necessary.

This short Bill completes the guarantee of confidentiality that anyone completing a census return is entitled to expect, bearing in mind the compulsory nature of that return. Census offices have a distinguished record of observing confidentiality and I am not aware of any significant breaches arising from information in earlier censuses.
