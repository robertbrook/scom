Representation of The People Bill

10.30 am

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Mr John Maples): I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee that the Representation of the People Bill ought to be read a second time.

The Representation of the People Bill is a short, technical Bill designed to correct an inadequacy in the current law as set out in section 29 of the Representation of the People Act 1983.

Section 29 of the Representation of the People Act 1983 provides for the reimbursement to returning officers of the expenses incurred in running parliamentary elections. Those expenses include the personal fees of the returning officers, presiding officers and poll clerks, the cost of printing and issuing ballot papers and polling cards, the travelling and subsistence expenses of all officials, the renting of rooms for use as polling stations and for counting ballots papers, and many other items. Hon. Members will be aware of all the tasks involved.

Section 29 of the 1983 Act provides that the Treasury shall, by statutory instrument prescribe a scale of maximum charges in respect of services rendered and expenses incurred by returning officers in organising parliamentary elections. In other words, the Treasury shall set out in regulations the maximum amount that returning officers may be reimbursed for each service.

Section 29 of the 1983 Act further provides that the reimbursement of such expenses shall be from the Consolidated Fund and not from voted money. Parliament intended when passing that provision that returning officers should be assured that they would be reimbursed for their reasonable expenses. The best way of providing that reassurance was to pay the money direct from the Consolidated Fund.

The provisions of section 29 of the 1983 Act go back to the Representation of the People Acts of 1918 and 1919. The 1983 Act is simply a consolidation of previous legislation. Under the European Assembly Election Regulations 1986, section 29 of the 1983 Act also applies to European elections.

The implementation of section 29 has not been straightforward. The problem is that the amounts spent by returning officers on the conduct of elections vary considerably, with the result that no maximum charges for those amounts would be reasonable for every constituency.

That difficulty applies in particular to travelling and subsistence costs, to the cost of printing and providing ballot papers and poll cards, of renting rooms and of other miscellaneous items. If the maximum charges are set too high to allow for high costs in certain areas, returning officers in other areas might spend or claim up to that maximum. However, if the maximum is set too low, great difficulty can be caused when returning officers reasonably Col. 4 and properly incur expenditure that exceeds the relevant amounts. In short, what is fair and reasonable in one constituency may not be so in another.

The solution has been to provide in the regulations that, for certain items of expenditure, returning officers will be entitled to receive their "actual and necessary costs". However, in 1987, Mr Speaker's Counsel advised that that was ultra vires. He pointed out that section 29 of the 1983 Act provided that the Treasury should prescribe a scale of maximum charges, so maximum charges should be prescribed and that "actual and necessary costs" would not do.

The Government accept that that advice is right, and that the way that the regulations had been drawn up for many years had strictly speaking, been inconsistent with the wording of the 1983 Act. Therefore a way had to be found of complying with the 1983 Act that still allowed returning officers in every constituency to be reimbursed for their reasonable expenses.

The device that was used to surmount that difficulty in the regulations for the 1989 European parliamentary elections was to provide that, for those items where it was not reasonably possible to specify scale maxima applicable to every constituency, returning officers would be entitled to their reasonable expenses up to an "authorised maximum". That in turn was defined as the expenses incurred in each constituency at the 1987 general election but uprated by 31 per cent. and subject to an overall addition of £25,000.

At the recent parliamentary by-election, a similar practice was followed of uprating the 1987 expenditure. That is clearly a complicated and unsatisfactory approach, Image Col. 5 and only tolerable on the understanding that the underlying inadequacy in section 29 of the 1983 Act will be corrected at the first opportunity.

Moreover, the authorised maximum approach will break down at the next general election. That approach can work only as long as the 187 general election constituencies remain unchanged. However, the Milton Keynes constituency is to be divided into two at the next general election. Then it will no longer be possible to apply the authorised maximum device to the two new constituencies.

The Milton Keynes problem could be overcome at the next general election, but the regulations would have to be drafted in an artificial and unsatisfactory way. The authorised maximum approach would fail altogether on the next general revision of parliamentary boundaries, which are to be reviewed shortly.

Section 29 of the 1983 Act causes one other difficulty. The expenses of returning officers may, in practice, exceed the statutory maximum laid down, but do so for good reasons for the purposes of running a parliamentary election. In such circumstances, extra-statutory payments to the returning officers concerned have been made by the Treasury. The payments have been made out of voted money rather than from the Consolidated Fund. They have also tended to be fairly small. Nevertheless, there is doubt whether it is right to pay them because section 29 of the 1983 Act expressly states that returning officers should not be paid in excess of the scale maxima set out in the regulations. In the circumstances, it seems highly desirable to amend the law to ensure that there is no need for such extra-statutory payments.

Mr. Peter Viggers (Gosport): As my hon. Friend has shown himself so resilient in depth under cross-examination by the hon. Member for Great Grimsby (Mr. Mitchell), can he tell me anything about the remuneration of returning officers? Does it vary much across the United Kingdom and, if so, by how much? I ask because the remuneration of returning officers is added to their other income in determining their pensions.

Mr. Maples: Will my hon. Friend allow me to deal with that later? I shall have to study the regulations before I can give him an answer.

The Bill solves the problems in the 1983 Act by amending it and essentially does two things. First, it provides that, when it is not reasonably possible to specify a maximum charge for an item of expenditure applicable in all constituencies, returning officers shall be entitled to their actual expenses, provided that they are reasonably incurred. Secondly, it provides that, for those items for which it is possible to specify a maximum charge, the Treasury may authorise payments in excess of the specified maxima, again provided that the expenses have been reasonably incurred.

In other words, the orders to be laid down by the Treasury will continue to specify the maximum amounts to be reimbursed for certain items of expenditure. Where, however, it is not reasonably possible to lay down the scale maxima applicable to all constituencies, it will be possible to reimburse returning officers their actual and necessary expenditure, provided that it has been reasonably incurred. Moreover, if for good reason a returning officer has to spend more than the scale maxima to ensure that a ballot is properly conducted, the Treasury will be able to reimburse him for the full amount spent.

Col. 6 These changes are essential if returning officers are to be reimbursed their reasonable expenses when organising parliamentary elections. They are nevertheless technical in that they do no more than enable section 29 of the 1983 Act to work in the way envisaged by Parliament when passing these provisions earlier this century: that returning officers should be reimbursed their reasonable election expenses, and that those payments should be made from the Consolidated Fund and not from voted money. I, therefore, believe that the changes are uncontentious. I hope very much that hon. Members on both sides of the Committee will welcome the Bill and recommend a Second Reading accordingly.
