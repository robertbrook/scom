Mr. Bryan Gould
(Dagenham)
: I beg to move amendment No. 238, in page 20, leave out lines 12 to 16.

Welcome back, Sir Michael. We look forward to a long and profitable afternoon and evening.

This amendment is meant to probe a slight mystery in subsection (5). If the subsection were omitted, as the amendment proposes, it would leave local authorities to calculate the tax rate for bands of property only where property of that band occured within the authority's area. For example, if there were no properties for band H in a certain local authority area, that authority would not have to calculate a tax for properties in that band.

We do not understand why subsection (5) is in the Bill and we should like the Minister to explain its purpose. It states that an authority should assume for the purposes of subsections (1) and (2) that one or more dwellings in each property band falls within its area and any part of its area that requires a separate bill to be set. However, there is a slight contradiction with subsection (1) which requires the authority to calculate only

"for each category of dwellings in its area"

the amount of council tax to be set. The only purpose of subsection (5) seems to be to ensure that the local authority calculates a tax rate for all bands, even though all bands do not exist in the area. We are puzzled why that should be so. Why should a local authority have to calculate a tax on the purely hypothetical basis that some properties that manifestly do not exist within its area really do exist and therefore attract a rate of tax?

I assume that the phrase

"each category of dwellings in its area"

in subsection (1) is a reference to each valuation band, although I am not sure that that is necessarily the case. Can the Minister confirm that that is so?

Why has this been done? The only answer that I can offer is that it is for purely propaganda and presentational purposes. It is meant to signify to council tax payers in a given local authority area that there is a wider range of liability than there is. For example, if there were only three bands in a local authority area, a person might be diverted if he were told that a range of tax liability had been established for all eight bands even though tax was raised on three bands only.

The Minister may say that it is necessary to calculate the tax in case properties move into bands that are not currently represented, but as he has adamantly said throughout our proceedings that once a property is allocated to a band it will remain there in perpetuity, that 712 hardly seems a convincing answer. I am therefore forced to conclude that subsection (5) is purely for propaganda purposes. It is meant to mislead and to pretend that the tax is fairer in that it covers a wider range than it does; and it is meant to conceal the very real problems that arise from compressing properties into a few bands. In other words, it is meant to conceal that in some parts of the country the new council tax will, to all intents and purposes, be virtually a flat-rate tax.

JPEG 717 Many authorities may wish to provide early notification to their tax payers, hoping to receive early payment to help their cashflow. If a county council has to set a substitute tax rate, authorities at district and parish level will also be affected. When a substitute tax rate is set and a tax payer has paid more than he should, whether at county, district or parish level, he will not be able to demand repayment, but the billing authority will decide whether a refund or credit should be given. Clause 31(4) will come into effect after a substitute bill has been issued, for example, if an authority is capped. Clause 31(4)(a) allows the taxpayer to demand the money back. Where no such demand is made, clause 31(4)(b) allows the authority to decide whether to repay or merely to grant a credit against future liability. The amendment removes the first option and leaves the treatment of overpayment to the discretion of the local authority.

Good reasons underlie the amendment. There is no doubt that the money overpaid belongs to the council tax payer, if the overpayment was due to an administrative arrangement, so there is no danger that the local authority could keep the money. If a substitute bill has been calculated, the collection department of the council will be under extreme pressure to send out new bills immediately. For administrative simplicity there should be just one rule for dealing with overpayments. It would be costly and time consuming to make special arrangements for those people who demanded their money back immediately.

If paragraph (a) is allowed to remain, the council tax payer may demand immediate repayment from the treasurer of the local authority. Without uniformity in the arrangements, difficulties will arise for the local authority. Furthermore, under the formula, there are various tiers in the local authority set-up. If the county is capped, people in the district and the parishes can demand the immediate return of the money that they have overpaid. If our amendment were accepted, the local authority would be able to repay or credit council tax payers after a specific time, which would be to the local authority's benefit. To tidy the matter, we believe that the local authority should have the prerogative to arrange credit or repayments en bloc, rather than piecemeal as people demand repayment.

In the vast majority of cases, the money that the council tax payer demanded would, presumably, become 718 repayable as soon as the next instalment was due. That is probably why the Government are content for the local authority to exercise discretion in the case of people who do not demand their money back. For people who pay by instalments, it would be easier for the adjustment to be made over future instalments. That happens now. Where a demand has been issued because of non-payment and an attachment order has been made to cover court costs, the local authority does not demand the money immediately; it is added to the instalment payment. The reverse should also apply. Any adjustment for overpayment should be made when the next instalment is due. That would be much better for the local authority. It would tidy up the exercise and help people to understand how the local authority functioned.
