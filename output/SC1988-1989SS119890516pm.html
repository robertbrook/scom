We are all aware of the findings of the 1986 HMI report on "Learning and Teaching and Religious Education". Weaknesses were identified in the delivery of religious education in both primary and secondary schools due

"either to uncertainty about the importance of the subject in the school curriculum or what it was seeking to achieve".

In more detail the HMI report found that while nearly all primary schools included religious education as part of the curriculum, the nature and extent of provision varied widely. The majority of schools had no planned policy and left it to individual JPEG 1467 teachers. The education authority guidelines which existed were not widely implemented and there was a failure of management to monitor and promote religious education throughout the school.

In secondary schools provision was unsatisfactory in many cases. Most non-denominational schools provided religious education in SI and S2, there was a fall-off in provision in S3 and S4, and only a minority of schools made any provision beyond that point.

The typical time allocation for religious education was one period a week and the subject was taken by a mixture of specialist and non-specialist—usually unqualified— teachers. Since 1986, in no small part due to efforts on the part of the Church, there has been some improvement, but not nearly enough. More schools now have policies for religious education, but still by no means all, and there are education authority guidelines in all the mainland authorities. But there is still a problem of implementing them. Head teachers and class teachers tend to give religious education low priority for curriculum development and in-service training. School census figures show that about 60 per cent, of non-denominational schools include religious education in the curriculum at SI and S2, about 50 per cent, at S3 and S4 and only about 30 per cent, at S5.

In particular, there are problems beyond S2. The Guidelines for Headteachers published by the SCCC in 1987 recommended a minimum of 5 per cent, of curricular time for religious and moral education in SI to S4. It is, I understand, extremely rare to find that amount of time given to religious education outwith denominational schools. Headteachers frequently interpret the guidelines to include social education in the 5 per cent. That may be legitimate where the social education programme deals with moral issues, but often the content is to do with careers and practical life skills with little reference to morality.

Where a deficiency is identified the inspectorate will follow up after six months, and if no improvement is evident the Department will then take the matter up with the director of education pointing out if necessary that the authority is in default of its statutory duty.
