PROVISIONS REGARDING ACQUISITION OF LAND BY LOCAL AUTHORITY

Mr. Robin F. Cook
: I beg to move Amendment No. 85, in page 23, line 27, at end insert:

'and the Secretary of State shall confirm such an order or instruct the local authority to rescind the draft order within a period of three months of the date of submission, except that, where the order is the subject of a public inquiry, the said period shall be six months'.

I make no apology for moving this amendment. It touches on a matter that I raised on Second Reading, namely, the very long time that it takes for St. Andrew's House to confirm compulsory purchase orders.

At various times in our proceedings, hon. Members have referred to bureaucratic delays and the time taken by local authorities. If we try to cut down a month here and 28 days there in local authority procedures, we are endeavouring to deal with the mote while not casting out the beam in our eye. The major 90 delays occur, not at the local authority end but at St. Andrew's House.

Confirmation of a treatment area order can take up to eight months. Confirmation of a compulsory purchase order can take between 18 months and two years. The Bill deals with the period for the confirmation of treatment order areas. Now that we have housing action areas, the period has been cut to 28 days. In my view, we should deal similarly with delays with compulsory purchase orders.

I suggest that the Secretary of State should have a maximum of three months in which to confirm a compulsory purchase order. In my view, that is not unduly restrictive. I recognise that, where a public inquiry is necessary, there will have to be additional procedures gone through, and therefore I suggest a maximum period of six months. That should be adequate time in which to call a public inquiry and to arrive at a decision. It is necessary to give only one month's notice of the date of a public inquiry.

I give one or two examples of delays that we face at present. An order promoted by Edinburgh Corporation—the Great Junction Street Order—was submitted to the Secretary of State on 6th June 1972. Confirmation was not received until 14th March of this year, nearly two years later. We cannot blame the delay on the need to hold a public inquiry. There was a public inquiry into this Order. But it was held on 31st January 1973. It was 14 months after the inquiry was heard before the Secretary of State made up his mind whether to confirm the Order.

Delays of that kind are inexcusable. They are inconsistent with the desire to get on with the swift treatment of Scotland's slums. Even with my amendment, the maximum time which can be taken is 14 months. I submit that further delay would be inconsistent with our desire to treat our slums quickly. I hope that my hon. Friend the Minister of State will accept the amendment.
