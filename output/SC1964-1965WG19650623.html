Mr. David Gibson-Watt
: This debate on agriculture takes place today in the Welsh Grand Committee at a sensible time of the year and also at a sensible time of day. The land is looking its best, or it was when we from Wales saw Welsh land, lambing and shearing are almost over and the lowland valleys are the scene of the cutting of hay and silage.

It is normal to confess an interest in the subject of any debate, and I do so today. I have an interest in Welsh black cattle, Welsh sheep and Welsh ponies. Long may they thrive. Just as the Secretary of State said in December that he spoke as a miner, I speak today as a farmer. Farming and mining account for a large proportion of the employment of Welsh people.

It is three months since the February Price Review. Therefore, it is a little easier to see and to understand how it has been received and how it has affected the general trend of farm economy. Even the most optimistic could hardly say that the Review was greeted with shouts of glee. It was, and remains, a disappointment. I am glad that the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food is here today with his responsibility for Welsh agriculture. We on this side of the Committee, and no doubt hon. Members opposite, look forward to hearing whether he has any good news for Welsh agriculture.

The farming industry has been very disturbed about the inadequacy of the Review. Of course, it was not an agreed Review. The National Farmers Union, soon after its launching, campaigned to 108 put over to the holiday public that the profitability of the industry had fallen in recent years and members of the Union put posters on trees and barns. Those posters were written in moderate language and were a sober appraisal of the state of the average farmer. This was an effort by the N.F.U. to tell the city dweller on holiday that the farming community did not believe that it was getting a fair crack of the whip.

In Breconshire we saw curious action taken by the planning committee. I refer to it this morning because I believe it is of interest and needs to be gone into. The Breckonshire Planning Committee, whether or not it was within or without the law, ordered the National Farmers Union to take down the posters, not only those on public buildings, such as are described as fly posters, but those on private property and farm buildings. This Committee may be aware that those posters were not written in insulting or heavy language. They were in the sort of language in which normally honourable people freely state their cases to the British public. I thought it extremely odd—

Mr. Iorwerth Thomas

Mr. Gibson-Watt

It was not done by the Radnorshire Planning Committee, which deals with the same branch of the Union as does the Breconshire Planning Committee. Nor do I think that any other Welsh planning committee took this action. Was the action due to any particular factor? The planning committee may have been carrying out the letter of the law, but things seem to have come to a pretty pass in Wales when a planning committee forbids such notices being placed on private property.

Are banners to be forbidden at protest meetings in future? Are notices advertising fetes and football matches to be forbidden? Is criticism of our largest industry, couched in very modest terms, to be so resented that the iron hand of planning is to fall on it in this way? This was unsatisfactory, even if the planning committee was acting, as it claimed, according to the strict letter of the law.

I would not put it too highly, but the Price Review White Paper did not give a quite correct picture. On page 44 it said that there was no change in the price. What it failed to note was that the administrative costs of the Wool Board mean an extra ½d. a 1b. So wool will fetch that much less this year. This may seem a derisory amount, but if we multiply it by the 10 million pounds in weight, we find that it makes £20,833, which is not an inconsiderate amount. Although people who do not know the industry are inclined to say that wool is all right and that producers are doing pretty well out of it, how many farmers today are bothering to shear their lambs? Many do not find it worth while because there is not so much money in wool production as there was a few years ago.

As to beef, the increase of 4s. a cwt. looks all right, but it is misleading because it all depends on when the stock is marketed. This is a very complicated trade. With world supplies and demand changing as they are, with more Argentine beef going to European countries and less to us, we shall need to increase our home beef production. I doubt if the present incentives of£18 per hill cow—£13 for the cow and £5 for winter keep supplement—will prove sufficient. The National Farmers' Union has suggested that a beef suckler herd which would not be limited to the hill areas might prove a long-term incentive to added beef production which certainly is needed. I ask the Minister his views about this sugges- 110 tion. Does he favour this idea? Does he think it would work? If so, can he tell us how it would work?

Another point we should look at is whether the balance between beef and milk production is correct. There are certain areas in Wales where sometimes there is the question of whether it was right immediately after the war to encourage those areas to go in for milk production. It may be that some of those producers are quite unable to get out of that particular line; they cannot afford to do so. The farmer's wife says, "I must have my weekly cheque. Whatever the price imposed by the milk guarantee system, the weekly cheque comes along." As milk is such a specialised and mechanised line nowadays, could not a case be made for looking at particular farms—this would have to be selective—to see whether or not there could be incentives for certain milk producers to change over to beef suckling?

The Minister of State for Welsh Affairs (Mr. Goronwy Roberts)
: It may help the Committee if I begin by inviting hon. Members to look at Welsh agriculture as a whole, and try to indicate the main structural features of the industry and the nature of its production. We might then consider whether Government policy is broadly suited to its proper development and encouragement.

We are dealing with an industry organised into about 50,000 holdings of various sizes, some of them quite small, and covering about 4¼ million acres of land. Owing to contour and climate, only about 2½ million acres of this land is cropped, and nearly 1¼ million acres are taken up by rough grazings. Indeed, a further 500,000 acres of agricultural land in Wales are common rough grazings. So much for structure.

As to manpower, about 42,000 of out people regard themselves as farmers, and obviously many of these are part-time farmers operating non-viable units. They employ, in total about 26,000 regular workers and about 6,000 casual workers, and we know that in addition the Welsh family farm depends very much on the farmer's wife. That is the manpower position. It is a large industry, perhaps the largest in Wales, in regard to employment, both self-employed and employed.

The main concern of this very hardworking section of the Welsh popula- 114 tion is livestock and its products. Whenever possible, the Welsh farmer grows crops, and in fact he benefits by about £1½ million every year from deficiency payments on cereals, but, nevertheless, grass is the foundation of his economy and his income comes mainly from milk and from fat and store stock.

Wales is a land flowing with milk, if not always with honey. We sell about 240 million gallons of milk every year, but it is also important to note that Wales is an important stock-raising country. Certification centres in Wales handle annually about 125,000 head of fat cattle. This is a high figure relative to our acreage, and indeed to our resources. They also handle about 1½ million fat sheep and lambs, and 300,000 fat pigs. Apart from that, many thousands of Welsh cattle, sheep, and pigs are sold for breeding or finishing elsewhere in the United Kingdom.

For instance, the fat lamb producer of Anglesey will get his ewes from the mainland and not necessarily from Caernarvonshire. The Leicestershire farmer may buy the strong Welsh black store cattle for his richer pastures, while a county like Carmarthen—and we have more than one like it in Wales—is justly famous as a source of young dairy stock. This points the moral, which we must understand in Wales, that our agriculture is not only distinctive, because of natural forces, but that it is linked very closely indeed with the agriculture of the rest of the Kingdom.

We therefore have a picture of an industry comprising a great many small and medium-sized operators working more often than not in upland country. These are the facts of life in Wales and they are inevitably obliged to make a virtue of geographical and climatic necessity and to concentrate largely on livestock and its products.

How does Government policy help that kind of industry? What should it do? My right hon. Friend the Minister of Agriculture, whose presence here we all welcome, particularly as his better half derives from the very Welsh county of Cardigan, will no doubt refer to various determinants of Government policy, and I should like to refer to one. It is a remarkable fact that a large proportion JPEG 115 of the support given generally to agriculture in the United Kingdom—quite £100 million or more—is given in the form of direct grants to encourage efficient production, to assist in physical improvements, and also to give special help to the small farmer and to hill and upland farmers. In a real sense, generally agricultural policy helps the Principality perhaps more than any other part of the United Kingdom.

At the moment, there are 17 items on the list of relevant production grants approved as a result of the Price Review. I think we are all very glad that over the past year these items, costing about £9 million in benefit to Wales, have included fertility subsidies, livestock subsidies for calves of hill stock, drainage grants, silo subsidies, and also, perhaps more important, looking to the future and bearing in mind what I had to say about the structure of the industry in Wales, the Small Farmer Scheme which my right hon. Friend proposes to extend and reshape to give greater value both to the participating farmer, especially in a country like Wales, and to the country's economy.

Not only are we in Wales encouraged in short and medium term activity of the type that I have described, but we are also substantially encouraged in the longer-term activities by longer-term grants. These include the Farm Improvement Scheme and the rehabilitation of hill and livestock rearing land. Under these schemes many farms are being provided with modern buildings and facilities which are basic to modern farming. We would like to see this process intensified and accelerated, and we pay tribute to the way in which the utilities, including nationalised utilities, have assisted in this matter.

We are particularly glad to see how more widely available in the countryside are those amenities which are often taken for granted in town and city. I am thinking particularly of electricity and water supplies, and also improved access roads which are vital to easier marketing and to social and cultural contact. Depopulation in rural Wales, as elsewhere, is prompted not only by economic necessity. It is due also to a lack of facilities of 116 a social and cultural nature in our villages and in our countryside. We ignore that aspect of the matter at our peril.
