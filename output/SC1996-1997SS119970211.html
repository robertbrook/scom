Mr. Chisholm
: As I said in the Scottish Grand Committee in Edinburgh, we welcome the clause, which is about turning "can" into "must" for local authorities. We welcome also the Government amendments which will replace surveys with assessments.

One of the main issues discussed in Edinburgh was how much money would be available for the work related to clauses 1 and 2. We were told by the Minister that £3 million would be available. My concern, and the aim of the amendment, is that that money should properly be targeted and that we should have regard to cost benefit in using that money. It would not be an efficient or effective use of resources to survey all watercourses in any one local authority area. In an area such as the highlands, it would probably be impossible to survey—or, as we now saying, assess—all the watercourses. The amendment contains a solution to that problem.

I emphasise the point about money because it was one of the main aspects of the debate on Second Reading. Some £3 million has been given and should be used in the most sensible way possible. I will not digress at length about whether that £3 million is real money—that transgresses on to the general local government finance debates which we have had and which we will have more of in the next two weeks. In a sense that money is notional money because there will be a cash cut in local authority budgets next year 4 and local authorities may struggle even to spend £3 million. Assuming that they will spend that amount, we must make sure that they use the money in the most effective way possible.

Various solutions to the problem have been suggested. For example, the Scottish Environmental Protection Agency—SEPA—suggests that the duty should be limited to surveying watercourses where

"cleansing, repairing and otherwise maintaining"

activities might be effective. That suggestion is even more restrictive than the one that I am putting forward. Another solution, suggested by the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities, is that there should be surveys only of built-up areas. There are problems in defining a built-up area. I am mindful of the amendments in the name of my hon. Friend the Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden (Mr. Galbraith), in which he points out problems that might arise in agricultural areas and have knock-on effects in built-up areas. There are problems with that formulation, although I understand the point that COSLA is trying to make. Perhaps the Minister can clarify exactly where he wants activities to be targeted.

The formulation that I have put forward relates to areas

"with a known risk of flooding"

because it is well known which watercourses and which parts of particular watercourses are most likely to cause trouble. The most effective use of resources would be to target the survey or assessment on those watercourses. The clause says that that is to be done "from time to time." Perhaps the Minister can clarify exactly what that means—it could mean an abundance of things. If we properly target watercourses with a known risk of flooding, we could carry out far more frequent inspections. It may well be difficult, however, regularly to inspect every watercourse in the highlands.

I am concerned that activities should be concentrated so that the formulation "from time to time" would mean frequent assessments in critical areas. That would be the most effective way to reduce the risk of flooding.

Mr. Kynoch
: I note that the hon. Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden says that I have said enough, but I think that he will be interested in the Government's response to one or two of the points that were raised on Second Reading and in consultation thereafter.

I shall speak as briefly as I can on the amendments. The Committee will have seen that the Government propose to change the term "surveyed" to "assessed". Before dealing with that, I shall deal with amendment No. 22, which was tabled by the hon. Member for Edinburgh, Leith (Mr. Chisholm).

The amendment would limit the amount of work that councils will have to do in "assessing" watercourses—if that term is agreed—by allowing them to assess only those with a known history of flooding. While, on the face of it, that may seem acceptable, flooding is unpredictable. It is not always those watercourses with a known history of flooding which flood. The condition of a watercourse is not static. Among other things, trees can fall into channels, debris can build up, vegetation and tree growth can occur, and screens can become blocked, all of which can result in restrictions of channel capacity and thereby increase the risk of flooding.

I understand that there are concerns that councils face the prospect of litigation if a comprehensive assessment is not carried out on a watercourse, or part of one, and flooding occurs in that area. The Government's view is that that would hinge on a test of reasonableness. Councils that act reasonably in the light of information available to determine their priorities and the period between, and the extent of, each assessment will not be likely to be successfully sued. The extent of an assessment and prioritising watercourses to be assessed will depend on local circumstances. Local councils are best placed to make such decisions.

As for the nature of the work to be done, it was never the Government's intention that duty should be an onerous or necessarily technical task. Following receipt of representations on that point, we propose to clarify the provision by replacing the term "surveyed" with "assessed" in clause 1 and "a survey" with "an assessment" in clause 2.

The hon. Member for Leith referred to the £3 million allocation to assist in assessments, and I am sure that he will return to the matter, as is his wont because he has an obsession with local government funding. New clause 2 deals with emergency powers of entry and raises an important matter. Section 8 of the Flood Prevention Act 1961 states:

"Admission to any land shall not be demanded as of right"

by councils

"unless fourteen days notice of intended entry has been given to the occupier of the land."

If, however, application is made to the sheriff and he is satisfied that there is a case of urgency, he may warrant the council to enter on the land.

The Bill places a new duty to act to clear watercourses if that

"would substantially reduce the likelihood of … flooding".

Blockages, particularly of culverts, can occur quickly as a result of natural debris or irresponsible dumping in watercourses. There can be no guarantee that a JPEG 13 regular maintenance programme would necessarily pick up those hazards prior to any storm or flash flood. There have been occasions when blocked culverts have quickly caused significant flooding of property. Therefore, if councils are to carry out their duties effectively, it is essential that they can access blockages in an emergency before floods rise to dangerous levels.

There are further amendments, one in the name of the Minister and one in my name, urging the inclusion in the measure of justices of the peace in addition to sheriffs, but in some circumstances even their actions may be too slow to prevent flooding. For example, in Paisley during the December 1994 floods, Tercet, which is a light engineering company making high-quality products for the electronics industry, was badly flooded. The flooding arose from a blocked culvert on an adjoining vacant site, which was in the ownership of a private development company, the whereabouts of which were not known at that time. Ownership had changed hands and was, in fact, initially denied. The flooding rose to a depth of 0.5 metres within two hours and the company was closed for approximately six weeks, with damage of £1.5 million to repair.

While it is not normal to allow emergency access without warrant, the speed and gravity of the consequences of flooding make this an exceptional case. The amendment would allow emergency access without warrant only if, in the view of the responsible officers, waiting for a warrant would result in significant flooding of property. In the circumstances, that would be reasonable and necessary.
