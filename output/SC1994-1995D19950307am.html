The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Sir George Young)
: Queen's Park Rangers also had a satisfactory weekend at the expense of Wimbledon. I say that before my parliamentary private secretary—my hon. Friend the Member for Wimbledon (Dr. Goodson-Wickes)—returns to the Room.

The hon. Member for Durham, North-West (Ms Armstrong) asked me to consider her amendment in a broader context and to describe the Government's progress in introducing the new scheme. It may help our subsequent debates on this clause and schedule 27 if I sketch in the background.

The measures introduce a number of changes to the special tax scheme for the construction industry. The scheme has been in existence since 1971. It was established to counter the tax evasion that was rife among self-employed building workers. The scheme requires a contractor to make a deduction on account of tax and national insurance contributions each time he pays a subcontractor for work relating to construction operations. However, those subcontractors who can demonstrate their compliance with tax and national insurance obligations can obtain certificates entitling them to be paid without deduction.

The aim of the scheme was to ensure that certificates were granted only to those carrying out genuine and substantial business operations. That was achieved by requiring that the applicant should show that his business was carried on from proper business premises, with proper equipment, stock and other facilities.

Those rules have allowed considerable scope for wide interpretation, however. As a result, the rules have proved to be increasingly ineffective in meeting the original aim of the scheme and certificates are now widely available. Many subcontractors who do no more than provide their own labour have been able to obtain a certificate with relative ease.

The weakening of the scheme has facilitated tax evasion in the industry through the misuse of certificates. Those having an itinerant or peripheral involvement with the industry cannot be denied a certificate on that ground alone. When they are not carrying out work within the industry, however—because they are working privately, are on non-construction-industry operations, or are unemployed—they have no immediate use for the certificate. Unfortunately, some individuals in such circumstances are tempted to sell their certificates or allow them to be used by unauthorised persons. That has caused a black market in exemption documentation, leading to substantial loss of revenue to the Exchequer. To overcome such problems, the Government propose to provide a more objective test of the size of the business by introducing a new turnover test. That will restrict eligibility for certificates to those subcontractors whose annual business turnover in construction operations exceed a prescribed level to be specified in regulations. The exact level of turnover will be specified in regulations, with the figures determined close to the probable commencement date of 1 August 1998. That will help to ensure that certificates are issued only to those carrying on substantial business operations within the construction industry. The remainder—probably more than 80 per cent. of all subcontractors, as opposed to less than half currently—will be made up of individual workers supplying their own labour and little else.

JPEG 591 Restricting certificates to the more substantial business operations will also help to curtail the current level of tax evasion through understatement of profits and the nonpayment of tax, with the deduction on account of tax and national insurance contributions safeguarding the Exchequer. Although the extent of the risk posed by fraud and evasion in the industry cannot be accurately quantified, known investigated misuse of documents amounts to more than £20 million and the tax written off through unpaid duties is estimated to exceed £50 million. The total tax at risk from all areas within the industry is believed to be far in excess of those figures.

The new rules will have no effect on the actual amount of the subcontractor's tax bill. Any deductions that exceed the actual tax liability are repaid to the subcontractor at the end of the tax year. The current rate of deduction is 25 per cent.—equivalent to the basic rate of income tax—but it takes no account of personal and other allowances. Thus the vast majority of subcontractors receiving payments under deduction currently overpay tax significantly during the year and have to claim a repayment after the tax year end. We intend to reduce the rate of deduction so that the new rate will more closely approximate to a typical person's effective liability after allowances have been taken into account. That will ease the burden on those switching over to the deduction side of the scheme.

We propose to dispense with the requirement that an applicant for a certificate must have worked in the United Kingdom for a period of three out of the last six years—a rule that has proved to bear harshly in certain cases and that has proved to be the source of much complaint. On the other hand, it will no longer be possible for a subcontractor who has failed to meet his tax obligations during the three years prior to his application to bring them up to date simply in order to qualify for a certificate. In future, he will have to ensure that he complies with all statutory tax and NIC requirements as and when they arise throughout that period.

We also propose to streamline the penalties for false certificate applications and misuse of documents. That will enable the Inland Revenue to consider civil penalties as an alternative to criminal sanctions in future. Government Departments which engage construction industry labour will be required to operate the scheme for the first time, bringing them into line with businesses and local authorities. The limit of expenditure on construction work that requires businesses outside the construction industry to operate the scheme will be quadrupled from £250,000 to £1 million.

All those changes will come into effect no earlier than August 1998, thus giving the industry plenty of time to gear up to the new regime. The Inland Revenue has consulted representative bodies and unions in the industry and the proposed changes have been greatly welcomed. As a former Minister with responsibility for the construction industry, I know that many in the industry will welcome the changes that we are introducing.

The clause and schedule contain the main principles of the proposals. The detail of the administration of the construction industry scheme—the nuts and bolts of how it will operate—will be contained in regulations, as is the case with the existing legislation. The new arrangements will not come into play for more than three years and it is 592 too early to lay regulations yet—especially those relating to the level of turnover or the tax deduction rate.

The Government are conscious that subcontractors and contractors within the industry and taxation professionals need as much notice as possible of the details of how the scheme will operate. I have authorised the Inland Revenue to publish draft regulations for the purpose of consultation and the Revenue will consult widely with the industry before regulations are laid before Parliament. That is the background and I hope that it will save debate on subsequent amendments.

I cannot accept the amendment, which would tie the rate at which deductions are made from the earnings of those subcontractors without certificates to the lower rate of tax. We intend the deduction rate to approximate more closely to a subcontractor's effective liability, taking account of allowances, which does not happen at the moment. That rate can be set only against the background of the income tax and NIC rates in force at the time. By arranging for the subcontractor deduction rate to be specified by Treasury order at the beginning of the relevant tax year, we shall be able to set a rate that can achieve that end. Pinning it to the lower rate of tax would not necessarily result in sufficient tax being deducted at source. I hope that the amendment will be withdrawn.

Sir George Young
: I am afraid that I cannot accept the amendment, which would bring forward the earliest date on which the appointed day may be set in regulations from 1 August to 6 April 1998. The purpose of the amendment is that the changes should take effect from the start of a tax year and not 1 August. That date was chosen deliberately as the likely start date for the changes because that will best ease the transition into the new arrangements for those subcontractors who will no longer qualify for a tax exemption certificate under the new rules. The consequences of the amendment would be to make the transition for those people more acute.

The choice of 1 August has its roots in the self-assessment system, on which the Committee has spent a great deal of time. Self-assessment is a major change in the system of administering personal tax and the Government decided that it would be sensible to defer implementation of the changes to the special construction industry scheme until subcontractors had gained some experience of self-assessment.

Under self-assessment, construction industry subcontractors, as with all self-employed taxpayers, will make a first payment on account of their annual tax bill on 31 January in the year of assessment; a second payment on account on the following 31 July; and a final balancing payment on 31 January following. Thus, in the calendar JPEG 593 year 1998, construction industry subcontractors will have to make the first payment on account of their tax bill for 1997–98 on 31 January 1998 and the second payment on account on 31 July 1998. Any balance left on the tax bill will be paid on 31 January 1999. For subcontractors who qualify for a certificate under the proposed new rules, it matters little from the point of view of tax payments whether the changes come into force from 1 August or 6 April. All payments will continue to be received gross and the tax due will be paid half-yearly.

The choice of the start date is important for those who, under the new rules, will no longer qualify for the certificate and who, from the date of implementation of the changes, will receive their income under deduction of tax. The Government propose that those subcontractors should be brought within the tax deduction arrangements immediately after they have paid the second payment on account of their 1997–98 tax bill. In that way, they will have cleared the greater part of the 1997–98 tax bill before they start to receive income net of tax, which will be assessable for 1998–99.

In contrast, the effect of the amendment would be to deduct tax from payments that are received on or after 6 April 1998, before the second payment on account for 1997–98 was made. There would be a risk that subcontractors would be misled into thinking that they would not have to make a payment on 31 July 1998 because tax was being stopped from their income, not appreciating that the tax deductions would be on account of their tax bill for 1998–99.

The most sensible approach is to ease the transition for subcontractors, who move into the tax deduction scheme from receiving payments gross, by timing the change to take effect from 1 August 1998 immediately after the second payment on account for 1997–98, as the clause intends. For those reasons, I hope that the Committee will reject the amendment.
