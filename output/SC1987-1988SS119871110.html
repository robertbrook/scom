Clause 1
INCREASE OF FINANCIAL LIMIT

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Mr. Ian Lang)
: The clause provides for the Scottish Development Agency's financial limit to be raised, as I believe the Committee will know, from £700 million to £1,200 million. That will provide statutory authority for the agency to provide loans, guarantees and other expenditure from public sources for the carrying out of its functions.

The Scottish Development Agency was given an initial financial allocation of £200 million raisable by order to £300 million by the Scottish Development Agency Act 1975. It was unusual for such an allocation to be specified for a body in receipt of grant in aid, but the Labour Administration of the day felt that the agency was a new and powerful body with an unusually wide range of functions and it was therefore appropriate for Parliament to have some say in the scale of resources to be provided. The initial allocation was not limited to any particular period, although then, as now, a five-year period was the norm.

Despite the fact that the agency has only used £140 4 million in its first three years of operation, the then Labour Administration decided to raise the agency's financial limit to £500 million, raisable by order £800 million, in the Industry Act 1979. That strikes me as being rather like the dying throes of a doomed Government. It was not exactly a blank cheque, as the figure was specified, but it was certainly unsigned, undated and not underwritten.

During deliberations on the 1979 Bill, and then again in our 1979 election manifesto, this Government made it clear that, by and large, we were happy with, and would continue fully to support to the maximum extent that financial resources would allow, the agency's activities, but that we would take a critical look at the way it performed its role in practice.

Given the agency's expenditure in its first three years of operation, the new limits set in the 1979 Act were clearly exaggerated, as experience has shown. In coming to office, therefore, the Government decided to inject some realism into the funding of the agency and to set it on a new, more sharply defined course. The Industry Act 1980 therefore stabilised the agency's long-term budget at £500 million and removed the provision to raise that limit by order to £800 million.

In the 1981 Act, which was needed to increase the financial limits of the National Enterprise Board for the period prior to abolition and of the Welsh Development Agency, the opportunity was taken to increase the SDA's financial limit to £700 million. That was a realistic limit that would have lasted until 1988 but for the discovery that certain expenditure had been wrongly excluded by the agency from expenditure counting towards the limit. Because of the necessary retrospective changes, the agency is now expected to reach its financial limit before the end of this financial year, hence the need for this legislation. The Bill therefore seeks to set a new limit of £1,200 million. The Government feel that it is realistic, given the present spending patterns, and should provide the agency with sufficient funds for about the next five years. At that time, it would obviously be right to have another fundamental look at the agency against the then prevailing financial climate.

As I suggested, that was the sort of review period envisaged by the previous Labour Administration when the agency was set up and we see no need to change the position. Had we not drastically altered the course of the agency it would be not be the success story that it is today. It has changed out of all recognition since its inception in 1975. Even when it was clear that the agency could not spend the unrealistic amount that it was initially allocated, the previous Labour Administration raised its financial limit even higher in 1979. The Government have brought some sense to prevail both in the funding of the agency in 1980 and since and in how it carries out its functions. Since then, under the guidance of the present Administration, the agency has become much more receptive to the needs of the market and more conscious of the need to include, at all levels, the private sector in the economic development of Scotland.
