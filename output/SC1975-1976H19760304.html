: When the Committee rose on Tuesday, Mrs. Jeger, I was within sight of concluding my remarks on the Question of which you have just reminded the Committee. My purpose in those remarks was to emphasise the all-important distinction, so often blurred, between an intention and an objective, which we might—and in this case all do—share, and the appropriateness or otherwise of specific methods proposed to be adopted for the achievement of the objective, and to reiterate what, but for experience, it might seem unnecessary to reiterate—namely, that acceptance of the objective does not bind those who accept it to accept also whatever methods of obtaining it may be proposed, and conversely that no attack, however sharp, which my hon. Friends and I may launch upon the methods proposed in the Bill can fairly or reasonably be taken to imply any divergence or weakness on our part in support of the objectives.

Earlier in my remarks I had ventured to suggest that often representative bodies, and more particularly what I called archetypal representative bodies like the CBI and the TUC, are particularly vulnerable to the temptation to give way to what they believe is the prevailing fashion and therefore, unhappily, add their apparent weight and support to measures which do not commend themselves to their prudence or their experience.
