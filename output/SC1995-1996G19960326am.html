Mr. Curry
: Let me respond immediately to the allegation made by the hon. Member for Greenwich that there are parts of the Bill that do not sit comfortably with each other. If, on reflection, I find that there is inadequate precision in the wording, I shall of course take steps to ensure that the Bill is coherent by the time it completes its passage.

The new provisions require authorities to adopt a graduated approach to housing problems. Initially, if someone is homeless, threatened with homelessness, or at risk of becoming homeless, he can approach the advisory service that the authority will be required to have available. The advice may enable him to remain in his present accommodation, or may show him how to gain access to a range of alternatives, including social housing, the private rented sector, low cost home ownership, and— in appropriate cases—hostels and lodgings. In some JPEG 799 authorities a common housing register may serve to match needs and opportunities across the whole range of accommodation. The advice will also explain households' rights under the homelessness legislation.

That person may at that stage apply for assistance under the homelessness legislation. If, after making the necessary inquiries the authority considers the applicant to be in priority need, unintentionally homeless, and eligible for assistance, it will need to consider whether there is suitable alternative accommodation available within its area.

In that context, the word "suitable" covers a range of attributes including whether the accommodation is of the right size; whether it is, where necessary, affordable for someone on housing benefit; or whether the landlord is willing to take someone with children. If the authority is satisfied that such accommodation is available, its duty is therefore to provide such advice and assistance as it considers appropriate to assist the person in the attempts which he may make to secure that accommodation.

Many local authorities will already be familiar with the private rented sector in their area. As other authorities develop private rented sector strategies, they will become familiar with what property is available and will know where to direct people. They may forge links with particular landlords or accommodation agencies that give them access to a particular set of property. They may also assist people by offering rent guarantees where appropriate.

The alternative accommodation provision is a halfway stage between the purely advisory role of authorities and the full homelessness duty. It requires the individual to take some responsibility for arranging his own accommodation, but it also requires the authority to be confident that the accommodation, really is available, and to give the individual the requisite support. Simply handing out a list of advertisements taken from the local newspaper would not be sufficient to meet the requirements of the legislation.
