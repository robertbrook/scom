STANDING COMMITTEE ON SCOTTISH BILLS.
Tuesday, 14th March, 1933.

[Mr. ENTWISTLE in the Chair.]

HOUSING (FINANCIAL PROVISIONS) (SCOTLAND) BILL.

[OFFICIAL REPORT.]
CLAUSE 1.
—(Reduction of subsidies under 14 & 15 Geo. 5. c. 35 in respect of houses subject to special conditions provided by local authorities themselves.)

Sir MURDOCH MCKENZIE WOOD
: I beg to move, in page 2, line 31, at the end, to add the words:

"Provided that any house included in a general statement furnished to the Department in pursuance of section twenty-two, sub-section (2), of the Housing (Scotland) Act, 1930, shall be deemed to have been included in proposals submitted to the Department within the meaning of this section."

The point raised in this Amendment is similar to that which has been already dealt with in previous Amendments. It is the question as to the point at which the line is to be drawn between the old and the new terms to be given for the building of houses. The Act of 1930 lays a statutory duty upon local authorities to send in a general statement as to their housing requirements, and as to what they propose to do to meet those requirements over three years. The first period of three years would not have come to an end till October of this year. What was the object of Parliament in laying this statutory duty upon local authorities? It was obviously, I suggest, to give them an opportunity of making a full survey of what they needed, and also an opportunity of spreading out the work over the whole of the three years, so that the work might be done as economically as possible and in the way they would suit the particular local authorities best. The local authorities which put forward this general statement, obviously must be the best judge as to the way in which 822 they did this. This Bill proposes summarily to abbreviate that period, and it will almost certainly take away from the local authorities the power of carrying out certain proposals which they had meant to carry out.

I see that the Secretary of State for Scotland has put down an Amendment to Clause 2 which, I imagine is intended to meet a similar point. I am glad to see that Amendment, but it seems to me that the Amendment which he thinks is required to Clause 2 is equally required to Clause 1. It seems to be meant to meet a grievance of the larger authorities rather than of the smaller authorities. It is quite likely that in a general statement, such as the Act of 1930 had in mind, details would not be put forward by the smaller authorities. After all, they did not have the facilities at the beginning for drawing up their schemes in detail. They had not the highly paid and qualified architects and engineers who can draw up general statements in sufficient detail in the beginning as to meet the requirements of this Amendment. I hope that the Secretary of State will realise that the small authorities are in a very different position from the larger authorities in this way. What is the argument which the Under-Secretary put forward as a defence against the breaking of a pledge, as I suggest that this is? He said that any Parliament is able to undo anything which has been done by any previous Parliament, or, indeed, anything that has been done on previous occasion by the same Parliament. No one disputes the power of Parliament to do that. The question is the expediency and equity of it.

If any Government is going to argue that any pledge put in an Act of Parliament is not binding upon the Government of the day, there is no use at all in putting pledges into Acts of Parliament, and local authorities will not pay the slightest attention to any pledge that may be given by a Government, whether it is enshrined in an Act of Parliament or not. I suggest that this is a case where something should be done by the Government to meet an undoubted grievance. I can understand that some of the general statements may be couched in such vague terms that it may be difficult to accept this Amendment in the fullness with which it is put on the JPEG 823 Paper, but I hope that I have convinced the Secretary of State that there is a point to be met, and I hope that a real attempt will be made to meet it.

The UNDER-SECRETARY of STATE for SCOTLAND (Mr. Skelton)
: The main proposition of my hon. Friend was that the three years' programme, which under the 1930 Act the local authorities were compelled by Statute to submit to the Department of Health, involved a pledge. A pledge by whom, and to whom? I think that the Committee will see, as soon as we try to answer that question, that there has been no question of a pledge, and the relations between local authorities and the Department of Health in this matter during the currency of the three years have made it clear that neither side regarded the thing in the nature of a pledge at all. The original form of the return in the 1924 Act was two years, and the local authorities were originally required to send biennial returns. If that was a pledge, it was broken in the 1930 Act, when the two years was changed into three years. However, that is a comparatively small point.

Let us look at the facts. If there had been a pledge, was it a one-sided or a two-sided pledge? Were the local authorities to be compelled to complete their programmes within three years? Is that my hon. Friend's view, or is it that they were not to be held by the pledge, but that the State was to be held by its pledge? Surely if there were a pledge at all, it must have bound both parties. I think that my hon. Friend will agree with that. If he does, he will also have to agree that the Department of Health would have been the first to insist—in some cases, no doubt, against the interests of the local authorities, and against their own judgment—on the completion of this programme by the end of 1933. Nothing of that sort has happened. It was merely a general statement of the amount of work which they anticipated they would be able to get through by the end of 1933, and the Section does not make it obligatory upon the local authorities to fulfil the work, or upon the Department of Health to insist upon that work. Therefore, there is no question of a pledge. Had there been a pledge or a duty upon the local autho- 824 rities to make, and a duty upon the Department of Health to insist upon, a complete fulfilment by the end of 1933, the situation would have been statutory, and, in fact, quite different.

Mr. SKELTON

The truth of the matter is that the statutory return of 1930, the triennial return which was substituted for the previous biennial return, was not in the nature of a pledge given either by the local authorities to build, or by the State to insist upon the completion of these houses, or to give any particular amount of subsidy for the houses. In the circumstances, I must ask the Committee not to accept this Amendment, because the basis of it is quite untenable, and the attempt to make local authorities fulfil what is regarded as a pledge to build 36,000 houses before December next is something which is incapable of fulfilment, and I venture to say that it would be unreasonable for the Committee to insist upon it.

When we come down to the hard-and-fast facts of the situation, I do not think the arguments of the Under-Secretary meet the position. In the figures which he has given us, the Under-Secretary has grouped together all the authorities, those which have tried and those which have not tried, and I venture to say that while Glasgow could not have fulfilled the whole programme it came nearer to fulfilling the whole programme than any part of Scotland. Why should a local authority which came very near to fulfilling the bargain be penalised? It ought not to be penalised because it took the Government's circular at its face value. How would any Member of this Committee have read that circular? We should have said that we JPEG 827 were dealing with men who were reasonable and honest and who would give us a fair deal, and we should have acted accordingly. In view of the situation, I submit that this is a very modest, indeed, even a meagre, Amendment, and I think the Under-Secretary ought to have accepted it. I think this is the least which the Scottish Standing Committee had a right to expect from the Government. I was hoping we should get more. From my conversations with City of Glasgow representatives, I think they have been expecting more. Therefore, I hope that the Under-Secretary has not closed the door upon this Amendment, and that he will reconsider his decision and put the local authorities in the position to which they are entitled.
