Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: With the Committee's permission, I shall deal with all the amendments together, because they are related. The 10 amendments would introduce a rigid definition of a rave and restrict that definition in a number of ways. I am concerned that they would severely restrict the practical impact of the Bill.

The policy intention behind the Bill, as I am sure hon. Members will all appreciate, is to introduce safeguards for the people who attend events in licensed premises. Amendments Nos. 1, 7, 11 and 22 would introduce a definition of a rave to clause 1 and make it refer to "raves and other gatherings" instead of the neutral word "events". That would restrict the application of the clause. It does not matter what the event is called as long as the Bill ensures that all events, regardless of their name, that may pose a risk to the health of patrons are covered by the new provisions. I am therefore extremely wary of using the word "rave" in the Bill because it would restrict the type of event to which the clause applies.

Several of the witnesses who gave evidence to the Special Standing Committee, in particular the Scottish Drugs Forum and the Association of Chief Police Officers in Scotland, also said that the word "rave" should not be used to define events. The meaning of the word "rave" has changed over the past few years and it may just as easily change again. We should therefore avoid using a word that could soon change or even become obsolete. By simply describing the three characteristics of the JPEG 119 events that boards may make conditions to regulate, the Bill provides a framework within which boards can define more precisely the activity that they intend to control.

Amendments Nos. 2, 15 and 18 would all delete references to dancing in clause 1. I hope to persuade hon. Members that they are unnecessary. I believe that the amendments have been suggested out of a concern that the reference to dancing will exclude so-called concert-type raves from the operation of the Bill.

I am advised that a concert-type rave is one at which people go along primarily to listed to a band or to admire the skills of a disc jockey.

Mr. McFall

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: There are too many to name.

I should like to draw hon. Members' attention to the definition of dancing that appears in new clause 1:

"'dancing' includes any movement apparently to the accompaniment of music".

That would include the movements of people tapping their feet or nodding their heads to the performance. I assure hon. Members that attendees of concert-type raves also engage in what we would consider to be more conventional dancing of the modern type. Therefore, the reference to dancing is not unduly restrictive: it is an important feature of the Bill. We want to regulate events that pose a threat to health. They are primarily dance events, at which frenetic dancing often, but not necessarily exacerbated by drug misuse, leads to exhaustion and dehydration.

Amendments Nos. 12, 13 and 14 would introduce new restrictions to the definition of the type of event to which clause 1 applies. The result of that is that some potentially dangerous dance events are not caught by the new provisions. The three amendments would bring the definition of 'rave' in clause 1 more into line with the definition of "rave" used in section 63 of the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994. But for what purpose?

10.45 am

The provisions of the 1994 Act were designed to deal with large, noisy outdoor gatherings at night that were likely to cause a public nuisance and cause distress to local residents. The definition in the 1994 Act— a minimum attendance requirement of 100, music that is amplified and played at night—reflect that. In contrast the Bill is designed to introduce safeguards for the health of people who attend dance events of whatever size in licensed premises. Because they may not be held outdoors and smaller numbers may attend, those events do not necessarily present public nuisance problems. The definition in the 1994 Act is simply not appropriate.

Applying a minimum attendance requirement could exclude a good number of smaller venues, especially pubs, from having to meet the conditions imposed by the licensing boards. Applying requirements that music must be amplified and played at night would also exclude events, because music is not necessarily amplified at an indoor event and because such events can be held during the day. Our policy is to encourage best practice in all clubs and pubs, not to provide a let-out for certain organisers or venues.

New Clause 1
LICENSING CONDITIONS FOR CERTAIN EVENTS

'.—(1) In the Licensing (Scotland) Act 1976 (in this Act referred to as "the 1976 Act"), after section 18 (giving reasons for decision of licensing board) there shall be inserted the following section—

"Licensing conditions for certain events."

18A.—(1) In granting—

  1. a licence under section 17 of this Act;
  2. an occasional licence under section 33 of this Act; or
  3. an occasional permission under section 34 of this Act,
in respect of premises on or a place at which it appears to the licensing board that any event to which this section applies may be held, the licensing board shall attach to the licence or permission conditions for the purpose of safeguarding the health and safety of persons attending any such event.

(2) The duty of a licensing board under subsection (1) above to attach conditions to a licence or permission is without prejudice to any power of the board under this Act to attach or impose conditions to or in respect of a licence or permission.

(3) This section applies to any event—

  1. at which music is played or relayed;
  2. which consists of or includes the opportunity for persons attending the event to engage in dancing and
  3. at which it appears to the board that offences may be committed in relation to controlled drugs within the meaning of section 2 of the Misuse of Drugs Act 1971.

(4) Without prejudice to the generality of subsection (1) above, the conditions may—

  1. provide that no event to which this section applies, or no such description of such events as may determined by the board, shall be held without the consent of the board; and
  2. require the holder of the licence to meet such requirements as are specified by such person as may be determined by the board.

(5) The holder of a licence may appeal to the sheriff against—

  1. the terms of any condition imposed under subsection (1) above; and
  2. the refusal of consent under subsection (4)(a) above.

(6) The holder of a licence or his employee or agent shall be guilty of an offence if he contravenes any condition attached to the licence or permission under subsection (1) above.

(7) It shall be a defence for a person charged with an offence under subsection (6) above to prove that he took all reasonable steps to comply with the condition.

(8) In this section—

"holder of licence" includes a person to whom an occasional permission has been granted under section 34 of this Act;


"dancing" includes any movement apparently to the accompaniment of music; and


"music" includes sounds wholly or predominantly characterised by the emission of a succession of repetitive beats.

(9) In the application of section 67(1)(a) and (c) and (2) to an offence under subsection (6) above, "licence-holder" includes a person to whom an occasional permission has been granted."

(2) In section 31 of the 1976 Act—

  1. in subsection (2), at the end insert—
  2. "(c) that there has been a breach of the conditions attached to the licence under section 18A(1) of this Act.";
  3. in subsection (3)(a) after the word "thereunder" there shall be inserted the words "or of any condition attached to a licence under the said section 18A(1)".
