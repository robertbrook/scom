Annexe to Letter from the Association of Chief Police Officers in Scotland:

Letter to Scottish Office Home and Health Department

Licensing (Amendment) (Scotland) Bill

I refer to your letter dated 5th December, 1995, in connection with the above subject and would advise that this matter has now been considered by our General Purposes Standing Committee.

The Association notes that this matter will be considered by a Special Standing Committee and we have already received correspondence in this connection from the Clerk to the Scottish Affairs Committee. In the meantime we would wish to make the following comments on the Bill.

While we recognise that the Bill is an attempt to safeguard the health of those persons attending "raves" in licensed premises by allowing Boards to impose conditions relating to such matters as the availability of water "cooling off areas and medical facilities, we foresee problems arising over the definition of what is or is not a "rave". Moreover, the Bill does not cover events held outwith licensed premises and so-called "underground raves" cannot therefore be controlled. Many of these are regulated by the issue of a Public Entertainments Licence granted under Section 41 of the Civic Government (Scotland) Act 1982, and while the Bill proposes to amend the Licensing (Scotland) Act 1976, perhaps the 1982 Act should also be amended which would allow Licensing Committees to impose similar conditions.

The proposals enable Licensing Boards to exercise greater control over the holding of raves, and this appears to be a generally good idea but we are concerned that the proposals might suggest to some that the taking of drugs at such events is considered acceptable provided certain precautions are in place. It may create difficulties for the 90 police should objections or observations be raised in relation to the grant or renewal of a licence when the very wording of the Bill seems to almost expect Licensing Boards to acknowledge the commission of drugs offences at these events.

Finally, it was felt that the second part of the Bill, which relates to the composition of Licensing Boards, has no particular implications for the police.

Memorandum by the Law Society of Scotland

The Licensing Law Working Party and the Criminal Law Committee of the Law Society of Scotland have considered the Licensing (Amendment) (Scotland) Bill. The Society has accordingly the following comments to make:

General Comments

The Society is pleased that the important stages of this bill will be taken in Scotland. It is particularly noted that the Bill will be reviewed by a Special Standing Committee which will take evidence from those with an interest in the measure. This procedure was employed in relation to the Children (Scotland) Bill and ensures that Members of Parliament are fully acquainted with all the issues arising from the measure. The Society contributed to that exercise and is of the view that that procedure enhanced Parliament's consideration of the Bill. It is hoped that a similar result will ensue from the procedure adopted for the Licensing (Amendment) (Scotland) Bill.

Specific Comments

Clause 1 (Licensing Conditions for Raves)

The Society commends the proposal that licensing boards should be under a duty in making byelaws to have regard to the need to safeguard the health of persons attending raves.

The Society nevertheless considers that there are a number of difficulties which could preclude the effective operation of the measure. These are:

1.

  1. The provisions of new section 38A(1) apply only if a licensing board decides to make byelaws. No duty is imposed on boards to make byelaws and accordingly, if a board does not opt to make such measures, the provision will have no effect. At present there are 56 licensing boards in Scotland of which 43 have issued byelaws of any description.
  2. The Society is of the view that the provisions of new section 38A(2) could be improved so that those who misuse drugs at raves could be better protected from the effects of drug abuse. However, the clause does not provide that other measures can be taken to prevent or restrain the misuse of drugs at raves.
  3. The Society is of the view that licensing boards should have a duty to impose conditions to discourage the misuse of drugs at raves. In particular there should be more policing at such events, adequate provision of properly trained and regulated stewards, control of the 98 numbers of persons attending such events, and provision for raves to be recorded by closed circuit TV.
  4. The Society considers that the provisions of new section 38A(3) are vague and open to a number of interpretations.

The word "event" is not defined and could be interpreted as relating to events not currently recognised as raves—e.g. discos or other types of dances. The Society is of the view that the Bill should be amended to apply specifically to "raves". The Society has framed amendments to ensure that raves are defined as "raves or other gatherings" which

  1. are held in licensed premises;
  2. at which amplified music is played at night;
  3. where 100 persons or more attend; and
  4. where a licensing board has "reasonable suspicion" that offences against the Misuse of Drugs Act 1971 may take place.
This definition of "rave" bears some relationship to that contained in the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994 particularly in respect of the qualification that a certain number of persons must attend and that the rave involves playing amplified music at night.

The Society's amendments delete any reference in the Bill to "dancing" so ensuring that "concert type" raves as well as "house-parties" are brought into the scope of the measures such a definition obviates the difficult issue of interpretation of the Bill.

(d) The society also considers that the provisions of 38A(3)(d) could result in the unreasonable exercise of discretion by boards which could provoke an increased number of actions for judicial review. The Society's amendments seek to require "reasonable suspicion" that offences could take place rather than mere appearance.

2. The Society is of the view that the provisions of the Civic Government (Scotland) Act 1982 relating to public entertainment licences could produce a more effective result within the context of existing legislation. In particular, it could be provided that obtaining a public entertainment licence should be a condition for the grant of an occasional licence or occasional extension for a rave. This would enable the local authorities to employ the provisions of the 1982 Act. The Society has not presented an amendment in these terms but makes the suggestion for the consideration of the Committee.

3. The Society is also of the view that it would be useful for the Secretary of State for Scotland to issue guidance as to the types of events which he would expect to be covered by these provisions and the types of conditions which he would expect licensing boards to impose for the purpose of safeguarding the health and safety of persons attending raves.

4. The Society considers that licensing boards should be permitted to raise complaints under section 31 of the Licensing (Scotland) Act 1976 ex proprio motu and that the appropriate amendment should be made to section 16(1) of the Act. At present the following persons or bodies can complain to licensing boards; 5 SC63-PAG1/5 JPEG 99

  1. owner/occupiers in the neighbourhood;
  2. Community Councils;
  3. Churches;
  4. Chief Constables;
  5. Fire Authorities; and
  6. Local Authorities.

Licensing boards are not empowered to make complaints under Section 31. The Society is of the view that licensing boards are well placed to make such complaints and that they should be allowed so to do.
