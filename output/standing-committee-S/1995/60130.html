Licensing (Amendment) (Scotland) Bill

Submission by Sheriff Neil Gow QC

  1. I am delighted that the Standing Committee has selected Ayr as one of the places where evidence is to be heard. The sad deaths of the three young men at Hanger 13 and the subsequent Fatal Accident Inquiry which I conducted have focussed public attention on the drugs menace.
  2. I am also particularly gratified that the house has seen fit to give early implementation to the recommendations which I made at the FAI. I am only too well aware that judicial recommendations of this kind are suitably welcomed at the time, but are then placed in a pigeon hole in the Scottish Office and forgotten about for years.
  3. I have read the terms of the draft Bill, and also the official report of the Scottish Grand Committee hearing in Glasgow on Monday 18 December 1995, which was kindly provided to me by the Hon. Member for Ayr.

Most of the points which I am going to offer for consideration by Hon. Members are probably technical points for the Committee stage.

Definition of a Rave

There is already a definition of "rave" in Section 63 of the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994 which applies to "a gathering on land in the open air of 100 or more persons (whether or not trespassers) at which amplified music is played during the night etc". This definition of a rave did not in fact apply to the premises at Hanger 13. It is to be noted that "land in the open air" includes land partly in the open air, see Section 63(1)(10) and would thus include a barn or an aircraft hangar (sic) with the doors open, but not such buildings with the door shut. Clause 38(A)(3) gives a pretty effective definition of a rave and in my opinion is probably adequate. The vital part is the reference to "offences may be committed in relation to controlled drugs", which in the normal way would exclude boy scout dances or Scottish country dance music evenings. The definition of "music" in clause 38(A)(1)(5) is the same as that given in Section 63 of the 1994 Act. It is obviously convenient that the definition given of a word in one particular Act should be the same as that given in another Act, otherwise confusion could result.

Training of Stewards

The proper training of stewards is very important. Stewards are usually selected more for their brawn than their brain. I quite frequently hear cases of breach of the peace and assault where stewards appear to have exceeded their normal powers and used excessive or unnecessary force.

Stewards Right to Search

Stewards have a right at common law to detain any person whom they suspect of carrying or dealing in drugs pending the arrival of the police. The position regarding searches is not so clear. When a person enters premises where a rave is being conducted, he is invariably subject to quite a severe search. It is of course an implied condition of his entering the premises that he subjects himself to such a search.

I recently heard a case at Ayr which highlighted the problems with regard to searches by stewards. In that case stewards saw a young man whom they suspected loitering outside Hanger 13 in an area resorted to by drug suppliers, and suspecting that he might be involved in drugs, took him inside the building and called the police. As they did so they felt a bulge under his arm, and suspecting that he might be carrying drugs or a weapon such as a knife, they searched him. They did not find a knife, but they found 78 Temazepam tablets which they took from him and handed to the police when they arrived. The search was undoubtedly "irregular", but the legal question was whether, in the circumstances, the stewards were entitled to search the young man, having suspected he was carrying drugs or a weapon. I held that although the search was irregular, the evidence was competent, and having heard the evidence, I duly convicted the accused and sentenced him to the maximum of three months imprisonment. The case has now been appealed to the justiciary appeal court, and it will be interesting to see whether the appeal court decides that stewards have a right to search at common law in such circumstances. The alternative would be to put a power of search in the model regulations, but this could be difficult, as important matters of principle and civil liberty would become involved.

Right of Appeal to Sheriff

The draft Bill includes a clause allowing for a right of appeal to the Sheriff, and impliedly to the Court of Session on a point of law. The Sheriff Clark suggests that after the reference to right of appeal to the Sheriff the words "by way of summary application" should be added. Administrative appeals of this type usually proceed by way of summary application for which there is a standard procedure laid down, and it might be advisable to make this clear in the clause.

Sheriff Neil Gow
: These are technical matters. I would certainly like to see more consideration given to the qualifications of stewards. As I said in my observations, stewards are to my mind chosen for their brawn rather than for their brains. I am also concerned about the right to search, which I mentioned in my observations. If you will permit me, Mr. Mckelvey, I would like to enlarge on the subject for a moment. I know of a recent case from Hanger 13, which I narrated in my observation. A young man was suspected of having drugs on his person for supply to others. He was put outside but they did not find any drugs on him. Later in the evening he was seen loitering in an area immediately outside Hanger 13 which was known to be frequented by drug suppliers. The steward got hold of him and felt something suspicious on his person. The young person was then taken inside the building on the grounds that he either had a weapon on him or was carrying illegal drugs.

He was searched there and then, which was strictly beyond the normal common law powers of a citizen who suspects a person of committing an offence. The stewards were entitled under common law to detain him, pending the arrival of the police, but the question arose whether they were also entitled to search him. There was no doubt in my mind that the search they carried out was irregular, but the legal question was whether the evidence, having being obtained in an irregular way without a search warrant and so on, was none the less competent in my court.

I ruled in that case that although there was no search warrant, the circumstances—with the stewards being afraid that the person might be carrying a knife, which he was not—entitled them to search him in the interest of their own safety. The stewards found 78 temazepam tablets on him, which they handed over to the police. That evidence was admissible. I convicted the young man and sentenced him to three months' imprisonment, but the case has been referred to the Justiciary Appeal Court and I understand that the case will probably be heard in the late spring—round about April. It will be a very significant case in this particular area of the law. It will clarify whether stewards can search in those circumstances.
