I think that I can say that the Committee would be unanimous in the view that it would be wrong that a child who, at the end of the day, might be acquitted and Image Col. 1033 found not to be guilty of the perhaps very serious crime with which he was charged, should in the interim run the risk of having his identity splashed across the newspapers and the media and thereafter always be known as the child who was charged with murder and, no doubt in the minds of many people, was lucky to get away with it. That is another factor covered in the clause but which does not apply at present in the law and where there would be unanimity that the law ought to be reformed at the very least to ensure that all youngsters who have not even been convicted of a crime but merely charged with one should not have their identity revealed in the course of the trial when it is not known whether a guilty verdict would be returned.

Another area of importance is the fact that we are dealing not only with children charged and convicted of crimes but with children who are witnesses in a trial. We have to subdivide to make the debate meaningful. There are two kinds of child witness. There is the child witness who is the victim of the person accused, whether it is incest, rape, interference or a serious assault. It is often thought to be very much in the interests of that child that his or her identity should not be revealed, not only because it may be embarrassing, but because the fact of knowing that identity will be published may dissuade people from coming forward in the first place. That is another important consideration that the Committee will want to bear in mind.

There is a second kind of child witness—the witness who is only incidental to the crime and who just happens to see something. The child might be walking along Sauchiehall Street when he sees a man running out of a bank. He may, therefore, be able to give important evidence in the trial as to that man's identity. It might be a child who has seen a road traffic accident and who could identify the registration number of the car. This category covers a child who has not been involved either as a victim or as the accused but happens to be an important, or a vital, witness. I do not see any compelling reason in terms of public policy why there should be an assumption from the beginning that that child's identity should never be revealed.

There may be cases when the court would consider it inappropriate even in these circumstances to reveal the identity of the child, but I think that there is a strong case for saying that, whatever we Col. 1034 think about other matters, the onus should be against publication in the case of a child who is neither the accused nor named in the charge but who is intimately involved with the crime or the offence committed. The Government's amendments would have taken that matter into account.

Clause 22 provides that in solemn as well as in summary jurisdiction, as is the case at present, a child who has been convicted of an offence should, in normal circumstances, not have his identity published. But there is a qualification to that: it is unless the court decides otherwise. My hon. Friends are right to point out, as the press has done, that it is all very well to give the court the right to use its discretion, but who will remind the court of that right and who will put the point to the court so that the court can consider whether, in the particular circumstances of a crime, it might be desirable and in the public interest that publication be released?

I accept that there is no specific provision in the clause that relates to that matter, but I can give the Committee some information that will greatly reassure my hon. Friends and may be perfectly acceptable to the Opposition as well. It is the intention of the Crown Office to issue a standing instruction to all advocates depute and fiscals that at the end of a trial which has involved children which in normal circumstances would not give the press an opportunity to publish the identity of the children, the court should be reminded by the depute or fiscal that it has the discretion to allow publication if the court believes it to be in the public interest. Therefore, in all cases it will be drawn to the attention of the court that this is not an absolute prohibition but a discretionary matter that the court is entitled to overrule if it is satisfied that, in the circumstances of the case, there would be no reason why publication should not be permitted. That is a substantial change on the existing practice.

Under existing practices, as my hon. Friends will be aware, unless the court remembers the discretion or unless it happens to be mentioned by accident during the course of submissions to the court, the possibility may not arise and the opportunity may be lost.

There is also a specific provision in the clause that, even if the court has not permitted publication, if there are circumstances which suggest to the Secretary Image Col. 1035 of State that publication in the public interest would be appropriate he may so require, and that would then take place. May I take solemn and summary appeal procedures together and make just a few brief remarks? The most important aspect arises out of the change which allows a court to call for a retrial. I recognise that retrial has been successfully operated in England for a lengthy period. There is a strong case against it and I know that there is strong opposition to it—or at least anxiety about it—throughout the legal profession in Scotland.

The Solicitor-General for Scotland, who I think is replying to the debate, will remember that under the new Section 228 of the 1975 Act, subsection (1) (c) says that there can be an appeal to the High Court in this case—it is the same in summary cases—

"against both such conviction and such sentence".

Among the things that can be done in settling that appeal is order a retrial.

The difficulty about a retrial is that it is obviously a highly artificial situation in which witnesses are going through the panoply of examination-in-chief and cross-examination again. There is also a possibility that the second jury will be heavily prejudiced by all the press reports and publicity that we know from our earlier debates attend any murder trial in Scotland. There is a strong feeling among many involved in the trade that it would be better to deal with this not by retrial but by hearing any fresh evidence that there may be. It is contemplated in terms of section 228(2) where there is an alleged miscarriage because either evidence was not available or fresh evidence has come to hand. If those are the grounds for allowing the ordering of a retrial, it would surely be better just to hear the fresh evidence than to go through all the previous evidence. I can say no more about that Image Col. 1059 matter. I hope that the Government will give it consideration.

I turn to new section 236A of the 1975 Act. I do not understand it. It says:

"As soon as is reasonably practicable … the judge … shall furnish the Clerk of Justiciary with a report",

and, in subsection (2):

"Where the judge's report is not furnished … the High Court may call for such report".

What puzzles me is that surely a report is mandatory, because subsection (1) says "shall furnish", yet clearly that is wrong because, in subsection (2),

"the High Court may call for"

it, and that is certainly naught but permissive. Presumably, on any layman's interpretation of that language, if the High Court did not exercise option 2, the report might never be furnished. That seems to me, at best, a confusion, and it should be made clear that the report will always be furnished.

May I draw attention also to the case, which will be familiar to the Solicitor-General, of Ferguson, Petitioner, which appears in the Scots Law Times of 18 January 1980. In that case a man marked an appeal and was not granted legal aid and, as so often happens, the solicitor—I ought to declare an interest; it is a firm which is well known and with which I am, in fact, connected—found, as a service, a friendly counsel who was prepared to appear on the spot and give what advice he could. His advice was that there was no possible appeal. The man then said "Please abandon the appeal", but, because it was not in writing before the case was called, they were told that the appeal could not be abandoned. The High Court took its undoubted power of increasing what I remember to be a sentence of 18 months to one of four years.

This brings up two matters under section 244. One is the question: when may one abandon an appeal? It is not clear from section 244 that the Ferguson situation could not recur. It is important that we make clear that an appeal may be abandoned at the calling in order to avoid such an unfortunate situation.

Secondly, there is a strong case which perhaps Ministers should think about on Report. That is that we should abandon the High Court's right to increase sentences as it has done. It is anomalous. I know that it is a useful disincentive to appealing. Col. 1060 In the days of the previous Lord Justice General, appeals were almost outlawed. I have one further important point. There are many textual points about time limits and so on into which we ought to go, but there is one of real importance about which I feel strongly. That is, for example, in section 231, where the new wording begins:

"Subject to section 236B(2) of this Act, where a person desires to appeal …, he shall, within two weeks of the final determination of the proceedings…"

This applies to summary appeals also. Its effect is that, if one wishes to appeal against conviction, one cannot do so until one has been finally disposed of.

In the summary courts in particular, if one is put, for example, on deferred sentence for a year—which is a common disposal—one cannot appeal against conviction until the end of the deferred sentence. It may be over a year later, and one must live with, possibly, a good appeal against conviction but be unable to lodge it. I believe strongly that one ought to be allowed to lodge an appeal against conviction only, as soon as the conviction has been recorded, and not have to wait until the final determination, which may be so long delayed.

There are many other points, but I hope that these at least the Minister will consider.
