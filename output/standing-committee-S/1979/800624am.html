EVIDENCE BY LETTER OF REQUEST OR ON COMMISSION


"(1) In any criminal proceedings in the High Court or the sheriff court the prosecutor or the defence may, at an appropriate time, apply to a judge of the court in which the trial is to take place (or, if that is not yet known, to a judge of the High Court) for—

  1. the issue of a letter of request to a court, or tribunal, exercising jurisdiction in a country or territory outside the United Kingdom, Channel Islands, and Isle of Man for the examination of a witness resident in the said country or territory; or
    2. 1482
  2. the appointment of a commissioner to examine, at any place in the United Kingdom, Channel Islands, or Isle of Man, a witness who by reason of being ill or infirm is unable to attend the trial diet.
  1. A hearing, as regards any application under subsection (1) above by a party, shall be conducted in chambers but may be dispensed with if the application is not opposed. The application may be granted only if the judge is satisfied that—
    1. the evidence which it is averred the witness is able to give is necessary for the proper adjudication of the trial; and
    2. there would be no unfairness to the other party were such evidence to be received in the form of the record of an examination conducted by virtue of that subsection.
  2. Any such record as is mentioned in paragraph ( b) of subsection (2) above shall, without being sworn to by witnesses, be received in evidence in so far as it either accords with the averment mentioned in paragraph ( a) of that subsection or can be so received without unfairness to either party.

    1. solemn proceedings, any time before the oath is administered to the jury;
    2. summary proceedings, any time before the first witness is sworn,
  5. or (but only in relation to an application under paragraph ( b) of that subsection) any time during the course of the trial if the circumstances on which the application is based had not arisen, or would not have merited such application, within the period mentioned in paragraph ( a), or as the case may be ( b), of this subsection.

The Solicitor-General for Scotland

(Mr. Nicholas Fairbairn)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

There has always been in the civil law of Scotland the right to take by commission and diligence the evidence of witnesses who could not be present. There has never been such a right in any criminal courts in solemn or other proceedings.

New clause 11 introduces the right of either the prosecution or the defence to take the evidence of a witness who is abroad by letter of request before a commissioner who will be judicially sworn. There is an absolute safeguard, as either side can apply for such a request, and either side can object to such a request if there is the slightest suggestion that the JPEG 1483 evidence will be controversial and cannot properly be taken and cross-examined by this process.

But in recent years there has been an increasing number of witnesses who had to give formal evidence at a time when they were on holiday with their families, particularly police officers and forensic experts, and had to return to the United Kingdom—sometimes having to abandon their holiday—to give formal evidence which was unchallenged, in order to meet the requirements of the law of evidence.

It was recommended by the Thomson committee that we should have that procedure. It seems to me to do nothing to interfere in any way with the standards of evidence but may in many cases go a long way towards reducing the resentment which appearance in court, and certainly the inconvenience, is imposed upon those who serve the public in one capacity or another. The matter is restricted to the High Court and the sheriff court. It will not be available in the district court and it may not frequently be used, but it seems to be a useful reform and I commend it to the Committee.
