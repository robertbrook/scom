Clause 44
DETENTION OF YOUNG OFFENDERS

Question proposed [ this day], That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

4.30 p.m.

Question again proposed.

Mr. George Foulkes
: I am interested to note that the Under-Secretary of State has been translated—I was about to say promoted—to the end of the Government Front Bench. I had an opportunity of exchanging a brief word with him informally outside the Committee. He admitted—I do not think that he will mind my saying this—that what we are dealing with is not an easy matter.

What I was saying in a somewhat whimsical way when we adjourned has a serious underlying intention and concern. The Government are responsible for the matter. They are putting forward the Bill; they are putting forward the clause. They are reorganising the system for dealing with young offenders. But I am not clear about their understanding of the purpose of the system. Is it deterrence? Is it punishment? Is it treatment? What amalgam is it? How do they see the short, sharp shock acting as a deterrent? Will it really act as a deterrent to some people, who might find it a quite pleasant interlude? Will it be seen as real punishment? For some people it will, indeed, be a harsh punishment, if they are the wrong people—as I remarked about the constituent to whom I referred earlier.

Above all, do the Under-Secretary and the Solicitor-General for Scotland share the Opposition's view—which my hon. Friend the Member for Renfrewshire, West (Mr. Buchan) put so eloquently—that the purpose of the institutions in question is to provide treatment? They are there to try to rehabilitate people and to make them useful members of society, not 1210 to equip them better to commit crime.
