Clause 57
AMOUNT OF COMPENSATION ORDER

4.30 p.m.

Question proposed, That clause 57, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

Mr. John Maxton
: I want to take up a point made by the Solicitor-General for Scotland, when he replied to the debate on clause 56 stand part. It was that people were already being fined, so there was no need to think that local authority social work departments would be overburdened by the new imposition on them of compensation orders, in that they had to take into account what the fine was and the person's ability to pay.

Would the hon. and learned Gentleman expand a little on that matter? Presumably, for many offences, there is a range of fines that can be imposed, but there will be a statutory minimum that can be imposed at any time. No matter what the person's financial circumstances are, therefore, the court will have to impose at least that minimum fine on the offender. The court is also, however, fining not only on the basis of a person's circumstances—that is probably the last factor that it will take into account—but on the basis of the nature of the offence, on the value of the goods stolen if it is a theft and—if the court intends to continue a fine—on the basis of a person's previous conviction record in this respect. The person's financial circumstances will be, therefore, if not the last of all, certainly towards the end of the list. Many other matters have to be taken into account.

The clause says:

"the court shall take into consideration his means so far as known to the court."

 There is a clear indication there, which will be written into statute when we finish our deliberations, that a court "shall"—it does not say "may"—take into account the offender's means.

1316 If the system is to be fair, someone has to make an assessment of those means. In passing—to be honest, if I had spotted this point sooner I would have tabled an amendment—may I say that I do not like the term “his means". I think that “ability to pay" is a better way of phrasing it, for a person may appear to have means but not the ability to pay. He may have a high income but a large family, or large commitments in other respects, so that he cannot pay.

However, someone has to undertake the consideration of that person’s means before the court can put a compensation order on him, because the court on its own, without investigation, cannot do it. If all that the court had before it was a simple statement of the person’s income, in terms of wage or salary, that does not take into account many other factors such as other sources of income that he may have, the outgoings that he has from his income and, therefore, his ability to pay.

If the compensation order is to be fair and is to mean something, it must be done, presumably, by the social work department which is qualified to make that type of investigation and to give a full report on the person’s means. Despite the remarks of the person’s means. Despite the remarks of the Solicitor-General for Scotland in our previous sitting, I believe that the consideration of a person’s means is an important factor that social work departments in the regional authorities will have to undertake. They are already overworked and have had added to their workload other commitments resulting from this legislation. This is yet another task that is being added to that workload. The Solicitor-General for Scotland should take that into account and the Government should say whether they are prepared to allow more money to be made available to regional authorities for social work departments.

The second point I wish to make—my hon. Friend the Member for Renfrewshire, West (Mr. Buchan) may expand on it in more detail—concerns subsection (2):

"In solemn proceedings there shall be no limit on the amount which may be awarded under a compensation order."

 I am worried that there “shall be no limit" to the amount awarded. A person could have enormous sums to pay out, for example, in the case of a theft of a valuable antique, which the solicitor-General for Scotland mentioned. If the antique has not been recovered and it can be proved that the person stole it, and if the antique JPEG 1317 were not insured—I shall come back to that point later—the person would have to pay the full amount.

I accept that the means of the person must be taken into account, but the court has only to "take into consideration" that person's means. The court does not have to limit the order in terms of his means. That is slightly different. Therefore, enormous amounts could be involved.

The Dunpark report states that there should be a full investigation of the person's means, that the true value of the property should be determined, and that both should be put on a certificate signed by the court and given to the person. A procedure should be clearly laid out in the Bill saying how the process should be carried out, even in the solemn proceedings.

I assume that the reason is that in solemn proceedings limitations on fines are not there, as they are in summary proceedings, and that is why the legislation has been drafted in this way. However I and some of my hon. Friends feel that some clearly defined process should have been written into the Bill so that the accused or convicted person can understand the process in terms of the solemn proceedings, as the Dunpark committee suggested. We ought to take that way forward.

I ask the Solicitor-General for Scotland to answer those points concerning the workload on the social work depts. and the consideration of means, and to comment on how we could tighten up subsection (2).

Mr. Buchan
