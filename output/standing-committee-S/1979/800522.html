SEARCH FOR OFFENSIVE WEAPONS

Mr. Donald Dewar

Mr. Dewar
: I regard amendments Nos. 81 and 82 as being of some importance in 520 terms of being probing amendments. This is an occasion on which a probing amendment may get us valuable information which will help us greatly to limit and perhaps to give structure to the "stand part" debate which undoubtedly we shall want to have on this clause. I have no intention on this amendment of arguing the case for or against increased powers of search or of expressing my attitudes in any general terms to the merits of the clause.

I am anxious to discover exactly what the Minister thinks is being achieved by clause 4, exactly what powers it will give the police and how they can be exercised; in particular, what extension it is over and above the powers that presently exist in section 1(3) of the Prevention of Crime Act 1953. It is a matter, in other words, of definition, because it may be, for reasons that I will explain in a minute or two, that if we did not get a proper explanation from the Minister we could have a rambling "stand part" debate in which we would be tilting at imagined fears about something which was not in the Bill at all and was certainly not in the Minister's mind. It is just conceivable—I do not rule out the possibility—that, having listened to the Minister in response to this debate, many of my worries about the effect of clause 4 will be removed. That would be a considerable help to me and to other Opposition members.

It is necessary to approach this in a detailed and serious manner. I have re-read with great care, and I hope in a reasonably open and fair-minded way, the debates about the powers of search that occurred during the last Committee stage. Having studied very carefully the words of the Solicitor-General and the speeches of the hon. Member for Edinburgh, West (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton) and various other hon. Gentlemen, I am totally confused as to exactly what is in the Government's mind, and exactly what this extension in the powers of search amounts to. It seems to me that there are two possibilities. If I oversimplify the arguments, it is in order to illustrate what I mean and perhaps help the Minister when he comes to reply.

We know one thing which this new clause 4 is not; it is not a random power to stop and search. This is something that has been bruited around in the past, particularly by the hon. Member for Southend, East (Mr. Taylor) in his Scottish incarnation. We were all relieved when the amendment was JPEG 521 moved on the last Bill—the same virtue is in the present drafting—to see that there was no suggestion that there should be random powers to stop and search but that there had to be a trigger of reasonable suspicion.

Having established that limited virtue, we are then in a difficult morass of textual interpretation and drafting. There are two general approaches. One is that we might take this interpolation into the Bill, this new clause 4, as merely a way of tidying up the powers that already exist under section 1(3) of the Prevention of Crime Act 1953. Members of the Committee will remember that there are in that section a number of features which might be thought to be inconvenient and administratively difficult to exercise. For a start, if one is to search under section 1(3) of the 1953 Act one has to arrest, and, while that may be a technical nicety, I see that there might be an argument for saying "We do not want to have to arrest. Let us have the power to search on reasonable suspicion merely by detaining in the street." It is the kind of argument that was used by the Solicitor-General on the last Bill when distinguishing the powers to detain and take to a police station under clause 2 of that Bill. He argued that, in order to search, one did not want to have to cart someone to a police station; that that was an unreasonable imposition upon the suspect as well as upon the police. Similarly, we might be arguing that clause 4 was merely to get rid of this necessity to arrest, and I might have some sympathy with that, except that I think it is a very arguable case.

One might go on to say that we have to tidy up the powers under section 1(3). They are perhaps rather formal restrictions, but they are there in statute and statute ought to be listened to by police officers. One might want to get rid of the restrictions and say that this power to search could be used only if there was confusion about a person's identity or place of residence or if the constable

"has reasonable cause to believe that it is necessary to arrest him in order to prevent the commission by him of any other offence in the course of committing which an offensive weapon might be used."

I think there would be a strong case for saying that we do not want all these restrictions; we do not want this hedged around with all these caveats and subtle sophistications. What we want is a simple 522 straightforward power for the police, where there is reasonable suspicion that an offence is being committed, to detain and, with the minimum of fuss, with the minimum of "aggro" and friction, to carry out a search of that individual on the spot and then either act, because an offensive weapon has been found, or let him go, presumably with something of good grace, if the search is entirely negative.

If that was the spirit in which clause 4 was drafted, I might well have some reservations about it in detail, but, to be honest, I would not want to obstruct it in principle. I think that the reservations in the 1953 Act to which I have referred are ones that we could do without. If it is a tidying-up operation, if it is a matter of reasonable cause for suspicion crystallising on an individual, and the right to detain on the spot to search that individual—full stop—I think that would remove an enormous number of my fears about the clause. There was some evidence in the 1979 debate that that is what was in the then Opposition's mind. I will quote from what the present Solicitor-General said in column 59 of that Committee stage. I suppose this was an attempt to encapsulate what the Conservatives were doing. He said it was an amendment

"in order to make the very brief 1953 Act effective."—[ Official Report, First Scottish Standing Committee, 23 January 1979; c. 59.]

If we took that in the narrow sense of "effective" and used the words "clarifying the powers", I should be very reassured, and it would reduce my further participation in any "clause stand part" debate to a minimal one.

But there is another possibility. I go to extremes in a sense, but I have to canvass it in view of a number of quotations that I have found in the 1979 debate. The possibility which I find a little more alarming is the suggestion that this is not merely a matter of removing the restrictions in section 1(3) of the 1953 Act but a radical extension of power for the police which would allow them to do things, particularly in terms of group and mass searches, which could not in any way be countenanced under the present statute. If that is the situation, I think the Minister would accept that we are in the area of controversy and in the area where it is certainly possible to argue in terms of counter-productive friction between the public, particularly groups of the public, and young people. Clearly, these matters JPEG 523 must be fully canvassed in the "clause stand part" debate. I am anxious to know in which area we are.

I recognise that this is a rather technical debate but I should like briefly to develop this point because I think it is important. I have been fascinated by the drafting of this new clause. One of the difficulties about the Bill is that once one has decided what is intended in this new clause 4 one has to decide whether the actual words in the legislation could possibly support the construction which might be put upon it by Conservative spokesmen. The reason I find it so difficult is that, having carefully looked at this new clause 4, I cannot see that it is a great extension over the powers that we already have under the 1953 Act, apart from the tidying-up process to which I have referred.

Let me make a point which may sound like a narrow textual point but which I hope I shall illustrate as one of major importance. When originally the new powers of search, so-called, were introduced into the Labour Government's Act by the Conservative Opposition, they introduced it in the following very important terms:

"A constable who has reasonable grounds for suspecting that a person may be carrying an offensive weapon may require that person to allow the constable to search his person".

I draw the Committee's attention to the phrase "may be carrying". The interesting point is that in this new clause 4 the words "may be carrying" have disappeared, and what we have in their place is:

"Where a constable has reasonable grounds for suspecting that any person is carrying an offensive weapon."

Members of the Committee may think that there is no importance in the change from "may" to "is", but there is enormous importance. One of the first things I should like from the Minister is an explanation why the word "may" has disappeared. The reason why I put enormous emphasis on this is that again and again when challenged in the 1979 debates to explain what the new power was, which part of the new drafting would allow a real extension of police powers, we were told that it was the word "may". Now the word "may" has gone. Perhaps I am being over-hopeful, but does that mean that the claim for this radical extension of police powers has gone with the word "may"?

I give one or two quotations. I will not weary the Committee but I think it import- 524 ant that we establish the significance of the point I am raising. The present Solicitor-General, challenged by myself on this very point, said:

"That is not so".

He was distinguishing between the new powers of search and the clause 2 powers. He quoted clause 2 that:

"A constable has grounds for suspecting that a person has committed or is committing an offence."

In grammatical terms it is very like this new clause 4 as drafted. He went on to say:

"Our amendment says 'may be carrying a weapon', which is not the same. For instance, if a constable came across young Donnie Dewar and on 10 previous occasions he had been convicted of running about the streets with a knife, he would have no grounds for suspicion whatsoever that Donnie Dewar was carrying a knife, and therefore was committing an offence. But he would have very good grounds for suspecting that he 'may be carrying' a knife. That is the distinction, and the important one."—[ Official Report, First Scottish Standing Committee. 23 January 1979; col. 62.]

So, the present Solicitor-General, when challenged, was saying very specifically that that was a distinction. The word "may" is the important word, and I think that we should know what difference there is with the disappearance of the word "may". Would "may" allow Donnie Dewar or anybody else, merely on the grounds that he had previous convictions, to be an object of suspicion and search under this new clause 4? It is a subsidiary point but one to which no doubt the Under-Secretary will want to turn his attention.

The third point of distinction is that the 1953 Act can only be used where the person who is to be arrested is in any public place. The offence in question is the possession of an offensive weapon in a public place, and it is only if the constable comes across him in a public place that he can then, with the other safeguards, apply the particular power that the 1953 Act provides.

There is a series of very narrow requirements which have to be satisfied before a constable can use the power provided for him under the 1953 Act.
