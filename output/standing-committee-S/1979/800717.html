Mr. Dewar
: The amendment gives us an opportunity to clear up one or two minor points on which the Under-Secretary can help us in very short order.

My understanding is that the provisions in clause 1 mean that the liability and qualification of a person to serve as a juror depend on a number of conditions. I assume that it is a self-policing system—namely, that the authorities will make up their jury lists from the 4 parliamentary and local government rolls and that it will be the responsibility of those who are summoned for jury service to examine the text sent to them to ensure that they are within the categories set out in the clause.

The amendment proposes the removal of the condition that the juror

"has been ordinarily resident in the United Kingdom, the Channel Islands or the Isle of Man for any period of at least five years since attaining the age of 13 years".

I assume that when I receive my summons I must ask myself whether I have lived for five years in the United Kingdom, the Isle of Man or the Channel Islands. If I have not, I must advise the authorities that I am not liable for jury service.

The amendment has been tabled to ascertain whether that is the position and that it is not for the authorities to establish those facts.

I hope that the Minister will say something about penalties. Again, we require clarification. The penalties are set out in clause 3. Those who are ineligible can be fined £200, and those who are disqualified can be fined £1,000. There is a third category of those who claim falsely to be excusable, and they can be fined £200. Those three qualifications refer to schedule 1 under the headings "Persons Ineligible", "Persons Disqualified" and "Persons Excusable as of Right".

If someone wickedly thinks that there might be some excitement in serving on a criminal jury and decides to serve when he has not lived for five years

"in the United Kingdom, the Channel Islands or the Isle of Man … since attaining the age of 13 years",

what will be the penalty for so doing? That person would not fall into any of the three categories in the schedule to which the penalties are tied.

The word used in clause 1 is "qualified". Someone who is disqualified and serves is liable to a fine of £1,000. It is the heaviest penalty.

If someone deceives the authorities by serving without the proper residence qualification—having been put on the list because he happens, after only two or three years' residence in the country, to be on the electoral roll—by which of the penalty clauses would he be caught? He would think it rather unfortunate if "qualified" in clause 1 JPEG 5 meant that he came within the category in subsection (l)( c) of clause 3 and liable to a £1,000 penalty. It will be helpful if the Minister explains the policing of the system and the way in which penalties are likely to arise.

The second amendment would leave in subsection 1( c) but remove the phrase:

"the Channel Islands or the Isle of Man".

It would leave the five-year qualification merely for residents of the United Kingdom.

The Minister will appreciate that those on the Channel Islands and the Isle of Man do not vote in this country. I believe—although I am open to correction—that they have their own court systems. It seems rather strange that residence in those far-flung island outposts should make someone liable for jury service in Scotland.

I understand the point of the five-year provision. I assume that it is some sort of guarantee that people will be familiar with the ways of this country and that there will be no problems concerning language or ability to understand what is happening during a jury trial. However, I still find the matter a little strange. Surely there is a simple straightforward way of saying that if someone is eligible to vote in a parliamentary election he may also perform jury service. It is clear that the Government take a different view and I hope that the Minister will tell us why that is so.
