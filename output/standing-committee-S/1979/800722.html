Mr. Dewar
: Someone on the Opposition should respond to the invitation issued by the Under-Secretary and briefly put on record the reason why new clause 6 was tabled. I begin by thanking the Under-Secretary again for not merely redrafting but totally reconstructing the clause. I believe that I can see the kernel of the Opposition's version in one line of the clause. It appears in subsection (1)( a)(i), 70 which states that, in relation to the Sheriff Courts (Scotland) Act 1907,

"section 31 (jury trial in sheriff court) shall cease to have effect".

There has been much debate over the years about the continuance of civil jury trials both in the Court of Session and in the sheriff court. The subject was originally considered in the Strachan report way back in the year dot. It was so long ago that I cannot remember the date. The Grant committee—which I believe reported in 1967—also considered the matter at some length. I am sure that the Committee will remember that the Grant report came out in favour of the abolition of civil jury trials in the sheriff court.

Subsequently, the matter was discussed again by the Law Society and other interested bodies. The Law Society recommended that which is embodied in the clause 13—namely, the abolition of civil jury trials in the sheriff court—and so the abolition of such trials in the Court of Session. However, Labour Members did not take up that issue, and therefore, a more modest proposal is before the Committee. The reasons for that can be stated succinctly.

I think that there is little evidence that civil jury trials now take place in sheriff courts. I used the word "think" because I put a written question to the Secretary of State for Scotland to which he replied on 14 July. That reply gave the figures for the Court of Session and stated:

"Figures for the sheriff courts are not held centrally, and I regret that they could only be obtained at disproportionate cost."—[ Official Report, 14 July 1980, Vol. 988, c. 409.]

However, the Court of Session figures indicate that in 1977 there were five civil trials, in 1978 there were seven and in 1979 there were eight. I consider that such trials would be considerably less common in the sheriff court than in the Court of Session, despite the greater weight of business. The figures suggest that they have fallen almost into desuetude.

The Committee may wish to be reminded that at the time of the Grant committee report the figures for civil jury trials could be gathered without involving disproportionate cost. It may be that the committee was prepared to expend the cost. In any event, the grant report gave figures for the years 1960–65, which varied from a giddy high peak of 21 such jury trials to only eight in the last of those years.

JPEG 71 Therefore, we are talking about a form of action which is now, I suspect, almost unknown. That is understandable because the procedure is complex and expensive. In effect, the legal profession and its clients have been voting with their feet by not taking up the option of civil trials. I do not want to sound too chauvinistic, but such actions are—to use the Grant committee's word—a "transplant". To that I shall add the word "alien". It is an alien transplant from the nineteenth century English system which is now, I understand, very little used even in England.

In general I believe that the jury system should be defended strongly. For example, I should go a long way to try to stop anyone who was ill-advised enough or misguided enough to suggest that criminal jury trials should be abolished. A criminal jury decides on a comparatively comprehensible—I shall not use the word "simple"—concept of innocence or guilt. The Committee will probably agree that a civil jury has to deal with much more outre and likely concepts that are artificial and complicated. It has often been said— as it was to the Grant committee—that it is difficult even to change a jury in a civil case because of artificial and contrived concepts of negligence and so on that are likely to come up in reparation cases.

There are also complicated issues concerning the appointment of fault, contributory negligence and the assessment of damages to be considered. Most of us, including myself, although we do not wish to exclude the public from the administration of justice, think that the issues to which I have referred are possibly best determined by a judge sitting alone, who has the continuity of experience which no juries can be expected to have and which cannot easily be imparted to them in a simple charge.

If civil juries were to be used to any extent in the sheriff court, they would be extremely disruptive of the routine and administration of the courts, which already presents a difficult problem in itself. If a civil jury trial is listed, a certain amount of time has to be set aside for it and nothing else can be cited or called for that period. Very often settlements are reached, perhaps at the eleventh hour or even at the eleventh hour and the fifty-fifth or fifty-ninth minute, with the result 72 that the sheriff and the court are left idle for the two or three days that were set aside for the full proof or whatever else.

Therefore, put very simply, the system is not used, it is not wanted, it is disruptive when used and seems not the most efficient way of discharging the complicated issues which are apt to arise in, for example, reparation proof or one of the more complicated forms of civil action.

For all these reasons, it seems extremely sensible to get rid of it. We thought that perhaps there was a case for the Court of Session. We are not sure about that, and believe that there might have been something to be said for going root and branch and extirpating civil jury trials altogether. We have decided to stay our hand and in the Court of Session it will remain an available option.

In the sheriff court I believe that the balance of convenience and argument was clear, hence the new clause and our gratitude to the Under-Secretary for adopting the clause and putting it into good, shipshape and Bristol fashion so that it may reach the statute book.

Question put and agreed to.
