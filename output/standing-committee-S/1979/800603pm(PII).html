Apart from the practical difficulties, the reason why all these organisations are opposed to this doubtful innovation in the law of Scotland, this move out towards the inquisitorial system, is that is seen as an attack on the right to silence. Some people have gone as far as to say that it is an attack on the presumption of innocence. I think that that is a slightly less sustainable argument, although I think that one can follow the course of logic and end with that conclusion. But my main point is the attack on the right to silence.

I cannot see—I am sure that the Solicitor-General knows the argument that I intend to deploy—how one can say that the right to silence is not attacked when the decision to exercise it is open to adverse comment. I hasten to concede, and accept immediately, that in another place that damage has to some extent been mitigated. If the accused party is prepared to sit through his trial not giving evidence, not advancing any substantive defence, his silence becomes not a matter for comment and not a matter for issue in the courts. But, as I understand it, as soon as that accused party puts forward a substantive defence, he is immediately open to the attack that he exercised the right of silence at an earlier stage.

I am surprised by the high proportion of members of this Committee who have had the job of pleading before juries and trying to peruade juries.
