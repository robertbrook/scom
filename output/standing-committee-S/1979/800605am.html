Mr. Dewar: I beg to move amendment No. 65, on page 14, leave out lines 43 to 45.

The amendment is perhaps of minor importance in itself. However, it deals with a most important clause. The clause has caused much doubt—I almost said resentment, but that might be overstating the case—in the legal profession. There is a good deal of confusion about its intentions and anxiety about the manner in which it may work.

It may be helpful—it would certainly shorten the later debate—if I say a word or two about the background to the amendment. I shall discuss the general intention of the clause and give Ministers an opportunity to explain briefly their point of view.

The clause introduces an intermediate diet in summary procedure. That is totally new, a procedural innovation. On the face of it, it will create difficulties. New procedures always give rise to problems of organisation, use of staff resources and the taking up of more time in an already violently overcrowded court programme. I should have thought that Ministers would have strong prejudices against cluttering the court calendar with new peremptory diets, intermediate diets, first and second diets, and so on. One of my hon. Friends told me yesterday that every time that he attended the Committee he felt like reaching for his knife and fork—I have some sympathy for that view. Most of my prejudices are against any new intermediate diet. Ministers will have to convince me that the innovation would be useful.

Nevertheless, I understand the problem that the clause is intended to tackle. There is much anxiety both among lay hon. Image Col. 921 Members and, no doubt, among the legally qualified hon. Members on the Government side of the Committee, about the habit of withdrawing a plea of not guilty at the last minute—very often on the morning of the trial. By then, much public money will have been spent, not only by the defence from the legal aid fund, but also in the fiscal's department—getting extending police complaints into precognitions. Witnesses, and even jurors if it is an indictment case, will have been inconvenienced by having to come to court. Anyone who has been a witness in Glasgow sheriff court or who has seen the sheep pen conditions in which people giving their time and information to help in the administration of justice are kept will recognise that late pleas are thoroughly undesirable. If there were a practicable way of dealing with that problem without harming the administration of justice, the Opposition would be happy to support it.

However, I know from my experience and from that of many colleagues that most solicitors try hard to avoid late pleas. I am sure that the Solicitor-General for Scotland and the Under-Secretary will accept that.

The problem is often structural or in the mind of the client. I caution the Committee against the assumption that there are a large number of solicitors inciting their clients to plead not guilty, when they know that there is no proper defence, so as to build up a large legal aid account under the comfortable assumption that on the day they can scramble out of it by twisting their client's arm to plead guilty at the last moment. I do not think that that happens. In every professional group—in every group in humanity—there are those who are over-careful of their own interest rather than that of the public. That is inevitable. I do not rule out that possibility in a small number of cases. However, a lot of the abuse is accidental.

Such abuse is also a product of the system. If one is appearing as the duty solicitor in the cells in the Glasgow sheriff court—or any other sheriff court, because this is a universal problem—one is given the complaint and has two or three minutes to see one's client. It might be 10 minutes. One says to him "You are charged with breaking into a house at such and such an address. In this true?" He says "No, sir. Not guilty." Then one says "Were you there?" He replies "No, sir. I Col. 922 was in the area. I was with my friend. I had been down to the pub. I was walking up the road when suddenly the police came round the corner and grabbed me. It was nothing to do with me, sir."

At that stage, by definition, the solicitor has no precognitions, no information—nothing. He must take his client's word for it. He may be a little sceptical. He may press the client. But, if the client insists that he is not guilty, there is no other plea that the solicitor can offer. Therefore, at that stage, the plea of not guilty goes on the record and one cannot challenge the intransigence, if that is what it may be. Let us not assume that anyone is ever guilty of an offence.

However, the plea of not guilty cannot be challenged until one has fully investigated the case and has all the Crown precognitions which give the strength of the Crown case. Only then can one sit down with the client and say "Look, mate, how can you account for the fact that Mrs. McQuaggle was looking out of her window, which is immediately opposite the shop that was broken into, and saw you doing it? Please tell me how you can account for that. They carted you along to the local police station and you said 'It was a fair cop, guv. You'll find the stuff at number so and so,' and the police went along to number so and so and found it."

It is only at that stage that a responsible solicitor can start to say to his client "It is not in your interest to go ahead with a trial which is, clearly, a vain and hopeless chase." Even then, if the client says "I did not say that to the police. They are lying." or "Mrs. McQuaggle is blind in both eyes and a dotard, too", and he insists on going to trial, no one can prevent him having that right and privilege.

When we are talking about incipient abuses in the system, I believe that a more general abuse, and a more dangerous temptation for the solicitor, is for him to make a judgment, having seen the evidence, that his client has no good defence, and try to twist his arm into changing his plea so that the solicitor may get away to do more valuable and rewarding work.

I believe that all of us will have been to court at 10 o'clock on a case about which one may be wrong, but where one believes that there is no real defence, and being quite testy with a client who says "That may be your position, but I intend to continue to plead not guilty, and I want Image Col. 923 to go to trial."

We have to get this in perspective. It is because of the anxiety about these late pleas that we are being burdened with the intermediate diets which, quite clearly, will be a considerable imposition on shrieval time, on the time of solicitors and the time of the sheriff clerk's departments.

This innovation is worrying the legal profession. I shall not go through all the memoranda that we have had, but will take that from the Law Society as representative. The Law Society gave a more detailed argument against the clause than against many of the other clauses which the society opposes. I know, from private conversation—I hope that I am not in any way embarrassing the Law Society by saying this—with those within the Law Society that this is one of the clauses which, in a technical sense, they regard as being particularly unwise.

The Law Society has argued—I paraphrase—that there are three possible situations. One is that the client genuinely thinks that he is not guilty, and an intermediate diet will not in any way affect that. He will adhere—quite properly—to his plea and take his chance before the court. The second is where the plea is totally false and is a subterfuge. This is not an activity of the criminal classes alone. I may say that the situation in which I have most often suspected that the totally false plea of not guilty has been put in as a subterfuge is when an impeccably middle-class client is faced with charges under section 6 of the 1972 Act and is anxious to postpone to the last possible moment the loss of his driving licence. But when that is so—and it is deplorable—an intermediate diet will not cure it. Therefore we are bringing in here a totally ineffective weapon.

The third category is that of which I have been speaking. A client, not anxious to admit his guilt, will to a late stage hold on to his plea of not guilty. Often, thanks to the efforts of his solicitor, he is persuaded at a late stage to change his plea. There we do not need the intermediate diet because we get the plea from him—properly, I hope, and without undue pressure. As the Solicitor-General knows, we can then use the admirable and extremely expeditious machinery set up in section 102 of the 1975 Act.

This type of appraisal at the moment takes place only at a very late stage, and in a high proportion of cases leads to the late change of plea. We shall have that process at an earlier stage.

This might be a helpful suggestion. No doubt the Solicitor-General will already have thought about this and may want to comment upon it. As the clause stands, I believe that these intermediate diets are misguided and will be ineffective.
