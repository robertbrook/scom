Mr. Maxton
: The purpose of the two amendments is to give an ease of working for the police in dealing with detainees, to give the detainees greater protection against abuse of powers by the very few members of the police who could abuse their powers—which we emphasise are the only members of the police we are speaking about—and to allow for democratic accountability for that. I shall deal with the amendments on the basis of those three points.

If we have a standard form—laid down and approved by Parliament through an order laid by the Secretary of State— used in every police station throughout Scotland, the job of the police will be eased, because when they detain someone and arrive at the police station, all they will have to do will be to ask the desk sergeant or whoever is on duty at the desk for a form. That person is being detained. It is not a matter of that person being arrested, which would require a separate sheet, but a matter of a detention. The form will be available, and everybody who is detained and every policeman making a detention will know that that is the form to be used. It will make the job of the police considerably easier in JPEG 293 such a case because they will know the exact procedure and there will not be different forms and books in different parts of Scotland or even in different police stations within the same police authority. The procedure will be the same, laid down by this Parliament, and that is the correct way to proceed.

More importantly, so far as we are concerned, this will be another protection for those who will be detained. We have said over and over again that we are opposed to the principle of detention in the clause as it stands because we feel that it is against the civil liberties of people in this country. At the same time, if the clause is passed—and Conservative Members will probably be able to force it through when we vote on clause stand part —we shall need that protection. The amendments would ensure that the information would be separately recorded and clearly seen as being separate from other information recorded by the police during the course of a normal day, week or year. Presumably information about detainees collected on such forms would be filed separately.

The major point is that at the end of a period of detention, whether a person were released or charged he would be given a copy of the form. It would contain in a clear form the information laid down in subsection (4). The detainee would be able to read exactly what had been recorded. He would know the time at which the detention started, the time at which it finished, the time at which he was told his rights and the time at which he was told under what suspicion he was being held. All that would be clearly specified at the time that the detention came to an end.

As well as being a protection for the detainee, that would be a protection for the police. The point at which the information could be challenged would be at the point when the person left detention, or was charged. It would be much more difficult for the detainee to start querying the period of detention when he arrived in court, because he would have received a copy of the form containing all that information. If there were anything on the form that he did not like, any information that he considered wrong, he could query it at that point. The amendment would therefore protect both the detainee and the 294 police. The detainee would be allowed to say so immediately if he thought that the information on the form was incorrect.

Another protection is that at any point during the detention period the detainee would be able to ask the police constable, who would be filling in the form as he went along, "What did you record on the form at that point?" or "At what time did you say that the detention started?" If the constable said that he had put down half-past six, but the detainee said that he was actually stopped, or told that he was to be detained, at seven o'clock, the detainee could say that he wished the time of seven o'clock to be recorded. At least then there would be a question mark over the information. The detainee would know what information was being recorded.

It is also important that the detainee's solicitor should have access to the form, not just during the period of detention but at any time afterwards, and whether the person is charged or is released but is complaining about some abuse of police powers of detention. I have therefore stated in the amendment that the solicitor should have the right of access.

My last argument in support of the amendment is a most important one. If separate forms are kept, each police station will presumably file them under a separate heading. We shall not then receive answers such as that given to my hon. Friend the Member for Oldham, West (Mr. Meacher) when he asked the Secretary of State for the Home Department why it would be disproportionately costly to provide information on arrests under the "sus" laws. He had previously asked for figures for the number of arrests under the "sus" laws, after which no charge had been pressed. That answer was:

"I am informed by the Commissioner of Police of the Metropolis that information concerning arrests followed by no further action is not collected centrally in the Metropolitan police district and to obtain it would require an extensive manual examination of the entries in a large number of registers held at individual police stations. Such a procedure would be extremely costly, and as some entries may not be sufficiently detailed any resultant statistics could be unreliable".—[ Official Report, 31 March 1980, Vol. 982, c. 30.]

If in two years' time Opposition Members tabled a similar question to the Secretary of State for Scotland on the number of people in Scotland detained under this Bill and received that sort of answer—that the Secretary of State could JPEG 295 not provide information on the number of people detained but not charged, as opposed to those who had been charged, because the police were incapable of getting that information as they would have to search through all sorts of different records and books—we should be very disturbed. It would be a desertion of the democratic principles of accountability that Members of Parliament should have access to such information when they are passing this sort of legislation through the House.

My amendment would at least mean that all these detainees would be recorded on a form separately from any other recording done in police stations and kept separately there. Each chief constable would find it easy, if the Secretary of State asked for the information, to ask each police station to give him the figures. That would be a very important matter for me.

Conservative Members might agree with my other arguments for the amendment. It will help the police to do their job and it will give them some protection against accusations of abuse, because they will be able to say "That is the form that we gave to the detainee. He agreed at the time that the particulars on it were correct." Therefore the amendment will allow the whole process to be eased through.

Anyone who works in the courts in Scotland knows that there is a perfectly clear and simple precedent for this in the pro forma used under the Road Traffic Acts. Anyone who has had the misfortune to try to persuade a sheriff that there has been some irregularity in procedure in a charge brought under section 6(1) of the 1972 Act will know that the most depressing moment in court is when the Fiscal says to the policeman in the box "There would, of course, be a pro formal?" and the policeman says "Yes, indeed, there was a pro forma." I see the amendment as a logical extension of the admirable intentions of the clause as presently drafted. I do not know of any policeman—I have talked to a large number—who does not regard the pro forma used in section 6 cases as extremely helpful. Anyone who thinks of the realities of a policeman's position in dealing with these matters would see why this is so.

The Minister may say that there are minor drafting difficulties, and I am sure that my hon. Friend would be prepared to listen to him.
