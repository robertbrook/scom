Mr. Buchan: I beg to move amendment No. 312, in page 42, line 22, after "person", insert "who is over 16".

This is partly a probing amendment. It will depend on Government replies whether it remains so or whether we shall seek to push it to a Division.

The hon. Member for South Ayrshire (Mr. Foulkes) is fond of saying that he has not read and does not intend to quote from the Thomson Report, nor does he intend to read it if he can help it. However, I am sure that he and others will agree that the Dunpark Committee's report is extremely important. That report deals at some length with the problem tackled by the amendment.

The clause deals with the fairly new concept of reparation for the victim other than that brought about by diligence. The accused can be directed to make reparation to the victim. It seems possible to combine the application of that principle with fines or even with custodial treatment; we shall be asking for a closer definition on clause stand part. It is not clear from the wording of the clause whether the principle is intended to apply to those under 16.

There is a lot to be said for having a young person make reparation as a means of making him face the consequences of his actions. However, the Dunpark Committee saw the difficulty that the vast majority of under-16s are dealt with not in the criminal court but by children's Col. 1268 hearings. Paragraph 18.01 of the Committee's report points out that if its recommendations became law, which the Bill is bringing about,

“all criminal courts in Scotland will have power to make compensation orders against offenders convicted of a crime or offence, regardless of their age".

We seek to know whether the Government intend to follow through the phrase "regardless of their age," or to make a distinction. If the latter, how may that best be achieved? The report goes on to say:

“but it will be a court power and will not, therefore, be exercisable by children's hearings. As the vast majority of young offenders under the age of 16 are dealt with by children's hearings, our recommended power will not benefit the victims of such offenders".

It goes on to say that it wanted to bring such victims within the ambit of the scheme if that were possible.

The problem is that the measure is designed only for courts and is not applicable to hearings. The position could arise in which there would be a distinction in the treatment of the offender in relation to reparation, depending on whether he appeared at a hearing or in a court.

The Dunpark Committee had to face up to that difficulty. It regarded as unsatisfactory the fact that such liability for compensation would depend on the precise forum at which someone appeared rather than on any judgment made by a court—or by the children's panel, for that manner—about the desirability of the procedure. We should like to hear a comment on that.

The other problem which arises is that someone who is under 16 is not usually financially able to pay such compensation. But someone who is earning money and has to pay compensation will suffer disadvantages. Therefore the point is made in a double way—the victim secures some compensation, which will not happen when an under-16 is involved, and the offender has to face up to the responsibility for his actions.

Different criteria apply in respect of the under-16s. We regard the purpose of the children's hearing as being to remove the necessity for court action, to do away with a purely punitive attitude towards the child and to be part of the rehabilitation process. In this instance the element of rehabilitation that might be involved in the paying of compensation by an Image Col. 1269 offender clearly does not arise, as it is the parents who would have to do that.

There is a problem. If it were possible to place a compulsory compensation order on an under-16 who is appearing in court—I recognise that he can be treated differently if that happens—the responsibility will not lie with him but with his parents. If the parents refuse to pay, two questions arise.

Let us consider what would happen if parents were to say "We do not regard ourselves as having been able to have sufficient control over the child or as being responsible for him" and refused to pay the compensation. They would be faced, it seems with the same punitive action that would be taken against adult offenders. There is no provision in the Bill that would lead to imprisonment or other punishment for the parents because, as the report points out, there is no such offence. Indeed, it would be necessary to create a new offence. The report states:

“ We have no mandate to recommend the creation of new statutory offences, such as 'culpable lack of parental control'."

Clearly it would be a serious step for us to make lack of parental control a criminal act. Under such a concept, none of us who is a parent would escape retribution. There are difficulties. I should like to hear what the Government have to say about the twin problems of hearings and courts. When a court is concerned, how are matters followed through and what is the parental responsibility? We should like clarification because we believe that children should be treated in the same way, regardless of the forum. One way of doing that would be to adopt the amendment. In other words, the measure should be applicable only to someone over the age of 16.
