The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking the following amendments:

procedure

Mr. Donald Dewar: At the conclusion of our last sitting I was making the point that these were comparatively narrow amendments and could be dealt with on that basis. They are to elucidate the Government's intentions. No doubt we shall soon be debating whether those intentions are valid and whether they should have the commendation and fair wind of the Committee.

We are entering the area of the short sharp shock. I have a suspicion that this is a series of changes in the law in which imitation is one of the primary motives. We are following the mood of the Home Secretary south of the Border, and it totally undermines my confidence in the Union.

The first amendment is to remove the upper limit of four months on detention centre training and restore the status quo of three months. This is a matter of judgment but traditionally in Scotland we have an established pattern that in a local detention centre three months is an adequate period. The Committee may well be in favour of short sharp shocks rather than very long hauls. However, if Col. 1160 one is to jolt a youngster to his senses—I have reservations about that whole concept—by injecting him into an artificial Army type glasshouse regime, to do so for three months, as the equivalent of a penal cold shower, is sufficient, without extending it to four months or, possibly, five months.

I said that it was a matter of judgment, and in my judgment—I should be interested to hear the views of my hon. Friends—three months would be a more sensible upper limit for a period in a detention centre.

I turn fairly briskly to amendment No. 301. As the Committee will recognise, this is an attempt to remove from subsection (7) the words

"(a) for a period of less than 28 days".

I can take with this amendment No. 302, which adds at the end of subsection (7)

"and any such additional period of detention will run concurrently with the period of detention being served by the convicted person in terms of the said subsection (5)".

I tabled these amendments because, in reading the clause, I had some difficulty in understanding the Government's exact intentions, and this is a useful way of getting the Solicitor-General to clarify the position.

As the Committee will recognise, under subsection (2) anyone between the ages of 16 and 21 will be sentenced to a period of detention instead of imprisonment. Detention does not mean "detention centre", as is made clear in the clause, so we have two possibilities. Clearly, we are excluding borstal and imprisonment and we are left with detention which, as I understand it, can be subdivided into detention centre or young offenders' institution. Subsection (5) goes on to state that if the period of detention is more than 28 days but not more than four months, the options of the court are circumscribed and it must be detention centre.

I seek to take out the phrase

"for a period of less than 28 days"

in order to establish exactly what happens in these circumstances. A youth between 16 and 21 may be sentenced to 14 days' detention—an extraordinary sentence to impose and one that I hope would be very rare. Perhaps the Solicitor-General can confirm that that is possible in terms of this phraseology, because the provision specifically refers to where the sentence is less than 28 days, in which case, presumably, the Image Col. 1161 youth does not go to a detention centre unless he is already in a detention centre for another reason and in connection with another offence.

I say that somewhat tentatively. I hope that I have understood the situation correctly, but this is a somewhat confused and difficult piece of legal drafting. However, as I understand it, if the period were under 28 days, the youth would go to a young offenders' institution; again, if under 28 days, if he had already been under sentence at a detention centre, he would serve the sentence in a detention centre.

I presume that paragraph (b)—

"imposed other than by way of sentence"

—will refer to non-payment of a fine. Again, for non-payment of a fine, I presume I am right in saying that what the Government envisage is that the unfortunate will go to a young offenders' institution to serve his detention unless he is already in a detention centre, in which case he will remain there to thole his second assize.

I thought it was well worth while drawing attention to this matter as it is not very clear, certainly not to the lay reader—or, indeed, to me when I ploughed through it. Having established that and encouraged by the nods that I have it right, I can only say that I do not like the situation but at least I understand it.

We then come to the substantive amendment, No. 302. As I understand the provision, it is envisaged that when a 28 days or less sentence is being served by someone who is in a detention centre, or if he goes to it as a result of non-payment of a fine, the period shall be served consecutively and not concurrently. There is a proposal that the four-month normal upper limit on a period in a detention centre may be pushed out to five months.

The whole point of amendment No. 302—the drafting may of course be inaccurate but the intention is clear, I think, and that is what we want to debate— is that in a situation where someone is given an additional sentence in lieu of a fine, or for some reason is given a sentence of such a small period as under 28 days and is already serving up to four months in a detention centre, he should serve it not consecutively but concurrently, so that he would be discharged at the end of the four months, or four months less remission. Perhaps the Solicitor-General for Scotland could calculate exactly how long Col. 1162 he would serve. It was nice when he was dealing in terms of three months because one could then say that a third off was two months. A third off four months I have not quite calculated, but no doubt his computer brain will quickly produce the figure.

If there is any virtue in the short sharp shock approach, it seems to me that it has been achieved within the four months period—or three months if our amendment is successful—which is laid down in the statute. It is an embittering point, and of very little penal validity, to say that a small sentence of under 28 days should be tagged on consecutively. If the whole of clause 5 is based on the assumption that four months is the upper limit which someone can usefully serve under this type of punishment regime—which is how the Conservative Members see it—we should say "Right, that is it. That is the maximum and we shall not extend it to five months by adding a sclutter of sentences of under 28 days for nonpayment of a fine.”

The slate should be wiped clean. The offender should have had his punishment, his cold shower, and should have been brought up short—and all the other cliches that will probably echo around this room during the hours that lie ahead. At that point he should be released for a new start in the hope that the punishment will have taken effect.

To put him in for what the Government, after mature consideration, think is the maximum useful period to give the courts the right to add on consecutively sentences of under 28 days seems to make no sense in regard to the accused, to public expenditure, or to the organisation of the detention centre.

I hope that no sheriff in his right mind would want to sentence someone to less than 28 days, anyway, having been told that he is already serving a period of detention centre training which can extend up to four months. Let us not rely on the good sense of the shrieval bench and let us ensure that any such sentence imposed, perhaps merely as a mark of public disquiet, should be served concurrently and not consecutively.

I hope that the elucidation at the beginning of my remarks has helped the Committee, as they have helped me. I hope also that the Committee understands the point of amendment No. 302 and, having understood, will give its commendation.
