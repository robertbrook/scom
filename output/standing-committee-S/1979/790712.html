Mr. Fraser: The amendment is to clause 3(7), which reads:

"A constable may arrest without warrant an accused who has been released on bail where the constable has reasonable grounds for suspecting that the accused has broken, is breaking, or is likely to break any condition imposed on his bail".

While I recognise that it is desirable and necessary that the police should be given powers to arrest without warrant where someone is on bail on conditions, I consider that the circumstances in which a police officer would be empowered to make an arrest without warrant are, in the terms of subsection (7), too wide. "Has broken" causes no problems; "is breaking" causes no problems; but the phrase

"is likely to break any condition".

seems to be a very wide provision indeed. I have some doubt whether it is really necessary to extend police powers in this wide fashion.

When my hon. and learned Friend the Solicitor-General for Scotland was dealing with this matter, which had been raised by my hon. Friend the Member for Aberdeenshire, East (Mr. McQuarrie), he rejected the complaint by reference to the example of a fraudster on the steps of Concorde heading off to the sun. The Solicitor-General for Scotland said that in those circumstances it would be desirable for a police officer to be able to arrest such a man. Of course I accept that, but my argument is that if the police were to be given the power to arrest simply where there are reasonable grounds for suspecting that a man has broken or is breaking the conditions, that power would be wide Col. 60 enough to empower the police to arrest such a person on the steps of Concorde. The man has already put his luggage on board. He has already been through passport control. Without straining the language of the subsection in any way, he is already breaking the condition imposed upon him.

What concerns me is the very point to which my hon. Friend the Member for Aberdeenshire, East turned his attention. It is the fact that the conditions that might be imposed by a sheriff are potentially of an extremely trivial nature or an extremely detailed character. If, say—this is again a type of example that the Solicitor-General for Scotland mentioned—the accused is to be prohibited by a condition from going to a public house, a football ground or, possibly, his former wife's house, at what point of proximity to the pub, house or football ground is he so close that a constable might be said to have reasonable cause for suspecting that he is likely to break the condition? Might he walk down Easter Road on a Saturday afternoon when Hibs are playing at home? That is the sort of problem that arises.

More seriously, what concerns me is that this provision gives the police a wider power than I believe is strictly necessary. I appreciate that much of this Bill, with some changes, is a reflection of the equivalent English legislation, where similar provision is made for arrest without warrant when there are reasonable grounds for believing that the accused is likely to break any condition. From what research I have been able to carry out, I know that this provision has not caused any difficulty in England since it came into operation. But in view of the nature of these conditions—potentially of a trivial nature; there may be one of not being allowed to drive a motor car—there will be considerable difficulties. In my opinion, the Bill would be better framed if it did not allow such wide powers of police arrest.

In my view, the intention in the clause is one which should not be countenanced by the Committee, because it bastardises the existing criminal law. It introduces a new category of offence under the guide of observance, or maintenance of observance, of bail conditions.

Hitherto, as far as I am aware, the three main categories of offence—there may be others, but these are the three of which I have always been aware—were criminal activity, attempted criminal activity and conspiracy to commit a crime. Here we are moving into preventive medicine with a vengeance, where anybody who is likely to evade a condition of bail is considered capable of being arrested. That is a new classification of offence. Those who are likely to commit a breach of their bail conditions may be arrested without a warrant.

Frankly, I doubt whether it can be imposed. The police would have considerable difficulty. It would have to be deployed only on very rare occasions. Apart from the fact that so far Concorde has not parked in a Scottish airport on a regular service, the likelihood of that specious example happening is remote. Apart from anything else, in the particular offences where a person may be keen to go abroad in order to avoid trial, I should have thought that the court would have withdrawn the passport. I believe, therefore, that a curious and dangerous provision has crept into the Bill. The intentions are probably sound. If we are getting rid of money bail we must be seen to be tough. The fact that that has caused concern has led the Government to insert an extra safeguard in the clause that was not present in the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Bill. Under clause 3(7) a constable does not have unrestricted discretion as to how he exercises his power to arrest without warrant. Before he is able to exercise the right to arrest without warrant persons likely to be considering breaking a condition, the constable must be satisfied that he has reasonable grounds for believing that it is indeed the intent of those whom he is contemplating arresting.

The requirement of "reasonable grounds" was not in the original Bill. When we last debated the matter, my hon. and learned Friend the present Solicitor-General for Scotland argued cogently that it would be appropriate to do as the Thomson committee originally recommended and to insert the qualification that before a constable was allowed to exercise this right, he must have reasonable grounds for believing that the action he suspected was about to happen. Then the Minister, the hon. Member for Stirling, Falkirk and Grangemouth (Mr. Ewing), made clear that the Labour Government accepted that that would be a desirable improvement in the Bill. The question remains whether this power is necessary, even in its restricted and controlled form. I am happy to com- Col. 66 ment on the reasons why the Government feel that it is appropriate.

The understandable concern that hon. Members on both sides of the Committee have shown regarding the possible use of this power is not likely to be proved justified. The constable's power relates to a situation in which there might be a breach of condition of bail. As the constable must have reasonable grounds for believing that there will be such a breach, at the very least, he will have to demonstrate that he is aware of the conditions in the first place. We are talking about conditions of bail. In the vast majority of cases, an individual constable, walking down the street, meeting an individual member of the public, would be unlikely to be aware of the specific conditions that might have been imposed by the court on that individual. Therefore, unless the constable were aware of the conditions, he would not be able to satisfy the court that there had been reasonable grounds for believing that those conditions had been, or were likely to be, broken.

The trouble with that argument is that it entirely depends on the specific wording of the condition that may have been laid down by the court.
