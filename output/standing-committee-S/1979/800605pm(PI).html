Mr. Donald Dewar
: I was saying that these amendments related to the clause which gave courts power to conduct trials in the absence of the accused. I was reminding the Committee that that was the subject of a very spirited debate in the Committee which considered Labour's Criminal Justice (Scotland) Bill. I also remember that on that occasion I was in alliance with the Conservatives. Perhaps it is as well that my hon. Friend the Member for Stirling, Falkirk and Grangemouth (Mr. Ewing) is not here at the moment. A number of my hon. Friends and I voted with the Conservatives, defeated the clause and excised it from the Bill. It was not a matter of amendment. We carried the day or clause stand part. I am at the moment adhering strictly to the discussion of the amendments; however, in passing, irrespective of what happens to them, I say that we shall almost certainly want to make a similar attempt, though I recognise that it may be on clause stand part.

Our reason for putting down these amendments is to attempt to mitigate the scope of a damaging provision. In our view it is quite wrong—I deliberately use moderate language—that a trial should 970 proceed in the absence of the accused. In amendments Nos. 55 and 56, we attempt to ensure that if this power survives at all on the solemn procedure—and I hope it does not—it will be available only to the High Court of Justiciary. In other words, it will not be available to a sheriff and jury trial.

Amendment No. 54 seeks to ensure that there will be no such power in summary trials, so the only place where a trial could proceed in the absence of the accused would be in the High Court on Solemn procedure. That seems to me a sensible set of proposals.

I remind the Committee that the justification for this clause as set out by the Lord Advocate under the Labour Administration—unless new arguments are adduced by Conservative Members—was a highly tendentious and, I believe, hypothetical argument which envisaged that at some future date we might have an outbreak of Baader Meinhof terrorism or something of that sort in Scotland. It was suggested that forethought leads to forearming, and that we need this provision in order to stand against that possibility.

I do not believe that it is sensible to make laws on the basis of hypothetical fears. I was greatly strengthened by what the present Solicitor-General said in discussing this argument, as reported in column 387 of the Committee proceedings on Labour's Criminal Justice Bill. I see that the hon. and learned Gentleman is not now in his place. I hope that he will return soon, because I trust he will make at least a brief intervention in the debate in view of his very strong recorded views on the matter.

The hon. and learned Gentleman said:

"Let us be clear that this"—

that is, the then Labour Government's clause—

"is designed to meet a phenomenon which has not happened in Scotland. I am not one who takes the view that, because it has happened in Germany or in Holland, it will therefore happen in Japan, Zululand and Scotland."

I am not sure that that is how I would have put the argument, but I certainly agree with him that it would be quite wrong to change our law on the basis of a hypothetical fear which has not been realised.

Against that background—leaving aside the problems of the clause stand part debate—I believe that we should attempt to limit those powers to the High Court of Justiciary and exclude them entirely JPEG 971 from the sheriff court. I know that that may not greatly appeal to the Solicitor-General, for he has made clear that he did not think that amendment of the clause was a possiblity.

I remind the Committee—I know that these words will be taken seriously by the Under-Secretary—of the extraordinarily strong opening to the hon. and learned Gentleman's speech:

"The Committee should understand what a fundamental constitutional suggestion clause 24 makes."

Clause 24 was the equivalent clause.

"The reason that I omitted any amendments to it is that I could draft no amendment which in my opinion cured its dangers. If one were to find safeguards which made it acceptable or even a bearable though loathsome necessity, they would have to be extensive and rigid."

But the important point that I make is that the Solicitor-General's view then was that it was a fundamental constitutional change, and that he did not draft amendments because it was his opinion that

"I could draft no amendment which … cured its dangers."

That is quite extraordinary. The Solicitor-General is absent. His very place has at the moment been usurped by the Under-Secretary, and the Solicitor-General is not here to give us the benefit of an explanation why a clause that was so essentially evil that it was impossible even to draft amendments has turned up, like a bad penny, in the Conservative Bill. It has, I recognise the important but comparatively limited difference that, once a person is consigned to the cells and the trial proceeds in his absence, there will be legal representation.
