Mr. Buchan: I have tabled a substantial amendment. Amendment No. 244 seeks to remove the entire wording dealing with a recommendation by the court. I do this for two reasons. First, it is argued that the deterrent effect of a life imprisonment has diminished. People are no longer concerned about the length of a life imprisonment because in practice it generally means only a few years' imprisonment.

We know that it is extremely difficult to average the length of life sentences. There have been attempts to add them all up and divide by the number of convictions and say that the average is X. This is an example where an apparent averaging makes nonsense of the facts. There is such a difference between the number of people involved and the nature of the offence, as we know, and therefore averaging the penalties does not make sense.

It is therefore argued that because the deterrent effect of a life sentence has diminished, it should be re-strengthened by the judge making a minimum period recommendation. A curious fact is that in Scotland that was thought to be the position when the matter was examined in the Emslie report. In England the Davies report came to exactly the opposite point of view. It was said that if a minimum sentence was stated, that really did diminish the awesomeness of the life sentence. This time, in my opinion, Davies got it right and Emslie got it wrong.

If the life sentence is to deal with the imprimatur that society puts on the awful crime of murder, the life sentence is the Col. 1120 appropriate sentence to pass. That is society saying that this is our attitude to that crime. Then the circumstances must be looked at in a certain way.

I reject the suggestion that the court should say that there should be a minimum sentence. I do not think it emphasises society's attitude to murder. In other words, that which is put in to make the life sentence seem worse than the minimum period in fact decreases its effect. I do not think it is satisfactory from the deterrence point of view and I do not think it is satisfactory from the point of view of trying to express society's view of this most awful of crimes.

I come now to how we do deal with it. Frequently the last person to decide is, in fact, the judge. His task is to hear evidence, to hear the case and to pass sentence. It is then up to the other bodies involved, the parole board, and the executives in control of the imprisoned person. They should then make the decision in two ways—on how the person has been imprisoned, their assessment of rehabilitation and the cause of the circumstances of the crime, so that the deterrent that society wants to place upon it can be properly seen.

It is the Executive and its social and punishment bodies, which should make that kind of decision, and not the judge. The judge—as is anyone else—is subject to the temporary nature of the hearing of the crime and making a decision. There are other factors later which have to be considered.

There is no doubt that if the judge makes, say, a high minimum sentence of 15 years Or 20 years, the Executive and its bodies may decide later that the sentence should be less. We nearly had a debate on the freedom of the press last week in order to look at the kinds of comments that would come from the press and the public on the basis of what the judge had said. Any releasing that has to be done can be done with the minimum of publicity and the minimum of controversy. That is another reason why it should be removed from the judge.

The judge is human and is caught up in the same feelings and attitudes engendered at the time of the trial, as is everybody else. He may do his best to elevate himself beyond it. One thinks of the recent unfortunate case in the Gorbals in Glasgow. If such an unfortunate person as Docherty had not been involved, what would have happened if a smallish minimum sentence Image Col. 1121 had been passed? The judge cannot himself be immune from that.

Therefore, we utterly reject this as a concept. I do not think that as it was in the Bill originally, when it was mandatory, it was right. It is also wrong for it to be changed from "shall" to "may".

For those brief but strongly-felt reasons, I hope that the Government may reconsider going this far along on the argument adduced by Emslie.
