Mr. Donald Stewart
: I beg to move amendment No. 32, in page 3, line 36, at end insert

"and provided that no answer given shall be admissible in evidence in any trial of the person unless the prosecutor shall, at the first examination before the sheriff under section 20(1) of the 1975 Act have put to him questions calculated to enable him to make any denial, explanation, justification or comment he may wish to make in respect of answers given by him while detained under the provisions of this section of the Act".

I need not detain the Committee long on this amendment, the purpose of which is to implement paragraph 7.22a of the Thomson report. The paragraph states:

"We recommend that nothing alleged to have been said to or in the hearing of the police by a suspect or an accused shall be admissible in evidence for the Crown at his trial unless it has been put to an accused at a judicial examination."

The last sentence of that section states:

"If an accused has to make that challenge within a day or two or even a few hours of making the statement, this would reduce any chance of police unfairness to the accused and also of false challenge, so protecting the police against unjustified attacks upon their integrity."

If, as has been claimed—although I cannot now remember who claimed it—the Bill is to implement the Thomson report, this amendment could provide the protection envisaged—that is of fairness to the police. I hope that the Solicitor-General and his colleagues will be able to accept the amendment.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Malcolm Rifkind)

Gentleman has moved would make inadmissible any statement given by a suspect during the 348 period of detention unless the alleged statement was put to him at his subsequent judicial examination. The Committee will be aware that judicial examination is now a feature of the Government's proposals. It was not part of the previous Bill and, therefore, the situation would not have arisen as the right hon. Gentleman indicated.

Clause 6 on page 6 at paragraph ( b) provides that one of the purposes of judicial examination should be that there should be put to the suspect

"the alleged making by the accused, to or in the hearing of an officer of police, of an extrajudicial confession (whether or not a full admission) relevant to the charge"

and there is a special proviso that questions under this paragraph may be put only

"if the accused has, before the examination, received from the prosecutor or from an officer of police a written record of the confession allegedly made".

Therefore, it is intended that in all normal circumstances the matter should be put to an accused at juducial examination and he should have had the opportunity to see in advance any statements that are being made. Mr Buchan
: This is yet another of the amendments tabled against the background of a proposal with which we disagree in an attempt to secure some safety for an accused person, for a suspect or for a detainee in the whole chain and process that is to be set in motion by the Bill—a process leading eventually to judicial examination and trial.

There is a tendency for the Thomson report, the answers we have been getting from Ministers and general comments from the Government side to say "After all why the anxiety, we can always rely upon good police practice?" Part of our anxiety comes from the fact that societies have been destroyed in the past through relying merely on the practice of police behaving well. History is littered with societies in which this has been found to be insufficient. This is especially true at present—the difference between modern tyrannies and past tyrannies. Modern tyrannies are worse precisely because they have such an apparatus of technology and power that the previous inefficient tyrannies could allow liberties by default. One compares the present Soviet tyranny and previous czarist tyranny. The present 350 Soviet tyranny is worse precisely because it is more efficient as the apparatus of a State bureaucracy.

It is against this background that we are concerned to write in what safety we can for the liberty of the subject within these proceedings. We have had enough cases in recent history to know how easily this can be proscribed. We shall be discussing the question of detention in a more principal way under other amendments, but many of us have to record here our detestation of a system of arrest and questioning which is not subject, as the Thomson Committee says, to normal safety that comes from the formality of arrest itself. Indeed it is almost precisely within this situation that our amendment and the following amendment, No. 26, become crucial.

On this occasion we have great authority behind us. We may disagree with some of the argumentation of the Thomson report, but certainly those who accept that argumentation cannot then reject those measures which they see as essential if their proposals and argumentation are accepted and the proposals carried out. On this one we have almost a gospel authority. Paragraph 7.13c does not merely say "We recommend", it says:

"Interrogation of suspects in police stations must be recorded on tape."

This is probably the strongest phrase used in the course of any of the recommendations. This is an essential. At that point it says that it will not tolerate—not allow the process of questioning to take place without the safety and security of tape recording. I draw attention to that four-letter word "must". It is the first time that it is used in a report in that way. Clearly, enormous stress has been placed upon it. "Must" is highly categorical, and if we are to accept, as the Bill accepts, the argument of the Thomson report, it is an essential concomitant.

Even the presence of this will not satisfy us with regard to the proposals. But it is a sine qua non of their acceptance.

I do not want to be told that we can rely only on police practice. We are placing an undue burden on the police and I should have thought that they would be the first to seek the security of their own actions and their own questioning being taped. The Solicitor-General when in Opposition warned us more than once during the proceedings on the previous bill that this JPEG 351 kind of complaint, the kind of challenge to police evidence and the kind of denial that takes place about questioning, can only increase under these new proposals, if we do not have the security for both the police and the suspect that will come from tape recording. We are concerned not only with the liberty and security of the subject, but with the good name of law itself. We are on the side of law and order and of good policing care.

The second argument that I do not wish to hear is that, as the Government are instituting a trial period with tape recorders, we can go ahead without them. We are firmer than that. We are saying that we cannot proceed without tape recorders. I hope that the Government will accept that proposition. They can rest on the Thomson report for that.

Will it be expensive? I should be the last to complain about their saving money but, as the Thomson report points out, tape recorders and tapes are comparatively cheap. The manpower needed is probably less than that needed for recording an interrogation in typescript. There is no reason to reject it on the basis of cost. I should like to see it extended to videotape. On that we have good allies. We have that bold spokesman for individual liberties, the hon. Member for Perth and East Perthshire (Mr. Walker).

But I do not regard video as a must. I want what is said to be taped. I should prefer the videotape process and we hope that it can be extended to that under the terms of the damnable financial resolution.

That is the skeleton of my case. We shall argue it at greater length when we hear the Minister's reply.
