Mr. Norman Buchan: I beg to move amendment No. 329, in

Mr. Buchan: This is in the nature of a probing amendment. There has been some discussion on the proxy situation in which people not themselves directly or contractually employed in a particular undertaking—it works both ways—may be held responsible for activities under clause 67, which relates to:

"Alcohol on vehicle travelling to or from sporting event".

The question arises as to the extent to which one can hold responsible a person who has merely been employed to turn up at 9 a.m. to drive the bus and has permitted alcohol to be carried on the vehicle. The holder of the licence may have gone through the proper negotiations and made it clear to the supporters' club that no alcohol must be carried. The employee then turns up at a garage, gets his duty bus, and alcohol is carried on the bus. He has taken no part in the discussions but when the alcohol is discovered, he is held responsible.

This amendment is tabled so that the Minister can say whether this kind of proxy guilt should be in the clause. One can see that the drivers of these contracting firms merely turn up for duty at a particular time and take the appropriate bus— the duty bus, as it were—and have had Col. 1422 no means whatever of checking the contract, but because alcohol has been carried or smuggled on to the bus, he is then held responsible.

There is a problem if, in the course of the journey, he discovers that alcohol is being carried. If he knew nothing of the operation of the law, but had a hunch that it was wrong, it would be very difficult for him as an isolated position with a bus full of football supporters to take actions which would exonerate him of the charge that alcohol was carried on the Vehicle.

Consequent upon that, in line 23 it says that "the employee or agent shall be guilty of an offence". But the driver might be found guilty, although not responsible, and be subject to a fine of up to £200. We should try to clarify this matter to see whether this proxy relationship—in this case the bus driver—should apply.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Malcolm Rifkind): The amendment moved by the hon. Member for Renfrewshire, West (Mr. Buchan) seeks to delete the words "employee or agent". As he recognised, we are, for all practical purposes, talking about the driver of a coach or bus and what his responsibilities might be in the event of alcohol being carried.

First, the offence is not an absolute offence. In other words, the driver is not automatically guilty of an offence simply by virtue of the fact of alcohol being carried on the coach. The offence only arises if the employee or agent:

"permits alcohol to be carried on the vehicle".

Therefore, the question is as to the meaning and extent of the word "permits". If it can be shown that the employee or agent did not permit the carriage of alcohol, clearly there is no way in which he could be guilty of an offence under the provisions of the clause.

We are dealing here with two practical possibilities, because if the driver was totally unaware of the carriage of alcohol, he could not, in any sense, be said to have permitted it. Therefore, there are two situations. First, where the driver becomes aware that alcohol is to be carried on the coach before the journey has begun; secondly, where during the journey, either while the vehicle is in motion or perhaps when it stops at a motorway service station, or some such place, he becomes aware that Image Col. 1423 alcohol is being carried on the coach.

The question, therefore, is what obligation is reasonably to be imposed on the driver if he is not to be found guilty of an offence, having become aware of alcohol being on the coach.

In that context, again we come to the significance of the defence offered in clause 69, that it is a defence if

"he did all he reasonably could to prevent such carriage."

If, therefore, a driver becomes aware that alcohol is being carried on the coach and seeks to persuade those responsible to remove that alcohol, and is not in a reasonable way able to ensure the removal of that alcohol from the coach, he could not be said to have permitted it if the alcohol remains on the coach. If, by a normal request or intervention—without the need for physical force or other matters of such kind—the driver could have occasioned the removal of the alcohol, but has failed to do so, it would clearly be a case where he had permitted the alcohol to be carried.

The way in which the courts have in the past interpreted the word "permit" means that it would only arise where the driver could have meaningfully refused permission. Where he could meaningfully refuse permission but failed to do so, he would be guilty of an offence, but if that was not the circumstance, he would be perfectly able to say that he had not permitted the alcohol to be carried and, therefore, the offence would not have been committed by him.

That is the background. It does not provide an absolute obligation on a driver. He is not automatically guilty of an offence by virtue of alcohol being on the vehicle. Only if these requirements of the clause had not been complied with would that position arise.
