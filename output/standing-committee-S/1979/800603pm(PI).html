Clause 5
CONSTABLE MAY TAKE DRUNKEN PERSON TO DESIGNATED PLACE

4.30 p.m.

Mr. John Maxton
: I beg to move amendment No. 189, in page 5, line 1, leave out subsection (1) and insert—

"(1) Where a constable has power to arrest a person without warrant for being drunk and disorderly, drunk and incapable drunk and in charge of a child, and drunk and attempting to enter licensed premises, then the constable shall take him to any place designated in Schedule (places designated as suitable for the care of drunken persons) to this Act, unless ( a) the person refuses to go to such place, ( b) the constable has reason to suspect that he has committed another offence or ( c) there is no such designated place reasonably accessible. Where a constable does not take a person to a designated place and charges him with any of the above offences then he must enter on the charge his reasons for failing to take the person to a designated place.


Where a constable has power to arrest a person without warrant for any other offence and the constable has reasonable grounds for suspecting that that person is drunk, the constable may if he thinks fit, take him to any place designated in Schedule (places designated as suitable for the cure of drunken persons) to this Act.".

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to take the following:

Amendment No. 85, in page 5, line 3, after "drunk", insert "and incapable".

Amendment No. 188, a new schedule—


"PLACES DESIGNATED AS SUITABLE FOR THE CARE OF DRUNKEN PERSONS:

PART I


  1. Within six months of the coming into force of this Act each Regional authority in Scotland shall submit to the Secretary of State for Scotland a list of places within each region which they consider suitable for the care of drunken persons.
  2. Such places shall be those that having been inspected by the Social Work Department and the Local Health Board, are considered to have met the criteria listed in paragraphs 3 and 4 below.
  3. The Social Work Department and the Local Health Board shall be satisfied that the place, whomsoever it is run by, has suitable accommodation for the care of drunken persons and 638 meets the statutory, fire, health and safety standards.
  4. The Social Work Department and the Local Health Board shall be satisfied that there is one person on the staff of such places who either is qualified to deal with drunken persons or has considerable experience in dealing with drunken persons.
  5. Where a regional authority is of the opinion that there are inadequate numbers of places in their region for the purposes of section 5 of this Act, then they may, if they so wish, establish such places under their own authority.

PART II


  1. When the Secretary of State has received from each regional authority the list of places considered suitable for the care of drunken persons then he shall designate such places if he is satisfied that they are suitable, and accordingly inform the regional authorities.
  2. The Social Work Department and Local Health Board shall inspect the designated places every six months to ensure that the standards as laid down in Part I of this Schedule are maintained.
  3. If at any time the regional authority reports that a designated place is no longer suitable then the Secretary of State shall move that place from the list of designated plans.
  4. A regional authority may, if it so wishes make grants to other bodies in order to maintain such places.

PART III


Purposes of places designated as suitable for the care of drunken persons

  1. Designated places shall take into care those persons brought to them by a constable and shall offer such treatment as the qualified or experienced person considers appropriate in order to resolve the problems the drunken person has.
  2. A drunken person shall be asked to stay for such time as the qualified or experienced person considers necessary for the treatment.".

Mr. Maxton
: Clause 5 has considerable merit. The same provision appeared in the Labour Government's Bill. It is unfortunate that it appears in part I, with some very objectionable clauses, as it is rather more liberal-minded than the rest of part I and takes a more liberal approach to the law and to police powers. The rest of part I increases police powers and encroaches on the rights of the individual, but in some ways clause 5 is an attempt to try to improve matters.

I think most members of the Committee agree that, as at present worded, clause 5 is a step in the right direction. It is an attempt to see the problems of the drunken offender in a new light—to see the drunken offender as someone who, in many cases, needs treatment rather than prosecution and the full process of the law. That view is borne out by the criminal statistics relating to this problem. The figures show that very few people charged with any of the drunkenness offences are sentenced to JPEG 639 imprisonment. Very few people are sentenced to imprisonment for being drunk and disorderly or drunk and incapable, but I should be interested to know how many are finally imprisoned because of their failure to pay the fines imposed. I should be grateful if the Solicitor-General could answer that question, among others, because it is one that concerns us. It appears to be the case in England and Wales that large numbers of people are fined for the offence, but eventually serve a prison sentence since, because of the position they are in, they are incapable of paying their fines.

The purpose of the clause is worded at present is to give the constable a pure right to make up his own mind whether he will take a person to any place:

"designated by the Secretary of State".

Amendment No. 189 is designed, first, to clarify the circumstances in which a person shall be taken to a designated place. I have separated subsection (1) into two parts. First, there is the case when a constable would have arrested a person without warrant on a drink charge—that is:

"for being drunk and disorderly, drunk and incapable, drunk and in charge of a child, and drunk and attempting to enter licensed premises".

I left out of that list at least one other instance—drinking or drunk in a shebeen. There is still such an offence on the statute book in Scottish law, but I did not think it entirely relevant to the clause.

Those charges are clearly drunkenness charges. They have nothing to do with any other offence. They are purely concerned with the effect of drink. Where that is the case, the constable shall take the person to a designated place. I have tried to say in amendment No. 188 what those designated places will be. Being fair-minded, I acknowledge that in certain cases the constable may not be able, or may not wish, to take a person to a designated place. My amendment allow some modification of that absolute "shall."

First there has to be some procedure for a case where a person refuses to be taken to a designated place. For example one could imagine that for the first time in five years the Solicitor-General was out on a bender and became so drunk and incapable that the police picked him up. He is not in need of treatment for drink; he does not require to be taken to a 640 designated place so he says "I should prefer to be taken straight to the police station and charged with an offence, and that's it. I don't need treatment. I don't need to be taken to this designated place." Such a person may not wish to go, and he should be given that right.

Secondly, if the constable thinks that his initial charge would have been drunk and incapable, or one of the other drunken charges, but he feels that another offence has been committed by that person, he should be able to take him to the police station and charge him.

Thirdly, there will be many places in Scotland where designated places, certainly in the short term, will not be available or accessible. For example, if someone who is drunk and incapable is picked up in one of the Western Isles, or even in a boat, the nearest designated place may be Inverness. The constable could not take the person there within a time during which it would do much good. In that case, the constable would take him to the police station and charge him.

I have given a certain amount of leeway, but I think that there should be a mandatory imposition on the constable to take to a designated place a person accused of drink charges, with the exceptions I have made, rather than that it should be something that the constable may do if he wishes as the clause states.

In the clause and in the amendment, we are attempting to find some way of doing two specific jobs. One is to try to cure the problems of the drunken person in our society. We are too well aware in Scotland that this is an immense problem. We want to take such people off the streets and cure them of their problem and their dependence upon alcohol. That is the first thing that we should attempt to do.

The second aim is the public expenditure aspect, although I agree that there may be some public expenditure involved in the first. We want to take pressure off the police station, particularly on a Friday or a Saturday night, off the courts, and off the prisons by trying to treat such people rather than locking them up in the cells, charging them with offences, bringing them before the courts, having them fined, finding them incapable of paying their fines and then having them finish up in prison. That is public expenditure which most hon. Members would agree is not warranted and we should try to stop it.

The Government may intend merely that there should be night shelter accommodation at designated places for the care of the drunken offender. In amendment No. 188, which is a new schedule, I have given specifications of what designated places should be. Various principles are involved in the schedule. The first is that this matter should be the concern of the regional authority. It should submit to the Secretary of State a list of places to be designated by him as being suitable for the care of the drunken offender.

In drafting the new schedule, I was in difficulties because I was aware that I could not subvent the money resolution, so I had to word it so vaguely, almost, that while it was not exactly meaningless, it did not have anything like the clarity that I preferred. Can the Minister say whether under clause 77, which allows money that presumably is included in the money 642 resolution to be given, such establishments may benefit in that way?

Such places should be under the control of the regional authority, and the social work department, in conjunction with the local health boards, should oversee them. It is, of course, the responsibility of the Secretary of State to designate, but I suggest that within six months of the Bill being enacted each authority should submit a list of places it considers suitable for designation.

I have laid down certain criteria for accommodation which most night shelters would not meet. The first is that the accommodation meets the needs of the drunken person. That is rather general and wide, but it means that there has to be a bed or somewhere for the person to sleep not merely a mattress on the floor, which often happens in shelters for drunken people, or some hammock accommodation. Equally—no one would deny this, although some night shelters would not meet this requirement—the statutory and local authority health and safety standards must be complied with in the building. Thirdly—this is in some ways the most important requirement—there shall be on the staff of the designated place at least one person who is qualified to take care of a drunken person. It could be a doctor—although it is unlikely—or a social worker or someone with a nursing or other paramedical qualification.

We are in a new area here, so I have left it that if there is no qualified person, if the social work department or the local health board feels that there is a person on the staff who has considerable experience in dealing with drunken people that, too, shall be suitable. I did not wish to make it so absolute that there had to be a qualified person. There are many people who have devoted their time voluntarily to this kind of work and have given much effort and experience—perhaps more than a young newly qualified social worker or even a newly qualified doctor—and simply through their experience these people should be allowed to play their part in the institution or designated place. It would be totally wrong, when we are dealing with something as new as this—it is a new concept—to bar from dealing with drunken people those who have experience, even though they have no paper qualifications. I think that we all know of those who have given a lot of time to voluntary social work without necessarily gaining qualifications.

The new schedule then states—which is where I must use the word "may"—that where local authorities feel that there are not sufficient places, they may, if they so wish, establish their own. Equally, they may give grants to other organisations.

Part III of my new schedule makes it clear that we are concerned not with just getting rid of the drunken person from the police station and the court and allowing him back on the streets but with rehabilitation. It was suggested to me rather late by the director of social work of Lothian region, and I gather that it came from the Association of Directors of Social Work, that it would have been sensible—if I had had time I probably would have done it—to have included after the word "care" in subsection (1), and perhaps in my amendments, the words "and rehabilitation of drunken persons". Depending on the Government's response to my amendments, I may consider doing that on Report.

Part III of the new schedule takes care of that to some extent. It provides that a person taken into one of the designated places shall be offered treatment for his problems. I think that most of us would accept that those who are found drunk and incapable have a problem of alcoholism, particularly those regularly found so. Most people now recognise that alcoholism is not a crime. It is a medically recognisable disease and should be treated accordingly. I am not saying that the treatment should be mandatory. It should be offered by the qualified or experienced person and the offender should be asked to stay for such time as the qualified or experienced person considers necessary.

My amendment and the new schedule give some meat to what the clause is trying to suggest. Without them, the clause will be wide open. If we had a Government committed to expanding public expenditure and the social services, we should say "Hear, hear", and let the clause go ahead as it stands. But we do not have such a Government. Therefore, we must tighten up the clause and give some indication where the Government and the Committee intend it to go to ensure that the principle behind it is carried out in full.

The clause could work and be a panacea to all the ills of alcoholism if certain people, the Secretary of State included, used it rightly and spent money to ensure 644 that facilities were available for rehabilitation. Equally, if there is no commitment to provide money and ensure that there are proper designated places with trained and qualified people available on the premises, and if the idea is that the places should be the night shelters, or wet shelters, as they are sometimes called, the clause is a dead duck from the word "go".

I do not think that the Government will feel that my amendments are provocative or are trying to get across some wild socialist point. They attempt to clarify. I should be interested to hear what the Minister has to say on the matter, although I accept that he may point out that the drafting of these amendments is not in order, or is faulty. I am still a comparatively new boy here, and I accept that my drafting may not be correct. The drafting is very much my own, but I hope that the Minister will accept the principles that lie behind the amendments. If he considers the drafting faulty, I hope that he will at least promise to come back on Report with amendments better than my own, but which embody the principles that I have tried to insert.

To the extent that statute law is necessary at all, it is desirable as far as possible that it should not only clear but flexible.
