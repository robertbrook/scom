Mr. Donald Dewar: After that slight break in continuity it would be well to remind the Committee that the amendment that we are considering, in the name of my hon. Friend the Member for Renfrewshire, West (Mr. Buchan), is important because it suggests the setting up of independent complaints procedure in connection with the police at the same time as any of the rather controversial powers of detention are introduced.

It will not surprise the Committee that I rise to support my hon. Friend's amendment. It is a useful debate. I do not expect the Government to say that they will accept the amendment in the form in which it appears on the Notice Paper, I hope that the Under-Secretary will at least approach the matter sympathetically and give some indication that the Government are prepared to move towards an adequate police complaints procedure machinery in Scotland.

I start with the perhaps rather simplistic view that there is genuine benefit in an impartial element in a complaints procedure. No one will quarrel with the proposition that the police have a particular place in our society. They are entrusted with very special powers and a position of authority. We are fortunate that those powers are seldom abused. But when they are abused it is of fundamental importance, because of the special position Col. 466 of the police, that investigation is rigorous, thorough and seen to be fair. That is not the present position.

I am not making a blanket attack on the integrity of the senior police officers who carry out most of the investigations under the present system. I am perfectly satisfied that the vast majority of the complaints, given the difficulties of investigation, to which I shall refer in a moment, are looked at rigorously and honestly. But, for all that, there is the matter of the perception of the inquiry and the level of public trust in the mechanism. If watchdogs are part of the system which they are supposed to be watching, one is open at least to the charge that justice is not seen to be done, however energetically one may argue that everything possible is done. I believe that trust and confidence in the police are essential to the proper running of our society. It is something that, by and large, we have at the moment and something which we must buttress on all occasions; we must support it and we must strengthen it in the interests of the police themselves and of every citizen working within our present framework of law and order.

I would maintain that there is a very strong case for an independent element in this, the most sensitive of all complaints procedures and if that is to be rebutted, it must be rebutted for strong and powerful reasons.

I recognise that it may be said that those who argue for this are attempting to introduce some sort of steam hammer to crack a nut. If we look at the report of Her Majesty's Chief Inspector of Constabulary in Scotland for the year ended 31 December 1978, which is the last issue available to us—we do not have the 1979 figures—and turn to table 11 we may, prima facie, get some support for that argument, because it is fair to say that in 1978 there were 1,183 complaints against the police, and although 499 of them—which is a very high percentage—were referred to the procurator-fiscal, only seven of them resulted in prosecutions. The Committee might think that that would suggest that there was no real cause for anxiety in the way in which the present system is working: a high referral to the procurator-fiscal, who is, I willingly concede, totally independent of the police, and at the end of it, when it has been winnowed down and sieved, only seven ended up in court.

Again, we can look at the English experience, because the English have a Image Col. 467 system with an independent element in their complaints procedure. I would argue that it is really fairly unsatisfactory in form, but it exists. We can look at the report of the Police Complaints Board for 1979. It is interesting that we have the 1979 figures for England whilst we are still struggling for the 1978 figures for Scotland. If that is one result of having an independent element in the complaints procedure, it will be one very happy by-product that we have the figures rather more quickly.

If we look at the English experience we find in paragraph 20 that 12,344 complaints were submitted under section 2.1 of their procedure. The Committee will remember that under the English system the independent tribunal sees the cases, and if it disagrees with the findings of the senior police officer about investigation, it can insist on disciplinary proceedings being taken, whatever the police may think. In fairness, one ought to record that, of that massive number, in only 18 cases did the complaints board insist on an independent disciplinary procedure being taken in defiance of the view taken by the police authorities.

This is a situation where it could be said that we are worrying about nothing, but that would not be a fair deduction to draw, because we go back to the point about confidence and we also go back to the point about the circumstances in which these complaints arise. In the Scottish figures there were some 358 complaints at the end of 1978 which were still in process, so it is an ongoing matter. The Committee might well think that many of the more difficult cases were likely to have been caught in the pipeline because investigation would be so lengthy.

In any event, one of the problems about the present system is that there is a weary cynicism about complaints against the police, which anyone who has been a solicitor in practice in the criminal courts, or, indeed, any MP, will be well aware of. People will come to one the day after the incident very angry, very het up, very indignant. The procedure is explained to them, and it slowly comes home to them that the investigation against the police is being carried out by the police. This is a great tendency after a little while, particularly as inevitably—I do not necessarily make a complaint about this—the investigation itself is unpleasant. It obviously involves the complainant being put through the mill in terms of very careful question- Col. 468 ing, which is very proper because allegations against the police are serious matters. It may also involve friends of the complainant being put in this difficult position where they almost see themselves as being in the dock. That is, perhaps, part of the necessary process. But when people consider the inconvenience and difficulties of this process and see that there is no independent element in it, often—I have seen this again and again, and I am sure the Solicitor-General has seen it—the general attitude is "What's the point? The game's no' worth the candle. We're not going through with this—we are just going to drop it."

There is a high number of cases, in my experience, which on the face of it are serious and solid complaints, but fall by the wayside. The cynicism to which I have referred, which contributes to this, is very bad for the police and for the health of the whole public perception of police procedures. It is very difficult anyway to investigate a police complaint, because by definition there is seldom any corroboration. The classic police complaint is something that happens in a police cell, or a police van, and the only witnesses apart from the complainant himself are likely to be the two or three policemen against whom he is making allegations. I accept that it is human nature to stand by peer group loyalty. It exists in the police as it exists in any other section of society. I accept that however careful we are about investigation, many of the complaints will run into the sand, leaving behind them just a lingering doubt in the minds of those who have been investigating. If there had been other people there, the corroboration would not have been lacking.

The important point is that an enormous number—much larger than are revealed in the figures which I have quoted—simply drop out of the system because of this disillusionment about the total lack of an independent element in the procedure. Therefore, the public perception of the police is damaged, and also the safeguards to the police themselves.

The most worrying police complaint case that I ever came across involved a policeman who was dismissed from the force after an internal discipline inquiry which had no independent element. As I discovered after correspondence with the chief constable, the young man had never Image Col. 469 been given an adequate explanation of why he had been dismissed from the force. I had a confidential letter telling me why he had been dismissed, but also conceding that it had not been thought wise to tell the man himself. When we talk of an independent element in complaints procedures in the police, we should be thinking about the policeman who is himself at risk, as well the fate of members of the public.

I do not want to labour the point. I have, I hope, made my position clear. It may be said that it would be wildly expensive. I admit that I do not like the English system, which I think is a rather uncomfortable half-way house, but at least it accepts the principle. It is interesting to note that in 1979, with that massive number of 12,000 complaints, it was done with a total staff of 32, on a budget of under £400,000. I am not suggesting that one could have a pro rata cut in the Scottish figures, but if the English could manage at least some sort of independence input on that very modest budget—well under £500,000 and a staff of only 32—we are not necessarily talking about wildly expensive innovations in Scottish terms. It would be a small price to pay if it did something to reinforce trust in the police.

I do not want to give too much comfort to the Opposition Benches by going into that. My point is that the police have a good image. The vast majority deserve a good image in this country, but we could do something to ensure that that happy state of affairs continued if we were to look seriously at introducing an independent complaints procedure.

It is for that reason that I am strongly in favour of the proposals put by my hon. Friend the Member for Renfrewshire, West (Mr. Buchan) and my other hon. Friends. I was interested in the Solicitor-General's intervention on Tuesday when he gave, in my view, a wrong impression that nearly all the complaints went to the procurator-fiscal. When my hon. Friend asked who decided whether they went to the procurator-fiscal, we really did not receive an answer. What happens at the moment, when complaints are made against the police, is that the chief constable does not even see them. The matter is dealt with by the deputy assistant chief constable. He either investigates the matter himself or, more often than not, appoints a senior police officer, normally a chief superintendent, to investigate the complaint and then report to the deputy assistant chief constable.

At that stage the deputy assistant chief constable may decide on a number of courses of action. He may decide that there has, as the Solicitor-General said, been a breach of the law, as distinct from police discipline code procedures, and, therefore, it is a matter for the procurator-fiscal to decide. At that stage he would send the complaint to the procurator-fiscal, and rightly, as far as the police were concerned, that would be the end of the matter, unless, of course, the matter went to court and the police officer was found guilty, when the police discipline code procedures would come into effect.

It may well be that the complaint is not of a criminal nature but relates entirely to police discipline code procedures. The deputy assistant chief constable would then consider whether he should refer the matter to the chief constable, not for a finding of guilt or innocence but for disposal as regards the action which should be taken in relation to the police discipline procedures. The only time the chief con- Image Col. 473 stable sees the complaint is when he comes to dispense the discipline as a result of the deputy assistant chief constable sending the complaint to him.

Whether one likes it or not, the procedure for dealing with complaints against the police is totally unsatisfactory.

What would an independent element in complaints against the police actually consist of? We are under an obligation to say not only what should happen but what it should consist of. Like my hon. From my own experience, I know that police officers, particularly in Scotland, are worried about the problem of double jeopardy. A policeman will ask why he should be faced with the rigours of the police discipline code and then, at the end of the day, if that is not satisfactory to the complainant, the matter is taken through the courts for further procedure. So I suggest what was agreed by the Police Advisory Board when I was responsible for this matter. In my time, we submitted a paper to the Police Advisory Board in Scotland to suggest that an independent element should be introduced into complaints against the police procedure. As members of the Committee know, the Police Advisory Board is made up of a multifarious group of people. Image Col. 475 None of the police associations was in favour of introducing an independent element into complaints against police procedures. At the end of the day, after one or two meetings when we had fairly serious and detailed discussions, the position of the police associations emerged as being that, while they were not in favour of the independent element being introduced, if it had to be introduced the proposals contained in the document that I put to the Police Advisory Board were acceptable.

These proposals were, first, that where someone complained against the police and the matter was eventually sent forward, the existing procedure would continue until the matter was disposed of to the procurator-fiscal. Of course, if the matter is sent to the procurator-fiscal, he is the independent element and that complaint would in no circumstances go to the independent body for consideration. Where the normal procedures that are presently in being were followed—in other words, where the case went to the deputy assistant chief constable and the assistant decided that it was a matter for the chief constable to dispose of in terms of the police discipline code—the complainant would, even at that stage, be approached and asked if he was content to allow the chief constable to dispose of the matter within the terms of the police discipline code, or whether he wanted the matter sent to the independent body. So there was another checkpoint along the road. Even where the complaint had been considered and investigated and the deputy chief constable had decided that there was a case to answer, and it was now for the chief constable to dispose of it within the framework of the police discipline code, the complainant would be approached again, and would get the opportunity to say whether he wanted the chief constable to dispose of the matter within the discipline code or whether he wanted the independent body to look at the complaint.

If the complainant said that he wanted the chief constable to dispose of the matter, again there can be no question of double jeopardy, the chief constable would dispose Col. 476 of the matter and the complainant would obviously have to accept whatever decision was made. If the complainant decided that the complaint should be referred to the independent body, the new procedures would take over only at that stage.

All the safeguards were built in and we were able to assure the three police associations that there was no possibility whatever of double jeopardy where a complainant had decided to adopt one procedure and changed his mind and adopted the other procedure. We had eliminated any possibility of that happening.
