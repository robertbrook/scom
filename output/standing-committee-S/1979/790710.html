Clause 1
RELEASE ON CONDITIONS

Mr. Harry Ewing
: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 9, leave out from beginning to "shal" in line 10.

The Chairman
: At the same time it may be convenient for the Committee to discuss the following amendments:

Mr. Ewing
: I intend to concentrate my remarks on amendment No. 4, which I see as the main amendment in this group of amendments. Amendment No. 4 seeks to leave out subsection (3), which deals with the continuing practice of retaining money as a condition of release on bail. This matter formed a fairly substantial part of our debate on Second Reading and during the Committee proceedings of the previous Government's Criminal Justice (Scotland) Bill. It is an extremely important matter.

From the researches I have been able to do since Second Reading, I have ascertained that there is only one group of people in Scotland which appears to be in favour of the retention of money as a condition of release on bail. That group appears to be the judges. I have made inquiries. My views on the previous Government's Bill have been confirmed. Neither the sheriffs nor the police are in favour of the retention of money as a condition of release on bail. I have found little opinion in favour of the retention of money as a condition of release on bail.

It is difficult to avoid going over the old arguments that we had on the previous Bill. The argument appears to be that there are certain offences—mainly fraud, corruption, embezzlement and so on—where it is considered that the retention of money as a condition of release on bail would serve as a deterrent to the possibility of the accused person not appearing for the trial.

Of course, it is important to understand JPEG 5 the setting in which bail is, or is not, granted. In the first place, bail certainly is not granted in the High Court. Bail is granted at sheriff court level. When an accused person appears before the sheriff and his solicitor asks for bail, it would appear now that the procurator fiscal will be under guidance—I shall not use the word "instruction"—from the Solicitor-General for Scotland to consider what kind of offence the person is accused of before asking the sheriff to consider imposing, as one of the conditions of release, the deposit of a substantial sum of money.

That is why I raised the point of order at the beginning of the debate. Unless we know precisely what kind of cases will be treated in this way by procurators fiscal, this Committee can only come to a decision on the matter in complete ignorance of the facts. I am astonished that the Solicitor-General for Scotland should suggest that the guidance should be made up only after the Committee has come to its decision. I should have thought that some kind of guidance could have been presented to the Committee this morning, at least for discussion. That has not been done. We are expected to take a decision on this important matter, while the Solicitor-General for Scotland and the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland keep to themselves the question of the guidance that will be issued to procurators fiscal.

Then we come to the question of the effectiveness of money as a condition of release. In the Committee that considered the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Bill, I mentioned the fact that of those people who were offered bail on money, 27 per cent. were remanded or retained in custody because they could not find the money. At that time the present Solicitor-General for Scotland was leading for the Opposition. He asked me what happened to the 27 per cent. after they had appealed against the imposition of such a substantial sum of money in the form of bail. I openly admitted that I was not sure by how much the number was reduced or whether it remained steady following appeals. But the significant argument in favour of retaining money bail is that the person is more likely to turn up at his or her trial. The argument against retaining money bail is that far too many people were being retained in custody simply because the substantial amount of money that was being imposed as a condition of release could not be found.

6 The danger that we are moving into is that if money bail is to be retained as one of the conditions of release, basically, nothing will change, because, despite the guidance given to procurators fiscal, it is not they who will decide whether the money is made a condition of release.

The procurator fiscal can plead in breach to the sheriff until he is blue, red yellow, green or whatever in the face, but it will not make a blind bit of difference to the sheriff. If the sheriff decides to impose money bail as a condition of release, the sheriff will do so.

What we shall see is a continuation of the present practice whereby money bail is used extensively as a condition of release, and to the money bail will be added the other conditions that we thought should stand alone and which we still consider should stand alone as a condition of release on bail. I refer to the surrender of an accused's passport, reporting to the police station, say, every afternoon, and making sure that the police are aware if the accused person changes his address.

Therefore, the Solicitor-General for Scotland should understand that he can issue what guidance he likes to procurators fiscal, but if the sheriff decides to ignore that guidance, there is nothing that he can do about it, there is nothing that procurators fiscal can do about it, and there is even less that the accused person can do about it. I see that as a very serious situation.

Then we had an exchange of views on Second Reading about the instructions—as the Solicitor-General for Scotland put it—that the hon. and learned Gentleman intended to issue to chief constables. I explained to him—he was not prepared to accept the explanation—that no Government had any power to issue instructions to chief constables. The Solicitor-General for Scotland, in response to my right hon. Friend the shadow Secretary of State for Scotland, quickly called in aid the the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1975, saying that Lords Advocate did have the right to issue instructions to chief constables on the matter. I tell the Solicitor-General for Scotland that he is still wrong. Section 9 of the 1975 Act does not deal with the question whether police should release persons who are accused of an offence. It has nothing to do with the release conditions that chief constables should consider when persons are accused of an offence. The Solicitor-General for JPEG 7 Scotland quoted only part of section 9. That is the trouble with Ministers. I used to do it myself. One quotes only part in the hope that one will not be found out. But the Solicitor-General for Scotland has been found out. The part he quoted was:

"The Lord Advocate may from time to time issue instructions to a chief constable with regard to the reporting, for consideration of the question of prosecution,"

There the Solicitor-General for Scotland stopped. But there is more. It goes on:

"of offences alleged to have been committed within the area of such chief constable, and it shall be the duty of a chief constable to whom any such instruction is issued to secure compliance therewith."

That has nothing to do with the question whether a person should be released or held in custody—the decision that the officer in charge of the police station has to make, almost at a minute's notice, as to whether the person should be released or held. But it has everything to do with the conditions under which the offence should be reported—reported, no doubt, to the procurator fiscal, for whom the Solicitor-General for Scotland and the Lord Advocate are responsible—and it has nothing to do with the situation we are now discussing.

The Solicitor-General for Scotland
: On a point of order, Mr. Gourlay. We are, I understand, dealing with clause 1 and amendment No. 4, relating to money bail. As I understand it, the hon. Member for Stirling, Falkirk and Grangemouth (Mr. Ewing) is talking about a matter which arises under clauses 8 and 9. In order to make progress, perhaps we could get back to the subject.

The Chairman
: On that point, I think it is fair to say that, in my opinion subsection (3) covers the majority of the argument which has been adduced so far.

Mr. Ewing
: I am grateful, Mr. Gourlay. My argument is that money bail would not be an effective deterrent to stop an accused person from absconding and not turning up at his trial.

As I go through my argument, I know that most understanding members of the Committee—those who will not follow the path of blind loyalty to a Government who intend to retain something that is not only outdated but relates more to the Victorian ages than the twentieth century—will understand that this will probably be the 8 only opportunity that we shall have for the remainder of this century to update the conditions on which accused persons are released following their appearance before a sheriff charged with any sort of serious offence.

10.45 a.m.

I must, with respect, refer to the mid-wives of the suggestion in the Bill that money bail should be retained. The mid-wives, of course, were the judges. I remember the Solicitor-General for Scotland reading out that letter at great length. Many of my hon. Friends and right honourable Members on the Conservative Benches have great legal experience and they know better than I that unless one makes judges change their minds, unless one changes the system for them, the last thing which they are inclined to do is to change the system. They do not want the system changed and it is our job, with the responsibilities which we have as Members of Parliament, to change it responsibly, to make it more fitted for the twentieth century and to take it out of the Victorian ages.

Whatever decision the Committee may come to on the amendment, I say in all honesty that if money bail is retained as a condition of bail we shall have made no progress at all and the Second Reading time that we took on the Bill and the Committee time that we take over the next five or six weeks on it will be time which has been absolutely wasted, because it will have achieved no purpose.

I hope that what I have said will have persuaded the Committee that we should impose our will on the Government, as was done from time to time when the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Bill was before the Committee. The Committee always has the great good fortune to be made up of a mixture of lawyers and those of us who bring common-sense to these matters. I hope that the common-sense view will prevail and that at the end of the debate the Government will see the wisdom of the argument and not proceed with the proposal.

Mr. Michael Ancram
: As one of those who fall within the former category mentioned in the closing words of the hon. Member for Stirling, Falkirk and Grangemouth (Mr. Ewing), I hope, nevertheless, that I shall be able to bring some JPEG 9 common-sense to the debate on the amendment.

I have listened with great interest to what I know to be a very impassioned declaration in favour of totally removing money bail, and I was interested to hear the hon. Member for Stirling, Falkirk and Grangemouth pointing out that only one group of people supported the retention of money bail in any form. However, I suggest to the Committee that it is not a question of pressure groups. We are dealing with a law which is either to work or is not to work. It is not a question of who is for or against it, but one of whether the particular subsection fulfils a purpose. I suggest to the Committee that it does.

I accept a lot of the arguments put forward by the hon. Member for Stirling, Falkirk and Grangemouth in relation to money bail as it has been used in the past, but I draw to the attention of the hon. Gentlemen and the Committee what, to me, are the essential words in the subsection. Those words appear in line 13 of subsection (3)—"special circumstances". So far as I understand it, what subsection (3) tries to do is to recognise that there are certain instances where the other conditions of bail would not apply satisfactorily and that, in effect, those conditions would be " special circumstances" which would allow sheriffs to impose money bail.

On Second Reading, mention was made of the question of fishing boats transgressing the fishing laws of our country. I suggest that this is a very important example where the sort of conditions of handing over passports, giving addresses and being within the general view of the police at all times would not apply unless one were, in effect, to impound the boat, to hold working fishermen for perhaps as much as three or four months while awaiting trial, and that those conditions would be inappropriate in those circumstances.

I suggest that those are the sort of special circumstances to which the subsection is directed. Another circumstance that could arise is where there might have been a company fraud, where the principals of the company, who would become the accused, happen to live abroad and carry on their business abroad, and where, again, it would be impracticable to hold them in this country, to remove their passports and so on. Again, in such 10 an example, these would be special circumstances that would warrant the sheriff imposing money bail.

In brief, therefore, while accepting that there are dangers, I would hope to hear from my hon. and learned Friend the Solicitor-General for Scotland that those special circumstances are the sort of special circumstances to which we are referring. If that be so, I believe that in the interests of having a complete system-—not one that will allow the rich criminal to escape the conditions of bail—subsection (3) should remain part of the Bill.

Mr. Donald Dewar
: I was touched to hear with what a warm glow of nostalgia my hon. Friend the Member for Stirling, Falkirk and Grangemouth (Mr. Ewing) recalled rebellions on the then Government Benches on the Committee considering the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Bill. It is extremely encouraging.

We ought not, perhaps, to spend too much time in rehashing arguments here, as we did not have them on Second Reading. But as my hon. Friend said, we have all had the opportunity of looking again at the matter and considering where the balance of convenience lies. I can see that it is a balance of convenience. There are dangers on whichever side of the argument we end.

It is right that I should explain briefly why subsection (3) should be excised. The other amendments that we are taking with the group are consequential upon it and depend upon the fate of the subsection.

I am sure that the hon. Member for Edinburgh, South (Mr. Ancram) will receive the assurance that he wants from the Solicitor-General for Scotland. I would be amazed if he did not. He also knows, for reasons which I shall come to, that that assurance is probably not worth the paper it will ultimately be written upon in Hansard. That is not an attack upon the integrity of the Solicitor-General for Scotland, who I believe, in this case, has impeccably liberal sentiments in the matter. I am sure that he intends that money bail shall be retained only for special circumstances, and that those circumstances shall be very tightly controlled, and shall refer to the small, narrow and specialist cases, of the type to which the hon. Member for Edinburgh, South has referred.

JPEG 11 But the trouble, of course, is that the Solicitor-General for Scotland, however eloquent and persuasive, cannot control the situation. The sheriff is the master in his own court and if the power is open to that sheriff, he, the sheriff, is in a position to use or abuse that power. There is nothing that the Committee can do in terms of exhortation, of guidance, of persuasion, or of admirable liberal sentiments, totally to guard against that danger.

I do not believe that it is worth running that risk and retaining money bail for the very small number of cases that in any given year are likely to fall into the narrow and carefully-drawn categories of which the hon. Member for Edinburgh, South has spoken. I have not done the research—it may be that the Solicitor-General for Scotland can help us—but the number of big company frauds that take place in Scotland, and which might be thought suitable for a heavy monetary bail order, must be small indeed. The number can be counted on one hand. Maybe only once in two or three years such a case would come up. I can think of only a couple in recent years.

Again, on fisheries, I am not convinced that there are no other ways in terms of conditions or expedited trials—and it is normally an expedited trial for the reasons given by my hon. Friend—by which we cannot get round the difficulty. We are now providing statutory authority for conditions. In the past, conditions for bail could not be used, I understand, with any certainty of success because if, having agreed to report to a police station at 7 p.m. every Saturday, one said "Goodbye, I am not going to do it", as long as one turned up for the trial, one was all right. There was the case of Greene v. H.M.A. in 1976, with which the Solicitor-General for Scotland will be familiar. One might say that it was a bond of honour between the sheriff and the accused if conditions were set. But now we have statutory authority and if those conditions are breached, it is a separate criminal offence with very heavy penalties, which we shall be discussing later.

That is a complete change of situation. By and large, in the vast majority of cases, I believe that bail conditions can be made effective without recourse to money bail. Therefore, the number of cases where courts may feel that they 12 are handicapped or embarrassed because they cannot impose money bail is tiny. In the totality of the Scottish criminal system, statistically, socially and legally they are insignificant. I do not believe that we should retain money bail merely for this tiny group of exceptions.

The danger, on the other hand—and it seems to me to be a much more real and larger danger—is that when in years to come the Solicitor-General for Scotland looks back at his handiwork, he will be genuinely embarrassed to discover that money bail is being abused.

If one gives guidance, one is giving it to procurators fiscal. One can say to procurators fiscal "You will only oppose bail in certain narrow circumstances". Perhaps procurators fiscal—although I am, perhaps, not as confident as the hon. and learned Member—will follow those instructions. But there is no reason why a sheriff ex proprio motu cannot say "Perhaps the fiscal is not objecting to bail but I certainly object to bail and I shall impose money bail in a very considerable sum. I am allowed to do so because I have an open discretion as subsection (3) of this Act still remains."

I have seen examples of this when working in the Glasgow sheriff court. It happened to me only a month or two ago. A Glasgow sheriff insisted on setting a very high figure for bail in a case in which I was appearing, although during discussion with the fiscal I had agreed that there would not be bail and that the person would be ordained. I make no objection to that. It was not improper. That was a discretion open to that sheriff—and no doubt he probably would have doubled the bail sum had he known that I had done a deal behind his back with the fiscal. I use that example to illustrate that giving instructions to fiscals is absolutely worthless if one leaves unfettered discretion to the sheriff.

I know the views of the Solicitor-General for Scotland on this and in general terms I am sure that he will sympathise with this view. An eccentric bailie in the district court, someone idiosyncratic in the extreme, may say " The procurator fiscal can have all the guidance that the Lord Advocate and the Solicitor-General for Scotland can provide, but, at the end of the day, I do not like glue-sniffing cases, nor do I like drunk and disorderlies, and if this man has the cheek to say that he is not guilty, we shall set bail at £50, and if he JPEG 13 does not have it he can go to Barlinnie and it serves him jolly well right. He is guilty, is he not?—otherwise he would not be here."

There is no way that one can stop a bailie doing that, however worlds removed it may be from the special circumstances of which advocates, in their rarefied atmosphere, are thinking. That option will be open to bailies so long as subsection (3) remains on the statute book. That seems to be a very much bigger danger to the whole legal system that the tiny handful of highly specialist cases in which, arguably, it may be more useful to have the additional option, apart from conditions, of money bail.

We shall later be debating a new clause which gives a right to bail. At present we do not have a right to bail in the Scottish courts. It is not explicitly stated as it is in section 4 of the Bail Act 1976 in England. That might give reassurance because it would encourage sheriffs at the end of the day, if there was no objection, to recognise that they had to grant bail. But we do not even give that negative encouragement to sheriffs to heed the advice that has been given to procurators fiscal but inevitably not to them.

On balance of convenience and on looking at the competing dangers, I do not think that there is any argument here. I am not so black and white as to say that there is nothing in the argument about the small specialist cases of big embezzlement, company fraud and so on. Of course there is something in that argument. But in the real world, measured against the dangers of abuse and common use in the district and sheriff courts up and down Scotland, of idiosyncratic judges saying "To hell with this. We go our own way. We shall impose money bail", it seems much safer to secure the intention of this reform. It must be the intention of the Government Front Bench to remove subsection (3) and, therefore, to remove temptation—I am sure the Solicitor-General for Scotland is against temptation in all forms—from the lower courts to abuse the intent of the Bill.

Mr. Gordon Wilson
: I rise as a member of the solicitors' branch of the profession, which is renowned for its common sense. When the parent version of this Bill first came before Parliament, in the form of a criminal justice Bill, I had one or two reservations about the exclusion of any 14 kind of money bail. On consideration over a period of months, I think it was right for the then Government to have excised completely any kind of reservation about money bail. The reason is that I suspect that some of the judges in the sheriff court and the lay magistrates referred to by the hon. Member for Glasgow, Garscadden (Mr. Dewar), would succumb to the temptation to use money bail. From that basis of a few cases, if they got away with it, a broader path would be beaten out by subsequent members of the judiciary or by the magistrates, restoring a provision which some of them feel should never have been abolished in the first instance.

I should have had a bit more confidence if the money bail reservation had been restricted to the High Court of Justiciary, but when it has been left open to the sheriff courts or district courts, I have severe reservations.

The term deployed in subsection (3) refers to what

"is appropriate to the special circumstances of the case."

I assure the Committee that there will be many sheriffs and lay magistrates who would find it very easy to secure some justification under the "special circumstances". They would ensure that money bail would be the best way of securing the attendance of a particular person in court. They would use it in ways in which the Solicitor-General for Scotland had not envisaged.

We are giving licence to the courts to use money bail. It will be limited, we are told, by instructions that will be given to procurators fiscal by the Lord Advocate's Department. They are instructions that we shall not see. Subsequently the police, under another part of the Bill, will also be given guidance from the Scottish Office. There is a hint—although I do not think it has yet been expressed—that the judges might give some guidance to sheriffs and lay magistrates about how this provision should be used. In other words, we are issuing a licence for the use of money bail, but we have no idea whatsoever about the conditions under which it will be deployed.

The hon. Member for Garscadden mentioned that the judges, sheriffs or lay magistrates may ignore the guidance which they receive from on high. That is possible. But it is very difficult for members of this Committee to give permission for something which may be open to abuse, without JPEG 15 knowing what the guidelines are intended to be.

I wish to deal breifly with two of the points that have been raised. The first instance where money bail may be deployed is in regard to fisheries cases. I understand that the sheriff courts which normally deal with such cases are not as busy as some of the other courts in industrial areas and they tend to give priority to dealing with foreign boats which have been caught fishing illegally in Scottish waters. In those circumstances the matter can be taken care of by the courts proceeding very swiftly with the trial. If there were an objection by the skipper concerned or his agents about the time required for presentation of the case, it would be for the sheriff to obtain appropriate assurances that the skipper would be available for trial. Whether those assurances were given through the embassy or the consulate concerned does not really matter. However, they would have to provide suitable assurances, which the sheriff could accept. If one skipper thereafter broke the assurances, no doubt the sheriff at, say Lanarkshire sheriff court would look upon future applications for bail in a much stricter light.

The second way of dealing with the matter, of course, is to impound the boat. Indeed, that might be a very useful way of stepping up some of the provisions against illegal fishing in our legislation, although that is not the intention.

The second specific example which was adduced by the hon. Member for Edinburgh, South (Mr. Ancram) was that, in effect, special privileges should be given by statute to those tax dodgers who are living abroad, or company fraudsters, and so on. In my view, those people should be treated in exactly the same fashion as other citizens of the country. It would be up to them to provide suitable conditions or assurances under which they would be released. I do not think that the special grounds and privileges that were mentioned by the hon. Member for Edinburgh, South should be accepted.

It also seems to me that if this provision on bail is abused, some of the cost savings that are mentioned in the preface to the Bill would also not materialise. For those reasons, I wish to add my voice to those who are opposed to the retention of money bail.

Mr. Alex Pollock
