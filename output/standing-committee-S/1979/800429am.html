I understand that this addition to the Bill—the compelling of witnesses to give their names and addresses—springs from the belief that that which is moral should be made legal. The main justification that I have heard in remarks made during the debate in the other place—the simplification of it made by the Minister of State at the Scottish Office—was that it was a question of making that which was moral a legal liability. In law and in morality the position is not quite as simple as that. There are numerous examples of cases where that which is immoral is not illegal. One remembers the classic response that Cyril Osborne once gave during the moving of a Bill to make legal homosexual practices between adults in privacy. In the course of inveighing against it he tried to make the equation between sin and crime, whereupon Eric Lubbock, as he then was, interrupted him and said, "After all, adultery is a sin, but it is not a crime"— except in Saudi Arabia. He got the classic response from Cyril Osborne "One question at a time".

One is, therefore, always nervous about bringing in that which is immoral as a crime for no other reason than that it is a sin. That is manifestly true in this case. We have to decide whether it is conducive to law and order to bring in this clause, and, if it is conducive to law and order—which I hope to show it is not—whether it is balanced or counter-balanced by the effect it has on the liberty of the individual citizen.

Image Col. 119 One can think of numerous examples in history which disprove absolute. One can do it very simply by looking at the experience of Nazi Germany—and, indeed, other countries—in this century, when we all applauded, as basic supporters of law and order as we would understand it, those who were busy in breaking the law because they would not accept the law of Nazi Germany.

That is the absolute, so the absolute goes. We now have to look at the relative. Many of us are worried by the relationship between the law that is proposed here and the law that is proposed in the Employment Bill on the question of picketing. We are immediately confronted with two moralities, the morality of law and the morality of those people who believe in a rather higher argument—that that which they are doing is justified and moral. Before looking at the immediate position, let us recall the infamous Industrial Relations Act 1971 of the previous Tory Administration. In implementation of the law we were stupid enough inevitably to have made arrests. The law was a bad law yet had to be fulfilled. That is the trouble with bad law. We pass bad laws and then have to carry them out. The persons carrying it out, as you will recall, Mr. Gourlay, arrested five dockers. They were then put in an intolerable position because most of the working people of this country could not recognise that what the five dockers had done was wrong. We all recognised that it was illegal, and therefore there was a clash between the morality of acceptance of the law and the morality of accepting that that which was wrong should be opposed.

The action taken by the Government was to avoid the dilemma, not by abolishing the law—the Labour Government had to do that—but by using a legal device. We had the "tipstaff through the tulips"—the pulling in of the Official Solicitor in order to get the five dockers released. First, the law was a bad law. Secondly, it was broken by people who were acting, in terms of any other kind of judgment, in a correct fashion. Thirdly, in order to avoid the dilemma, the Government had to use a piece of legal trickery. This does nothing but bring the law into contempt. Therefore, on the question whether it is conducive to law and order to pass a bad law such as this, that is our answer.

Col. 120 Why is this a bad law? It is a bad law, first, because it infringes the liberty of the individual. Secondly, it is a bad law because it is of itself conducive to law breaking. I refer again to the Employment Bill. There are strong feelings engendered on a picket line. Inevitably, picketing arises out of disputes, and this by definition means conflict. Inevitably, with the best will in the world on all sides, there is tension, hostility and conflict. In that situation, the worst possible thing to happen is for the police to be seen, rightly or wrongly, to be acting apparently on one side in a conflict which has arisen for perfectly right and legal reasons. I do not say the police take sides but that the law would force them to do so.

Secondly, the action that the police would be taking, far from easing the situation, would exacerbate it. If the Employment Bill goes through, the laws on picketing will be tightened up. Many, many working people in this country genuinely feel that the kinds of definitions that the Government have taken regarding secondary picketing are wrong and, because they are wrong, they are immoral. People see the suppliers of a firm with whom they are in dispute as themselves part of that dispute, whatever the law may say. Suppose that, arising out of this, action is taken under the law to prevent pickets doing what they regard as their moral duty—to stop people in order to talk to them and seek an opportunity to discuss with them—and a policeman intervenes, and an incident occurs with someone who brushed aside a picket during a period of tension such as this. The policeman then seeks to get the name and address of all the witnesses.

On a picket line there could be as many as a dozen people and probably more, all of whom would be witnesses, but all of whom would regard the law as wrong and therefore be unwilling to give their name and address, because they could then be called to court and under oath would have to tell the truth about the incident. In the process of telling the truth about the incident they would, as they see it, have immorally punished someone who took part in the picket line and was accused following the incident, whatever it was—an argument with a policeman or whatever.

In other words, one is imposing an intolerable strain by producing a clash between the desire to do that which is lawful and the desire to do that which is Image Col. 121 right. For that reason, many of them would seek to refuse to give their name and address. The moment they have done so, they are then faced with the question of punishment. You will recall, Mr. Gourlay, that among the punishments for nonpayment of a fine there could be imprisonment.

The effect of this on industrial relations would be catastrophic. Certainly its effect would be that other people would be prepared to test the law—indeed, they would be justified—in order to get rid of a bad law, because they feel they are doing no more than exercising what they regard as a right. Therefore, from the very moment of conflict—I do not use this in a high sense, but in the low sense of "dispute"—the law itself is exacerbated and a situation is brought about which would be exactly the opposite of the fulfilment of law and order.

This is the crucial question which we have to decide; it is the first point of our objection. The proposed law is not of itself conducive to law and order but is opposed to it and helps to break it down.

We then come to the second plank, which is the freedom of the individual. On this matter a good deal of discussion took place in the Lords. Most of those who spoke were opposed to the proposal.
