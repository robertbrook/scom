STANDING COMMITTEE ON SCOTTISH BILLS.
Thursday, 19th June, 1930.

HOUSING (SCOTLAND) BILL.

[OFFICIAL REPORT.]
CLAUSE 41.
—(Amendments of principal Act, etc.)

  1. The principal Act shall have effect as if the provisions of this Act with respect to the repair, closing, or demolition of insanitary houses and the acquisition of buildings consisting wholly of closed houses and the provisions thereof with respect to the clearance or improvement of unhealthy areas were inserted in Parts I and II respectively of the principal Act in substitution for the provisions therein contained with respect to the power of a local authority to repair houses, to make closing and demolition orders, to acquire closed houses and to remove obstructive buildings, and for the provisions therein contained with respect to the making, confirmation and execution of improvement schemes and reconstruction schemes.
  2. The amendments specified in the second column of the Fourth Schedule to this Act (which relate to minor and consequential details) shall be made in the sections of the principal Act specified in the first column of that Schedule.
  3. The amendments specified in the second column of the Fifth Schedule to this Act (being amendments consequential on the Local Government (Scotland) Act, 1929) shall be made in the sections of the Housing, &c., Act, 1923, the Housing (Financial Provisions) Act, 1924, and the principal Act specified in the first column of that Schedule.
  4. In provisions of the principal Act relating to offences, for the words "on summary conviction," wherever they occur, there shall be substituted the words "on conviction by a court of summary jurisdiction."

Amendment proposed [17th June]: To leave out Sub-section (4).—[ Mr. MacRobert.]

The UNDER-SECRETARY of STATE for SCOTLAND (Mr. Johnston)
: I think we can accommodate our views to the 1150 views of hon. Gentlemen opposite on the point that was under discussion when we adjourned at our last meeting. It will be remembered that, just after my right hon. and learned Friend the Lord Advocate had explained what the Clause means, the late Lord Advocate, in his concluding sentences, just before the adjournment, said:

"If there are any particular offences under the Bill which it is thought can more appropriately go to another court, such as the Justice of the Peace Court, I have no objection to that, providing it is specifically stated, but I object to a general repeal of the present system."—[OFFICIAL REPORT ( Standing Committee on Scottish Bills), 17th June, 1930; col. 422.]

On further reconsideration of the matter, there is at least one point on which we think it advisable that an appeal should go to the Sheriff. I hope that hon. Gentlemen opposite will agree that on trivial matters, about by-laws and minor offences, it would be a sheer waste of public money and of legal and official time for the Procurator Fiscal to take these questions, say, to the county town and to a Sheriff for decision. I am sure that that is also the opinion of the late Lord Advocate, as it is the opinion of the present Lord Advocate. But when we come to an offence such as is referred to in Sub-section (2) of Section 107 of the Act of 1925, where a member of a local authority, or of a committee of a local authority, may vote on a matter relating to housing in which he is personally interested, we agree with the late Lord Advocate and other hon. Gentlemen opposite that it would be inappropriate and inadvisable that in such a case the trial should take place before the fellow magistrates of the member in question, who would also be members of the local town council, and may also have given a vote on the issue. We think that in such a case it is clearly advisable that the trial should be before a Sheriff, and not before members of the city council or town council who themselves may have a vested interest in the case.

Mr. BROOKE
: Does that apply to district councils?

Mr. JOHNSTON
: It applies to a member of the local authority. The point is that, where a local authority has given a vote on a housing question, such as the letting of a house, and a member of the council has given a vote, which he is not legally entitled to do, on the question, JPEG 1151 say, that he himself should get a house, if such a case is taken by the Procurator Fiscal, we agree that it would be inadvisable that it should be tried in a local police court, where the magistrates would also be members of the council. We say that, in the interests of justice and of fair play all round, it is advisable that in such a case the trial should take place before the Sheriff. If it will meet the wishes of hon. Members opposite and in all parts of the Committee, the Government are prepared, in Sub-section (4) of Clause 41, after the word "offences," to insert the words:

"other than under Sub-section (2) of Section 107 of the Housing (Scotland) Act, 1925,"

the obvious purpose being to exclude from decision by a Court of summary jurisdiction the kind of offences which I have just specified. If that meets the general wishes of the Committee, we shall be happy to hand in such an Amendment and let it be agreed to.

Mr. MARCUS
: I think it is apparent that there is an attempt, in Sub-section (4) of Clause 41, to depart from what is considered in Scotland to be a well established legal practice, namely, that a justice of the peace should not be called upon to adjudicate in cases connected with housing legislation. I appreciate very much the statement of the Under-Secretary, but I still feel very strongly that we must keep in view the fact that in every case where proceedings are taken in regard to such an offence it is at the instance of the local authority, and, even in cases of minor offences, it should be the Sheriff's Court that decides. I would point out that at the present moment very minor offences in connection with other legislation—motor legislation and so on—are dealt with by the Sheriff. The fact must also be kept in view that justices of the peace, if I may say so without any disrespect to them, are not competent to judge in cases of this kind, because their present experience is confined to passing judgment on such matters as claims for theft under the sum of £5, and I do not see how, with the best will in the world, they can be called upon to deal with the points, very often of a technical and legal character, involved in offences of this kind.

There is a further point in support of what I am saying. The Sheriff has special knowledge in the civil field, as distinct from what may be called the criminal field, in connection with housing legislation and the administration of Acts, and he is, therefore, particularly competent, from his special knowledge of the application of Housing Acts, to judge in the case of offences with which persons may be charged under such Acts. Therefore, I think that the Government ought to give this matter still further consideration, and ought to preserve intact the jurisdiction of the Sheriff in all cases connected with housing legislation.

The LORD ADVOCATE (Mr. Craigie Aitchison)
: This is a matter which has already received very full consideration JPEG 1153 by the Government, having clearly in view that what we propose does involve an alteration in practice as far as offences under the Housing Acts are concerned. But I rather demur from the view that there is any alteration in principle involved. My predecessor in office said at the last meeting of the Committee that it was undesirable that local authorities, or rather the magistrates who were identified with local authories, should be judges in matters in which they themselves had an interest. That point is a good point in so far as there is any personal interest held by the magistrate in the matter, and we are dealing with the situation in the Amendment which the Under-Secretary is prepared to insert. But it is quite wrong to say that the magistrates are disqualified from judging because they have to deal with a matter affecting their own administration. Magistrates every day discharge judicial functions connected with their own administration. A very familiar instance is where magistrates have to deal with a breach of the by-laws which they themselves make under statutory powers, but I never heard it suggested that because they had to deal with breaches of by-laws which they themselves make therefore they do not discharge their judicial functions.

Mr. MARCUS
: I would like to point out that these by-laws have to do with the local authority, but this legislation deals with other matters.

The LORD ADVOCATE
: I do not think that affects my point. I am dealing with the argument put forward by my predecessor in office that the magistrates should not be the judges because they are members of the local authority which has an interest in the matter. I am pointing out that if you push that argument to its logical conclusion you deprive the magistrates from judging in regard to matters relating to breaches of the bylaws which they as members of the local authority themselves make. There are two further observations which I should like to make. If we were here proposing to extend the jurisdiction of the magistrates with reference to crimes I should agree with the opposition to any such extension. The question of the power of the inferior courts in relation to crimes is a very difficult question, but we are dealing here not with crimes but with 1154 statutory offences under the Housing Acts the great majority of which are of a comparatively minor character. Let us take one example. Section 104 of the principal Act provides a penalty where a person obstructs an officer in the discharge of his statutory duty. Why should not that be dealt with by a magistrate or a justice of the peace? I could give illustration after illustration of the same kind. Section 106 provides a penalty for a person wilfully damaging property. Why should not that be dealt with by a magistrate? The fact that it is an offence under a Housing Act does not make any difference.

Major ELLIOT
: Is it not the case that these offences are already dealt with by summary jurisdiction?

The LORD ADVOCATE
: Yes, but summary conviction will not do; it must be conviction by a Court of Summary Jurisdiction. It is a technical distinction, but it is one which has been recognised by a decision of the High Court. I would like to make this final observation in asking the Committee to accept the Amendment which the Government proposes; the Committee must keep in view that when prosecutions take place for offences under the Housing Act those prosecutions are not at the instance of the local authority at all. The prosecutions are at the instance of the Public Prosecutor. [An HON. MEMBER: "Who appoints him?"] I do not care who appoints him. I demur from the suggestion implied in the interjection that the Public Prosecutor will not discharge his duty judicially, and that because he is appointed by the local authority he will incline the scales in favour of the local authority's view. I think that is an altogether unwarranted reflection upon the public prosecutors. In every case the matter will be given very careful consideration before a prosecution is lodged. Consequently, in my view, that objection fails and in the whole circumstances, especially having regard to the very heavy burdens that are placed upon the Sheriffs at the present time, who have had to deal within the last 10 years with statutory offences which have multiplied 10 and sometimes 20 fold, it is very desirable that so far as these comparatively minor statutory offences are concerned we should confer jurisdiction upon the magistrates and upon JPEG 1155 the justices of the peace, both of whom act upon expert advice. In the case of the magistrates there are legal assessors and the stipendiary magistrates are in most cases qualified lawyers able to deal with any legal question which may arise. Accordingly I hope the Committee will accept the proposal of the Government that this Clause shall stand with the Amendment indicated.

NEW CLAUSE.
—(Power to local authority to purchase cleared land which owners have failed to redevelop.)

  1. Where land has been cleared of buildings in accordance with a clearance order made under this Part of this Act the local authority may, at any time after the expiration of eighteen months from the date on which the clearance order became operative, determine to purchase any part of that land which, at the date of the passing of their resolution, has not been or is not in process of being used for building purposes or otherwise developed by the owner thereof.
  2. A local authority shall deal with any land purchased by them under this section by sale, letting, or appropriation, in accordance with the provisions of section four of this Act.—[ Mr. Johnston.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

: I beg to move, "That the Clause be read a Second time."

It will be remembered that when we were dealing with clearance areas the point was raised about cases where land was cleared and buildings were knocked down, but the owner did not clear away the rubbish, or where alternatively he did not develop the area. All we are 1156 seeking to do here is to give the local authority power after 18 months have elapsed to purchase the land which is undeveloped in the clearance area at its site value. In these circumstances I trust that the Committee will agree to let the local authorities have that power.

NEW CLAUSE.
—(Validity and date of operation of clearance and compulsory purchase orders.)

  1. The provisions of this section shall have effect with respect to the validity of clearance orders and compulsory purchase orders made under this Part of this Act and the date on which such an order is to come into operation.
  2. So soon as may be after an order has been confirmed by the department, the local authority shall publish in a newspaper circulating in their district a notice in the prescribed form stating that the order has been confirmed and naming a place where a copy of the order as confirmed and of the map referred to therein may be seen at all reasonable hours, and shall serve a like notice on every person who, having given notice to the department of his objection to the order, appeared at the public local inquiry in support of his objection.
  3. If any person aggrieved by an order desires to question its validity on the ground JPEG 1161 that it is not within the powers of this Act, or any requirement of this Act has not teen complied with, he may within thirty days after the publication of the notice of confirmation, make an application for the purpose to the Court of Session, and if such application is duly mader—
    1. the operation of the order shall be suspended until the final determination of the proceedings; and
    2. the court, if satisfied that the order is not within the powers of this Act, or that the interests of the applicant have been substantially prejudiced by any requirement of this Act not having been complied with, may set aside the order either generally, or in so far as it affects any property of the applicant, or do otherwise as shall appear to the court to be just.
  4. Subject to the provisions of the immediately preceding sub-section an order shall not, either before or after its confirmation, be questioned by reduction or interdict or in any legal proceedings whatsoever, and shall become operative at the expiration of thirty days from the date on which notice of its confirmation is published in accordance with sub-section (2) of this section.
  5. Except by leave of the Court of Session, no appeal shall lie to the House of Lords from a decision of the Court of Session in proceedings under this section.
  6. So soon as may be after an order has become operative, the local authority shall serve a copy thereof on every person on whom a notice was served by them of their intention to submit the order to the department for confirmation.—[ Mr. Johnston.]

Mr. JOHNSTON
: I beg to move "That the Clause be read a Second time."

This Clause appears very formidable and difficult but I think I can explain its purport very briefly, and I trust that after my explanation the Committee will see fit to add it to the Bill. The first object of the Clause is to secure that adequate publicity shall be given to the confirmation by the Department of either a clearance order or a compulsory purchase order. Its second object is to ensure that the power of legal appeal against such confirmation shall be limited in point of time. Hon. and right hon. Gentlemen opposite have put down a number of Amendments to the Schedules to ensure that an owner shall have an adequate opportunity of appeal against any such confirmation. They suggest that there should be a period of 30 days within which an owner can institute an appeal. We have accepted the period of 30 days and the Clause provides that, if 1162 the usual steps have been taken, and the Department has confirmed a clearance order or compulsory purchase order, the proprietor then may appeal, on a point of law, to the Court of Session within 30 days. It is obviously necessary that some limit should be placed on the period of time within which an owner can make such an appeal. If there were no such limit it might be possible for an owner to make an appeal 10 weeks, or 10 months, or even 10 years after an order has been confirmed; when perhaps the local authority has built the houses. The local authority would thus stand to be shot at on points of law over a very considerable period of time. This new Clause is proposed to meet that difficulty and also to meet the fears of hon. Gentlemen in this Committee and widespread criticisms in the Press and in Parliament as to the possibility of the Department being the final court of appeal in these matters. The Clause will ensure that there shall be a court of appeal on points of law, but we ask that the time shall be definitely limited to 30 days and that if, after 30 days, no appeal has been made, then the confirmation of the order shall have the force of law.
