STANDING COMMITTEE ON SCOTTISH BILLS.
Tuesday, 8th July, 1930.

[Mr. JAMES BROWN in the Chair.]

ADOPTION OF CHILDREN (SCOTLAND) BILL.

[OFFICIAL REPORT.]
CLAUSE 1.
—(Power to make, adoption orders.)

  1. Upon an application in the prescribed manner by any person desirous of being authorised to adopt a child under the age of twenty-one years who has never been married, the Court may, subject to the provisions of this Act, make an order (in this Act referred to as "an adoption order") authorising the applicant to adopt that child.
  2. A person so authorised to adopt the child and a child authorised to be adopted are in this Act referred to as an "adopter" and an "adopted child" respectively.
  3. Where an application for an adoption order is made by two spouses jointly, the Court may make the order authorising the two spouses jointly to adopt, but save as aforesaid no adoption order shall be made authorising more than one person to adopt a child.

Mr. MacROBERT
: I beg to move, to leave out Sub-section (3).

This is a point on which certain difficulties may arise, and if the Committee have any differences of opinion in regard to it I would rather drop my Amendment in order that the Bill may go through. While there may be certain technical points in the Bill, those technical points may never arise in practice, and I would rather that the Bill should go through, even to a certain extent imperfect, than that it should not go through at all. Later on, if we find that there are imperfections in the Bill, they might be put right by an amending Bill. I thoroughly agree with the principle of the Bill as applied to Scotland. The point that I am raising is that this Sub-section prevents two people from 1230 adopting a child. That was, apparently, the English view. I am not satisfied that a couple of maiden ladies should not be allowed, jointly, to adopt a child, or that a brother and sister, jointly, should not be allowed to adopt a child. The question in regard to "jointly" raises a very difficult question in this respect that if you have the appointment of two spouses jointly and one spouse dies then, as I understand Scottish law, it would mean that the appointment would fail. If the survivor is a woman, I think that, quite clearly, she should be the adopter. If it was only a question of a woman, the matter would be easy of adjustment.

No male person is to be allowed to adopt a female child, and I see the reason for that. In the case, for instance, of the wife dying and the husband being 35 or 40 years of age and the girl who has been adopted being, say, 15, I doubt very much the propriety of that man being the adopter of the child in those circumstances. That is why I doubt whether the Clause in these terms should have been put in. It would probably have been much better to allow the appointment of one person. That would mean that in the case of spouses, the spouse who asked to be the adopter would have to get the consent of the other spouse. I think the Clause as it stands will create a certain amount of difficulty, but I doubt whether we should adopt the drastic step of leaving out the Clause. I have, however, proposed my Amendment in order to raise my point. It would simply mean that the rest of the Bill would have to be remodelled, and that would be very difficult at this stage. Accordingly, having raised my point with regard to Sub-section (3), I do not propose to press for the deletion of the Sub-section unless the Committee are unanimously of that opinion.

CLAUSE 2.
—(Restrictions on making adoption orders.)

  1. An adoption order shall not be made in any case where—
    1. the applicant is under the age of twenty-five years; or
    2. the applicant is less than twenty-one years older than the child in respect of whom the application is made:
  2. Provided that, where the applicant and the child are within the prohibited degrees of consanguinity, it shall be lawful for the Court, if it thinks fit, to make the order notwithstanding that the applicant is less than twenty-one years older than the child.
  3. An adoption order shall not be made in any case where the sole applicant is a male and the child in respect of whom the application is made is a female unless the Court is satisfied that there are special circumstances which justify as an exceptional measure the making of an adoption order.
  4. An adoption order shall not be made except with the consent of every person or body who is a parent or guardian of the child in respect of whom the application is made or who has the actual custody of the child or who is liable to contribute to the support of the child:
  5. Provided that the Court may dispense with any consent required by this subsection if satisfied that the person whose consent is to be dispensed with has abandoned or deserted the child or cannot be found or is incapable of giving such consent or, being a person liable to contribute to the support of the child, either has persistently neglected or refused to contribute to such support or is a person whose consent ought, in the opinion of the Court and in all the circumstances of the case, to be dispensed with.
  6. An adoption order shall not be made upon the application of one of two spouses without the consent of the other of them:
  7. Provided that the Court may dispense with any consent required by this subsec- JPEG 1239 tion if satisfied that the person whose consent is to be dispensed with cannot be found or is incapable of giving such consent or that the spouses have separated and are living apart and that the separation is likely to be permanent.
  8. An adoption order shall not be made in favour of any applicant who is not resident and domiciled in Scotland or in respect of any child who is not a British subject and so resident.
