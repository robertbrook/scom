Mr. Barry Henderson
: The subject to which we particularly wish to draw attention this morning is the problem of rates. This debate takes place in what is in one sense a happy time for Scotland but in another sense, in terms of what is happening with Scottish rates, it is a bleak and unpleasant one for some people. We are faced with a cascade of unprecedented rate rises in Scotland at the present time.

In recent weeks my hon. Friends and I have been worrying the Secretary of State for Scotland and asking Questions to try to get some preliminary information about what would happen at this time of the year. We sought to make the right hon. Gentleman think about these problems before they actually arose. It seems to me that in the replies that we have had we have met with a great deal of insensitivity and, some of us suspect, almost indifference. Our impression has been that it was felt we were moaning about something that was not very important.

I hope that there will be no impression of moaning from this side of the Committee this morning. I hope that our message will be much louder than that, and much more positive. The anxieties which we have been trying to express over recent weeks are being replaced by howls of anguish from the ratepayers as the 110 facts are now emerging in the various burghs and counties throughout Scotland. The Government took office with a pledge to extract howls of anguish from the rich, but the howls of anguish are coming from a far wider section of the community than that.

Perhaps I can illustrate some of the problems that immediately confront us by referring to the problems in my own constituency, I am sure that other hon. Members will be able to bring similar facts before the Committee this morning.

In Dunbartonshire, the increase in rates is about one-third. For every pound that was paid in rates last year, another 30p will be paid this year. This is a staggering rise. As it happens, about 17p of the 29p increase in the rate poundage is on the housing deficit. Bearsden has announced a domestic rate increase of 10p in the pound. That, by any traditional standard, is a staggering increase. In old terms, it is 2s. in the pound, yet there was a time when rates were not so very much more than that in total—[ Interruption].

I am not as young as some hon. Gentlemen think I am!

I think hon. Members will agree that a 10p increase in the rate poundage a few years ago would have been held to be quite remarkable, yet by the standards of today, in the west of Scotland, this is a very modest increase indeed, which reflects considerable credit on that town council. But the fact that the increase of 10p in the pound in Bearsden is modest by other standards in the west of Scotland is little comfort for the large number of newly married couples and retired people who live there and who will have to face these substantial increases.

When the Cumbernauld Town Council met to decide its rates, it was so horrified with the facts that it could not bring itself to announce what the rates should be this year. Instead, it has sought a meeting with the Secretary of State for Scotland. In that context, I hope that the Minister will encourage his right hon. Friend to meet Cumbernauld Town Council. I hope that there will be some thoroughgoing straight talking over the issues which confront the town.

Whatever the reasons for the particular problem in Cumbernauld which made the town council decide that it needed to increase the rates—some people feel that the council spends too much time discussing matters which are not of immediate JPEG 111 concern to its ratepayers—the ratepayers there will clearly be hit very hard. I hope that the Secretary of State will not be carried away by any political bias when he meets the town council, and that he will seek to help it in any way that he can.

For many years I have advocated—so far without success—that special assistance should be given to local authorities which have new towns in their areas. There is a substantial increase in infrastructure—of schools in particular, but also other services—which the local authorities have to provide as the town expands and develops. This places a heavy burden on existing ratepayers before the new ratepayers come along to play their part in carrying these burdens.

I think had that principle been established for the substantial new development that is taking place as a result of the actions of central Government, it would have helped the situation considerably. I understand this is happening in the northeast of Scotland, where actions outwith the control of Government but in which central Government play a considerable role, have created a situation that local authorities, in order to develop the necessary services, are having to carry a heavy burden on the rates, just as has been the case with local authorities in which new towns have been established.

Kirkintilloch normally announces its rates in August. I am afraid, therefore, that I do not have the figures for that burgh but the county requisition for the burgh has been increased by 20 per cent. this year. When one remembers that the county requisition is the largest part of that burgh's expenditure, one realises that this will have a major impact on the rates.

The other staggering figure is the 71 per cent. increase—nearly three-quarters—in the requisition from the Greater Glasgow Passenger Transport Authority. With increases such as these and with what is happening elsewhere, it is small wonder that the Kirkintilloch ratepayers are awaiting their fate with some trepidation.

I should like to say a word about this Greater Glasgow Passenger Transport Authority. It is causing much unrest among my own constituents and probably among those of other hon. Members. I understand that the Glasgow PTA was set up following legislation by the previous 112 Labour Government. There is great anger and resentment among my constituents that they are paying through the nose for a service from which they receive no benefit. Secondly, they are angered by the fact that, having seen no benefit, they now face staggering rises over which they have no control whatsoever. I hope that the Minister will address his mind to the problem.

In the past I have not been in this House and therefore I am in no position to criticise or defend the records of previous Governments to any extent. But having looked at these matters from outside as a ratepayer—one has to declare an interest—one has felt that previous Conservative Governments have expressed in action a genuine concern for the ratepayers. Perhaps the most important measure to help the ratepayer was the rate rebate scheme which will come into effect this year. It will help about 300,000 people.

The rates programme has been consistently increased by Conservative Governments despite the fact that it stagnated in the latter years of the previous Labour Government. We particularly gave help to the domestic ratepayer. In 1973–74, we saw from a Conservative Government the greatest increase in the percentage of grant to reckonable expenditure since rate support grant began.

The Government should have taken steps some time ago to ensure that the ratepayers would be shielded against the rate of inflation which is now escalating faster than ever before. It is not helpful to the ratepayer to introduce a rent freeze by Government action if the Government do not pay for it. The other matter, which is the fault not of Ministers but of some of their friends in the country, is that a large number of Labour councils will not accept any reasonable basis of self- discipline at a time of national inflation for which, when Labour Members were in opposition, they were blaming, in a most irresponsible way, the Conservative Government. Mr. Henderson

The situation which faces us now is this staggering rate rise which is causing great hardship to ordinary people, and it would have been far worse were it not for the rate rebate scheme which was planned by the previous Government. Rate rises are hitting living standards very directly, and they have no relevance whatsoever to the ability of people to pay.

There is a second way in which people are being hit more subtly and less obviously and that is by the very heavy level of rates on non-domestic subjects. People will have to pay more for the goods in the shops to help pay for the rates which the shops must pay.

Mr. Henderson
: I do not know much about agriculture, but no doubt my hon. Friends who do will deal with that. Hardship and injustice are staring many people in the face, and we want to know now what this Government will do about that.

It is a fact that the Government, regrettably, are in the seat of power. The Opposition do have not the power to change the current situation. The Government can do something, or they can do nothing about the situation. Whichever they do, they will have to face the fact that there is a grievance. We are drawing it to their attention, and we want to know what they intend to do.

In the short term, the Government can provide cash, and they can look again at JPEG 115 the rate support grant formula, which a number of local authorities believe is not equitable. They can look at the question of the old debts and deficits which will have to be considered with the reorganisation of local government.

For the long term, the Government should be showing some signs of action. In the short term the main thing is to find out what is the problem, where the remaining burdens are falling in local government, and what central Government can do to help. In the longer term, we must look at the relationship between central and local government more carefully. It is not clear to me whether the Secretary of State for Scotland has been consulting the Secretary of State for the Environment about future investigations of rates. I hope that that will be clarified and that Scottish interests on any Committee of inquiry will be carefully looked after.

In any long-term change in the rating system, we must recognise that central Government have an overall responsibility for the management of the national economy and that the total expenditure of local government is of crucial importance to that economy. Central Government therefore, must have some control over the total level of local government spending. But we have a long way to go in freeing local government from day-to day waddling up to St. Andrew's House over the most trivial matters. Even if it is only a matter of obtaining borrowing consent for a greenhouse, local government must go to St. Andrew's House.

Whatever system is introduced, it must be one in which, when local government knows what money is available, it can get on with spending it in the way that is most beneficial to the ratepayers. In the long term we may, because of the reform of local government, have a clearer opportunity for a radical reappraisal of local government finance. I am for a radical solution, but I recognise that it will be some time before we shall see the total change that I wish to see.

One of the reasons why rates are a particularly obnoxious form of raising money is that they are non-buoyant, and there is no expenditure in this country as buoyant as that of local government. Local government provides the services. They are personal services, many of which central Government have insisted that local government should provide. 116 Because they are services, they require people; they are personal and people services. Therefore, because of the rapidly increasing cost of employing a person, local government service costs have risen and will continue to rise more rapidly than those for any other services.

Local government expenditure has increased much more rapidly than has the wealth of the nation. The financing of that expenditure has been based on a principle which does not allow for greater wealth to be provided naturally.

In order to receive more money from the rates, one must increase the rate poundage, whereas in many other taxes the same tax level, because of buoyancy, will produce a greater income as the wealth of the nation rises.

If we are to see really substantial sums of money drawn from a tax of this kind, it becomes increasingly clear that the present system cannot be right and fair in the more complicated society of today. In the medium term, the right thing to do to rectify the situation is to share the cost between the Exchequer and local ratepayers. I ask the Minister to consider seriously the possibility of ensuring that after the reorganisation comes into effect rates are used to finance the personal, local services that one sees in this district of politics, and to recognise that most of the regional authority services which are provided by statute or by central Government decree are more properly a charge upon the national Exchequer. It can be done without any loss of local control over the way in which the money is spent. The idea that local initiative can be exercised only if the money is raised locally is not a sound argument.

Today is 4th July, 1974, and it is a significant date. It is my wife's birthday. It is also Independence Day in the United States. It could be made an even more significant date as a result of the debate this morning. Let it be the date on which a Labour Government at last responded to parliamentary pressure and gave substantial help to Scottish ratepayers at a time when they were suffering severely. Let it also be the date on which the Government signalled the beginning of the end of a system which no one likes.
