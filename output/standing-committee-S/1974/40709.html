Mr. Michael Ancram: I shall do my best, Sir Myer, not to require you to use your voice too much, by trying to keep in line during the course of the debate.

It is with a sense of urgency that I have the honour to initiate the debate on agriculture today, not only because I realise that it is a short debate and I do not wish to detain the Committee for too long, but—more importantly—because it is a debate on a matter of growing national importance.

May I begin by declaring my interest. I am a partner in a tenant farm in the Borders, and I would have hon. Members believe that this in itself allows me to speak with some experience about the actual day-to-day problems and the effects of Government policy on agriculture in Scotland generally.

I know—and this fills me with some apprehension—and I was reinforced in this belief by comments made by certain Government Members in this Committee last week, that agriculture is a dirty word among certain hon. Gentlemen opposite, but rumour has it that some of them have been seen buying and eating food. Some of that food is Scottish produced. What Col. 162 we are talking about, and what it is time Government Members realised, is that a farmer is not a rich capitalist bogeyman, as is so often suggested, but is the producer of one of the most essential commodities that we consume in this country, and that is food.

Of course there are rich farmers, but the vast majority of farmers in Scotland are working farmers, farmers with limited means, farmers with stretched finances, who are merely trying to produce the nation's food as economically as they can. If we intend to be realistic this morning, it is against this essentially mundane background that the debate should be conducted.

We have had sufficient debates on agriculture in the House recently to know in advance what the Government's line will be. I hope that I am not being unfair in suggesting that that line follows two themes: first, the theme that has been taken by the Government over the past four months that all the present problems were the previous Government's fault; and, secondly, that the present Government have done enough now to satisfy the needs of the farmers. We would do well this morning, if we do nothing else, to examine the validity of those two theses.

The Secretary of State for Scotland said forcefully in the debate the other night that the problem did not start on 1st March. I agree with him, and I shall give him another date concerning beef; namely, 23rd March when his right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Agriculture came back from Brussels with a package which, as it turned out, contained nothing more than the torn-up hopes of Scottish beef producers.

Of course problems existed before, as was shown in terms of a lowering market at the time of the election, but the farmers at that time were sustained by a knowledge that a price guarantee existed to catch them if the price fell too low. That confidence, so essential in every aspect of the farming industry, was totally eroded when the Secretary of State for Agriculture effectively announced that he had taken the bottom out of the beef market.

He realised this too, I believe, because, in an attempt to shore up that confidence, he let it be known that he had taken what he called an option on intervention. But, in statement after statement thereafter, he let it be known that he had no intention of intervening. In indisputable terms he Image Col. 163 removed completely the guaranteed price from the beef producers of Scotland for the first time since 1948, yet hon. Gentlemen opposite expressed surprise that confidence in the beef part of the market had collapsed.

This was not the end. At the same time, the right hon. Gentleman allowed the gap between the community's guide price and the United Kingdom guide price for beef not to decrease, as was the intention under the transitional arrangements, but to increase from £3.80 to £5.16. In effect, this quickly and cleanly undercut the home beef market, allowing, at no benefit to the British people£which was the prime concern£subsidised European beef to be imported at the expense of the British£ and, in our terms£the Scottish, farmer. It is little wonder that the farmers in my constituency sought urgent meetings with me to tell me that their livelihoods and the future of their beef production units were severly threatened.

We have now had some response from the Government, and I shall deal with that in a minute. On the line taken by the Secretary of State for Scotland that it was the last administration's fault, I make two comments. First, farming is a long-term business, requiring both confidence and an act of faith. The present Government belied that faith and destroyed that confidence. Secondly, the hon. Gentleman and his Friends are the Government now, and they must realise that they must stop looking back into the past for excuses and accept their responsibilities to provide solutions to the problems which in large measure, they have created themselves.

The food producers in my constituency rightly stress that they cannot produce food on excuses: they want answers and actions. We have been told that the right hon. Gentleman and his hon. Friends have taken action£and so, in part, they have£ and I shall look, briefly and realistically where possible, at what they are doing.

I return for a moment to the question of beef production. After intense pressure from the opposition in Committee and the threat of yet another Government defeat in the Chamber, the Government finally announced a certification scheme for fat cattle. The farmers of Scotland, like the Opposition, gave that announcement a qualified welcome, although we believe that it was to little and too late. Nevertheless, the welcome has been reflected in Col. 164 a slight firming in the market. It was the sort of welcome that a drowning man gives to someone who throws him a rope, but it becomes meaningful only when the rope begins to pull him to safety. It is not enough if it merely prolongs the time before that man drowns.

First, when will the scheme come into effect. We know that the machinery exists and that the industry cannot afford delays.

Secondly, what will this scheme entail? We have heard that the return will not drop below £18 per live hundredweight, but this in itself is ambiguous because we do not know whether this will be the initial rate of the certification or whether it will be an average seasonal rate. If it is seasonal, then it is quite clear that the summer guarantee will be far too low to do any good at all. We and the farmers already regard £18 as being too low, and if it is allowed to drop below that during the summer months the scheme might as well not be there.

Thirdly, what is the reason for the delay in announcing the specific details of the scheme? The farmers of Scotland require these details now, even before the scheme goes into operation, so that they may start budgeting and working out their plans for the future.

Why was it not until the end of June and the collapse of confidence in the industry that action was taken at all? Why could this action not have been taken when the crisis became apparent three months ago? For this, and this in particular, no one but the present Government can take the blame. If I may move, not quite from the sublime to the ridiculous, but from lime to pigs, I have to say that the picture is not so bright. Pig farmers in my constituency are facing vast financial difficulties, and most of them are now operating at a loss. It does not take much business sense to realise that this situation cannot; be allowed to continue if we still wish to have a pig-producing industry in this country.

What they seek, and what we seek today from the right hon. Gentleman and his hon. Friends, is a long-term policy to restore essential confidence. What the pig farmers have received so far under this Government has been totally inadequate and totally ineffective.

There have been subsidies to hold the price, but they have not, and cannot, reverse what is perhaps the most frightening trend of all in agriculture. They have merely increased consumption in a commodity where production is reducing. In short, it is the classic recipe for severe shortages within a fairly short time and, ultimately, for rationing.

Mr. Ancram: the hon. Gentleman's question shows nothing so clearly as that Image Col. 167 he does not understand farming. Gentlemen opposite realised that to a farmer farming land is the tool of his trade, and without which he cannot farm or produce. The very real fear of farmers about the wealth tax is tied to that.

This has one more frightening implication, and that is that young farmers, whom we need desperately to come into farming to ensure the security of our farming industry over the coming years, are being put off by the threat of what they sec as a law of diminishing returns operating because of a wealth tax.

To sum up what I fear has been a rather lengthy speech—although it is warranted by the seriousness of the situation—it is my opinion the right hon. Gentleman and his hon. Friends are presiding over a series of agricultural policies which some might call madness, but there is a growing suspicion that there is a method in this madness. The Government seem to take the view that if, by these methods, they can hold prices and hold back the growing floodwaters until after October, it does not matter if the dam bursts after that.

But if that is their purpose, they are playing politics with the nation's food supplies and threatening the future of the nation's food producers. They are threatening the livelihood of millions of farm workers who, by the nature of their employment, need a prosperous industry to secure a decent and acceptable income for the essential work that they do. The Government are playing roulette with the housewives' future. For the moment, the Government are able to maintain stable prices at the expense of a diminishing stock of future food supplies in the country. They know that the moment of truth will come, when short supply will inevitably cause prices to rise. The days of cheap food from abroad are gone, and the Government's continued complacency in this matter has become dangerous to the nation.

Scottish farming is sometimes Image Col. 169 overshadowed by industrial developments elsewhere in Scotland but, as I have said already, because it employs so many people and because of its importance to the community, the farming industry makes a substantial contribution to the economy and we are all concerned about seeing increased output and efficiency. That sector is of particular importance to Scotland, but I think that it would be right to take this opportunity of giving the Committee a more comprehensive view of the industry and its prospects. The hon. Gentleman covered a fairly wide area.

Looking back to the year 1973–74, one sees that the value of agricultural output in Scotland is estimated to have risen by 30 per cent. as compared with 1972–73. Most of this increase—perhaps as much as 90 per cent.—was due to higher prices. Egg prices rose by three-quarters, cereal prices by two-thirds, poultry meat prices by more than one-half, and smaller increases were the general rule for most other commodities. In terms of volume, the increase amounted to about 3 per cent., which is similar to the average increase over the previous few years. This in itself is a tribute to the industry. Fat cattle production was the main contributor, and there were also increases in the volume of farm and horticultural crops, poultry meat and eggs.

The increase in farm product prices was matched by large increases in farming costs due almost entirely to higher prices. Interest charges rose substantially, the feed bill increased by one-half, and a good deal more was paid by farmers for fuel, fertilisers, seeds and labour.

In this, of course, agriculture is not dissimilar from many other industries. I am not suggesting that all the problems were left by the previous Government, but no one would be foolish enough to claim that the problems of inflation started after 28th February. Such things as the increase in interest charges, which have a significant effect on farming, certainly cannot be laid at our door. The hon. Gentleman was a wee bit unfair in what he said. He left the impression that the main theme of the Government's case was that all the problems had been caused by the previous Government.

Col. 170 It is too soon to say exactly what effect these changes in output and costs will have had on income in the year 1973–74. Dairy farm incomes may have fallen back by about one-quarter to little more than their 1971–72 level, even after allowing for the special lump sum payment provided for at this year's review. On the other hand, arable farm incomes are expected to have risen substantially, and incomes in the hill and upland sector are also expected to have shown some improvement. I realise that it is dangerous to single out one section of this industry, because one can make a case for almost anything that way, so perhaps hon. Members will take note of that.

I turn now to the prospects for 1975–76. I said at the outset that I did not wish to concentrate unduly on livestock. Nevertheless, this sector, which accounts for more than 75 per cent. of the industry's earnings in Scotland, must take first place in a general account of this kind. It is true to say that, whereas the previous administration had belatedly attempted to tackle the question of milk at this year's Farm Price Review, it had no solution for the problems of beef and pig producers. Within a few weeks of coming to office, we increased the calf subsidy by £10 per head—more than double. No one should underestimate the importance of this for Scottish producers. About one-fifth of the calf subsidy is disbursed in Scotland, and the increase will give Scottish farmers about £7 million in a full year. Of that sum, two-thirds is estimated to go to farms that have been classified as eligible for the hill subsidies. To them, it is the equivalent of an increase of £10 or more in the hill cow subsidy. This was a substantial measure to safeguard future production.

We have also reversed the decision of the previous Government to abolish the lime subsidy. The hon. Member for Berwick and East Lothian referred to this. This is also of special importance to Scotland. The Scottish share of the subsidy is £1·3 million, and it contains Image Col. 171 an element to offset the higher production and transport costs in Scotland. It is of particular benefit to the hills and uplands and to the islands.

Of course, the prosperity of the hill cattle farmer depends above all on confidence in an adequate level of returns for fat cattle. Until June of this year, the fat cattle market remained remarkably firm, though everyone knew that there was a good deal of beef on the way.
