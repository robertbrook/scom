FIRST STANDING COMMITTEE ON STATUTORY INSTRUMENTS
Wednesday 3rd July 1974

[Mrs. JOYCE BUTLER in the Chair]

DRAFT ELECTRICITY (BORROWING POWERS) (SCOTLAND) ORDER 1974

10.30 a.m.

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Mr. Bruce Millan)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft instrument.

The draft order would raise the limit on the borrowing powers of the Scottish Electricity Boards from its present level of £950 million to £1,200 million, the maximum provided for in Section 47(7) of the Electricity Act 1947 as amended by the Electricity Act 1972. This limit applies to the combined total of the outstanding borrowings of the two Scottish electricity boards, including any borrowing from abroad and any temporary borrowings.

The explanatory memorandum which was published with the Electricity Bill, which became the Electricity Act 1972, explained that the purpose of inserting the interim limit of £950 million in the Bill was to ensure that Parliament would have an opportunity of reviewing the progress of the electricity industry in Scotland within two or three years. By the end of May this year, outstanding borrowings by the Scottish electricity boards stood at £870 million, with a considerable amount of borrowing in prospect during the next few months. That means that we have to take action now to increase the limit. Hon. Members will note that this debate is taking place at about the time that was forecast in 1972.

We have two separate electricity boards in Scotland, but we have a joint borrowing limit. The reason is that this assists the joint planning and operation of the two Scottish electricity supply systems. Hon. Members who were here at the time, and perhaps others as well, will remember the recommendation of the Mackenzie Committee in 1962 that the two boards should be amalgamated. But there was 4 a strong feeling then, as there is now, that they should be maintained as separate electricity boards, and that is the present position.

Nevertheless, in order to achieve the most economic development of generating capacity in Scotland, planning is considered and undertaken jointly by the boards on the recommendation of a joint planning committee. The systems themselves are linked so that we have, to a considerable extent, an overall Scottish system instead of two separate s.), stems. Because generating capacity gives rise to such a large proportion of the need for borrowing by the Scottish boards, we have a joint financial limit.

If we increase, as I hope we shall through this order, the limit to £1,200 million, it may be asked how long the new limit will last. The rate of increase in electricity demand has been lower than was expected at the time of the passing of the Electricity Act 1972. Demand will be further affected by the recent increase in electricity prices, among other things. Forecasting of future electricity demand in Scotland is particularly difficult in present circumstances. It is not only that electricity prices have gone up; oil prices have, too. There is always the overall economic situation to take into account, and there is a particularly difficult energy situation not only in Britain but world-wide to be considered. As an indication of the kind of changes in forecasts that have already been made by the electricity boards in Scotland, perhaps I should tell the Committee that the load estimate for the winter of 1977–78, which was 8,607 MW, as shown in the brochure "Plans for the Future 1971–1978 "published with the 1972 bill, has now been reduced to about 7,700 MW.

It is not possible, however, to reflect the reduction in demand as forecast in a reduction in the present power station construction programme, since so much of it is already in train. The construction of the third set at Inverkip, an oil-fired generating station, was advanced by one year by the previous Government as one of the measures taken to improve employment in Scotland in the years 197–73 and 1973–74. The construction of Peterhead power station, an oil and/or gas-fired station, has had to be held to its timetable because of the local need for power, which JPEG 5 is particularly acute in the North-East, especially as industrial and other demands increase, owing to North Sea oil development.

The boards therefore expect that in 1978 there will be a larger than usual margin of generating plant, and when the Peterhead station is completed in 1979 no further additional capacity will be required before 1982. As a result, despite price increases, particularly for fuel stocks, the new borrowinig limit of £1,200 million is still expected to last until well into 1977–78.

I shall mention some of the other projects that are under construction at present. In the North Board's district the pumped storage scheme at Foynes is expected to be fully operational by the end of the year. A good start has been made on the new oil and gas dual-fired station at Peterhead.

In the South area, as hon. Members will be aware, there have been considerable problems in the construction of the AGR nuclear station at Hunterston B, including modifications necessary as a result of corrosion experienced in the earlier Magnox stations at Hunterston A. These various difficulties have delayed the completion of the station. Similar problems have been encountered by the CEGB south of the border, but the board now hopes to feed electricity into the grid from Hunterston B by the end of this year, and to have both reactors operational from next year. Further up the Clyde, the large oil-fired generating station at Inverkip is progressing satisfactorily.

The boards are planning further ahead although no further stations will need to be commissioned before 1982. As the Committee knows, suitable sites for power stations are scarce, partly because so few locations can meet the strict physical requirements, but also because power stations raise in an acute form considerations of amenity and environment.

The boards are, therefore, looking as far ahead as possible to identify suitable sites and are setting in motion the formal procedures for their approval. Three such sites in the South Board's area are the subject of applications before the Secretary of State: at Carriden near Bo'ness—where there has already been a public 6 inquiry—for a dual oil and gas-fired station; at Torness Point near Dunbar, where the public inquiry is still in operation; and Chapeldonan near Girvan, where there has yet to be an inquiry. In the latter two cases, they are sites for nuclear stations. There is also an application covering the installation of a fourth generating unit at Inverkip.

The board's purpose in making these applications well in advance of the requirement for these stations is to build up a bank of sites which would give a choice of sites and a choice of fuel and take into account the geographical distribution of the sites that might be required.

I ought to make clear here, as I did in our debate last Thursday in the Scottish Grand Committee, that even if the sites are cleared—this is a matter for my right hon. Friend—the construction of any power station is agreed under a separate and later procedure. Therefore, even the clearance of the site does not by itself settle the construction of power stations of any particular type and at any particular point in time. As I said that is a separate procedure.

When the board sees the need for a new station, at that point it considers the type of fuel it would want to use and the particular location which will meet its requirements. The Secretary of State then considers the timing. He will also consider the question of fuel and the other implications for the environment and the rest. The environment question will have been cleared basically at the planning stage, but the other considerations will come into operation at that later time. In particular, it is at that time that the overall question of energy policy and the relationship of any particular application to overall energy requirements becomes one of the most acute considerations.

It is obvious that this kind of procedure presents considerable advantages from the boards' point of view, importing an element of flexibility into the situation, and it presents considerable advantages from the point of view of the Government, too. But I appreciate that it imposes certain disadvantages, or what will seem to be considerable disadvantages, for the people living near the projected sites, for which planning applications are submitted long before JPEG 7 the actual requirement. This is an almost unavoidable problem at the present time. If one leaves the application until too late, one then has the position which arose a number of years ago at Inverkip when the board said that, unless it obtained that particular site, there would quickly be a shortfall of electricity generating capacity which would be extremely serious from the Scottish point of view. A stringent timetable presents the worst kind of situation in which to make a planning decision. If we pay certain penalties, as I fear we do, from the point of view of the local population for planning in advance in the way I have described, these are more than outweighed by the penalties that could be imposed not only on the boards and the Government but on the people of Scotland, especially those in a critical location, if matters have to be decided with a stringent time element which would not permit the best decision from everybody's point of view. I hope I have explained the balance of advantage on one side or the other in proceeding as the boards are at present.

I have talked basically about electricity generation because it is the capital expenditure which is involved in that which makes up the bulk of the electricity boards' borrowing requirements. I should be happy to try to answer questions on any other aspects of the boards' affairs which are relevant to this order.

Mr. Millan

On the narrow point which the hon. Gentleman has raised, I have given the only answer possible. I dealt principally with electricity generation because that is the basic component of the demand by the electricity boards for increased borrowing powers, but, of course, there are other demands as well.

If there are other points, naturally I shall be glad to try to answer them, but I hope that there is a general feeling in Scotland—this came out in last Thursday's debate, despite the high feeling generated by the recent large price increases—that the Scottish electricity boards perform a very useful service in a highly efficient way. They need the money to continue to do that, and I therefore have pleasure in commending the order to the Committee.
