10.31 a.m.

The Minister of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs (Mr. Roy Hattersley): I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft European Communities (Definition of Treaties) Order 1974.

With permission, I shall make a brief statement of introduction and explanation, attempting to clarify why today's procedure is necessary and why we conduct ourselves over these matters in the way that we do. I hope I may, therefore, simply introduce the five items referred to in the order in the briefest of terms, leaving the majority of the time this morning to hon. Members, in the knowledge that if your eye is caught, Mr. Wells, and if it is the Committee's wish, my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary of State for Trade will do his best to deal with the substance of the five items, their content and their importance. He will deal not simply with those three items which are directly his departmental responsibility but will do his best to answer questions on two other treaties which are more properly the responsibility of the Department of Overseas Development.

I hope that the Committee will understand that I mean no disrespect if briefly this morning I absent myself from the proceedings. These are difficult times in the Foreign Office and a number of urgent matters are at this moment under consideration. It may, therefore, be my painful duty to leave briefly in the middle of our proceedings, and I hope that the Committee will forgive me if that proves to be necessary.

The European Communities Act 1972 gives effect in the United Kingdom to all rights, powers, liabilities, obligations and restrictions embodied in treaties of the Community. Those Community rights and obligations do not apply in this country Col. 4 unless they arise under the Community treaties as defined in Section 1 of that Act—a guide for the assistance of members of the public being appended to that Act in the form of a list of treaties which were then appropriate to, and are now governed by, that Act.

That is how acts of the Communities obtain the force of law in Great Britain. Agreements entered into, or acts made, after 22nd January 1972—the date of the Treaty of Accession—are not regarded as Community treaties and therefore do not carry the relevant Community advantages, rights, privileges and penalties unless they are designated officially and formally under a procedure covered by Section 1(3) of the European Communities Act. That is what we are aiming to do today, giving under those powers full right and status for Community treaties so that the force of law as discharged in the courts of this country, and discharged in other ways, applies to them.

In practice, most of the treaties which need to be designated in this way are treaties not of the European Economic Community but of the European Coal and Steel Community. That comes about because treaties carried out by the ECSC, and arrangements entered into by it, are traditionally—for reasons of history which need not detain the Committee—normally made as agreements between individual States and then need to be given this Community status by procedures under Section 1(3) of the European Communities Act. That, basically, is the procedure we offer and ask for in terms of the first three items in today's order.

The draft order was laid on 8th June as a definition of and treaties order. Five treaties are listed in the schedule, and each of those treaties with texts in their entirety has been available in the House to hon. Members either as a Command Paper or as part of the official journal of the European Community. Of the five treaties specified, three are instruments of the European Coal and Steel Community, laid for the reason that characterised that Community, which I have tried to describe. With the Committee's permission, I shall now deal with the three treaties relevant to the European Coal and Steel Community.

The first of the three treaties provides for tariff preferences for products covered Image Col. 5 by the European Coal and Steel Community which originate in Finland. In fact, under our obligations to and in the EFTA, the United Kingdom is already providing tariff-free access for Finnish goods. Therefore, all that the treaty does is to oblige us not to re-impose tariffs on Finnish material. We are giving the free trade provisions which have long been the practice of this country the status of Community law and committing ourselves not to move away from that practice in the future.

The second and third European Coal and Steel Community treaties provide for tariff preferences and quotas for certain steel goods which originate in developing countries. They form part of the general scheme of preference of the Communities, which the British Government hope to see extended as part of our negotiating aims. Therefore, the second and third items in the schedule are greatly welcomed by the Government.

The fourth and fifth items deal with overseas aid. They are protocols extending earlier agreements entered into by the United Kingdom, extending the Wheat Trade Convention and the Food Aid Convention, both of 1971 and both now incorporated into the International Wheat Agreement of 1971. They appear in the schedule for a reason that is almost but not quite technical. As the original acts were said to be Community acts, in the extension of them by these protocols it was thought right to include them in today's order so that the protocol extension itself could be given the status of a Community agreement.

Today's draft order follows procedures and precedents laid down by the House in 1972 and 1973. I assure the Committee that all the treaties for which we ask designation in this form are treaties which the Government believe are in no way inimical to the Government's objects of renegotiating the terms of British entry into the EEC. I believe, too, that the benefits, although they may be technical in terms of the fourth and fifth items, are necessary for a prudent Government to provide to ensure that laws which affect this country and those with whom we trade have a clearly defined and precise status. I hope that the Committee will feel it proper to accept the provisions.

This debate is a further demonstration that Parliament can debate EEC acts. The order offers a better opportunity of debating EEC acts than we should have if each of the matters under discussion had been an agreement made directly between the United Kingdom acting alone and the other international organisations referred to. I am advised that the Wheat Trade Convention of 1971 was never debated in the House. We have an opportunity to debate the protocol today. As regards the first three matters—Finnish products and the generalised scheme of preferences—had they been matters which were the subject of agreement directly between this country and other countries, they would have been subject to the negative resolution procedure and not, as is the case here, to the affirmative resolution procedure. Thus, they might not have been debated. The United Kingdom scheme of generalised preferences for developing countries was debated only because it was put down by my hon. Friend the Member for Leicester, South (Mr. Boardman) in 1971.

The second feature of the five matters we are debating is that they are all enlarging benefits which are enjoyed by other countries. In my opinion, we, too, shall benefit from them. They all encourage trade or aid. Therefore, I welcome them. But I should point out that they give no encouragement to those hon. Members who take the view that the Communities would be inward-looking bodies.

With regard to trade with Finland, the Minister of State outlined clearly and concisely what the decision does. We welcome the decision to preserve our free trade with Finland. It also reduces the tariffs imposed by other members of the ECSC on Finnish goods to 60 per cent. of what they were formerly. This is a process of gradual reduction to nil over a period of years.

I wish to put one question to the Minister of State. The decision is an interim one pending the coming into force of the agreement between the member States of the ECSC and Finland, which was signed last October. I take Image Col. 7 it that the reason for the interim decision is simply that the processes of ratification by the various member countries have not yet been completed, and there is no particular reason for the delay. Perhaps the Minister of State or his hon. Friend will answer that question when winding up the debate.

The next two decisions concern the scheme of generalised preferences of the Community. We have now come to an agreement, a compromise, with the other members of the Community on the sort of scheme to be brought into force, and 1974 is the first year in which there has been a joint scheme, a harmonised scheme, of generalised preferences. It has involved certain changes in the techniques which we formerly used and, no doubt, certain changes in the techniques which the other countries formerly used, but the net result has been an enlargement of the area of free trade in the world.

I understand—perhaps the Minister will confirm this—that the generalised scheme of preferences of the Community has shown a steady liberalisation over the years. The Community was, I think, the first body to bring in a scheme of generalised preferences. I am told that the coverage of the current scheme. which I think is the 1973–74 scheme, is 40 per cent. more generous than the scheme in the previous year. That is, of course, allowing for the fact that the Community has been enlarged by the adherence of three member countries. I am told also that the number of sensitive items which enjoy special protection under this scheme has been steadily reduced, which again is a process of continuous liberalisation. I understand that in 1973 there were 60 sensitive items, that in 1974 there are 52, and that the proposals of the Commission for the next year of the scheme involve a substantial reduction in the number of sensitive items to seven.

I understand also that the Community is proposing an expansion in the information service for developing countries so as to make sure that the developing countries are aware of the benefits which are available to them under the generalised preference scheme. So what is happening with the generalised preference scheme is that we are spreading the benefits of freer trade with the Community across Col. 8 the whole developing world. That is one of the objectives which the former Conservative Government believed and the present Government believe to be desirable, that the benefits of trade with the Community should not be limited only to the African, Caribbean and Pacific countries of the developing world but that they should be more widely spread across the other continents as well.

In relation to the last two items—the Food Aid Convention and the Wheat Trade Convention—will the Minister clarify certain points? Command Paper No. 5624 gives the text of the protocols, but it does not say how they have been worked out or who the parties are. Is the Committee to assume that the parties to the protocols are the same parties as originally concluded the earlier agreements?

Second, in the food aid protocol no figure is set down for the amount of food aid which it is intended that the Community shall provide. In the previous agreement, which this is prolonging, there was a figure of just over 1 million tons of grains which was intended to be provided by the Community of Six. As I recall, the Report of the Select Committee on Overseas Development, issued last summer, said that the intention was that the amount of food aid in the form of grains which would be provided by the enlarged Community after the adherence of the new members would be increased to 1,287,000 tonnes, of which 225,000 tonnes would be provided by this country.

Why does no figure appear in the protocol for the Community? It may be —I surmise that this is the reason—that the intention is to preserve flexibility so that we in the Community can take a decision about the exact details of our food aid programme both for this year and for coming years. I surmise that from a recent report from the Commission which, I understand, was debated by the European Parliament only last Friday, so that I have not seen the report of the debate. The report dates from April 1974, Document 37/74, and sets out a comprehensive proposal for food aid for the coming three years. It makes the point that the developing countries are facing a serious food crisis, more serious than any other since the war, and that this is especially true, as hon. Members Image Col. 9 will be well aware, of the poorest countries, those which have not benefited from the increase in the price of oil nor from the increase in the price of other primary commodities such as minerals.

The immediate reasons for this serious crisis for the developing countries are fully set out in the document—the recent droughts, the big purchases of grain by the Soviet Union, the increase in demand from many parts of the developed world. In the longer term, the reasons are the failure of the green revolution to come up to expectations, the rapid increase in population, the social structures of some of the developing countries, and scarcity of capital.

The forecasts of the dimensions of the food problem for the poorer countries of the developing world are very alarming, and they tie in with forecasts which are coming from the Food and Agriculture Organisation and from the United Nations. They are relevant for a num- ber of reasons, but they are relevant also to the optimism which has been expressed about the prospects for prices of food on the world market. If these forecasts are correct, it would be unrealistic of us to expect that world food prices will come down. That means that the Community's food aid programme will be even more important than it otherwise would have been.

The Commission proposed that the Nine members should adopt a three-year indicative programme with minimum and maximum quantities each year a firm figure should be set, depending on the needs of the various recipient countries. The minimum quantity of food grains specified is 1,700,000 tonnes, which is a substantial rise on the figures I quoted which were reported by the Select Committee a year ago. The maximum figure for food grains is 2,500,000 million tonnes. There are also proposals for food aid in the form of other commodities such as skimmed milk, butter, oil and so on. The report takes note also of the fact that the food aid programme of the United States, which until now has been one of the principal food programmes in the world, is declining in size.

The conclusions of the Select Commit- tee which reported last year were that, first, food aid had a valuable role to play; second, that in the long run we should not encourage the developing countries Col. 10 to rely on food aid but we should encourage them to develop their own agriculture so that they could be more self-reliant; and third, that the programme of food aid should not be used as a means of getting rid of surpluses resulting from the agricultural policy of the Community. If this were to happen the developing countries would be discouraged from developing their own agriculture.

I am glad to see on reading the document from the European Commission that it takes full account of these points. It makes another point, too, which will be welcome to members of the Committee, namely, that while some of the aid proposed should be direct Community aid, supplied by the Community to recipient countries, a substantial and possibly increasing part of it should be channelled through multilateral bodies, notably the World Food Programme which is a specialised agency of the United Nations.

I should, therefore, like the Minister to tell us whether I am right in believing that the purpose of the form that the protocol takes, which we are being asked to approve as part of the order, is to provide flexibility so that we can fit into it the food programme of the Community on the lines outlined in this Community document—if that is approved by the Council of Ministers. It has not yet, of course, been to the Council of Ministers.

I want the Minister to answer three questions. First, quantities. The proposals in the document are expressed in quantities, and I mentioned some of the quantities just now. If they are to be expressed in quantities, do we not have difficulty in estimating in advance what the cost will be, bearing in mind the wildly fluctuating prices for world food that we have seen in the past two years? I am not suggesting that this is an insuperable problem, but it seems to me to be a problem.

Second, what are the budgetary implications for this country in connection with our contribution to the Community budget? This is one of the matters which the Government have taken up with the Community, and it will be useful for the Committee to know how the cost of the food aid programme, so far as it affects the United Kingdom, will affect our contribution to the Community budget.

Image Col. 11 Third, I should like an assurance that this food aid programme, if it is adopted by the Council, will not be at the expense of other aid programmes in this country.

I end by making one suggestion. When the food aid programme is adopted by the Council, as I hope it will be, arrangements should be made for much better publicity, both in the member countries and world-wide, for the fact that the Community already has an ambitious and generous food aid programme which we intend to improve.

With those words, I welcome the document before us.
