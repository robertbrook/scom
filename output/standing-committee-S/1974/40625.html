SCOTTISH GRAND COMMITTEE

OFFICIAL REPORT

Tuesday, 25th June, 1974

[SIR MYER GALPERN in the Chair]

LAND TENURE REFORM (SCOTLAND) BILL [ LORDS]

10.30 a.m.

Resolved,

That if the proceedings on the Land Tenure Reform (Scotland) Bill [Lords] are not completed at this day's sitting, the Committee do meet on Thursday next at half-past Ten o'clock.—[ Mr. William Ross.]

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. William Ross)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the Land Tenure Reform (Scotland) Bill [Lords] relating to the principle of the Bill.

The subject matter of the Bill is already familiar to many of us who took part in the deliberations on a Bill with the same Title which was introduced by the previous administration in November, 1973, and which had passed through all its stages in the Commons and had received a Second Reading in another place before the Dissolution of Parliament in February of this year.

We on this side of the Committee gave a broad welcome to that earlier Bill, although we criticised it for its very tardy appearance and because it did not, in our view, go nearly far enough. In view of the delay which had already occurred, the present Government felt that it was important to ensure that this legislation reached the Statute Book as soon as possible. Rather than taking further time in discussions in relation to a longer and more complex Bill and to work out the considerably more extensive proposals which we should like to see, we thought it right to introduce this Bill, which is generally based on its predecessor.

We have, nevertheless, been able to improve it in a number of important respects, with the result that the Bill which we are considering today goes a good deal further in the direction of getting rid of feuduties. To that extent it will bring about a much speedier end to this 2 quite outdated annual payment. It will, at the same time, simplify in a number of ways the further legislation which is needed to abolish the feudal system entirely—which remains our aim.

In these circumstances, it is not necessary for me to take up the time of the Committee by describing all the provisions of the Bill in detail, but I should like to say a word or two about the two main features of it. The first relates to the primary purpose of the Bill, dealt with in Clause 1, to prohibit the creation of new feuduties. The previous administration's Bill contained a provision whereby in new feus there was still to be a feuduty of one penny, if asked. I remember my right hon. Friend the Lord Advocate criticising that at the time as a "feudal fiction." Other hon. Members, including my hon. Friend the Member for Dundee, West (Mr. Doig), pointed out the difficulties that might arise from the retention of a feuduty, however small, even although it was intended to be of a purely nominal nature.

The reason for this somewhat curious proposal was that some nominal money payment was thought to be essential to preserve the feudal link between proprietor and superior, until such time as the feudal system as a whole could be abolished. The present Government accept that it is necessary to keep this link, although as a purely temporary arrangement—for example, in connection with the enforcement of land conditions, which can often be as much in the proprietor's interest as the superior's. We propose, however, to do this in such a way that it is not necessary to have any feuduty in future feus. This means that Clause I of the Bill, unlike its predecessor, really does prohibit new feuduties completely.

The second major improvement which we have introduced into the Bill is even more far-reaching. We have provided in Clause 5 for the compulsory redemption of feuduty on the first sale of a property. This will greatly speed up the phasing out of the whole money side of the fuedal system. I understand that in Scotland about 75,000 houses change hands each year, and most of these properties are subject to feuduty. This compulsory redemption will be carried out by the person who sells the property, and the redemption price will be calculated by the same formula, in Clause 4(2), related to the price of 2½ per cent. Consols— JPEG 3 as applies in the case of voluntary redemption of feuduty by a proprietor, for which the Bill, like its forerunner, also contains provision.

I am satisfied that these arrangements will be fair to all concerned. The person redeeming the feuduty will have money in his hands from the sale of his property. It is the seller who is responsible for the transaction. The redemption formula will produce an equivalent income for the superior calculated in accordance with current interest rates. The effect will be that just as a person buying a new house will not in future find that his property has been burdened by some new feuduty or other perpetual money payment, so the person who buys an existing property will find that any individual feuduty with which that property has been burdened in the past is redeemed before he goes into it.

With the greatly increased turnover of house property in recent years in Scotland, this will make an immediate and sizeable impact on the wiping out of existing feuduties; to that extent it is a major step forward towards our ultimate goal.

I shall now go very briefly through the parts of the Bill.

Mr. Teddy Taylor
: Before the Secretary of State continues, will he deal with one point? What will be the position if the sale of the property relates to the compulsory purchase by a local authority of a property of which the owner has disappeared, or of whom there is no trace?

Mr. Ross
: I will deal with that point. The hon. Member will probably find that it will be covered by something that I shall say about compulsory purchase.

Part I deals with the prohibition of future feuduties and the redemption of existing feuduties. The main clauses are Clause 1, with which I have already dealt; Clause 4, which gives any proprietor —and remember it is the proprietor, not the superior, who has the right— if he so wishes, the right to redeem his feuduty at any term of Whitsunday or Martinmas; and Clause 5 dealing with the compulsory redemption of existing feuduties on a sale. I should perhaps also mention Clause 6, which perhaps covers the point which the hon. Gentleman raised. It extends the compulsory redemption provisions to the 4 case where land is acquired by an authority possessing compulsory purchase powers—for example, in the course of redevelopment of one of the older parts of a town. In this case, the redemption of feuduty will become the responsibliity of the compulsory purchasing authority, and so it will cover the case that the hon. Gentleman mentioned.

Part II of the Bill, which limits the residential use of property let under future long leases, is in substantially the same form as the corresponding part of the previous administration's Bill, and I do not think it should give rise to any controversy. Basically, the purpose of Part II is to restrict future long residential leases to a maximum term of 20 years. This will, in effect, implement one of the points in the seven-point plan which the previous Labour Government put forward in a White Paper, "Land Tenure in Scotland", published in July, 1969.

This is an important element of the Bill. It is essential that we should legislate to prohibit long residential leases at the same time as we abolish feuduty, because the long residential lease could easily be used by a landowner as a means of getting round the ban on feuduties. From the point of view of the prospective owner of a house, it would be of little comfort to get rid of feuduty, and then find himself subject to an annual payment, virtually an annual rent, under, say, a 99-year lease. Indeed, he would be even worse off because he would never really own his house at all, and at the end of the day it would revert to the landlord.

This brings me to the other reason why it is essential to prohibit long residential leases—the serious social difficulties to which these leases can give rise, to which, indeed, they have given rise in practice in the past, not just in England and Wales, but in Scotland. Hon. Members who were here at the time will recollect that we had to introduce a special measure in 1954 to deal with questions of long leases falling-in in the Lanarkshire area, the village of Stonehouse and other places. We thought that there were no long leases in Scotland; then we discovered that there was a considerable number, even extending up into Aberdeenshire, Ayrshire, Lochmaben and so on. The Bill produced on the basis of the formula gave the right to change those leases into feuduties provided it was done within five years; most people did.

Mr. T. G. D. Galbraith
: I wonder whether 20 years is long enough, or whether it should be allowed to be the lifetime of the lessee. What would be wrong in a lease between a lessor and a lessee which extended for the life of the lessee? Presumably the lessee would have agreed to it otherwise. Would the right hon. Gentleman consider that aspect?

Mr. Ross
: I am prepared to consider that. We have looked at the number of years— 20, 30, 40 or 50. One has to draw a reasonable line which shows clearly the intentions behind the legislation. If one goes beyond that, the lifetime may be considerable; and one could find oneself virtually back to the troublesome 99-year lease. One must keep it within a manageable period.

The long residential lease is not at present common in Scotland, though there are some. We must make absolutely sure that landowners do not switch into this form of tenure when they can no longer impose feuduties. What we are proposing is in line with the previous administration's Bill, and I think there will be general agreement on all sides of the Committee that these provisions are right.

Part III of the Bill deals with miscellaneous matters. Some of the clauses in this Part are included to prevent possible methods of evading the restrictions imposed by Parts I and II. No doubt, there are people who will seek to do that, and we have sought to obviate it in advance. Other clauses make desirable technical changes, mainly in relation to leases.

Part IV is largely formal. I would, however, draw attention to the proposed commencement date of the Bill which under Clause 24(2), is to be 1st September, 1974. We want to ensure that the prohibition of new feuduties and the compulsory redemption of existing feu-duties when property is sold begin to bite as soon as possible. The proposed date will also mean that proprietors who wish to exercise their right to redeem their feuduties voluntarily will have ample time to make the necessary arrangements to do so at Martinmas 1974, which is 11th November. It is particularly important that this right should be available at the next term-day, because I understand that, partly in anticipation 6 of the Bill, increasing numbers of superiors are already giving proprietors the opportunity of redeeming their feuduties on a variety of terms, not all of which are as favourable to the proprietors as the redemption formula contained in the Bill. This situation is likely to give rise to doubt and confusion in the minds of many proprietors, so that the sooner the statutory terms and their rights are known and made available to them the better.

I emphasise that there is nothing in the Bill which will allow the superior in any circumstances to dictate when redemption of a feuduty is to take place. The proprietor may choose to redeem his feuduty if he so wishes at any Whitsunday or Martinmas, and if the proprietor decides to sell his property he must then redeem the feuduty. If the Bill were in operation today, the redemption formula in Clause 4(2) would work out at about 6½ times the amount of the annual feuduty.

In conclusion, I stress that the Bill is the second stage of the Government's overall programme of land tenure reform, as set out in the 1969 White Paper. The first stage was the enactment of the Conveyancing and Feudal Reform (Scotland) Act 1970 which, amongst other things gave proprietors the right to apply to the Lands Tribunal for Scotland for the variation or discharge of unreasonable or obstructive land conditions. I understand that proprietors have already derived considerable advantage from this provision.

The primary purpose of the Bill is to deal effectively with the money side of the feudal system. There remains the final winding-up of the system as a whole, with which are inextricably linked future arrangements in relation to the creation and enforcement of land conditions. The Government's intention is to bring forward further legislation on this as soon as practicable. In the meantime, I commend the Bill to the Committee. It has already passed through its stages in another place and I hope that we can give it a fair wind to allow it to reach port soon, after its somewhat erratic voyage through the vicissitudes of Parliament with its dissolutions and elections.
