10.30 a.m.

Question again proposed.

Mr. George Younger
: Before we were interrupted at the last sitting I was complaining that the extra money which the Government have produced and which local authorities are to be required to produce for the rent freeze was being concentrated upon people with higher incomes and was being paid only to a small extent to those with lower incomes, and I was suggesting various ways by which this could be put right, particularly by raising the needs allowance. As I said, I welcome the Government's proposals for housing action areas and the improvements which the Government have been able to make in the Bill. Small though they are, they are quite important.

In our debate last Tuesday the hon. Member for Roxburgh, Selkirk and Peebles (Mr. Steel) referred to various discussions I had had with local authorities, particularly with Galashiels. Galashiels has a particularly difficult problem with older homes which are widely spread and in small parcels all over the town. I ask the Minister of State to spell out a little more how flexible the action area procedure will be in that sort of situation. When I visited Galashiels I assured the town council that the Conservative Government's intention was that housing action areas could, if necessary, be very small, just as they could be very large. If there are any towns which like Galashiels, have old homes which need attention, possibly on one side of a street or in part of a street, will the Minister assure us that it will be possible for local authorities to declare even such small areas to be housing action areas if they satisfy the Secretary of State that the criteria are fulfilled?

56 It happens, through nobody's fault, that the circumstances today are as unfavourable as they may be for some time for carrying out a dramatic drive on the remaining unfit areas. The higher rate of grant generally is being discontinued and the money is being concentrated only on action areas. The old local authorities in their last year of life are unlikely to make a major drive on the problem. Nor is it likely that the new local authorities in their preparatory year will find the time and energy to plan ahead a programme for older houses which are falling into decay. The Scottish Office should, therefore, put its finger firmly on the problem during the next 12 to 18 months.

Would the Minister of State consider it a good idea to set up within the Scottish Office a monitoring unit with sole responsibility for keeping under monthly scrutiny local authorities' plans for dealing with the remaining unfit houses? I know that the Scottish Office does its best to check up, but inevitably, unless a small unit within the Scottish Office has sole responsibility for keeping local authorities under continual pressure, in two or three years' time we shall come back again to the problem which has been with us for the last 50 years. I hope that the Minister of State will consider that suggestion, as I am sure that it would be helpful to him, to the Committee and to Scottish Members of Parliament to feel that firm pressure was being exerted.

While we worry about older houses which have fallen into disrepair, through nobody's fault there is a danger of many of our comparatively new houses, perhaps no more than 10 years old, becoming substandard unless something is done about them. The only way to prevent these houses from falling into decay and becoming beyond repair is to raise the priority given by local authorities and all of us to housing management generally.

When I was at the Scottish Office we appointed a part-time adviser on housing management. I hope that that will be a useful appointment, and I wish the adviser and all who are associated with him every success. Housing management generally has not had a high enough priority in the past. I pay tribute to housing managers generally. Not only do they carry an extremely difficult burden, often with little thanks, but local authorities expect them to carry out improvements without giving JPEG 57 them adequate resources to do so. In general, our housing managers do a magnificent job and deserve a great deal more help and support.

Housing management has two functions. First, it tries to ensure that our existing stock of perfectly adequate housing is used to its best advantage and kept in decent repair. Secondly, it tries to ensure that the customer—the council house tenant—gets from the housing service what he is entitled to receive.

For those functions we have not given the housing management enough priority or resources. Often, the housing management office is one of the less well equipped offices of the local authority and has inadequate staff. The equipment with which it has to work is often not of the most modern type. I suggest that the Minister of State might consider sending out a circular to local authorities urging them to review the resources that are given to their housing management departments to see whether they can be upgraded or improved. The inadequacy of resources can best be seen in the way that the housing manager and his staff have to deal with the flood of daily complaints, problems, demands and requests made to them by the people who are on the housing waiting list. It is here that our thinking has fallen behind the desires and needs of the people during the last 10 years.

The rising standard of living of the vast majority of Scottish people has led them to expect, demand and require a higher standard of living accommodation and surroundings than we have been planning to give them. It is no longer any use for the housing department just to provide any old council houses for Scottish families. It is no longer any use expecting people to accept blindly what they are allocated by an over-pressed and hardworking housing management department. People, rightly, are much more choosy. They expect a council house to be in a good state or repair and they want to enjoy the comfortable surroundings they see their neighbours enjoying, as they are encouraged to do by mass advertising. That is wholly to be welcomed. We must provide adequate resources for council houses in Scotland, because half the population are living in them. We must provide for the people who live in council 58 houses the sort of accommodation they require.

I will give two examples of what I mean. A case was brought to me recently of a gentleman who was a joiner by trade and obviously a competent one. He is not in my constituency, but his case was brought to my attention. He and his wife decided that they would like to make one corner of the kitchen in their council house into a dining area with built-in bench seating. They did this and made a nice job of it. Nobody would deny that the alteration immeasurably improved the appearance of the kitchen and the use of it. Yet, when that man moved out of the house to another area, the local authority insisted on the removal of that installation which the tenant had put in so skilfully, and, what is more, he was required to pay for the cost of the removal.

My second example concerns families who have become split. It is wholly unacceptable that most housing letting rules regard a split family—that is to say, where the husband and wife have separated but are not divorced or are not legally separated—as being one family. That can lead to a most undesirable situation. I have knowledge of a case which is typical of many. A husband and wife with three children separated. The wife had to find somewhere else to live. leaving the husband in the former matrimonial home, a council house. Some months later it came to the wife's notice that the husband was planning to exchange that council house for another in another area. There appears to be no way of preventing him from doing that. The result is that someone from a completely different area will move into what had been the matrimonial home of the family, leaving the wife and children to find other, perhaps sub-tenant, accommodation.

I can see how in the past there was a good argument for saying that central Government must approve all the details but now the new system of housing finance means that the local authority has an absolute, direct and full interest, as do the ratepayers, in the cost of new houses to be built, and housing costs will be put on the housing revenue account. There is a built-in safeguard to stop local authorities from being wasteful in spending money. Every extra pound spent will fall to a considerable extent on the local authority's housing revenue account. The indicative cost system should be eased and modified. It has served a purpose in the past, but the amount of bureaucracy, detailed consideration, and interference with details of local authorities' building plans which it involves, is probably not worth what the system now achieves. Local authorities have to be economically minded in what they spend on housebuilding. The time has come when we must consider what is to happen to the indicative cost procedure.

I was Chairman of the Housing Committee in Edinburgh before my election here. I appreciate the necessity to be brief and I shall, therefore, raise only one major problem, although it is a key JPEG 63 problem, concerning ownership.

The typical Scottish slum is a small one, usually a single end, one room and a kitchen. It is possible to instal a bathroom but only at the cost of reducing space so much that the work is of little or no use. The typical Scottish slum is also to be found in a tenement, which will require serious structural repair if its stability is to be guaranteed for the next 20 years. If the tenement is in a major conurbation it is almost certain that it will be in a square and probably the most deplorable feature is the environment of the back area. Radical treatment is needed if these houses are to get a life of 20 years, and this treatment can be carried out only of there is common ownership. The work cannot be done under the present multi-ownership, with perhaps half a dozen occupiers and a couple of undercaptitalised owners. In advocating common ownership I do not necessarily say that the owner has to be the local authority, but certainly common ownership is required, whether through a housing association or residents' co-operative, yet there is nothing in the Bill to help on this. We are in danger of following a procedure largely developed in the English situation in which the standard slum in a terrace can be treated individually but that is not sufficient for the problems we face in Scotland.

I appreciate that I may be told about the effect of compulsory purchase orders, but it can take at least 18 months to get a compulsory purchase order because in any area of any size which may be involved there are always at least one or two owner-occupiers, a public inquiry is needed, and this takes time. I appreciate that any legislation must try to strike a balance between the rights of the individual and what is the most expeditious way of proceeding, but I cannot see the proposed procedure as safeguarding the rights of the individual in any way. It is questionable whether there was a single case in the past two years in which we sought compulsory purchase and failed to get a slum clearance order. The present procedure does not safeguard the rights of the owner; nor does it help us to tackle the problems. From the first approval of a draft resolution for a housing action area till confirmation of a compulsory purchase order there will be three years. That is a major delay and we must look 64 for improvement in procedure so that action can be taken more quickly to treat slums.

A wide range of minor points also concern me. I am, for instance, concerned that improvement grants will still go only to owners. If the landlord fails to take advantage of an improvement grant the grant should be available to the tenant if he is prepared to carry out the work. I am also deeply disquieted by the proposals to remove security of tenure from tenants in housing action areas. This contrasts sharply with all the rights provided for owner-occupiers. I hope to be able to press home successfully these minor Committee points by twisting the arms of the usual channels.

It was not intended to be permanent. I think that that was the right decision, given the terms of our housing resources. We have made a concession which, we think, will bring into the higher rate of grant all those who at the time of getting local authority approval could reasonably have expected the work to be completed by the initial deadline of 23rd June. I am not saying that there will be no one excluded in the concession which we have made. However, we have tried to deal with as many as possible of what would otherwise be extremely hard cases.

I now turn to the subsidy and the help which will be given to local authorities by an order produced under the terms of 104 the Bill in respect of the loss of rent income arising from the rent freeze. Interestingly enough, as far as I know no local authority has complained to central Government about the 80 per cent. announcement that my right hon. Friend has made. In that instance there is a strong case for saying that there should be substantial contribution from central Government and a certain contribution from the local authorities. After all, many local authorities were asking us to impose a rent freeze, including most of the Scottish Labour authorities. I think that we have the balance just about right, but that is a matter which we can discuss laer. As I say, there will have to be an order produced under the terms of the Bill.

I reassert, as there seems to be some misunderstanding, that the rent standstill applies to private rents as well as local authority rents.

Second, on a point brought to my attention by my hon. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Queen's Park, where applications for increased rents are being made under the fair rent scheme they will not become operative till 1st January 1975. I have taken steps to see that rent registration officers, in giving notices to tenants, make that matter clear in the letters that they send out.

I have dealt with some of the points that have been raised during our debate. I obviously have not answered all the points in what has been a wide-ranging discussion about housing in Scotland. I repeat that the Bill is an important contribution towards our housing policy but is not by itself the total of that policy. We have introduced certain pieces of policy already and more will be coming. The Bill, along with other items of policy, represents a determined attack by the Government on what are still appalling housing conditions in many parts of Scotland.

One of the Government's most important commitments is that we do everything possible on our own initiative, and in consultation and co-operation with local authorities, to reduce and eliminate as far as possible the difficult and damaging housing problems that we still unfortunately face in Scotland.
