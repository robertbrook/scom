PORTHMADOG HARBOUR REVISION ORDER 1974

10.30 a.m.

Mr. Dafydd Wigley
: I beg to move, That the Committee have considered the instrument.

I am grateful to the Leader of the House and to the House itself for facilitating the Committee. I believe that the step was absolutely necessary, not only because a fundamentally bad decision was taken by the previous Government in over-ruling the inspector's report on the revision order, but because this sort of decision should be taken with Parliament's express consent. I do not want to go into the legal considerations of the matter, but rather the parliamentary and political aspects.

Let me say from the outset that it is clear that this is not a party political issue. In the Porthmadog area the issue cuts right across party lines. It unites Tories and Socialists, Nationalists and Liberals; it unites Welsh and non-Welsh speakers.

Furthermore, it is an issue that rings deep in the Welsh political consciousness. The Rebecca riots of the last century spring readily to mind, and the problems of absentee landlords have been central to Welsh rural politics for a century and a half. It was the oppression by the landlords that fed the great radical revival in the nineteenth century. The public democratic control of the undertakings that greatly affect the public has been the political theme of Socialist Wales in this century.

Today I hope that we shall find the same unity of opinion as exists in the Porthmadog area. We have had signatories to an early day motion for the annulment of the order that brought together Labour, Liberal and Plaid Cymru MPs. First, I want to say something about the background of the matter so that hon. Members may appreciate how the present situation has arisen.

4 Prior to the turn of the last century, the area known now as the Llanfrothen Valley and the Glaslyn Valley, on the border of Merioneth and Caernarvon-shire, was partly or totally covered with water. The land was reclaimed for agricultural purposes by a Mr. W. A. Maddocks, who was given title to the land by an Act of Parliament of 1807. The Act provided for the construction of embankments to hold back the sea water, together with sluice gates. It provided, too, that Mr. Maddocks, his heirs and successors, should have title to about 30,000 acres of agricultural land made usable.

The town of Porthmadog grew as a direct consequence of that land reclamation. The major reason for its growth was that in diverting the rivers it was found that a natural harbour had been formed and that it had great economic potential, particularly in handling slate from nearby Ffestiniog. The slate had formerly been brought down river in barges and transferred to ocean-going ships. Now, with the emebankment and harbour, it became possible to run a railway from Ffestiniog to Porthmadog and for the railway to be used for taking slate to the ocean-going ships.

The potential of the development was secured by a further Act of Parliament in 1821. That Act recognised that the newly created harbour did not provide sufficient security and accommodation for the vessels using it. The Act referred to the need:

"to construct and make and maintain the Pier, Quay and other works"

It further stated that this

"would, when approved, be of great benefit to "

those

"trading to and from"

the area and

"of great public utility "

Clause 6 of the 1821 Act provided for the erection and construction necessary for the convenient lading and unlading of ships and associated activities within the purposes of that Act.

The Act provided that Mr. W. A. Maddocks, his heirs and successors, should provide moorings in the harbour and should appoint a harbour master. It JPEG 5 stated specifically as well that Mr. Maddocks was responsible for the maintenance of the harbour, embankment and sluice works. Section 5 stated that neglect of these functions for a period of 12 months would make void the powers conferred by the Act.

The 1821 Act also refers to Cei Newydd, the South Snowdon Wharf, which has recently been sold to a property development company, with considerable local misgiving. By the Act, Mr. Maddocks was entitled to harbour dues, charges and so on and the 1821 Act has not been repealed.

During the nineteenth century, Porthmadog boomed and by the turn of the century steam was replacing sail and the export of slate declined. In fact, it is clear that by 1913 problems were arising concerning the navigation of the estuary, and the local council minutes of 1913 refer to these problems and to the drawing up of a draft revision order. It is clear that by this time the harbour and its maintenance had become a liability and no person would willingly accept the responsibility.

In 1914, a special council meeting discussed the proposed harbour order and pressed for greater public representation on the proposed new harbour trust. The order came into effect in 1915, although there is some dispute about its validity. The order provided for a new Porthmadog Harbour trust which would comprise 16 trustees, including six from the local district council, six to be elected by specific interests and four to be nominated by interests. It provided for the new trust to acquire the harbour and on the acquisition of this harbour the relevant sections of the 1821 Act were to become inoperative. The order also provided that no sale or lease of land or property should be undertaken without first offering the same to the local district council for purchase.

In this way the 1915 order was a progressive step, vesting control in a representative trust with a strong public element and also providing for public acquisition of any parts of the undertaking that might be disposed of. It is in stark contrast to the present order, as I hope to show shortly.

The local council undertook its obligations following the passing of the 1915 order by electing its six representatives on 6 14th March 1916 in accordance with the order. However, the new trust appears never to have come into operation, although this must remain an unsolved mystery. In fact, the present order provides for the repeal of the 1915 order, which implies that those drawing up the present draft order were under the impression that the 1915 order was still valid.

Following the First World War, the harbour decayed still further and the heirs and successors of Mr. W. A. Maddocks, known as the Tremadog Estates, showed virtually no interest in the harbour. From 1915 until 1960, the local residents of Porthmadog and the users of the harbour were allowed unfettered use and enjoyment of its facilities. They may therefore be forgiven for having assumed, on the basis of usage, custom and practice, that the harbour had become part of their inalienable rights.

As I said earlier, the harbour was created by building an embankment and by the control of water through sluice works. The Tremadog Estate, having become utterly dormant as far as the harbour was concerned, remained active in the area through another wing of its activities. The estate owns a tollgate on the embankment along which crosses the A497 trunk road from Menai Bridge to Fishguard. As such, it was therefore natural for the local community to turn to the Tremadog Estate when the non-maintenance of these sluice works threatened to flood the whole of the Llanfrothen and Glaslyn valleys in the 1950s.

In 1953 it was reported to the Gwynedd River Board that the sluice works and embankment were in a deplorable condition, and I quote from the report.


"generally it would appear that the whole of the works inspected have had little or no attention by way of maintenance for many years."

In 1955, the river board finally instructed the Tremadog Estate to carry out the repair works, but the steps taken by the estate were unsatisfactory. Consequently, the river board was forced to take over responsibility for maintenance of the works. Under the corn-mutation of liability for the maintenance of the works, under Section 9 of the 1.and Drainage Act 1930, Tremadog Estate paid some £32,000 to the board. But this was a totaly inadequate sum and the JPEG 7 cost of repair works to date has been over £70,000. All future expenditure on the maintenance of the works will be from public funds.

It was not until about 1960 that the Tremadog Estate started taking any interest in the harbour. From this time onwards, a growth took place in leisure yachting, and the potential of Porthmadog Harbour for this purpose became evident. The trustees began to see the financial potential of the undertaking, and started collecting mooring fees once more. This caused irate local reaction—particularly since the local people had been using the harbour without paying such fees on more than a sporadic basis within the living memory of most people. They flatly refused to pay such charges. The local people were also upset by the way in which new moorings cut across their activities. By 1963 there was open hostility between the various interests and this began to have violent manifestations such as the cutting of moorings and the lifting of anchors.

The situation was aggravated when the estate trustees sold off harbour land and facilities for private speculative housing development, such as happened with Cei Newydd, the South Snowdon Wharf, which was directly referred to in the 1821 Act, and also to Greaves Wharf. These transactions were for direct financial gain and not for purposes remotely connected with the harbour undertakings as intended in the 1821 Act, or the order of 1915.

To add to the public's resentment, the trustees even failed to adhere to a planning permission condition protecting public access to the harbour. When a gate was erected upon what the public regarded as a right of way, with a notice to the effect that there was to be no trespassing, the gate was three times forcibly removed and deposited in the harbour. The third time such action was sufficiently successful as to cause the gate never to be found.

The allegation of past mismanagement of the harbour is indisputable. The buoy system has been described as useless for yachting. The deep water channel changes position, but the positions of buoys have remained unchanged. They have often disappeared in a swell because 8 they have been wrongly connected. Because of the bad placing of buoys, some three or four years ago a boat hit a bank and sank. Fortunately, there were no fatalities. There was no control of power boats in the estuary, overcrowding in the harbour was adversely affecting the mussel industry, and there was a lack of orderly moorings.

Furthermore, the Tremadog Estates specifically neglected the repair and replacement of the dangerous sluice gates and the rotten and broken wooden stanchions supporting the harbour wall. There have been no improvements to harbour facilities, nor the provision of new buoys, and there has been no dredging of the harbour mooring area.

All this may be contrasted with the safe and orderly manner in which the beach and harbour in the Borth-y-gest area have been operated by the Porthmadog Council with full-time employees maintaining public facilities. In contrast, if I may say so in passing, in 1957 the Porthmadog Harbour had one part-time harbour master paid the grand sum of five shillings a week. The council has passed the necessary by-laws in Borth-y-gest to control and regulate the boating. It is ironic that in the draft order the Tremadog Estates sought to extend their area to take over Borth-y-gest.

During the 1960s, the public in the Porthmadog area became aggravated by the mismanagement of the harbour. In 1967 the council was a party to a petition sent to the then Minister of Transport calling for better management of the harbour. In 1968, a body known as the Porthmadog Harbour Users' Association was formed, representing the interests of fishermen, musselmen and ships chandlers as well as local authorities. This body pressurised the council to take out its own revision order in 1968 under the 1964 Harbours Act. It was probably to forestall the local council that the existing trustees of the Tremadog Estates promoted their own revision order.

The Porthmadog Harbour Revision Order of 1971, as drafted, provided for the redefinition and extension of the trust's territorial limits, empowered the trustees to dispose of land and provided for a purely advisory harbour committee —whose advice, it is expressly stated, the trustees could freely ignore. Objections JPEG 9 to the proposed order were made by a number of groups, and public feeling in Porthmadog mounted prior to the inquiry.

Those feelings arose from several considerations: that traditional free access to the harbour would be restricted; that parts of the harbour would be sold off for property speculation; that the fishing and mussel industries would be squeezed out in order to provide for leisure boating; and that prohibitive charges would be introduced for moorings, harbour dues, slipway fees, and so on, which would price out local people in competition with wealthy holidaymakers.

Incidents occurred that bear out this feeling. Boats were cut away from moorings. Moorings themselves were removed. Fencing posts were cut down, and on three occasions heavy steel gates were ripped down and sunk in the harbour. A public meeting in the town attracted more than 2,000 people and when the public inquiry took place, it had to be reaccommodated to allow an average of 150 persons from the general public to attend during its four days of sitting.

In June 1972, the inspector who conducted the inquiry advised that the order be not made. In June 1973, his recommendation was turned down by the then Minister for Transport Industries—to the utter amazement of the people of Porthmadog. Eventually, on 8th February 1974, the day after the General Election had been called, the Minister signed the order.

The basis on which the Minister ostensibly took his decision—and I say " ostensibly " advisedly, as I have already had the opportunity to hear the ex-Minister voice his disaffection with public ownership in another Committee—was a report by the National Ports Council which considered that the trustee's failure in the past gave no indication of their possible future actions; that there was a need for a revision order acceptable to all interests; that it did not really matter whether the harbour should be in public control—despite the fact that public money was sustaining the facilities that enabled the harbour to remain in existence—and that any application by the local council for an order would take a considerable time to come into force.

10 I quote from the document that was published by the Department of the Environment on 23rd June 1973 announcing the Minister's decision in paragraph 19:

"The Minister's decision to make the Order …is based primarily on the clear and urgent need for the Harbour authority to have proper powers of control."

Forgive me, Mr. English, if I stiffle a hollow laugh at that "urgent need ". The inquiry was in 1972. It took nearly two years to get the Minister's signature to the order. I shudder to think of the speed of progress of non-urgent documents through his Department.

Furthermore, despite the National Ports Council's confidence, shared by the Minister, that the Tremadog Estates would function more efficiently in the future, the experience of the past two years has not borne this out. Buoys are still unmaintained; charging remains haphazard; harbour walls and supporting timbers are delapidating. These things could have been seen to without any new order. Their neglect is the symptom of absentee landlordism whose only motivating force is the certainty of quick financial payback. To suggest that a solution has been reached that is acceptable to all interests defies credibility. Only persons totally ignorant of the area, and arrogant in their ignorance, could have made such a statement.

The Porthmadog Council and its successor, the Dwyfor Council, have presented a petition against this order, which is being heard elsewhere. Suffice it to say that the Dwyfor Council, which is already acting as harbour authority for neighbouring Pwllheli, contests the order on two main points: the harbour is of public concern and should be publicly controlled in the interest of its users, and not purely for profit motive; that any land being disposed of by the harbour should first be offered to the local authority, as in fact was provided in the 1915 order.

It also proposes to put forward its own order as soon as the present one has been annulled. A draft order is already available and could be proceeded with immediately. The petition is supported by the Porthmadog Community Council, and, I understand, by the Gwynedd County Council.

JPEG 11 Finally, let me stress that no one disputes the need for a revision order to provide proper management and control. What is in dispute is the correct body in which such powers should be vested. It is a question of whether, in the twentieth century, the ugly face of feudalism should be enshined by parliamentary order. It is a question of whether the most amazing Ministerial decision of all time will be allowed to stand by a Labour Government.

From what I have said, it can be seen that it was Parliament that conferred upon these beneficiaries the rights of levying charges and of managing the harbour. From those rights for almost a century, William Maddocks and his heirs benefited considerably. When the financial benefit dwindled, no consciousness of public responsibility took over. It was the residents of the area who were left to live with a derelict eyesore. Only when the financial benefit re-emerged did the interest of these people rekindle and then propped up by public funds that had paid for the maintenance which the trustees were unable or unwilling to undertake. What Parliament has given, Parliament can also take away.

Let there be no mistake about it. This is an issue between the people and the vested powers of wealth. The question before us is whether Parliament is on the side of the people. It is Parliament's duty to find a way of annulling this loathesome, obnoxious and reactionary order. If it does not, it will gain the people's contempt, and the people will then know that their democratic voices count for nothing. The real danger is that they will take the power into their own hands.

Gentleman indicated, the general interest stresses that there are important principles at stake in a decision over the future control of the harbour.

Porthmadog is one of a number of small estuarial harbours along the Cardigan Bay coast. I am more knowledgeable about the harbours in my own constituency and I should therefore like to refer briefly to the position of Barmouth harbour which has been under the control of the local authority since the Barmouth Urban District Council Act of 1921 empowered the council to

"make and maintain a sea wall, promenade and road",

and to take unto itself all powers of the harbour trust. They were vested in Barmouth UDC on 13th April 1930.

Since then the harbour has been effectively maintained by the local authority and has now been taken over by its successor, the Merioneth District Council. The council of Barmouth, in my view and in the view of evidence presented to the inquiry in Porthmadog on the draft order, has been able to maintain an efficient and clean harbour. That is in contrast to the situation in Porthmadog. The navigational buoys at Barmouth are maintained in precisely their correct position and incidents of fishing boats or pleasure craft running aground due to faulty buoy placing are minimal in Barmouth. Indeed, Barmouth Harbour is now one of the largest fishing ports on the whole of the North Wales coast and, in contrast with the position at Porthmadog, there is no conflict in Barmouth between the interests of fishing from the harbour and the interests of the 150 16 pleasure craft that are moored on the estuary.

It is my considered view that it is high time that Porthmadog harbour was run in a similar fashion to Barmouth and other harbours along the coast as a municipal undertaking. I believe that a publicly accountable form of management will be far more able to resolve the serious conflicts of interests in the use of Porthmadog Harbour. That is something with which I should like to deal.

The general conflict at present in the use of the harbour is not confined to Porthmadog. It is a conflict repeated in most tourist areas, both within and outside national parks. This conflict takes place between local interests and needs and outside control. It also takes place between individual potential uses of the harbour.

I begin by mentioning the traditional industry in the harbour, the " y cregyn dnon", the Porthmadog mussel industry. The yacht moorings, according to my constituent, were a significant factor in the depletion of the mussel beds, because the beds had been depleted out of all recognition, that chain moorings.

This traditional mussel industry, which has been a feature of Porthmadog, is being threatened with extinction because the conflict of interests between yachting and the traditional industry has not been resolved. The yachting facilities are a recent development at Porthmadog and although these moorings are regularly used by a high proportion of visitors, there are a few local users, too.

Clearly, there is room in Porthmadog Harbour in all senses of that expression, for yachting and fishing to co-exist, as happens in Barmouth. Finally, I should like to mention one possible future use of this harbour which cannot have escaped the notice of the promoters of the order. I am referring to the potential for oil and natural gas development in the Celtic Sea. There has already been offshore drilling in Cardigan Bay for natural gas.
