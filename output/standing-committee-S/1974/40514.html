The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. William Ross): I beg to move,

That the Chairman do report to the House that the Committee have considered the Housing (Scotland) Bill in relation to the principle of the Bill.

The last time I presented a Bill on this Image Col. 7 subject to this Committee was in March of 1969. On that occasion I stressed that the problem of older housing in Scotland was complex and that the solutions attempted by various Governments had produced imbalances between the private and public housing sectors; between the rate of new building and the rate of slum clearance in some local authority areas; and between the proportions of new and old houses in the country. I believe that the 1969 Act went some way to correcting these imbalances.

The proposals included by my predecessor in the Housing and Planning Bill which was introduced in February of this year demonstrate that he and his hon. Friends recognised this. They were based on the foundations of the 1969 Act. The basic consensus between both sides of the Committee on this issue is demonstrated by the fact that I am now reintroducing most of these provisions although we have made important changes and additions.

Our first priority, apart from new buildings, must be to improve the living conditions of those tens of thousands of families who are still forced to live in houses with no inside wc, no hot water and no bath. The effect on children and young parents of living in such conditions, often in houses too small for the size of family, and with too often a filthy and depressing environment, does incalulable damage to society as a whole. The Bill will, I believe, help local authorities to clear slums quickly and ensure an immediate improvement to unsatisfactory houses. If a house has got to remain in use, even for a few years, it may not be a waste of money to ensure that it has the basic amenities.

I think it would be helpful for the Committee if I first identified the chances which we have made to the provisions included in the Housing and Planning Bill which was introduced by the previous administration and had a Second Reading. I will then go on to describe how the provisions of this Bill differ from the present legislation.

The first change we have made is to have a separate Scottish Bill. The Scottish proposals in the Housing and Planning Bill were in 11 clauses in the middle of a Great Britain Bill, with other new clauses hidden in an amending schedule Col. 8 and mass of further amendments in other schedules all very difficult to follow and inconvenient for those who have to administer legislation.

Secondly, we have raised the tolerable standard—in Clause 14—so that a house which does not have exclusive use of a wc suitably located within the house itself will fail the standard and be subject to local authority action.

Thirdly, to meet a request from Glasgow Corporation we have altered the criteria by which housing action areas for improvement can be declared—Clauses 16 and 17. Houses which lack standard amenities can come into the reckoning as well as those which fail the tolerable standard. This will bring some 45,000 additional houses into the field for area improvemnt.

Fourthly, we are empowering—Clause 11(5)—local authorities to pay improvement grant in housing action areas at up to 90 per cent., instead of the normal 75 per cent., in cases of special financial hardship.

Fifthly, conditions attached to improvement grants will apply for a period of five years instead of three, and authorities will be able to attach the occupancy condition to owner-occupiers as well as to landlords. They will have a discretion not to recover grant from owner-occupiers when the condition has been breached if they are persuaded that recovery would be unreasonable. For example, somebody may have to change his job and sell a house which has been improved.

Sixthly, we are extending—Clause 44 —local authorities' power to lend money to enable people to buy, to construct or to improve houses. In using their present powers they are particularly encouraged to act as lenders of last resort: they lend for example to people who have attempted, but failed, to obtain a building society mortgage, or for conversion works to sub-standard houses which building societies are reluctant to support.

Clause 44 extends their powers in three minor but important respects. First, they will be able to lend money to repay an existing mortgage: in the case, for example, where an owner-occupier wishes to borrow money to improve his house but is unable to persuade the original lender to make a further advance. Secondly, they will be able to lend money in relation to premises which are not Image Col. 9 entirely residential; for example, for a house which has a small shop attached, always provided that the primary aim is the provision of housing accommodation. Thirdly, they will be able to grant low start mortgages under which the borrower repays less in the early years of the mort- gage and more later, and maturity mortgages where the principal is paid in one lump sum on the expiry of the loan.

The total amount of lending by Scottish local authorities for house purchase, some £7 million a year, may be small in relation to the sums lent by the building societies, but its real value is that in many cases a local authority loan provides the only means of meeting the aspirations of some intending house purchasers. I should like to see local authorities making as great a use as possible of their existing powers to lend money for this purpose and of the extended powers with which we are now seeking to provide them.

Seventhly, we are providing—Clause 45 —for a new subsidy, the rent income subsidy. There can be no doubt that the large rent increases under the Conserva- tives—Just under £50 during their last two years in office—contributed to inflationary pressures. Rents were allowed to rise while wages were held in check. Within days of taking office the present Government took action to freeze rents for the rest of the year. while we make a fresh assessment of housing finance. Under the 1972 Act arrangements local ratepayers would have borne most of the cost of the rent freeze. We recognise the unfairness of this, and by the new rent income subsidy we shall meet the major part of the loss arising from the standstill on council house rents, thereby safeguarding the interests of ratepayers. Details of the subsidy will be a matter for regulations in due course, but, in general, local authorities may expect to receive subsidy amounting to at least 80 per cent. of the lost rent income which is attributable to the standstill.

Part I, Clauses 1–12, contains revised provisions for a house improvement grant scheme. In recent years the improvement grant scheme has certainly been popular. In 1969 fewer than 4,000 grants were paid by local authorities to private owners. Last year the total was more than 22,000. Much of this increase was certainly due to the higher grants introduced for a limited period by the previous adminis- Col. 10 tration, and I pay tribute to the Conservative Party for that stimulus to improvement work.

At the same time, I think it would admit that the handout has been indiscriminate. We know that a number of these grants have gone to houses which were already perfectly satisfactory—for example, to put in a second bathroom, or to holiday houses. We also know that some of the money has gone to property developers for conversion work which was profitable enough without the grant. We cannot allow this when there are still some 180,000 houses in the private sector below the present tolerable standard. The main purpose of the changes to the improvement grant scheme is, therefore, to make the scheme more selective and to ensure that assistance is concentrated on the houses most in need of improvement.

We have also simplified the grant scheme considerably, by integrating the discretionary and standard grants while retaining owners' right to a grant for the installation of missing basic amenities and have made it more generous towards works of repair.

10.45 a.m.

Part II—Clause 13 to 35—contains the most important provisions in the Bill. They provide for the designation of areas as housing action areas, which are a logical evolution of the housing treatment areas established in our 1969 Act. I think I can best explain what we are trying to achieve by discussing four defects which experience has shown in the present procedure. I assume that hon. Members will be aware of the basic outlines of the housing treatment area procedure. If they are not I commend the 1969 Act to them.

The first defect of the housing area for improvement procedure is that it offers no specific incentives to owners or local authorities to take action. There is nothing to induce local authorities to concentrate on the worst areas.

In the housing action area for improvement there will be a number of incentives. Anyone who has to carry out works to bring his house up to the required standard will have a right to grant at 75 per cent. towards that work—the rate elsewhere will be 50 per cent.—and, as I have said, in cases of special hardship at up to 90 per cent. Local authorities will receive Exchequer contribution at 90 per cent. towards their expenditure on grants Image Col. 11 to private owners in housing action areas, compared with 75 per cent. towards expenditure on grants outside them and they will get an Exchequer contribution of 75 per cent. for work on houses which they own in such areas compared with 37½ per cent. for work on houses elsewhere.

There will be a special grant in housing action areas for repairs even where no other improvement work is needed. This will be available, at the discretion of the local authority, at 75 per cent. towards up to £800 worth of work. An owner of limited means whose house was otherwise up to standard could therefore receive up to £600 towards essential repairs.

Finally, in an action area, if an owner cannot afford to meet that part of the cost of works of improvement or repair which is not covered by grant, the local authority will be obliged, under Clause 24, to offer him a loan of the balance.

The second defect is that the present procedure takes no account of the view of the people in the area. They do not have to be consulted about whether an area should be improved or demolished, and in many cases the first they hear about the housing treatment area is when they receive notice of a compulsory purchase order for their house from the local authority. Naturally, people resent this and tend to object bitterly to compulsory purchase orders. This can lead to long delays which impede progress in this desirable work.

Under the new procedure a local authority is obliged to publish its proposals; serve notice of them on all the people affected; and to have regard, before making a final decision, to any representations received from the people in the area. They will have two months from the date of notice being given within which to make these representations.

This is a real attempt to improve people in planning of the area in which they live.

Mr. George Lawson: When a local authority hears representations on a matter of this sort is it not the case that the very committee that makes a decision to do something is the committee which hears representations and that it is finally this committee which decides whether to accept or to throw out those representa- Col. 12 tions? Is it not worth while looking to see whether it is right that a local authority committee should be judge, jury, and virtually policeman on its own behalf?

Mr. Ross: We would want to try to instill a spirit of greater co-operation and appreciation of one another's difficulties among those authorities and individuals who are involved. I take my hon. Friend's point, but it must be remembered that proposals are submitted to the Department, and, therefore, we can see whether fairness and justice are being done. We should not start on the basis that there is obviously antagonism but rather that the local authority is striving to find a way to get over the difficulties so that desirable improvements can be made as quickly as possible. But what my hon. Friend has mentioned is a valuable point. I hope that the result will be fewer disputes between us individuals and local authorities if there is consultation before final decisions are taken; and, indeed, I think that that outcome is likely.

I hope that local authorities will take advantage of this procedure and I shall be issuing guidance to them in due course on the best way of operating it.

The third defect is that there are certain situations in which landlords who are willing to comply with the local authority's wishes can be obstructed by their tenants. The sort of situation I have in mind is where a landlord is being obliged to improve a house in a housing treatment area, and is willing to do so, but the works cannot be carried out satisfactorily with the tenant in the house, and the tenant refuses to vacate the house temporarily. In this situation the landlord is liable to be faced with a compulsory purchase order, because the obligation is on him, if he does not carry out the necessary works, but there is no specific provision enabling him to cope with the tenant's objections.

Under the new provisions in Part III of the Bill—clauses 36 to 41—the landlord will be able to bring this type of problem before the sheriff, who will be able to grant the landlord an order for possession, or, as the case may be, to require the tenant to vacate the house temporarily to enable works to be carried out. The Sheriff will have full discretion to attach such conditions as he wishes to Image Col. 13 the granting of an order. If the sheriff considers that the tenant has a reasonable case for staying in the house it will be for the landlord to settle the issue with the local authority, if necessary at a compulsory purchase order inquiry. The landlord and tenant provisions are needed to prevent unnecessary obstruction of improvement and demolition work, but the final decision in all cases rests with the sheriff, so the rights of tenants are safeguarded.

The fourth defect arises in areas for improvement. The maximum standard to which the local authority can compel improvement is the tolerable standard. Local authorities have taken the view that it is scarcely worth embarking on a housing treatment area for improvement if they cannot even ensure that at the end of the day every house will have a bath. I sympathise with this view.

Under the new procedure the local authority will be able to require, in the case of houses with a life of 15 years or more, that the house is equipped with all the standard amenities, meets the tolerable standard and is in a good state of repair. We do not think that the local authority should be able to compel this higher standard in all cases. Where it considers that houses have a life of less than 15 years it need only secure that they are brought up to the tolerable standard and into a good state of repair.

Other provisions are designed to ensure that no one suffers as a result of improvement or demolition work undertaken in a housing action area. The local authority will be obliged to rehouse anyone who is displaced as a result of such action and who cannot find suitable alternative accommodation. Anyone who is displaced will qualify for a home loss payment and a disturbance payment in the same way as if his house had been compulsorily purchased. No one should end up without a home as a result of improvement or demolition in a housing action area. The compensation arrangements which exist for houses in housing treatment areas will apply to housing in housing action areas, and the blight provisions of the Land Compensation (Scotland) Act 1973 will apply where there is any demolition.

I think hon. Members will appreciate the difficulties of devising a procedure which gives adequate powers to local authorities while safeguarding the rights Col. 14 of the individual, which offers the chance for swift action while allowing time for public consultation, and which gives sufficient financial incentives without throwing money away. I think we have here the right mixture.

Apart from Clauses 44 and 45 dealing with local authority lending and rent income subsidy, Part IV contains miscellaneous and formal provisions. One merits special mention. We are empowering, under Clause 43, local authorities to act as the agents of private owners in arranging for the execution of house improvements. This will be by agreement with and at the expense of the owner.

These are the major points in the Bill. Scotland's sub-standard houses cannot be removed by legislation alone. It is the Government's duty to provide the right legislative framework, to offer the necessary advice and encouragement to local authorities, and to provide the necessary financial support. In this Bill we are giving local authorities extended powers, improved procedures and financial encouragement to stop the slide into slumdom of a substantial number of the country's houses. Many of the proposals have been specially requested by local authorities in the light of their experience with the present improvement grant scheme and housing treatment area procedure. They were prepared in consultation with a working party of representatives of the local authorities associations and I am sure the Committee will join me in thanking them for giving us the benefit of their experience. This Bill will be welcomed by local authorities and by the families living in miserable conditions. I feel sure that they would join me in urging this Committee and the Standing Committee to deal with it as swiftly as proper consideration will allow.
