legislation DRAFT NATIONAL RADIOLOGICAL PROTECTION BOARD (EXTENSION OF FUNCTIONS) ORDER 1974

10.30 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for Health and Social Security (Dr. David Owen): I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft instrument.

This is a simple order. The National Radiological Protection Board was set up under the Radiological Protection Act 1970 to undertake functions concerning the protection of mankind from the hazards of ionizing radiations. At the time it was considered that it might be necessary to extend the remit of the board to cover the potential hazards from nonionizing electro-magnetic radiations such as microwaves and lasers. Accordingly, Section l(16)(a) of the Act gave the Secretary of State the power, after consultation with the Atomic Energy Authority and the Medical Research Council, to make an order—subject to the affirmative resolution procedure—to extend the terms of reference of the board and of the Statutory Advisory Committee appointed under Section 4 of the Act to cover the dangers arising from the use of non-ionizing electro-magnetic radiations.

In view of the increasing use of lasers and microwaves, and other forms of non-ionizing electro-magnetic radiations, and of the national and international moves to ensure their safe use, the time has come to extend the board's terms of reference to cover this group of radiations. The order is limited only to this widening of the board's remit.

Mr. Timothy Raison: We on this side of the Committee are satisfied that this order is necessary. I have read some lurid accounts of the kind of things that may arise from this form of radiation and it Col. 4 is entirely appropriate that the order should be made.

Question put and agreed to.

Resolved,

That the Committee have considered the draft instrument.

legislation DRAFT SCOTTISH SPECIAL HOUSING ASSOCIATION (LIMIT OF ADVANCES) (SCOTLAND) ORDER 1974

10.34 a.m.

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Mr. Bruce Millan): I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft instrument.

The purpose of this order is straightforward. It is to increase to £290 million the limit of the aggregate amount of advances which may be made by the Secretary of State out of the National Loans Fund to the SSHA to finance its operations in providing houses for rental.

The statutory limit on these advances was first set in 1955 at £75 million and it has been raised from time to time by subsequent legislation. In terms of the Housing (Amendment) (Scotland) Act 1970 it now stands at £230 million with provision for increase to £290 million by order of the Secretary of State.

The total of advances made to the SSHA at the end of the 1973–74 financial year was £199 million, since when further advances of £4 million have been made. The association also has firm building commitments, against which no advances have yet been made, amounting to £22 million. In addition, it has a future programme of new building and improvements authorised in principle, for which detailed costs are not yet approved, which will amount to well over £150 million at today's prices. It is clear therefore, that the present limit of £230 million will be reached by about the middle of 1975, and it seems prudent to make an early approach to the House for an increase in the authorised limits.

The activities of the Scottish Special Housing Association are well known to members—at least to the Scottish members—of this Committee. It has a very wide remit. Rather than attempt at this point to deal with that in any detail, I Image Col. 5 prefer to make one or two brief observations about what the association is doing. If hon. Members have particular points of information or argument to put to me about the SSHA, naturally, I shall be glad to try to catch the eye of the Chair again to answer them.

The association has already provided about 80,000 houses throughout Scotland, and it has thus made a major contribution to meeting public sector housing needs. It is now heavily engaged in providing essential infrastructure for the areas of Scotland where economic expansion is taking place. Since 1966 the role of the SSHA has basically been to provide in areas of economic expansion for incoming workers and also for Glasgow overspill. It has a programme at present of some 17,000 houses for completion over the next four or five years, and 4.600 of these are to be built in the areas of oil development around Aberdeen, the Moray Firth and in Shetland. These houses are intended primarily to meet the needs of incoming industrial workers in areas where economic expansion is stretching the resources of local authorities or where special overspill agreements have been concluded.

It is my impression in visiting those areas that the help which the local authorities obtain from the SSHA in this respect is very welcome. Indeed, the association's building programme is vital to the development of those areas, to bringing labour into the areas, and to seeing it satisfactorily housed. We have told the local authorities in these areas that we shall give them every assistance in providing the houses and other infrastructure developments which are needed to provide for oil development or economic expansion. We and our predecessors have therefore authorised a considerable programme for the SSHA.

That is only one important part of the work which the SSHA is doing at present. It is for that reason that this additional money is required now, and I am sure that the Committee will be happy to allow this money to be made available to the SSHA in view of its record over the years since its inception. I shall be glad to take up any detailed points about the Association's activities when I wind up later.

So far as balancing the books is concerned, from the SSHA point of view that is not a particular difficulty because the books are balanced in relation to rents by the Government contribution. In a sense, this answers the points raised by hon. Members on both sides about the level of SSHA rents. So long as we have a system under which the deficits are borne by the Government—to the very considerable advantage, of course, of the local authorities in the areas concerned, as they then do not have to make any rate contribution towards the deficit—I think that the Government must Image Col. 19 have an interest in the level of SSHA rents.

If one has an association which builds houses all over Scotland and has a rent policy which is basically a Scottish one, then in particular areas the rents charged are likely to be out of step with those charged by local authorities. I also think it is human nature that it is only in those areas where SSHA rents are higher than local authority rents that Members of Parliament and Ministers are likely to hear about it. Taking Scotland as a whole, however, the latest published figures in the annual White Paper in May of this year for rents at November 1973 show that on average SSHA rents were not markedly out of step at that time. Admittedly they were rather higher, but on the other hand probably the gross annual value of the houses was also higher than the average for local authority houses, no doubt arising from the fact that the SSHA started later than local authorities in building houses to rent.

Although it is true that the SSHA houses rents are higher, they are not a great deal higher, as some of the tenants, unfortunately, often believe. That does not mean to say that in particular areas there are not disparities which naturally cause the tenants concern. But, given the present situation and the financial set-up, I see great difficulty in reaching a conclusion which would produce comparability between local authorities and SSHA.

It would be against the philosophy of the Government who wish to restore as soon as possible freedom to local authorities to determine their own rents if at the same time we were to look for exact comparability with SSHA rents which are to a considerable extent the concern of the Government and which are influenced by Government policies.
