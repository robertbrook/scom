Mr. Teddy Taylor
: On a point of order, Sir Myer. One of the difficulties of being Chairman of a Committee is that one gets plagued with points of order which are not always relevant. I should like to put to you four specific points of order which are important and relevant and which affect the whole constitution of the Committee.

The first one arises out of Standing Order No. 62, which reads:

"In nominating a Committee the members of the Committee of Selection shall have regard to the composition of the House."

I suggest that there may be a case for the Committee not sitting at all because it does not represent the composition of the House.

As there has been an increase in the number of Liberal and Scottish Nationalist Members of Parliament, my understanding is that at least one of the members of each of those minority parties should be taking part in the proceedings of the Committee. I understand that this matter was considered by the Committee of Selection. To my utter astonishment, I heard through the usual channels that, although a place on the Committee was offered to the Scottish Nationalists and to the Scottish Liberals, both parties declined to take part in the proceedings of the Committee. This is an utterly unprecedented situation. I have never before known an occasion on which a party has been unwilling to take its share of what we know as the donkey work of the House of Commons. We appreciate that the Committee proceedings may be lengthy and complicated, but it is utterly unprecedented that parties represented in the House of Commons should be apparently unwilling to do their share of the work.

For the first time in my 10 years in the House of Commons we have a Committee which does not reflect the composition of the House, and this raises an important question of principle. I ask you, Sir 4 Myer, what protection the major parties have against action of this sort by minority parties.

My second point of order is that one consequence of the Scottish Nationalists and Liberals refusing to play their part in the Committee is that the Opposition have had to provide eight Members to serve on the Committee, which is about 'half our total parliamentary strength. In addition, my hon. Friend the 'Member for Edinburgh, Pentlands (Mr. Rifkind) has to serve not only on this Committee but also on Standing Committee C. An unreasonable burden is put on members of the majority parties if they have to fill up places simply because minority parties are not prepared to play their part.

I 'have studied the composition of all the Standing Committees which are sitting today. They are the Standing Committees on the Rehabilitation of Offenders Bill, the Trade Union and Labour Relations Bill, the Finance Bill, the Housing Bill and the Housing (Scotland) Bill. Although, if all the Scottish Nationalists and,Liberal Members had been busy serving on other Standing Committees there might have been a case for making a special arrangement, we find that not one Scottish Liberal is serving on a Committee and only one out of the seven Scottish Nationalists, namely, the hon. Member for Aberdeen, East (Mr. Henderson), is serving on the Trade Union and Labour Relations Bill. We are, therefore, being forced to do more of the donkey work simply because the Scottish Nationalist and Scottish Liberal Members are not paying their full part in the work of the House of Commons.

My third point of order is that I believe there is justification for you, Sir Myer, as Chairman of the Committee—I hope with the support of all members of the Committee—to make a report to the Services Committee of the House of Commons suggesting a reduction in the salaries of the Scottish Nationalist and Scottish Liberal Members of Parliament if they are not prepared to do the full work involved in serving in the House of Commons.

We know that serving on Committees, which involves detailed deliberations, long sittings and getting up early in the morning, is the main part of our work. It is not glamorous. We shall not get newspaper headlines because of the work we JPEG 5 do here. On the other hand, it is utterly irresponsible and lets down the people of Scotland when two parties, which claim that Scotland is their main interest, are not prepared to serve on the Standing Committee on the Housing (Scotland) Bill.

The Bill is concerned with trying to alleviate the problem of slums, improving old tenement properties and setting up housing action areas. The Bill is important and will help the people who live in poor housing conditions in Scotland. I therefore feel that you, Sir Myer, as Chairman, with the support of all members, should make a report to the Services Committee suggesting that the salaries of Scottish Nationalists and Scottish Liberal Members of Parliament should be reduced until such time as they are prepared to play their full part in the work of the House of Commons.

My fourth point of order concerns precisely what action we can take as a Committee to protest against the blatant hypocrisy of members of the Scottish Liberal and Scottish National Parties. Last year there was a sit-in by members of the Liberal Party when we discussed the Local Government (Scotland) Bill. We had to explain that, because of the rules of the House of Commons, they could not be guaranteed a place on the Committee. Since then the Liberals and Scottish Nationalists have had electoral success. They have won more seats and they are entitled to a seat on the Committee. On the Second Reading of the Bill there was a demonstration by the Scottish Nationalists objecting to an English Member of Parliament being present. Yet on this major Scottish Bill not one of the seven Scottish Nationalists and not one of the Scottish Liberal Members of Parliament is willing to do his share of the donkey work.

We have some parliamentary layabouts who are not doing their job. It is utterly wrong, and something should be done about it. We as a Committee should protest but, more important, I suggest that you, Sir Myer, as Chairman of the Committee, responsible to the Speaker of the House of Commons for the management of the affairs of the Committee, should do something about it. It is utterly wrong and shameful that these two parties, which allegedly 6 represent the interests of Scotland, are not prepared to do a share of the work.

I apologise to you, Sir Myer, and to the Minister for taking up valuable time. We want to get ahead and discuss Scottish housing, but I feel that this matter is so important that I should put it to you and ask you to give it serious consideration.

Clause 2
GENERAL PROVISIONS RELATING TO IMPROVEMENT GRANTS

Mr. Taylor
: I beg to move Amendment No. 1, in page 3, line 7, at end add

'(5) It shall further be the duty of local authorities to process and come to a decision on such applications within a reasonable period of time'.

This is an important amendment. Clause 2 sets out the general provisions relating to improvement grants. In recent years, largely because of the work of the last Government, there has been a dramatic increase in the number of improvement grants offered to the people of Scotland, and this has made a considerable contribution towards improvement of our housing stock. But there has been a definite difference in the attitudes which local authorities have taken to improvement grants. Some have offered grants to most people who have applied for them, where there were the appropriate qualifications, while others have had a different attitude. There have also been variations in the manner in which the applications have been processed.

In some cases, because of the ease of administration there has been considerable speed in arriving at decisions on improvement grants, but in other instances JPEG 9 there have been long delays, sometimes because of staffing shortages and sometimes simply because of slow administration. It is a little unfair that the speed with which an improvement grant application is approved depends largely on the local authority area in which the applicant resides.

Bearing in mind that these grants are going ahead, admittedly after 23rd June at a lower level in some areas of Scotland, it is important that we should place a simple duty on local authorities to process and give decisions on such applications "within a reasonable period". It may be alleged that these words are not necessary and that they would not impose any specific obligation on an authority. What is "reasonable"? We know from experience in the House of Commons that the word "reasonable" is in many Acts of Parliament and lays an obligation of a general sort on a local authority.

I have received from people who have written to me indications of considerable delays. In recent weeks, but more particularly in recent months, there has been a rush to get improvement grants approved and work completed by 23rd June, the cut-off date for the 75 per cent. grant, and the public have become more acutely aware of the importance of a speedy decision upon their applications. Some applications are simple, while some are complex and have to take longer than others to be considered, but it would be fair and reasonable to put an obligation such as we suggest on local authorities. It may be contended by the Minister that there is no need for it because local authorities act reasonably and give decisions within a reasonable period. But if that is the case, no damage would accrue to local authorities or to people in Scotland by our amendment. I hope that the Minister can agree to it, but if he cannot, I hope he can give an assurance that steps will be taken by him and by the Scottish Development Department to ensure more uniformity of practice and more speed in coming to conclusions on applications.

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Mr. Bruce Millan)
: I agree with the intention of the amendment that local authorities should make decisions on applications as quickly as possible, but I doubt whether the amendment would 10 add anything of value to the Bill from that point of view.

There is a lack of definition in the phrase

"within a reasonable period of time."

I am not sure that in practice one would be able to determine in any particular case, with any certainty which would help the applicant, whether the application had been dealt with within a reasonable period of time. If we were to admit such a defect and put into the Bill something more specific, there would be two difficulties. There would, first, be difficulty in determining the precise period which we should insert in the Bill; secondly, there would be a danger, since we are dealing with what is largely a discretionary system, that, in particular circumstances, by imposing an obligation on the local authority, of the net result being that the local authority might use its discretion not to approve an application whereas in other circumstances, given more time, it would have approved it.

The amendment would not basically add anything to the Bill and might do more harm than good. Generally speaking, we are not anxious to impose additional obligations on local authorities, particularly in discretionary areas in which we have to rely in other respects on their good judgment. I ask the hon. Gentleman not to press the amendment.

However, we shall encourage local authorities to approve applications within a reasonable period of time. If there is evidence from any area that an authority is not doing so, I will be happy to take it up with the authority. Apart from standard grants and grants in housing action areas, we are dealing with a discretionary system, involving a discretion which rests with the local authorities.

Mr. Taylor
: I thank the Minister for what he has said, particularly for his assurance that if we have evidence of unreasonable variations in the way in which applications are dealt with, he will take the matter up with the local authorities. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 2 ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Clause 3
CONDITIONS FOR APPROVAL OF APPLICATIONS FOR IMPROVEMENT GRANT OTHER THAN APPLICATIONS RELATING EXCLUSIVELY TO THE PROVISION OF STANDARD AMENITIES

Mr. Taylor
: I beg to move Amendment No. 2, in page 3, line 12, leave out 'sufficient' and insert:

'appropriate in relation to their general responsibility to residents and ratepayers in the area'.

The amendment deals with an important matter. The Minister of State said that the improvement grant position is an entirely discretionary arrangement for local authorities, apart from some provisions for standard grants, but it is a little unfair, when we are passing a new law and making millions of pounds available so that people can imrove their homes, that there should be such wide variation in practice between authorities.

Some local authorities have recently taken decisions virtually to put a ban on improvement applications, while others are being more flexible and reasonable in granting them. Clause 3(1) states:

"A local authorty may in any case, other than in respect of an appliction for an improvement grant to which section 7 of this Act applies, refuse to approve an application for an improvement grant on any grounds that seem to them sufficient."

Some people consider that it is important to maintain this position because it is one of the real discretionary powers of local authorities and it would be unfair to fetter it, but it is a little unfair that success in obtaining an improvement grant should depend on the area in which the applicant resides. Therefore, instead of leaving a total blanket flexibility for the local authority to refuse or grant an application, we should include the words:

"on i any grounds that seem to them appropriate n relation to their general responsibility to residents and ratepayers in the area."

This would do two things. It would, first, reduce to some degree the total freedom of local authorities to reject improvement grants on grounds which were not sufficient, bearing in mind the housing needs of the community as a whole; secondly, it would prevent any question of discrimination against particular areas. It has been suggested by 12 some people—I am not sure whether it is true—that some local authorities provide grants in some parts of their areas while they are not so agreeable to giving grants in other parts. The amendment would put an obligation on local authorities to use their powers in such a way as not to discriminate against particular areas or people.

I drafted the words of the amendment myself, and I appreciate that they may not be totally appropriate legally. But it is important that we should put a general restriction of this sort upon the absolute freedom of local authorities to refuse grants on any ground which appear to them to be sufficient. The grounds should at least be appropriate in relation to the general responsibility of members of local authorities as elected councillors.

Mr. Millan
: We have here a similar difficulty to that in the earlier amendment, for we are dealing with what is basically a discretionary system. We have to make up our minds whether we wish local authorities to exercise discretion. Later in the clause, we lay down certain conditions in which a local authority will not grant an improvement grant. Those conditions are reasonable because they are directed towards avoiding what could be abuses of the system.

But when it comes to other cases, if we give discretion to local authorities we have largely to do just that, and there is a limit to the extent to which the Secretary of State can then interfere in legislation or otherwise in limiting that discretion. Apart from that, the amendment is deficient, because all it does is take out the word "sufficient" in line 12 and put in other words which it is not clear to me would substantially alter the meaning of the clause. But the important thing is the discretion given, as I understand it as a layman, in line 11 in the words " … that seem to them …" That is what imports into the subsection the real discretion to the local authority. If we change the word "sufficient" and put in other words, we are still left with the words " … that seem to them …" and those words would seem to give the local authority considerable discretion.

I do not think that the wording proposed would substantially change the meaning of the clause. If there was strong JPEG 13 feeling that we should attempt to circumscribe the discretion of local authorities in any way—and I am not convinced that we should—I should be willing to consider other wording. But on the whole we have to allow local authorities discretion in this matter. Many local authorities would be extremely aggrieved if we were to limit their discretion in this or in any other way which might be suggested, and I cannot recommend the Committee to accept the amendment.

Mr. Taylor
: The Minister has been understanding and I am grateful for the information he has given. If we have evidence of local authorities appearing to discriminate against particular areas in the giving of improvement grants, will he take the matter up?

Mr. Millan
: I would not want to give an absolute commitment on that because I would want to know the particular kind of discrimination. It might be a discrimination which, in the circumstances of the authority's case, is perfectly reasonable. The authority may decide, given its other financial commitments, that it wants to give priority to particular types of improvement rather than others, and to concentrate, for example, on areas of particularly bad housing rather than on those in which there are basically reasonable standards of housing. But if I had evidence that authorities were behaving in a way which suggested that we ought to circumscribe their discretion in some way, I would certainly consider it, although the wording involved would probably be very difficult to draft, and I doubt whether it could be done successfully. If evidence is brought to me between now and the Report stage, I will consider it.

Mr. Taylor
: I am grateful to the Minister. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 9
CONDITIONS TO BE OBSERVED WITH RESPECT TO HOUSES IN RESPECT OF WHICH AN IMPROVEMENT GRANT HAS BEEN MADE, AND REGISTRATION THEREOF.

Mr. Taylor
: I beg to move Amendment No. 7, in page 8, line 42, to leave out subsection (3).

I hope the Minister will give serious consideration to this Amendment because the matter to which it relates has caused a great deal of worry. It relates to the conditions to be reserved in respect of houses to which an improvement grant has been awarded. Subsection (3) states:

"it may be a condition of the grant that the house at all times at which it is not occupied by the applicant or by a member of his family will be let or kept available for letting."

I can envisage circumstances in which a person will have to leave his house for a period of time after an improvement grant has been given. We appreciate that later in the Bill there is a let-out clause.
