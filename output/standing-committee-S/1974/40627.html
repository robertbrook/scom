    • Mr. Russell Fairgrieve
    : It is with mixed feelings that I open this first Scottish Estimates debate of this Parliament on electricity prices. I have a sense of pleasure mixed with humility that my colleagues have seen fit to ask me to open this debate from the Front Bench so early in my career in Parliament.

    I should like to discuss electricity prices in a more general way and I shall try not to be too fractious politically, though I have no doubt that some of the things 1 may say may not find absolute agreement on the benches opposite or even from some hon. Members on this side of the Committee. I shall try to be constructive rather than destructive, because I have spent over 20 years in competitive manufacturing industry, competitive both within this country and without. I shall look not just at electricity prices today but at electricity prices yesterday, today and tomorrow.

    We know that this industry was nationalised in 1948, and since that date it has been the duty of both parties to make it work efficiently. If there have been faults of omission or commission there have been faults on both sides. Both parties are responsible for the capital debt that has built up in this industry. I believe—and this applies not only to electricity but to other industries—that we in this country must learn to live with, and operate efficiently, a mixed economy. There are extremists on both sides. We cannot go back to the Manchester School or 2 Economists of the last century and complete laissez-faire, nor can we go forward to the complete implementation of clause 4, as asked for by extremists on the other side. Therefore, we in this country must live and work with a mixed economy. In my opinion, at the present moment we are just about right. Too much more to private enterprise or too much more into nationalisation would be a mistake on both sides. As far as I am concerned, for Scotland particularly, the foolery of Bennery would be an absolute disaster.

    May I now turn to how I see us, both parties, running this industry efficiently. When the political decision is taken, politics must go out of it, and we roust start looking at it industrially, commercially. The first thing lacking in this industry, as in some other nationalised industries, is competition. Whether we like it or not, it is competition that keeps industry efficient.

    Let us look at it in another way. In Scotland at the moment, there is nothing so topical as football and the way that our country has performed in the World Cup. Can one imagine the Scottish League if there were no competition? Where would Hearts, Rangers, Celtic and the Dons be if we were not allowed competition, or a First Division? That is why I say that we wish to bring competition into these industries; otherwise we go to the convoy system, which means that we all move at the speed of the slowest.

    What are some of the ways of bringing competition into this industry? One simple, known method in the private sector of industry, or large conglomerates, is inter-firm comparison. There are 12 area boards in England and Wales, and two in Scotland. If these were made to subject themselves to the budgetary disciplines of inter-firm comparison, we could do something about the less efficient. If we could compare unit costs and look at the labour employment from one board to another, this would be the way to bring up the laggards to the efficiency of the best.

    Another suggestion applies not only to electricity, but also to gas and transport. These nationalised industries have a duty. In the case of electricity, it is to produce electricity and take it to the point of distribution. The industry should not engage in periphery activities like retailing electric fires or competing with firms for estimates for wiring domestic JPEG 3 houses. These are not the jobs of a nationalised board. Its duty is to produce electricity and take it to the point of distribution.

    I turn to the consumers, because they are very much affected by electricity prices. Who are the consumers of electricity? They comprise virtually the whole of our country. This includes pensioners, retired people, the old, and schoolchildren. They are all consumers of electricity.

    While I shall shortly speak of different types of fuel that can be used for electricity, I should like to mention one at the moment, because it will become topical, and that is coal. We are seeing the possibility of a serious rise in the price of coal. I think it was a member of the party opposite who said, "One man's wage increase is another man's price increase". If Mr. McGahey gets the £5,000 a year miner on four days a week, this is bound to affect electricity prices. If there is to be a social contract between the unions and the Labour Party, might this social contract be widened to include the categories of people I have mentioned —pensioners, schoolchildren, the old, the infirm, the retired and those on fixed incomes?

    What are the fuels that are used for electricity? I have mentioned coal. There are also gas, hydro, thermo and nuclear. In another century there could be solar and tidal. In the meantime we must have a fuel policy that mixes the use of them all, so that we do not rely too heavily on one fuel. We have five possible fuels and we should power some of our stations on one fuel and some on another, and also make them convertible.

    At the same time, having got a balance of fuel use in this industry, may I make another point which I think worthy of consideration in the long term. As we see oil coming on stream throughout Britain, we must realise that it is probably correct that there should be one price for oil. The time has now arrived when we should consider the possibility of one price for electricity throughout the country. That is sensible.

    I have tried to widen the subject and to throw out ideas for the future efficient running of the industry, which is the responsibility of both parties, in the years ahead. In Scotland, I wish to see it 4 efficient and competitive. The Leader of the Labour Party mentioned the electoral difficulty of Marks and Spencer being run like the Co-op. May I say something in defence of the Co-op. It does not give me any pleasure to talk about any Scottish institution as inefficient. Therefore, I hope that some of the ideas that are being thrown up will be put into practice, enabling us to proud be of the North of Scotland Hydro-Electric Board and the South of Scotland Electricity Board, so that when the league of 14 that I have mentioned is printed, these two teams will be at the top and will remain there, and it will be a source of pride to Scotland that our two electricity authorities are efficient, active and competitive in the interests of the consumers.
