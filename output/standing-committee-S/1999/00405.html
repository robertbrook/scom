Budget Implications

10.30 am

The Chairman
: We begin with a statement from the Secretary of State. There is no time limit on the statement and subsequent questions, but I shall have regard to the time needed for the next debate in deciding when to bring the questioning to and end.

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Dr. John Reid)
: Mr. Chairman, I am pleased to have this opportunity to make a statement about the implications of the Chancellor's Budget for Scotland, and to answer questions. Copies of my statement are available on the Table.

It is important to start by recognising that this year's Budget builds on the Government's achievements so far in our prudent management of the economy. The Budget takes further measures to build a stronger and fairer Britain, with actions to benefit every region and every part of the United Kingdom. But it does so only because the Government have established a record of stability and prudence in economic management, including management of the Exchequer, so that we are now able safely to release substantial new investment for public services especially, although not explicitly, for the NHS.

On the management of the economy, I am pleased to say that economic growth in the United Kingdom last year ran ahead of the forecasts of the experts. We expect the United Kingdom economy to grow this year at between 2.75 per cent. and 3.25 per cent., before reverting to trend growth rates of around 2.5 per cent. next year.

That growth has happened without triggering inflation, which for the third year running is within our target of 2.5 per cent. Careful control of inflation now means that long-term interest rates in Britain are down to the levels of Germany, and lower than in the United States.

At the same time, 800,000 new jobs have been created in the United Kingdom. Employment levels have risen to the highest for over 30 years and unemployment has fallen to the lowest levels for over 20 years—both historically and in international terms. Those figures are reflected throughout Scotland.

The Government's own finances are also in better shape. We inherited a current deficit in 1997 of over £20 billion. For the year just ended, the current surplus is forecast to be ©17 billion. We have respected the rules on public spending and public borrowing which we introduced on taking office and laid down in order to promote stability and public confidence. As a result, the national debt now imposes less of a burden on our current expenditure. A total of £4 billion a year has been saved from debt interest payments and £3 billion from expenditure on unemployment benefits.

It is on this basis of sound management of the economy and of the Government's own programmes 2 that we are now able to release substantial new resources for health, for education, for tackling child poverty, to help pensioners, to improve public transport and to tackle crime. Those are the priorities that people expect the Government to tackle, whether they live in Scotland or elsewhere in the United Kingdom.

For Scotland, this will mean a £2.4 billion boost for public spending over the next four years. At the centre of the extra resources announced in the Budget is the largest ever package of spending on the NHS. Across the United Kingdom, this provides for an immediate £2 billion boost in the coming year and growth averaging 6.1 per cent. a year in real terms over the next four years—the longest period of sustained high growth in the history of the NHS.

For Scotland, decisions on the allocation of resources—allocation from within the global sum—are for the First Minister and the Scottish Executive. A statement on spending plans for the new financial year was made in the Scottish Parliament last Thursday. In that statement, Jack McConnell confirmed that the extra resources would be targeted at turning round Scotland's appalling health record, and there will be a £173 million increase in the coming year alone. That comes on top of previously agreed increases and means an extra 7.3 per cent. in real terms or £34 more for every man, woman and child. The additional resources in the Budget will provide a massive £2.2 billion investment in the NHS or almost £450 per person over the next four years.

In 2000–01, additional resources will be allocated to priority areas, with £87 million for education to guarantee improvements and excellence in every school, nearly £9 million to guarantee safer communities by tackling crime and nearly £16 million for transport.

As well as providing better public services for all, Budget 2000 takes further steps to build a fairer society by providing extra help for those who need it most. Families and children will benefit. The Government have made no secret of the fact that we want to ensure that every child has the best possible start in life and that the tax and benefit system should support all children and recognise the extra costs and responsibility that parents face. Our ambition is to halve child poverty by the end of the decade as we move forward with our commitment to abolish it within 20 years.

More than 640,000 families will be better off as a result of measures in the Budget. Those include an additional £4.35 a week increase in the under-16 child credit in the working families tax credit from June 2000 and a 50p a week increase in the children's tax credit when it is introduced in April 2001. That will make it worth no less than £442 a year—more than twice the value of the married couple's allowance, which it replaces. All that comes on top of the increase in child benefit that takes effect from 10 April. Child benefit will rise to £15 for the first child and £10 for subsequent children—a 40 per cent. increase since the Government took office.

JPEG 3 The Budget is also good news for Scotland's 920,000 pensioners. From next winter, they will benefit from the further £50 increase in the winter fuel payment to £150, which will be paid to every household with someone over 60. More than 700,000 households will benefit. There will also be an increase in the capital limits attached to the minimum income guarantee, which will mean that pensioners who have managed to save something for their retirement can still qualify for extra support. That will benefit 9,000 of the poorest pensioners. All that is in addition to the free TV licences that will be available from November to all pensioners aged 75 and over. That will benefit 250,000 households.

The Government have set out to encourage and reward work. We are determined to help into work those who are unemployed and those who are outside the labour market, but who would like to find work. The Government have already taken significant steps to increase employment opportunity for all through the new deal programme to help people move from welfare to work and through measures to make work pay. More than 77,000 people have already participated in the various new deal programmes, and nearly 28,000 have moved on to jobs. The national minimum wage, introduced in April 1999, will benefit 100,000 workers in Scotland, and up to 2.5 million Scottish taxpayers will benefit from the 10p starting rate of income tax introduced in the last Budget. Around 2.2 million will benefit from the cut in the basic rate to 22p, which takes effect from tomorrow.

On top of that Budget 2000 introduces new action teams, which will help to match unemployed people to suitable vacancies in nearby labour markets. Scotland will benefit from two action teams, each receiving funding of £1.5 million. That comes on top of Scotland's employment zone which, from this month, will provide tailored help for around 4,500 long-term unemployed people. It extends and intensifies the new deal for those aged 25 and over from April 2001; provides a £100 job grant from spring 2001 to ease the transition from welfare to work; and extends the choices available to lone parents on income support so that, from April 2001, all lone parents on income support with children over 5 will be required to meet with a specialist personal adviser who will guide them through their choices—including help to try work, help to move into part-time or full-time work, and the opportunity to undertake education and training. That will help 62,000 lone parents in Scotland.

We are also building stronger businesses. The Budget further promotes enterprise and job creation across all regions of the UK. More than 53,000 small and medium-sized businesses in Scotland will benefit from: further reforms to capital gains tax to strengthen the incentives for business investment—with a shortening of the business assets taper from 10 to 4 years and a reduction in the percentage thresholds for qualifying business asset shareholdings; permanent 40 per cent. first-year capital allowances; 100 per cent. first-year capital allowances for small firms investing in information and communications technology 4 equipment for the next three years; final details of the new enterprise management incentives scheme, the all-employee share ownership plan, the research and development tax credit and corporate venturing tax relief—all introduced from this month; and a new £1 billion target umbrella fund to help finance enterprise growth across all regions of the UK over three to five years. That scheme will significantly enhance access to early-stage venture capital for growth potential businesses across the UK.

All those measures will be of undoubted benefit both to individuals and businesses in Scotland. Many of them apply throughout the UK, but I wish to highlight several measures that will be of particular benefit to Scotland.

Transport and transport costs are an important issue for rural and remote areas. Budget measures will not only help those people who live and work in rural areas, but will also encourage more visitors and provide a further boost to local economies. The Budget this year leaves road fuel duties unchanged in real terms; extends the reduced rate of vehicle excise duty to cars up to 1200cc from next March; helps the haulage industry through cuts in VED for many lorries and a freeze for most others; and introduces a new, fairer structure for air passenger duty. In particular, the duty on economy flights within the UK will be halved to £5 from November 2001 and all flights from the highlands and islands will be free from duty. With around 900,000 passengers using highlands and Islands airports each year, abolishing this charge will represent a significant boost for local economies.

The Chancellor has also recognised the importance to Scotland and the UK of the whisky industry in terms of employment and revenue—and no doubt in terms of pleasure and enjoyment. The Budget leaves the duty on spirits unchanged for the third year running. [ Interruption.] I hear a sedentary intervention from the Liberal Democrats saying that it should have been cut—no doubt to be paid for by yet another penny on income tax.

We look forward to hearing from them how these cuts are to be paid for.

The Budget is unambiguously good for Britain and good for Scotland. It builds on our achievement of economic stability and our record of job creation of the past three years, setting out the next stage of reforms that will boost enterprise, increase employment and deliver a fairer and more inclusive society. It provides a £2.4 billion boost to Scotland's public services, benefiting all 5.1 million residents of Scotland. It provides extra help for Scotland's 640,000 families with children, 920,000 pensioners, and 53,000 small and medium-sized businesses. That is a clear sign that the Labour Government are working hard for hard-working families. It means that by April 2001, personal tax and benefit changes under this and previous Budgets will have delivered an increase in the average household income in Scotland of more than £8.80 a week—more than £450 a year.

This Budget also demonstrates the benefit that Scotland gains within the UK from its stake in the larger UK economy. I might add that it also JPEG 5 demonstrates one of the benefits that Scotland contributes to the UK, based on our traditions—I might even say our flair—for solid, prudent financial management, epitomised by the Chancellor of the Exchequer himself.

The Scottish economy benefits directly from the Government's sound and stable management of the UK economy. Manufacturing output is growing in Scotland. Indeed, the Bank of Scotland's recent survey, reported in The Scotsman on Monday, showed that output is rising strongly in both manufacturing and service sectors, and that it has been rising steadily for more than a year. Exports of manufacturing goods from Scotland are 8 per cent. last year. Employment in Scotland is up by 46,000 since spring 1997; and unemployment—especially youth unemployment and long-term unemployment—has been substantially cut. In short, the Scottish economy as a whole is in good shape under the Government's stewardship. Every individual in Scotland benefits. Step by step, we are building a better Scotland. The Budget will help to sustain and promote that goal.
