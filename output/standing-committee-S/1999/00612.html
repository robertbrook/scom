National Textiles Strategy

The Minister of State, Scotland Office (Mr. Brian Wilson)
: I am pleased to make a statement about the United Kingdom textile and clothing strategy announced by my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Trade and Industry last week, and about other issues currently relevant to the industry. Copies of my statement are available on the Table.

I warmly welcome the Government's 12-point plan to support the British textile industry, which has lacked an overall strategy and adequate support for too long. To pre-empt any question, I can assure hon. Members that I am clad head to toe in garments produced by British textile workers—visibly, and invisibly.

The textile and clothing industry makes a major contribution to the UK economy. It is our ninth largest manufacturing sector, with a turnover of £18 billion and exports of more than £5 billion. Employment UK-wide is 270,000. In some parts of the country, particularly the east midlands, Yorkshire, the Scottish borders and Northern Ireland, textiles and clothing is the main, if not the sole, manufacturing industry. In the Scottish borders, the textile industry employs more than 4,000 people and accounts for almost 50 per cent. of all manufacturing employment.

It is also a diverse industry, producing the clothing and accessories that we see on high streets and catwalks, and traditional fabrics such as linen, Harris tweed, woollens and worsted. It is also responsible for weaving woollen carpets and polypropylene fibres, and for creating products from the finest silks and cashmere, as well as uniforms for civilian and armed forces, and high-tech, bullet-proof fabrics.

We all know the challenges that the industry faces. The global market for textiles is changing rapidly. We know that we cannot, and should not, try to compete on labour costs alone, because many of our competitors pay wages that would be illegal, as well as immoral, in this country. We need to recognise that 2 and build an industry that is less vulnerable to such competition. We need to build on our strengths and capabilities, emphasising such facets as design, branding and the distribution and supply chain mechanism. We succeed when we add value to our products.

The industry can do that by exploiting knowledge and making better use of skills, know-how and talent. We need to create new markets, introduce new processes, invest in new technology and reduce unit costs. We must foster and retain design talent, and adapt to fashion changes. However, let us not run the risk of talking down what already exists. Many segments of the British, and specifically Scottish, textile industry already compete successfully, on the basis of high-quality products and design. Those companies have tended to concentrate on higher-value production. They continue to employ substantial numbers of people and have a bright future.

The Government are determined to work with the industry to achieve the necessary competitiveness. It is crucial that we retain the skills of talented people who can make a real difference to the success of the industry. The Government and the private sector must continue to work in partnership to enable our manufacturing industries to adapt and succeed. Nowhere is that message more important than in the textile industry.

That is why the Department of Trade and Industry last year appointed an industry-led group to take a long-term view of the industry and recommend actions that will improve the sector's competitiveness. That group reported last week, and the Government responded by launching their national strategy for textiles and clothing.

The Government's 12-point plan will help companies to compete more effectively in the global marketplace. It aims to exploit what is good in the industry and build on the work already taking place in companies, trade associations and academia. It will provide the support that businesses need to move into and grow in overseas markets. We will review its rules on the number of times that firms can attract Government support to attend overseas trade fairs, and British Trade International will provide funding to support better-focused export promotion by the British apparel and textile export advisory group.

The plan will stimulate the flow of ideas between research departments and business to ensure that we exploit as fully as possible the ideas coming out of universities. I am delighted that the Government will designate a centre of manufacturing excellence in textiles at Heriot Watt university's Galashiels campus. That is a testament to the excellent work and the fine young designers and technicians that the Galashiels college produces.

The measures will also build on, and aim to increase, the number of collaborative projects, which so often lead to innovation in products and processes. Once again, Heriot Watt is involved, joining Leeds university and the university of Manchester institute of science and technology. Those universities will work with the British Textiles Technology Group and up to JPEG 3 100 firms in a partnership to encourage collaboration among leading universities, research organisations and industry. They will provide co-ordinated programmes of research, education and training that will produce new products and processes.

The plan will also help to improve supply-chain mechanisms and improve skills in the industry. It will build on our excellence in world-class design with the aim of keeping more talent within the United Kingdom. It provides for future discussions with the industry and public procurement agencies about how public procurement decisions can better support British firms. Finally, it will also help those who have lost their jobs to develop new skills and find new employment.

However, that is only the beginning of the Government's response. The Scotland Office will also work closely with a special unit at the DTI to consider how it can respond positively and quickly to the other recommendations.

The textile industry is extremely important to Scotland. Despite the decline in recent years, in 1997—the most recent year for which we have comprehensive figures—textiles was still the fourth largest manufacturing sector in Scotland. It employed about 30,000 people and accounted for more than 10 per cent. of manufacturing jobs in Scotland. It is estimated that every 100 jobs created directly by the industry support an additional 83 jobs in supplier businesses.

I have already said that textiles accounts for half the manufacturing jobs in the Scottish borders. However, the industry is also important in other parts of Scotland. More than 16 per cent. of manufacturing jobs in Ayrshire, 14 per cent. in Tayside, 13 per cent. in the Forth valley, 12 per cent. in Lanarkshire, 9 per cent. in the highlands and islands, and 4 per cent. in Moray, Badenoch and Strathspey rely on textiles. The Scottish textiles and clothing industry is also a major exporter. In 1998, it exported goods to the value of £498 million.

I fully recognise the difficulties that the industry has experienced in recent years. Like other developed economies, the Scottish economy is losing volume garment manufacture to lower-wage economies. That is why we keep saying that we cannot begin to compete on labour costs alone.

We also know that recent problems have been caused by major retailers sourcing more products overseas. We cannot force Marks and Spencer or any other retail company to buy British. That is a matter of of their commercial judgment.. The best way of encouraging them to do so is through the exercise of consumer preference. I do not single out Marks and Spencer, since, despite recent cutbacks, it sources a higher proportion of goods in the UK than any comparable chain.

We are already funding work to find ways of improving the domestic textile and clothing supply chain and so increase domestic sourcing. We shall continue to talk to manufacturers and major retailers to identify ways in which UK sourcing can be made more attractive.

4 There is real danger in talking down the industry. There are plenty of examples in Scotland of companies with superb, quality products, and I know that the industry gets very frustrated about being written off when there are so many good stories to tell. Traditional Weatherwear in Cumbernauld is producing traditional mackintoshes for the world's top design houses—and the company has a marvellously impressive apprenticeship programme. In the past couple of months, I have visited Hawick Cashmere, Johnstons of Elgin, Clan Douglas, Lochcarron, and Kenneth Mackenzie in Stornoway—each of those companies has an excellent story to tell. Tremendous work is also being done by the Galashiels campus of Heriot Watt university for the whole of the United Kingdom textile industry.

The announcement of Heriot Watt as a centre of manufacturing excellence in textiles and the award of funding through the Faraday Partnership is public proof that that is recognised by the industry and by the Department of Trade and Industry. Of course, all the measures for the UK textile industry will equally apply in Scotland. The Scotland Office will work closely with the Scottish Executive and the DTI to ensure that the measures are fully implemented.

I am delighted that my hon. Friend the Member for Central Fife (Mr. McLeish) considers that the importance of the textile industry to the Scottish economy merits further action by the Scottish Executive. That is why, earlier today at the first meeting of the Scottish Textile Forum, he announced a further comprehensive package of additional measures to support the textile industry in Scotland.

The forum will represent the interests of the Scottish textile industry, and the Scotland Office will continue to work closely with the DTI and the Scottish Executive, the industry and trade unions to ensure that the views of the industry are fully known whenever necessary on issues such as the multifibre arrangement.

We will continue to work closely with the commercial posts in overseas embassies and consulates, to promote the marketing of Scottish goods. Here at home, we plan to use Dover house, in the heart of Whitehall, to promote Scottish excellence. Dover house is a magnificent venue and an underused resource which we want to put to work in support of Scottish industry and Scottish jobs. We will show the way in September when two major, and, I predict, spectacular, promotional shows will be held—the first by the Harris Tweed Authority and the second by Scottish Cashmere. These will not be isolated events but will fit in closely with the international trade promotion efforts of those sectors.

It would be remiss of me to make a statement on the textile industry without commenting on the renewed threat to the cashmere and other industries from retaliatory action over the dispute between the European Union and the United States of America on bananas. Not least because of the contacts developed during the previous "banana wars" episode, I have a close working relationship with the cashmere industry. Last year, the Government took prompt and decisive action to safeguard the industry when the same threat JPEG 5 arose. I am appalled that even the possibility of renewed innocent involvement in the same dispute should now have arisen. Hon. Members may rest assured that we will continue to lobby at every level on behalf of cashmere, and all other products, to remove United Kingdom items from the retaliation list. However, it is crucial to understand that no decision has yet been taken by the USA on the retaliation shortlist, far less to add cashmere knitwear to it. The consultation period ends on Wednesday, and the final list will be produced on 19 June.

In the meantime, two messages must be hammered home: the first, to the United States authorities, is that the United Kingdom is part of the solution in Brussels and is fully committed to the introduction of a World Trade Organisation-compliant banana-importing regime based on the agreement that has been reached between the United States and the Caribbean producers. That position is unambiguous and unequivocal, and on that basis, there is no logical reason why retaliatory action against the United Kingdom should be escalated or even retained.

The second message must be to the European Union. It is, quite simply, that the introduction of a WTO-compliant banana-importing regime is not a matter for further delay. It is an urgent necessity if the process of retaliation is to be brought to an end. Fifteen months have elapsed since the WTO panel once again found against the European Union on the issue. To any objective observer it is incomprehensible that these 15 months have not been sufficient to bring the matter to a WTO-compliant conclusion. Throughout that period, there have continued to be innocent victims in the UK and other EU member states. Now that list of victims threatens to change, or even lengthen. That is intolerable and all those who are responsible for negotiations in Brussels must recognise that it is intolerable.

There is no doubt that the textile industry, throughout the UK and in Scotland, needs our support both within the domestic market and in the complexities of international trade policy. With my own proposals, the packages announced by my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Trade and Industry last week and by my hon. Friend the Member for Central Fife earlier today, offer a series of practical measures to help the industry, from the earliest design stages to finding new overseas markets. Together they offer a coherent plan for the future of the industry and of those people linked to it. We will work together to take the industry forward while supporting those affected by the changes that will inevitably take place.
