Constitutional issues in Scotland form an interesting and important subject for a debate, and within it one could roam widely over a range of topics—for example, the importance of the role of the House of Lords, the Government's negotiations on the future of Europe, or the reform of local government upon which the Government have embarked. One could also debate the willingness of the Liberal party to do a deal with another party—almost any other party, regardless of policy or principle—in order to secure a new voting system. It would not necessarily be a fairer voting system; it is known to be potentially destabilising and it would undermine the political integrity of our Parliament. However, it is one that would just happen to favour, with a wholly disproportionate share of power, the small and largely irrelevant parties. The debate could include the separatist threat from the Scottish National party, although it is hard to discern a burning flame of passion on that issue. It too has made the first tacit admission of its declining fortunes. One of its leaders has said that it should join with the Labour party—not to secure separation, 2 although the Labour party is half-way to accepting the policy, de facto and de jure, but simply for social and economic reasons.

Mr. Lang
: It is reassuring to learn that the flame of passion for independence and complete separation for Scotland still burns brightly, even if it is rather heavily qualified.

That, and other, matters may crop up in the debate but I wish to concentrate on one issue—the deception, delusion and fraud that the Labour party are perpetrating the people of Scotland through the policies that it mistakenly and misleading groups under the general heading of devolution, a word that has long since been robbed of its meaning. The word has gone stale, just as the arguments advanced in its name have long congealed. It has been hijacked to disguise the Labour party's plans for the advance of socialism. Good devolution is the dispersal of power in a sovereign unitary state: diffusion from the centre in order to spread decision-making, to strengthen democracy and to enhance the unity of the state. However, under the malign distortions of Labour Governments, the concept has been soured and deformed into a bogus and ersatz deceit of which George Orwell would have been proud.

We should not be surprised, because the impulse for Labour support of devolution has no philosophical or idealistic base. Socialism is a centralising, not a devolving, force. It seeks to control and direct and not to diffuse power. The Labour party's motives in embracing devolution were two-fold; to head off the nationalists in the 1970's and to secure a local power base in Scotland because it could not secure a national power base in the United Kingdom. The concept of devolution has been corrupted in the Labour party's hands because its support for that concept springs from opportunism and self-interest. That is why Labour's policies are wrong and why that party has embarked on a route that would destroy the United Kingdom if its policies were to be put into effect. What the Labour party calls devolution is halfway to separation and partial secession and the deliberate destabilisation of the constitutional integrity of the United Kingdom.

Many of the older Labour Members realised that and opposed devolution because they understood that it was alien to their socialist roots and because they were loyal unionists who could see the slippery slope down which their party was heading, but the younger ones, who are hungry for power have, one by one, climbed aboard. They do not care about the consequences, only about power. The non-taxing Assembly proposed in the Scotland Act which was so signably rejected by the Scottish people in 1979, has been replaced by an altogether fiercer and more damaging animal. It is no longer referred to as a Scottish Assembly but as a Scottish Parliament because the Labour party thinks that that name has more ring to it. It certainly has a ring, but it is the ring of the cash register because the body is about taxing rather than talking, levying rather than legislation and debiting rather than debating.

4 True devolution is not moving power from government at Westminster to government at Edinburgh—still less to the Opposition at Edinburgh—but moving power from the Government to people, from the centre to the hinterland. As such, that devolution is central to the Conservative party's philosophy.

Our party has put into practice true devolution. That is apparent in the creation of the Scottish Office in 1885 and in countless developments involving administrative devolution since that time.

We see devolution in our general approach to cutting taxation, controlling public expenditure, privatising state industries and in the more specific measures that we have introduced.

Mr. Donald Dewar
(Glasgow, Garscadden)
: That was the Liberals.

Mr. Lang
: No, it was not the Liberals. They advanced the proposition but the Conservative Government implemented it under Lord Salisbury and created the Scottish Office. We have gone on creating devolution through various administrative forms. For example, we created the new body Scottish Natural Heritage, from the Nature Conservancy Council and the Countryside Commission for Scotland, and brought it to Scotland against the opposition of the Labour party, who wanted to leave it as one body, based in Peterborough. We see devolution in our creation of Scottish Enterprise, which brings training from a United Kingdom base to a Scottish base. We have merged it with the Scottish Development Agency, and have devolved powers and 80 per cent. of the spending capacity of that body to local enterprise companies with the majority of private sector individuals on the boards.

We see devolution in education where, as a result of parental choice introduced by this Government, 160,000 children have been able to go to the school of their choice. We see it with the creation of school boards and the creation of councils for further education colleges and with the transfer of funding for universities from United Kingdom bases to Scotland. We see it in housing with the right to buy for tenants, with tenants' rights, with housing associations and in other ways, We see it in the setting up of executive agencies, such as Historic Scotland.

We see it in the partnerships that we have entered into, such as urban partnerships involving central Government, local government, trade unions, voluntary bodies and the private sector. We see it, too, in our approach to the citizens' charter, in which we are developing rights of redress for individuals in dealings with nationalised industries. Such rich, diverse devolution is organic and vibrant development of a democratic constitution in which freedom and unity are strengthened by the dispersal and the sharing of power. While others talk, we have acted when it counted, at both local and European level. We shall continue to act always in a way that maintains the unity of the United Kingdom.

On Monday last week, I published my consultation paper on the structural change in local government. Our objective is to secure a new local government system in which the units of government reflect local loyalties and are strong, cost-effectively resourced and capable of discharging their statutory functions effectively and efficiently. We are doing that for its own sake, not as a byproduct of the creation of a tax-raising Assembly, or as JPEG 5 an insult of local government in making it only secondary to the creation of an Assembly in Edinburgh, which would drain powers from local government and centralise them in Edinburgh.

We have also acted in our relations with Europe. Some hon. Members seem to think that their call for Scotland to play a direct role in Europe is new, but surely they have noticed the achievements of the past 18 years.

Member for Aberdeen, North (Mr. Hughes), who is unfortunately absent at present.

Scottish businesses and financial institutions are already preparing to make a success of the single European market. The Opposition believe that the future government of Scotland is important. However, we recognise that a meeting of minds between the Government and the Opposition on the issue is unlikely to occur. Ministers have shown a sad determination to defy Scottish opinion on that issue above all others. They argue the case for the status quo—a policy that attracts minimal support from the Scottish people.

My duty today is to set out our case briefly and reasonably clearly. The Labour party believes that Scotland should have a Parliament that operates within the framework of the United Kingdom. It should be built into our constitutional arrangements. Our intention is to strengthen the union by ensuring that it is sufficiently flexible to meet Scotland's special needs. We seek reform within, not secession from, the United Kingdom. With respect, I say that there is no point in the nationalists' demand that our scheme, which was endorsed by the Scottish Constitutional Convention, should meet their tests. Our case would not and should not meet such demands. We do not make a case for separatism.

Our case is essentially democratic. We live in a centralised state where power is in the grip not of Parliament but of Whitehall and the Government machine. It is essential that power is transferred to the people to be exercised in the areas where they live. That is important not just for Scotland but for Wales, the north and other parts of the country.

The Scottish experience is not now unique as perhaps it was in the 1970s, but is shared throughout the country. There is a different atmosphere from that in 1978 and 1979. Reform in one part of the country will spark movement in others. That movement will complement, even if it does not reproduce precisely, what Labour intends in Scotland. The atmosphere now is for change, but the Government are defying the mood of the time.

The Secretary of State claimed, as will others, that the scheme would be unworkable because Britain could not accommodate such a fundamental change. The opponents, especially the Conservatives, are hard at work encouraging echoes of the 1970s debates. That is wishful thinking; the work of politicians who use the fear of change to block progress.

The Secretary of State should remember that the recent research carried out by the Rowntree Trust found substantial support for Scottish devolution in England, by a majority of 2:1, and a clear majority—over 50 per cent of the voters—in favour of the Scottish Constitutional Convention scheme in Scotland, despite the multiplicity of choices.

10 The essence of the case for Labour is that unlike the Government, we trust the people to take their own decisions. We want democratic control over an existing Scottish Office that employs 8,000 civil servants. Devolution is not about introducing a new expensive and expansive bureaucracy. The budget of £11 billion and the administration of its services is already in place. Such radical reform would have practical and important consequences: that is the point of the exercise. The Scottish Parliament will have the power to make law. That is what matters in a democratic society. It will control all the major areas of domestic legislation: health, education and housing. The list is well known, well defined and widely accepted in Scotland. Its decisions will reflect Scottish views, traditions and needs, unlike those taken today.

The past decade was riddled with mistakes, some of which were monumentally insensitive. Anyone who wanted to spend £1,000 million on buying bitterness and confusion could not have done better than to introduce the poll tax. The irrelevant education policy fragmented the school system and created stress rather than put in place the most effective support for the pupils' benefit. The challenge to the national health service is such that the NHS cannot reasonable be expected to survive another term of a Government who believe that health care is another commodity to be bought and sold in the marketplace.

In the past 12 years, there has been an impressive sustained argument for change. It is a perverse tribute to the Government that, under their stewardship, public opinion has moved substantially and decisively in favour of devolution.

The core of the case is legislative power, but the Scottish Parliament will have a wider remit and influence. I referred to the budget of £11 billion with its consequent purchasing power. There will be substantial economic responsibilities—development agencies, training for skills and regional policy. The scheme is well-rounded, well-constructed and worthwhile. That comes as a great shock to the Secretary of State who was reported in the newspapers in the past couple of days as saying that Labour is planning a costly and dangerous constitutional upheaval, "a nightmare scenario" of a centrally planned economy that would destroy all the achievements of the past decade. I sympathise with the aim of wanting to end the achievements of the past decade.

Scotland is plunging into recession. Investment and productivity are falling and unemployment is rising. It is an eccentric definition of achievement but those dire warnings are based on the discovery that a Scottish Parliament will have power and defined and effective influence. For the vast majority of Scots, that is its essential justification. We are not prejudging how such power might be used, but if a Scottish Parliament had no power, the game would not be worth the candle.

It is odd to criticise such a Parliament for having worthwhile powers and responsibilities. The Secretary of State sees it as doubly dangerous because the power will be in the hands of politicians who reflect Scottish opinion. One of the saddest things about the Government's stance is that they will not trust the people and they consider that Scots exercising their franchise cannot be trusted to act responsibly. Ministers who persist in those attitudes will not be trusted by the people.
