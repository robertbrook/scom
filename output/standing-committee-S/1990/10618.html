Local Government Structure and Finance in Scotland

10.30 am

Resolved,

That during further proceedings on the matter of Local Government Structure and Finance in Scotland the Committee do meet on Thursday 20 June at half-last Ten o'clock.—[ Mr. Lang.]

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Lang)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the matter of Local Government Structure and Finance in Scotland

I welcome the opportunity offered by the debates today and on Thursday to review the local government in Scotland and to reflect on the current review exercise. I also welcome the opportunity to debunk some of the myths that have grown up over our years in office and to put the record straight.

Over the past decade, we have promoted considerable changes in the way that local government does its business. We have introduced competitive tendering. We have encouraged local government to work with new organisations such as the local enterprise companies. We have made changes to local taxation. We have set up the urban partnerships.

As established organisations are rarely comfortable with change, and the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities is, of course, dominated by the Labour party, the public response of local government to all these changes has been somewhat negative. If one believed the doom and gloom that is preached by Opposition Members, one would think that local government had never been in such a sorry state. It is alleged constantly that the Government have reduced financial support for Scottish local authorities with the result that there has been a deterioration in the quality of local services. In fact, as so often is the case, the truth is very different from such allegations. It may be difficult for some members of the Committee to face the facts, but I propose to outline some of them.

I shall start with financial support. Government grants to Scottish authorities—rate or revenue support grant and specific grants—have increased in real terms from £2,562 million in 1979–80 to £2,809 million in 1991–92. So far from Government support having been reduced in the Government's period of office, there has been an increase in real terms.

What of the alleged deterioration in local authority services.

Mr. Gavin Strang
(Edinburgh, East)
: Is the right hon. Gentleman including in that the current year, in which a bottom-up increase in Government support 2 has been achieved by putting up value added tax? Is he seriously including that in the Government's increased contribution to local authority expenditure?

Mr. Lang
: The figures that I gave relate to the period from 1979–80 to 1991–92, so they include the current year. Far from there having been a cut, as Opposition Members repeatedly allege, there has been a substantial, real terms, increase in Government support of 9.6 per cent.

What of the alleged deterioration in local authority services as a result of the Government's actions? The facts are very different from the allegations. Let us look at the three services that account for the biggest shares of local authority current expenditure—education, social work and the police. Since 1979 the percentage of pupils staying on until fifth year has increased from 35 to 54 per cent. The percentage staying on until sixth year has increased from 14 to 25 per cent and the percentage entering higher education has increased from 16.8 to 24 per cent. Expenditure per pupil has increased in real terms by 49 per cent. The number of field social workers has also increased.

Mr. Lang

I gave examples of the quality of local government services from education and social work. The number of field social workers has increased from 2,985 to 4,700—an increase of 47 per cent. There has been a 26 per cent increase in all social work staff, including a 13 per cent increase in home care staff. The number of places in local authority day care centres for the elderly has increased by 152 per cent. Places in such centres for the mentally handicapped have increased by 59 per cent and those for the physically handicapped by 49 per cent. Places for the mentally handicapped in local authority residential homes have increased by 77 per cent. The number of people who receive assistance from home helps has increased from 58,000 to over 80,000—an increase of 39 per cent.

Expenditure on the police service has increased in real terms by more than 50 per cent. The authorised establishment of police officers has increased from 13,275 to 14,045. Actual police strength has increased from 12,831 to 13,841. There are over 1,000 more police officers in Scotland now than in 1979. Such statistics do not show a deterioration, or even a marginal improvement, in the quality of local services. Services have improved greatly since 1979. The figures firmly dispel any doubts above the Government's commitment to local services.

Staff and other resources are being used more effectively. In those services that are subject to competition, an improved standard of service is achieved with the same, or fewer, staff. I doubt whether that would have happened without the spur of competition, which the Government had the courage and foresight to apply to the unwilling flanks of local authorities.

It was always possible for local authorities to test the market to ensure that they provided the best services. Very few did so. The Government had to legislate to secure competition in some local authority services. The Local Government Act 1988 has been in force for three years. It shows that the discipline of competition works. Some authorities claim that competitive tendering leads to increased costs. That may be so in some cases, especially when initial, one-off costs are considered. However, the savings that result from competitive pressures outweigh the extra cost.

Competitive tendering means more than savings. Benefits accrue from setting clear specifications, the acquisition of new and improved management skills, the cultivation of a more business like and innovative approach, the removal of restrictive practices, the setting of targets for standards and productivity and the development of performance indicators. The whole process, whether authorities have won the work themselves or awarded contracts to private firms, has been the catalyst for increased efficiency, better value for money and the best possible service for the consumer.

That is the background against which it has been possible and appropriate to consider change in the structure of local government. The Government have done a great deal to ensure that the needs and wishes of the consumer of local authority services are better identified and better met by authorities.

The hon. Gentleman knows that Lothian region has by far the densest population of all the regions, and that the circumstances there are very different from those obtaining in areas such as Dumfries and Galloway.

Local authorities are now providing services that are more cost-effective and more closely geared to what their electorates want. They are doing that because of Government policies.

We have also encouraged local authorities to look beyond the provision of services at their own hand. That is often derided as eroding the role of local government. In fact, our efforts have been directed not at taking powers to the centre but at pushing power down to consumers of services or at encouraging a pluralistic approach, with local authorities working alongside specialist bodies such as Scottish Enterprise, the local enterprise companies and Scottish Homes as well as the private and voluntary sectors and community groups. Local government cannot be all things to all men, nor should it be. However, it can be a catalyst and an enabler, with its own special role to play. Our policies have encouraged and sometimes forced local authorities to be more outward-looking, to forge new and creative relationships with a multiplicity of organisations and groups. That may not have been comfortable, but it was necessary.

In sum, the past decade has been one of substantial change and real progress. I see no evidence that local authorities in Scotland are a shadow of their former selves—quite the reverse. Local government in Scotland is alive and well. It is expanding in terms of overall staffing levels, unlike my own Department, where staff numbers have been reduced by 22 per cent since 1979. In addition, its expenditure has increased substantially.

Those changes in the role of local government, and the importance of maintaining accountability, have led us to conclude that the time has come to prepare for a move to a single-tier structure. The argument about JPEG 5 single-tier versus two-tier structures is not new. It was considered by Lord Wheatley. The Stodart committee clearly wished to consider the matter but its remit did not allow it. Even so, that committee felt it necessary to put up a clear marker that a single-tier approach would command widespread support in local government circles.

I believe that the Stodart committee was absolutely right, and that the response to the consultation paper which I published yesterday will demonstrate widespread support for a single-tier system of local government in Scotland. I hasten to add, in view of one of today's newspaper headlines, that such a system will not involve the wholesale abolition of the regions or of the districts. We intend to establish a system that meets the specific needs of all parts of Scotland.

6 The consultation process will establish whether there will be more or fewer councillors. I expect that we shall end up with fewer councillors, but that would not diminish local government's capacity to deliver good local service. We propose to eliminate the two-tier system whereby there are two councillors for every part of the country, but that is a matter for more detailed examination in the consulation period and in subsequent consultation.

I am clear about the need for change. The changes which have taken place in local government since 1975, the shift in emphasis from direct service provider to enabler, the friction, duplication and waste that result from the two-tier system, the confusion in the public's mind are powerful arguments for a move to single-tier.

I am also clear, however that the cases for change and for a single-tier structure need to be properly set out and considered. The principles that should underlie the new system also need to be set out and debated. That is what the consultation paper deals with and I assure the Committee that the consultation process will be thorough and not just a hollow exercise.

The paper seeks views on several important issues such as what factors should determine the size of the new units; whatever there is an optimum, minimum or maximum size, and how we develop and sustain effective community links.

Instead of dealing with the real issues as the consultation paper does, it is significant that Opposition parties have tried to distract attention from the central thrust of our proposals, with which they seem to agree, by arguing for a commission, as there is to be in England, or arguing that reform is not necessary if we are not to have a Scottish assembly or Parliament. The truth is that the argument for a commission is a diversion to create some bogus controversy. I have made it clear that we shall set up whatever advisory body may be appropriate. An assembly would do nothing to make local government more local, powerful or effective—the reverse is true. To argue that the present structure of local government can be reformed only if a new layer of central Government is set up in Edinburgh is to insult those involved in local government.

I must press on with the taxation aspect of our proposals for the council tax. Effective local government requires a system of local taxation that will be stable, easy to administer and will be seen to be fair. I believe that the council tax will meet those objectives.

Under our proposals, each household will receive a single bill that takes account of the value of the property and the number of people resident there. There will be discounts of 25 per cent for single households and 50 per cent for empty second homes. There will also be discounts for students, student nurses and apprentices, while those on income support will be exempt. There will, of course, be a rebate scheme. [ Interruption.] In deference to the hon. By ensuring that local authority spending above or below a standard level will be reflected in its council tax bills, local taxpayers will have a clear message as to how their local authority's spending decisions influence what they are asked to pay.
