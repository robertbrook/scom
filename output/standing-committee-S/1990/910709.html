Draft North Hull Housing Action Trust (Area and Constitution) Order 1991

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Mr. Tim Yeo)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft North Hull Housing Action Trust (Area and Constitution) Order 1991.

 I very much welcome this opportunity to debate the order. It is a chance for us to examine what the Government are doing for the city of Hull, especially for the tenants in the area covered by the housing action trust. I welcome also the opportunity to examine the enormous attractions that the Government's housing action trusts policy now offers. It is very timely that the Committee is discussing this matter today as the ballot, which is due to take place in Waltham Forest, on the possibility of a HAT being established there, will commence on Saturday.

There is no doubt that HATs are an idea whose time has clearly come. That is not something that is recognised only by the Government. I am glad to say that there are now some members of the Labour party who have begun to see the merits of HATs. Only two weeks ago Liverpool city council—that well-known group of moderates, which has been endorsed regularly in the last few weeks by the right hon. Member for Islwyn (Mr. Kinnock)—asked whether it could open negotiations with the Conservative Government about the possibility of establishing a HAT in Liverpool.

In Hull the HAT is the result not only of the freely expressed wishes of the tenants but also of an initiative taken by the Labour council. The Labour party, in some parts of the country at least, is starting to accept the Conservative philosophy of HATs. We are always ready to welcome new converts.

This morning will be a good opportunity for the hon. Member for Hammersmith (Mr. Soley) to explain what his position, and that of his party on the Opposition Front Bench, is on HATs. The Committee will look forward to that because on this issue, as on so many others, the Labour party's position is extremely confused. I look forward to hearing whether the hon. Gentleman stands by his previously stated commitment to disband HATs, or whether he is beginning to—

Obviously, he seems to be in a muddle. I am sure that the tenants in Hull will be glad to have the clarification that a Labour Government would not continue the housing action trust, regardless of the fact that those tenants voted by a substantial majority—69 per cent. out of a 77 per cent. turnout—in favour of the HAT proposal. They must be disappointed to know that the official Labour spokesman still intends to disband the HAT. Has the hon. Gentleman consulted his Front-Bench colleague, the hon. Member for Kingston upon Hull, East (Mr. Prescott)? In a letter circulated to tenants, he said:

The trust proposals give you the opportunity to make North Hull the flagship estate of the city."

 The hon. Member for Kingston upon Hull, East seems to be a strong supporter of the HAT; I am sorry that both he and the hon. Member for Hammersmith cannot get together to establish a more coherent position on the issue. I am sure that there will he great interest in Hull, Liverpool and elsewhere on the hon. Gentleman's views on HATs.

The HAT area forms part of the North Hull estate. It contains 2,436 dwellings, of which 2,109 are council owned; the remainder are owner occupied, as a result of purchases under the right-to-buy scheme. The area consists almost entirely of unimproved, traditional inter-war semi-detached and terraced houses. Social JPEG 5 and economic problems are markedly worse than the city average. The houses and the environment are in a state of considerable disrepair. The houses lack basic modern facilities.

However, the potential of the area, as Hull's economic base gradually diversfies, is an important factor and the HAT will establish strong links with the Orchard Park and North Hull local economic initiative, which the city and county councils have set up to create new economic activity, jobs and an improved qualtiy of life for local people. The city council saw the HAT as the right vehicle to ensure the regeneration of the estate, before the spiral of decline accelerated out of control. The council took the initiative of producing a feasibility study and of approaching the Government. Between November 1990 and January 1991, we agreed a policy framework with the council, and the council intensified its consultation with residents. Ministers supported the effort by appointing a shadow board for the HAT. That board has embarked on an intensive programme of meetings with tenants, the city council, the county council and other voluntary and statutory bodies. A community planning weekend was sponsored by the shadow HAT from 17 to 21 May to start open discussions on all relevant issues among residents, the city council, the shadow HAT and service providers.

The Government attach special importance to tenant involvement. Consultation with tenants is a key part of any HAT proposal. Although the North Hull tenants and residents association has existed only since January, when it was created in response to the proposal. the chairman and the secretary, who are members of the shadow board, have actively and positively supported the HAT proposals. A major aim of the strategy is to encourage greater tenant participation.

Once the order is approved and the HAT is formally establsihed, local authority housing within the designated area will be transferred to the HAT, on terms to be agreed with the city council. I hope that that can be done by around the end of this year. The order will allow about £50 million to be released to be spent on the homes, in a programme of major physical, social and economic regeneration. I commend both Hull city council and the shadow board of the HAT, under the enthusiastic and energetic chairmanship of David Liggins, for always putting tenants' interests first. The proposals will unquestionably bring enormous benefit to the tenants and to Hull as a whole. It deserves the Committee's full support.

That document clearly stated—indeed a diagram was included to demonstrate how it should be done—that the purpose of HATs was the successful transfer of council properties to the private sector. Tenants were deeply angry at such a surreptitious attempt to push them into the private sector. If the Minister has consulted his hon. When the Hull authority said that it wanted to enter the scheme I had no objections, provided that it could JPEG 7 secure the sort of deal that the then Under-Secretary said that he would consider. I was prepared to go along with that, as long as the tenants voted for it.
