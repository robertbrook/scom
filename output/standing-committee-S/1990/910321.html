DRAFT BUYING AGENCY TRADING FUND ORDER 1991

10.30 a.m.

The Parly Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Mr Tim Yeo)
: I beg to move,

that the Committee has considered the draft Buying Agency trading Fund Order 1991.

The Chairman
: With the agreement of the Committee, it would be sensible to discuss at the same time the other order before us, namely, the draft Property Services Agency Supplies Trading Fund Order 1976 (Revocation) Order 1991.

Mr. Yeo
: Today's business finds us at the end of one process and at the beginning of another—the demise of one organisation, and the rebirth of another. The Committee has two draft orders before it —one to wind up the last trading fund, which still subsists under the Government Trading Funds Act 1973 and a second to establish a new fund under the Government Trading Act 1990. So we must look both backwards and forward.

Both orders are relatively straightforward. The purpose of the first is to wind up the Property Services Agency supplies trading fund. It was originally established 15 years ago at a time when Property Services Agency Supplies had a more substantial role than it has today. The order is required because decisions on the future of the trading fund's component parts have now been taken.

Last summer, we announced that, in the absence of any acceptable bids, the Crown Suppliers' loss-making contract furnishings business—its core business, which employed almost 500 people—would be closed. The Liverpool-based procurement body, the Buying Agency, with just over 100 staff, was to be retained in the public sector because of its potential to earn a return on the costs of providing its service, a service that was of benefit to many Departments.

Discussions continued with bidders for Crown Suppliers Transport, which comprises a vehicle hire fleet and workshops, and for the Interdepartmental Dispatch Service, which served a number of Government offices with mail delivery. However, in November we concluded that the IDS could not be sold on acceptable terms. It will continue to operate in Government in my Department. Last week, we reached and announced decisions on the remaining part, Crown Suppliers Transport. Because the negotiations with the sole bidder failed to reach an acceptable agreement, the Government have decided to close the vehicle hire and workshops organisation during the next four months. Everything possible will be done for the staff, as it has been for contract furnishings staff.

4 Since August, the closure of the contract furnishings business has proceeded smoothly. All the projects that were underway at that time have either been completed or handed over to the customer to complete. All the regional offices and showrooms have closed and the contents have been sold. The stock that was held at the Crown Suppliers' warehouse complex at Milton, in south Oxfordshire, was first reduced by discount offers to Departments, and the remainder was sold by public auction. The five warehouses are now on the market. Most claims from suppliers have been settled, and only a small number of enquiries is outstanding. The Crown Suppliers headquarters building has been sold to Property Holdings, as part of the common user estate, and the Department of Health is currently moving in. Only a small residual team of around 20 people is now needed to finalise the accounts, to clear up the small element of remaining debt and to deal with the remaining staffing issues. I pay tribute to all the staff involved, who have worked constructively towards the long close-down of a part of government that has a great history and tradition.

The Buying Agency meanwhile has continued to develop its separate identity in presentational, management and financial terms. The agency has set up its own finance and computer divisions, and will be investing in a new computer system to suit its more modest requirements as a standalone body.

All that means that, by the end of March, we shall have taken decisions on all the Crown Suppliers businesses, completed the closure of the contract furnishings business, except for residual matters, prepared the IDS for its future in Property Holdings, and started the closure of Crown Suppliers Transport. We have reached the view that the existing organisation known as the Crown Suppliers will, in effect, have ceased trading by then. It is, therefore, expedient to wind up the trading fund. That is the purpose of the first of the orders before us.

The second order, which will be made only after the first has come into effect, is to establish a new trading fund for the Buying Agency. The main task of the Buying Agency is to enable Departments and other public sector customers to obtain a wide range of engineering, building, maintenance and domestic products and services under arrangements that provide quality assurance and value for money, and which comply with public purchasing requirements.

On 17th January 1991, we announced that the agency would become an executive agency of the Department of the Environment under the next steps initiative launched by the Government in February 1988. The aim of that major initiative is to improve management in Government to give greater efficiency and quality of service. The chief executive and staff will have greater responsibility and freedom to get on with their job, within a framework of policy and resources agreed between the Minister and the agency. They will be answerable for the agency's success in achieving its financial aims. In the case of the Buying Agency, those are to increase the volume of goods and services procured while covering the costs of providing that service and generating a small return. Creating a trading fund for the agency, as the second order proposes, would improve the business-like operation of the agency, and its ability to react quickly to its customers' needs. The order takes the normal form: schedule 1 describes the operations to be covered by the fund established by article 3. Article 4 enables the Secretary of State to lend working capital and JPEG 5 article 5 sets a maximum on the amounts of loans to be issued to the fund. Schedule 2 describes the assets and liabilities to be appropriated to the fund; the amounts and values may need to be amended to reflect actuals on 1st April 1991.

A Treasury minute is to be laid before the House shortly, setting out the financial targets for the agency. That will be only one test of success, and will be supplemented by other measures such as the price that the agency can arrange by comparison with the cost to the customer of buying direct from the supplier, the respone time for setting up new contracts, and the quality and delivery performance of the goods and services supplied. Converting the agency to next steps status and providing it with a trading fund is wholly justified to enable it to provide its public sector customers with better quality and value for money. It is not part of any plan to sell the agency. Indeed, the change in its business methods since our announcement that it would remain part of the public service has made privatisation unfeasible.

We hope to be able to launch the agency on 1st May, under a new chief executive and underpinned by a framework document that is still under discussion with the trade unions and within Government. The proposed trading fund is essential to the successful long-term future of the agency in the public sector.

: Obviously, the Minister's speech is the result of the protracted discussion that we had about the privatisation of the PSA and the Crown Suppliers a couple of years ago. At that time, the Opposition pointed out the turmoil that the Government were inviting by ending the operations for the PSA and the Crown Suppliers. The orders represent a further chapter in the story.

Certain matters will still need to be under Government control. The Minister said that different Departments would have the opportunity to buy the domestic furniture and services that they required, instead of that being done centrally by the Crown Suppliers. That system must have been cheaper, however, because fewer people were involved in purchasing. Security is another issue. It was much better when the Crown Suppliers was in charge of the Government car service. Now various Departments will provide their own service. That will be untidy and could often be unsafe because a number of organisations will be involved. The dispatch service, which was one of the services provided by the Crown Suppliers, could be split between various Departments. Security furnishings was another core service provided by the Crown Suppliers but now we are told that each Department will be provided for separately. That change will apply to every item of furnishing, whether it is floor covering or anything else, and workmen will be going in and out of offices to meet the needs of the client Department. That is not satisfactory when there is a greater need than ever for security. Previously, the Crown Suppliers monitored all the workmen entering this building and security was good. It knew the workmen involved and it made arrangements with contractors before their workmen were allowed into any offices or into this building. Will the Minister explain how security will be maintained when the various Departments contract out their work.

The present situation is not new. In 1986 the Government untied the PSA for business arrangements 6 and, for a while, the Crown Suppliers operated on the open market and made profits. The hon. Member for Ealing, Acton (Sir G. Young) was the Minister who dealt with that. He accepted that the Crown Suppliers, operating under the untied arrangements, were efficient and in profit and that further action towards privatisation, or altering the system could not be justified.

There is another side to the issue. The Minister referred to the fact that 500 employees were working in the furniture division of the Crown Suppliers and that they provided a useful and very good service to the Departments. The question of their future must be addressed. I hope that the Minister will tell us in greater detail what will happen to those people. If the Government take their proposed action most of them will lose their jobs.

The Crown Suppliers once provided the core function of carrying out all the purchases and services required by the various Departments. It is a pity that when we already have a good, efficient, well-run service, the Government deny it the opportunity to continue. Presumably, the Government will set up more quangos in each Department because each one will tender for its own services. Their own officers will do that and their own people will monitor the tenders and establish their needs. That used to be done by the Crown Suppliers for all the Departments.

One must doubt the value of setting up a new body. We are told that the old process has ended and a new organisation is being created. One must doubt the value and sense of introducing a new service when the old one has worked so effectively and efficiently, producing the means and obtaining the ends for all Departments. There is no reason to change it. The orders represent sheer Government dogma. The Minister should say why they are necessary.

What of the future of the 500 people employed in the furnishings division? The Minister said that discussions were taking place with the trade unions that represent those people. Before any final decision is taken, the question of how those employees will fare in the future should be resolved.

The Minister should also address the matters relating to security. The Government must ensure that the car service and other relevant services are provided, thus maintaining the fullest possible security needed for Ministers and for hon. Members.

The Crown Suppliers is bound by European Community rules on procurement. Those rules have been the subject of debate on the floor of the House on several occasions. As 8 a Government procurement agency, the Crown Suppliers should observe the European Community and general agreement on trades and tariffs—GATT—requirements that stipulate that all public sector procurement contracts above a certain annual threshold, at present some £92,000, must be open to international tender and be advertised in certain international journals. I first raised that matter on 8th May 1989. I asked the then Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, the hon. Member for Southampton, Itchen (Mr. Chope)

"whether the Crown Suppliers continue to comply with established public procurement procedures and relevant EC/GATT regulations." [ Official Report, 8th May 1989; Vol. 152 c.354] The Minister gave me the dear undertaking, "Yes."

After the reply from the Under-Secretary, I understand that a number of civil servants protested and the Under-Secretary was told that for the previous one or two years, the Department had been breaching EC regulations and that contracts that breached the regulations had been extended. By 16th December 1989, seven months after I asked that question, 120 contracts had been extended. On 6th December 1989, on the Second Reading of the Crown Suppliers Bill, I asked the then Secretary of State for the Environment, the right hon, Member for Bath (Mr. Patten):

"Is it true that the Crown Suppliers has been in breach of European directive No. 77/62 by failing to tender for certain contracts over a value of £92,000 and by extending those contracts? Is it true that the Government's Law Officers have advised the Government that they are in breach of that directive? As I have now raised this matter in the House, which will alert the European Commission, what action will the Government volunteer to take?"

The Secretary of State replied:

"My hon. Friend the Under-Secretary will be delighted comprehensively to answer that question when he replies to the debate, and I am sure that he will do so to the hon. Gentlemens complete satisfaction".—[ Official Report, 6 December; Volume 163 chapter 352.]

In his reply that day the Under-Secretary did not say one word in response to the allegations that I had made. However, on the 11 January, in Committee, the Under-Secretary said:

"Since the Second Reading debate, the internal audit service of the PSA has submitted a report to the permanent secretary that includes comments on the Crown Suppliers' procurement procedures … Given the commercial considerations facing the Crown Suppliers as an organisation that is to be privatised, such is the need to retain suppliers and ensure continuity of production in a period of uncertainty that it seemed that extensions of existing contracts, which had originally been let subject to EC/GATT directives, were appropriate. The internal audit report now being considered by the PSA puts that policy under question. While not disputing its good sense, the report questions whether the two proviso articles to which I have referred can be relied upon as justifying the use of the negotiated procedure for these extensions."—[ Official Report, Standing Committee D, 11 January 1990; chapter 61.]

I was right According to the then Under-Secretary, the internal audit report said that the Crown Suppliers was in breach. He asked about contract extensions by the Crown suppliers, so it may be helpful if I fill in a little of the background. The overall policy on public purchases requires purchasers to base their requirements on value for money—in other words quality and delivery against price. We believe that goods and services should be acquired by competition unless there are compelling reasons to the contrary. Competition avoids any suggestion of favouritism and discourages monopoly. It also helps to promote efficiency and economy.

The form of competition must be appropriate to the value and complexity of goods and services to be acquired. Some contracting authorities are required to be advertised internationally under EC and GATT rules. Even when those rules do not apply, it can be a useful means of ensuring that the potential of the market is fully tested.

The problem concerning the application of EC and GATT requirements in the Crown Suppliers started in the middle of 1988, when negotiated procedures were adopted to extend contracts for six months. The reason for doing this was to extend the expiry dates beyond the then expected date of privatisation, after which different procedures would apply to the letting of contracts. At that point the justification for the policy was said to be the urgency of supply and the unavailability of an alternative supplier. The Crown Suppliers believed that these reasons were sufficient to constitute an exemption from the EC directive.

However, in March 1989, the Treasury advised the Crown Suppliers that extensions to contracts were not recognised under EC and GATT rules. Each had to be considered as a new contract which had to be consistent with the rules. Negotiated agreements could be adopted for technical or artistic reasons, or on the grounds of extreme urgency. Each case would have to be considered on its merits, against these criteria.

During 1989, PSA internal audit working for the Crown Suppliers began to question whether the practice of extending contracts was strictly in accordance with EC and GATT regulations. That led the Crown Suppliers to consult the Department's legal advisers. Their advice was that negotiated procedures were permitted on certain grounds, although difficulties arising from privatisation were not among those grounds. They also pointed out the need for a written report setting out the circumstances of negotiated procedures.

The Crown Suppliers set about preparing that report. I shall not trouble the Committee by going through the detail JPEG 21 of that process. The next stage was further legal advice that none of the considerations set out in that report seemed to be justified under European Community directive.
