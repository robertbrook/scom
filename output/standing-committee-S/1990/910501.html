Mr. Cohen
: That is what I want to discuss Sir John. The hon. Lady adopted a technical approach. The concept of accuracy is only one of the eight data protection principles on which the 1984 Act is founded. The Act is concerned with privacy. I remind the hon. Lady that it stemmed from 10 the Council of Europe convention on the protection of indivuduals in respect of the automatic processing of personal data. The Government signed that convention and thus were committed to passing legislation to protect individuals's privacy.

Registration allows any member of society to discover whether an automated personal data file exists about him or her and, if so, who controls it. In the United Kingdom, that registration is the only public statement by a data user that describes the type of data that are held, how they are used, to whom the contents of the file is likely to be disclosed, how it was gathered, and whether there has been an overseas transfer of data. Registration is a complex procedure, especially for the small businesses that have already been mentioned. The data user has to compile a public document describing what happens to the personal data.

The rationale behind registration is that the processing of personal data by computer can cause invasions of privacy. The public declaration has two purposes—to ensure, that the use of personal data does not get out of hand, and to reassure the public. Charging a fee for such a public document runs counter to those purposes.

An analogy can be drawn with the Register of Members' Interests, in which Members of Parliament make a public declaration of their external interests, such as the directorships that Conservative Members often enjoy. According to the Government's logic, hon. Members should be charged for the entries that they make in that register. Of course, that does not happen because Members of Parliament are considered to be fulfilling a public duty to keep people informed and reassured. However, I believe that the analogy with data registration is exact.

The Government have made it clear that the purpose of the fee is to raise revenue for the Data Protection Registrar. No consideration has been given to the principles of the Data Protection Act 1984. The aim is simply to make the DPR self-financing. My hon. Friend the Member for Kingston-upon-Hull, West was right to refer to the element of inflation in the fee. In 1986, it was set at £22. Last year it was raised to £56, and it will be £75 in 1991. That is an increase of 350 per cent. over five years, which is inflation of Latin American proportions. My hon. Friend was also right to draw attention to the fact that the period of time over which the initial set-up costs of the Data Protection Registrar were to be written off was much too short. The Minister did not answer that point very well.

Why should public-spirited data users, who are obliged to make statements, be penalised for helping the public? If I witnessed a crime and reported it at the local police station, I would not be charged for doing my duty. That example is directly analagous to data users who make statements that help the public. It has never been suggested that the Register of Members' Interests should be self-financing. Members do not pay the salaries of the staff who are responsible for the register. Why should data users have to fund the Data Protection Registrar's office? Compliance with the Data Protection Act 1984 is onerous enough without touching data users for £75 for each entry.
