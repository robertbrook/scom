Some species have become extinct because of major climatic changes. The moose, lynx and tarpan fit into that category. However, other large mammals have become extinct because of man's activities. Man has persecuted and hunted them to extinction. The aurochs, brown bear, beaver, boar and wolf fit into that category.

To put the extinction of those species into context, it is necessary to examine our rural population. In about 1801, the rural population of the British Isles, not including Ireland, was about 8.4 million. In 1911, it had fallen to 5.6 million and in 1990, to 3 million. Those figures are staggering. Much deforestation has taken place since the middle ages, but the process is now being reversed. Throughout the British Isles, including Northern, though not Southern, Ireland, overall woodland cover is 8 per cent and rapidly rising. In the past 20 years there has also been a significant increase in levels of control over organo-chlorine pesticides. The otter is one species that has benefited from that.

The last wolf seen in England was in 1509. Two dates exist for the last wolf killed in Scotland—1701 and 1743. It was killed on the Findhorn, but no one can decide which date is correct. I asked the Library to research into when the last wolf was seen in Ireland and was staggered to find that the date was 1961. I am sure that that date will surprise the Committee, but I have had it confirmed.

One of the aims of the new body will be to embark on creative conservation. What does that term imply? I believe that SNH must examine the possibility of reintroducing several extinct indigenous species. When I first tabled the amendment it was unfairly ridiculed in the media. It has given rise to much correspondence. I have received many letters from responsible biologists and zoologists throughout the country. Dr. D. W. Yalden is an eminent lecturer in the department of zoology at Manchester university. He sent me a copy of his recent paper, "Opportunities for reintroducing British mammals", and provided much detail about the NCC national nature reserve on Rhum, where he carried out research. He pointed out that when the NCC acquired Rhum, it had a population of 1,700 domestic sheep, which were removed, and 1,500 deer. His paper states:

"At that time, the island appeared severely overgrazed, and about 16 per cent of the Deer starved to death each winter…The level of culling was increased to about one sixth of the population each year, that is about 250 deer; even so a further 4 per cent of the Deer still die of starvation each year (some 60 Deer), so that the culling has not eliminated natural mortality; nor is it sufficiently precise to remove all of the weak, or potentially weak, deer."

Dr. Yalden then carefully examined recent studies of wolf predation on the Isle Royale, which is in Quebec, Canada. Most studies show that a wolf requires 3.6 kg of meat daily. If a stag weighs 120 kg JPEG 195 a hind 90 kg and a lactating hind 70 kg, the cull on Rhum would produce about 25,500 kg of meat which would sustain a population of roughly 19 wolves. The paper goes into great detail and is backed up by other research conducted by zoologists and biologists. They make a powerful case for the reintroduction of the wolf on the island of Rhum.

In the past, there has been a great deal of media emotion about the danger that the wolf presents to the human species, and many of those reports have been grossly exaggerated. After all, it is easy for the press to whip up great excitement about the issue. We know that the wolf is a vagrant animal. Recently one wolf in Alaska was found to have travelled 700 miles from its den. That is extraordinary, but it shows that wolves travel and I accept that they can damage livestock.

There are other parts of the world where efforts have been made either to reintroduce the wolf or to sustain a dwindling wolf population. In Yellowstone park in the west of the United States of America, attempts to bring back the wolf met with great resistance. In Sweden, the wolf have been hunted almost to extinction. In Norway, few wolves remain and the last wolf may have been shot in the mid-1980s.

I asked the Library to conduct research into wolves in Scandinavia and, above all, to investigate the degree of emotion that can be generated by the publicity surrounding that species. An article headed "Wolf fever in Norway" cites as an example a wolf that was spotted in the county of Adger, which is about 200 miles south-west of Oslo and is an important livestock area. That particular wolf was suspected of killing sheep. A great deal of publicity ensued and within months the 1973 royal resolution of the Norwegian Crown, which afforded protection for wolves, was amended so that the animal could be shot.

Conservationists kicked up such a tremendous fuss that the matter was debated at length in Cabinet, but even though the animal was one of the very few wolves left, a death sentence was passed on it and a £2,000 bounty placed on its head. On 11 January 1984, the wolf was shot. the article states:

"The unofficial hunt leader, Lars Saga, had himself photographed pouring champagne over the corpse in his sitting-room. He then drove it to the parliament steps in Oslo where Adger MPS "interviewed" it for TV and radio as part of a media extravaganza that lasted for three days."

I hope that similar events will not be repeated here, but that example illustrates the public hype and emotion that can be stirred up.

The evidence must be examined carefully. It points overwhelmingly to the fact that the wolf is not an animal that will attack human beings. It is a shy animal and, except when provoked or wounded, it generates no danger. I urge the Minister carefully to consider the idea of a pilot project on the isle of Rhum or to instruct SNH to carry our further research to complement that conducted by Dr. Yalden.
