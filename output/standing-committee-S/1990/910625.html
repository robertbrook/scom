legislation Draft Building Societies Act 1986 (Continuance of Section 41) Order 1991

10.30 am

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. John Maples): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Building Societies Act 1986 (Continuance of Section 41) Order 1991.

When the Building Societies Act 1986 came into force, the Building Societies Commission was given a variety of powers to regulate the affairs of building societies. The Commission can revoke a licence, impose conditions, or instruct a society to seek a renewal of its authorisation by applying for a licence, as if the society did not already have one. Not all building societies were required to renew their authorisation when the Act came into effect, because that was felt to be an unnecessarily cumbersome and expensive procedure. However, being able to instruct a society to seek a renewal of its authorisation was felt to be a valuable transitional and regulatory provision.

That power, which was originally given in section 41, was to last for five years once the Act came into effect. The commission found—and we agree—that that was a valuable regulatory power, enabling it to act in circumstances in which it would not want to take the high-profile step of revoking a licence or imposing conditions, but in which it would be appropriate to require a building society to take a serious look at the way that it runs its affairs and to deal with any weaknesses. The order will renew the effect of that section for a further five years.

Image Col. 11 As to the matter now before us, I deny that there was a mistake and if there was, I did not make it—so I have nothing to be sorry about.

My hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield asked in which circumstances section 41 might be used. Such circumstances might be when there had been a substantial change in the nature of a building society's business and the commission did not feel that its directors and management had taken account of that—or felt it necessary to focus the attention of the board of directors on the way in which the society was being run.

Re-application for a licence is an elaborate procedure, but not as dastardly as revoking a licence—nor is it of such a high profile as the imposition of conditions would be. For example, I believe that a condition was imposed on one building society that it should appoint a finance director.

If there was a specific weakness, the commission might impose a condition to correct that weakness. If it believed that there was not a specific and imminent danger but a more general problem on which the directors' attention should be focused, section 41 might be invoked. Its use is not as drastic as revocation of a licence or the imposition of conditions, but it could be applied if it were thought that something needed to be done to strengthen management and procedures for financial control, to ensure that there was no danger to the society's funds in the long term.

I was asked why the provision was for only five years in the first instance. Two thoughts were behind that. First, it was felt that might be a useful power for the commisson to have. Secondly, when the Banking Acts were introduced, all banks were obliged to reapply for licences but, after the Building Societies Acts, they were all grandfathered and it was thought to be unnecessarily cumbersome to make all of them go through the reapplication procedure.

However, the Government thought that the commission should have an essential power to instruct a society to observe that procedure if it considered that necessary. It has been used only once, but there are more than 100 building societies—most of which were, I believe, in existence before 1986. Potentially, therefore, there are 100 building societies to which the provision could be relevant. From the viewpoint of transition, and of it being a regulatory power, it would be valuable to continue it.

I cannot be as ingenious as my hon. Friends the Members for Beaconsfield and for Eltham in keeping within order a refutation of their arguments on clause 50. Therefore I shall not strain your patience, Mr. Knox, by trying too hard. We had an extensive debate at that time, and all the issues that anyone would have wanted to raise were raised. It is possible that the subject will be raised on Report —although I hope not.

I ask my hon. Friend the Member for Eltham to reflect on two points. First, perhps if he had read the court judgments, he would not have concluded that the courts had held that the provision meant double taxation. Judge Nolan, in the court of first instance, reached that finding, but the Court of Appeal comprehensively reversed it, saying that there was no element of double taxation. The only reference to it was in the House of Lords, which decided four to one, on a technicality, that the Government had power to impose the regulations, but did not have power to tax the receipts in the gap period at 1985–86 tax rates, and that they could do so only at 1986–87 and 1987–88 rates.

Col. 12 Having read all the House of Lords judgments several times, I conclude only that the decision turned on a technical point, and that there were no moral strictures on either party from either the Court of Appeal or the House of Lords. Even Lord Lowry, who delivered the dissenting judgment, said that he was not impressed by the arguments about double taxation or inequity.

On the point about retrospection, it is common practice that where it is discovered that the tax law does not have the effect that the Government and everyone else thought it had, it is appropriate to restore the position to where it was. That is what we are doing. The debates at Report stage of the 1986 Finance Bill make clear what was intended.

I shall conclude, so that my hon. Friend the Member for Eltham may speak again, if he wishes. The 1985 Inland Revenue press release could not possibly be taken to mean anything other than the situation that I described. The fact that increased revenue of £250 million was not reflected in the Red Book or elsewhere means that there was no increased revenue. My hon. Friend will no doubt pursue that point himself if he wishes to do so.

In 1987–87, the Inland Revenue received £76 million less in tax receipts from building societies that it would have received had the old system continued, because a series of changes were made—only one of which was the bringing into tax of the gap period. Another change based the tax on interest payments to building society investors, rather than on interest accruals to them. The effect was that they conterbalanced each other in the aggregate, with the result that we received £76 million less revenue than if the old system had continued. It is not surprising that the Red Book did not reflect an increase in forecast revenue—because what resulted was a decrease. I do not think that the substance of the order is in dispute, and I commend it to the Committee.

Friend the Economic Secretary could clear up many problems by publishing any working papers shown to Ministers in 1985, at the time of the Budget, which showed some of the effects that I described and explicitly demonstrated that at the time that Parliament was told about the change—of no additional revenue—Ministers knew what was proposed.

I am willing to wager a "I am sorry. I was wrong", admission if my hon. Friend the Minister is prepared to do the same. That would be a way of clearing up the problem. If there is a working paper showing a clear intention to opt Image Col. 13 for transitional arrangements to draw in an extra £250 million of tax in March and June 1985, we would be left only with the question of whether Ministers misled, or were not open with, Parliament. At the moment however, there is the additional question of whether Ministers themselves knew that £250 million was at stake.

My hon. Friend the Economic Secretary is a reasonable person, as I am. I am sometimes described as a semi-establishment man. I try not to cause rows—especially with my own side. I am, however, willing to do what Members of Parliament are supposed to do—to ask whether building society commissioners and the Inland Revenue have specific powers. Most building society directors might wish to take issue with what my hon. Friend the Economic Secretary has said—just as legal advisers did in an unprecedented letter to The Times. The Court of Appeal decided that the Inland Revenue had the power to opt for additional taxation. There was no question of whether it was right or wrong that it should do so, but whether the retrospective changes to the Finance Act 1986 legally gave the Revenue power to do so. I agree that the House of Lords decided that the retrospective changes to legislation allowed the Inland Revenue to have a go, but that attempt could not succeed because there was no effective tax rate. I do not know whether it is right to call that a technicality.

I must stress that the Court of Appeal and the House of Lords were not asked to decide whether it was right or wrong, in a non-legal sense, to try to collect extra taxation for a year in which the Inland Revenue, on the admission of Treasury Ministers, had been satisfied that the full tax liabilities of building societies had been fulfilled. That is the crucial issue.

I want to pose eight simple questions for the commission and the building societies to consider—assuming that this order is passed. The first is, when was tax last paid under the old system? The answer is 1 January 1986. The source for that information is the Treasury.

Question two is, what period of liability did the payment satisfy? The answer—again, according to the Treasury—is the Inland Revenue tax year 1985–86. The Treasury confirmed that covered the societies up to March 1986.

Thirdly, what should the Chancellor have told Parliament in the 1985 Budget? He should have confirmed what my hon. Friend the Financial Secretary told us in a parliamentary answer yesterday—that revenue liability had been satisfied.

The fourth question is, what did the then Chancellor say? He said that there would be no extra revenue, yet we know now that £250 million of extra revenue was generated by the transitional changes. The source of the Chancellor's statement is Hansard: The source for what he did not say is the press notice, and the Committee debates of June 1985 and others.

The fifth question is, if extra tax was brought into the tax year 1985–86—that is taxed but not paid for that year—is that extra revenue, that extra tax, double taxation? I use those words interchangeably. The answer is clearly yes—to the amount of £250 million of extra tax —which many, including some judges, call £250 million of double tax. The source of that opinion is judges and counsel, as the transcript will show. I am still waiting for a parliamentary Col. 14 answer about that. Building society advisers and directors are another source of that opinion—and common sense too.

Question six is: how can the Revenue claim extra revenue or tax for part of the tax year 1985–86? The retrospective change in the Finance Act 1986 allows the Revenue to have a go at making such a claim, but the judgment of the courts is that Parliament authorised double taxation and the extra revenue. However, in 1985 the question was not put to Parliament in that way. The 1986 debates were inconclusive, and left the issue to the courts.

Question seven is, what should the Government do, or say now? I have already suggested that no personal mistakes were involved, and that I appreciate the position of my hon. Friend the Economic Secretary. However, he could say, "A mistake was made. We are sorry. Let us talk."

My final question is, need other societies have joined in the application for judicial review? The answer, judging by the Law Officers' comments and a written answer that I was given two months ago, is no. It is different from a clever tax dodge. It is an open application for judicial review to clarify the law. That was made clear during the Report stage of the 1986 Finance Bill.

The Government should get away from the one good point that my hon. Friend makes —that not one pound has been identified as having been taxed twice, and ought to return to the solid point that the situation was not made clear to Parliament. The case of the Leeds Permanent building society, for example, was included in the debate on 17 July 1986. It should not lose its £57 million.

Whether or not clause 50 is enacted, it would be wrong for building society directors to let the matter drop—and for the commission to let them drop it. There are few occasions on which I feel strongly about straight dealing by the Government. Normally, Governments can be accused of straight dealing, even when it hurts. On this occasion, because of the passage of time the changes in the Ministers involved, Parliament should go back to the beginning and say to the Government, "If you want an extra £250 million, or an extra £180 million,"— equivalent to between 1½ per cent. and 1 per cent. of building societies' receipts since 1985–86—"consult Parliament from the beginning." Let us scrap the sorry business of legislating year after year, and of having judicial reviews year after year, and let both the Building Societies Commission and society directors carry out their duties. Parliament should give them an open playing field, which enables them to understand what is going on."

To call £250 million

"some effect on the public sector borrowing requirement"

is the most clouded curtain-pulling that I have seen in my 16 years in Parliament.
