Draft Civic Government (Scotland) Act 1982 (Licensing of Houses in Multiple Occupation) Order 1991

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Civic Government (Scotland) Act 1982 (Licensing Houses in Multiple Occupation) Order 1991.

Despite the length of its title, the order is short—but because the background to it is rather complex, and the processes that led to it have involved a good deal of time and consultation, the Committee may find it useful if I go into a little detail.

As hon. Members will know, houses in multiple occupation are found in a number of Scottish towns and cities, but especially in cities and areas where there are many single people, often young people, who need accommodation. University and college students are among the largest group in that category, and HMOs are prevalent for that reason in the west end of Glasgow, Edinburgh, and Stirling.

The existence of HMOs in large numbers, and the fact that landlords find it worth their while to provide them, clearly indicate that they are giving a service for which there is a real demand, and it is not our intention to interfere, or to prevent that demand being met. However, in recent years there have been increasing indications that many HMOs are falling below—sometimes, well below—proper standards, and we are concerned that local authorities should be enabled to tackle that problem effectively.

Dr. Norman A. Godman
(Greenock and Port Glasgow)
: Will the Minister say whether the definition of a house in multiple occupation would include, for example, a large Victorian house in the west end of Greenock that has been divided into four or five flats by professional builders?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: I will give the hon. Gentleman a specific answer later, when my remarks will cover the subject of definitions.

A consultative paper issued by the then Scottish Development Department in July 1988 set out the problem in some detail. The kind of HMO which creates a real problem, it suggested, was typically a property.

"let by an absentee landlord, on something less than a legal tenancy if that can be obtained, to a large number of people who have no connection with each other."

Bad HMOs have a number of features that make them unsatisfactory, or worse, to their occupants, neighbours, and society. Such properties often charge much more than the service that they provide is worth. They have few, or no, places for occupants to wash themselves or their clothes; or inadequate cooking facilities, or none at all; and provide insufficient heating, lighting and hot water—and may overcharge for what is supplied by using high-tariff meters.

4 Such properties are badly furnished and overcrowded, seasonally or permanently; are poorly maintained, repaired and decorated; and annoy their neighbours by being the source of noise and other nuisances. To sum up, common failings are the lack of washing and cooking facilities; inadequate heating, lighting and furnishing; and poor maintenance and overcrowding.

Local authorities have powers to control HMOs under various Acts, going back to the Public Health (Scotland) Act 1987. The largest and most important group of such powers is in Part VIII of the Housing (Scotland) Act 1987, and the 1964 regulations for the management of houses and buildings in multiple occupation, which enable local authorities to register HMOs in their areas, obtain information about them, see that work is carried out to keep them in proper repair, and, if all else fails, to take over the management of an HMO.

As we wanted to know how much use local authorities made of their powers, and whether they thought that they could be improved, in 1988 we asked all those in Scotland to make returns showing to what extent they made of their Part VIII powers. We followed that up with a consultation paper setting out the problem in detail and inviting comments.

The results showed that authorities found Part VIII powers rather difficult to use; that they would welcome action by the Government to draw up and to issue guidance on the various Acts and regulations that apply to HMOs, so as to set out clearly local authority powers and duties; and that they were generally strongly in favour of a scheme that would require the operator of an HMO to be licenced.

Dr. Godman
: The Minister mentioned local authorities' powers. What discussions have taken place recently between the Scottish Office and officials at the Department of Social Security headquarters in Lady Lawson Street, Edinburgh, on the role played by the DSS, where that organisation pays the rates of tenants living in houses in multiple occupation?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: We are in touch with the DSS about that. Key representations were made by local authorities and interested groups. I cannot give the hon. Gentleman a snap answer to his question because it requires some thought, but if the houses concerned were separate, they would not come under the order. A conversion into separate flats, none of which is occupied by a number of persons in excess of the qualifying number, would not be covered.

Mr. Donald Dewar:
(Glasgow, Garscadden)
: I hope that the Minister can say something about the specific test that will be applied before granting a licence. He gave an impressive list of disasters that could befall neighbours and tenants if houses in multiple occupation are not regulated properly. What will that test be?

The order's definition of a house in multiple occupation is

"the only or principal residence of more than 4 persons".

Glasgow, which has more than 50 per cent. of the houses in multiple occupation in the whole of Scotland, reckons that 60 per cent. of its HMO stock will be excluded from the licensing scheme by the cut-off. Glasgow is unhappy about that, and I hope that the Minister will deal with that matter at length.

JPEG 5

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: The order specifies that a house in multiple occupation must be occupied by more than four persons, and that those persons must be

"not all members either of the same family or of one or other of 2 families."

The reason for those numbers is to form a commonsense definition of an HMO. We did not want it to cover a home occupied by a single family of five or more persons, a family home where a lodger had been taken in, or a flat occupied by a group of three or four students. The definition adopted excludes a home occupied by a single family, because all the occupants are members of the same family; a lodger, even where the total number of occupants is more than four, because they are members of no more than two families; and the student flat, because even though the occupants are from more than two families, their total number is fewer than five.

That is a sensible approach to the question of numbers. We considered carefully the responses to our consultations, and found that Glasgow's view differed from that of many other authorities, which suggested a different limit. We will review the numbers limit in the light of experience, but I am happy to give an undertaking that if the order is approved, and if the numbers undermine seriously the licensing power's effectiveness, we shall consider amending it.

Mr. Dewar
: My hon. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Cathcart (Mr. Maxton) will press the Minister later on the issue, but I am intervening now because I have been approached by people whose views I respect.

I recognise the differing opinions that exist among local authorities. I have spoken also to Edinburgh district council, which takes a different attitude. Does the Minister dispute Glasgow district council's claim that 60 per cent. of properties that it defines as HMO stock will be excluded from the licensing scheme that the order will introduce?

There can be no dispute that when a family of six, seven or 12 members live together, that must be classed as multiple occupation. However, there is a danger that bad landlords may buy smaller flats and pop in four students or single people to avoid licensing. That would push the problem further down the range of housing in a dangerous and unacceptable way.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: I do not dispute the hon. Gentleman's figures. On a commonsense basis, we are uncertain whether the problem relates to the properties that he cited or to larger properties in multiple occupation, where we think that the worst abuses have been committed. If we find evidence of abuses in future, notwithstanding the terms of the order, we shall consider amending the order as appropriate. At present, the order is the sensible way forward and covers the majority of cases that have been highlighted in the past.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: The answer to both questions is yes. The district council has that discretion, and the order will provide it with an additional power.

Mr. Dewar
: I shall not intervene much more, but I want the Minister to say a word or two about the interaction between the licensing system and planning control. I understand that certain requirements are made under the planning Acts that local authorities enforce.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: The order does not impair the power of local authorities to act; it is additional to their existing powers. Therefore, no problem arises in that regard.

While we do not at present have a legislative opportunity to revise the statutes that apply to HMOs, we have taken steps to deal with other important points. For example, a code of guidance setting out the variety of powers and duties that are available to local authorities is being drafted, which—should the order be approved, —would give guidance on the use on the new powers. That guidance will be ready for issue shortly, at about the time that the order comes into effect. The order will increase the powers and effectiveness of local authorities in dealing with HMOs, for they will be able to impose conditions that must be met before a licence is issued. If a landlord does not comply with those conditions, he would be committing an offence.

When the matter first arose three years ago, in the context of the discussions on the Bill that led to the 1987 Act, I was asked why so few authorities in Scotland exercise those powers. We now know the answer, because of our extensive consultation.

Replies from authorities to our 1988 paper highlighted two matters from a number that I have already mentioned. First, local authorities were certain that there would be a universal welcome for the issuing of a code of guidance on HMOs that draws together all the powers and duties of local authorities. Secondly, they left us in no doubt that the licensing of HMOs would be widely welcomed. So we are acting on both issues. I regard the publication of a code of guidance—which, as I said, we debated several years ago—as particularly important.

Dr. Godman
: May we obtain copies as soon as possible? Where there is a growth in the number of houses in multiple occupation, there is often a deterioration in the neighbourhood. That has a dismal effect on many of the decent people living in such localities. That is why I ask for a copy of the code.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: As soon as the order comes into force, we intend that the code of guidance should also come into operation. That should happen very quickly—within weeks. I shall ensure that each member of the Committee receives a copy.

I shall deal with the contents of the order and the point of the hon. Member for Glasgow, Garscadden (Mr. Dewar) about testing. The order adds the use of a house in multiple occupation to the activities for which a licence may be required under part II of the Civic Government (Scotland) Act 1982. That means that, where a local authority resolves to make a licensing scheme, every person wishing to carry on that activity must have a licence. He or she must comply with any reasonable conditions that the authority attaches to that licence. Such conditions are a matter for the authority concerned. He or she must give JPEG 7 access to local government officers and provide relevant information on request, and will be liable to a penalty if there is failure to meet those requirements.

Licensing involves two important requirements. First, the licensee must observe all reasonable conditions. Secondly, he or she must be a fit and proper person. That means, a person who is fit and proper to carry out the terms of the licence. That general term was used in the 1982 Act.

Mr. Dewar
: I am intrigued by the suggestion that a fit and proper person is a fit and proper person to operate such premises. Do the Government envisage that there will be a check with the police to see whether potential licence holders have previous convictions? Will there be other ways of checking applicants' records? The obvious method would be to look back through the licensing records, to ascertain whether there had been trouble with previous licences.

Presumably, I could have three or four people staying with me, and I would not have to apply for a licence. If at some time I decided that was nonsense and shoved someone else into the spare box room, I would then become licensable, and would commit an offence if I do not apply for a licence. Is it a self-policing system, with the landlord having to bear that in mind in deciding his occupancy policy?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: Someone who has a criminal record and engages in criminal activities, would be excluded. Similarly, if someone had a drug or alcohol problem, he or she might not be classified as a fit and proper person. It is a matter of common sense and evidence. I do not envisage witch hunts, but practical, commonsense measures can be taken where necessary.

The order defines the terms "house" and "house in multiple occupation" for the purposes of licensing. The definition that we have chosen of a "house" is one that we think should cover all the categories of properties within which a house in multiple occupation may fall. As hon. Members may be aware, we considered an alternative form of words, on which comments were invited when we consulted about the content of the draft order in October last year. However, although it was closer to the current definition in part VIII of the 1987 Act, it gave rise to certain unwanted consequences. In particular, there were certain circumstances in which it could have applied to houses in a tenement occupied by a single family. Therefore, we decided in favour of the present wording.

For the purposes of the order, we had to take a view on what constituted the sort of house in multiple occupation that it was desired to catch. At one end of the scale, no one doubts that the landlord of a "17 doorbell" size of HMO should be liable to be caught by licensing; at the other, it seemed unreasonable that it should apply to a single family that takes in a lodger. Therefore, we decided that on commonsense grounds the qualifying level should be a house that was the "only or principal residence" of more than four people who were not all members of one family or of one or other of two families.

I know from the responses to consultations that some would have preferred a lower number than four, or no qualifying number at all, to be specified. To that I would say simply the order is directed at houses in multiple occupation, and not at the rented sector generally, and that we think it only reasonable to include a working definition 8 of "multiple occupation". But we have made it clear that, if the experience of local authorities that operate schemes suggests that a differenct level would be appropriate, we shall consider it.

Finally, the order excludes from the conditions that a licensing authority may impose on that grant of a licence any condition concerning rents or other charges. Controls on rents, and the relationship of rent control to housing benefit, are regulated separately under the rent and housing Acts, and it would not be appropriate to bring them into a system aimed at regulating the physical condition of premises. This order meets a real demand, and it will usefully strengthen the hand of authorities that have to deal with houses of poor standard providing a low level of accommodation, at what is frequently a high price.

JPEG 15 The question was raised of whether a local authority will have the power to enter premises where it believes that activities requiring a licence are being undertaken without one. Under the 1982 Act, a licence will not be renewed or granted to a landlord if he has had a licence revoked within a specified previous period, or withdrawn by a court. The general condition that a licence should be granted only to a "fit and proper" person gives the local authority broad powers to determine whether a landlord's previous record makes him unsuitable.

I was asked about harrassment. That is a criminal offence, not simply a civil matter. It is dealt with fully in the 1982 Act. With regard to statistics on HMOs, we do not have the actual numbers, but we believe that the April 1991 census will provide further useful information.

Let me comment on the protection of the mentally handicapped and vulnerable people. Under the new community care arrangements, social work authorities will be responsible for providing a care plan for individuals and for liaising with housing authorities where there is a housing need.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
