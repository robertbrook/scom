FINANCIAL SERVICES ACT 1986 (EXTENSION OF SCOPE OF ACT) ORDER 1991

The Minister for Corporate Affairs (Mr. John Redwood)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Financial Services Act 1986 (Extension of Scope of Act) Order 1991.

The Chairman
: With the agreement of the Committee, it will be convenient to discuss at the same time the other order before us, namely the draft Financial Services Act 1986 (Delegation) (No. 2) Order 1991.

Mr. Redwood
: The purpose of the draft Financial Services Act 1986 (Delegation) (No. 2) Order 1991—a catchy title, as always—is to transfer to the Securities and Investments Board the power to regulate investment and borrowing for certain types of authorised unit trusts.

The power to make regulations dealing with the constitution and management of authorised unit trusts was transferred to the SIB in 1988. The Secretary of State retained the power—under section 81(2)(d)—to make regulations governing investment and borrowing powers.

In September 1990, I announced that the SIB would draft new, updated investment and borrowing powers regulations.

The SIB has consulted extensively. Copies of its final proposals, which were published last month, are available in the Library. I hope that the hon. Members have seen those. The SIB's draft regulations cover pricing and scheme particulars as well as investment and borrowing powers. They include detailed guidance on the investment objectives of the investment limits imposed on each category of fund. Funds will have more scope to employ efficient portfolio management and stocklending techniques as a result of these proposals.

The categories of scheme subject to the delegation order include all the current categories of funds, and four new categories of funds; futures and option funds, geared futures and options funds, property funds and warrant funds.

I welcome the new categories of funds which will increase customer choice and enable managers to utilise the sophisticated facilities now available in the City of London to the benefit of a wider range of investors. The SIB has set out extensive investor protection requirements.

The draft delegation order is submitted under section 114 of the 1986 Act. The Secretary of State is satisfied that the SIB is able and willing to discharge the functions being transferred and that its detailed proposals will afford investors adequate protection. The Secretary of State, in accordance with sections 121 and 122 of the Act, following a report by the Director-General of Fair Trading, is satisfied that there are no significant competition problems.

4 The Financial Services Act 1986 (Extension of Scope of Act) Order 1991 extends the provisions of the Financial Services Act 1986 to building society deferred shares.

Most building society shares are similar to interest bearing savings deposits and are repayable to the investors by the Society on demand or when the requirements are satisfied. Because such shares are deposits rather than risk bearing equity they are not regarded as investments under the Financial Services Act 1986. Investors are largely protected from the risk that they will not get their money back by the Building Societies Act 1986, and the attendant investor protection scheme.

Deferred shares, in contrast, are a special type of building society share, originally used to capitalise new societies. The concept has now been extended to allow established societies to strengthen their capital position in the more competitive and risky market conditions that now exist and to give extra protection to their ordinary shareholders.

Such deferred shares will to all intents and purposes be risk bearing equity share capital but will not usually be repayable on demand. Investors in them and all other building society deferred shares should have protection under the Financial Services Act 1986.

The effect of the order will be that anyone carrying on an investment business in building society deferred shares, unless an exempt person, must be authorised by the Securities and Investments Board, a self-regulatory body or a recognised professional body and will be subject to rules made under the Financial Services Act 1986.

The orders offer welcome additional choice for customers and scope for financial businesses. I commend them to the Committee.

(Redcar)
: In relation to the first statutory instrument, I should like to emphasise one of the Minister's points. The order transfers to the Securities and Investments Board the powers to make regulations governing investing and borrowing for the specific instruments that already exist for authorised unit trusts. Security funds, money marketing funds, feeder funds and umbrella funds are already in the domain. The order provides for additional funds which will be granted authorised unit trust status. We are concerned about geared futures and options funds—the GFOFs—and warrant funds.

I should like to outline our concern. The Minister said that extensive investor protection would be provided when the additional funds attain unit trust authorised status. We do not accept that; we believe that the new instruments will mean additional risks for investors, primarily because of the way that the Minister has introduced them. We are concerned not merely because GFOFs and warrant funds achieve unit trust status but because they have not been meshed at the same time with the marketing rules that the Life Assurance and Unit Regulatory Organisation is introducing. The instruments and the marketing mechanisms do not mesh.

There is a distinct possibility that GFOFs and warrant funds can not only be sold in the retail market on the same basis as conventional units trusts but could be damaging to small investors who inadvertently take an imprudent risk, not understanding the higher volatility and risk associated with GFOFs. We are equally worried that the instruments JPEG 5 will create a contagious difficulty for the unit trust industry. Difficulties with GFOFs, because of their higher volatility, will reflect badly on the unit trust industry. Those are our concerns about two of the proposed instruments.

In addition, we do not understand why the Minister has introduced GFOFs and warrant funds under the collective instrument of unit trusts. Why do they have to be treated as unit trusts? Unit trusts diversify risks and have a very good public image. Why has not the Minister created GFOFs and warrant funds as separate entities, perhaps bracketing them with investment trusts? In some ways, that would be much better. The closest that we come to understanding why the Minister has done that is by looking at the SIB consultative document No. 49. The funds are at present offshore and unregulated. Why not bracket them with other investment trusts outside the unit trust industry?

Paragraph 9 of the consultative document asks whether the unit trust is an appropriate vehicle for investment in derivatives. It poses the question clearly, and states that some concern was expressed in response to CP 37—an earlier SIB consultative document—about the appropriateness of the authorised unit trust regime as a vehicle for derivatives investment. It went on to the state that some respondents argued that futures and options could be highly volatile in price so that unit trusts investing in such instruments could be perceived as high risk, a perception which could be damaging to the reputation of the established unit trust industry. We share that worry.

The document stated that particular concern was expressed over the possibility of a futures and options unit trust incurring liabilities in excess of its assets and investors possibly losing all their investment, and that other respondents to the consultative paper argued that the unit trust was not the ideal vehicle for futures and options funds, but for a different reason—that closed-end funds or open-ended investment funds would be more appropriate vehicles for collective investments in these instruments.

Here is the crux of why it is going into unit trusts and not some other corporate vehicle for futures and options: the SIB believes that the arguments for the development for new corporate vehicles have considerable weight and deserve careful exploration. The SIB sympathises with the Opposition's argument. It goes on to say that at present the only vehicle available that offers a significant tax advantage for collective investment is the authorised unit trust. The SIB accordingly has proceeded to address the question of how this vehicle might best be adapted to enable new products to be developed.

The Opposition go slightly further than that. It is not only a question of the present tax structure. The collective vehicles that are available are not regulated in the Financial Services Act 1986. If the Minister does not want to acknowledge the extent of the problems and confusion created by the Financial Services Act 1986, he must either stay with the unregulated offshore use of GFOFs or adopt his present option, which is the unit trust format. There is a middle road, but partly because of Inland Revenue pressure and because of the dog's breakfast of the Financial Services Act 1986, he has not been able to adopt it. We are now faced with a situation that will create great difficulty for the unit trust industry.

The first problem was the risk to the investor; the second was the status of GFOFs and warrant funds within unit trusts because of the confusion created by the Government. The third problem arises from the way in which 6 negotiations have been carried out between Life Assurance and Unit Trust Regulatory Organisation, the SIB and the Department of Trade and Industry. They have not been coordinated. That resulted in the ridiculous situation of the SIB having two consultative documents, Nos. 37 and 49, and LAUTRO starting its 10 days consultation on the marketing process on 19 April, while the SIB did not print its final proposals until 1 May 1991. There was one day on which the industry had both the final proposals in document form and the market mechanisms to apply them. When the SIB and LAUTRO clearly need to interface, how can they agree to particular instruments without knowing the market regulations that will be applied? They had one day in which to do it. We accept that LAUTRO is still in discussion. Yesterday afternoon and evening LAUTRO was discussing the regulations. I understand that on the marketing side LAUTRO will be able to continue to do that until June. To suggest that there has been a competent process of negotiation by the Minister and the Department of Trade and Industry is to go too far.

JPEG 7 There is no reason why the instruments must involve the unit trust format. Other vehicles can be used which would not have a negative effect on the future and options derivatives industry and would be a plus for it as we approach 1992 and beyond.

There are four matters on which I should like the Minister to expatiate. The first is the initial outlay of the GFOFs. As I understand it, the proportion of GFOF as initial outlay, which is the amount that can be invested in margins on future contracts or on premiums on options, was initially set by SIB at 10 per cent. That means that a minimum of 90 per cent. of the fund would be available for direct investment, securities, liquid assets and so on. The final resolution is 20 per cent. In France, Luxembourg and America, initial outlay margins are higher. It will not be enough for the Minister to point that out in his response. What worries me is not solely the raising of the limit of the initial outlay margin to 20 per cent. but doing that at the same time as removing the rules on diversification. The combination of the two will cause problems with the introduction of the GFOFs. If the limit is raised to 20 per cent. and there are no limits on diversification, volatility will increase and present difficulties for the investor. As a result, the investor will be more exposed to individual incompetence or malice. The GFOFs market regime—that is, the LAUTRO rules that are still under discussion—should take that into account.

In the SIB's initial CP 49 document, which is what the industry thought it was going to receive before the final document was introduced in May and which was considered for the market rules, it suggested that no more than 2 per cent. of the total value of the fund should be devoted to initial outlay for any single derivative contract and that no more than 4 per cent. of the total value of the fund should be devoted to any single category of derivative contract. That could be shares, commodities, or anything like that. There were limits on diversification which provided a balance against the initial outlay.

In the final document that we were given on 1 May, the SIB states that regulations have removed these diversification rules entirely, replacing them with a simple requirement that the fund manager should ensure that investment in derivatives is suitably diversified.

The third aspect on which I wish to focus is commodities. The SIB regulations will permit the geared futures and options funds to invest in derivatives based on the commodities market—cocoa futures, for example. It would be useful for both right hon. and hon. Members and the unit trust industry if the Minister clarified the position with regard to liability in the event of a GFOF insolvency. The SIB regulations state that in no circumstances will an investor in a GFOF stand to lose more than his or her initial investment. The difficulty is that the gearing effect of trading on the margin means that price movements in the underlying asset have a disproportionate effect, much like the gearing problems with the poll tax. Variation in the underlying price could lead to the investor owing more on a derivative contract than his or her initial outlay. In extremis, the whole fund could cost more than the initial outlay.

Who is responsible for the GFOF's liabilities in such circumstances? I understand that the SIB has decided against setting up a special fund to cover eventual insolvency, remote as the Minister will tell us that such a possibility is. Are the trustees or the fund manager responsible, or is there a joint indemnity between them? Who will pick up the pieces in the event of insolvency?
