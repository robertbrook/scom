Draft Fisheries (Amendment) (Northern Ireland) Order 1991

10.30 a.m.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Northern Ireland (Mr. Jeremy Hanley)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Fisheries (Amendment) (Northern Ireland) Order 1991.

The Order amends a number of the provisions of the Fisheries (Northern Ireland) Act 1966, which I will refer to as the 1966 Act, some of the equivalent provisions in the Foyle Fisheries (Northern Ireland) Act 1952 and one provision of the Diseases of Fish (Northern Ireland) Act 1967.

The 1966 Act was last amended in 1983 when, following the report of a committee of inquiry into angling in Northern Ireland, known as the Black report, the Fisheries Conservancy Board was restructured and enlarged. When the restructuring was completed the Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food and the new board worked very closely in identifying all the amendments that were required to the 1966 Act and this formed the basis of the draft order published in 1988.

The order differs in a number of respects from the proposal which was issued for consultation to take account of points that have been made during the process. Most of the comments received were generally favourable, but there were valid concerns raised and the Government has endeavoured very hard to accommodate these as far as possible.

I shall briefly explain the main provisions of the Order. Article 4 extends the existing powers of the Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food to develop derelict waters for angling—that is to say, waters where it is impossible to establish or to trace the owners of the fishing rights. The article provides that such waters may be developed either by the Department or by other organisations, for example, angling clubs, under schemes approved by the Department.

New provisions are included under article 6 relating to fish farming and the procedures to be adopted on receipt of applications for fish culture licences following representations made during the consultation stage that all applications for fish culture licences should be published and that there should be mandatory public inquiries to hear objections. The public inquiries will be held by the Water Appeals Commission for Northern Ireland.

The new provisions in article 11 will enable the Fisheries Conservancy Board to restock waters where pollution has caused fish to be killed and to recover the cost of restocking from the person convicted under the Fisheries (Northern Ireland) Act 1966. The new measures will provide a mechanism to make polluters pay for the damage they cause, in addition to the fine imposed for the criminal offence of pollution.

4 Articles 13, 15 and 16 increase protection for migratory fish in the vicinity of dams and mills by requiring the installation of fish passes in specified circumstances in dams built before 10 August 1842, by clarifying that the provisions on closure of mill sluices at weekends and when mills are not in use also apply to disused mills and by reducing the spacing of gratings at the point where water returns to a river or lake.

The remaining provisions of the order introduce a number of changes to the existing legislation that are aimed mainly at improving the protection of fisheries and the enforcement of fishery legislation.

I am satisfied that the order represents a sound, forward-looking approach to the needs of ail involved in the fisheries of Northern Ireland. It has generally been welcomed by local interests, and the measures being taken will improve the development and protection of fisheries in Northern Ireland. The new provisions on the licensing procedures for fish farms will go some way towards easing public concern about consultation on what are important issues to the public concerning an industry that is playing an increasing valuable part in the economy of the Province. I commend the order to the Committee.
