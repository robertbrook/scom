Draft Food Safety (Northern Ireland) Order 1991

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secrertary of State for Northern Ireland (Mr Jeremy Hanley)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Food Safety (Northern Ireland) Order 1991.

 I hope that the Committee will bear with me while, for just a few minutes, I describe the most important provisions that will eventually be introduced into Northern Ireland legislation through this order. As regards timing, we very much hope that, should the order pass the Committee today, it will be presented to the Privy Council on 20 March and become law on 20 May. The Government are very keen to introduce this legislation as soon as possible for the benefit of consumers.

The Government's underlying concern in producing the order has been, and will continue to be, to ensure that consumer safety comes first, wherever practicable. Our aim must be to ensure that the highest possible standards are maintained at each and every stage of the food manufacturing, preparation and distribution process. But we must be realistic. There must be a proper balance between legal controls and the need to allow—indeed, encourage—the food industry to innovate and compete successfully in the single market post-1992. Furthermore, the consumers of Northern Ireland would not tolerate an impossible pursuit of perfection that would mean that only the wealthy few could afford the wide choice and quality of food that we all want to see in the shops. The order, which reflects the provisions of the Food Safety Act in Great Britain, aims to achieve just that balance.

It follows that if we are to maintain this-balanced approach it is necessary to have regard to the changing nature of the food industry. In updating and improving the food safety law, we must make provision not just for 1991 but for the foreseeable future. This law must stand for a considerable time, particularly to alleviate uncertainty. Accordingly, the structure of the order provides a tough but flexible framework that does two things. First, it extends and strengthens the existing law where appropriate; secondly, it provides the enabling power for a wide range of regulations that can be used to make sure that the law keeps pace with the rapid technological advances in the industry.

I turn now to the main provisions of the order. First, it will re-create existing offences, but will be much wider in scope than the present law. In passing, speaking not only as a health Minister but as an argiculture Minister, I believe that farmers now recognise that their responsibility does not end at the farm gate. The industry has been badly hit by consumer scares in recent years. Therefore, farmers are now becoming far more realistic in the way that they produce food for final consumption. To this end the order 4 will introduce a new concept of "commercial operations", which will include every conceivable thing that can be done to food in the course of producing, storing, delivering, selling, importing and exporting it.

Indeed, the definition of food itself will be wider and will now include dietary supplements and water used in food production or taken with food, and bottled waters. The definition of food premises will encompass vehicles and stalls, and there is provision for including ships and aircraft. The order will also apply to any food given away at a public entertainment or as an advertisement, and will cover materials in contact with food.

The order will create a new offence of selling food that does not comply with food safety requirements. This is food which has been rendered injurious to health, which is unfit for human consumption or which is so contaminated that it would not be reasonable to expect it to be used for human consumption.

If an enforcement authority discovers that part of a batch of food does not comply with food safety requirements, the whole of the batch can be presumed not to comply, unless the owner of the food proves otherwise. The enforcement authority will have power to inspect, detain or seize food suspected of not complying with food safety requirements.

The order also contains a substantial range of new enforcement powers. For example, a system of legally enforceable improvement notices will be introduced. If a food business fails to comply with food hygiene or food processing regulations, an enforcement officer can serve a notice on the proprietor requiring him to take measures to remedy the defects within a given period. Failure to comply with an improvement notice will be an offence. We take this matter very seriously. The maximum fine on summary conviction will be £20,000, and there will be an unlimited fine or a prison sentence for offences on indictment.

Additionally, following conviction for an offence under the food safety law, a court will have powers to make a prohibition order. The effect of such an order will be to close down unsanitary food premises, to prohibit premises from being used for particular kinds of food businesses and to prevent the use of a piece of equipment or a particular process. It can even prohibit a person from carrying on or managing any food business, on public health grounds.

Where the carrying on of a business poses an imminent risk to health an enforcement officer can impose an emergency prohibition notice, stopping the operation of the business in its entirety. Where this type of notice is imposed the enforcement officer must apply to a court within three days for the issue of an emergency prohibition order. If he does not do so, it will lapse.

There will be new powers to enable Departments to issue emergency control orders requiring any food that poses an immediate risk to health to be withdrawn from the market. These are not intended to replace the present voluntary system, whereby arrangements are made with manufacturers, wholesalers and retailers to have a hazardous food withdrawn from sale. In general the voluntary system has worked well, and I would expect most incidents still to be dealt with under these arrangements. However, the power to make emergency control orders needs to be available in the event of a breakdown in those voluntary arrangements.

JPEG 5 The order also introduces wide-ranging and flexible regulation-making powers. These powers are necessary to provide the detailed controls needed on the composition, labelling and hygiene of food, residues in food, materials and articles in contact with food and to permit and control novel foods and processes. The regulations will also require that food handlers should undergo hygiene training. Powers will also be given to require registration or licensing of food businesses.

I mentioned registration and licensing. We expect that registration will apply generally and will ensure that district councils in Northern Ireland are aware of the existence and nature of any food businesses in their area. However, where there is only occasional use of premises such as church halls as food premises—there has been some concern about this—which we would measure as no more than one day a week, those premises will not require to be registered.

Licensing will be limited to certain high-risk operations and businesses where novel foods are produced or novel processes such as irradiation carried out, and to milk producers and dairies. A novel food is one that is not sold at present in Northern Ireland or a process that is not currently a normal food process in general use. The regulation-making powers are supplemented by provisions to enable any European Community obligations to be implemented.

Certain of the regulation-making powers will, when appropriate, apply to food sources, such as live animals. That will permit the effective enforcement of provisions on antibiotic residues in animals or pesticide residues in foodstuffs.

The provisions that I have outlined are the main provisions on enforcement and consumer protection, but the order also provides for an enforcement authority to bypass the immediate offender and take action against the "real" offender. For example, defective food might be purchased in a supermarket, and the retailer would therefore be the apparent offender and first point of inquiry. However, it may become clear to the enforcement authority that it is not the retailer but the manufacturer who was the real offender. In such circumstances, action can be taken direct against the manufacturer, or perhaps importer, if the manufacturer is abroad.

This bypass provision will complement a new defence— that of due diligence—to be introduced by the order. It provides a statutory defence for persons charged with an offence who can show that they

"took all reasonable precautions and exercised all due diligence to avoid committing the offence".

 The order sets out the circumstances in which a person may be deemed to satisfy that defence. They include, for example, a retailer who buys goods under the brand name of a supplier. A person can also be deemed to comply with the defence if he can prove that the offence was the fault of someone else and that he could not reasonably know that an offence was committed.

The remaining provisions—I am sure there is no need to go into them in detail today—deal with the analysis of food, the provisions of the necessary powers of entry and arrangements for prosecutions. It will also be an offence to obstruct an enforcement officer.

Enforcement of the order generally will be by authorised officers of district councils. Indeed, I am to address a meeting of them in the very near future. We are very grateful to the enforcement officers. They will have an 6 extremely important role to play in protecting the consumer throughout Northern Ireland. Therefore, their training and appreciation of the problems are of the highest importance. To ensure uniform enforcement, the order enables the Department of Health and Social Services to issue codes of practice on the implementation and enforcement of the legislation, and to issue directions on specific steps to be taken to give effect to codes of practice.

The Government recognise that the order will impose additional duties on district councils. They will certainly need more staff if they are to discharge their responsibilities effectively. Accordingly, we have agreed to make available to district councils an additional allocation of some £850,000 in 1991–92 and subsequent years. Why that amount? It is equivalent on a pro rata population basis to the additional funds allocated for this purpose to local authorities in Great Britain. The multiple of 2.78 per cent, is the one normally used for this purpose. The figure that it was based on was £30 million, which, while not a totally exhaustive calculation of the likely costs, was a best effort at estimating those costs. If there are any surplus costs, they will naturally be considered. Furthermore, regulations will provide that district councils can charge for the training of food handlers and for certain enforcement functions.

I am convinced that the order will make a great contribution to the protection of the consumer by ensuring that food is safe to eat. There is logic in having health and agricultural responsibilities together in Northern Ireland. As I say, too rarely have the consumer and the consumer's health been considered in the past when it came to food manufacture. Now the power of the consumer is understood, and the agriculture industry is becoming far more realistic in its desire for perfection in quality and cleanliness. The consumer, in this regard, has won the battle, and he deserves to. I hope, therefore, that the consumer will feel that the order provides a better framework for consumer protection. I therfore commend it to the Committee.
