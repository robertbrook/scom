I should stress at the outset that the Scottish Office supports the development of the marine fish-farming industry to achieve long-term economic and social benefits to local communities, but it can only be used in a sustainable manner. I am grateful to the honourable Member for Western Isles (Mr. Macdonald) for tabling the amendments and for allowing me the opportunity to stress the important role that we expect SNH to play in marine environment. Certainly, it is our intention that SNH should play an enhanced role in that context. The Bill makes adequate provision for SNH to operate in that area.

There simply is no need for amendment No. 35. The legal definition of Scotland for most purposes, including those in the Bill, includes territorial waters which extend generally to a range of three miles. Therefore, I trust that the hon. Gentleman will accept that there is no need for his amendment. The Committee will be aware that many coastal features of Scotland are unique and contain aquatic life forms that are found in virtually no other environment. SNH will take over the important work of the NCC in that area. That work will not be diminished, rather it will be enhanced. In any event, the term "coastal waters" without definition would give rise to difficulties of interpretation.

With regard to the much wider marine environment and all that that entails, it would not be reasonable to expect that SNH could possibly hope to cover every single cubic metre of the ocean. Nevertheless, we certainly intend that it should have a broad view of, and play a developed role in, such an environment. The development of our understanding JPEG 43 of marine life is continually evolving, and I hope that SNH will play a significant part in this research and development. In the wider area beyond territorial waters SNH will be able to play a role in conjunction with the Joint Nature Conservancy Committee as those waters would be considered as international territory.

I trust that that will satisfy the hon. Member for Western Isles and that the reassurances that I have given on SNH's marine and aquatic role will allay his fears.

Mr. Dalyell
: I do not want to nit-pick, but when the Minister uses the expression "a broad view of", what does that cover?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: It covers the whole environment and relevant matters. SNH will not want to become bogged down in unimportant details, but it will have flexibility to choose priorities that it considers to be important.

On new clause 2, I recognise and understand the anxiety of the hon. Member for Western Isles to safeguard the role of SNH in the development of the marine fish-farming industry. It is our intention that SNH will continue the existing work of the NCC and the Countryside Commission for Scotland.

The advice of the statutory bodies concerned with nature conservation and landscape matters has been an important component in the development of Government policy towards the fish-farming industry and it is important that the role of SNH is developed further.

However I do not believe that the new clause is necessary or appropriate. As members of the Committee will know, clause 2(1)(a) and (b) place a general duty on SNH to advise both Ministers and the wider public on a wide range of matters concerned with the natural heritage of Scotland. Marine fish-farming is, of course, an industry with clear implications for the natural heritage of Scotland and will, therefore, be an obvious area of activity for SNH. Rather than specify which types of advice SNH should provide, we have deliberately drafted the clause in a general sense to allow the new body wide flexibility in deciding its priorities. I therefore trust that the hon. Member for Western Isles will agree that the first part of the new clause is unnecessary.

As to the second part of the new clause, members of the Committee should be aware that an advisory committee considers cases when a consultee objects to a proposed decision by the Crown Estate commissioners on the granting of a lease. Both the NCC and the CCS sit on this committee, as does the local authority involved. The committee's chairman and vice-chairman are appointed by the Secretary of State and can recommend that a case be referred to the Secretary of State if the advisory committee is unable to resolve it. The advisory committee is now fully operational and is, I believe, a valuable mechanism that provides the opportunity to discuss in depth questions of concern relating to individual cases.

In practice, the Scottish Office wishes to establish the environmentally sensitive use and management of our natural resources. We also seek viable economic activity and an increase in the standard of the quality of life. Those two objectives are not separate; they are indivisible. Economic activity, which degrades the natural resource base to such an extent that the activity cannot be sustained in the long term undermines our future and the future of succeeding generations.

With regard to the concept itself, I wish to quote from the second draft of "Caring for the World—A Strategy for Sustainability", which is an update of the World Conservation strategy. Eight principles are set out in the report and I hope that hon. Members will bear with me while I refer briefly to them and put them in the context of Scotland's natural heritage.

The first principle is to limit human impact on the biosphere to a level that is within its carrying capacity. That is a crucial but technically difficult issue. We hope and expect SNH to stimulate fundamental research on the carrying capacity of, for example, land for food and timber production, and for recreational pursuits so that it can advise on how the management of uses and activities can be made sustainable.

The second principle is to maintain the earth's stock of biological wealth. I am sure that members of the Committee will recognise that it will be SNH's duty to conserve and enhance the flora and fauna by protecting endangered species and safeguarding wildlife habitats. The third principle is the use of non-renewable resources at rates that do not exceed the creation of renewable substitutes. There is frequent criticism that sustainability cannot be applied to non-renewable resources, but the principle seeks to place that in the context of technological development. I am sure that the Committee will 56 know of many examples of the use of renewable substitutes in Scotland, for example, in the production of electricity from water and wind energy.

The fourth principle aims for an equal distribution of the benefits and costs of resource use and environmental management among and within countries. That is a difficult principle to put into practice but, focusing on Scotland, consideration is being given by many different interests to various pricing mechanisms. To promote technologies that increase the benefits from the given stock of resources is the fifth principle. There is ample practical evidence of that already in Scotland. For example, there is increased extraction of hydrocarbons from North sea reservoirs as a result of technological advance, and much further research is being undertaken in that and in similar areas.

The sixth principle is to use economic policy to help maintain natural wealth. Again, there are several examples of that, and other policy instruments are being developed, such as assistance schemes for the maintenance of woodlands and forests, the development of extensification and set-aside schemes. At a more theoretical level, a great deal of research is being done into methods of economic valuation of new activities to ensure that environmental costs and benefits are built into the assessment of new developments. To adopt an anticipatory cross-sectoral approach to decision-making is the seventh principle. That lies at the heart of the Government's philosophy as set out in the White Paper on the environment. Finally, the eighth principle is to promote and support cultural values that are compatible with sustainability. We recognise the importance of a major public education process about the environment as a whole, especially its sustainable use.
