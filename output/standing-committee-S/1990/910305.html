Mr. Macdonald
: I beg to move amendment No. 55, in page 3, line 29, at end add—

"(11) SNH shall prepare statements of policy for all national nature reserves and shall consult interested parties on their content and implementation.".

I hope that the amendment will receive a positive response from the Minister because it is reasonable and sensible. With hindsight, however, I would have made one change to the wording. It states:

"SNH shall prepare statements of policy for all national nature reserves and shall consult interested parties on their content and implementation."

When I prepared the amendment, I was thinking of "statements of policy" that applied to each specific nature reserve and not of a general statement of policy covering all nature reserves. In that context, SNH should prepare an individual statement of management policy for each nature reserve and, in the process of doing so, it should consult widely to ensure the greatest possible input. When consultation is completed and SNH has drawn its conclusions, that statement should be made public.

It may seem extraordinary that our nature reserves lack such public statements of policy but that is the case under the Nature Conservancy Council's current management of many of them. I should have thought that if such statements were available for each nature reserve, they would provide a rational, predictable framework within which SNH could operate. The 162 future decisions of all the other interests that have a locus in each nature reserve, such as landowners, could be guided by those public statements. They would also allow interested authorities and outside environmental bodies a framework within which to respond to proposals or actions that affect nature reserves.

A public statement of policy would also aid public accountability of SNH in its management of those nature reserves. We could see whether SNH was pursuing a wide mandate, which we hope will follow from the legislation, or whether it restricted itself to the pure biological, scientific focus of the old NCC. If there was a publicly stated policy for the nature reserves, we could see which of those two courses was being pursued by SNH. That would also aid public accountability of expenditure undertaken by SNH in its management of nature reserves.

I shall illustrate the need for such a public statement of policy for each nature reserve by examining in detail the history of the Cairngorm estates, which has been under NCC management for many years and which forms Britain's foremost nature reserve. Indeed, the Government propose that it should be a world heritage site. That nature reserve was established in 1954 and then covered about 34,000 acres. The NCC set up the Cairngorm consultative panel that was presided over by Professor Matthews of Aberdeen university. It included representatives from local authorities, mountaineering and other outdoor interests who expressed an interest in the management of the reserve.

The panel was intended to meet regularly to provide an input for all the varied interests and to provide a public framework to discuss the management policy for the new nature reserve. However, the panel met only once on 28 June 1954—a mere 10 days before the formal declaration of the reserve. It never met again and no explanation was given. Nevertheless, without the help of the panel, a management plan was eventually produced by the NCC in 1958. It provided a framework within which nature reserve agreements were negotiated with individual owners of the three main estates that were the heart of the nature reserve.

That plan was updated in the 1960s, by which time the nature reserve covered about 70,000 acres and that was the last publicly available document about the management of that nature reserve. The document gave a comprehensive account of the reasons for the reserve establishment, the research work that was being undertaken, the management objectives in its oversight of the reserve and the policies being pursued to achieve those objectives. An annex to the 1960s plan contained details of the individual management agreements that were drawn up with each estate, but that annex was kept confidential. Nevertheless, the framework plan was publicly available and it guided outside bodies and local authorities on the public debates that took place on the future management of the reserve.

By the 1970s, the 1960s plan was outdated, just as the 1958 plan became out of date, so new policies for the reserve were introduced. Those were needed to JPEG 163 reflect the growing public interest in the reserve, partly because of the skiing developments of Aviemore and the Cairngorms in the 1960s, and because of the general increase in leisure and outdoor activities in that period. Equally important was the growing realisation that new approaches were being made in the management of reserve habitats. For example, overgrazing by red deer was widely perceived as preventing the regeneration of the native woodland.

In 1974, 1975 and 1976, the NCC convened three special meetings, each of which was attended by local community interests, user groups and landowning interests to discuss the problems facing the reserve and to exchange views on potential solutions and the future management of the reserve. Following those meetings, the NCC drafted proposed policies to cover the period 1976–1980. The draft policy statement, entitled "Document for consultation in advance of planning" was sent to a wide range of interest groups in 1976. Amazingly, two days after the distribution of that draft policy statement, the NCC's regional officer wrote to all the recipients asking for the document to be immediately returned to the NCC. The suspicion at the time was that landowning interests in the reserve had objected to the distribution of the draft policy statement. That document has never reappeared and neither has any other policy statement about what the NCC believes should be happening in what is supposed to be Britain's most important nature conservation area.

Since the failure of that round of consultation and the lapse of the last publicly available management plan, most of the nature reserve agreements conducted with individual landowners have come up for renewal. With the passage of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981, opportunities have arisen for landowners to gain financial compensation for managing their land in ways that benefit nature conservation.

The NCC has been obliged to carry out negotiations and to realise individual agreements without help from policy guidelines governing the management of the reserve and without public input into that process.

From 1976 onwards, the NCC, in response to inquiries from voluntary conservation organisations, has regularly confirmed that it is working on the production of a statement of policies for public consultation. Even as recently as September last year, the NCC suggested that such a statement was expected to be publicly available by the end of 1990, but, predictably, no such statement has appeared. During that 15-year period from 1976 to date, the NCC, when asked to explain why a statement of policy is not circulated, has almost always answered that further discussions are needed with landowners who own the estates within the nature reserve.

Perhaps the most detailed examination of the Cairngorms by a Committee of this House took place in 1984, when the Select Committee on Scottish Affairs undertook an investigation into the workings of the Highlands and Islands Development Board. 164 During that investigation, the Committee visited the northern slopes of the Cairngorms, taking evidence from a wide range of interests. Inevitably, the management of the Cairngorms as a whole came under scrutiny. In its report, published in 1985, the Select Committee considered the question of management planning for reserves, such as the Cairngorm nature reserve. Paragraph 102 of the report states:

"The third proposal from the North East Mountain Trust, that there should be a management plan for the whole of the Cairngorm Estate and those areas which abut it (the National Nature Reserve and the Glenmore Forest Park) is one that deserves sympathetic hearing."

The case then for a management plan for the Cairngorm area was strong. It is interesting to note that one member of that Select Committee, who agreed with its unanimous recommendations, was the hon. Member for Stirling (Mr. Forsyth), now Minister of State. Yet, we are still waiting for that management plan.

Most of the Cairngorms nature reserve is in private ownership. If the past 20 years is anything to go by, there is little prospect of the NCC initiating a public discussion of its management policies. Meanwhile, the quality of the reserve—the centrepiece of a proposed world heritage site, which the Government endorse—continues to degenerate. A key requirement in arresting that process is the production of public statements on management policy. The same should apply to all nature reserves in Scotland. Such statements should be based on the widest possible consultation with environmental interests, landowners and local authorities, and should be made publicly available. A requirement that the new agency should formulate such management policies and make those policies publicly available is surely reasonable and sensible. It can only enhance the public accountability of the management of Scotland's natural heritage.
