HIGHER AND FURTHER EDUCATION

10.30 am

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Ian Lang)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of higher and further education in Scotland.

On the day that our White Paper on the future of higher and further education was published, I wrote to the hon. Member for Glasgow, Garscadden (Mr. Dewar) suggesting that we debate in the Scottish Grand Committee the proposals that affect Scotland. Today's debate is the result. I am most grateful for the opportunity to debate our plans for the future of higher and further education.

I was particularly pleased that my right hon. Friend the Prime Minister made clear the importance that the Government attach to our proposals by making such a telling contribution to our press conference on 20 May. A few weeks ago I met the eight Scottish university principals and on Friday of last week my hon. Friend the Minister of State and I had a valuable meeting with the principals of the 17 centrally funded colleges. Those meetings have helped to set the scene for what will now be an important transitional phase when we work through the detailed arrangements for setting up the new Scottish Higher Education Funding Council. The Government are justly proud of what we have already achieved by our higher education policies. Participation by young Scots in the decade to 1990 has risen from under 17 per cent. to almost 25 per cent., or by nearly one half.

Mr. Tony Worthington
(Clydebank and Milngavie)
: Will the Secretary of State admit that that achievement is relatively easy? If there is a 25 per cent. fall in the number of 17-year-olds, one has only to let into universities and colleges those rejected in 1980 to alter the figures from one in five to one in four.

Mr. Lang
: It may be easy, but the hon. Gentlemam did not succeed in achieving that when his party was in power. Moreover, there was not a fall in the number of 17-year-olds of the size that he suggests in that decade, although more recently there has been a demographic change. A substantial expansion of higher education has taken place which has increased the number of those achieving degrees and diplomas. The figure was up by 12 per cent. between 1983 and 1989—a period when the number of young adults in Scotland fell by 3 per cent.

Mr. Alistair Darling
(Edinburgh, Central)
: The Secretary of State made an announcement that was welcome by both sides when he said that in future Scottish universities would be subject to the control of a Scottish Universities Funding Council. Will the funding under the new regime be any more secure than it is under the present regime? Can he give a long-term view of how much money universities can expect to receive? I have in mind the university of Edinburgh. One of its difficulties is that it cannot enter into any long-term planning because the United Kingdom 4 Universities Funding Council cannot give it any idea of what assumptions it can make over the next five to 10 years. We have seen the absurd spectacle of the university having to sell its art treasures—Scotland's treasures—to bale itself out because of the Government's inability to enter into any long-term planning. What confidence can we have that the situation will be any better when the Secretary of State's track record so far is abysmal?

Mr. Lang
: The hon. Gentleman knows that the problems of Edinburgh university do not stem from the funding that it has received from the Universities Funding Council so much as from an inadequacy of internal financial controls. The university is tackling that matter according to its priorities and it is right that it should decide its priorities.

The Government's public expenditure programmes are carried forward on a three-year rolling basis. The situation has not changed since the last Government were in power and I do not envisage any substantial change arising from out present proposals. However, having a funding council based in Scotland that is relevant to Scotland's needs will mean a more direct application of priorities and principles to the use of funds. There will be more opportunity for Scottish universities to be involved in those decisions.

Mr. Donald Dewar
(Glasgow, Garscadden)
: The Secretary of State will remember that we have pressed for this change of structure for many years. I would be ungracious not to congratulate him on another U-turn. Scottish universities and colleges are suffering a sharp financial crisis. One has only to listen to what vice-principals have been saying about wages and the maintenance of buildings. Is the Secretary of State satisfied with the present level of funding? If not, will he guarantee that matters will improve in the immediate future?

Mr. Lang
: The hon. Gentleman can hardly accuse me of a U-turn when I have only just moved into the driving seat, but I am grateful to him for welcoming the decision that we have announced.

There has been a 10 per cent. real terms increase in funding over the past decade or so. I cannot stipulate the precise levels of future funding, but funding will come from the Scottish bloc. The allocation of funding will be at the discretion of the Secretary of State. For as many years as I am Secretary of State, I shall endeavour to bring to bear sensible judgments on priorities in Scottish universities.

Mr. Tam Dalyell
(Linlithgow)
: To return to the Secretary of State's answer to my hon. Friend the Member for Edinburgh, Central (Mr. Darling), I wish to ask the right hon. Gentleman a question about which I notified his office last week. What is the Secretary of State's policy on the sale of assets, such as the Audobon books by the university of Edinburgh? It would be helpful to the Committee to hear Government policy on the sale of assets.

Mr Lang
: That is essentially a matter for the university itself. With regard to the merits of particular arts treasure, there are mechanisms to identify national interest and bring to bear appropriate decisions and resources. That example is essentially an internal matter for Edinburgh university.

I was talking about the expansion of higher education. The numbers qualifying for entry to higher education also rose substantially. In the decade to 1989, the proportion of Scottish school leavers with three or more highers rose from below one in five to almost one in four. We have also JPEG 5 been successful in widening access to higher education for those young people without standard qualifications. We have virtually quadrupled the proportion of such entrants, to over 5 per cent. As a result of all those successes, the full-time equivalent numbers in Scottish higher education have risen by over 17 per cent. since 1983, to over 100,000.

Building on those successes the overall participation rate by young Scots is likely to rise, not to the 33 per cent. level that we estimated until now but, to over 40 per cent. by the end of the century. As part of that achievement, we now seek in the next decade to push up participation in higher education by those without standard qualifications to over one in five.

Mr George Foulkes
(Carrick, Cumnock and Doon Valley)
: The Secretary of State is talking about participation by young Scots in higher education. He would agree that funding for individuals is important. Will he confirm that the take-up of student loans in Scotland is low and that it is causing considerable hardship for Scottish students, as a result of which many are in great financial difficulties? Will the Secretary of State rethink the loans scheme and reintroduce full grants that take account of the full rate of inflation since the introduction of the loans scheme? When the hon. Gentleman answers will he speak up and use his Footlights voice?

Mr Lang
: I discern a non sequitur in the hon. Gentleman's logic. If students are as hard-pressed as he says, one would expect the student loan takeup to be higher rather than lower, so he is not making a good point.

Mr. Foulkes
rose—

Mr. Lang
: No, I will not give way to the hon. Gentleman again. I look forward to him making his point later in the debate.

I was talking about those without standard qualifications. To focus on that group is wholly realistic, given the demographic downturn in the numbers of young Scots through most of the next decade. That downturn presents us with the challenge of awakening the latent talents of those sold short in their learning experiences at school by encouraging them to achieve their true potential. That challenge will be met by the policies in our White Papers, which are designed to broaden access and maximise the currency of training skill levels in the national work force. Also, the Scottish wider access programme, which is designed for mature students, will this year see some 1,000 additional students entering higher education; and the advanced courses development programme of SCOTVEC will encourage increased take-up among those pursuing higher national certificate and diploma courses.

Mr. Malcolm Bruce
(Gordon)
: The Secretary of State talks about increased access. Will he comment on the development of the Open university in Scotland, especially in rural areas, where many people feel shut out through a lack of support facilities? Will the Scottish Office accept some responsibility for funding Open university courses in such areas?

Mr. Lang
: On the question of wider access, expansion has been dramatic—64 per cent. over the past decade. The Open university will continue to operate on a United Kingdom basis. However, we shall ensure that a full Scottish input is included in the provision of Open 6 university courses. I agree with the hon. Gentleman about the Open university's importance in rural areas—not to mention Carrick, Cumnock and Doon Valley.

I emphasise that the further expansion of higher education will not signal a dilution of quality and standards. Quality considerations will be at the centre of the development of our proposals for further and higher education and I am confident that our institutions will continue to maintain and enhance their standards under the new regime.

I have to say to the Committee that we have been greatly encouraged by the welcome given to our proposals, which are designed to give a unity of purpose and policy direction to Scottish post-school education. As an example, I shall quote from a letter, which I received from Principal Forty of Stirling university on behalf of the Committee of Scottish University Principals. He said:

"We are unanimous in our support for the provisions of the White Paper. We also welcome the Government's intention to set up a Scottish Higher Education Funding Council and declare our determination that it should succeed. It is in everyone's interest that it should do so."

The same unanimous welcome was given to the provisions at a meeting with college principals and is reflected in the responses of political parties in the House. When added to our proposals for further education, which I announced in my statement of 21 March, and the positive early signs of Scottish Enterprise's success in effecting training strategies with a vocational emphasis, our total package is both coherent and forward looking. Our plans seek to build on the established successes of Scottish post-school education, especially the high and still expanding levels of participation, the development of multi-level access to encourage mature students into education and the high quality of course content which ensures that Scottish students are so highly regarded at home and abroad.

Mr. Tom Clarke
(Monklands, West)
: Will the Secretary of State give way?

Mr. Lang
: I should like to press on.

Our aim in the two White Papers is to break down two outdated but still pervasive barriers. The first applies to both documents. It is the artificial distinction between a vocational and an academic education. Neither is superior to the other. Both are essential to the social and economic well-being of the nation. In both cases, students and teaching staff alike have, as their objective, the attainment of a qualification that is founded on standards of excellence and commands the respect of prospective employers. That objective, the pursuit of quality, must be retained while we expand the numbers that benefit from higher and further education. When we examine the energy and commitment that our major competitors invest in the education of their future work force, we can see all too clearly the need for us to respond. We aim to maintain our existing strengths, but we also seek to foster new talents and skills, especially in the development of inter-disciplinary specialisms where new employment opportunities are opening up rapidly.

Mr. Menzies Campbell
(Fife, North-East)
: In addition to the traditional strengths to which the Secretary of State refers, is there a place for the traditional element of research in universities? I visited St. Andrews university, of which two Scottish Office Ministers are graduates. There was much apprehension in the astronomy department about the Government's failure to ensure proper funding for JPEG 7 scientific research. How will the proposals outlined this morning, give the astronomy department of St. Andrews university confidence about the Government's commitment to scientific research?

Mr. Lang
: If the hon. and learned Gentleman examines the White Paper, he will find such a commitment. The funding council decides about the allocation of resources for basic and strategic research. Universities usually receive most of the resources for such research. Applied research, which is usually funded by industry, is the prerogative of central institutions. I do not expect that balance to change dramatically, because the missions of the central institutions will not change greatly in the years ahead. However, the central institutions will be able to compete for funds. That competition will gradually change the balance and will lead to research of higher quality being carried out.

The second of the unnecessary and unnatural distinctions that we seek to remove is that forming the so-called binary line in higher education. Our benchmark for comparison is the attainment level of the student, which is a product of native intellect allied to the skills and commitment of the academic staff of the institutions concerned. I should make it absolutely clear today that I have the highest regard for the standards now being achieved in Scotland's higher education institutions, and the Government are committed to doing everything possible to enhance those standards still further.

As we break down the barriers, we must seek to build a national consensus in Scotland on the direction and structure of our further and higher education. That consensus has had some early encouragement in first responses to the White Paper. I look to the Opposition to join us—[ Laughter.] There are truly historic opportunities and we all must engage in a thoroughgoing, positive debate about the ways in which we might improve our education system still further. In particular, we should consider the opportunities that our proposals present for increasing the effectiveness of our investment in higher and further education to the benefit of students and their potential employers.

In any process of such major reform it is of course essential that we consult with those affected. There are over 15,000 full-time lecturing and academic staff in further education and centrally funded colleges and universities, and over 4,000 part-time staff. Those institutions and staff, in subject range, geographic location and tradition, have a very diverse set of circumstances. In approaching consultation on our proposals, I should emphasise our concern to indentify and remove any regulatory or bureaucratic obstacles to achieving the integration that we see as affording a positive way forward. We are committed to individuals being able to realise their full potential in the new framework.

We will also consult on the quality assessment and quality assurance aspects of our higher education proposals. On those, I should make it absolutely clear that our primary intention is to advance excellence. Students can readily discriminate between courses on offer. They will exert most of the market forces necessary to weed out poor courses. The other major issue on which we will consult will 8 be funding for research in higher education, which is especially important to Scotland, where there is now a considerable research base on which to build.

The esssence of our higher education proposals and their broad sweep are well understood, but I can encapsulate them for the Committee as follows. First, a new Scottish Higher Education Funding Council will be created, with responsibility for funding both teaching and general research in all major Scottish higher education institutions. Secondly, degree awarding powers will be extended to Napier polytechnic and Glasgow polytechnic and, subject to criteria to be agreed, to some other major institutions. Thirdly, polytechnics—

Mr. Worthington
: Will the Secretary of State give way?

Mr. Lang
: Let me finish my encapsulation first.

Polytechnics and, subject again to the development of satisfactory criteria, certain other major higher education institutions will be allowed to include the word "university" in their title if they wish. Fourthly, following the disbanding of the Council for National Academic Awards, a United Kingdom-wide academic audit unit will be established to ensure satisfactory quality control in teaching throughout higher education. Finally, a quality assessment unit will be set up within the Scottish Higher Education Funding Council to monitor relative quality standards across Scottish institutions and to collaborate with the parallel units in other funding councils to maintain common standards throughout the United Kingdom.

Mr. Worthington
: This is an extremely important matter for the institutions involved. It seems to be clear that Napier and Glasgow polytechnics will become universities. However, there is no similar clarity about Robert Gordon's or Dundee and Paisley polytechnics. Those institutions are worried that the doubt about their status will count against them when they seek to attract students in the next few years. There should be no equivalent of planning blight in this matter. What does the Secretary of State intend to do to clarify those institutions' status?

Mr. Lang
: We have already had one meeting with the principals of the central institutions, at which they set out their criteria for eligibility for university status. We undertook to consider those criteria, and I made it clear that we did not rule out the possibility that university status would be extended to institutions other than Napier and Glasgow polytechnics. However, only those two institutions will be eligible to adopt the title "university" after the legislation is passed. The ability of other institutions to award degrees and to assume university status will be the subject of further consultation.

Mr. Dewar
: I recognise that the Secretary of State wants to make progress, but will he tidy up one matter? Presumably, the right to the term "university" is in the gift of the Government. However, on degree-giving courses, the Council for National Academic Awards is being abolished. What will replace it and who will monitor standards?

Mr. Lang
: We are consulting on the criteria that would lead to eligibility for degree-awarding status. Accredited status, which the two polytechnics and the three colleges mentioned already have, would be a key component. The colleges suggested others, for example, academic maturity, track record of high quality, cost-effectiveness, innovative JPEG 9 ability, responsiveness to academic needs and so on. Those criteria will be taken into account in the consultation process.

In summary, the reforms mark the start of a new era for higher education in Scotland. I have every confidence that they will further increase access and improve standards.

The White Paper "Access and Opportunity" presents our plans for education and training for those over 16. Education and training are vital for our future economic prosperity. At present fewer and fewer jobs are unskilled and that trend will continue. We want to create a culture in which all Scots have the opportunity and incentive to unlock their potential, by making more accessible the training and education that they and our economy need. We aim to encourage expansion by the introduction of the new general Scottish vocational qualifications. It will be a broadly based qualification which will open doors to a range of occupations or to higher education.

An important theme of our policies is the need to foster close links between employers and the education system. The formation of Scottish Enterprise and Highlands and Islands Enterprise, and the network of local enterprise companies are important in forging a new partnership between education and industry. I mentioned choice in relation to our reform of vocational qualifications. Choice also lies at the core of our plans for stimulating demand among young people for vocational education and training. That is why we propose to expand the training credits scheme to permit local enterprise companies to develop new solutions to encourage young people back into education and training. Local enterprise companies will be invited to submit bids for local schemes to operate training credits from April 1993. The aim will be to extend the coverage of the scheme to a further 10 per cent. of the 16 and 17-year-olds leaving full-time education. Thereafter, we aim to extend credits progressing year by year to ensure that by 1996 every young person in Scotland who leaves full-time education can have a training credit.

Mr. Robert Hughes
(Aberdeen, North)
: Will the Secretary of State give way?

Mr. Lang
: As Grampian is the scene of the trial scheme, I shall give way.

Mr. Hughes
: I am glad that the Secretary of State said that Grampian is the scene of the trial scheme as I do not want to cast doubts on the bona fides of Grampian Enterprise. When I met representatives of Grampian Enterprise a couple of weeks ago, they were honest and straightforward but they could not answer questions about the monitoring of the scheme, how employers would be brought into training, or how contact between youngsters and employers would be made. They had no idea how many companies would take part and they said, honestly, "We cannot tell you that because this is a pilot scheme and we do not know how it will work." That being so, how can the Secretary of State say with such certainty that the scheme will be extended? It might fail; he should perpare himself for that.

Mr. Lang
: I do not know whether the hon. Gentleman welcomes this creative, forward looking scheme or not. If I were the Member of Parliament for a Grampian constituency, I should be delighted that Grampian was one of the 10 or so areas in the United Kingdom, and the only one in Scotland, to have secured the funding to develop a 10 pilot scheme. I am encouraged by the positive commitment of the Grampian local enterprise company to develop the scheme. Grampian will take advantage of it sooner than the rest of Scotland. My ambition is that the scheme should spread to the rest of Scotland as soon as possible.

Local enterprise companies will also be expected to take a greater interest in and collaborate with the careers service. Good careers work is crucial. Young people need insights into jobs and to experience the world of work. They need to be made aware of the wide range of further education and training opportunities that exist. Choice can be effective only if information is available to make an informed decision. Our policies will aim, therefore, to ensure that the careers service raises its standards, works more closely with schools, colleges and employers and encourages more young people to continue in education and training.

A further measure to develop the partnership between education and employers will be the expansion of the Compact initiative. That is a valuable way of building links between schools and employers and encouraging young people to set challenging goals. Local enterprise companies will play a key part in that process in future.

If we are to succeed in our goals of increasing participation, higher levels of qualifications and widening access, the colleges of further education must play a major role. They are increasingly popular, particularly with adults, and over 200,000 Scots each year study in them. Most communities are within easy reach of college, with 46 colleges spread throughout Scotland. The main part of their work is the provision of vocational education and training, but I recognise that they have a wider role in relation to their local communities.

Our policies aim to enhance the prominence of the colleges and give them a new freedom to release their energies. The colleges are ready for that new status. They have grown up from the days when they were essentially low-status technical colleges, and are now mature institutions providing a wide range of courses up to and including higher national diplomas.

Sir Nicholas Fairbairn
(Perth and Kinross)
: Will my right hon. Friend be mindful that Opposition Members always oppose any form of classification? If we call a college of excellence, such as Napier which has magnificent single disciplines, a university, it will immediately want a vice-chancellor, a library, a department of mediaeval French and much other paraphernalia irrelevant to what such colleges teach.

Mr. Lang
: Perhaps my hon. and learned Friend will consider bequeathing his library to a college. He makes an interesting point and I hope that he will develop it during the debate.

The colleges are now mature institutions and are increasingly developing links with higher education institutions and providing a bridge for their students to progress. Their links with local employers are also strong. That helps to ensure the relevance of the colleges' work and that their students are sought after.

The growing maturity of the colleges was recognised when we made provision in 1989 for education authorities to give strengthened college councils new powers relating to management, supervision and financial control. Those powers are substantial and over the next two years college councils will gain valuable experience in exercising them. Mature institutions, however, should have wider freedoms JPEG 11 to develop their role further. That is why we plan to introduce legislation to establish the colleges as freestanding corporate bodies with power to employ staff and own land and buildings, funded by grant from central Government.

If colleges are to be successful they must aim to provide a high quality, relevant education. Only by doing so can they generate a greater demand for their courses and qualifications. The Fast Forward with Further Education initiative, which I launched last year, has helped to raise awareness of further education and in March this year the colleges contributed to a successful further education week, the first of its sort. That initiative will continue. I also attach great importance to systems that will ensure the quality of education and training provided by the colleges. In funding the colleges in future we shall, therefore, seek to achieve a balance between rewarding those colleges that perform better than others and achieving the financial stability necessary during a period of change. We shall assist colleges in the drive for higher standards, and the Scottish further education unit, which we recently created, will be well placed to support colleges in preparing staff for the challenges that lie ahead. I should like to pay tribute to the staff of the colleges and what they have achieved in recent years in implementing the reforms of vocational qualifications. I should also like to assure them of their future after 1 April 1993 and the support that we shall give them in preparing for it. The future of further education is bright and exciting. There is a reservoir of unskilled people who need to be prepared to play their part in the economy of the future. Our policies will create the framework for that.

We have the vision to see our proposals for what they are—a watershed in the long and distinguished history of Scottish higher education. Local authorities often provide the vital access through community colleges, as do voluntary bodies, most notably the Workers Educational Association, who describe that function as a recovery and relay system for adults wanting to re-enter the education system but lacking the confidence to walk into a college of further education and enrol for a mainstream course.

Placing all the emphasis on the college mode, as the White Paper, does, will deny some people the right to reaccess the education system. That represents a wasted opportunity. It is wrong to suggest that getting a SCOTVEC qualification is all that matters. For many people it is important, but for others the opportunity to reorient themselves and give something back to the community is equally important.

The impression given by both White Papers is that the Government want education on the cheap. They want to keep education policy within a narrow vision of what can be done in the most cost-effective manner.
