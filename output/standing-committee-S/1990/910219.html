That the Bill be considered in the following order, Clause 1, Schedule 1, Clauses 2 to 4, Schedule 2, Clauses 5 to 12, Schedule 3, Clause 13, Schedule 4, new Clauses and new Schedules relating to Part I, Clause 14, Schedule 5, Clauses 15 and 16, Schedule 6, Clauses 17 and 18, new Clauses and new Schedules relating to Part II, Clause 19, Schedules 7 to 9, Clauses 20 and 21, new Clauses and new Schedules relating to Part III, Clauses 22 to 26, Schedules 10 and 11, Clause 27, remaining new Clauses and new Schedules.

It is simply an extension of that principle to suggest discussing new clauses and schedules relating to each part of the Bill immediately after completing discussion of that part. Sir Nicholas Fairbairn

Mr. Wilson
: I am grateful for that typically helpful remark. The difference is that there are even more Polish speakers in Poland. The distinguishing feature of Gaelic is that it is an indigenous language. If it is not given status in Scotland, it is unlikely to be given status in Poland or anywhere else. That is significant because the areas of Scotland that are predominantly Gaelic-speaking are those where the new agency will be most active. It is an unfortunate truth that those areas have not shown conspicuous sensitivity to the agency's predecessors.

It would be symbolic and worthwhile if the new agency, which will be setting off on the right foot in many other ways, could demonstrate empathy and respect for the people of those areas and all that they hold dear by adopting a Gaelic title. That would impose no burden or expense, but would put down a marker that the agency could deal with the people of that area in their language. Such bodies have sometimes paid insufficient regard to the culture and values of the area in which they operate. If the principle is accepted, I would be happy to work with the hon. Member for Argyll and Bute (Mrs Michie) in leading Gaelic scholars to sort out the semantics, but if the idea is not accepted, I shall press the amendment to a Division.

New clause 1, tabled by my hon. Friend the Member for Western Isles, is a brief extension of the same principle and would provide that Scottish National Heritage:

"shall have a duty in all its dealings with other persons or bodies to use also the Gaelic form of its name".

That means that the stationery of the new body should show a biligual title. Many organisations, not impelled to do so by legislation, manage to do that. For instance, the banks are good at using alternative Gaelic titles. They operate under commercial pressures rather than purely cultural pressures. I see no difficulty in a Government Department doing that.

I freely acknowledge that the Scottish Office has done useful work for Gaelic over the past few years. There is still much progress to be made on the official status of the language. People whose first language is Gaelic should have the right to communicate with Government agencies in that language. It is a small representation of that principle that Government agencies should show themselves able and willing to communicate in Gaelic.

I shall not labour or prolong the issue. No one in the Committee has anything to lose by accepting the amendment.
