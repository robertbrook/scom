TRAFFIC AREAS (REORGANISATION) ORDER 1990

10.30 am

The Minister for Roads and Traffic (Mr. Christopher Chope)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Traffic Areas (Reorganisation) Order 1990.

 The object of the order is to reduce the number of traffic areas from nine to eight, redrawing the boundaries where necessary. Subject to the approval of both Houses, the changes will take effect on 1 June 1991, or two months after approval, whichever is the later. The changes are part of a reorganisation of work that involves the closure of the traffic area offices in Newcastle, Nottingham and London.

Cumbria has been omitted from Schedule 1 of the order. That is an oversight, correction of which will require a separate order. I hope that the Committee will agree that the order is a sensible reform.

10.31 am

Ms. Joan Ruddick
(Lewisham, Deptford)
: The Minister suggested that we should regard the order as a sensible measure. In part—we do, but there are some issues on which the Minister should comment.

Traffic commissioners and their offices have operated since the 1930s. Vehicle licensing is the mechanism to control the excesses of unlicenced operators and ensure a standard of safety on our roads. The system has worked well, partly because of the strong regional organisation.

We are pleased that the Government have decided to retain the strong regional base for the operations of the traffic commissioners, despite the recommendations of the Palmer report, on which the order is based. I have received representations from several bodies, including the Association of Metropolitan Authorities, the Transport and General Workers Union and the National Union of Civil and Public Servants, stressing the importance of the local aspect of the reorganisation.

Operators and drivers can at present visit their regional offices to obtain advice on how best to complete the forms that they must use to apply for or change their operating licences. They appreciate not having to travel far to deal with inquiries from the traffic commissioners. Anxiety has been expressed about the proposed closure of some offices. It is not strictly necessary for people to visit an office in person, but that personal contact is valued by operators. Traffic area offices receive several personal callers every day. Problems will arise for London operators who will have to travel to Eastbourne to obtain such advice.

The proposed closure of the Newcastle office has caused especial anxiety and is strongly opposed by the Tyne and Wear passenger transport authority, the passenger transport executive and several metropolitan district councils in the area. they are worried about accessibility. 4 Under the proposals, some conurbations, such as that in the north-east, will be remote from an area office. The authorities there do not believe that it will be possible to run the same efficient operators' licensing service that existed in the past. The creation of a divisional office in Newcastle has been suggested. What consideration has been given to that suggestion?

Has the Minister considered personal callers? The Department has already closed the Aberdeen office and transferred all Scottish business to the Edinburgh office. How has that change affected the work load and the number of personal callers?

As always, the Government claim that such a reorganisation will save money. What calculations have been made? Will the operators' licensing fee be adjusted? That fee is linked to the total turnover of the income of area offices.

What are the Departments proposals for staff at the offices? Will staff be redeployed, or will some be made redundant?

The reorganisation offers an opportunity to re-examine and improve the role of the traffic commissioners. During our consideration of the Road Traffic Bill, we discussed impounding vehicles. The Opposition supported measures that provided for impounding vehicles for an imprisonable offence. A power to impound vehicles used in fly-tipping exists in the private Member's Bill that I introduced.

The traffic commissioners are very worried about unlicensed vehicles. The traffic commissioner of the metropolitan area, which will soon be abolished, said in a report in 1989;

"There are still many people operating lorries within my area who do not have an 'O' licence. And several others who continue operating with impunity, despite having had their licenses removed. There is a need for a concerted effort to deal with illegal operators."

 In 1989, that commissioner arranged a check on 5,500 vehicles in the east end of London. It showed that almost 10 per cent. did not display operators' licenses. Subsequent checks in another area showed that 30 per cent. of vehicles operated illegally. Illegal operation is a massive problem in London. Can the Minister assure that Committee that the abolition of the metropolitan traffic area office will not curtail checks on vehicles that operate illegally and are a danger to everyone.

JPEG 5 A traffic commissioner located in Eastbourne will not be as well informed about or sensitive to the considerable problems posed by illegal operators in the metropolitan area. I hope that the Minister will consider what further action should be taken to prevent the continued existence of illegal operators. As the Minister will know, the responsible sector of the industry, to which most operators belong, is pressing hard for more action to be taken against the cowboys. I hope that the Minister will say how the Government will compensate for the way in which the reorganisation may undermine some of the activities of the enforcement authorities.

There is also serious anxiety about the effects of reorganisation on traffic commissioners' responsibilities in relation to bus operators. The Palmer report, to which I have already referred, noted that a number of organisations, including the Association of Metropolitan Authorities, were worried that the commissioners do not conduct any systematic monitoring of registered bus services. The report made it clear that at present the traffic commissioners use their powers in relation to operators who fail to run bus services according to the registered details lodged under the Transport Act 1985 only when serious and substantial complaints of failure to run registered services are brought to their notice. The AMA considers that the public has a right to expect that the provisions of the Transport Act 1985 are efficiently and effectively implemented and policed. The spirit of the relevant provisions in sections 6 and 111 of that Act are that operators are required to run bus services in accordance with registered particulars, and that the responsibility for enforcing that requirement rests with the traffic commissioners. However, unlike other legislation, the 1985 Act contains no requirement that action should only be taken in response to a specific complaint. The commissioners can and should act without a specific complaint being brought to their notice. It would also be appropriate for them to monitor registered bus services systematically.

Finally, is the Minister content with the traffic commissioners' current performance in relation to the policing of the operation of bus services under the 1985 Act? What steps, if any, does he plan to take to improve that performance? There is a real anxiety that the requirement that the traffic commissioners should cover large areas without a commensurate increase in staff will mean that the information about bus registrations that they supply to local authorities will become even less timely than at present, and that the commissioners' resources for monitoring bus services and dealing with complaints will be even more stretched.

I do not apologise, Mr. Howells, for taking the Committee's time this morning, as I believe that the proposed reorganisation will have a significant deleterious effect on the policing of unlicensed vehicles and the monitoring of bus services. Moreover, operators and authorities are worried that the planned reorganisation will undermine the services carried out by the traffic commissioners in the area offices. I look forward to the Minister's response.

The eastern traffic area will lose Nottinghamshire and Derbyshire to the north-eastern and north-western areas, but will be extended south and west to include Buckinghamshire, Hertfordshire and Essex. I am sorry if I did not make that clear earlier, but I tried to keep my opening remarks as brief as possible to allow time for hon. Members to ask questions.

I hope that I have been able to allay anxiety about the reorganisation's effect on the quality of service. The reorganisation will reduce administrative costs and bureaucracy, thereby ensuring better value for money. It will not result in less adequate levels of enforcement, nor will it reduce the effectiveness of the commissioners, most of whom have to travel around taking public inquiries on the question of proposed operating centres. Frankly, it does not matter whether their desks are in Eastbourne or London, although it is obviously less expensive to site their main office and support staff in Eastbourne. The change will help us to make continuing savings as a result of lower rental costs and the ability to recruit clerical labour at a lower wage rate than in the metropolis.
