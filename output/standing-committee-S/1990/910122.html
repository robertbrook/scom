Value Added Tax (Construction of Dwellings and Land) Order 1990

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Treasury (Mrs. Gillian Shephard)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Value Added Tax (Construction of Dwellings and Land) Order 1990.

The Order will restore the taxation of the long-term leasing and sales of new holiday accommodation. In principle, the provision of holiday accommodation has always been taxable. However, long leases and sales of new holiday dwellings by builders or developers were zero-rated until 1 April 1989, which left mainly short-term holiday accommodation taxable at the standard rate. In the Finance Act 1989, the Government abolished the zero-rating, following the findings of the European Court in 1988, which stated that zero-rating for housing was appropriate when in pursuance of a clearly defined social policy. I stress those latter words, as it can hardly be the case that VAT relief for the long-leasing or sale of holiday chalets and the like, which cannot be used as permanent housing throughout the year, formed, or forms part of a social policy. It was the Government's intention to tax all forms of holiday accommodation, including sales and long leases.

The Government believed that the existing law, which referred broadly to the provision of holiday accommodation and defined it as accommodation advertised or held out as such, was sufficient for that purpose. A recent test case before a VAT tribunal, brought by Haven Leisure Ltd., has shown that that was not so and that the taxing provision should be interpreted more narrowly.

Representations have been made to hon. Members by the British Holiday and Home Parks Association and some of its members. I was pleased to meet and to discuss thoroughly the issues with a deputation from the association last week. Subsequently, the association's solicitor met Customs officials. The representations made much of the fact that the order was totally unexpected and that there was no prior consultation. It should not have been totally unexpected, because Customs officials' correspondence had made it clear all along to the association that taxation was intended. I can give the Committee details of that correspondence.

After the Haven Leisure decision Customs wrote to the association advising it that an appeal to the High Court was being considered and to await further developments. Obviously, it could not say that the law might be amended until the appeal to the High Court had been considered.

Given that the policy had been decided, announced and adhered to in 1989, the Government did not believe that further consultation was needed. The pol- 4 icy was decided in 1989. It was the legal means by which the policy had been put into effect that had been found wanting by the Haven Leisure decision.

In the context of a broad-based tax such as VAT, which applies to many necessities of life, there cannot be an objection of principle to taxing those who can affort to purchase holiday accommodation where such accommodation can be clearly defined.

The Government have taken the opportunity to tax the provision of plots for the construction of holiday chalets and other holiday accommodation, and to tidy up some of the legal provisions. Amendments to both group 8 of schedule 5 and group 1 of schedule 6 to the Value Added Tax Act 1983 are required to achieve that. However, I should like to emphasise that the long leasing of the sale by developers of homes that can be occupied throughout the year as principal private residences will not be affected by any of those provisions and will continue to be zero-rated. Nor will the short-term provision of accommodation other than holiday accommodation be taxed as a result of the changes. For the convenience of the Committee, I shall now explain the order in more detail.

Article 1 merely confirms the title of the order and its effective date of 1 February 1991.

Article 2 has a revised legal note (7) to group 8 of schedule 5, to the 1983 Act, which extends the existing limitations on the zero-rating of new dwellings in two ways. First, it prevents a developer of holiday homes seeking to avoid the tax charge by leasing or selling, as a separate transaction, the building plot for a holiday home that cannot be occupied throughout the year as a principal private residence. The site for such a home is now excluded from zero-rating.

Secondly, as well as a dwelling being excluded from zero-rating if residence throughout the year is precluded by the existence of a covenant, statutory planning consent or similar permission, a dwelling is also excluded if a covenant, statutory planning consent or similar permission prevents its use as the owner's principal private residence.

A note will reveal the answer. I may have flown off on that commonsense basis, but I hope that I was correct.

Article 3(a) contains a new paragraph (d) for taxing holiday accommodation by excluding it from the general exemption afforded by item 1 of group 1 of schedule 6. It is now made quite clear that it covers not only JPEG 5 the short-term provision of such accommodation but a long-lease or outright sale of such accommodation.

Article 3(b) refers to the new legal note (10), which refers back to the limitations on zero-rating for restricted-occupancy dwellings, and brings such dwellings, and the building plots for such dwellings, within the definition of holiday accommodation. The result is that sales or long leases of new dwellings that cannot be occupied throughout the year or as a person's principal private residence, because of a covenant, statutory planning consent or similar permission, are taxable as holiday accommodation, as are the building plots for such dwellings.

In article 3(c), a new legal note (10A) excludes from taxation the freehold sale or any premium paid for a leasehold sale of holiday accommodation that is not new. A building is defined as new in legal note (4) to group 1, as one that was completed less than three years before the date in which the interest in it was granted. The effect is to prevent the repeated taxation of holiday accommodation that comes back into the hands of the developer or holiday park owner for resale by him. However, any annual rental or other payment such as ground rent or payment for the upkeep of the holiday park and the provision of its facilities is taxable.

A further legal note (10B) defines holiday accommodation as including any accommodation in a building, hut—including a beach hut—caravan, houseboat or tent that is advertised or held out as holiday accommodation or as suitable for holiday or leisure use, but excludes hotel and similar accommodation. The effect of that note is, largely, to repeat the definition of holiday accommodation in the existing law, but it also makes it clear that it includes beach huts, and that it includes other accommodation, which, although it may not necessarily be held out or advertised for holiday use, is nevertheless held out for leisure use. That is necessary to prevent tax avoidance by the elimination of the word "holiday" from advertising brochures and its substitution by the word "leisure" or something similar. Hotel and similar accommodation is already taxed under paragraph (c) of item 1 of group 1.

In short, the order re-establishes the liability to VAT of freehold and leasehold sales of new accommodation when it is intended for holiday and leisure use only and is not suitable for use as permanent residential accommodation throughout the year because of planning restrictions on such use. I commend the Order to the Committee.

"whether he has any plans to ensur there is no discrimination as far as value-added tax is concerned between those people buying a purpose-built holiday home and those purchasing from current housing stock a dwelling for use as a second home: and if he will make a statement."

The Minister replied:

"There is, and should be, a distinction drawn for VAT purposes between second homes which are capable of being occupied throughout the year—and which may be treated as principal private residences—and purpose-built holiday homes which the buyers cannot occupy lawfully throughout the year or as their principal private residences. There is no reason why the latter should not be taxed under a broad based consumption tax such as VAT when other forms of holiday accommodation are taxed."

It would be churlish of me to say that there is nothing in that argument. Of course, there is something in it, but the question is where the balance lies. I believe that it lies the other way. If holiday accommodation is taxed, there will be a socially divisive wedge between people who can buy a holiday home that can be used as a principal private dwelling and pay no VAT whatever, and those people—generally not so wealthy—who will buy a holiday home and, because of the order, must pay value added tax on it. That is a bigger social division than the one to which the Minister referred. I cannot agree with her that a distinction should be drawn between those two classes of homes.

The Minister's reply continued:

"Failure to do so would conflict with European Community law." [ Official Report, 21 January 1991; Vol. 184, c. 13.]

That is where I take issue with the Minister. She mentioned that it was part of the sixth VAT directive, and I wish to dwell on that matter further. It could be said that the European Court of Justice rejected the Government's argument that all new buildings should be zero-rated, and it could be said that the decision required the United Kingdom to tax at the standard rate all new buildings that were not housing. In accordance with article 171 of the Treaty of Rome, it was up to the United Kingdom to implement the court's decision.

However, the decision of the European Court of Justice was much narrower. It upheld the European Commission's contention that the UK was in breach of its obligations under the sixth directive and the treaty, in that it applied the zero rate to the construction of industrial and commercial buildings and to community and civil engineering works. We all remember that famous judgment and we spent much time on it in our discussion of the Finance Bill in 1989.

The buildings that the proposed changes tax at the standard rate are clearly dwellings, not industrial or commercial buildings. The changes required by the European Court applied to commercial buildings. These are not commercial buildings—they are dwellings. It is my contention that the changes go beyond the requirements of article 171, in that they go beyond the decision of the court. I have a copy of a written answer on 6 February 1989 from the former Financial Secretary to the Treasury, the right hon. Member for St. Albans (Mr. Lilley). He stated:

"I am including several minor changes from the current provisions for construction and property in zero rate group 8 and exempt group 1 which do not flow directly from the European Court judgment. These have been inserted to rectify weaknesses in the law which have become apparent in recent years, often as a result of litigation. These changes are marked with an asterisk in the explanatory notes to the draft clauses."—[ Official Report, 6 February 1989; Vol. 146, c.549.]

JPEG 9 Clearly, there is no justification for this measure on the basis of the European Court judgment. There may be justification if the Government want more money, but that is a different argument. I beg the Minister not to try to justify introducing the Order because it is required by Europe, as that is not true. The Minister could argue that even if the decision in the infraction proceedings did not require the United Kingdom to implement the proposed changes, the European Community law requires the UK to standard-rate new dwellings when they can be used only as second homes. That would be a plausible argument. The Minister could say that it has nothing to do with the infraction proceedings, but they are second homes and should be standard-rated.

It could be argued that article 4(3) of the sixth directive imposes compulsory standard rating on all new buildings. That provision overrides the exemption for the supply of buildings in article 13B(g). The only authority for the United Kingdom not fully to implement article 4(3) is contained in article 28(2). To satisfy that condition, an exclusion from standard rating must satisfy the last indent of article 17 of the second directive. That indent allows reduced rates or exemption with refund to be retained if in force on 31 December 1975, if the measure is taken for clearly defined social reasons and for the benefit of the final consumer. It is on that that the Minister will probably hinge her argument because that is the place where social reasons are defined.

It could be said that dwellings that could not be occupied for a whole year cannot fulfil a housing need. Let us suppose that someone has two holiday homes. That may be an extreme example, but someone may occupy one holiday home for six months of the year and another for the other six months. He would have to pay VAT on the purchase of those two holiday homes, yet one of them, or perhaps both, should be regarded as a principal private dwelling. Anyone in those circumstances would clearly be disadvantaged. However, I shall not make too much of that as it is a rather extreme example.

The last indent of article 17 of the second directive provides for a reduced rate of VAT or exemption with refund. The existing exemption for long leases of restricted-occupation dwellings is neither a reduced rate nor an exemption with refund. Therefore, it is irrelevant whether those chalets fulfil a clearly defined social purpose. They do not specifically fall within any of the provisions when it is mandatory under the second and sixth directives to subject them to the standard rate of tax. The Minister's arguments on that matter are slightly wrong.

Second-home dwellings constitute housing and as such satisfy the social need test. The Advocate General gave his opinion on the infraction proceedings, which we had to put right in the Finance Act 1989. Paragraph 26 states:

"If it was for the court to consider whether or not the decision to apply a zero-rate in respect of the construction of private housing was well founded, it could undoubtedly hold that measures of such a discriminate nature afflict the very wide view of the social reasons relied on. As I have already said, however, the exercise of such a supervisory role will impinge on the powers of the Member States. In any event, the approach adopted by the United Kingdom, that is to say facilitating home ownership for the whole population, clearly 10 does not go beyond the discretion which it undoubtedly retains in this field."

It is my contention that the Government have discretion in the matter and need not put standard rate value added tax on second homes. They should not argue that they must do so because of Community legislation. If they argue that it is for other reasons, such as needing the money, that is a different matter.

I hope that having listened to what I have said, the Minister is prepared to think again about a problem that is important to the industry involved in supplying second homes and for those who wish to buy them. There may be existing contracts that will suddenly be subject to VAT. That could drastically change firms' and customers' budgeting. I ask the Minister to withdraw the order, consult the industry and return with new proposals later in the Session.
