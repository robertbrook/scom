The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Mr. Michael Forsyth): I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of fishing and fish-farming in Scotland.

Fishing and fish-farming are rarely far from the top of the Scottish political agenda, and rightly so. They are important industries which make a sizeable contribution to the Scottish economy and are the life-blood of many of our coastal communities.

The fishing industry currently employs more than 8,000 fishermen and more than £267 million worth of fish was landed into Scotland in 1990. That is backed up by a substantial fish-processing industry and by services such as boat-building. The Government are acutely aware that this is a difficult time for the Scottish fishing industry and those who depend on it. A run of poor year classes of cod and haddock, combined with continuing high levels of fishing pressure have led to a serious decline in those stocks; and that has inevitably affected those who fish mainly for those species.

I am at a disadvantage in this debate because I do not have ministerial responsibility for this subject, but I know enough to recognise how difficult and intractable are the problems of the industry. My noble Friend the Minister with responsibility for agriculture and fisheries, who was at the latest Fisheries Council meeting earlier this week, is working hard to achieve a European solution on technical conservation. He has to achieve a proper balance between the short-term interests of present fishermen and the interests of their successors in another generation. In addition, we are not free agents in this area of policy and have to secure agreement in the European Community.

All of that is difficult enough without the kind of outrageous attacks that we have witnessed recently, particularly from the hon. Member for Banff and Buchan (Mr. Salmond). I am sure that all of us on the Conservative Benches would wish my noble Friend well in the negotiations that must continue in Brussels in the autumn. We can contrast his excellent efforts on behalf of the Scottish industry with the negative views expressed by the hon. Member for Banff and Buchan.

The issues facing the fishing industry today include key issues such as effort limitation, selective fishing gear and fleet structure. I want to bear in mind the broader context of European fisheries management and conservation, and, in particular, the issues arising from the mid-term review of the common fisheries policy—the CFP—which is taking place between now and the end of 1992. The Government have taken the lead within Europe on conservation of fish stocks and my noble Friend fights resolutely for the interests of the Scottish fishing industry.

The CFP was agreed in January 1983 after protracted discussions and endless council meetings. I pay tribute to the role in those negotiations of my right hon. Friend the Member for Kincardine and Deeside. (Mr. Buchanan-Smith). I know that he would have liked to be here today and I am sure that the Committee wishes him a speedy recovery from his recent period in hospital.

The Government achieved their two main objectives in the negotiations—restricting access to our inshore waters and securing a high share of the fish for United Kingdom fishermen. That affects Scottish fishermen especially as they take 75 per cent of the United Kingdom catch. Some member states believe the United Kingdom was too successful in the negotiations and we must defend our position against periodic attempts to chip away at what we achieved in 1983. The CFP was designed to apply for 20 years until 2002 with a limited mid-term review in 1992. The only aspects of the policy which the European Community is committed to review next year are access to coastal waters and the north of Scotland box.

The main issues that the review will cover are already becoming clear. A communication from the European Commission at the end of last year coincided with the Government's analysis of the position. The basic framework of the CFP is sound. The arrangements on access to fisheries and the relative stability that underlines the allocation of quotas have served the Community well and should be retained.

Mr. Forsyth: I am grateful for your protection, Mr. Hogg. The hon. Gentleman is correct—local fishermen would like the area of the Shetland box to be extended and the number of vessels allowed to fish in it reduced. We shall consider that idea sympathetically.

The United Kingdom will hold the presidency of the Council of Ministers during the second half of 1992 when the review reaches a critical stage. The Government will try to bring the review to a successful conclusion with a stronger CFP to take us through the following 10 years.

The present rules largely restrict access to waters within 12 miles of the United Kingdom to United Kingdom fishermen, except where there are historic rights of access by other member states. The system of total allowable catches and quotas provides a mechanism for establishing the shares of each stock that each member state is to receive each year through the principle of relative stability, which safeguards the United Kingdom share of the stocks. In addition, the Hague preference arrangement recognises the importance of fishing to the northern part of the United Kingdom, including Scotland and Ireland.

The setting of quotas, however, is meaningless unless adequate arrangements are in place to ensure that those quotas are adhered to. The Commission communication has recognised that in most member states, the level of enforcement of fisheries rules must be improved. The United Kingdom was singled out as the only member state putting adequate resources into the surveillance of fishing activities; but we must not be complacent about that. In Scotland, our fisheries protection services was reorganised into an agency in April this year. It has been set clear performance targets, which should lead to greater efficiency through better co-ordination of enforcement services at sea, on land, and in the air. We will be pressing to ensure that all member states meet their obligation to enforce the fisheries regulations.

The basic conservation problems facing the Scottish industry are common across the European Community. Fishing pressure on most stocks is too high, and too few fish are being left in the sea. That is hopeless short-termism, which totally ignores the interests of future generations of fishermen.

The first strand to a conservation policy must be to make fishing gear more selective so that juvenile fish escape. My noble Friend the Minister has been taking a leading role within the Community in pressing for urgent measures on this. We are faced on the one hand with Commission proposals that are so extreme that very few fish would be caught at all. On the other hand, there are a few member states who are opposed to any meaningful progress on conservation. My noble Friend and other ministerial colleagues have been building an alliance with other member states who see, as we do, that action on conservation is necessary. We have persuaded them of the merits of the introduction of the square mesh panel and they agree the case for a moderate increase in mesh size. The Commission's proposals for 110 or 120 mm mesh are totally unacceptable.

In view of the enormous importance to our fishermen of some of the stocks we have taken Col. 6 unilateral action meantime by requiring our larger fishing vessels which go after cod, haddock and whiting to incorporate a 90 mm square mesh panel into their nets, which will result in a great many small fish surviving which would otherwise have been caught, and then thrown back into the sea dead. That is an important step towards conserving the stocks.

In the longer term, we are talking with the Commission and our Community partners about a strategy for reducing fishing mortality. The immediate reaction from some quarters is to propose a decommissioning scheme. By popular request I shall clarify the Government's position on that matter.

We accept that there is surplus capacity in the United Kingdom's fishing fleet. However, we are not persuaded that a grant-aided decommissioning scheme would be either the best way in which to tackle the problem or that it would be an effective way of doing so. However, we do not have a closed mind on the issue. [Laughter.] Hon. Members will no doubt be delighted to hear that. We will be prepared to consider a decommissioning scheme if it is part of an appropriate conservation package, and provided that the industry itself is prepared to make a contribution.

When I say that decommissioning is not, in our view, the best way in which to tackle overcapacity, I have two points in mind. First, we cannot overlook the serious criticisms made by the Public Accounts Committee of the previous decommissioning scheme. I accept that those criticisms were particular to the details of that scheme. Nevertheless, it is bound to be difficult to ensure good value for money in such expenditure. The appropriate size of the fleet and, therefore, the appropriate level to which it should be reduced, is a moving target that will depend on the level of fish stocks at a particular time, the prices they bring and the input costs that go into catching them. The Government are not best placed to take decisions of that kind although the EC does attempt to prescribe desirable fleet sizes through its so-called multi-annual guidance programme.

When I said that I was not persuaded that decommissioning would be effective, I had in mind that under the present system of full pressure-stock licences, we could easily spend a lot of money decommissioning a number of vessels only to discover other fishermen fishing more intensively with the remaining vessels. Those other fishermen would, of course, secure a better economic reward—I acknowledge that. However, the pressure on the fish stocks would not be correspondingly reduced. Before we attempt to embark on decommissioning, we need to be a lot more confident that that would not happen.

Finally, it does seem not unreasonable to ask the industry to contribute to any decommissioning scheme since the benefits of the scheme would, of course, accrue to the remaining part of the industry in the form of enhanced fish stocks and better catches in future years.
