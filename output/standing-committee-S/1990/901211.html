legislation Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 (Codes of Practice) (No. 2) Order 1990

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Peter Lloyd): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 (Codes of Practice) (No. 2) Order 1990.

My right hon. Friend the Secretary of State, in accordance with section 67(7) of the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984, may, from time to time, revise any codes of practice issued under that Act. He is required to lay any such revised codes before Parliament for its approval and to bring them into force by statutory instrument.

You have, Mr. Hill, chaired a number of Committees in which I have spoken on behalf of the Government. You therefore know that I try to be brief to allow time for questions from members of the Committee. However, this is a complex matter, which is of great public interest and importance. It would therefore be useful if I explained the changes being made to particular codes.

Mr. Andrew F. Bennett (Denton and Reddish): I intervene to apologise on behalf of my hon. Friends on the Opposition Front Bench who are in difficulty because they are supposed to be serving on this Committee and on that considering the Criminal Justice Bill. When my hon. Friend the Member for Huddersfield (Mr. Sheerman) has moved his amendment to that Bill, he will return to this Committee to speak. He means no discourtesy, but it is impossible to be in two places at once.

Mr. Lloyd: I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman. I understand the position. Sometimes one is required to sit on two Committees and an hon. Member must be in the appropriate Committee, if he is moving an amendment.

The existing first four codes of practice cover the police's exercise of their powers of stop and search; the search of premises and the seizure of property; the detention, treatment and questioning of suspects; and the identification of persons by the police. They came into force on 1 January 1986 as a bold novelty—the first ever attempt at the comprehensive regulation of police procedures with regard to suspects—and, in the main, they have been a success.

Before the introduction of the codes, allegations of police malpractice were commonplace and the practice of "verballing" —the falsification of interview notes by the police—was allegedly widespread. Certainly it took place. The strict provisions in the codes for the contemporaneous noting—now tape recording—of statements made in an interview under caution have made it difficult to fabricate evidence in that way. Similarly, the creation of the role of custody officer who has sole responsibility for the suspect's welfare while in custody and that of the custody record, which details everything which happens to a suspect in Col. 4 custody and a copy of which he may take with him when he leaves custody, have largely done away with allegations of physical abuse that used to be made almost routinely by defendants.

The codes represent a major step forward in protecting the suspect and making the police more accountable. Breaches of the code are disciplinary offences and may result in the exclusion of evidence at the trial. Because the codes were such an innovation, however, it was Parliament's intention to keep them under review. After five years of experience there are a number of additions and improvements that can usefully be made. The review process, which has been very thorough and has taken nearly three years, has produced comments and suggestions from a wide range of individuals and organisations, including those representing the police, the legal profession, civil liberties interests and community groups. Wherever there has been disagreement we have tried to reach the best compromise we can, balancing the practical demands of effective policing against the need to safeguard the interests of those coming into contact with the police.

I shall go through the codes in turn and say a little about the changes that are proposed.

Code A covers the stopping and seaching of people who are thought to be carrying weapons or stolen goods. The police have made it clear that they are not happy with the guidance it currently contains about the level of suspicion —"reasonable" suspicion—which is required before they can use their powers. They have found the wording of the guidance contained in the annex to the code so restrictive that, in many cases, it is easier to arrest someone than to justify a compulsory stop and search. That defeats the original intention of Parliament, which was to give the police a useful power which stopped short of arrest when that was unnecessary. Only about one in six or one in seven stops leads to an arrest; all the other people who are stopped may go free at once.

We have attempted to shorten and simplify the guidance on stop and search to make it easier to understand and apply, while retaining the important safeguards agsinst stopping people on the basis of racial or other stereotyped images.

Code B deals with the searching of premises and the siezure of property. The comments we have received suggest that it is working well. The police are, it seems, scrupulously following the guidelines and there is no suggestion that they ever exceed their powers. However, there was a general feeling that there should be a better system for telling someone whose premises are being searched about the extent of his rights and those of the police. With that in mind, we have proposed the introduction of a new notice of powers and rights which the police will give to the occupier of premises that are to be searched. It will explain the powers under which the police are acting, what they are allowed to do and to what the occupier is entitled. We all realise that having one's home searched is a traumatic experience and we hope the new notice will help to ensure at least that people are properly informed about what is going on, and under what power.

Code C, which is important, covers detention, treatment and questioning, and it received easily the most attention during our review. There were two main concerns—access Image Col. 5 to legal advice and the recording and verification of conversations between police and suspects. Major changes are proposed in both areas.

While there was some initial disagreement on legal advice between the police on one side and the legal profession and academics on the other, the consensus that emerged was that the code as it stands contains loopholes which enable the police to impede a suspect's access to legal advice. I am certainly not suggesting that that happens as a matter of course, but research, notably by the university of Birmingham, suggests that some police officers sometimes use what are described as "ploys" to prevent or delay access. Examples might include reading a suspect his rights too quickly, suggesting that legal advice will not help him, or emphasising that it might lead to a longer period in custody. We are putting forward a number of amendments to the code intended to ensure that a suspect will get full and fair access to legal advice. The draft revision will place emphasis on the custody officer giving the suspect clear information about his right to free legal advice and acting quickly in response to any request to see a lawyer. There will be a specific prohibition on dissuading suspects from getting legal advice by any means and posters advertising the right will be displayed in all charge rooms. There will be a new requirement for reminders of the right to access to be given prior to any interview, detention review, indentity parade or before taking an intimate sample. Also, greater emphasis will be placed on providing the information for non-English speakers in translation. Linked to all that will be a new and tight restriction on the circumstances in which interviewing can take place away from the police station. That will be based on Court of Appeal decisions and means that interviews outside the police station, which are usually conducted without legal advice, will be substantially ruled out.

The other main improvement proposed for code C involves a significant strengthening of the rules for recording and verifying interviews and other comments made by suspects. The new definition of an interview as the questioning of a person regarding his involvement or suspected involvement in a criminal offence or offences is wide-ranging and borrows from a series of Court of Appeal judgments. Under the revised code, the interviewee will normally have an opportunity to read the record and sign it as correct, or say how he disagrees with it. Also—and very importantly—a similar system will apply to comments made outside an interview that might be relevant to the offence.

In summary, the main purpose of our work in revising code C has been to improve information about, and access to, legal advice, while at the same time tightening the rules on recording what is said and checking those records. I think that the result is a fairer system that will provide better evidence and increase public confidence in it. I will not, however, go through all the other changes we are proposing to code C, many of which are of a fairly minor nature.

Mr. Andrew F. Bennett: May I press the Minister about the recording evidence, especially where statements are recorded on tape? Col. 6

Mr. Lloyd: The hon. Member raises a difficult problem of practicality because, although the full statement is available and its accuracy as a true record is verified or otherwise by the interviewee, the Crown prosecution service requires a summary of the statement when deciding whether to prosecute. Various methods of providing that summary have been tried, for example, it may be made at a later date or at the same time as the original statement, especially when the officer himself summarises the main points of the interview for the CPS and the interviewee agrees them. The hon. Gentleman has put his finger on something on which there has been no final agreement about what is the best practice. Efforts are made to find a method that places as little pressure as possible on police time—the hon. Gentleman will appreciate that the codes demand much police time. The police will, however, produce the necessary summary material—for the CPS in particular —in a manner that the suspect accepts and approves.

There is a new requirement in paragraph 3.2 for detained persons to be provided with a notice of entitlements. We have been encouraged to introduce that notice—which will supplement the main notice of basic rights—by several boards of lay visitors, who have pointed out that the text of the codes, which people at police stations are entitled to consult, are difficult to understand. The new notice will address, in simple language, such things as visits, food and drink, toilets and washing facilities, clothing, medical attention and exercise.

Under code D, there has been significant re-organisation of the material covering identification of suspects by witnesses. The main addition proposed is a whole new section about identification using video technology. The police have already begun making use of "video" parades in certain circumstances to replace traditional identity parades. The witness is shown a video film of a sequence of persons, including the suspect, and is invited to make an identification. That can be very useful where geographical factors, or the fact that there are a large number of witnesses who cannot all be brought together, make it difficult to use conventional methods. The new provisions have been drawn up in close consultation with the Metropolitan police who have already had success with them.

If approved, the revised codes will come into force on 1 April 1991. Officers will receive training in advance of that date on the basis of a training package that is being devised centrally. Further guidance on the codes and the layouts for the new forms will be included in a Home Office circular to the police that will be issued in the new year.

The review shows that the codes have been a successful innovation and that selective improvement rather than wholesale redrafting is necessary. We have done that. Obviously, we have not been able to please everyone, but I am satisfied that the new codes will be better than their predecessors in guiding the police in the exercise of their powers and in protecting the well-being of a suspect and the integrity of his evidence.

Mr. Andrew F. Bennett: I believe that the new codes were intended to come into practice on 1 January, but that, following further consultation, they will now do so on 1 April. What changes were made following those further consultations?

Image Col. 7

Mr. Lloyd: Some of the original changes made in the summer have been withdrawn. One change, for example, related to obtaining an inspector's approval to carry out a search of a prisoner's home. It is not always practicable, however, to follow that practice because an inspector is not always available. We want to find a way in which such a search could be undertaken in exceptional circumstances without written approval and that will be covered by the circular to be issued in January.
