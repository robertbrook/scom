Mr. Robert Maclennan
(Caithness and Sutherland)
: Whatever criticisms are made of the present structure, it should be stated at the outset that the efforts of local councillors in Scotland are not given public acclaim as often as they should be. That goes back a long way. Ayrshire Members, especially, will recall the description by John Galt in his famous novel "The Provost" of the public perception of how local councillors operate in their local area. One episode in the novel relates how Provost Pawkie tried to introduce public lamps in the royal borough of Gudetown. He was opposed in that valuable initiative by three people. One was a Mr. Hickberry of clearly Thatcherite tendencies, who suggested that, rather than public lighting, the inhabitants of Gudetown should be ordered to keep their shutters open at night. The second opponent Mr. Dribbles, the inn keeper, warned the council that folk returning home would not like the profane populace to see them after dark. Mr. Dippings, the candlemaker, felt that public lighting would encourage the commonality to keep late hours and that the gentry would have no need of them because they had glass lanterns with two candles each.

That image of local authorities is too ofen perpetuated. It makes amusing reading, but it does not fairly represent the long hours, expense, travel and personal sacrifice that councillors put into local government. Although Provost Pawkie got no support for his efforts in Gudetown, he would have been pleased about developments in the Highland regional council since reorganisation, as there is now scarcely a township that is not lit by sodium lamps.

The Government announced their proposal in any detail, and then not very much, only this week, so we cannot give a detailed response to the pale blue paper—it is not a Green Paper—asking for advice on the structure of local government. So my comments will be of a preliminary nature as I and my right hon. and hon. Friends intend to consult widely in local government, particularly with councillors in our constituencies. Even in the short consultation period allowed, I hope that it will be possible to provide answers.

Today I shall speak of some general principles that the Government have enunciated and others that they should bear in mind in proposing a further reorganisation. Provost Pawkie was operating in a system of local government in Scotland that remained unchanged between 1473 and 1833. Changes have been rather more frequent in the post-war period and such unsettlement carries a cost.

The Government have asked for comments on how to minimise the disruption. One area for consideration is what to do about capital debts that local authorities have accumulated. Will loan charges be met by 100 per cent funding from the Government? Such detail would more appropriately be left until later.

It is instructive to go back to the most exhaustive examination of local government in Scotland in modern times. It took place under the Wheatley commission, which looked carefully at the principles that should underline restructuring and asked questions JPEG 45 that are as germane and relevant to the debate today as they were then. Furthermore, those questions help us to judge whether the structure that was backed by Parliament has achieved what was hoped of it when it was put forward with broad cross-party support.

The first principle that Wheatley espoused was that

"Local government should he enabled to play a more important, responsible and positive part in the running of the country—to bring the reality of government nearer to the people."

It is doubtful that the local government reforms have achieved that principle. However, the reason for that has less to do with the structure of local government than the propensity of central Government, whether Conservative or Labour, to draw more and more power—especially financial power—to the centre.

I was speaking of the failure of the last reorganisation to enhance the powers of responsible local government. Consideration of new reforms ought to go beyond structure. I agree with my hon. Friend the Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire (Mr. Kirkwood). He reminded the Committee the other day of the attitude of the late John Mackintosh who, in a powerful series of articles and speeches, drew attention to the folly of considering reform of finance, structure and function separately. The Government seem to be institutionalising that disjunction in a way that makes a coherent debate on the future of local government almost impossible.

Wheatley's second objective of reorganisation was:

"Local government should be equipped to provide services in the most satisfactory manner, particularly from the point of view of the people receiving the services."

My experience of the highland region has given me a sense that the structure of local government there has not impeded the provision of good and valuable personal services. Where there are shortcomings, local authorities are able to recognise them and set them right, although it is not always certain that they have the resources.

Wheatley's third objective was the enhancement of local democracy, which 46

"should constitute a system in which power is exercised through the elected representatives of the people, and in which those representatives are locally accountable for its exercise."

That has perhaps turned out to be the area of greatest weakness, certainly in the highlands. For that reason alone, reorganisation would be justified. Wheatley called for a change that would ensure that councillors should not be trammelled by being accountable to some other body than the electors. However, in the exercise of their functions, councillors are hamstrung by central government, to whom they are accountable for almost everything that they do. Their scope for decision-making has been systematically whittled away.

Wheatley also noted that local authority members must be able to exercise their power and responsibility without practical hindrance. In some areas of Scotland, distance is a severe practical hindrance to the proper exercise of power and responsibility. In my constituency, regional councillors have to make round trips of 260 miles to attend regional council committee meetings. That is a practical impediment to effective service.

The Wheatley commission was exercised about the need to have a wide range of candidates at the elections. It is striking how many council seats, particularly in highland region, have not been contested in local elections. We must assume that that is because of the difficulty of service in the regional councils that afflicts those without independent means or those who are not self-employed. That serious problem has resulted in a narrowing of the democratic base and must be kept in mind in determining the geographical extent of the proposed unitary authorities.

Wheatley warned of such danger and spoke also of avoiding

"emptying "local" of all its content, with the result that too many decisions are taken on a plain which to the man in the street seems just as rarefied and inaccessible as St. Andrew's House or Whitehall."

That fear has been justified and confusion remains in planning about who is responsible for what. Many decisions are actually made at St. Andrew's House or Whitehall.

I should have preferred the paper to contain a more strongly-argued case for strengthening democracy in local government in Scotland. That would include widening local government's competence by statute to enable it to fulfil locally perceived needs according to financial capacity and not, as has happened, a progressive restriction on local government that excludes it from more functions by transferring them to unaccountable bodies, such as housing associations and St. Andrew's House, and to non-appointed bodies, such as the local enterprise companies which are responsible for promoting local development. In their paper—a pale blue paper—the Government speak of the importance of forging links between those bodies. That is true, but the need to strengthen local government is scarcely recognised.

How effective has local government been since 1973? I find that difficult to judge for Scotland, but there has been a debit side and a credit side in my JPEG 47 constituency. Major capital works, which have been embarked on and completed, have been beneficial to the community. They include building Kylescu bridge in west Sutherland, the money that the roads and transport committee put into the development of the ports of Kinlochbervie and Lochinver and many good works in social service provision. However, ultimately the restructuring of local government, which took place then, has not proved beneficial.

There are great gaps in the provision of the roads network in my constituency. The regional council had to stand on the sidelines and call on the Government—so far with virtually no response—to bring up to a proper modern standard the main arterial road, the A9, which runs north to John O'Groats. Local government has not succeeded in making effective that basic infrastructure requirement. However, other road problems are more directly within its power to tackle, following the Government's decision to end the Crofter counties road scheme: for example, the A836 along the north coast of Scotland and the A838 from Durness, Laxford Bridge to Lairg. Those roads have barely improved beyond a medieval condition, although they carry millions of pounds worth of fish from the port of Kinlochbervie to the south. Lorries hurtle down the roads—they have no choice. They are a threat to safety. It is more difficult to gain access to the market and the region has been able to do nothing about it. The reform of local government cannot worsen that specific predicament.

There are other examples of confusion, such as the proposed redevelopment of Pulteneytown in Wick. That was first mooted in 1970 by the royal burgh council and was subsequently tossed back and forth between the Highland regional council and Caithness district council. It was left to the Scottish Development Agency and then returned to the Highlands and Islands Development Board, which doubted that it had funds to carry out such environmental improvements. The reorganised local government has failed to make an appreciable impact on a long-standing problem of modernisation and environmental renewal in the former county town of Caithness in 20 years. That is an indictment.

Most schools in my constituency were built before local government reform occurred. One project, to build a school on the west coast of Sutherland, was approved by the late Frank McElhone when he was Under-Secretary of State for Scotland in the last Labour Government in 1979. That project has never been funded. It still cries out for funds. It does not receive the priority that it deserves under existing local government structure.

Those examples show the need for change. I can give another instance of post-Stodart tinkering. The responsibility for leisure and recreation in the highland region has been transferred to the district council. Consequently, the proposal for the construction of a swimming pool in Wick was snatched away. It has not been built, although the local authority intends to go 48 ahead. It is a hugely expensive project, which will drain the limited resources of a lower-tier authority.

I have given illustrations of a situation that is not ideal. However, when the Secretary of State considers the principles for reform, I hope that he will examine the cases for a fairer system of election. In some parts of Scotland—I do not refer to the Highland regional council, where the problems are different—minorities within communities are not effectively represented. All sections are not necessarily represented by the first-past-the-post system.

The key consideration for the Committee is the relationship between central and local government in Scotland and the proper balance of power between them. The Secretary of State would have us believe that the Government are not a centralising Government but are keen to encourage a pluralistic approach to government in Scotland. The facts do not support his arguments. The facts put the Government right in the frame over the centralising of power, responsibility and influence here in undemocratic Westminster.

The Secretary of State can cap local government expenditure. He can abolish or cut housing support grant and Revenue support grant and limit the amount of money that local councils can borrow. The Government are the most centralising Government in the entire post-war era of Scottish political history, mainly—but not always—by deliberate intent.

Sometimes the Government centralises when they do not need to do so. We were told that the poll tax was introduced to make councils directly accountable to the local electorate. We now know that it did not work out as it was intended. When the poll tax was scrapped, so was any semblance of electoral accountability. So the Government achieved the very opposite of what they intended.

The current make up of the Government was established by an internal Cabinet coup last November in a blind electoral panic. One of the first actions of that Government was to transfer £4.25 billion from local to central control across the whole United Kingdom. The situation was best summed up in an editorial on 22 March in the Financial Times, which normally does not support the Labour party. It said:

"A tier of Government dependent on central Government for almost 90 per cent of its resources and controlled not only in its overall spending but in the detail of much of what it delivers, barely deserves the name of Government."

That is the sorry state to which the Government have delivered local government in Scotland, England and Wales.
