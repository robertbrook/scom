Mr. John Fraser
(Norwood)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Civil Legal Aid (Assessment of Resources) (Amendment) Regulations 1991.

The Chairman
: With the agreement of the Committee, it will be convenient to discuss at the same time the Legal Advice and Assistance (Amendment) Regulations 1991 and the Legal Aid in Criminal and Care Proceedings (General) (Amendment) Regulations 1991.

Mr. Fraser
: I wish to make it clear that the Opposition wanted to debate the three regulations on the Floor of the House and the Government denied us that opportunity, for reasons that I understand. There is pressure of business on the Floor of the House. Had we had that opportunity, we would have voted against the regulations, which are regressive, mean and representative of a further increase in the intensity with which the Government are besieging the legal aid system.

The regulations raise the floor below which a person who needs civil representation, advice or a criminal defence need not pay anything. The minimum disposable income above which one must contribute will be raised to £55 a week. Disposable income is what is left after payment of travelto-work expenses and certain housing expenses; if it is less than £55, the person will henceforth not have to make any contribution. For eligibility for legal advice—generally called the green form system—the minimum income is raised to £70 a week, and for criminal legal aid to about £65 a week. Below those figures, no contribution is necessary.

Those provisions are bad enough, because the figures do not follow the rise in average earnings. However, perhaps the greatest objection to the regulations is that they do not raise the upper limits of income or capital and consequently many people will be outside the scope of the legal aid system. The upper limit for civil legal aid remains £122 of disposable income a week. No legal aid is available above that limit except in personal injuries cases, where the upper limit is £134 a week. I shall not go into the details of the advice and criminal legal aid limits, as the figures are broadly similar.

The regulations do not raise the capital limits; the present limit is £8,000, above which there is no eligibility for legal aid. In the past, it has generally been the practice to update the limits yearly, broadly in line with the increases in limits applied for the purposes of social security payments. The increase in the lower levels is less than the rise in wage levels. Because the increase follows the social security index rather than wages index, proportionately more people will have to 4 pay for advice and assistance. The 1990 increase was less than both the wages index and the retail prices index and therefore even the lower limit represents a further substantial application of pressure on the legal aid system. The Opposition calculate that the absence of revised upper limits will leave about 1.1 million people beyond the scope of legal aid. For those reasons, the regulations are vindictive and mean. They put greatest pressure on the people from whom the Government should canvass support. Those are people who go out to work and who have a moderate income and moderate savings. They are the people to whom the regulations will be vindictively applied.

Because there has been no review of the upper limits, the capital limit for eligibility for legal advice under the scheme remains at £800. If someone saves more than £800 but has a low income, it will be impossible for him or her to obtain any advice or assistance under what is known as the green form scheme. If the capital limit had been raised even in line with inflation, it would be at least £250 higher. The failure to increase the limit is a real and painful suppression of the right to advice. The suppression of the ability to obtain advice is the suppression of people's rights, because rights are useless without access to the advice and assistance that is necessary to enforce them.

In April 1989, the single person's upper limit of disposable income beyond which civil legal aid was not available was below £120. If it were linked to the retail prices index, that upper limit would be raised today to £193, but the regulations do nothing to raise the limit. To keep roughly the same percentage of the population within the scope of the legal aid scheme we must consider the earnings index. That index suggests that the upper limit for single persons for civil representation and advice should be raised to £234, but it is only £122. That is a savage and cynical reduction of people's ability to assert their rights.

It was reckoned that between 1979 and 1989 about 5.5 million households—about 14 million people—lost the right to legal aid. In 1991, the number of people disqualified from the legal aid scheme will be even greater and it has been authoritatively recalculated that about 37 per cent. of the population will now be eligible for legal aid as a result of the regulations and the failure to update limits. When the Labour party introduced the legal aid scheme in 1948, about 90 per cent. of the population were eligible for civil legal aid—at that time, there was criminal legal aid.

Mr. Fraser

Whenever the lower limit reflects inflation and not earnings, more people are driven to contribute and more people are driven out of eligibility. Whenever the upper limits, whether of capital or of income, fail to reflect the rise in average earnings, or as in the regulations, fail to be increased at all, more people are driven out of eligibility for legal aid. Whenever both limits are lower that the rate of inflation—and recently they have been—more people are driven out of the scope of legal assistance. More people then ever before are being driven out by the regulations' failure to increase the upper limits.

I tabled a parliamentary question to the Attorney-General on that matter, to discover whether the Government had introduced a new policy of freezing the upper limits. The Attorney-General replied:

"It is not the Lord Chancellor's policy to freeze the upper limits of capital and income for legal aid eligibility. A consultation paper will be issued".—[ Official Report, 28th March 1991; Vol. 188, c. 483.]

That consultation paper came out yesterday and was given to Opposition Members a few minutes ago. What one divines from that parliamentary answer, and from a debate on Scots regulations, which are almost identical in their content, is that the freezing of the upper limit is connected with the Lord Chancellor's consultations and review document. That is pretty cynical. It is almost deceitful to say that fewer people will be eligible for legal aid because there is a consultation document that gives them absolutely nothing. That is an odd way to deal with matters.

We are greadually moving towards a first-class legal service that is available only to companies, to the state, to people who have adequate resources and to those whose legal costs are tax deductible an who receive a VAT input from them. A first-class legal service will be available to those who can afford to pay for it. There will also be a second-class legal service—very often the present legal aid service—because, if one looks at the rates of remuneration for legal aid work and at the constraints imposed upon it, one can see that it is a second-class service. There will be people who fall between those two categories, who do not qualify for legal aid, but who are not rich enough to take proceedings without financial assistance. For those who fall in the middle there may be no service whatever.

All that is against a background of crippling cuts in funding to advice services and law centres. It is not as if the shortcomings in the legal aid scheme are being made up elsewhere. We know that, as a result of poll tax capping, advice centres and law centres are closing in many boroughs throughout the United Kingdom. All that the Government offer as an excuse for freezing the upper limit—and that is the Opposition's greatest objection to the regulations—is a bran tub of gimmicks in the form of the consultation document. The central issue of that document is the safety net proposals under which people may have to use their life savings to start legal proceedings before they know whether they will receive legal aid.

The regulations constitute the most frightful and cynical attack on the legal aid scheme that I have seen during my time on the Front Bench. the Opposition wanted the JPEG 9 regulations to be debated on the Floor of the House and, if we had the opportunity to vote, we should certainly have voted against them.

The Solicitor-General
: Certainly, Mr. Hogg. It is a difficult point because legal aid is almost always discussed in broad terms. Eligibility upratings are usually considered in the context of the whole picture. Naturally, when the Lord Chancellor takes the initiative of publishing a consultation document, the House is interested in it and it is relevant to the uprating. The annual uprating can be debated by itself, but it cannot be debated in a complete vacuum. I shall do my best, but I wish to help and inform the public debate.

With regard to this year's uprating, it has been the practice to increase legal aid eligibility limits in April each year, in line with the uprating of income-related social security benefits—that is, the RPI exclusive of housing costs. Housing costs are allowed separately in calculating an individual's disposable income. However, this year, only the free income limit has been raised, by 8.1 per cent. The upper limit for eligibility remains the same. The purpose of uprating the lower or free income limit, while we consider a new system for those at the upper end of the eligibility band, was to continue to protect the most needy—those eligible for free legal aid—pending the outcome of the first stage of the review of the financial conditions.

The way in which people pay for legal aid should be put in the context of the wider debate. Unless litigants are among the 80 per cent. who receive legal aid absolutely free, they pay a contribution. The contribution paid depends on their income within the span of the eligibility limit, from the point where a tiny contribution is paid, just above the free level, to the point where the maximum contribution is paid. The maximum contribution, for those just within the income limits, can run to many hundred pounds or even to a few thouand pounds.

There are two factors behind the Lord Chancellor's thinking on the uprating for this year. First, we need to address the rapidly escalating costs of legal aid. I shall come to the point about the cost of litigation that was sensibly raised by the hon. Member for Sunderland, South (Mr. Mullin). The second point is how we should deploy the moneys that the legally aided litigant who pays a contribution has to pay out in one form or another. The Lord Chancellor has put foward for consultation the suggestion that there might be merit in introducing a system that would do away with upper eligibility levels for those paying the higher level of contribution. If one was undertaking an expensive legal case—some can get very expensive—one would first pay out what one could reasonably afford, then the legal aid system would come in, provided that one's case could be shown still to have merit, to pay for the rest of the case. That point is worth considering in the context of eligibility uprating.
