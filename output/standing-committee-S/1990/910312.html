New Clause 15
NATURAL HERITAGE AREAS

"(1) Where it appears to SNH, after consultation with such persons as it thinks fit, that an area is of outstanding value to the natural heritage of Scotland, and that special protection measures are appropriate for it, it may recommend to the Secretary of State that the area be designated as a Natural Heritage Area.

(2) Where the Secretary of State receives a recommendation under subsection (1) above he may designate the area by a direction under this subsection as a Natural Heritage Area.

(3) Where the Secretary of State proposes to make a direction under subsection (2) above he shall publish notice of the proposal in the Edinburgh Gazette and in at least one newspaper circulating in an area which includes the proposed areas.

(4) Before making a direction under subsection (2) above, the Secretary of State shall consider any representations received by him about the proposal within six weeks of the date which is the later of the dates on which the publication under subsection (3) above of notice relating to the proposal occurs.

(5) Where the Secretary of State makes a direction under subsection (2) above he shall publish notice of the designation in the Edinburgh Gazette and in at least one newspaper circulating in an area which includes the Area.

(6) Where it appears to SNH, after consulation with such persons as it thinks fit, that it is no longer appropriate that an area which has been designated as a Natural Heritage Area or any part of it should continue to be so designated it may recommend to the Secretary of State that the designation be cancelled or, as the case may be, varied.

(7) Where the Secretary of State receives a recommendation under subsection (6) above he may, by a direction under this subsection, cancel or, as the case may be, vary the designation of the Natural Heritage Area; and subsections (3) to (5) above shall apply to a direction proposed to be made under this subsection as they apply to a direction proposed to be made under this subsection as they apply to a direction proposed to be made under subsection (2) above, and for the purposes of such application the reference in subsection (3) to the proposed area shall be construed as a reference to the Area.

(8) Section 262C of the Town and Country Planning (Scotland) Act 1972 (National Scenic Areas) shall be amended as follows—

  1. in subsection (3) for the words 'National Scenic Area' there shall be substituted the words 'Natural Heritage Area' under section [ Natural Heritage Areas] of the Natural Heritage (Scotland) Act 1991; and
  2. in subsection (4) for the words 'National Scenic Area' there shall be substituted the words 'Natural Heritage Area'.

(9) Notwithstanding the repeal by section 26 of and Schedule 11 to this Act of subsections (1) and (2) of the said section 262C or the amendment by this section of subsections (3) and (4) of that section, any area which, at the date of such repeal, was designated as a National Scenic Area shall continue to be so designated and, until such designation is cancelled by a direction under the said section 262C, the provisions of that section shall continue to have effect in relation to the area as if they had not been repealed or, as the case may be, amended.".—[ Lord James Douglas-Hamilton.]

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: In introducing new clause 15, I should point out that hon. Members will now have had the opportunity to read our consultation paper on natural heritage areas, which was sent to all members of the Committe last week. JPEG 225 In view of the information now available to the Committee, I do not intend to speak at great length in introducing new clause 15, but I am sure that hon. Members will find it helpful if I mention some of the most relevant points.

First, I want to make it clear that our proposals for natural heritage areas are not a substitute for national parks. As we stated in the consultation paper, they have a different origin, would have a different function and would be applicable throughout Scotland. There have been many representations, including those from the Nature Conservancy Council and the Countryside Commission for Scotland, pointing out that sites of special scientific interest are not necessarily an appropriate mechanism for habitat conservation over large areas of Scotland. However, SSSIs are at present the only statutory means of protecting such habitats. That emerged clearly from the assessment carried out by the NCC of the flow country of Caithness and Sutherland. Although it is generally recognised that there is a large area of national and sometimes international nature conservation interest, it is also recognised that the level of protection afforded by SSSIs is not necessary for the whole area, given the variability of the scientific interest over it. We therefore believe that a less rigid means of protection of habitats would be appropriate, within which areas of higher scientific significance could be afforded the stronger protection under section 28 of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981.

By contrast—I am sure that this will be evident from the speech by the hon. Member for Dumbarton (Mr. McFall)—national parks have been devised as a means of encouraging recreational provision and managing recreational access in mountain areas. I appreciate that the CCS wishes to widen the concept of national parks to cover landscape conservation and, potentially, nature conservation. We are aware that the concept of environmentally sensitive areas, which has received widespread support, should be applied in a natural heritage context to appropriate parts of lowland, as well as upland, Scotland. There are, for example, areas of lowland Scotland where damage to the landscape has been considerble, and I am sure that hon. Members will recognise that that statement could apply also to parts of central Scotland. Priority must be given to the rejuvenation of those areas and the designation of a natural heritage area might be an appropriate way forward—for example, to reinforce work being undertaken by Central Scotland Woodlands Ltd.

More specifically on new clause 15, hon. Members will see that our proposals are based on a consultative approach among all relevant interests. It is essential that those who use, own and manage land should help to develop the proposals and, after designation by the Secretary of State, put them into practice. We recognise that some will argue that that approach, which is based on the voluntary principle, is inadequate and that more stringent powers are required. We do not agree with that, largely because an integrated approach to the conservation and management of areas of natural heritage significance has not been carried out in the past. That is a reason 226 why we propose the merger of the NCCS and the CCS. Furthermore, there will be available to Scottish Natural Heritage a wide range of statutory powers, which we believe will help to bring about a more co-operative approach. Some of these have been underused in the past and others, such as management agreements under section 49A of the Countryside (Scotland) Act 1967, have never been used. We wish to avoid over-elaborate procedures and new bureaucracy. That is why we intend SNH to have a key role in developing proposals and working in partnership with local communities, landowners, occupiers and other relevant interests concerned with conservation and the enjoyment of these areas.

They are primarily winter grass eaters, competing with domestic grazing animals such as sheep and cattle. I discussed the matter on many occasions with Lord Sanderson, the then Minister of State, Scottish Office, who advised me at the time that that would not be possible and suggested that I wait until Scottish Natural Heritage was established.

Well, here we are, and the number of geese continues to grow. In 1966, about 12,000 geese were counted on the island. By November 1988, their number had risen to 25,000, and now there are about 32,000. The Royal Society for the Protection of Birds bought Gruinard dairy farm to be used as a sanctuary. That, of course, meant the end of the dairy farm, but that and other designated areas can cope with only 10,000 geese, so 22,000 geese must graze outside those areas.

Farmers outside SSSIs are permitted, and are paid man-hour costs, to scare those geese, but with little success. To be effective, that would be a full-time occupation. Geese are not stupid; they are even known to graze overnight when concentrated scaring takes place during daylight hours.

Islay's importance as a haven for wintering geese is due to the high standard of efficiency that is achieved by its farmers, with their good land management and quality grass. All that fanners are seeking is an equitable balance between their industry and conservation. It is unacceptable that one farmer should receive compensation while his neighbour does not, and such a problem should be remedied.

Farming on Islay will decline to the point at which it will hardly be worth continuing in its present form. However, designating more SSSIs is not the solution. People want to farm in the way that they have always farmed. Continually paying compensation could, as one fanner said, turn the whole island into a goose farm. It is unreasonable to expect local farmers to be reconciled to unlimited encroachment by geese by unlimited possibilities of compensation.
