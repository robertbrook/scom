Housing in Scotland

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of housing in Scotland.

I am glad that we are having this matter day debate on housing. It is always useful to hear the views of hon. Members. Many of the ideas put forward in such debates are acted upon either immediately or later in the year.

I should like to start by making a number of announcements about the pressing subject of homelessness. The plight of young homeless Scots people in London was raised recently in Scottish questions by the hon. Member for Glasgow, Central (Mr. Watson). I was urged to visit Centrepoint, an emergency centre for roofless youngsters in Soho, and have since done so. I was shown round by Mr. Ian Brady, who is a Scot, and who is doing a very impressive job. I was particularly struck, while talking to some of the youngsters at Centrepoint, by how unprepared they were when they left Scotland. They seemed to believe that they could leave home, jump on a bus to London and find accommodation and work immediately they arrived. Evidently, they do so with no preparations and little money.

Centrepoint highlighted the urgent need to educate young people about the pitfalls of drifting to London. That is why I intend to give priority to initiatives that discourage young homeless Scots from moving to London without adequate preparation, job opportunities or money.

Mr. John McFall
(Dumbarton)

Dumbarton district council submitted to the Scottish Office the stopover project for homeless people in the Dumbarton district. Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: I am aware of that matter, which involved an urban aid application. I undertake to look into it. I shall deal later with urban aid. Substantial funds are being made available in Scotland. I shall write to the hon. Gentleman fully about the contents of the application, explaining why it was refused and what opportunities his council may be able to pursue, and I shall deal with the issue thoroughly.

The Committee will know that I announced last December that the Scottish Office commissioned the centre for housing research at the university of Glasgow to undertake a study into the role of social work in the prevention and management of homelessness. It is hoped that the study, which will cost £80,000, will provide the 2 basis for guidance on the best social work practice for both voluntary and statutory social work agencies. In particular, the study will examine policies and procedures to prevent homelessness in the first place and will consider what arrangements are needed to support those who become homeless. The Government understand the need for further efforts specifically to dissuade young Scots from moving to London. We consider it essential to encourage ways in which, through voluntary agencies and educational bodies, young people can be warned of the problems and dangers in advance. I am therefore pleased that the Government are today announcing offers of grant totalling £150,000 over the next three years to voluntary bodies for projects.

Mr. Tom Clarke

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: I want to explain what is being offered. I shall give way later.

We invited applications for grant and our decisions represent a package designed to help voluntary agencies dealing with youth homelessness. The package has three elements. First, there are grants to bodies to provide advice and educational material to dissuade young Scots from drifting to London. There are grants to Shelter to provide advice to roofless young people and to act as a referral point for London agencies working with young Scots wishing to return home. Working in co-operation with Shelter will be the Scottish Community Education Council, which is receiving a grant to develop and produce educational material to set out for young Scots the reality of going to London without adequate preparation for accommodation and employment.

Secondly, there are grants to bodies working in the major centres of population in Scotland. The Glasgow Council for the Single Homeless will receive a grant to develop a strategy to tackle the needs of city centre homeless in Glasgow. The Carolina House Trust will receive a grant to provide advice and guidance for young people leaving care and to assist young people returning from other parts of the United Kingdom to re-settle in Dundee. In Edinburgh, the Bethany Christian Trust and the Rock Trust will receive grants, the first to fund a furniture scheme for young people setting up in their own accommodation and the second to provide accommodation in Edinburgh for homeless young people.

The third element is to provide grant for Borderline, a valuable project set up by the Church of Scotland in London to assist young homeless Scots in London wishing to return to Scotland. Borderline provides young Scots in London with travel warrants where it is certain that they have a place to go home to. It spent about £1,500 in the past year and expects to spend at least £3,000 this year.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: I wish to answer a point raised earlier.

I am particularly anxious that more should be done to help relieve homelessness by making better use of empty houses. The majority of the estimated 127,000 empty houses in Scotland are in the private sector. Scottish Homes' rural housing strategy is making an important contribution to dealing with this problem. This year, Scottish Homes expects to spend £54 million in rural areas. That will fund an expanding programme of initiatives, especially in the 10 rural demonstration areas. Scottish Homes grants for rent and ownership are available to help builders and individuals to provide low-cost housing. Scottish Homes is also expecting to introduce other initiatives shortly, including ways of bringing empty housing in rural areas back into use, especially for the rural homeless. For example, Scottish Homes is currently working up proposals with the Forestry Commission on ways in which surplus commission housing can be brought back into beneficial use.

There are some 27,000 empty houses in the public sector, of which more than 3,000 have been empty for more than four months. I am determined that all landlords, whether local authorities, Government or Government agencies should examine their strategies for dealing with empty houses, including the scope for sale to housing associations. That is why we are setting up a research study beginning this autumn to examine empty public sector houses in Scotland. The aim of the research is to look at the extent, location and characteristics of empty property in Scotland owned by the public sector, including local and central Government. The results of the study will provide a sound basis for planning the more effective use of empty houses.

As Sir James Mellon stated recently when the spoke to housing associations, Scottish Homes is giving increasing priority to homelessness. Last year, one third of the nominations made by local authorities to Scottish Homes for filling vacancies in its own stock were in respect of people who were homeless. More than 1,000 housing units have been grant-aided by Scottish Homes in the past two years specifically to cater for the homeless. For our part, the Government have this year increased by £2·2 million the already substantial resources available to Scottish Homes specifically to address the problems of those in acute housing need in both urban and rural areas.

I wish to refer briefly to the problems which can arise from houses and flats offered for rent and in multiple occupation. In May this year, we gave local authorities in Scotland the power to introduce licensing schemes for houses in multiple occupation, under which they can more effectively enforce the provision of proper standards. We issued—for consultation purposes—a draft code of guidance to the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities on the way in which the range of local authorities' powers 6 in relation to HMOs should be used. We are considering the convention's response, and I hope that we can issue the final code of guidance within the next few weeks.

Since 1979, 230,741 public sector houses have been sold, of which 223,400 were sold to sitting tenants. As a consequence, the proportion of owner-occupied dwellings in Scotland increased from 35 per cent. in 1979 to nearly 52 per cent. today. Home ownership is now the majority tenure, with more than 1 million owner-occupied homes. That is a significant achievement and we are determined to build on the progress that we have made.

As many as a quarter of a million public sector tenants could have bought their homes by the time of the next election.

I must, however, draw attention to the fact that not all authorities have been as active as they might be in encouraging council house sales. I regard it as unacceptable that some authorities take more than a year on average to process applications into sales. That hinders tenants who wish to exercise their right to buy and harms existing council tenants because capital receipts which could be used to boost investment in council houses are less than would otherwise be the case. Processing times of more than a year are quite unnecessary.
