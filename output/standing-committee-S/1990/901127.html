Draft Merchant Shipping (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Order 1990

10.30 am

The Minister for Aviation, Shipping and Public Transport (Mr. Patrick McLoughlin)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Merchant Shipping (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Order 1990.

Before I explain the order, I welcome the hon. Member for Stoke-on-Trent, North (Ms. Walley) to the Committee as this is the first time that, together, we have dealt with merchant shipping regulations. I congratulate the hon. Lady on her appointment and I look forward to good debates here and elsewhere.

The order amends the Merchant Shipping (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Order 1987, which enables regulations to be made to give effect to annex II of the international convention for the prevention of pollution from ships, 1973. The order will empower the Secretary of State to amend the regulations to incorporate updated requirements that have been adopted by the International, Maritime Organisation. It will also empower the Secretary of State to make regulations to give effect to annex III of the convention.

The international convention for the prevention of pollution from ships—better known as the MARPOL convention — is the principal international instrument for controlling pollution from ships. It contains five annexes dealing with oil, noxious liquid substances in bulk, packaged dangerous goods, sewage and garbage. It is revised by the IMO as circumstances dictate which enables its provisions to keep up with newer practices and reflect current public concern. The convention commands wide international support and it is only through its regulations that we can improve the cleanliness of our seas.

Annex II of the MARPOL convention sets out the measures for preventing the pollution of the sea from noxious liquid substances in bulk. It categorises those substances into four groups according to their potential for pollution, prescribing for each category how it should be controlled and monitored. The list of substances is continually assessed by internationally recognised experts and, in the light of discussions in the IMO, the severity ratings of some substances are altered and new substances are added to the list.

Annex III to the convention deals with the prevention of pollution by marine pollutants carried in packaged form. It has been decided by the IMO that, for convenience and good order, annex III should be implemented by incorporation into the international maritime dangerous goods code. The code was developed for substances that are dangerous, rather than pollutants. However, the general precautions that need to be observed—whether a substance is dangerous or a pollutant—are the same and in transporting those substances on board ship, the trade does 4 not differentiate between the two. A completely new edition of the code has therefore been developed by the IMO that incorporates pollutants and, for the first time, applies to them requirements on packaging, marking and labelling, documentation, stowage and segregation.

Annex III, though ratified by the United Kingdom as long ago as 27 May 1986, is not in force internationally because the number of ratifications by member Governments of the IMO is just short of that necessary to bring it into effect. The delay is a cause for concern and the IMO has urged member states to implement the annex in advance of it coming into force internationally. Ministers attending the North Sea conference in March of this year agreed to give effect to the new edition of the international maritime dangerous goods code by 1 January 1991.

Regulations made under this order are being prepared to give effect to annex III. They will incorporate broadly the same duties and responsibilities that are to be found in the Merchant Shipping (Dangerous Goods) Regulations and will include a variety of ancillary provisions.

I am sure that the Committee will appreciate the need for the order, which is intended to help make cleaner the seas and oceans around the world.

10.34 am

Ms. Joan Walley
(Stoke-on-Trent, North)
: First, I should like to thank the Minister for his generous remarks. This is the first statutory instruments Committee I have attended in my new capacity and I look forward to learning as much as possible, so that a future Labour Government can ensure that similar regulations are properly enforced.

In the past 25 years, the pollution of the world's oceans has caused increasing international anxiety. Most pollution comes from land-based sources, and includes the byproducts of industry, run-off from agricultural pesticides and herbicides, and effluence discharged from urban areas. Shipping and maritime activities, however, also cause a great deal of pollution, although the substances discharged vary in quantity and potential harm to the environment. The measures introduced by the IMO have provided a framework for reducing marine pollution from ships, but the problem remains. It is important to ensure the effective implementation of existing measures, as well as the adoption of new regulations, because only when that is achieved can the Government introduce new regulations effectively.

The regulations are a typical example of the Government's hands-off, minimalist approach. Little is stated in the order about reception facilities or enforcement authorities. The Government have left implementation to local and other authorities, and have provided them with little support.

Annex III(2)(a) of MARPOL deals with the documentation and certificates for surveys and inspections. Documents relevant to the substances carried on board should be kept on the ship and on shore by the shipping company, the port authorities and the local authorities responsible for the transportation of hazardous material. Annex III(2)(d) tackles fines,

"not exceeding the statutory maximum"

and convictions. However, the level of the fines should be stated and increased above the minimal levels that form the present statutory maximum.

JPEG 5 Annex III(2)(f) mentions:

"detaining any ship in respect of which a contravention is suspected to have occurred".

The problem with MARPOL protocol is that the regulations will only be effected by those countries that have ratified the proposals and not necessarily by those who have signed MARPOL as member states. Countries that have not ratified the annexes should be banned from our waters as that would maintain the higher standards and encourage those countries to ratify the proposals.

Annex III is not in force internationally. By October 1990, only 41 countries with 48 per cent. of world tonnage had ratified the proposals. Annex III(3)(b) provides for "exemptions for different circumstances" without specifying what would qualify for an exemption. It is a catch-all phrase that needs to be tightened.

Annex III(3)(c) provides for:

"the delegation of functions exercisable by virtue of the regulations".

Which enforcement body should carry out the regulatory functions—local authority organisations or trade associations? Who will be responsible for monitoring the implementation of the regulations? The Government should stipulate that. I would welcome the Minister's comments.

Within the climate of deregulation and privatisation, the Government have no intention of providing a coherent package of regulations but the lack of a unified network system seriously undermines shipping and transport infrastructure. Integrated pollution control has not been implemented, despite the Environmental Protection Act 1990. You know all about that Act, Mr. Leadbitter, as you, chaired so many of the Committee sittings.

If new regulations are introduced when existing regulations are not properly enforced, what safeguards exist to ensure that present requirements are implemented? One of our main anxieties arises from the lack of expertise and the shortage of trained staff. We hear a great deal at the moment about the need for greater investment in the infrastructure for training and skills to match that of other countries. That infrastructure provides all levels of operatives and officers, and I hope that the Minister will comment on the training and staffing levels needed to implement the regulations. I also hope that the Minister will describe the existing staffing levels within the Department of Transport's marine inspectorate. My experience of the matter is limited, but I understand that the skill shortage within that inspectorate is analagous with that in her Majesty's inspectorate of pollution.

Ships become more sophisticated each year, therefore, their operation requires even higher educational standards and experience. It is signifgicant that most incidents of maritime pollution, whether from direct casualties, collisions, oil spills, incorrectly stored cargo, the washing-out of tanks or general disposal operations, involve flag of convenience ships. Such ships do not have to comply with the saftey and seaworthiness standards demanded by the Department of Transport. Even though they may be inspected by the Department on arrival at United Kingdom ports, the number of inspectors employed by the Department has been cut. I think that the Committee should be given some information about that.

The need for well-trained inspectors could not be more apparent. The Minister may be aware that a survey carried out in Southampton in 1989 showed that nearly 80 per cent. of the flag of convenience ships inspected were found to 6 have one or more defects. Given that only one in 20 or 30 flag of convenience ships were inspected, that does not inspire much confidence in the operation.

Flag of convenience ships are invariably crewed with low-cost seafarers from developing countries, whose training and qualifications fall short of safe standards and who are chosen to circumvent income tax, social security payments, trade unions, western wage levels and health and safety regulations.

The deregulatory climate in which shipowners operate has led them to seek registration in offshore tax havens. Any anti-pollution measures regarded as direct costs will be resisted, as flag of convenience ships have little incentive to comply with them. United Kingdom shipowners pledged to decent standards, including pollution control, cannot survive without direct or indirect Government assistance through the shipping industry. Although I am new to my post, Mr. Leadbitter, I have made a point of visiting the Baltic exchange and, from the discussions there, I have learnt that there is much unease about the decline in the number of ships that sail from this country.

Regulations to tighten up on marine pollution are irrelevant unless they are enforced and carry meaningful penalties. In 1989, a study by the Dutch group Werkgrope Noordzee contained 300 reports from states bordering the North Sea of violations of MARPOL 73/78 discharge rules. It showed that only 51—that is, 17 per cent.—of those reports were followed up by the flag states of the ships involved. There was no prosecution in 64 cases because the proof given by the reporting states appeared insufficient. Only 18 of the 300 cases were subject to penal prosecution, and even then the fines were very low compared to what the vessel would have to pay for the use of port reception facilities.

The introduction of crude oil washing and segregated ballast tanks has reduced some of the need for oily-waste reception facilities. Improvements in the efficiency of the discharge of chemicals have had a similar effect. According to the IMO, reception facilities are still inadequate despite its efforts to promote and encourage their construction and use.

We must get rid of waste—a ship does so by discharging while in port, which leaves the port with the problem. Treatment of wastes involves expenditure, but under the present system, the chances of avoiding detection and punishment are good. The natural inclination of free marketeers is to avoid facilities entirely and dispose of wastes elsewhere, which underlines the need for enforcement.

The IMO study of mechanisms for financing reception facilities is welcome. However, it is the Government's duty to overcome such problems during the 1990s and to formulate an integrated transport system that takes environmental protection fully into account.

One cannot consider the new regulations in isolation and, in that context, the ageing of ships is a serious problem. During the 1980s, the number of ships of 10 years old or less fell from 62 per cent. to 36 per cent. Older ships are far more vulnerable than new ones to accidents, breakdowns and the like and that places a greater responsibility on the enforcement agencies of flag states and port states to implement international standards. The high cost of applying standards retrospectively to older ships should not be used to circumvent the needs of safety and pollution prevention.

JPEG 7 The new regulations are a step forward in tackling problems of marine pollution but, without enforcement, they are meaningless. The problems of ageing fleets and the increasing use of flag of convenience ships must be urgently addressed.

In 1989, the Perentis, a former second world war landing craft built in 1944, sank off the Channel Islands while sailing under the Indonesian flag. It was carrying a cargo of toxic lindane, which is still to be found. I note that the Minister is furiously looking through his notes. I hope that he can explain how we are to secure a cleaner marine environment when we cannot even find such a potentially lethal and dangerous cargo. It is critical to ensure that such an incident never happens again.

Deregulation is a free marketeer's dream, but if it applied to road haulage, Department of Transport standards would not be effective—there would be no MOTs; no restriction on the hours of drivers; no compulsory insurance; and a 60 per cent. cut in the number of police on duty. That would lead to a huge public outcry.

Shipping, in comparison, is all too often out of sight and out of mind—until a disaster happens. It is the Government's duty to ensure that the proper legislative and enforcement framework is in place so that disasters never happen. I stress that enforcement is crucial; without it we cannot have any confidence that the regulations will make any difference.

However, Christmas comes but once a year and so, it seems, does the annual oil slick in the Channel. My hon. Friend the Minister and his officials know that I raise this matter every year.

An oil slick occurs with monotonous regularity in the Channel just after Christmas and does exceptional damage to our sea birds off the south coast. The slick wanders somewhere between Land's End and North Foreland. My hon. Friend has assured me that the crew of the marine pollution control unit's spotter aircraft do not rest for 8 Christmas turkey and are on duty over the Christmas period. Therefore, the allegations that have appeared in the press that unscrupulous shipowners take advantage of the control unit's relaxed guard to pump out dirty bilges or to clean their tanks in the Channel are unfair. However, I require reassurance from my hon. Friend on that issue.

My hon. Friend knows that the company, Vikoma, in my constituency produces much of the pollution control equipment used in the oil industry throughout the world. In addition, an inventor on the island has named his invention half after himself—the Frogmat boom. It uses straw stubble from the fields to make a long sausage which absorbs about seven or eight times its weight in spilt oil. I am concerned that the Government continue to put the emphasis on spraying to clean up oil slicks.

The Frogmat boom has a real role to play in fighting pollution in our estuaries. The Dover Straits are a natural chicane for about 40 per cent. of the world's shipping traffic as it approaches Europe and Europort. For the life of me, I wonder why it is not possible for us and the French to get together and find out the manifest of every ship passing through the Straits. They are a natural constriction point and it should be possible to establish a reporting procedure to give us greater control over shipping.

Oil slicks are often spotted long after the offending ship has moved on. Such ships proceed at an average speed of 16 knots, so they do not suddenly switch to hyperspace and disappear over the horizon. I am told that even when a sample of oil is taken from a ship's hold, it is impossible to match it with a slick and secure a prosecution. The number of successful prosecutions for oil spills in the channel and surrounding waters is very low.

I doubt whether my hon. Friend can tell me today, but perhaps he will write to say whether the order will apply to balloon racing. I have been responsible for setting off more than a few balloon races in my time, but the World Wide Fund for Nature is worried about the number of balloons that have been ingested by dolphins to their lasting harm.
