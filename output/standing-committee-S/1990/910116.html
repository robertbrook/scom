COMMUNITY CHARGE BENEFITS (GENERAL) AMENDMENT (NO. 3) REGULATIONS 1990

10.30 am

Ms. Clare Short
(Birmingham, Ladywood)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Community charge Benefits (General) Amendment (No. 3) Regulations 1990.

The Chairman
: With the leave of the Committee, we shall also consider the following: The Income Support (General) Amendment No. 3 Regulations 1990, the Housing Benefit (General) Amendment (No. 2) Regulations 1990 and the Family Credit (General) Amendment No. 2 Regulations 1990.

Ms. Short
: Some important points are raised by the statutory instruments. The problem of pensioners and other people with savings who are living on benefits has come up time and again. Because they have savings, many are not entitled to benefits. On several ocassions the Government had to change their policy because of its cruelty. The problem also faces people who get compensation. In my area a number of women who have got compensation for rape from the Criminal Injuries Compensation Board have had their benefits stopped because of the level of compensation. I know of two recent cases of people who got compensation from the Ministry of Defence for injuries or illnesses which arose from serving in the forces but which were recognised only later. The compensation put right an old wrong, but the consequence was that their benefit entitlement was reduced. This is a serious and sensitive issue.

The four statutory instruments deal with among other things, the treatment of notional capital in assessing entitlement to means-tested benefits. They cover income support, family credit, housing benefit and poll tax benefit. Under the old supplementary benefit regulations a person who had deprived himself or herself of capital by spending it would be treated as still possessing it, but there was room for discretion, which was used to reduce the notional capital over time. I am sure that Conservative Ministers have had the same sort of cases as I have. Was buying a carpet legitimate expenditure, or was someone deliberately reducing his or her capital to change his or her benefit entitlements? The notional capital was reduced over time so that the money would eventually be treated as having been spent on normal living expenses and the person would be entitled to benefit.

Since the Government's horrendous social security reforms in 1988, that discretion no longer exists and there is doubt as to the legality of treating notional capital as diminishing. In a recent case, the tribunal of commissioners 4 took the view that the diminishing capital rule still applied, and pointed out that if a claimant had actual capital, he or she could reduce it by

"reasonable expenditure on living and other sensible expenses (which we do not consider need be limited to the relevant amount of income support…)".

That is an important question for the Government. Must people with large lumps of capital spend at the level of benefits, or is it reasonable for them to spend a bit more because, as we all know, living on benefits is very difficult? The commissioners suggest that it would be reasonable for people to spend more than the minimal level that benefits allow.

The Department of Social Security solicitor conceded the point. The commissioners said that the same should apply to notional capital and that the claimant should be entitled

"to be deemed to diminish that capital by reasonable expenditure related to his own particular financial and other circumstances".

That it is not necessarily the same as the amount of income support that he receives.

The statutory instruments remove any doubt as to whether the diminishing capital rule still applies. We welcome that. The rule is written into the regulations for the four benefits. But they limit the rate of diminution to the amount of benefit forgone. If Conservative Members vote for them today, the regulations will assume that if the claimant still possesses capital, she or he would spend at the same rate as if they had no capital. That is what adjudication officers are instructed to assume at present. Plainly, that is unrealistic and raises an important question. Consider the case of an elderly person, living on income support, who had been raped and received compensation from the Criminal Injuries Compansation Board. If she spends a penny above what would have been her income support level, she is assumed to have spent that capital. Such people are not allowed to treat themselves in any way without forgoing future entitlement to benefit.

The proposal is mean, insensitive and unfair. I understand the need for the regulations because we must have the discretion to deal with notional capital, but why be so mean? Could not we be a little more generous and allow people, within reason, to spend amounts of their capital on normal, reasonable expenditure? People who have saved that capital or who may have received a lump sum because of hardship in their lives should be allowed to spend a little more than income support levels without being penalised as the Government suggests.

There is another anomaly in present practice, which the statutory instruments would write into the regulations. For the purposes of housing and poll tax benefit, the capital is assunmed to diminish only by the amount of the benefits forgone, ignoring any income support or family credit forgone. That is convenient, because discovering how much income support or family credit claimants may have been entitled to if they had not had their capital would be a nightmare, but it is also unfair. The obvious solution is to allow a more generous rate of diminishing. That would allow for the possible loss of other benefits.

That is my main point but I also want to ask the Minister about more detailed matters. Some of them have been brought to our attention by the Child Poverty Action Group. With regard to regulation 51A, that body is worried about the provision in paragraph 5, which appears to require intervals of at least 26 weeks between JPEG 5 redeterminations of the amount of the reduction of the notional capital. I have great sympathy with the point made by the Child Poverty Group that there should be no limit on when such recalculations can be made.

For example, what will happen if there is a relevant change of circumstances two weeks into the 26-week period, such as the birth of a child, a child moving into a different age band, an uprating and so on? Furthermore, if there are to be any restrictions on when a redetermination can take place, claimants could need advice about the timing of further claims so that they do not make them just before an uprating or when a relevant change in circumstances is likely to occur. The effect of that restriction on recalculation of the reduction of the notional capital could be that a claimant would have to use up her or his notional capital at a rate below the amount of the person's income support entitlement for up to 25 weeks.

I hope that the Minister can reassure me on that point. If someone makes a claim, is refused and has a change of circumstances one week later, he or she will be penalised for six months because of the rigidity of the regulations. That seems deeply unfair.

With regard to the diminishing notional capital rule, the Child Poverty Action Group felt that reduction by the rate of income support entitlement alone was not sufficient. A figure should be built in to recognise that those claimants will have no access to free school meals and other passported benefits such as free dental treatment, prescriptions and so on. Furthermore, they will have, no access to community care grants and budgeting loans while they are not getting income support. In addition, and most importantly, an element of discretion should be introduced to allow reduction of notional capital at a reasonable rate above income support level in appropriate circumstances.

That is my general point, but a specific matter is most important. While such people are expected to live on their capital, and so cannot claim income support, they will not get the passported benefits. Therefore, they will be even worse off than they would have been had they been on income support at that time. That is gravely unjust.

If people can lose a severe disability premium as a result of the changes to regulation 3(2)(d), will the Government arrange for a concessionary payment, re-awarding the amount of transitional protection lost that was first awarded under that regulation? That payment could be along the lines of the special concession made when people lost their severe disability premium following the October 1989 change to regulation 3(2)(c). That sounds technical, but it is most important to those with severe disabilities.

Is it right that under regulation 57(l)(b), payment of maintenance in respect of a child should be attributed to the needs of anyone other than that child? The effect of that rule could be to take a woman and her children off income support for a time, which could lead to the loss of passported benefit. It could also discourage fathers from making such payments. Whatever arguments we may have about the Government's proposals, we all agree that absent parents—usually fathers—should maintain their children. That is yet another discouragement and a potential injustice. It is wrong that income from maintenance can be attributed to someone other than the child for whom it is paid.

Why does the carers premium in new paragraph 14ZA of Schedule 2 have to be restricted to people in receipt of invalid care allowance? Why will not the fact of caring itself 6 suffice? Pensioners still will not have their caring role recognised if they are too old to apply for invalid care allowance. People live for so long now that it frequently happens that those caring for frail elderly people are often pensioners also. That happened when my mother cared for her mother and it is quite common nowadays. That group of people is not entitled to invalid care allowance. The carers permium will not be applicable while the people being cared for are serving the qualifying period for attendance allowance or have to resatisfy the residence test if they have been abroad for more than six months.

I hope that the Government will reconsider the meanness of the level of diminution of notional capital. That will lead to many appallingly cruel cases, which will embarrass the Government and, more important, will hurt people. I hope that the Minister can give a considered response.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Miss Ann Widdecombe)
: I congratulate the Opposition on the detailed way in which they have examined the amendments and on their interesting arguments this morning. I am especially grateful to the hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire (Mr. Kirkwood) for his kind offer to accept responses in writing, but I can respond now to most of the points that were raised.

I was somewhat surprised that Opposition Members saw fit to challenge four sets of regulations that are overwhelmingly beneficial to claimants. Even where the Government were challenged on the detail of the proposals, I was pleased at the general welcome to the change of rules on notional capital and other matters.

I shall answer the specific questions posed shortly, but shall start by outlining some of the most important changes that have been introduced to income-related benefit schemes by the regulations before us and explaining their effects on individual claimants. It has been rightly said that many of the regulations are already in force. Under the two main changes that were introduced last October, as many as 30,000 carers and 65,000 lone parents stand to gain up to £10 a week at a total cost of more than £25 million a year. I draw that figure to the attention of the hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire.

The Committee will recall that my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Social Security announced his proposals for the carer premium back in October 1989 as part of a 20-point plan to improve the position of long-term sick and disabled people. At the same time, he made clear his intentions to provide extra help for working lone parents.

Those two groups have been particularly considered by the regulations, which have given effect to those commitments by introducing the new carer premiums of £10 a week into income support, housing benefit and 10 community charge benefit and by increasing the earnings disregard in housing and community charge benefits for lone parents. The carer premium provides an improvement in the position of about 30,000 of our poorest carers. Many had no premium at all before October and the regulations recognise the wholly legitimate worries that were expressed about that extremely deserving group of claimants.

Carers qualify for the premium in income support, housing benefit or community charge benefits if they receive an invalid care allowance. They also qualify if they have claimed invalid care allowance since the premium was introduced, but cannot receive it because they claim another benefit. That ensures that no one will be disadvantaged because of the overlapping benefit rules. For example, widows who are carers will have access to the premium.

I find it hard to believe that the Opposition would wish to remove those improvements from the statute book. Although I accept that they are challenging some of the detail, it is crucial to stress that the regulations are wholly beneficial and I hope that they will be welcomed by the Committee.

I remind hon. Members that we shall extend the scope of the premium from October this year and that the premium will continue for up to eight weeks after the caring role stops. I should add that during this time, former carers can receive income support, including the premium, without the need to make themselves available for work, which will give them time to adjust to their new circumstances.

The particular needs of another group of claimants was also very much in our minds when we decided to increase the lone parent earnings disregard in both housing and community charge benefits. In so doing, we have recognised that many lone parents who want to work can face extra problems because of the need to combine the roles of both child-career and breadwinner. Lone parents on housing and community charge benefits already receive additional help—an enhanced lone parent premium. Prior to the implementation of the regulations, the first £15 of their earnings was ignored. That amount was higher than for many single people and couples. However, under the regulations before us today, we have increased that amount by over 60 per cent., to £25. We have targeted that improvement on lone parents in full time work or not otherwise on income support. We estimate that about 65,000 working lone parents will benefit from this measure, which will provide a real incentive to those who wish to return to work.

Much has been made this morning of capital. Before dealing with the details about that, we should consider the improvements that have been made overall. The hon. Member for Birmingham, Ladywood (Ms. Short) mentioned the problem of those who receive payment in consequence of personal injury. We have ensured that where the payment is used to set up a trust fund, the value of the fund and of the right to receive payment in accordance with the trust will be disregarded for an indefinite period, when previously, the value was only disregarded for two years. We have tried to help in that respect.

The change also recognises that an injured person may have or may develop long-term needs, and provides greater flexibility for these to be met. Existing rules already allow for certain payments to be made from trust funds for various purposes. We have also taken steps to ensure that JPEG 11 self-employed people who are temporarily unable to work because of sickness or disability do not have the value of their business assets taken into account for income-related benefit purposes. That means that those assets will not exclude them from benefit eligibility, nor reduce the amount of benefit to which they are entitled. I am sure that the Committee will welcome this change, which will remove some of the anxiety felt by the self-employed who may need to claim for a few weeks because of ill health.

We have also clarified the position with regard to payments made in respect of prescriptions and other NHS charges, payments for travelling expenses to attend hospital or visit prison, and cash payments in lieu of milk tokens. Hitherto, there was some confusion about the way in which regular payments relating to these expenses should be treated in the income-related benefit. The regulations put it beyond all doubt that all these payments should be fully disregarded—a very beneficial measure indeed.

In disregarding arrears of special payments to pre-1973 war widows, we have fulfilled the Government's pledge that these would not affect entitlement to income-related benefit. Regular £40 a week special payments are already fully disregarded, along with up to £10 per week of war widows or war disablement pension. We wanted to clarify in law that arrears of these payments made in lump sums should not affect benefit entitlement either. The regulations make specific provision for that.

I shall now deal with an issue that has been widely debated this morning—the diminution of notional capital. Hon. Members will be aware that we are dealing with deprivation of capital—the crucial issue—for the purpose of securing entitlement to benefit. That seems to have been overlooked in some of the arguments that have been made earlier. We have decided that the crucial fact to be established is not that a person has deprived himself of capital but that he has deprived himself of his capital for the specific purpose of enhancing his right to benefit.

Ms. Short

They might often treat a grandchild or do up the house with their money and subsequently arrive to claim benefit. In those circumstances, how they disposed of their capital was examined. The authorities wanted to know about everything they had spent, and receipts had to be produced. These people were not making any calculations about acquiring the right to benefit. The hon. Lady is trying to reassure us in a rather misleading way.

Miss Widdecombe
: I am not trying to mislead the Committee. I am trying to reassure the hon. Lady about the starting point, which is defined not in terms of a person who had deprived himself of capital, but of one who has deliberately and wilfully deprived himself of it in order to gain a better position for benefit. If the officer is not satisfied that that is the case, then these rules will not apply. If a redundant person has spent money for reasonable purposes—keeping up rents, repaying mortgages and the like—the mere fact that some of his capital has been disposed of will not count against him.

12 However, the question posed by the hon. Lady about what would happen to people who had spent their capital buying carpets and doing up their houses is important. A difficult judgment has to be made. Our intention is to safeguard taxpayers' money and to ensure that it is used properly to help those in genuine need. Without a specific reason for such expenditure, it is difficult to justify paying out benefits, which are collected from the taxes of both low and high earners, to people who have deliberately used money to enhance substantially the quality of their lives. However, the crucial starting point is that such people must be judged to have done so with the deliberate intention of depriving themselves of capital.

I hope that the hon. Member for Ladywood is reassured by that. I suspect that her real complaint is not with the law's intent, but with its implementation, and that she has found one or two cases in which people have honestly believed that they were entitled to act as they did before becoming eligible for benefit. That is a different matter, but the law is clear: the motivation behind the deprivation of capital must be to secure additional benefit.

Mr. Kirkwood
: That is fine as far as it goes, but the hon. Miss Widdecombe
: The hon. Member makes a clear case for the necessity of rules about deprivation of capital. It is dubious practice for a person to take a world cruise when he is made redundant and has no expectations of future employment and then, on returning, to ask the taxpayers— many of whom may never have taken a trip to Brighton, let alone a world cruise—to pay for him.

We need the notion of capital rules. The changes that the Government have introduced have tried to cater for people such as the hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire described by making it clear that any attempt on their part to deprive themselves of capital should not be held against them in perpetuity. The expenditure rate is restricted to the rate of income support because that is how such people would be supported and it represents the degree to which they would benefit from their actions. If we are to clarify the rules and ensure that people are not discriminated against in perpetuity, we need clear guidelines for calculation procedures.

Ms. Short
: The Minister is trying to reassure her hon. Friends and, without being deliberately misleading, is making the picture overly rosy. Before 1988, the commissioners had discretion to examine how people had spent their money. Professional people who were always able to find employment easily, such as the many skilled men in the west midlands in the early 1980s, might very well JPEG 13 have decided, on acquiring a suitable sum of money, to go on a world cruise, secure in the knowledge that they would find employment on their return. They would have acted completely honestly, only to find that the labour market had changed in their absence and that they could not get a job.

In the past, there was discretion to examine each individual case and to make a fair judgment in all the circumstances. That is all being changed. A further beneficial change that these regulations introduce relates to the treatment of charitable and voluntary payments. The Committee will be aware that the first £10 of any regular payment of a charitable or voluntary nature may be ignored when calculating entitlement to housing benefit or community charge benefit. People are free to spend the payments in any way they wish, including those day-to-day living expenses such as clothing and fuel. These regulations go considerably further and provide that charitable and voluntary payments that are intended and used for items other than food, clothing, footwear or fuel, all those housing costs and charges already catered for in benefit, will also be fully disregarded in the calculation of housing benefit and community charge benefit. That change brings these two benefits into line with income support and family credit and has been widely welcomed by charities and claimants alike.

The Committee might appreciate an example of how the change can help. An elderly claimant whose family helps with telephone bills and television rental will gain from the change. Previously, only £10 of the weekly value of that help would have been disregarded; now, the full amount will be disregarded. Again, that is a beneficial change.

Another such change involves the non-dependant deductions in housing benefit and income support. Usually, deductions are made from benefits when the claimant has a non-dependant, such as a grown-up son or daughter, living in the household. That is because it is reasonable to assume that the non-dependant is contributing to the claimant's housing costs. The regulations take into account the position of parents of students who return to the family home during the long summer vacation, but who are unable to find work or who choose not to work. Deductions will no longer be made regarding students during the summer vacation unless the student is in remunerative work. That is a positive step that will help low income families with a student member.
