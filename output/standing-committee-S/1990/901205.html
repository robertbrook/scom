DRAFT EDUCATION SUPPORT GRANTS REGULATIONS 1990

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Education and Science (Mr. Michael Fallon)
: I beg to move,

The Committee has considered the draft Education Support Grants Regulations 1990.

May I begin by welcoming you to the Chair, Mr. Hill? I am sure that we are in safe hands.

The draft regulations are designed to allow my right hon. and learned Friend the Secretary of State to pay education support grants. They consolidate, with amendments, the various regulations on ESGs which have been made in the past six years under the Education (Grants and Awards) Act 1984.

It may help members of the Committee if I start by explaining some of the background. In particular, I want to outline the new arrangements for combining education support grants with local education authority training grants in a single grants programme.

Last year, my hon. Friend the Member for Stratford-on-Avon (Mr. Howarth) introduced the debate on the ESG regulations for the 1990–91 programme. He explained that a review had been established as part of the efficiency scrutiny programme to consider the Department's two main specific grants—education support grants and the LEA training grants scheme. The scrutiny was to examine the administration of the two grants. That included reviewing whether they were effective instruments for supporting the policy objectives, that they were intended to support, and making recommendations for improvement. That report was published in February. It concluded:

"ESGs and the LEATGS are powerful instruments for advancing the policy objectives for which they were framed. Specific grants for educational activities should continue."

The scrutiny also concluded that there was no longer a case for two separate grant systems. Instead, it recommended:

"To improve efficiency and effectiveness, and to secure maximum economy of operation, a unified grant system should replace the separate ESG and LEATGS grant systems."

The report considered that such a move would improve co-ordination between training and other forms of grant support, because the two schemes, over the years have covered similar ground. In particular, it is now a key objective for both to help schools, colleges and local education authorities implement the changes resulting from the Education Reform Act 1988.

Both schemes provide funds to help to put the national curriculum in place, but the training needed for the national curriculum is considered quite separately from the other forms of support needed such as books, equipment and non-teaching staff. That does not make sense. It would be better if training and support could be considered in the round, as complementary parts of a single package.

4 Therefore, the Government readily accepted that bringing the two grants together would be a sensible step forward. That principle has received a general welcome, as it helps to ensure that resources are used to best effect. The scrutiny concluded that, ideally, new legislation might be introduced to provide for a single grants scheme, but it recognised that the prospect for early legislation was uncertain. Short of that, it considered that the best option would be to pay the whole of the grants progamme under the powers in the 1984 Act. That would mean allowing the statute under which training grants are currently paid—section 50 of the Education Act 1986—to lie fallow. The advice that we received from the House's authorities was that that would not be acceptable.

Therefore, for the time being, we intend to keep the two grants formally separate and paid under their respective statutes, but we will administer them side by side. That will allow us to exploit the benefits of a co-ordinated approach. The combined programme will be known as the grants for education support and training, of GEST programme.

The ESG regulations are drawn largely on the previous ESG regulations, but they make several changes of substance.

First, the schedule to the regulations lists all the purposes for which education support grants may be paid within the 1991–92 programme. We have taken out of the schedule the activities specified in previous schedules which are coming to an end in 1990–91. We have updated the descriptions of activities which started in previous years and are continuing into 1991–92, and we have added the new activities which will begin in 1991–92. Item 28 in the schedule covers the new activity on improving the planning and co-ordination of provision for the under-fives.

Secondly, the regulations will allow the Department to pay grants each term instead of quarterly, as in the past. That will bring ESGs into line with training grants, which have been paid each term for some time. That should help to reduce the administrative load. It will allow both grants to be claimed and paid at the same time.

Thirdly, the regulations will give my right hon. and learned Friend the power to require LEAs to provide information to verify that grant has been properly paid, and to require them to comply with any requirements he may specify, including requirements on the repayment of grant. That will parallel provisions included in the new regulations governing the training grants scheme.

To avoid confusion, I should make it clear that the power in regulation 10 about complying with requirements is only for use in the last resort. Its purpose is to ensure that my right hon. and learned Friend can protect the taxpayers' investment. In particular, it is designed to allow him to require LEAs to repay an appropriate amount of grant when they sell assets bought with ESG funding. That is a standard requirement for grants.

In addition to including those points of substance, we have taken the opportunity to bring the wording of the regulations into line with the parallel training grants regulations.

As in previous years, we have circulated a list of all the activities to be supported in the 1991–92 programme. I hope that Committee members have found it helpful.

We announced our proposals for the size and pattern of the programme in July. We plan to support total spending of £364 million in England, of which £215 million is provisionally attributed to education support grants, and JPEG 5 the remainder to training grants. The exact sums to be paid under each set of regulations will be decided once all the grant allocations to LEAs have been settled.

In July, we also issued a draft circular to LEAs inviting them to submit bids for allocations under the combined GEST programme. Each activity was separately identified in the circular as payable under ESG or training grant regulations. By grouping activities together, the circular was designed to help LEAs draw up plans which treat training and other support in each activity as a single package.

The deadline for bids was 19 October. We are currently considering them with a view to deciding the final shape of the programme. We hope to announce allocations to LEAs before Christmas, subject to the draft regulations receiving approval.

The Government's priority for the 1991–92 programme is to help schools, colleges and LEAs to implement the education reforms. Of the £364 million total programme, £270 million, or nearly 75 per cent., is being targeted on different aspects of the reforms.

Within that £270 million, £170 million will be allocated for projects related to the national curriculum and collective worship. They include: £35 million for implementing the new assessment procedures, which will be used to help LEAs administer the new arrangements, promote consistent assessment standards and give teachers and other staff the necessary training; £30 million for information technology systems for teaching, within which we are giving priority to primary and special schools and to supporting children with communication difficulties; £15 million for extra books for the national curriculum; and £16 million for other ESG support for materials and equipment, and for the appointment of staff such as technicians.

In addition to supporting the national curriculum, the programme will support projects to improve the management of schools and colleges. That will particularly help schools, colleges and LEAs implement schemes of local management. More than £100 million will be available for that. It will cover expenditure on management training for staff, computerised management information systems and additional administrative support.

That leaves a further £90 million for activities not related to the education reforms. One of the most important of those is teacher recruitment. We are proposing to increase to £10 million the ESG support for projects to increase local recruitment of teachers, particularly former teachers and mature entrants to teaching. We shall carry on supporting projects on health education, focused particularly on alerting pupils and students to the risks associated with alcohol, smoking, drugs and AIDS. There will be continuing support for a range of ESG activities in school discipline, youth and community work and adult education.

So far, I have concentrated on the ESG programme in England. My right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Wales is responsible for the operation of education support grants in Wales. There, too, ESGs are being brought together with training grants in the same way as in England. The programme in Wales will support expenditure of £25 million in 1991–92. The activities to be supported closely match those in England but, in addition, funds will be available to support the study of Welsh in the national curriculum.

6 The value of ESGs is plain to see. The efficiency scrutiny report, which I quoted earlier, commented:

"The evidence we have received, from all sources, provides overwhelming support for the two schemes. Specific grants have funded important and beneficial developments which would not have happened otherwise."

However, we recognise that we can do more. I have explained our approach to improve further the operation of ESGs and training grants. The regulations will allow ESGs to continue to provide earmarked funds to help schools, colleges and LEAs to respond to the education reforms and to other priorities.

I hope that he will not share the fate of his three predecessors, which, I am sure, is not related either to our debate or to the quality of my interventions. I wish the Minister well in his new post.

The Minister's opening remarks were about the need to integrate the local authority training grant system with the education support system. We support the basic principle that he stated, and we understand the procedural reasons why it is not possible to link the two schemes formally. It makes sense to administer them side by side and not to see work on teacher training for the national curriculum for instance, separately, from developments in the national curriculum that have been supported by the education support grant. We welcome these developments and look forward to the Minister announcing that that integration has worked and that further legislative steps will be taken.

The Minister said that expenditure of £360 million was required. He sent a letter to you, Mr Hill, which I am sure you enjoyed reading in preparation for our sitting, and he kindly sent a copy of it to each Committee member. However, it might have been more helpful to hon. Members who are not as numerate as you, Mr Hill, if the amount of Government support had been shown in the tables and not just the supported expenditure. We have to alter each figure in the table by either 60 per cent. or by 70 per cent. With your gifts, Mr Hill, I know that that would be no problem, but for some of us it was a difficulty. It would be useful if a table showing the expenditure that the Government are committed to were produced in future.

The Government have said that the grants have been increased. However, the increase assumes a 2 per cent. rate of inflation. Major changes may have taken place, but not substantially or quickly enough to bring that figure into line with inflation rates that are faced by local government, of 10.5 per cent. to 11 per cent. or more. Expenditure has been cut in real terms. The Minister has announced a cut in the money available for supported teacher training for the national curriculum, and for a range of other activities related to the development of schools and further education colleges. Those activities have much support on both sides of the Committee, but unfortunately the Government have reduced the real level of expenditure on them. I hope that the Minister will address those points later.

Previously, local training was supported by the local authority training grants system. As the Minister will know, local authorities feel that the new system does not necessarily provide sufficient flexibility. All local JPEG 7 government recognises that central Government are responsible for laying down priorities and, through this mechanism, for funding those priorities.

Anxiety exists that some activities that are essential to the overall development of the education service—for example, the training of educational psychologists, the upkeep of educational technology centres and training for staff in further education—may not fall within the range of activities for training that will be supported by the new grant regime. That would be a disappointment as some flexibility would be lost. I hope that the Minister will have further talks with local government to ensure a system that reflects some local priorities. If the system is to work effectively, it must have a sense of central priorities and of local need and priorities.

The Opposition have strongly criticised the Government for not proceeding with a national scheme for teacher appraisal, which is important to improve teacher morale, teacher standards and general classroom performance. A sum of money is allocated in the grants for local authorities to bid to support schemes of teacher appraisal.

There are rumours that, under the new Secretary of State for Education and Science, the Government will announce a nationwide scheme of teacher appraisal. If so, I welcome that. I always welcome the Government adopting Labour party policy. Is the Minister simply adopting the haphazard approach of the education support grants or the much more comprehensive system of a national teacher appraisal scheme? I fear that one of the problems of leaving the matter to individual local authorities is that, where a teacher appraisal scheme would be most needed, the local authority would be least likely to bid for the money. That is why a national scheme is necessary.

The supported level of expenditure under activity 9(91) of the table provided by the Minister is £8 million for 1991–92, although there is committed supported expenditure from the previous year of £2·1 million. That sum of £8 million represents no more than £90,000 per authority—the cost of employing probably four teachers if salary and additional employment costs are taken into account. That is not an effective response to teacher shortages or to the fact that between 350,000 and 400,000 qualified teachers, to use and often quoted figure, are not in the profession, although I believe that many would return to it if the Government took a much more active approach to recruitment and retraining. Two measures are necessary for that: first, the provision of child care facilities for the many women teachers who left the profession to assume parental and domestic responsibilities and, secondly, and perhaps more important, the introduction of refresher courses. Many teachers wish to return to work but are worried about the pace of change that has already taken place. They do not criticise it, but feel inadequate to deal with it. The money that the Government have made available for that purpose is hardly sufficient to present the image of an active campaign.

There was a nationwide Department of Education and Science campaign with photographs of the previous Secretary of State and film star videos. I do not recall the Minister appearing in them—perhaps he was not in office at the time. Had he been so, the programme may have been 8 more successful. I believe that a local recruitment approach, aimed at already qualified teachers, is likely to be more successful.

Activity 13(91) is training in the management of pupil behaviour. That is a consequence of the report of the committee chaired by Lord Elton. It suggested that there is not a major problem of behaviour in our schools but what I believe Lord Elton called a difficulty with constant disruption in many schools and the problem that teachers face in handling the disruption and dislocation that occurs in many classes. We all know from experience how difficult it is to teach effectively when such dislocation occurs. Occasionally, we see organised dislocation on the Conservative Benches in the Chamber but dislocation is not as organised in many classrooms because they do not have the benefit of Tory Whips. However, Lord Elton pointed out that there was an element of unruly behaviour and suggested that the Government should give a commitment to training teachers more effectively in the management of classrooms.

For 1991–92, the Government have made available supported expenditure of £1·8 million. If my sums are right—I shall not go into the 60 per cent. calculation—that is £18,000 per local education authority, which is hardly a substantial sum to deal with the problem of unruly behaviour. They should have given this much greater priority.

I shall finish on a point that was much in evidence in last year's debate, which I am sure the Minister has read with enthusiasm and enjoyment. He will have noticed that we ran full time on that occasion. We were blessed by a long speech from the hon. Member for Southwark and Bermondsey (Mr. Hughes), although from experience it is not possible to get any other speech from the hon. Gentleman. That was probably one of the reasons why we went full time.

A matter that concerned both sides of the Committee last year was the work of the Workers Educational Association and the changes introduced to support its activities from the centre through the education support mechanism. We have read that the WEA's support is 70 per cent., rather than 60 per cent. for other grants. We were concerned that the new mechanisms would lead to an overall reduction in the WEA programme. That view was expressed by members of the Committee, in particular by the hon. Member for Banbury (Mr. Baldry), who has now gone on to greener pastures. At that stage, as he announced in Committee, he was doing an excellent job as the Parliamentary Private Secretary to the then Secretary of State for Energy. Such is the pace of change, not just in education, that I cannot work out who then was Secretary of State for Energy. However, the hon. Member for Banbury expressed anxiety about what would happen to the WEA programme in the Oxfordshire area.

I know that it is relatively early days and that we are not far into the WEA programme, but will the Minister give us some idea how it is working, whether it is holding up nationwide and whether there are pockets within it where individual local authorities are not making the bids and it is collapsing as a result? I shall not delay the Committee further but look forward to the Minister's response.

Mr. Peter Bottomley
(Eltham)
: What matters to working teachers is not matter whether training is supported by central Government but the knowledge that they have support in their work so that when they need retraining, extra training or guidance it is available to them.

I now that many teachers look enviously at employees of other parts of the local government service who can take time off work for training. Teachers, especially in primary schools, have a responsibility day after day and hour after hour to give their full-time attention to children who respond well to their teaching, and most teachers know what a great job they are doing. What those teachers do not know is that other people know what a good job they are doing because, almost by definition, other people are excluded from schools and from the classroom. Some parents may go to schools and others have jobs linked to the profession, but it is an isolated profession.

Many teachers have, in the past few years of prosperity, noticed not only how teachers' pay has improved since 1979—we need not go into that in the absence of the hon. Member for Blackburn (Mr. Straw)— but how people who were at school, college or university with them have apparently done substantially better. However, they may not have noticed the redundancies in other professions during what might be called the present recession.

All of us in public service, in politics at national and local level, as Members of Parliament and councillors, have a responsibility to try to ensure that the rest of the population knows what a teacher's life is like. Clearly teachers must earn the respect of others, but if others cannot see the work that they do, we must point it out to them.

On the appraisal of teachers, the person who has the most interest in ensuring that the struggling teacher gets help is the head teacher of the school. Perhaps we should do more to acknowledge the head teacher's role. It is important that more resources, whether they are allocated through the national grant or through normal systems of local education authority funding, reach head teachers so that they can identify experienced teachers or trainees who need extra help. There would then be a chance of achieving the results that teachers want, and standards would not be allowed to slip.

A major problem is the attitude of teachers to developments in the past two or three years. Many of them welcome the national curriculum, but most of them are appalled by the record keeping required for the assessment of pupils. I discovered yesterday when I visited Ealdham Square primary school that whereas the computer has replaced most clerical work in virtually every other sector—retail, production and commerce—teachers are expected to keep detailed manual records that no one is likely to look at again. It is ludicrous to think that, except in special cases, a receiving teacher in a secondary school will go through all the detailed records on pupils from the age of five, six or seven. I hope that my hon. Friend the Minister will allow me to visit Elizabeth House and be shown by a senior adviser the time that it takes for an ordinary teacher to do the assessment in the lower classes of a primary school. We can then multiply that by 30 and work out how much time is devoted to such assessment. I ask no more from my hon. Friend today than for him to arrange that.

10 Some of the national grants to aid local expenditure should be devoted to the excellent publications that have been produced for primary teachers, which would probably take a primary teacher about half a term to read and learn. During the first year of transition to a more regorous system, there is a multiplication of work, which will improve in the second year of the system. In the current year, we should take every opportunity to say to teachers, "We admire what you do, we recognise that you are achieving substantially more, and we recognise, above all, that the problem is not simply one of children who fail, but it used to be one of children who never experienced success." Our teachers are achieving a great deal more. There is recognition across the political divide that they need support. I hope that the grants will make it possible for them to continue to the standards that they want to achieve and that we need, and that they will get the recognition that will keep them going, even when times seem rough.

: Mr hon. Friend the Member for Leeds, Central (Mr. Fatchett) and I are probably the only two hon. Members present who served on the Committee of the Education Reform Act 1988. In the ever-changing political scene, it is difficult to know who is responsible for what.

The Minister's brief sounded very good and, with the support of his civil servants, put a gloss on the money that has been allocated as a result of the changes. My mind goes back to the Education Reform Act and the two or three months that I spent in Standing Committee, which was the longest running Committee since 1945. Many times we pointed out to Ministers the obvious problems that would arise from the mountainous task that was being placed upon the profession.

The Minister said that our purpose is to merge two grants to achieve better efficiency. Whenever Governments speak of mergers, people get less funding; it is an easy way to conceal reductions. It was stated that the allocation of funds will not be inflation-proofed. The Government are making reductions in the allocation of the most-needed funds.

The schedule to the regulations mentions training for school governors. The Education Reform Act has led to a serious problem in the training provided for school governors. Although the Government may say that they will provide adequate training, it can be prophesied that the lack of it will be a grave problem in many cities. Under the local management of schools and other such innovations, people are being confronted with demands that were not envisaged.

Only last weekend, my local vicar asked my wife whether she would help at one of the local high schools. Schools are having great difficulty in finding people. The demands made by Government, and the responsibilities imposed on governors, have resulted in fewer people volunteering. Being a governor is an onerous task and governors meet frequently. People do not want the responsibility.

I remember, during the long days and nights of the Committee stage of the Education Reform Act, that Ministers said that many people would come forward to act as governors and managers. It was emphasised that people from industry would volunteer to make schools efficient and work like clockwork, as industry is supposed to work. JPEG 11 But industry does not work like clockwork. People from industry found that they could not spare the time to achieve the efficiency that education Ministers said would result from those clever people volunteering to manage schools. I have grave reservations, and expect that the Department will have to give the matter further consideration.

The Minister listed the goodies that would be provided by the extra money—race relations and goodness knows what else. When it comes to the crunch, it will be a thin allocation. We shall see what always happens on such occasions—a mad scramble of people fighting for their share of the money.

I was interested to hear the hon. Member for Eltham (Mr. Bottomley) refer to the value of teachers. I wish that Conservative Members would speak more often about the value of teachers. The Government must face the fact that they do not have a good reputation.

Mr. Eastham

Member for Wanstead and Woodford (Mr. Arbuthnot) was not here then, but I 12 remember the period. If the hon. Gentleman looks at the records, he will find that some very strange speeches were made at that time.

I do not want to burden the Committee by talking about the value of teachers. Lecturers, teachers and others in the teaching world know that the Government do not come out smelling of roses when they supposedly place a value on teachers' professionalism. Some of the clever and bright people ask themselves why they should carry on teaching when they can go into industry, become yuppies and be rewarded handsomely for doing half as much.

I agree with the hon. Member for Eltham, who values teachers, that teacher training is important and essential. In inner cities especially, when local authorities want to send teachers on training courses, they find that there are no replacement teachers to take over classes. Consequently, the opportunity to train is not always available for teachers. Computers were mentioned. These days, it seems to be thought that computers will cut the work load and offers a magical solution. Anyone who knows anything about computers realises that that is not true. As the saying goes, if one puts rubbish in, one gets rubbish out. It is extremely time demanding for teachers to keep records and so on. Teachers often spend weekends working. They do not leave school at 4 p.m. on Friday saying that they have finished work until Monday. I know from family experience that teachers often spend time on Saturdays and Sundays trying to knock out reports, which are demanded under the Education Reform Act.

That Act has not been a magical solution. All the prophesies that were made about it have come true. The Secretary of State has had to make the alterations that the Opposition suggested. We pointed out its faults. The Government were determined to go ahead, but the reforms turned out to be a complete disaster, which led to the near collapse of some schools. Paragraph 16(b) of the schedule refers to education authorities

"providing advice in relation to the national curriculum (as defined in section 2 of the Education Reform Act 1988 and the delegation of financial management pursuant to schemes made under section 33 of that Act."

I assure the Minister that that is one of the nightmares in education. I received a letter from a constituent yesterday about the financial problems of LMS. A school was desperately short of teachers and needed two or three more, but when it sorted out its budget it realised that it did not have enough money for even one more teacher to satisfy its urgent requirements. Head teachers must now decide whether to have the grass cut or the windows cleaned, never mind the problem of finding £18,000 for an additional, urgently-needed teacher.

The Minister said that money was available, but I want to emphasise that he is offering less—his figure is not even inflation-linked. Schools will need much more money to keep their heads above water. The Education Reform Act will be strung around the Government's neck as one of their biggest nightmares. It could have been successful. We wanted reforms and were not opposed to everything, but changes must be matched with adequate funds.
