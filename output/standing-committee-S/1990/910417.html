AGE OF LEGAL CAPACITY (SCOTLAND) BILL

10.30 am

Clause 1
AGE OF LEGAL CAPACITY

Dr. Norman A. Godman
(Greenock and Port Glasgow)
: I beg to move amendment No. 2, in page 1, line 13, leave out "time" and insert "transaction entered into".

I am not here to perform the role of an awkward customer and I am sure that members of the Committee will agree that, especially at present, no measures concerning the rights of children and their interests should go through the House of Commons without scrutiny. For self-evident reasons, we had no Second Reading debate on the Bill. In principle, it has a great deal of merit. However, there are anxieties which, I hope, can be allayed by the hon. and learned Member for Perth and Kinross (Sir N. Fairbairn). I speak with some diffidence as the Bill is presented and supported by a bevy of parliamentary notables, but I expect to receive a reasonable hearing from the hon. and learned Gentleman.

In a recent article, Professor Thomson, a professor of law at Glasgow university said:

"At the very least, should the Age of Capacity (Scotland) Bill proceed any further, it should be subject to detailed and critical parliamentary scrutiny."

I am sure that we agree with that. Similarly, Kathleen Marshall, the director of the Scottish child law centre said:

"The effects of the Bill, if enacted, will be much wider and more radical than envisaged by the consultation process and the research. The Bill will alter the status of young people in Scotland"—

she is referring especially to clause 1—

"between the ages of 12 and 16, depriving them of fundamental rights to make or influence decisions bearing on control of their own persons."

Those legitimate anxieties are worthy of tough-minded scrutiny.

The helpful explanatory memorandum to the Bill states:

"subsection (1)(a) implements the new rule that, subject to certain limited exceptions set out in clause 2, a person under the age of 16 shall have no legal capacity to enter into transactions."

Later in the debate, I shall question the hon. and learned Gentleman on his definition of "transactions". It is fair to say that laymen and laywomen would interpret the word "transactions" as meaning a commercial or legal transaction. However, if one looks for definitions of the word "transaction" in the Scottish dictionary or the second edition of the Oxford English dictionary, one finds a plethora of descriptions of it, including "a covenant" and "negotiated agreement". For example, if a youngster is examined by a doctor and they come to an agreement, by way of the doctor's professional expertise, about the method of treatment to cure the young person's illness, is that a transaction? If a young girl is interviewed by a social 4 worker because of serious allegations of abuse in her family, is it a transaction if an agreement is reached whereby the child accepts that certain action should be taken to protect her?

I do not wish to dwell on the subject but I seek guidance from the hon. and learned Gentleman on the advisability of limiting clause 1 to "transactions", as my amendment would do.

Sir Nicholas Fairbairn
(Perth and Kinross)
: I compliment the hon. member for Greenock and Port Glasgow (Dr. Godman) on his civility to advise me that he would table amendments to ensure that the principles behind the Bill were certain and sure, and that its concept was not mistaken.

The purpose of the Bill is to protect children while they are presumed immature, without in any way invading their capacity to contract in normal ways. Present law is based on two extraordinary fantasies which are the father and mother of the law of Scotland. The first relates to fertility. A girl child is presumed to alter her contractual status on the basis of the fact that she starts ovulating at 12. A boy child is assumed to alter his status contract because he is presumed to be fertile at the age of 14.

Those factors have an echo in the corpus juris civilis of the law of Rome about the liberation of slaves. It is fatuous that we have continued to accept in the law of Scotland protections, alterations and language that have nothing to do with contractual ability. The Bill proposes an important reform that would sweep away provisions based on mirage and mystique with no basis of maturity.

I am unwilling to accept the hon. Gentleman's amendment because, although status is altered at the age of 16, any child can enter into any contract that a child is likely to enter into, such as buying a Mars bar or a bottle of Pepsi. That is a contract or, if the hon. Gentleman wishes, a transaction. In the Bill there are four exceptions, including making a will, but the amendment runs counter to the Bill and would take us back to the concept of minors and pupils. At present and, I hope, after the Bill is passed any child can enter into any transaction that at that age it is likely to enter into without consent. A child cannot buy an estate or a Roll-Royce because that is not expected, but it can buy a Mars bar, a Coca-Cola, a book or whatever else a child is expected to buy.

The general law of Scotland is not altered by the Bill. All that is swept away is the archaic concept of minority and pupillage and, with certain protections, the notion that until the quadriennium utile is passed a child can get out of a contract. At present, that is until the child is aged 22, under the concept that it would not enter into a contract that was offensive, which an adult of maturity—if that concept is known to the House—would not have entered into.

Mr. John Maxton
(Glasgow, Cathcart)
: Speak for yourself.

Sir Nicholas Fairburn
: I was speaking to the hon. Member for Greenock and Port Glasgow and I am obliged to him for raising the matter.

The amendment would merely reverse the situation that we are trying to abolish and bring back the concept of minority and pupillage. The Bill deals with four areas of transaction that are exempted from the general principle that the age of 16 gives full capacity, but to limit the JPEG 5 provision to "transactions" would be difficult, whatever the English dictionary may say. I do not know what a transaction is. My winking at the hon. Member for Greenock and Port Glasgow might be thought to be a transaction, but it would be unwise to introduce that into the Bill.

Dr. Godman
: From the silence of normally voluble hon Gentlemen, it seems that they agree with the assurances and accept what the hon. and learned Member for Perth and Kinross (Sir Nicholas Fairburn) has said in response to my amendment. On that basis, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Again, I seek assurances from the hon. and learned Gentleman on the means by which a child can protect itself in difficult family circumstances. I shall use as examples a couple of case studies that involve children in distressing domestic circumstances, I have been given the details of one case—of course, with the usual proviso of protecting confidentiality—that involves a 15-year-old girl who, because of the most appalling circumstances, sought to leave home and move in with her aunt.

Social workers are a much maligned body of people, but they are professionals who do fine work on behalf of children and others. Some may say that I must say that as my wife is a senior social worker, but I would say it even if I were not married to that delightful lady.

Sir Nicholas Fairbairn
: Did the hon. Gentleman say 15?

Dr. Godman
: Yes, the case involves a girl of 15. The social workers agreed to the girl moving in with her aunt, but they wanted to know whether the girl's parents could force her to return to her family home. As the girl was a minor, no offence would be committed if she left the family home, but the parents could seek a court order for her delivery back to them. Perhaps the hon. and learned Gentleman will correct me if I am wrong, but in those circumstances under existing law the minor's wishes would be regarded as exceptionally weighty and would be adhered to so long as they were not unreasonable.

I tabled the amendment because the Bill might reduce the girl to the status of a pupil. The aunt might be threatened with a charge of plagium—child stealing—which applies to pupil children only. The loss of minor's ability to move in with another family member might result in the greater use of the procedures for

"taking refuge in a place of safety"

and in greater intervention by social workers in cases where that was previously deemed unnecessary. I seek an assurance from the hon. and learned Gentleman about such cases.

A second case study involves a 14-year-old girl in Strathclyde who made allegations of sexual abuse to the 6 social work department. Her mother insisted on being present at the interview with the social worker. As the girl is a minor under current law, she has the right to decide on the circumstances of the interview and to have confidentiality respected. It has been claimed to me that an unintended consequence of the Bill would be to deny that young person the right of access to her files and to give that right to the parent. The parent might also be able to insist on being present at such an interview. Under the Bill, would that young girl lose such protection?

Without referring to matters that are sub judice, disclosure of abuse is important. My amendment is a probing one and I seek the preservation of the rights of minors to have a degree of control over their person. It is essential that every protection is given to the child in cases of wilful neglect, physical and sexual abuse. Such circumstances are difficult not only for the child, but for professionals, such as doctors, social workers, teachers and others, who try to defend such vulnerable young people.

In my usual way, I am seeking assurances from the hon. and learned Gentleman that help will be offered when the first disclosure of abuse comes to light and children seek help with the violations inflicted on them, perhaps by their own fathers or close relatives. There are cases pending in Scotland and we must ensure that such children are given the utmost protection in the intitial stages of an investigation. The Bill must not harm a vulnerable child who is caught up in such disgusting circumstances.

Member for Greenock and Port Glasgow (Dr. Godman) referred to Professor Thomson of the university of Strathclyde, he was referring to a speech that was made to the Scottish child law centre on 14th June 1989, which was the day after I spoke on the same subject in the Committee stage of the Children Act.

Dr. Godman

Mr. Devlin

Sir Nicholas Fairbairn
: My hon. Friend will, as usual, be amazed to know that we have the Law Reform (Parent and Child) (Scotland) Act 1986, which preceded by three years the English legislation and is considerably preferable.

Mr. Devlin
: The interesting point about that is that section 1 of the Children Act 1989 refers to the paramountcy of the child's interest and deals with the issue to which the hon. Member for Greenock and Port Glasgow referred.

There is anxiety about the way in which children's legislation is being implemented by social work departments throughout the country. It is not yet clear at what age children are capable of saying what their best interests are. In some circumstances, children may be too young to decide what those may be. That is why I referred to my speech on 13 June 1989.

The Children Act 1989, the Bill and the Scottish legislation do nothing about the conflicting specifications JPEG 7 of the age of majority. For example, one can own a shotgun at the age of 12, but one cannot buy fireworks until the age of 16. Similarly, one can drive a car at 17, but cannot make a will until 18, although one can join the army. When we consider what is best for children, at what age they should be consulted and at what age their wishes should be paramount, we are in limbo.

Social work departments are interpreting our legislation to mean that they should listen to a child of any age and treat his or her wishes as paramount. That is not what the legislation provides. It provides that the interests—not the wishes—of the child should be paramount. Children often have wishes that are not in their best interests. Because of their extreme youth they are not capable of taking into account all the factors to be considered when a decision is to be taken on their behalf.

Dr. Godman

For example, the English system would benefit from the introduction of children's hearings, and the Scottish legal system is lagging behind the English system in respect of the use in evidence of video recorded interviews, as proposed in the Criminal Justice Bill.

Mr. Devlin
: Certainly, when we discussed the English legislation, the system of children's hearings was put forward as a good example of what could be done south of the border. I agree with the hon. Gentleman that English and Scottish legal systems can learn from each other in many areas.

I have considerable experience of Scottish Standing Committees and in the past my honourable and learned Friend the Member for Perth and Kinross (Sir N. Fairbairn) has frequently told me that under the Act of Union, the Scots protected their system of law, that that is what distinguishes them from the English, and that there is no reason why the two systems should converge. I am, therefore, interested to hear him today recommending a measure that will do just that. Where will the future distinctions lie?

The Chairman
: Order. Perhaps I can help the honourable Gentleman. We are considering a Bill that relates exclusively to Scottish law.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton)
: I shall be extremely brief, Mr. Martin. The Government welcome the Bill. The report on which it is based is the first stage of a major project by the Scottish Law Commission on the law of children. In due course, there will be further reports on the related topics of custody and guardianship and, more generally, the scope of parental rights and duties.

It may interest honourable Members to know that the Scottish Office consulted various interest groups on the Commission's proposals and found that there was substantial support for the recommendations in the report. 8 The Bill is an important first stage, but other stages will follow.

Mr Andrew Welsh

I congratulate the honourable Member for Greenock and Port Glasgow (Dr. Godman) and the Scottish child law centre on drawing attention to possible problems in what is otherwise generally agreed by lawyers in my constituency to be a useful consolidation of the law on legal capacity. It is important to explore the issues and for the sponsors of the Bill to explore the issues and for the sponsors of the Bill to explain clearly how it would affect the existing rights of persons now categorised as minors.

The hon. Gentleman a most important issue. I should like to emphasise that the Bill does not in any way impede the protection of any child under 16—on the contrary, it promotes it.
