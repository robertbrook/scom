The National Health Service in Scotland

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Mr. Michael Forsyth)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of the National Health Service in Scotland.

The health service in Scotland is as much about health as about treating sickness. The quality of our national health service springs directly from the staff who are involved in it. They account for about three quarters of the expenditure on the service. If the staff are prevented from giving of their best, the quality of care will suffer.

The task for the Government and, indeed, for all of us is clear. It is to ensure an effective management structure within the health service and to ensure that it has the resources needed to achieve what is expected of it. Our task is also to ensure that staff are motivated to give of their best, that they have the information that they require, that they have the skills that they need, and that they are free to get on with the job. That is central to the health service reforms. All boards will be required to draft local health strategies, which will provide a rigorous analysis of the needs of the population.

In the past, health boards have been distracted by the minutiae of management of day-to-day affairs in hospitals. The boards' task is now to achieve the best possible health care for the populations whom they serve. That means negotiating service agreements with providers and monitoring performance. For the first time in the health service, standards of quality of service will be set down and there will be the means, through contracts, to ensure that they are achieved.

The fundamental part of the reforms is the separation of the providers from the purchasers' role. That ensures that services are determined by need, that resources are used effectively and that quality and performance are monitored from the top of the service to the bottom.

As I have said, it is important that staff should have the information that they need and freedom to act. Part of the information-gathering process, which is essential to ensure quality of care, is the introduction of medical audit. That is central to our NHS reforms. Medical audit means that doctors work together to identify the treatments that benefit patients most, as well as those that have little or no known benefit.

For example, the Lothian surgical audit has been carried out in the main specialties over 20 years. All surgeons have taken part in that audit and, because of their efforts, death rates have been significantly reduced. In cases of cancer of the large bowel, there has been a reduction of nearly one third in perioperative deaths; where seven died before, only five now die. In gall-bladder surgery, the death rate has been almost halved; where 167 deaths would have been expected in Scotland, only 97 have occurred in a recent three-year period. Seventy lives have been saved. That is an outstanding example of 2 collaboration, and we are now funding its extension beyond Lothian and the south-east of Scotland to Tayside and Grampian.

Hospitals in Greater Glasgow are developing their own parallel systems on a slightly different but compatible basis. We can look forward to gains similar to those already achieved in Lothian being repeated throughout the country. Indeed, we now have about 550 audit projects in Scotland, which are investigating variations in quality and encouraging changes in clinical practice to secure better care and use of resources. I should like audit to be extended to nurses, dentists, pharmacists and clinical professions. We have provided £5 million of additional resources this year to finance an expanding programme.

If performance and quality information is being gathered, what of the freedom to act? That brings us to the importance of NHS trusts.
