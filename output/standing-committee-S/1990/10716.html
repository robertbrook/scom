The Scottish Economy

10.30 am

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Ian Lang)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of the Scottish economy.

This is a good time to debate the Scottish economy. Given the mythology that will doubtless colour the debate once Opposition Members start to contribute to it later, it might be sensible to point out at the outset that the last time the Labour party was running the Scottish economy, from 1974–79, its record on economic growth in Scotland was of less than 1 per cent. per annum. Inflation reached a peak of 27 per cent. and averaged 15 per cent., industrial relations were in complete disarray and the unions imposed their will on the Government and individuals. Unemployment doubled in Scotland, productivity was abysmal and confidence in the economy was at a post-war low. The Labour party's current approach shows little change from the approach that produced those outcomes in the 1970s.

By contrast, the Scottish economy of today, after the Conservative party's tenure, is forward-looking and competitive. One evidence of this is the growth in self-employment. Over the seven years to last December, self-employment in Scotland rose by over three times the rate of employment growth generally. The number of registered companies in Scotland at the beginning of this year had increased by over two-thirds since the beginning of 1980. Those figures epitomise the greater spirit of confidence and enterprise that now characterises Scottish industry.

(Carrick, Cumnock and Doon Valley)

What is the Secretary of State doing about that? The people of Dumfries are not optimistic and have no such confidence. Mr. Lang
: I am sure that the people of Dumfries will have more sense than to risk that folly, and that they will be keen to return my hon. Friend the Member for Dumfries (Sir H. Monro). The hon. Member for Carrick, Cumnock and Doon Valley (Mr. Foulkes) should have contained himself and been patient a little longer because I was about to say that I readily accept that problems remain. Our long-standing dependence on traditional heavy industries has meant that the transition to a modern economy has taken longer and been more difficult to achieve than in many other countries, but it is the overall picture that matters.

2 I do not think that anyone could seriously dispute that the Scottish economy is beginning the 1990s in far better shape than it entered the 1980s. Perhaps the best indicator of that is the fact that Scottish gross domestic product per head was almost one quarter higher in real terms in 1989 than in 1979. In other words, after accounting for inflation, the people of Scotland are now making £5 of GDP for every £4 made in the past. The current levels of Scottish GDP per head and personal incomes are higher than in any other area of northern Britain, the west midlands and Wales—a sure sign of a relatively prosperous economy.

I do not seek to deny that, according to the latest figures, manufacturing output in Scotland, in common with the United Kingdom as a whole, has recently been falling. However, that has to be kept in perspective. Manufacturing output in Scotland in 1990 was 12 per cent. above its level in 1979. With productivity rising at an average of over 5 per cent. per annum in the 1980s, Scotland's productivity record has been higher than that of the United Kingdom as a whole and, indeed, higher than that achieved in the USA, Japan, Germany, France, Italy and Canada. In addition, Scottish manufactured exports rose by some 20 per cent. in real terms in the 10 years to 1989. Indeed, Scottish manufacturers in 1989 exported roughly £19,000 worth of goods per employee—a fifth higher than in the United Kingdom generally.

Mr. Lang
: We do not yet have all the figures for 1990, but after the dramatic expansion in investment, output and employment in 1990, the results have been less encouraging. That must be set against the background of real growth, about which I have spoken.

Taking these facts together, it is difficult to see how Scottish manufacturing can be described as being in a state of decline. Certainly, we are seeing a temporary downturn, in common with the United Kingdom as a whole. It would be surprising if that were not the case, given the integrated nature of the Scottish and United Kingdom economies, but it would surely be misguided to read across from the present situation to any prediction of the future performance of Scottish manufacturing industry. Recessions, by their nature, are temporary. There are differing views on how soon we may expect to see recovery in the United Kingdom as a whole, but on the basis of economic indicators, business surveys and the views of independent forecasters, there is no reason to doubt that the recession in Scotland will be briefer and involve a smaller loss of output than in the United Kingdom as a whole.

There is a vital contributory factor to the reverse in the migratory trend in Scotland. The performance of the 4 Scottish economy is directly related to the economic restructuring that has taken place in past years. The Scottish economy of today is much more broadly based and diversified than it was even a decade ago. That is particularly significant when we compare present economic conditions with those in the early 1980s. Then, a severe world recession coincided with the restructuring of British and Scottish industry. The adjustments that had to be made were massive because industrial productivity was much lower and employment was more heavily concentrated in industries undergoing long-term decline. Now that many of the adjustments that had to be made have been made, industry is far better placed to withstand the present difficulties. That is not just the Government's view; it is the view of 83 per cent. of the United Kingdom's largest manufacturers in a recent survey by Investors in Industry. I believe that that view has particular force in Scotland. Gone are the days when the Scottish economy was dependent on a narrow industrial base with little room for manoeuvre if things went wrong. Today, the skills and aptitudes developed from years of experience in the traditional industries are finding expression across the whole range of industrial activity in modern Scotland. Incoming industrialists testify to the fact that the Scottish labour force is one of the most flexible and adaptable in the world.

I see the 1990s as opening unparalleled opportunities for Scottish industry. With the advent of the single European market those opportunities will present themselves particularly in Europe, but I am confident that Scottish industry will also continue to develop markets in the rest of the world. I am confident, too, of the calibre of Scottish industry and of its ability to go on contributing to the high standard of living of the Scottish people. That was clearly achieved in the 1980s. I believe that there is every prospect that it will continue to be achieved in the 1990s.
