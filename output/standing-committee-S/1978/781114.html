legislation TOBACCO PRODUCTS (HIGHER TAR CIGARETTES) REGULATIONS 1978

The Chairman: May I first inform the Committee that I have given considerable thought to whether we could take these two regulations together. The disparity of the subject matter is such that it would not be for the convenience of the Committee. We shall, therefore, take them separately.

10.30 a.m.

Dr. Gerard Vaughan: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the Tobacco Products (Higher Tar Cigarettes) Regutions 1978 (S.I. No. 1156).

On the face of it these regulations are innocuous technical regulations until one looks closer, particularly at the circumstances behind them. I believe that it will help the Minister if I begin by raising the three points that concern us.

First, the regulations appear to be a clear breach of faith by the Government. For some years the industry has been cooperating with the Government to bring about a reduction in smoking and on a general health programme concerning tobacco products and how they should be marketed. The industry is pleased with the progress which has been made.

Earlier this year the industry had a clear understanding from the Minister of State that if it followed the programme which was discussed in March no further regulations would be introduced until 1980.

The industry has been pursuing a programme of health warnings on packets and on advertisements—which I believe has been very successful—as well as a programme for an advertising code of practice. The industry has been cooperating with the Hunter Committee on substitutes and additives.

The Minister of State, Department of Health and Social Security (Mr. Roland Moyle): The hon. Gentleman refers to the Col. 4 Minister of State. Is he referring to my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State or myself?

Dr. Vaughan: I will come to that in a moment.

The industry entered into a voluntary agreement on sponsorship which was agreed in December 1977. It also entered into a voluntary agreement about a move towards low tar cigarettes in March 1977. It is that agreement which has now been broken.

Mr. Moyle: If the hon. Gentleman is referring to me as the Minister of State, our agreement with the tobacco industry is that we would not seek to alter those matters in the agreement for three years from the date of entry. There is no mention of taxation in that agreement.

Dr. Vaughan: This is particularly relevant to the regulations before us today. On 8th March 1977 the Secretary of State announced a three year package, agreed voluntarily with the industry. The package was to include a whole series of items—I shall not go through all of them—but the immediate step was that no main media advertising should be carried out on brands of cigarettes in the high tar group.

By 31st December 1978—which we have not yet reached—there was to be no major advertising of the brands in the middle to high tar groups. The industry also agreed that no new brand would be introduced with a tar yield of more than 23 mg. The industry agreed that no existing brand would have its tar yield intentionally raised. No brands would appear in the high tar group of the Government's tar tables after 31st March 1979.

I am referring to these dates because we have not yet reached December 1978 We are not yet at March 1979, although, again there was an agreement. I will not go through the remaining items of the package, but the point I want to make is that here was an agreement entered into voluntarily by the industry with the Government embodying dates which have not yet been reached. The industry was moving steadily towards honouring its undertaking, and suddenly into this area are thrown regulations which are pushing at a process which was occurring anyway.

Image Col. 5 No doubt, one of the Ministers will tell us that the introduction of the regulations has been satisfactory in that since they were introduced there has been a marked fall-off in the tar yield. Frankly, that was happening anyway. What has occurred is that they have pushed at a process which was taking place. Quite naturally, therefore—and rightly, I believe—the industry is upset that it was honouring its side of the agreement yet the Government have not honoured their side.

The second point I make is that the schedule is technically faulty. Its criteria are difficult to carry out. The industry has been left in a state of uncertainty as to whether it was fulfilling the requirements. I do not think that the Government should have introduced regulations such as these which are sloppy in their wording and which I understand, are against the advice which the industry gave as to how this control should be regulated.

There is also the question of freedom of choice. Whether we like it or not, a large section of the population wishes to retain the right to exercise its own judgment on various matters. I, as a doctor, find myself in a dilemma, for example, over seat belt legislation. There is no doubt but that the introduction of seat belts is a strong safety measure. But a large number of people wish to be free to decide whether they wear seat belts. We had the same sort of issues with crash helmets as we have here with smoking. When this matter was raised during Committee stage of the Finance Bill it was put very strongly indeed that it was wrong of the Government to use legislation for social manipulation. My hon. Friend the Member for Weston-super-Mare (Mr. Wiggin) said:

"I cannot say that I approve of the prospect of directing social policy by penal taxation. Much is said about freedom of choice of the individual. Much is said also about Governments keeping away from altering the social patterns of life by artificial means. Yet here is a tax which, by the Chancellor's own admission, has been introduced purely to alter people's preferences by putting a higher tax on certain cigarettes.


Where will this principle end once it is established? Shall we be told next which drinks are good for us to drink, which shoes are good for us to wear, which places are good for us to visit—"—[Official Report, Standing Committee A, 17th May 1978; c. 8.]

Col. 6 I quoted that in full because it highlights a view which we take. There is a limit to the way in which legislation should be used to direct what many people regard as our personal lives. It was on that basis, that I, on behalf of my party, put down the prayer against these regulations.

10.39 a.m.

Mr. George Thompson: I must admit that I was completely flabbergasted to hear the hon. Member for Reading, South (Dr. Vaughan) speak as he did at the end of his speech. He mentioned the problem of freedom of choice and he instanced crash helmets. What we did in that case was to take away the freedom of choice and to insist on the wearing of crash helmets. In that case we as a society must have agreed that that was the right thing to do. If, in his opinion, seat belts give the extra safety that he says they do, we ought to consent to that measure, too. In these regulations I believe that we should bear in mind that aspect rather more than the other one.

I do not know how many Members of the Committee are addicted to cartoons, but I read a periodical called the Weekly News, not for what is written in it, but for the cartoons. That periodical regularly features a character called "Wee Hughie". One cartoon that appeared many years ago has stuck in my memory ever since. "Wee Hughie" is sitting in his pyjamas on the edge of his bed. The clock says seven o'clock—presumably in the morning, because he has a lot of shadow around his jaw. The next frame shows him lighting his cigarette, his wife beginning to look rather annoyed. Then there are other frames showing him spluttering and coughing. Finally, his face lights up and he says "Aa aye say, the first wan's the best wan o'the day", which I shall translate for anyone who is not expert in the Glasgow dialect as "The first one is the best one of the day." I thought that was funny. But I am quite convinced that there are households in this country where that is the first scene of the day. It would be better for us all if that were not so. One valid way of looking at the problem is through the eyes of the cartoonist. But for a more sober way, I turned to the reports of the Scottish Home and Health Department, and principally to the 1974 report, which devoted a whole chapter precisely to that subject and which put quite a different complexion Image Col. 7 on the matter to that put by the hon. Member for Reading, South. The chapter opens with a categorical statement:

"Cigarette smoking is the largest single avoidable health hazard in Scotland today".

The paragraph then gives a statistic:

"Scotland spends more per household and a larger percentage of total weekly expenditure on tobacco than any other area of Britain."

The paragraph concludes menacingly and laconically:

"Its health statistics show that it pays the penalty."

I believe that we should consider today's statutory instrument in the light of that report.

I want to refer to one further paragraph. Paragraph 6.7 deals with perinatal mortality. It states that:

"about 1,500 perinatal deaths occur each year due to smoking during pregnancy".

These babies are lost simply because of their mothers' smoking. In addition, it says that the great majority of children who survive:

"suffer slight but measurable long-term physical and mental ill-effects at least until the age of seven."

It is these early years that are so vital in a child's physical and mental development. Mothers will have played a significant role in lastingly harming their children. Therefore, we in Parliament should do everything we can to mend the ways of these people, and we should do it with a good conscience.

Finally, I should like to pay tribute to the Scottish Health Education Unit. It has done a fine job in its efforts to educate people away from smoking through the use of television, press, posters, leaflets and films. I mentioned "Wee Hughie", and I should like to say that the Scottish Health Education Unit is now using cartoons in Lowland Scots to get its message over. As a Scots speaker myself, I am delighted about that. I believe that Gaelic, too, should be used. A pointed cartoon in the Gaelic quarterly "Grairm" could carry the message to Highlands and Islands hearts, too.

By supporting these regulations we shall be doing a little to reduce the misery caused by lung cancer, chronic bronchitis, emphysema and coronary heart disease, all of which are among the modern curses in Scotland and, indeed, perhaps in Col. 8 a lesser way, in other parts of the United Kingdom. I hope that in his reply, the Minister will resist any blandishments, threats or talk of freedom of choice from the official Opposition.

Dr. Vaughan: Is the hon. Gentleman always in favour, then, of compulsion or is he in favour of education in these matters? Much of his speech seemed to be in favour of education and understanding.

Mr. Thompson: I think that I am in favour of both. I see it as a two-pronged fork, and the Scottish health education unit is doing an excellent job in persuading people. As a former teacher, I had the impression that there was less smoking among young people towards the end of my teaching career than there was at the beginning. I hope that that trend will continue. I am in favour of measures such as this in order to give encouragement. There must be a bit of the carrot and a bit of the stick; that is always good in education.

10.45 a.m.

Mr. John Cope (Gloucestershire, South): I start by declaring that I have a constituency interest. My constituency is near Bristol, which is well known for its tobacco trade. However, I have no personal interest in the tax. I am a non-smoker. I have not smoked a cigarette for a great many years, nor have I smoked many in total during my life.

This tax is intended not to raise revenue but to change habits. It is worth remarking that the last person who wants smoking stopped in this country is the Chancellor of the Exchequer, because he could not possibly afford the loss, of £.2½ billion in revenue which the actual stopping of smoking would cause him. Quite frankly, I believe that the tax is the muddled product of sloppy thinking. That was demonstrated to a certain extent in Committe and is demonstrated even more by these detailed regulations. It introduces a complicated new chunk of tax law, of which these regulations form a part, and which, so far as I can see, has been hastily shambled together. It involves a penal tax based on highly wobbly statistics, as I shall seek to show in a moment.

Anybody who has actually studied the details of how the tar measurement works could support the tax, which is based on Image Col. 9 that method of measurement, only if he were as blindly anti-smoking as the hon. Member for Galloway (Mr. Thompson) seems to be. He appears to be so against smoking that he will accept any measure of compulsion or anything else against it.

I think that, even if one is against smoking, it is not desirable to introduce a shambles of this kind into our tax laws, which already give rise to enough problems. I do not seek to argue in any way the health merits or demerits of cigarettes in general or of higher tar cigarettes in particular.

However, I should like to remark, in passing, that the fact—if it is a fact—that the lack of higher tar cigarettes may reduce the incidence of certain diseases does not mean that those who give up smoking on that account, or who move on to cigarettes in lower tar brackets, will therefore, live for ever. It means that they will die of some other disease, or of the same disease caused by some other factor. It may or may not be in the interest of their happiness, but whatever else it means it is likely to prove more expensive to the rest of us in paying old pensions than the present set-up.

Having said that, I wish to return to the details of the regulations. I should like to put to the Financial Secretary two sets of questions, as it were, the first related to the details of what the regulations mean and the second about the effect in practice of the regulations.

The regulations have been in force for two months. We complained at the time that the Finance Bill was brought before us that it would not be possible to have the detailed regulations available by the time they had to be put into effect, because of the very early date from which the tax was supposed to apply. Unfortunately, the amendment moved by the hon. Member for Nottingham, East (Mr. Dunnett) in Committee was tied. However, it fell later on and therefore the date remained as it is. The regulations have been in practice for a couple of months.

In considering the details of the regulations, the Committee should realise that tar measurement is not an accurate science. Tobacco is a natural product, and tar is not inherent in the tobacco; it is produced by the smoking process. The method of smoking makes an enor- Col. 10 mous difference to the amount of tar which is drawn out of the cigarette into the smoker's lungs—or into the smoking machine, if that is the object which is smoking it rather than a person—in particular, how many puffs are taken, what size of puff, whether the cigarette is held horizontally or vertically or pointing up or pointing down, the length of the butt that is left, and so on.

The regulations provide that, to produce his tables of high and low tar, the Government chemist shall test a very small sample of cigarettes—25 cigarettes of each brand, out of literally millions of cigarettes. The regulations, therefore, give the force of law to international standard ISO 3308 of 1977. The regulations say that the automatic smoking machine shall comply and be operated in accordance with that standard. However, I understand that the standard is a machine specification rather than a specification of the method to be used in testing cigarettes on the machine, and that it is therefore possible to get different results by using different methods of testing on it.

I understand also that there are different machines which can obtain different results, although they all comply with the standard specification. The two that are apparently in general use in this country now by both the Government Chemist and the companies are the Filtrona 300 and the Phipps and Bird machines. Is it intended that these machines shall be used in future, or are different machines, which are also apparently permitted under the international standard, to be used?

The next point is about conditioning. Paragraph 1 of the Schedule says that conditioning shall be in accordance with the ISO 3402–1975, using atmosphere A. I understand that this provides for tolerances in the conditioning of the cigarettes that are being tested of plus or minus 1ÂC, and that a draft amendment, ISO 3402/DAM 1, in the course of acceptance by the International Standards Organisation, would allow a tolerance of plus or minus 2 per cent, on the tolerances that are acceptable under ISO 3402.

As far as I can see, there is no provision in the regulations as they stand for the acceptance of that amendment without another statutory instrument Image Col. 11 being laid before the House. If that is so, the regulations will become out of date by comparison with the international standard. If it is not so, we should like to know how the amendment—or any future amendments for there may be many more to the standard in the future—will pass into British law from the International Standards Organisation.

My next point concerns the preconditioning of the filter discs and filter holders. This is not provided for either in the regulations or in International Standard 3308, yet it is important because it affects the measurement of how it is done.

These points may seem technical, but they are extremely important to the way in which the results of these tests come out. I am sure that they are not novel points for the Minister because they were all raised with officials at the beginning of July, so there has been ample time to deal with them even before the regulations were laid. I assume that they were considered at that time and I am sure that the Financial Secretary will be able to respond to them.

Other points were raised at the same time. For example, there is no indication in ISO 3308 as to whether there should be electrical or flame ignition. This again can make a difference to the way in which the measurement finishes. Also, different types of holders are allowed by ISO 3308, and I hope that the Financial Secretary will state which it is intended to use.

One particular point concerns cigarettes with an oval cross section. There a,re a few brands which have an oval cross section, and apparently at present there is no holder available under ISO 3308 which deals with these matters. Presumably it is this kind of point which the regulations are intended to cover in paragraph 7 of the schedule.

I consider paragraph 7 most extraordinary. It provides that

"Where owing to abnormality in the shape, size or construction of any cigarettes the procedure set out in paragraphs 1 to 5 … is … inappropriate"

—that is, in the judgment of the Commissioners, I assume—

"it shall be applied in respect of those cigarettes with such modifications as the Commissioners after consultation with the Govern- Col. 12 ment Chemist and the manufacturer or importer shall specify."

I am not sure whether that is legal under the Act. The Finance Act 1978 states:

"The Commissioners may make regulations—(a) prescribing how the tar yield of cigarettes is to be determined for the purposes of this section; .

It does not say that the Commissioners may make regulations prescribing that they shall specify some time in the future—without having to come to Parliament and without having to lay a statutory instrument—when they shall be allowed to decide how the tar yield of cigarettes is to be determined for the purposes of this provision.

I am not a lawyer and it may be that this phrase covers the point, but perhaps the Financial Secretary can reassure me about it.

The next section of the Act states that the Commissioners may, as far as duty being remitted or repaid is concerned—this is also covered in another part of the regulations—"specify or determine" in the regulations. So, in the case of duty being remitted or repaid, the Commissioners do have the power to determine what shall be done. But in the case of prescribing how the tar yield of the cigarettes is to be determined, there seems to be no power in the Act which would support paragraph 7 of the schedule to the regulations. If I am correct, that is an important point, and I should be grateful for the Financial Secretary's views.

My last point on this first group of questions concerns paragraph 3(3) of the regulations, which states,

"The tar yield shall be taken to be the arithmetic mean of the results of tests …"

That is all very well, and indeed it is necessary to have some such provision because the measurements, even of the 25 cigarettes which the Government Chemist takes, will vary several points in the milligrams of tar even when smoked by the machine in the controlled conditions used by the Government Chemist. But why the arithmetic mean? Why is not the median, or, for that matter, the geometric mean being used?

I turn now to how the regulations are working in practice. How is the Government Chemist getting on? Before the Finance Act and before this new tax came Image Col. 13 into force, he was testing about 100 brands—those, I understand, which sell more than 10 million cigarettes in a year. On that basis his latest results were about a year old. He did it on roughly a six-monthly basis and the latest results were a year old—not a very good basis for a tax, and unacceptable as a basis for forming this tax, particularly with the uncertainties of tar measurement.

I presume that the Government Chemist is now having to test not 100, but 200 brands. The fact that they are not popular brands makes no difference. He still has to test 25 cigarettes of even the smallest selling brand. So how far behind is the Laboratory of the Government Chemist in testing? How many brands have been tested, or whatever is the arrangement? To undertake this extra workload and to catch up with the backlog, has the Government Chemist taken on any extra staff, occupied extra premises, or taken over any additional equipment? If so, at what cost, because that is clearly part of the collection costs of this tax and it would be of interest to us—after just over two months in operation—to know the position.

Finally, as regards the tar measurement, can we be told what is the biggest difference so far found between the highest and lowest tar measurement in samples of similar cigarettes? That figure would indicate to the Committee the uncertainties of the tar measurement system, even under the process of these regulations and the details I have described. It would demonstrate the uncertainties of tar measurement and the deeply unsatisfactory nature of trying to base a deliberately penal tax on so haphazard a statistical exercise.

11.2 a.m.

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Robert Sheldon): The speech of the hon. Member for Reading, South (Dr. Vaughan) which initiated the debate surprised me. I did expect that some would argue that taxation should not involve itself in matters other than the strict raising of revenue despite clear resulting advantages.

But I would not have thought anyone with responsibilities for health would need to make that sort of point when there are others who have constituency interests and are readily available, such as the hon. Member for Gloucestershire, South (Mr. Cope). The health aspects of the argument could be left to someone for whom health considerations should be the overriding concern.

I recall going, with my hon. Friend the Member for Brent, South (Mr. Pavitt)—it was about 10 years ago—to the room at the Treasury which I now have the privilege of occupying. The then Financial Secretary was the present Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster. I visited him to show him how taxation could have some effect upon reducing death and sickness caused by the smoking of cigarettes. The hon. Member for Brent, South provided the medical evidence and I provided Image Col. 15 details of some of the ways in which, in a modest fashion, taxation might be used to this purpose. The idea of a taxation surcharge on cigarettes with higher levels of tar has been with us in discussions and consultations for a very long time. When the voluntary agreement was made the considerable attractions of the surcharge idea were voiced publicly. It would have been astonishing if we had failed to introduce this, simply because of some oversight in an industry as voluble, as skilful and as important as the tobacco industry. It has been my great concern that anything I do should not bring about the smoking of dangerous cigarettes and should not damage the industry as a whole, because it is an important industry. I respect the way in which the hon. Member for Gloucestershire, South represented the views of the industry, as it has a right to expect, because its affairs must concern all of us in so far as the manufacturing and selling capacities of the trade are concerned.

Dr. Vaughan: I am not surprised that the Minister takes a slightly ruffled approach to what was said, because it is a very serious criticism of the Government's handling of the matter. Does he not find it rather curious that the Government should enter voluntarily into an agreement with the industry, that on 8th March the Secretary of State should announce a three-year package, that there should be specific dates set out—at which we have not yet arrived—and then, with- Col. 16 out letting that run its course, the Government should introduce these regulations?

Mr. Sheldon: I would have thought that the hon. Gentleman would be delighted if the result of what we did reduced the incidence of those diseases attributable to cigarette smoking. I thought that the hon. Gentleman's purpose in moving this prayer was to come to this Committee and explain his pleasure at such a move. I expected him to go further and point out his own contribution to the action taken by the Government. As for the health of the nation, which must be his prime responsibility, I expected that he would be encouraged by what we have done. I shall say something about the desirable consequences of these regulations later in my remarks.

The hon. Member for Gloucestershire, South, with his constituency interest, pointed to some technical points with which he found fault. The hon. Member for Reading, South also dealt with some of these.

I must defend the regulations because our consultations with the industry resulted in these tests as prescribed in the regulations. During Committee stage of the Finance Bill I gave an undertaking—not a regular thing in such cases—that I would set out the detailed form of these tests. This does not normally find its way into legislation, as the hon. Member will know. We undertook to do this because that was what the Committee wanted and what I perceived the House wanted. I was happy to do this. But what is important is that these regulations meet the requirements of the legislation and also of the industry itself. These regulations, were well established by a joint committee of the Government and the tobacco industry—the Standing Scientific Liaison Committee—on a fully rehearsed statistical basis.

As for the number of cigarettes tested, the absolute minimum from any one set of samples is 25 and in practice tar yield is normally determined on the basis of results from at least 150 cigarettes. I hope that this makes it clear that what has been described, quite rightly, as a proper scientific method, is based very largely on internationally accepted standards, and that the results have a degree of accuracy which makes it possible to ensure that no cigarettes with a true Image Col. 17 mean tar yield below 20mg become liable to the supplementary duty. That is the important thing. I take the point that the hon. Gentleman makes. We have tried to ensure that we have that degree of accuracy which the nature of the industry requires.

The hon. Gentleman referred to paragraph 7 of the schedule. As he rightly points out, this is largely concerned with oval shaped cigarettes. He made some criticism of this. In fact, this paragraph was suggested by the industry because it felt that certain types of cigarettes—perhaps not in such common use as they used to be some years ago, but nobody can be sure of the vagaries of fashion—should be subject to special testing. We were happy to accommodate the industry on this point. I think that the requirements of the industry need to be listened to, that the consultative process must be maintained, and I have sought to do just that.

Mr. Cope: The Minister should not take it that I am solely the spokesman for the industry. Of course, I have consulted the industry, but I do not regard myself as solely the spokesman for the industry on this or any other occasion.

I should not like the Minister to deal with the principal point which I made in this connection, which is whether it is his view and his advice that paragraph 7 of the schedule is legal under the terms of the Act.

Mr. Sheldon: Yes. The vires are established in the way set out in the Finance Act 1978. If the hon. Gentleman refers to the Act he will see that the vires are dealt with and confirmed there. I accept, of course, that the hon. Gentleman is not a spokesman for the tobacco industry. I never sought to contend that. I know the very wide interests of the hon. Member for Gloucestershire, South. The House must always listen with respect to those who have a constituency interest, as well as those, such as the hon. Member for Gloucestershire, South, who have a wider concern in all fiscal matters.

I know that the medical profession as a body is anxious to ensure that, as well as an effective drug being prescribed, it is prescribed in its cheapest form. This will free resources which can be devoted to medical care.

All the side effects, contra-indications and warnings have to be placed on a data sheet for the product concerned—that is, a fact sheet containing full information about the product, which must be supplied to practitioners before a product is advertised to them.

We agreed on all that with regard to major advertisements. Then the industry told us that it wanted to continue to publish some advertisements—referred to in the regulations as "abbreviated advertisements"—which would not have to carry all this mass of information. The Government agreed, with considerable reluctance that smaller, abbreviated advertisements with reduced information could be published following up a major advertisement of a drug.

Some reassurance was provided by the inclusion in the agreement of a provision that abbreviated advertisements would occupy somewhat less than a full page in an appropriate technical journal. It came as some surprise to us, having made that concession, that the industry then thought that an advertisement which covered only 99 per cent, of the appropriate page would be regarded as less than a full page. It was not prepared to wear that one at all. We have now agreed that the size of such advertisement should be not more than 420 square centimetres. We discussed this with editors of the technical journals. Our original proposal was for A5 size advertisements but they said that that would cause difficulties in some journals. So we increased the size to 420 square centimetres, which is regarded as acceptable.

Col. 36 Abbreviated advertisements must contain adequate information about the product and there is no reason to suppose that this information need appear out of proportion to the rest of the advertisement, or make it seem unbalanced.

I have always been prepared to consider larger follow-up advertisements if the information they contained was confined to no more than the name of the product and the name of the promoter. But the industry has not been prepared to accept this, and therefore we have settled on the 420 square centimetres solution. It is, however, still open to the industry to take the alternative.

Regulations require that the generic name of the product—that is, the nonproprietary name—should appear close to the brand name in a minimum type size of 10 point to ensure legibility. This was agreed because generic names are used in medical education and are also used, as a matter of policy, by leading medical journals in their articles. It is, therefore, desirable that prescribers should be aware of the generic name of the product, and we have assured that.

Another requirement is that the cost to the National Health Service should be shown in the advertisements.

Reverting to the question of the 420 square centimetre size, the hon. Member for Northampton, South asked how journals were to accommodate this. I am pretty confident that there is no problem posed for journals which cannot be solved either by good advertising copy-writing, by good editing, or by a good commercial approach. The British Medical Journal, for example accepts abbreviated advertisements. I have in fact seen one example in the Journal of an advertisement covering three-quarters of a page with a subsidiary advertisement relating to another product filling the rest of the page. This has apparently been accepted by the two companies concerned and is accepted by the editor as appropriate. I expect no insoluble problems with other journals.

We have reached agreement with the Association of the British Pharmaceutical Industry and the journals on these requirements. There are, therefore, no major disagreements between ourselves and the leaders of these two groups, Image Col. 37 although, as the hon. Member for Reading, South said, there may be odd minority groups within them who are not happy with what their leaders have done. We are all familiar with such situations.

Mr. Moyle: I am not saying that there have not been discussions. Clearly the natural reaction of an editor of a paper is that he must have total freedom to advertise just when he wishes, to whom he wishes and in the form he wishes.

I am happy to say, however, that broadly speaking the technical press, once we put our worries to it about the quality of advertising in its publications and the impact that it was making on the medical profession, accepted our move in the spirit in which we made it. There has obviously been give and take and discussion, and at one stage some of the worries that the hon. Gentleman described were expressed at meetings between representatives of the editors and officials in my Department. But all those technical details were solved at that level, and at no stage on that issue did the editors of the journals ask to see me.

There was a proposal in relation to the code of practice which my predecessor made some two or three years ago—the details of which escape me at the moment—which caused the editors of the journals some concern and they saw me about it. I know of no reason why the regulations should cause journals to close down. The process that I have described was based on an agreement. If journals felt that their future was in danger they would Col. 38 have been in grave doubt as to whether they should have gone along with them.

The hon. Member for Northampton, South raised a number of points. First, as regards companies which wish to promote a product audio-visually on a case history basis, this is quite in order. They must, though, supply data sheets to accompany such audio-visual presentations. Companies who want to promote a product should not be too worried by the regulations but should turn to. the appropriate provision of the ABPI code of practice. If they conform to that I can see very little danger of them being hauled before the courts.

I deal now with the question of what is a new drug. The hon. Gentleman mentioned the definition which we have in the regulations. In so far as it relates to the regulations, the definition would be a matter for interpretation by the courts. It would be pointless if I sought to give the courts any guidance. As the hon. Gentleman knows, courts are not cognisant of our proceedings here. We have merely reproduced a phrase in the ABPI code of practice. The ABPI will decide what is a new drug in terms of the code of practice.

Turing to the subject of multiple-page advertisements, all the regulations that we have introduced are designed to ensure that multi-page advertisements are read as one and not as two separate advertisements. We feel that there is a need for that. The hon. Member for Northampton, South said that the regulations would be a challenge to the ingenuity of advertisers. I always resist legislation in situations of this kind on exactly those grounds. If one can obtain a voluntary agreement, it is likely to be more flexible and effective in practice than legislation. As hon. Members on both sides of the Committee must know, regulations automatically mean loopholes, and loopholes can be got through. That is why I stress that it is important to bear in mind that the regulations are a long-stop, and that we have a wicket keeper in the form of the voluntary ABPI code of practice. I suspect that most of our problems will be solved there.

I think that that effectively disposes of contention.
