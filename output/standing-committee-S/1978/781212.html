DRAFT WATER CHARGES EQUALISATION ORDER 1978

10.30 a.m.

The Minister of State, Department of the Environment (Mr. Denis Howell)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft Water Charges Equalisation Order 1978.

Before I make a few opening remarks on the order, may I with your indulgence, Mr. Costain, refer to the fact that, as I think I am right in saying that the hon. Member for Daventry (Mr. Jones) has announced his intention not to stand again for Membership of the House, this may be the last occasion on which we shall have the pleasure of his company on these water matters. I wish to take this opportunity to say that he has been a very tough adversary but we on the Government side of the Committee, and of the House have always respected his fairness and firmness and his contribution to what has been a very difficult Session for water legislation. I am sure that the water industry is grateful, as the Government are, for the attitude that the hon. Member has always displayed, and we wish him well for the future.

As will be appreciated by the Committee, the legislation from which these matters are derived was subject to a certain amount of controversy when it was introduced, but I think that we can claim in this second year that we have had to make an order that it is working extremely well. It was, indeed, at the suggestion of the hon. Member for Daventry and his colleagues that the private water companies were brought within the ambit of the order. Like the regional water authorities, some gain quite considerably from the principles applied in the order while others have to contribute some of their revenue in order to bring that about. But the Government remain convinced of the equity and fairness of the order and these processes.

The main features of the scheme used to calculate the levies and payments remain almost exactly as they were last year. The financing charges—both interest and depreciation—element of expenditure in- 4 curred before 31st March 1976, which is the base date, in the provision of water supply are divided by the number of properties with an unmeasured supply in order to give us the unit cost. An average unit cost is thereby obtained, and an individual unit cost for each authority and company.

The regional water authorities and the water companies with a unit cost below the national average pay into the equalisation pool and those with a unit cost above the national average receive from the pool. For the purposes of making this calculation, 38 such regional water authorities and private companies gave information in response to the detailed questionnaire.

The consultation process for this order took the form of a small working group comprising representatives from my Department, the Welsh Office, the National Water Council and the Water Companies Association, so I can assure the Committee that all the interested parties have been involved in the calculations and I do not think that there is any cause for concern as to the factual basis of the calculations.

I come directly now to the effect of the order. On our present estimates, it will reduce the range in the cost of water supplied for mainly domestic consumers in unmeasured bills from about 43 per cent, above the average and 45 per cent, below the average—which is a tremendous range—to 33 per cent, above and 34 per cent, below.

The effect of the order is substantially to give assistance to Wales and the Southwest where consumers have traditionally faced much higher charges than in the rest of the country. It will reduce the average unmeasured bills about £3.27 for Wales and £2.21 for the South-West. In percentage terms, that means that the average bill for the South-West will be brought down from 43 per cent, above the national average to 33 per cent., while the same calculation for Wales will reduce bills from about 31 per cent, of the national average to 17 per cent, above the national average. The order will therefore carry out the wishes of the House and the Government on charging policy.

On the other side of the calculation, I should say something about the authorities making the biggest contribution. This year it is the Southern Water Authority area, where the average bill will rise by about 4 per cent, from £22.25 to £23.15. Even after that increase the average Southern bill will remain about 3 per cent. JPEG 5 below the national average. I think that is important.

Of the private company consumers those served by the Mid-Sussex Water Company will benefit most from the scheme. Their average unmeasured bills will be reduced by over £4 and will thus fall from 41 per cent, above the national average to about 24 per cent, above the national average. So the order has an important benefit for those consumers and justifies the Opposition's view that the private companies should be brought into the scheme.

In West Hampshire where the average bill is over 42 per cent, below the national average, very considerably below the national average, consumers will face the largest percentage increase. Average unmeasured bills will increase by 23 per cent,—from £13.86 to £17.07—but they will still remain 29 per cent, below the national average.

I conclude by saying that the provisions of the Water Charges Equalisation Act 1977 have been successfully implemented. The order will even out the spread of the financial legacy of the past historic charges, which is its main purpose, and will give assistance to those domestic consumers who cannot, by their own actions, influence the charge for their own water supply. The desired objective has been achieved. I hope that that introduction will assist the Committee in its deliberations.
