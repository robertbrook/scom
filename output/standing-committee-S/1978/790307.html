10.30 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs (Mr. John Tomlinson): I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft European Communities (Definition of Treaties) (ECSC Decision on Supplementary Revenues) Order 1979.

The order specifies as a Community treaty the decision taken at the Council of Ministers on 30 October 1978 to allocate supplementary revenues to the 1978 budget for the European Coal and Steel Community.

If, after consideration by this Committee, the House approves, the order will formally define the decision as a Community treaty under section 1 of the European Communities Act 1972. I believe the Committee is familiar with this procedure, which we have considered on a number of occasions in the past.

The purpose of the Council decision specified in this order is to provide the European Coal and Steel Community with the additional revenue needed to make good a deficiency in its 1978 budget as a result of increased expenditure arising from the steel recession.

As the Committee knows, the steel industry has been hit hard by a sharp fall in demand at a time when world steel production capacity has been greatly increased in anticipation of a high level of demand which failed to materialise. The steel industry has suffered accordingly.

In human terms, the main brunt has fallen on the steel workers themselves, a distressing number of whom have been made redundant throughout the Community. The deficiency in the 1978 budget has arisen largely as a result of Community policies designed to soften the impact of the crisis on these men. As the Committee may know, they qualify Col. 4 for resettlement aid. This takes the form of making up for a period the earnings of re-employed workers to the levels they received before they were made redundant, providing additional unemployment benefits and making grants towards retraining. The 1978 budget provides for an increase of 35 million units of account on expenditure in this area. The Committee will wish to know that Community reimbursement to the United Kingdom of aids for resettlement is expected to be £7·7 million in 1978 compared with £2·1 million in 1977.

The Community not only provides resettlement aid, but, as the Committee knows, has agreed policies for encouraging the creation of alternative employment for redundant workers. Interest rebates, generally of 3 per cent, over 5 years, are given on ECSC loans for projects providing employment for redundant coal and steel workers. A recent example is the interest rebate given on an ECSC loan of £78 million towards the cost of the Ford motor company's projected new engine plant in South Wales. The 1978 budget increased expenditure in this area by 7·7 million units of account.

In addition, interest rebates are available on ECSC loans for coal projects and aid for those steel projects which are considered to be of particular value in restructuring the industry. The Community's objective here is to bring the steel industry's capacity into line with the level of demand forecast in the 1980s and to improve its efficiency so that it can meet world competition. An increase of 12–5 million units of account is included in the 1978 budget under this heading.

The total of expenditure provided for in this way in the ECSC operation budget amounts to 152 million units of account for 1978. That is some 38 million units of account more than for 1977. That figure takes account of the reduction from 18 million units of account to 5 million units of account in the ECSC's contribution to the general EEC budget towards the administrative expenses of the Commission.

Given these plans for increased expenditure, the Community was faced with the problem of deciding how to raise the necessary funds. In view of the difficulties currently facing the steel industry, the Community decided against raising above Image Col. 5 its existing level of 0·29 per cent, the production levy on the coal and steel industries by which Community expenditure is financed under the terms of articles 49 and 50 of the ECSC treaty. The Council agreed instead, in December 1977, to make up the anticipated 1978 deficit by means of exceptional contributions from member States totalling 32 million units of account—a figure which was subsequently reduced to 28 million units of account. In the event, the sum raised by the production levy increased as a result of a slight improvement in production levels in 1978. The Council subsequently agreed on the contributions that individual member States should make. The United Kingdom will contribute 17·4 per cent, compared with 21·3 per cent, contributed by France and 33·1 per cent, by Germany.

I accordingly seek approval for the necessary Order in Council.

Mr. Nigel Spearing: I had expected the Opposition spokesman to say a few words first as we are concerned with expenditure.

By way of introduction, I suggest that this may be the first time that this procedure, which is well known to members of the Committee, has been used to vote money. I do not know whether that is true, though I suspect that it is. Perhaps my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary of State will confirm that point. Even if this is not the first occasion, it is a mysterious and important one. The procedures surrounding the definition of treaty orders have been controversial in the past, and I suggest that this is a controversial use of a controversial procedure.

The order states:

"Community Treaty as defined in section 1(2) of the European Communities Act 1972."

The explanatory memorandum, however, refers to section 1(3), which my memory tells me is correct. Perhaps the Minister will confirm that is so. I apologise, Mr. Wells, that I have not brought the European Communities Act with me, but I did not think that would be necessary. However, it would appear that there is either a mistake or a conflict on the official documents.

It is well known that this procedure uses a domestic statutory instrument to Col. 6 define any Community treaty which is in draft and which has been sent to the House for ratification as a Community treaty. If the statutory instrument, which is an affirmative instrument, is approved, we are automatically tied to the terms of that treaty, whatever it may be. By definition, the material in that treaty is additional to the foundation treaties of the Community—namely, the Treaty of Rome, the ECSC treaty or the Euratom treaty—or it is using or taking powers not contained in those treaties. That is an important constitutional matter. The Treaty of Rome, in relation to the powers of the Assembly, was amended by this very procedure in 1975, and the House was not aware of what was taking place.

I turn now to the detailed wording of the order. I have drawn attention to what may be an anomaly or an incorrect part in article 2. The face of the order does not tell us anything about v/hat it is doing. For that we have to turn to the schedule:

"Decision of the Representatives of the Governments of the Member States of the European Coal and Steel Community, meeting within the Council, of 30 October 1978, allocating to that Community supplementary revenues for 1978 (Cmnd.)."

There is an awful lot in that and I wish to draw attention to at least three aspects. First, this is apparently a decision of representatives of member States. My hon. Friend mentioned a decision of 22 December, but the schedule refers to a meeting of representatives on 30 October 1978. It may be that what I understand is called COREPER met on 30 October. But, if it did, clearly that was before 22 December.

Are we to understand that civil servants, important though they may be, are permitted to take an executive act on behalf of the Council of Ministers at the EEC? I can draw no other conclusion from the wording of the schedule. Perhaps my hon. Friend will say whether I am correct. If so, I suggest that the House should be informed of that fact as it is a matter of some constitutional significance.

Secondly, "supplementary revenues" can cover a multitude of possibilities. It means that some moneys are to be voted to the ECSC over and above its existing authorisation to collect revenue. I guess that the ECSC's existing powers over Image Col. 7 money are in the ECSC treaty, articles 49 and 50. Cmnd 4863 is a reprint of this treaty. The high authority is empowered to procure the funds that it requires to carry out its tasks by imposing levies on the production of coal and steel and by contracting loans and receiving gifts. I presume that article 49 is the only authority in existence so far for the ECSC to get its money. We are now being asked to provide more.

I also presume that while, according to the memorandum, the amount is only £3·3 million—I say "only £3·3 million"; it is a little piece of paper which is a £3·3 million note—it could have been £30 million or £300 million. The procedure which we are debating this morning could have mentioned any sum. Indeed, the sum does not appear on the order at all.

That brings me to the decision, which is in an unnamed, unprinted Command Paper. It has subsequently been published—Treaty Series No. 22 (1979) Cmnd. 7464. This is the decision which my hon. Friend mentioned in passing. Article 1 provides:

"The Member States shall pay."

In other words, it is thoroughly binding. Article 2 sets out the contributions to be made by member States. For the United Kingdom, it is 4,877,513 EUAs. Article 3 states:

"The sums to be paid … shall be payable on 1 December 1978.


The payments may be made either in EUA or in national currencies at the EUA exchange rate on the day of payment."

Presumably, that is across the exchanges and will be debited to our balance of payments.

Article 4 states:

"The Member States shall, in close co-operation with the Commission, take all necessary measures to ensure the application of the aforegoing provisions."

Article 5 states:

"The Member States shall take the provisions necessary to implement this Decision.


Done at Luxembourg, 30 October 1978. J. Ertl, The President."

If that was done at Luxembourg on 30 October 1978, why was not the Command paper printed at the time of printing of the statutory instrument, which could not have been before 1 January 1979? I submit that any ordinary Col. 8 citizen—for example, in Sheffield or in any place where people are particularly interested in steel—would not have a hope in hell of getting hold of the information available in this decision.

The decision is not the only information that Members of Parliament have. We are privileged to have access to what is called an explanatory memorandum for the Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments, which is us. Here it is—printed by the Foreign and Commonwealth Office on 3 January 1979. This document goes a great deal further than what purports to be the explanatory note, which is the last item on the order with which we are dealing.

I will read the explanatory note, because it is worth putting on record. It starts:

"This Note is not part of the Order."

Then it states:

"This Order declares the Decision of the Representatives of the Governments of the Member States of the European Coal and Steel Community, meeting within the Council, of 30 October 1978, allocating to that Community supplementary revenues for 1978 (Cmnd.) to be a Community Treaty as denned in section 1(2) of the European Communities Act 1972."

Any citizen interested in the EEC or steel affairs reading that note would really be none the wiser. I do not think that even my hon. Friend could suggest that people would be any the wiser from reading that note.

My hon. Friend told us something about the money. But what it is for? The memorandum which Members of Parliament were given—if they asked for it —tells us a little about that aspect. It may be that other hon. Members will catch your eye, Mr. Wells, and talk about the application of the money. Incidentally, the memorandum states that the money will be paid by Parliament out of the Consolidated Fund.

Although it gives something of the background, this cyclostyled explanatory memorandum does not tell us how the money is to be expended. It was not until my hon. Friend mentioned retraining and—rather curiously—something about the Ford works that we heard where some of the £3·3 million was to be spent.

We have had trouble over the content of explanatory notes. We have had Image Col. 9 debates about this matter both in Committee and on the Floor of the House, and my hon. Friend or his right hon. Friends conceded a small point. They decided that they would at least state on these mysterious " Definition of Treaties" orders the subject of the orders. Therefore—and I am grateful to them—we see in brackets:

"ECSC Decisions on Supplementary Revenues".

The Government have gone that far but they have not gone very far on the explanatory note. Surely they could have stated that this was an additional treaty, arrived at by the Council of Ministers or their representatives, placing an additional charge upon the United Kingdom cf £3·3 million to cover excess expenditure, not authorised under the treaty, by the European Coal and Steel Community last year for retraining, grants or whatever it may be? I suggest that 10 lines might have been reasonable.

I should like to ask my hon. Friend why that was not done, especially as the Government boast of open government. This has happened before. We have had debates about the unsatisfactory nature of explanatory notes. Indeed, in order to cover the deficiency, my hon. Friend has in the past said that an extra explanatory memorandum would be given to the Committee. When we ask why the information is not put in the explanatory note, we are told "There are difficulties because certain treaties can have direct legislative effect. We do not always know which parts of a treaty will have direct legislative effect. Therefore, in view of the understood convention that the explanatory note does not attempt to define the law, we cannot give such explanations in it."

Is my hon. Friend really pretending that the parts of the explanatory note that I have suggested ought to be there in any way come under that heading? Although it may not be directly applicable in court, as it is confined only to Parliament, I should have thought that the whole of this treaty was directly applicable in terms of paying money. It is not optional. Therefore, the explanation that the explanatory note is venturing on to interpretation just will not do.

We have only an hour and a half for this discussion—another lacuna in proce- Col. 10 dure about which the Select Committee on Procedure has some recommendations. Therefore, I shall not pursue these matters further. But it is clear to hon. Members that the procedure under the Act is curious and deficient. The procedure for dealing with a domestic statutory instrument is also defective, because those of us entitled to a vote here will not make any difference. The matter will be taken on the Floor of the House, perhaps late at night, for a decision forthwith. That is an unsatisfactory procedure, upon which the Select Committee on Procedure has commented.

The statutory instrument may be defective in view of the points that I have raised. It is extremely curious that the representatives of the Government, rather than the Council of Ministers, have made the treaty. The dates between October and December are mysterious. The Command paper, which is the real nub of the decision, is not in the statutory instrument. No numbers were printed, although I should have thought there was plenty of time between October and January. Finally, the explanatory note is not an explanatory note.

The hon. Member for Newham, South was right to raise these points about the order. It is a peculiar order. I do not think that hitherto we have seen one merely voting money and calling an agreement to vote money a treaty. But I fear that is what we have let ourselves in for with this procedure of draft orders defining treaties under the European Communities Act 1972.

I do not understand why the ECSC cannot be content with its own approved resources— the 0·29 per cent, plus on items from elsewhere—and confine itself to expenditure within those proper resources instead of suddenly demanding a special payment of an extra 32 million EUA—approximately £20 million—not provided for in the European Coal and Steel Community treaty, which involves us in stumping up £3£ million.

I want to know what will face us in 1979. The explanatory memorandum is very cagey about this matter. It says:

What sort of sums will it come up with next time? Will it say that it will want an extra 100 million EUA from the Image Col. 17 member States over and above the regular income of the Community? I hope that this debate will be taken very seriously into account by the authorities of the House and by the Government, so that we can examine more closely how the budgeting, expenditure and income of this body can be more closely scrutinised and effectively controlled.
