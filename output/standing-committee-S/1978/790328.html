THE UNDER-SECRETARY OF STATE FOR HEALTH AND SOCIAL SERVICES (Mr. Eric Deakins)
: May I respond to the comments of the honourable Member for Wallasey (Mrs. Chalker) on the FIS regulations, S.I. No. 160?

The purpose of the regulations, as the honourable Lady pointed out, is simply to reduce from 30 to 24 the number of hours that lone parents have to work in order to be eligible for FIS. That was a recommendation of the Finer Committee. It has been strongly championed by the National Council for One-Parent Families.

We have been committed to the change for some time. As the honourable Lady recognised, it was announced some months ago. However, our pledge has always been that it could be introduced only when resources permitted. The importance that we attach to the change—which will cost, as the honourable Lady said, about £1.5 million—is shown by the fact that we have given it priority at a time when, as I need hardly remind the Committee, opportunities for additional public expenditure are few and far between.

As the honourable Lady pointed out, we estimate that the reduction in hours will bring up to another 10,000 lone parents into FIS. The estimate cannot be too precise, because it is based on a small sample of working lone parents in the family expenditure survey. The figures on which we are working in the FES are for 1976. It takes months, if not years, for all the figures to be statistically analysed. I hope that, later this year, we shall have the 1977 figures, and we can then perhaps update the figures.

The figure of 10,000 does not, however, take into account lone parents who may reduce their working hours to take advantage of the new rule. Unfortunately, it is impossible to say how many lone parents may do that. There are so many factors to take into account.

We do not think that the effect of the regulations in the current year will be to vary the load very greatly. Most of the 12 beneficiaries are already on the benefit, which is awarded for 12 months. We have no estimates yet of the load for 1979–80, because that will depend largely on the 1979 uprating. That will be the big factor.

Concerning the manipulation—I think that that is the kindest word that one can use—of FIS, whereby for the five weeks, or, if one is on a monthly basis, the two months before one gets on to FIS, one has to produce one's income slips and so on and may try to reduce one's income, we have no evidence that deliberate depressions of earnings are a major problem. There is inevitable difficulty with a 52-week award, but we still feel that it ought to remain a 52-week award because of the poverty trap effect, to which I shall turn in a moment.

We do not keep any record of people whose claims are turned down. We do not think it would be worth starting to do so, because we are trying to keep the administration of the scheme as simple as possible. I am told—I find this surprising— that our administration costs are only 4p per £1 of benefit paid, which is very low indeed.

I echo the honourable Lady's plea that we should not underestimate the importance of FIS for lone parents in low-paid jobs. One-parent families account for nearly half the families on FIS. Our present estimate is about 40,000 out of 90,000. The scheme favours one-parent families in two important respects. First, the child benefit increase—the lone parent's premium—is completely ignored when entitlement is assessed. Secondly, and even more significant, a lone parent on FIS gets exactly the same amount of benefit as a couple with the same income and number of children.

The average FIS payment is about £5 a week. Clearly, beneficiaries on very low incomes can obtain considerably more than that. For example, a lone parent with three children and a gross income of £29 a week—not a very high wage—will get £12.50 FIS, to which is added £12 child benefit and the £2 child benefit premium. That is a tax-free total of £26.50 a week on top of earnings, as well as a possible entitlement to housing benefits JPEG 13 and automatic entitlement to other benefits, such as free school meals and free National Health Service dental treatment.

That reduction in the hours of work rule will be a major concession, and a recognition of the special problem facing working lone parents.

: No, Sir Thomas. I respect your point. My honourable Friend has been very good, but I shall not follow him any further.

On the problem of working lone-parent families, we are conscious of the fact that lone parents may have to curtail their working hours to make satisfactory arrangements for the care of their children—a matter that the honourable 14 Lady emphasised. The problem often arises during after-school hours. That is important. In the uprating debate, to which I have already referred, the honourable Member for Wallasey told us about the growing problems in our cities, towns and rural areas of children not receiving all the parental attention they desperately needed because their parents were forced by the system to be at work for longer hours than were beneficial to them. It is because we share her concern that we want to improve the position for a particularly vulnerable group—the low-income one-parent families on FIS.

I echo the honourable Lady's plea to employers to take account of the fact that some of their workers could now be eligible for FIS, particularly lone parents, and to see what they can do to adjust hours of work to suit the convenience of working lone parents—mothers or fathers.

The proposal to reduce the hours of work rule has been favourably commented upon, as one would expect, by those concerned with lone parents. My officials have invited the major interest groups and advisory bodies, such as the National Council for One-Parent Families, Gingerbread, the CPAG, and the National Association of Citizens' Advice Bureaux, to help us publicise the change. Not surprisingly, they have responded helpfully and with enthusiasm. I am sure that that will be valuable.

One or two of the bodies have expressed interest in the advice to be given to lone parents who appear to be entitled to both FIS and supplementary benefit. This is a somewhat complicated issue but, briefly, the possible overlap occurs because the Supplementary Benefits Commission may well regard work of between 24 and 30 hours as part-time work. Which benefit will be more appropriate for a particular lone parent would obviously depend on his or her individual circumstances.

It is not possible to give general advice, but lone parents who want individual advice will be able to get it from their local social security offices.

An important point which I must stress is that an award of FIS will not rule out a JPEG 15 subsequent award of supplementary benefit or vice versa. We shall take steps to ensure that lone parents concerned know of their possible entitlement to both benefits.

I turn briefly to the question raised by the honourable Lady about the poverty trap. She will recognise that this stems not so much from poverty but from the means used to relieve poverty, that is, the means-tested benefits available to poor working families. We made it clear that if we could, we would do away with these benefits overnight. They are complex, costly to administer and, because they are not taken up in full, not completely effective. Therefore, as a means of increasing the incomes of low-income families, they are very much a second choice. But clearly we must live with means-tested benefits for some time to come. The resources needed to replace them by universal benefits, as we are doing with child benefit, are simply not available. In the long term, our aim is to reduce dependence on means-tested benefits. Meanwhile, they are giving essential protection to poorer families.

There is a preoccupation with the poverty trap and its theoretical effects. I stress the word "theoretical". In practice, the more serious effects of the trap are seldom felt because of the time lag between the receipt of a pay increase and the review of the benefit award, and because benefits are uprated regularly. With FIS, for example, an award continues for 52 weeks, regardless of any subsequent change in income—usually a rise—after the date of the claim and, since FIS is uprated every 12 months, at some time during the currency of an award of FIS the prescribed amounts will have been uprated: thus higher earnings are set against higher prescribed amounts when the claim is renewed.

Despite the emphasis placed in some quarters on the possibility that families might be worse off as a result of a pay rise, the estimates show that a comparatively small number—considerably fewer than 5,000—are theoretically in this position. Impeccable though the theory may be, the reality is that the Department has never yet seen an authenticated case where 16 a pay rise has led to a net loss of income. We are, of course, talking about 100 per cent, marginal rates.

I know that any extension of a means tested benefit may appear to be at odds with our policy of reducing dependence 18 upon such benefits. We have never liked FIS, but we have to be realistic.
