[Mr. Arthur Jones in the Chair]

NEW TOWNS (LIMIT ON BORROWING) ORDER 1979

10.30 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Mr. Guy Barnett)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the New Towns (Limit on Borrowing) Order 1979.

 As I look round at the composition of the Committee, I realise that it is perhaps unnecessary to remind the Committee that it is necessary to come to the House every so often to increase the limit on the amount of money which can be lent to new towns. The last increase was in the New Towns Act 1977. That provided £500 million extra immediately and a further £500 million as and when approved by the House. We expected to start drawing on that authorisation in the autumn of 1977 and thought that both tranches would be used up in a little over two years. That is to say, we would have expected coming back with a completely new Bill next autumn.

In the event, the new towns have been requiring public investment funds more slowly than we expected in 1977. Instead of the £40 million a month we predicted, the actual rate has been nearer £30 million. The first £500 million authorised under the 1977 Act is now only expected to be fully drawn next month, and the second £500 million, which the order now before us would approve, should last until late summer 1980. We will therefore need to introduce a new money Bill in either the first or the second session of the next Parliament.

Looking to the future, we expect expenditure by new towns to remain at about present levels for the next two or three years and then gradually to fall away as towns complete their programmes. New towns need these resources, of course, for constructing roads and sewers and building houses, factories, shops, offices and so on. In the last financial year, 61 per cent, went on housing, 15 per cent, on roads and 23 per cent, on industrial and commercial development.

4 There have been two main reasons, I think, for the slowing down in investment by the new towns. First, new towns, like the rest of the public sector, have been affected by general Government restraint on public expenditure. Secondly—in this case more significantly—there has recently been a considerable increase in private investment in new towns. This applies both to industrial and commercial development, where the financial institutions have been playing an increasing role, and to housing, where substantial numbers of private houses have recently been completed on land made available for development corporations. In the year to November 1978 in the English new towns, 3,763 starts and 2,820 completions were made by private enterprise as against 5,203 starts and 5,985 completions by the new towns themselves.

With the programmes of the third generation towns building up to a peak and the other development corporations hard at work putting the finishing touches to their programmes before they are dissolved, the demand for resources for new town industrial and commercial development has now reached a high level. As I am sure hon. Members are well aware, resources are now under particular pressure. We have not been able to provide the increased resources necessary to keep investment at the level which new towns would like. To help fill the gap, we have been encouraging greater private participation in the funding of industrial and commercial development. We now expect that more than half this programme will be privately financed in 1979–80 compared with about 22 per cent, for the current year. To augment the forward funding arrangements by which financial institutions have in the past participated, we approved last year a limited amount of "rollover" of completed industrial and commercial buildings.

Rollover will be permitted in those cases where the funds raised can with advantage be used for the continuing development of the town. For 1979–80 we think that towns might be able to raise as much as £65 million by forward funding and rollover.

There are, however, limitations on the use of private finance in new towns. For one thing, by no means all of it is in the industrial and commercial programme. It also includes landscaping, amenities, vehicles, workshops and so on, which are suitable for private funding. Also, while JPEG 5 private investment in our new towns is undoubtedly a sign of confidence, the Committee will recognise that it will have the effect of reducing the rental income of the corporations and this in time could affect their ability to repay the loans made to them for non-housing development. An adequate level of income is essential to the viability of the towns and this clearly sets a limit on the amount of private finance new towns can accept.

Whatever the role of private funding in new towns, however, we are still talking about very considerable sums of Government money in the present order. I know that the Committee will want to be assured that this money is being well spent and that new towns offer a sound investment. If we look first at new towns strictly in terms of financial success, there can in my view be no doubt that corporations and the Commission have managed very well, often in increasingly difficult circumstances. An investment programme of the size with which we are concerned cannot expect to produce its financial returns in the short term.

In 1977–78 the Commission made a surplus of £2 million, and four of the first generation towns made surpluses ranging from £200,000 to £900,000. At the end of the day new towns will have paid all the interest due and redeemed all their capital borrowing, and we shall still have a considerable accumulation of capital assets to generate further income. That in itself is no mean achievement, but we must also bear in mind the context in which that achievement has been made. New towns have, for instance, always borne many costs not normally incurred by other developers. They make substantial contributions to the costs of items such as roads and sewerage incurred by local and public authorities. Unlike local authorities, new towns receive no grants other than housing subsidies. Virtually all new town expenditure is financed by long term borrowing at current market rates.

Turning to the scale of development, there is now a total popultion of about 2 million. Over 400,000 new dwellings. 700 new schools, over 17 million square metres of factory space and 1 million square metres of office space have been constructed. Total employment in new towns is now over 1 million, and new town development corporations have been conspicuously successful in the attraction of new firms to their towns, many of them 6 from overseas. Corporations have made major contributions to modern urban planning and architecture and have created an environment which combines functional efficiency with visual attractiveness. In short, our new towns have won an international reputation for their social, economic and aesthetic achievements.

Demands and priorities, however, inevitably change with time, and the new towns programme has proved flexible enough to adapt to new situations. At the end of the last war when the Government's new town programme was just getting off the ground, the country's population was growing rapidly and the major conurbations were in many cases grossly overcrowded. Now, as I am sure hon. Members are well aware, many regions expect a drop in population and the conurbations are facing real difficulties in maintaining the vitality needed to redevelop their existing fabric. To meet this situation, our 1977 new town review substantially reduced the long term population targets of the six most recently designated towns. We also announced at that time that 11 of the development corporations of the earlier towns would be dissolved in the early 1980s.

It must not be assumed from these changes that the new towns, like Lewis Carroll's boojum hunters, will softly and suddenly vanish away and never be met with again. On the contrary, new towns have a continuing and valuable role to play for a number of years to come in providing an environment in which people and industry can thrive. There is still a great deal of work necessary to complete the towns currently under development.

In the case of the first and second generation towns, there is still plenty of rounding off to be done before the corporations are dissolved and assets transferred. The Commission for the New Towns has a major task ahead of it in deciding how it will manage and develop the large industrial and commercial estates which it will soon be inheriting. In addition towns such as Central Lancashire, Warrington and Northampton are becoming increasingly involved in the essential work of urban renewal and are completing the circle by moving from their traditional task of • rehousing people and industry on new sites to that of bringing back into acceptable conditions areas from which this movement started in the first place.

I am sure that hon. Members will agree with me that the new town development JPEG 7 corporations must be allowed to continue to press ahead with their development programmes. Even with the recent growth of private investment in new towns, public investment is indispensable. In my view, money made available by Parliament to the towns still represents an excellent social and financial investment for the nation I therefore commend the order to the Committee.

Mr. Michael Morris
: One of the benefits of this kind of Committee and the need to renew an order is that it enables the Government—in this instance the Under-Secretary of State for the Environment— to give a broad review of the position by new towns. This is one area on which, in terms of broad strategy, there is all-party agreement, although there are differences of emphasis. A review of policy over the last five years shows that at least the new towns are dynamic organisations and are meeting a changing scene.

I should like to look at some of the implications of what the Under-Secretary said and question him on his introductory remarks and matters arising from the Government's White Paper on expenditure plans. This order is a small piece of paper, but it has significant implications for existing new towns. Before we grant money on this scale, I feel that we should press the Government for clarification on some points.

First, as the Under-Secretary will recall, we used to increase the amount of money in tranches of £250 million. We then changed the policy—presumably in the light of inflation—and increased the amount to £500 million.

With the slowing down of the rate of growth of existing new towns, the fact that the majority of new towns are now coming to fruition, and even the third generation new towns have revised targets, and in the light of the increased inflow of private capital, the question arises whether Parliament should revert to tranches of £250 million to give us an opportunity to appraise the situation on a more regular basis.

Secondly, we ought to look at what is portrayed in the Government's White Paper on expenditure. There are two points that I should like to raise concerning Command 7439. On page 97 there is a reference to the number of permanent new 8 dwellings approved. New Towns, local authorities and housing associations are listed.

It is interesting to note that there has been a steady fall-off in the approval of dwellings in new towns since 1975. In 1976, 14,300 dwellings were approved; in 1977, 9,700 were approved; and, if I heard the Under-Secretary correctly, just over 5,200 were approved for 1978. This fall-off in the provision of public rented accommodation by new towns is similar to the fall-off in the provision of public rented accommodation by local authorities and underlines the unfairness of the charge that has been made on the Floor of the House that, for political reasons, some local authorities are cutting back on public sector housing. The Government have direct control of the rate of output, and we see a fall-off there.

It would be helpful if the Under-Secretary could give a forecast of the target number of approved dwellings for the new towns over the next three years. If he cannot do so this morning, perhaps he could let members of the Committee know in due course.

Thirdly, I turn to table 2.7 on pages 90 and 91, which again refers to housing, and in particular to the passages on capital expenditure. The reference is:

"Subsidies to new Towns and Scottish Special Housing Association".

 We see that expenditure in real terms has fallen between 1976–77 and 1978–79. We should expect that fall because of the review. What I find surprising is that an increase is forecast for 1979–80, and I wonder why.

We note that we must go back to 1973–74 for the peak turnover of assets of £75 million. Since that time, under the present Government, the figure has been running at between £13 million and £18 million per annum. Unfortunately, the forecast figures are no longer separated for ensuing years. However, I see that total sales, which clearly include local authorities sales, are not expected to change. In the light of the Under-Secretary's statement that about £65 million was forecast to roll over in 1979–80, I wonder whether those figures are included in the White Paper. It seems, from the evidence before me, that they probably are not. I should be grateful for clarification on that matter.

I turn now to one or two aspects mentioned by the Under-Secretary. Housing takes the greatest volume of money. The JPEG 9 Minister knows that we have had discussions across the Chamber and in this Committee on an earlier occasion about the ratio of owner-occupied to rented properties. There is a difference of opinion between us, and it is distressing. I believe that the Minister is beginning to accept that informed opinion on housing in general— expressed, for instance, in Shelter's periodical Roof, in Housing Review, or in any of the other publications by people who take a genuine and non-party interest—is becoming worried that the new towns have an unduly large proportion of rented accommodation and a small proportion of owner-occupied accommodation.

It is seen at its worst in Stevenage where the latest figures show that under 20 per cent, of accommodation is owner-occupied. Even in the expansion area of Northampton, only 19 per cent, of new dwellings are for owner occupation. We expect and hope that the Government will back up their present policy of a 50–50 ratio. What are they doing to ensure that the third generation new towns meet that ratio, because they have a long way to go?

Mr. Michael Ward
: I note what the hon. Gentleman said about private development and the need for a balance in the new towns. Indeed I agree with much of what he said. But is he aware that in Peterborough—I am sure this is also true of Northampton—despite enormous efforts by the development corporations to make sites available and attract private developers, the private sector has consistently failed to take up the opportunities offered? The hon. Gentleman should not expect the results to come from my hon. Friend. More attention should be directed to persuading firms in the private sector to go where the opportunities are provided.

Mr. Morris
: It is difficult for me to comment on Peterborough. Certainly in Northampton—about which I can speak with more authority—there was a decline in private sector house-building. That was happening nationally as well as locally. However, it was not helped by the policy of the development corporation, supported by the Treasury, to sell land at a price way above the then ruling market price. That undoubtedly constrained private development during that crucial time. It is true that there is now some pick-up, but the pick-up is slower than in the market as a whole because of the pricing policy on 10 land.

I think that the time has come for the Government once again to think about the opportunity of giving people the automatic right to buy their properties when the new town assets are handed over to the local authorities. That, of course, applies not to the Peterborough development corporation or the Northampton development corporation, but to the earlier ones.

The Government should remember that these properties were built not to help local needs, but primarily to help London's needs and other conurbations elsewhere in the country. There is no question of interfering with local authority demands, rights or needs. If 75 per cent, of people want to own their own homes, the point of transfer of assets is an ideal time at which to offer that possibility to sitting tenants. The Government do a grave disservice to the future of the first and second generation new towns by refusing to give those people the absolute opportunity to buy their own homes.

Finally, as the Minister knows, there is grave disquiet, in some of the earlier new towns about the condition of the properties on transfer and the financial burdens on those local authorities which are taking them over. That is not true in all cases, but it is true in some. It is particularly true of Corby in Northamptonshire, and I have read that it is a matter of concern to a number of other local authorities. Has the Minister any proposals for a review of the terms of transfer?

Turning now to commercial assets, the Minister's remarks this morning were fairly illuminating. I believe that this is the first time that there has been a public statement that a substantial amount of private money has been brought in to assist the shortage of available money. That is greatly to be welcomed. We have been pressing the Government in that regard for many years. It is a shame that it has taken so long to happen.

I thought that I sensed a certain reluctance on the Minister's part to take that matter too far. But there is an understanding on both sides of the House that there is a shortage of public money available for certain key needs. If there were an extra £100 million—all the evidence is that that money is there—and I had to decide whether it should go to the National Health Service or to new towns, I should have to say that if the money were available in the public sector to finance the rollover of JPEG 11 assets we should be totally irresponsible not to redeploy those resources against the National Health Service. The Conservative Party has made it absolutely clear that commercial assets should be rolled over. I hope that those concerned have done the necessary preparatory work so that it can be implemented at an early stage after the change of Government.

So much for one or two of the specific matters. But, as the money is for the future, one or two further points arise. The first concerns the inner city partnership and the concept of what some of us colloquially call the "old town corporation". When I look at the borough where I used to work—the inner city partnership area of Islington and Hackney—and the development of dockland, I question why we do not use the concept of the new town machinery to better effect to solve the problems there. As we know, that machinery has worked. I wonder whether we shall ever tackle that problem.

Secondly, is there any budgetary provision for supporting or promoting the export of the technique of the new towns? We have a unique pool of talent available to assist the Third World which, if skilfully deployed, could be of enormous benefit to British industry in terms of the follow through for British contractors and others. Is there any provision in the Budget fox such an extension?

Command 7439 is now totally out of date. The Government recognise that the options are difficult. They must either increase taxation, which will mean a switch from direct to indirect taxation, or make a substantial cut in public expenditure.

Having worked in the new town movement, I am concerned that the real benefits should not be lost. There has been a review and a slowing down. The Government could secure the future of the new towns if, instead of drifting—as they have for some time—they made positive moves, such as those I have suggested: the automatic right of sale on transfer to enable people to own their properties; the turning over, to a far greater degree, of commercial assets; and the use of the available expertise on the inner city problem as an invisible export.

Mr. Stanley Newens
: I support the order and to pay tribute to my hon. Friend the 12 Under-Secretary for the attention that he has paid to the new town movement since he took on his present responsibilities.

The new towns have been a successful venture, and public investment in a successful venture is always desirable. My hon. Friend mentioned the number of houses, schools, factories, offices and jobs created in the new towns. In many respects success has been achieved in creating growth points from which new industries and all kinds of new activities can be launched. It would be dangerous to move away from the concept of the new towns.

The success achieved in many of the new towns has not been characteristic of industry in general If it had been, this country would have had a far more successful economic record over the past decade or two than has been achieved. Therefore, we must strongly support the order to provide a new sum for investment in the new towns.

We must recognise that money available for new town investment is not unlimited. I welcome the fact that private funds are available. The fact that investors are prepared to put their own money into new towns is a testimony to their success. None the less, it must be recognised that much of the development that is necessary, particularly in the younger new towns, cannot possibly be carried out by private investment alone. As much of the development is infrastructure, we must strongly support the case for further public investment in new towns. Therefore, I welcome the decision that we are to take this morning I hope that, in welcoming it, we can make it clear that we do not believe that it will be possible in future, despite recent trends, to taper off the level of public investment in new towns beyond a certain level.

There are many aspects of new town development which it will not be possible to carry out if we rely on private investment. In those circumstances, I believe that, in the foreseeable future, we must be prepared to make available large sums of public money for new town development.

As my hon. Friend said, even in the older new towns, there is much rounding off to be done. Therefore, any cutting off of funds would be a severe blow. But, in the younger new towns, the cutting off of money would be even more devastating. Indeed, much of the investment which has already been carried out would be lost if further public money were not forthcoming. I represent one of the older new JPEG 13 towns. Therefore, I am not putting forward a narrow constituency view in making that point.

In the circumstances, it would not be desirable, as the hon. Member for Northampton, South (Mr. Morris) said, to cut the size of the tranche when these orders come before us. We should recognise that inflation has taken its toll. Therefore, it is desirable, that, when we make these orders, we see that sufficient money is made available for people to take a long term view of new town development.

I am glad that the hon. Member for Northampton, South noted the principle of making available public funds. In that sense, there is no basic division between the parties.

The hon. Member for Northampton, South raised the issue of private housing. I believe that it would be unfortunate if we did not recognise that part of the great success of new towns has been derived, from the provision of houses to rent. If houses had not been available to rent, many of the people living in the older towns would not have gone to the new towns. We must recognise that by and large, people living in London and older towns left their original homes not because they wanted new jobs, but because they were unable to find suitable housing accommodation where they lived. Such accommodation could be obtained only in the new towns. Without the provision of houses to rent in the first place, those new towns could not have got off the ground. The provision of large numbers of houses to rent was vital to their success.

Mr. Michael Morris
: I was not really arguing that basic point. I was making two points. The first was that the majority of people now want to own their own homes. We know that from many sources. I am saying that the Government should provide them.

Secondly, in the third generation of new towns we now have too much rented accommodation. There are nearly 600 empty properties in Northampton for rent and there are excessive numbers in Milton Keynes. I believe there are substantial numbers in Peterborough, too. The whole position has become askew. That was the point that I was making.

Mr. Newens
: I am not convinced that is so. I should not object to providing houses for sale. I bought and live in a house in Harlow. The Harlow development cor- 14 poration, as part of its policy from an early stage, built certain houses expressly for sale to members of the public who wished to buy them. I strongly defend that policy.

The hon. Gentleman said that many people wish to buy their own houses, and their requirements should be met. I have not found anyone—either coming to my advice bureau or writing to me—who has not been able to find a house for sale. In fact, according to the estate agent and the newspapers, there are plenty of houses for sale.

I find—and I am sure this is the experience of the majority of hon. Members who represent new towns—that I get a continual stream of letters from and visits to my advice bureau by people who say " I cannot afford to buy. I need an accommodation. I want a place to rent." Those waiting lists must be dealt with first. We must provide housing for people who cannot afford to buy.

We have an express duty to tackle the housing problem. We cannot fail to recognise that the real need in the community today is the provision of accommodation to rent. Until that state of affairs is reversed, I am not prepared to agree that the situation is askew, as was suggested by the hon. Member for Northampton, South.

The hon. Gentleman also commented on the sale of commercial assets. We must recognise that public property is bought with public money. It would be wrong to sell off assets without recognising our responsibility to the public, whose funds have been invested.

I cannot see why, as a matter of principle, the public should dispose of profitable assets. There may be arguments for the sale of particular assets. I am not laying down the principle that we should never sell off assets. But it is clear that unprofitable assets are not saleable. Why should we sell off profitable assets and leave new town corporations or their successors with unprofitable assets? If that were the case, hon. Members who take the same view as the hon. Member for Northampton, South would claim that public enterprises never make profits. The Harlow development corporation has been an extremely successful public enterprise. I do not think that its success, even in commercial terms, has been fully recognised. It would be wrong to wind up the development corporations and to sell all the profitable assets, leaving only the JPEG 15 unprofitable assets to their successors.

Despite some disagreements on these issues, which I feel I have a right to air, I am delighted that there is agreement on both sides of the Committee to the grant of this money. I wholeheartedly approve of the order. I believe that it is important for us to ensure that demands for cuts in public expenditure in future do not ruin the success of the new towns. Therefore, I take great pleasure in supporting the order.

I hope that the Minister will comment on that matter and that there will be opportunities to pursue it on the Floor of the House, because I believe it to be of considerable importance.
