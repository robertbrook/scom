The General Medical Council (Constitution) Order 1979 (S.I. No. 112).

Dr. Vaughan: I am very happy, Mr. Watkins, that we should take the two statutory instruments together, since we do not expect a lengthy debate.

I refer first to the order concerning overseas qualified practitioners rules. In Committee on the Medical Act we spent a great deal of time considering the problems of doctors coming into this country from overseas. This was an important part of the Act. We eventually arrived at a solution which appeared to satisfy everybody, which is unusual in this place. It is not surprising, therefore, that now we come to the first order to bring the new arrangements into operation we wish to ask the Government to comment on the progress being made with the Act, and this particular part of it.

In the order we see that the board is to consist of four elected members and two appointed members, but no nominated members. As we understand it, this will mean that there will be no lay representation on the board. There is also to be one member—we spent considerable time on this point in Committee—who is the special member, as it were. The idea is that there should always be somebody present who will understand the problems of the overseas doctors. The Committee arrived at this special category to cover that. We had some doubt whether there would always be somebody in this special category on the council who would fulfil this requirement.

Col. 4 I turn to a slightly different point. Having gone to all this trouble, we see that the board is to consist of seven carefully selected people. It appears surprising, if I understand the position correctly, that at meetings of the board not more than five of those seven are to be present at one time. One of them is to be from the special category. But any three of those five can make the quorum. At that stage, therefore, the whole objective of having the special category person could be lost.

I ask the Minister to comment on those points.

10.46 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for Health and Social Security (Mr. Eric Deakins): Many questions have been asked. It may be helpful if I try to deal with them not quite seriatim—since we have gone from one order to the other—but by taking the orders in turn.

By way of background, I should like to begin with the comment of the hon. Member for Reading, South (Dr. Vaughan) about progress in implementing the Medical Act. The language provisions—section 29 of the Act—came into force on 1 December last year. All doctors entitled to full registration must satisfy the G.M.C. about their knowledge of English.

The limited registration provisions— section 22 of the Act—came into force on 15 February 1979, and the review board provisions also came into operation on that date.

I shall take the constitution order first, as I believe that, in a sense, it is the wider order. The review board comes under it. Most of the provisions in that order rely heavily, as those hon. Members who are experts in such matters and who serve on committees and so on will appreciate, on the recommendations of the Merrison committee. Therefore there are few surprises for anyone who has followed the reconstitution question through from the beginning.

After that report was published in April 1975 we had wide consultation on its contents. Even at a very early stage, there was broad agreement on the recommendations for the future composition of the G.M.C. The Medical Bill was introduced in November 1977, and further discussions with the profession at that time brought us to the point where most of the details of the Merrison recommendations enjoyed wide acceptance.

We had a consultation paper in November 1978. It closely followed the Merrison line, and as a result attracted very little controversy. The provisions in the constitution order are the result of a long process of consultation and discussion with the profession and with others who have a close interest in medical matters.

Image Col. 9 One of the points raised by the hon. Member for Walsall, North (Mr. Hodgson) concerned the size of the council, the number of elected members, and so on. Certainly, there have been some doubts about the size of the body that is being created. That is understandable, because in some public bodies too large a membership can inhibit and undermine their effectiveness. On the other hand, too small a body could fail adequately to represent the different range of viewpoints that it should.

The existing G.M.C., with 46 members, is a body of considerable size. Hon. Members will recall that critics of the council in the late 1960s and early 1970s expressed considerable concern that a council of that size was not fully representative of the profession which it was regulating. The Merrison committee thought carefully about that and concluded that the council had to be large, in order, as the report puts it,

"to encompass and represent properly all those having a legitimate interest in the standards of the profession"

reflected in the committee structure laid down in the Act, for the day-to-day affairs to be undertaken officially by smaller groups.

The Merrison recommendations provided for 98 members. The order provides for up to 95. That small reduction was made possible by a reduction in the number of elected members suggested by Merrison. We believe that that small reduction is worth while, in terms of the costs involved—there is, of course, a cost to the G.M.C. in running elections—but it still leaves the elected members with a majority over the other members as the Act requires, which is very important, and by a sufficient margin, which is also important, to allow for casual vacancies arising among elected members. We have taken care to cover this point in our consultation with the British Medical Association which has no objection.

The only scope for reducing the size of the council still further, if it were felt that even 95 was rather too large, would have been to reduce the number of appointed members. This would have meant asking certain universities or colleges to combine to make appointments. We mentioned this possibility in our consultation paper but the education bodies were not in favour. I am sure that it would not be right either Col. 10 to force this kind of rationalisation on them or to try to decide that one university or royal college should appoint a member while another should not. That would have been most unwise. So the order provides for all the medical education establishments to appoint members. Each has its own contribution which will be relevant to the work of the council.

Looking to the future, on the issue of size I should like to point out that if circumstances should change—if, for example, the universities which have medical schools agreed on some form of joint appointment, which would be entirely up to them—it would be possible to provide for a smaller council by a new order. But our priority now is to get the new more democratic and fully representative council into operation as quickly as possible. I am sure that that view is shared by all hon. Members.

As well as providing for a more broadly-based council, the Act gives the new council additional tasks and responsibilities, some of which are extremely important, and obviously there must be enough members to undertake all this work. This includes the new task of giving advice on professional conduct and ethics, and the work of all the council's committees, including the new Health Committee, the Review Board for Overseas Qualified Practitioners and the Education Committee, with its new and wider powers. Even this list does not comprehensively describe all the functions that the new council will have, but I hope that what I have said illustrates not only the size of its task but also the range that it covers.

Apart from the question of representativeness, the new council would need a considerably larger membership than the present council to man these committees and to undertake these tasks. A large number of people representing a wide spectrum of medical interest and knowledge is necessary if the council is to operate effectively in the best interests of both the medical profession and the public.

On the issue of nominated membership, to which the hon. Member for Walsall, North alluded, there is the controversial matter of whether the four chief medical officers should be members of the council. That has been discussed before at length. Of course, the Government recognise that on this point we have diverged from the Merrison recommendations. This does not Image Col. 11 mean that we attach any less importance than Merrison did to the principle of separating the setting of standards from the provision of services. But we remain convinced it is right that the four chief medical officers should be nominated to the council because of their range of experience and the value that their advice will have for the council.

We could not accept that the inclusion of these four doctors who, although employed in Government service are in close and constant touch with all that is going on in the profession, could possibly, among a body of 85 other doctors and six laymen, compromise the independence of the body. Although no one has suggested that this morning, it has been suggested outside the Committee. That underestimates the calibre of the other members.

Having said this, I should make it clear that neither the Act nor the order actually specifies that the C.M.O.s will be nominated—that is a matter to which I shall come back later—and each time nominations are needed they will be made in the light of experience of the working of the council.

The hon. Gentlemen referred to how lay membership should be chosen. There are many organisations that regard themselves, and are regarded by the Government, as having a good claim to be consulted on this. Therefore, we have the necessary flexibility to nominate the most able and experienced people to the council, and to do that the order imposes no restrictions. It is not the intention that the lay members should speak as the representatives of particular interests. That is important because, as I believe Merrison pointed out, a more general perspective is required.

We are currently giving consideration to the best way to find such people. Ideas about that might change in time, and it would not be appropriate to narrow down our choice in the order. Flexibility will be needed in the light of experience. I am sure that a number of bodies will be thinking seriously about this now that the order is going through and will be putting forward names for consideration. The names will be put forward to Ministers, who will no doubt weigh up the circumstances and suggest that certain names are more appropriate than others. Those names will be put forward to the Privy Council, which, of course, has the final say.

Dr. Vaughan: Is the intention that such people should cover different parts of the United Kingdom, geographically, in the way recommended by Merrison, or should they come from wherever is the most suitable place, depending on the person?

Mr. Deakins: Of the 10 nominated members, if we have the four chief medical officers, at least one should come from each part of the United Kingdom. But I agree with the hon. Gentleman that there is a strong case for saying that the six lay members should at least have some geographical spread. It would, for example, be appalling if they all came from London. I cannot, however, give any undertaking, because much will depend on who the nominated members are. I do not suggest that a person without suitable experience and qualifications would ever be put forward, but in the event of only one person being put forward in a particular region there could be a dilemma for Ministers. Because the area concerned would otherwise be completely unrepresented on the lay side, they might feel that they should appoint that person rather than someone else from a region already represented who is better qualified to take part in the work of the committee on a lay basis.

However, there must be a balance. If there is a large number of nominations, one cannot deny that there will need to be full consideration of achieving a regional geographical coverage, in addition to the standard geographical coverage which there will be on the G.M.C. from the system of elections in various parts of the country.

I come to the issue of overseas doctors. The hon. Member for Walsall, North and my hon. Friend the Member for East Kilbride (Dr. Miller) made the point that this was a sensitive and important issue. I take that point entirely. I shall deal first with the G.M.C. and come to the review board in a moment.

The Overseas Doctors Association representing doctors in this country— which was one of the principal bodies to be consulted before the order was drawn up—has suggested that there should be one nomination always reserved for a doctor with overseas qualifications. This suggestion was made in response to a Government proposal that such a nomination Image Col. 13 might need to be made in certain circumstances which we think are unlikely to arise.

The contingency we had to provide for arises in this way. The Act requires that one member of the review board must always be a member of the council who has gained his medical qualifications overseas, but not in an EEC country. But there is no specific provision in the Act which secures that the total membership includes such a doctor.

It seems reasonable to expect that there will be one or more overseas qualified doctors among the appointed or elected members. That is not an unreasonable expectation, if only because they constitute a substantial proportion of all the country's doctors. But we can only wait and see how the elections and the appointments by the appropriate education bodies turn out. If—this is the contingency—it turns out that no appropriately qualified doctor has been elected or appointed to the new G.M.C., a suitable person can be nominated in addition to the other 10 nominated members. To use a cricketing expression, it is a longstop. We hope that it will not be necessary, but if it is necessary we have the power to make use of it.

The overseas doctor has to be an additional nominated member. Otherwise, taken with the four C.M.O.s, he would upset the requirement that the majority of the nominated members must be lay, so there has to be a six-five position on the review board. But unless this contingency arises—I hope it will not—we intend that there should be only 10 nominated members, as recommended by Merrison: the six lay members and the four C.M.O.s.

The wish of the Overseas Doctors Association to see overseas doctors fairly represented is entirely understandable, but membership is not generally on a basis of representing specific interests, and it seems right that the O.D.A. should in the first instance look to its own approach to the elections as a way of seeing that the standpoint of overseas doctors has its voice in the new council.

Obviously, it will be for each doctor to decide whom to vote for in elections to the new council, but we hope and expect that we shall see a fair proportion of overseas doctors standing for election and forming part of the council, so that the composition fairly reflects the make-up of the profession registered and practising in the United Kingdom.

Col. 14 The provision in respect of the nominated members has been criticised for its lack of precision. Certainly, it allows a fair range of discretion, but the Act itself imposes certain constraints. Here I come back to the hon. Gentleman's point about geographical representation. There has to be one nominated member each for England, Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland. In practice, given our intention of nominating the four C.M.O.s, there will normally be six lay members. In the selection of the six nominated members, the need for a reasonable geographical spread will have to be balanced with the abilities and suitability of the people put forward.

Mr. Lewis Carter-Jones: Listening to my hon. Friend, I am forming the impression that the drafting of the orders has been rather slovenly. Someone ought to take a second look at it. The whole structure of the committees can be weighted one way or the other without any recompense. Can my hon. Friend please develop this argument more carefully?

Mr. Deakins: So far I have been basically dealing with the constitution of the G.M.C., composed of 95 people. I shall now deal with the review board, about which a number of points have been made. I think that that is what my hon. Friend has in mind.

Mr. Hodgson: As I understand it, the Minister is saying that there will be the elections and the appointments, and then the Government will make up their mind about the nominations, as a longstop. In other words, the final nominated members will be selected only once the elections and the appointments have taken place, because the Government want to use them as a balance. Mr. Deakins: The timing of the elections will be a matter for the G.M.C. once the order has come into effect. Perhaps I have misled the hon. Gentleman. I certainly do not want to do so.

Image Col. 15 I should like now to turn to the order concerning the rules of the Review Board for Overseas Qualified Practitioners. Those rules are a very important matter which affect the overseas doctors who contribute so much to our health service, and it is right that we should spend some time this morning in discussing them.

As hon. Gentlemen will realise, the provisions of the Act as they affect overseas doctors were introduced at a late stage, but were nevertheless subject to wide and detailed discussion. The Merrison report, from which they stem, was, of course, subject to even wider discussion. The provisions of the Act relating to the setting up and the functions of the review board were no less fully discussed. Although there had to be compromise, they nevertheless commanded a wide measure of agreement, not least from the overseas doctors themselves.

As some hon. Members will know, during the early stages of formulation of the provisions of the Act, the O.D.A. suggested that one member of the review board should always be a doctor who had qualified overseas. The Government were pleased to accept that suggestion, and provision was made for it in the Act.

To ensure that an overseas doctor will always be available, the Government have provided that, in the event of such a doctor not being elected or appointed, one will be nominated. I am pleased to say that Dr. Chatterjee, the chairman of the O.D.A., has been nominated to serve on the review board until succession day, when we shall have another look at the whole matter, because we shall have the elections at that stage.

The question of the number of overseas qualified doctors to serve on the review board has been raised. The Act says that there should be a minimum of one, and I have explained how that will be assured. But the number of additional overseas qualified doctors who might be able to serve on the board will clearly depend upon how many are appointed or elected to the new council, bearing in mind that there will be other committees on which their presence will be desirable. It is not just the review board with which they will be concerned. Provided that sufficient are available, clearly some of the specified, elected or appointed council members appointed to serve on the review board could be Col. 16 overseas qualified, but this will obviously be a matter for the reconstituted council.

The hon. Gentleman raised the issue of the rule about there being five members, with a quorum of three. I understand that the chairman, who is an independent chairman—that is absolutely specific in the rules—would have discretion to decide whether to operate without the overseas doctor. I make it absolutely clear that at least one overseas doctor would be invited to every meeting of the review body. Any date for such a meeting will obviously have been arranged to suit the convenience of every member. We all know the difficulties of getting busy people together. The overseas member in any event would have seen the papers, and if prevented from attending —as a result of illness, for example—could give his or her views in writing.

Dr. Vaughan: We do not understand why, having set up the seven members, we then have a rule which says that "not more than five" members out of that seven shall attend for the hearings. It was certainly the intention of both sides of the Committee when we discussed the Medical Bill that the overseas qualified doctor should be present at the hearings. That was not the intention of the Committee.

Mr. Deakins: I can understand the hon. Gentleman's concern. Perhaps I may put it this way. Unless the quorum is to be the maximum number, there has to be a specified quorum. The order provides for a five-year term of office. That term was unanimously accepted by all those who were consulted, even though Merrison suggested a four-year term. The five-year term was suggested in order to give members a longer period during which to use the skills and experience gained in the early years of office, It would also marginally cut down the G.M.C.'s expenses, because there would be slightly fewer elections over a Col. 20 period of years.
