I echo what the hon. Member for Clackmannan and East Stirlingshire (Mr. Reid) said about the Emslie report. While we very much welcome the Bill so far as it goes, with reservations in certain respects, it seems a notable omission that the Emslie report is not covered.

There are three important reasons why the Emslie recommendations should be included under part IV. First, I take the murder record in Scotland. In 1963 there were only two, in 1975 the figure had risen to 37, and in 1977 it was 50. One of the contributory factors causing the rise in really serious crime is the lack of deterrent value of the present life sentence. I had an Answer last Monday which makes clear that a life sentence in fact averages somewhere between six and 10 years. If a judge lays down a minimum recommendation as to the length of time a convicted murderer should stay in prison, that would seem to carry considerably more deterrent value.

The first reason, therefore, is deterrence. George Watt, legal correspondent for the Glasgow Herald wrote last February.

"The Emslie Committee's report fairly reflected the public attitude in Scotland … The Emsilie idea certainly has public appeal, and doubtless also political merit, since a swingeing sentence in terms of actual years is much more telling than the indeterminate 'life' which arouses suspicion, especially in the minds of 74 opponents of parole and release on licence, and everything that smells of the soft option."

Perhaps, if a prisoner behaves perfectly for 10 years and at that stage it is considered that it would be safe to release him although the minimum recommendation has been, say, 12 years, the Secretary of State might override the recommendation. But I suggest that there is a much heavier onus on the Secretary of State to examine the problem very closely indeed. There was the case of one Mr. Forbes who committed murder, was released from prison a few years later and committed murder again. That kind of event would be much less likely to occur if the Emslie report were implemented.

The second reason why the Emslie report should be implemented is that it is far more fair and consistent. Let me deal with the question of fairness. In the summary of conclusions of his report, Lord Emslie said that:

"in addition to recommending a minimum period the judge should state the factors upon which his recommendation is based so that his view of the offence and the offender, on the basis of the evidence at the trial, might be recorded for future use".

What is unfair under the present system is that when a convicted murderer conies up for parole years later, the judge is asked to give his view. Six, seven or eight years after the judge has had all the evidence, that evidence is not fresh in his mind. Even if he reads every single word again and goes into the matter in depth, the actual demeanour of the witnesses and the character of the accused as he gave evidence in the dock is less fresh in the judge's mind, and his opinion, six, seven or eight years after the case, carries less weight than it would at the time of the trial.

The second reason, therefore, is that the Emslie recommendation is more fair because the judge can give a more accurate impression at the time of the case. In his article in the Glasgow Herald Mr. George Watt said:

"One of the merits of the Emslie view is that it would introduce some kind of consistency into sentences imposed by various Scottish Judges who are known to hold different views on the recommended minimum."

We all know the reason why it is not being implemented. I think it was the right hon. Member for Kilmarnock (Mr. Ross) who expressed the view some years ago that it was not necessary in the light JPEG 75 of experience, but we all know that in practice the probable reason is that the prisons are now overcrowded. I suggest to the Minister that the emphasis should be directed towards releasing minor criminals but that the more serious criminals who commit premeditated murder should be treated more toughly. The first aim should be to protect the public.

I move on from that to the suspended sentence. In clause 47, part suspended sentence is proposed. It may well be that, in a crime of passion, when a husband tries to strangle his wife after some gross provocation—for example, the wife saying that her baby is by another man, or something of that nature—and the husband then repents, a wholly suspended sentence might be much more appropriate than a part suspended sentence. In practice, the Bill as drafted might lead to a situation where a prisoner sentenced to six months would serve the whole sentence but a prisoner deemed worthy of a sentence of 12 months might serve only three months. Obviously, a man's employment opportunities may be affected.

My hon. Friend the Member for North Angus and Mearns (Mr. Buchanan-Smith), in a powerful speech on Tuesday, made clear on clause 4—the provision for alcoholics—that sufficient resources must be made available. I quote now what the chief inspector of constabulary wrote in 1977:

"Nowadays, police forces do not have the manpower available to patrol streets and public places to prevent some of the mass of petty crime arising through drinking or to make even token inquiry to trace the perpetrators … Society has the choice: either devote substantial resources to tackle the alcohol problem or accept that the nation's crime, disorder and road death and injury problems caused by alcohol will constantly increase."

I hope that the Minister will bear in mind the importance of providing resources to ensure that there are the necessary detoxification centres throughout Scotland.

On the question of bail, the hon. Member for Garscadden mentioned that, as chief reporter for Lanarkshire, his experience was that money bail was not an appropriate form of bail. In fact, there are certain criminals who are extremely wealthy, and money bail could be a useful option in their case. I suggest 76 that money bail should not be ruled out altogether.

Perhaps the most important issue in the eyes of the electorate in Scotland is the treatment of murderers who commit murder with complete premeditation. If life sentence in their case works out at only somewhere between six and 10 years, that is not sufficient, and implementation of the Emslie recommendations would be a considerable source of relief to a great many members of the public in Scotland.

The admissibility of evidence was another aspect of the problem. One purpose of the limited power of detention conferred by clause 2 is to enable the police to ask questions of a person who is suspected of having committed an imprisonable offence. Such questions may be appropriate to allow the police to continue the speedy and efficient investigation of the crime in question, for example, to discover the whereabouts of the suspect's known associates, to check on the suspect's previous movements and to recover stolen property or a weapon which is being sought. Powers may also be necessary to allow the police to confirm by further immediate investigation their suspicion as to the suspect's connection with the crime.

Both those aspects of questioning are at present part of current police practice and the purpose of the clause is to regularise what happens now. The clause does not make changes in the present law relating to the admissibility at his or her trial of answers given by a suspect in response to police questions. Therefore, I hold that that clears the point on the admissibility of evidence.

The question of the wholly suspended sentence was raised extensively on Tuesday. Our reason for not going for wholly suspended sentences is that here in England and Wales there is a policy of 112 wholly suspended sentences which has not been successful. The temptation before the court in sentencing an offender is to say that the offender will be sentenced to a year but that the sentence is suspended, but then, lo and behold, the offender commits another offence. Although the year's suspended sentence was passed in order to deter the offender from committing another offence, it has not worked. England and Wales are finding that many more people are entering prison as a direct result of the wholly suspended sentence than would otherwise have been the case.

We have gone for a part-suspended sentence under which the offender will serve part of the sentence and the other part will be held in suspense, which we feel is a much better approach.
