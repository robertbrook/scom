SCOTTISH GRAND COMMITTEE
Tuesday 14th December 1978

[MR. PETER DOIG in the Chair]

CRIMINAL JUSTICE (SCOTLAND) BILL

10.30 a.m.

Resolved,

That if the proceedings on the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Bill are not completed at this day's sitting, the Committee do meet on Thursday next at half-past Ten o'clock.—[ The Lord Advocate.]

The Lord Advocate (Mr. Ronald King Murray)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Bill in relation to the principle of the Bill.

The Criminal Justice (Scotland) Bill is a Bill of 55 clauses and five schedules which seeks to make changes in many aspects of criminal justice and criminal procedure in Scotland. It is the most substantial Scottish measure likely to be brought before the House by the Government in the present parliamentary Session. The Bill is not without complexity but this is necessary because of the complexity of much of the law which the Bill is seeking to change. This means that the Bill requires to be read, studied and debated with care, and I hope that both here and at later stages our discussions will be positive and constructive. I am sure that they will be.

The last major reform of criminal justice in Scotland was the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Act 1963. Since then there have been a number of measures affecting Scotland—the Criminal Justice Act 1967 which established the parole board for Scotland; the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1975, a very valuable and substantial measure of consolidation; the District Courts (Scotland) Act 1975 which introduced the new district courts', the Criminal Law Act 1977 which increased penalties for a large number of statutory offences and substantially increased the power of the sheriff courts and district courts to fine in common law cases tried summarily.

As many hon. Members will recall, it had been the Government's hope to 2 introduce a criminal justice measure last Session, but this proved impossible because of the many other pressures on the parliamentary timetable. However, time was found to introduce the Community Service by Offenders (Scotland) Bill, which completed its passage through both Houses and is soon to be brought into effect. This Act introduces a very useful new disposal for the courts—the power to order an offender who has been found guilty of an imprisonable offence to carry out work for the benefit of the community.

Thus, although there has been no major reforming legislation since 1963 on Scottish criminal justice, the Government have introduced some significant reforms in this field, particularly since 1975. We shall be giving early consideration to consolidating the earlier legislation and the present Bill, and, of course, we can build on the 1975 consolidation.

A further reason why the Bill is significant is the intrinsic importance of criminal justice. It must be one of the Government's chief aims to ensure that the criminal justice system is kept under regular critical review. Fairness and efficiency are the two aspects of the criminal justice system which we have had in the forefront of our minds when preparing this Bill. In the criminal justice field efficiency—in terms of staff resources, convenience of witnesses and so on—has to be sacrificed for fairness, which must remain the test of a criminal justice system. But the Bill seeks, so far as possible, to strike the proper balance.

The Bill, of course, is not a final solution to the problem of crime; nor does it pretend to be. There is, naturally, widespread concern at the general upward trend in crime over the years, a trend which continues to be reflected in the figures for 1977 which were published in "Criminal Statistics—Scotland 1977" earlier this month. That is a matter of great importance to many people, as the Government appreciate from the many letters received on the subject of crime.

There are three main ways in which to tackle crime: long-term prevention, principally through education and by involving parents and the community in fostering socially responsible attitudes among young people; short-term prevention, through improving police efficiency and morale and encouraging a preventive JPEG 3 role; and third, by ensuring that the criminal justice system is geared to deal fairly and effectively with those who are charged with crimes.

It is at the third method of dealing with crime—ensuring that the criminal justice system is geared to cope with those charged with crime—that the Bill is principally aimed.

Before turning to consider the content of the Bill, I should perhaps say a few-words about its parentage. The source of a great many of its provisions is the second report of the Committee on Criminal Procedure in Scotland which was chaired by Lord Thomson. We are all very much indebted to Lord Thomson and to his committee for the tremendous amount of work which went into that report, which contained no fewer than 195 recommendations. Many of these are already implemented administratively or through rules of court by act of adjournal. Many more which require legislation are being implemented in this Bill. The other principal source of the Bill is the Scottish Council on Crime and, in particular, its report on fines—a very useful report which was published in October 1974.

The working group on identification procedure under Scottish criminal law, which was chaired by Sir William Bryden QC and which reported as recently as April, called for changes, only one of which required legislation and that has been included.

Mr. Malcolm Rifkind
: Could the Lord Advocate say at this stage why the Government have not sought to include in this Bill the proposals of the Thomson Committee on appeals from conviction on summary procedure? He will be aware that this is a matter of some interest in Scotland.

The Lord Advocate
: I agree with the hon. Gentleman, and I shall have a word to say about that in a moment.

When the Bill was published on 29th November, the Secretary of State announced that, while the Bill as introduced did not contain any provisions in implementation of the third report of the Thomson Committee—"Giminal Appeals in Scotland"—it was the Government's intention that amendments would be brought forward at a later stage to 4 provide for changes in the stated case procedure for summary appeals and to introduce a right of retrial in summary cases. I give the hon. Gentleman and the Committee an assurance today that this is still the Government's intention.

I turn now to the Bill itself. Although there are many different strands to the Bill, it is possible to pick out certain dominant themes. Rather than go through the Bill clause by clause—there will be plenty of time for that in Committee and, besides, the purpose of each clause is set out in the explanatory memorandum—I propose to identify the prinicpal themes and say a little about each in the hope that that will not be so boring to members of the Committee. I have selected 10 such items as worthy of particular attention, but no doubt members of the Committee will not be slow to draw any others to my attention.

First, I take the provisions of powers relating to detention by the police. Clause 1 gives the police a limited power to detain persons in any public place, or at the scene of a suspected offence, long enough to ask a suspect about the offence or to obtain, and if possible verify, his name and address—by radio, for example. Power is also given to the police to detain witnesses or others who may have information about a suspected offence, again long enough to obtain and, if possible, verify their names and addresses—a limited power.

Mr. David Steel
: Could the Lord Advocate explain why that provision is necessary? Most of us had assumed that it existed in the law at present.

The Lord Advocate
: I shall elaborate on that in a moment, but perhaps I could simply say, in a nutshell, that the idea is to enact what is at present good police practice—to enact it and delimit it—so that the police know where they are and the citizen knows what his rights are.

Clause 2 gives the police power to detain in a police station or elsewhere, for up to four hours, any person they suspect of committing, or having committed, an imprisonable offence. The three purposes for which this power is exercisable are, first to facilitate police investigations, especially where there is a risk that evidence may be destroyed, witnesses influenced or accomplices warned; second, to allow police to JPEG 5 question the suspect, and third, to enable the police to seek to establish the suspect's identity.

Under the power of detention in a police station, the police may search the suspect's clothing and baggage. These clauses are to a large extent based on Thomson Committee recommendations and clause 2, in particular, seeks to regularise and delimit present police practice. At present, there is no legal power to require a suspect to come to a police station, and the police can do no more than invite a suspect to come with them to the police station.

The two clauses seek to put good police practice on a sound legal footing, defining both the police powers and the citizens' rights in this very sensitive area.

Mr. Teddy Taylor
: Am I right in saying that these proposals do not go so far the Thomson Committee report recommends, and could the Lord Advocate say why?

The Lord Advocate
: The hon. Gentleman is quite correct—the proposals do not go as far as the Thomson Committee recommendations. I do not wish to get into a discussion on the clause in opening what is, in effect the Second Reading debate in Committee, but, in a nutshell we have sought to strike what we believe to be a sound balance between law enforcement, on the one hand, and the rights of citizens, on the other. For that reason, we feel that the balance struck in the Bill is preferable to that suggested by the Thomson Committee. But that is, no doubt, a matter upon which the hon. Gentleman would wish to reserve his views and pursue it in Committee

Clause 3 gives a person detained under clause 2 and a person arrested under existing powers the right to have a message about his detention or arrest and his whereabouts sent to one person reasonably named by him, I should say that for Glaswegians "message" means "communication" in this context. That would usually be a relative or a friend. The existing statutory right for an arrested person to have his solicitor informed of his arrest and to see him before appearing in court will continue as before.

Second, part II of the Bill provides for the abolition of money bail and its replacement by an offence of failing to 6 appear and for a system of release on conditions. I indicated that there were 10 items. I am now on the second.

The Chairman
: Order, will hon. Members keep their voices down or go out into the corridor? Others want to hear the Lord Advocate, if they do not.

The Lord Advocate
: I am most grateful, Mr. Doig.

Mr. Nicholas Fairbaim
: I know that Lord Thomson recommended it, but can the Lord Advocate explain why it would not be more sensible to have money bail retained as an alternative?

The Lord Advocate
: I can see the force of the point, but if he analyses the reasons which the Thomson Committee gave on the question, the hon. and learned Gentleman will, I think, see that those arguments really go to the complete abolition of money bail. However, I shall have some more to say about it, and, no doubt, the hon. and learned Gentleman will raise it during the debate. It is a matter that cannot be resolved quickly on an intervention.

The Thomson Committee received much evidence to the effect that the finding of money bail by the accused was an irrelevance to securing the attendance of the accused at the subsequent court proceedings and that the system involved a great deal of work in collecting and accounting for bail money. The committee noted that the amounts of bail usually fixed were low, a finding strongly supported by the subsequent Scottish Office social research study entitled "Pretrial Bail and Custody in the Scottish Sheriff Courts", which was published in 1976.

That study found that although the amounts of bail fixed in the sample under review varied from 50p to £1,000, the average amount was as low as £13.80 and the most common sum was £10. Despite the smallness of the sums fixed for bail, as high a proportion of those granted bail as 27 per cent, were not able to find the bail money immediately and spent at least one night in custody. The study found also that there was no significant difference between the average amount of bail which had been fixed for those who subsequently absconded—that is £13.33—and the amount fixed for those who subse- JPEG 7 quently neither absconded nor offended again, which was £13.95, a very small difference. Commenting on the Thomson Committee's recommendation that money bail be abolished, the authors of the study report estimated that there would be a drop of over 2,000 a year in the number of needless committals in custody from the sheriff courts alone.

That is the answer I would give to the hon. and learned Gentleman. He may ask: Why not have a safety net of money bail? The answer to that is that the evidence of this report shows that to be unlikely to be of any value.

The question which we must ask ourselves is what is the purpose of bail. Principally, it is to avoid the unnecessary holding in custody of those accused of crimes of which they have not yet been found either innocent or guilty. Initially, the court has to decide, having heard the procurator fiscal, whether or not to grant bail, if the accused applies for it. Once the decision has been taken by the court to release the accused, it is clearly desirable to take steps to ensure, so far as possible, that the accused presents himself at future court sittings of which he is given notice.

That is what the present money bail system seeks to do, but there is strong evidence that it is not relevant to whether or not the accused absconds. What the Thomson Committee proposed, and the Government have accepted, is that there should be criminal sanction for failure to appear—that release should be on conditions. The principal condition, of course, is that the accused appears for trial when required, but the Bill also gives the court power to impose other conditions as it thinks fit to try to ensure that the accused does not commit an offence while on bail—

Mr. Gordon Wilson
: Will the right hon. and learned Gentleman say what guidance the Scottish Office of his Department will give to sheriffs in relation to the conditions which might be substituted for money bail?

The Lord Advocate
: That is a matter of which, I think, I should require notice, but I can say here and now that it is not the practice in Scotland to give guidance of this kind to judges. I see the point that the hon. Gentleman is making. It is 8 obviously desirable that information should be available—not just the bare bones of the statute—by way of general background so that judges are aware of all the facilities which can be made use of in imposing conditions. That is a matter to which we can return at a later stage.

The Bill also seeks to ensure that the person on bail, the accused, does not interfere with witnesses, tamper with evidence or obstruct the court of justice, and that he makes himself available for interview in connection with the preparation of, for example, social inquiry reports. These are all matters that have to be taken into account in imposing suitable conditions of bail.

The sanction which the court may impose on an accused who fails to appear at a trial of which he has been properly notified, or who breaks any of the conditions on which he is released, is a fine of up to £200 on conviction, or, if imprisonment is being imposed for the original offence with which he was charged when granted bail, up to 60 days' imprisonment on conviction in the district court or up to three months on conviction in the High Court or sheriff court.

The new system of release on conditions, which will continue, as I imply, to be called bail, has two chief advantages. It should help to ensure that people are not unnecessarily held in custody pending trial, and it should also prove a more effective means of ensuring that the aims of bail are met. The sanction for nonappearance or for breach, of conditions under the new system will be much more severe than the forfeiture of the petty sums of money currently fixed for bail. There will also be an incidental benefit that some savings of staff time and cost will be made by the abolition of money bail and of the machinery for collecting and accounting for it.

Third, the Bill contains a number of provisions designed to reduce the use of^ imprisonment, and I am sure that members of the Committee will welcome that. The fact that we in Scotland have one of the highest male prison populations in Western Europe per head of population is a matter of no pride whatever. The bail reforms which I have just mentioned should result in some reduction in the number of those being held in prison before trial.

The Bill also proposes several innova JPEG 9 tions which seek to reduce the number of people sentenced to imprisonment after conviction. Clause 36 provides that where a person of or over 21 years of age is convicted of an imprisonable offence but has never before been in prison or in detention, the court shall not impose a prison sentence on him unless it considers that no other method of dealing with him is appropriate, and then only after considering a social inquiry report.

Clause 35 provides that where any person is convicted of an imprisonable offence but has never before been in prison or in detention, the court may not impose a custodial sentence without the accused being legally represented in court—unless he is not eligible on financial grounds for legal aid or has failed to apply for legal aid despite having been told of his right and being given an opportunity to apply.

Clause 47 introduces a power for Scottish courts to suspend an part—one quarter to three quarters—sentences of imprisonment of over six months but not exceeding two years. The principle of this clause is to give suitable offenders, in particular first offenders, a taste of the unpleasantness of prison and the prospect of a further spell of imprisonment if they misconduct themselves.

Clause 43 amends the table of maximum periods of imprisonment which may be imposed when an offender defaults in the payment of various levels of fines to reflect changes in the value of money and the increased fining power of the courts. This should result in lower sentences being served in default.

Fourth, and closely related to the measures seeking to reduce the prison population, there are provisions in the Bill on the treatment of drunken offenders. Clause 4 is an indication of the Government's desire that in time—I accept that it cannot be achieved overnight—there will be developed in each major urban area a nexus of appropriate facilities for the care in the community of persons with drink problems, including in each case a unit to which the police can take habitual drunken offenders, instead of arresting and charging them, and where they will be dried out and directed to appropriate facilities for their further treatment and rehabilitation. This is an integrated whole required to deal with the problem. I have to stress that this will take time since the Government's 10 advice is that the provision of a detoxificaton unit before the treatment facilities are there would make matters worse rather than better, depriving those unfortunates of at least the food, shelter and rest which however inappropriately, prison provides for them. But in times of financial stringency and other demands on the resources of local authorities and health boards, these treatment facilities cannot unfortunately be developed quickly. It is encouraging that in a number of urban areas of Scotland plans for treatment facilities are being developed, including detoxification units.

Fifth, the Bill contains a group of provisions relating to fines enforcement. It is hoped that these changes will improve the efficiency of the fines collection system. This is clearly desirable for its own sake and should lead to a reduction in the proportion of those fined who are ultimately imprisoned in default of payment.

While on the subject of fines, I should mention that it is planned to introduce fines enforcement officers on an experimental basis in Airdrie and in part of Edinburgh for a period of three years. The experiment follows on a recommendation of the Scottish Council on Crime. The principal function of the fines enforcement officers will be to visit fine defaulters and obtain information to assist the court. In particular, it is hoped that personal contact at an early stage will persuade a larger proportion of those cited to attend means courts to attend, that obtaining details of defaulters will help sheriffs and justices in disposing of cases, and that the visits which the fines enforcement officers will make to fine defaulters when they are about to be imprisoned for default will reduce the proportion of defaulters who end up in prison for default.

Sixth, the Bill introduces a new sentence for the 16 to 21 age group. As far back as 1969, the Scottish Home and Health Department published a memorandum which recommended the abolition of the three existing custodial sentences for this age group—borstal training, detention in a detention centre or in a young offenders institution—and their replacement by a new single generic sentence. It received a fairly general welcome, and it is interesting to note that the basic proposition of amalgamation was subsequently adopted by the Advisory Council on the Penal JPEG 11 System in England and Wales in its report "The Young Adult Offender".

Clause 37 seeks to implement that recommendation by removing the three present sentences and substituting a single sentence to> a youth institution. The proposal recognises that the main effect under all three present sentences is the loss of liberty, and that the existence of three different sentences introduces harmful rigidities into the best use of our institutions to meet the needs of inmates for different regimes.

Mr. Donald Dewar
: Will my right hon. and learned Friend confirm that, in effect, that means the end of the indeterminate sentence? Borstal training, for example, is an indeterminate sentence. Will the sheriff on all occasions have to put an exact term on the sentence?

The Lord Advocate
: I can confirm what my hon. Friend says. The courts would have power to sentence young offenders for a period up to the maximum period of imprisonment which might have been imposed on an adult on conviction of the same offence in the same court.

Seventh, the Bill gives the Secretary of State power to make specific grants to local authorities for hostels for adult offenders under supervision. Again, I am sure that this will be widely welcomed. Local authorities in Scotland with social work responsibilities already have power under the Social Work (Scotland) Act 1968 to provide hostel accommodation for adult offenders under supervision, that is, offenders on parole, on compulsory after-care or on probation. However, only very limited accommodation has been made available, which is unfortunate because I believe that the provision of such accommodation would mean that there would be a suitable place to which the courts might send, for example, offenders whom they wished to have under fairly close probation supervision. One hopes that probation would be used more than it has been recently.

At present, offenders who would be suitable for a probation sentence involving supervision with a residential requirement are often being given prison sentences or released into the community with less supervision than might be desirable. Similarly, where it might be appro- 12 priate to try to isolate a released offender on parole or compulsory after-care from a particular home or criminal background, hostel accommodation would foe very desirable. I hope that clause 51 will encourage local authorities to bring forward proposals for hostel accommodation for offenders and to take advantage of the incentive provided by this new grant provision.

Eighth, the Bill extends the jurisdiction of the district courts. Section 285 of the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1975 circumscribes the power of the district courts. Clause 13 eases some of these restrictions. The Government hope that this provision will result in some work being diverted from the hard-pressed sheriff courts.

Ninth, the Bill extends the restrictions on the publication of information likely to lead to the identification of children involved in criminal cases. At present, the law prohibits the publication of children's names only in the summary courts and only in respect of an offence by a child. It does not apply to trials on indictments or to appeals against conviction in a summary court. While a higher court has power to prohibit the publication, this is not always used. Yet the same considerations—the notoriety, the distress, the damage to< a child's successful re-establishment in society—apply to children equally in these other courts. Clause 25 therefore extends the prohibition to all cases involving children and to all courts. As at present with summary courts, the court or the Secretary of State would have power to lift the prohibition if it was in the interests of justice to do so.

Tenth, and last—but by no means least—the Bill introduces a large number of improvements in criminal procedure. I do not intend to list all the many provisions in the Bill which fall into this category. Most of them derive from the Thomson Committee's second report. I shall mention one or two of the more significant changes. Clause 20 makes new provision for the prevention of delay in trials, in both solemn and summary trials. That includes the Thomson recommendation in regard to the 110-day period—that that should run to the commencement rather than to the conclusion of the trial.

Clause 17 implements a recommendation of the Bryden working group by giving an accused the right to apply to the JPEG 13 sheriff for an order requiring an identification parade to be held where the prosecutor has not held one at his own instance or has refused or failed to hold one when requested by the accused.

Clause 27 and schedule 1 simplify the arrangements for taking routine evidence, subject to appropriate safeguards for the accused. One provision that perhaps requires special mention is clause 31. It is a source of pride that in general no one can be convicted of a crime in Scotland on the uncorroborated evidence of one witness, and the Government will firmly defend that principle. But when it comes to the enforcement of parking regulations, such a safeguard reaches the point almost of absurdity, with increased cost and reduced enforcement, as traffic wardens parade in pairs to write down the licence numbers of offending cars.

Mr. Fairbairn
: Registration number.

The Lord Advocate
: Registration number—did I say something different?—the licence number or registration number, as the case may be—or no doubt both. In the case of these very minor "ticket" offences, the Government propose that the evidence of one witness should be sufficient.

Clause 14 provides that sheriff and district courts will not be required to sit on a Saturday, Sunday or court holiday, although the court may sit on those days if necessary. It also provides that each sheriff principal may prescribe up to 10 days per year as court holidays for the courts in his sheriffdom. This clause is a recognition of the great difficulty of finding staff prepared to work on the preparation of cases for Saturday courts. To avoid unnecessary detentions over the weekend, the power of the police to liberate arrested persons, at present confined to minor cases triable in the district courts, will be extended by clause 11 to sheriff court cases.

I believe that the Bill is a well structured measure which seeks to strike the right balance between the powers of the police and the rights of the citizen in relation to detention; between the public interest and the rights of the accused, innocent until proved guilty, in the proposed reform of the bail system; between custodial and non-custodial disposal in the treatment of offenders; and between 14 fairness and efficiency in reforming the criminal procedure system.

I commend the Bill to the Committee as a carefully considered and, I believe, constructive contribution to the evolution of our criminal justice system in Scotland.
