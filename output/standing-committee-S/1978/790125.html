FISHING BOATS (FAROE ISLANDS) DESIGNATION (No. 3) (VARIATION) ORDER 1978

10.30 a.m.

Mr. Patrick Wall
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the Fishing Boats (Faroe Islands) Designation (No. 3) (Variation) Order 1978 (S.I. No. 1650).

The Chairman
: I think that it would be for the convenience of the Committee if, with this, we take the following:

Fishing Boats (Faroe Islands) Designation (No. 4) Order 1978 (S.I. No. 1950).

Fishing Boats (Specified Countries) Designation (No. 3) Order 1977 (Variation) (No. 2) Order 1978 (S.I. No. 1651).

Sea Fishing (Specified Foreign Boats) Licensing (No. 3) Order 1977 (Variation) Order 1978 (S.I. No. 1652).

Mr. Wall
: I should like to ask the Minister certain specific questions on these orders. I think that he will agree that all forms of orders or statutory instruments that we get in this House are generally unintelligible to the layman outside, and are often unintelligible to Members of Parliament as well.

The first order—the Faroe Islands No. 3 order—as I understand it, increases the area of fishing for Norway pout and sprat for Faroese vessels. The second order seems to me to be much the same as the Faroe Islands No. 4 order. This allows the Faroese vessels to fish in certain British waters. Therefore, both these orders extend the amount of fishing that Faroese vessels can do in British waters.

We have debated this formal order, because it occurs fairly regularly, in the past. I have made the point before that we always seem to be giving the Faroese an extension of the waters in which they can fish but what do we get in return? The Minister will remember that in the past there has been a diminution of the amount of Faroese waters in which British vessels can fish. I do not know whether he would wish to do so now, but perhaps he could make clear whether these orders which confer increases" on the Faroese have a 4 quid pro quo. In other words, do we get more access to Faroese waters?

I move to the third order—Specified Countries (No. 3). This, I understand, varies the 1977 order for vessels from Spain and Sweden. What I am not clear about from the order, not having an atlas and the various regions delegated in the order immediately at hand, is whether it gives the Swedesh and Spanish vessels more rights to fish in British waters or less? I should imagine it would be more. Why does it apply only to Spain and Sweden who have, as I understand it, no historic fishing rights off our coasts and are not members of the EEC? Again, is there a quid pro quo here? I come finally to the Specified Foreign Boats Licensing (No. 3) order. This brings boats of Sweden and the Faroes—both of which were referred to in the previous orders—within the ambit of the 1977 order. This order states that the licences granted by the EEC allows fishing for these boats within British waters.

Perhaps I may take all these orders together. They increase the amount of fishing which can be done within the British 200-mile EEZ by Faroese, Swedish or Spanish vessels. May I ask the Minister under whose authority is this fishing actually carried out? The last order specifies EEC licensing. The three other orders do not mention the EEC. I assume that the first three orders come under British legislation. Does the EEC agree, or is this one of the conservation measures introduced by the Government, with the support of the Opposition, achieve better conservation within the British 200-mile EEZ? Is it or is it not agreed by the EEC, or is it one of these orders which we introduced unilaterally? In that case, what is happening to the foreign vessels? Are they obeying the order? What is the position of our own fishery protection vessel? are they effecting arrest and are they entitled to do so under international law?

The Minister will appreciate that the situation is muddled because some of the legislation is enforced through the EEC, and I for one do not understand where the dividing line is.

Some of the legislation we have passed and discussed in this Committee in the past 18 months is drawn up under EEC rules and some concerns purely British conservation measures. I think this vagueness and the repetition of these orders, JPEG 5 which have been in operation for 18 months, underline the very unsatisfactory position of the fishing industry today. We are told that in three months' time the Ministers responsible for fishing matters will be meeting in Brussels, or wherever, that there will be a settlement, we shall have a definite statement about the common fisheries policy and we shall know where we stand. Inevitably, after three months we find ourselves back in this Committee with a fresh batch of orders but with no solution to the problem. The Ministers have met, have agreed to disagree and have gone away.

I suggest to the Minister that there must be compromise sometime. Time is not on our side because by 1982, according to the EEC rules, the limit is the shoreline of all the EEC countries. How long are we to wait? I know that the Minister cannot give me a definite answer because it depends on the Secretary of State's colleagues in the EEC. But he will appreciate that we have been waiting a long time and it is beginning to seem to us that the Government are now waiting for the general election so that they can pass the baby on to the next Government. I remind the Minister that this is a very important matter for the industry.

I should like to refer the Committee to an article in the Hull Daily Mail of 23rd January. It says

"For the first time on record more than half the 1,200 fishermen registered in the Hull area are out fo work."

Over 600 men in Hull alone—fishermen, not people in ancillary industries—are out of work. That is the catastrophic effect on the distant water industry. Until we have an agreement with the EEC we shall not get the swap deals which we need so badly with Iceland, Norway, the Soviet Union and others so that at least some of our vessels can return to the waters they have fished for centuries.

I stress that we are living on borrowed time. The year 1982 is not so far away and there must be a compromise at some time.

The inshore industry is also suffering. I draw the Committee's attention to Fish Trader of Saturday 13 January reporting a big debate with Mr. Howell, the Chief Fisheries Officer for Sussex, who complained that the quota system just does not work. He alleged that the Belgians have completed the whole of their year's quota by March in each of the past three years. The Ministry was brought in, agreed to the 6 figures and did nothing. Maybe it can do nothing in these circumstances, but I think it is a matter the Minister should refer to in his reply.

Finally, because of the difficulties with the EEC the situation for the distant water industry is extremely serious.

It will be serious for the inshore men in future, and there must be some compromise settlement as soon as possible. I hope that the Minister will be able to throw some light on the situation.

The fact that 600 fishermen are out of work in Hull has nothing to do with the economic emergency—nothing to do with the lorry drivers' strike—tout, of course, it makes the situation worse. I have handed to the Minister a telegram which I received today from the fishing industry in Hull and Grimsby saying how serious the situation is. The fish stocks—fish meal, fish oil—are being picketed and the men are not being allowed to move them without signing an agreement with the union, which I suggest is pure blackmail.

10.40 a.m.

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. E. S. Bishop)
: It may be for the convenience of the Committee if I rise at this stage to outline the background of the orders so that hon. Members will know a little more not only about the orders, but about the context in which they have to be considered. It is in that respect that the hon. Member for Haltemprice (Mr. Wall) has already raised some queries. I may deal with some of his queries during my speech and if not perhaps I shall take the opportunity later.

I should like the hon. Member for Haltemprice to know that I have taken note of the telegram to which he has made reference and our officials will look into the matter to see what action can be taken to deal with the situation.

The opening comment of the hon. Gentleman suggested that the Government were waiting for the General Election to pass the baby. I can assure him, and others, that we anticipate continuity of policy in this matter under the present and the next Administration, with the continued support of all parties and of the industry, which we have enjoyed to date.

With that brief reference to the party situation perhaps we can return to our normal recognition that all parties are united in the matter of getting the best deal JPEG 7 for our fishermen in relation to the needs of the Community as a whole.

The four orders before the Committee make changes in the rules governing third country fishing within British fishery limits. I think it would be helpful if I were to put those changes in context by outlining the development of our fisheries relations with the third countries concerned over the past year or so.

Early last year, as hon. Members will know, the EEC Commission negotiated fishing agreements for 1978, based on reciprocity, with Norway, the Faroe Islands and Sweden. Formally the Council still had to approve the agreements. Our fishermen have traditionally fished substantial quantities in Faroese waters, and indeed in Norwegian waters-—both in the Arctic and in the North Sea. We therefore said that our attitude in Council would depend on our obtaining a satisfactory share of Community quotas in Norwegian and Faroese waters. The hon. Gentleman has already asked questions about how the deal is going—whether to our advantage or disadvantage. I may deal with that point in more detail later. In the case of Sweden, in whose waters United Kingdom fishermen have not traditionally fished, we said that we were prepared to agree the limited quotas offered to the Swedes in the North Sea—which obviously affect the quantities available to United Kingdom fishermen— but again we could do so only on condition that the Norwegian and Faroese agreements were dealt with to our satisfaction. In the event the Council did not formally approve the third country agreements until July, although reciprocal fishing carried on up to then under provisional arrangements which were also incorporated into United Kingdom legislation. As part of the Council agreement the United Kingdom obtained, inter alia, 70 per cent, of the Community's cod and haddock quota at northern Norway and 40 per cent, of the redfish; and in Faroese waters the entire Community quota for directed cod and haddock fishing and 30 per cent, of the saithe. We would naturally like to have secured even higher proportions but I am sure that we obtained the best deal possible at the time. Hon. Members may also recall that, as part of the package, the Council agreed that the quotas of herring West of Scotland, which had previously been offered to Norway and the Faroes, should be withdrawn. This was in recogni- 8 tion of the fact that the United Kingdom had banned further fishing of that stock in 1978 in accordance with the advice of ICES.

I now come to the reasoning behind the orders before us today. In August the Faroese asked to be allowed to fish part of their quotas of pout and sprat in area VI, West of Scotland. They argued that they were unable to take their full quota of Norway pout in the North Sea, partly because of the effects of conservation measures. They also argued that the omission of area VI from the 1978 agreement had been a mistake on the part of their negotiators because they had fished in this area for several years previously. Their request was agreed within the Community, subject to a limit on the amount of pout to be taken West of Scotland, and the change is incorporated in the Fishing Boats (Faroe Islands) Designation (No. 3) (Variation) Order 1978.

Perhaps I may summarise what that order says. It amends the principal Fishing Boats (Faroe Islands) Designation Order enabling the Faroese to fish for Norway pout and sprat in area VIA—that is to the west of Scotland—as well as in area IV in the North Sea.

The effect of the Sea Fishing (Specified Foreign Boats) Licensing (No. 3) Order 1977 (Variation) Order 1978 was to require Swedish and Faroese vessels fishing in our waters to obtain licences from the EEC Commission. Provision for the introduction of licensing was contained in the 1978 agreements, but it was not possible to complete the technical preparations before October. Licensing was therefore introduced from 1 November.

We very much welcomed this move, which meant that we could exercise closer control over the activities of Swedish and Faroese vessels in our waters. The introduction of licensing also balanced the fact that member States' vessels are required to carry licences to fish in Swedish and Faroese waters. We agreed, however, that Norwegian vessels should not be licensed— except at Greenland—because our own vessels do not need a licence to fish in the Norwegian sector of the North Sea. Nevertheless Norwegian vessels are required to report their position and catches by radio in the same way as vessels of other third countries.

To summarise, the effect of the order is therefore to prohibit fishing within British fishery limits by Swedish and Faroese JPEG 9 vessels, except under the authority of a licence. This brings the arrangements for Swedish and Faroese vessels into line with those for Spanish vessels, which for some time have been required to carry a licence.

I pass on to the next order, the Fishing Boats (Specified Countries) Designation (No. 3) Order 1977 (Variation) (No. 2) Order 1978. I shall briefly explain what that mouthful means. Its effect is to amend the principal designation order in respect of Spain and Sweden. It enables the Spaniards to fish for sardine in area VIII, only a small part of which falls within British fishery limits, and enables them to fish for tuna, or tunny, in areas VI and VII as well as in area VIII. At the same time, Spanish fishermen are excluded from fishing in the Irish Sea, part of the Bristol Channel, and British waters west of Scotland and the area south of 56°30' North. The other change made by the order is to prevent Swedish vesssel which have quotas of ling and blue whiting West of Scotland from fishing in waters south of 56°30' North.

That is what the order means, but perhaps I may fill in the background against which it has been tabled. The main changes made by the order are in respect of Spanish fishing. For most of the last year, the Council of Ministers authorised Spanish vessels to fish in member States' waters only for short periods at a time. The intention was to put pressure on Spain to negotiate a framework fisheries agreement with the Community. Negotiations for such an agreement—which provides for a gradual reduction in Spanish fishing to the low level justified by reciprocity— were completed early in September last year. It will come as no surprise to hon. Members that the United Kingdom is blocking signature of the agreement, as of other framework agreements, in accordance with out demand for progress to be made simultaneously on all parts of the common fisheries policy, including those parts of most concern to us.

We did, however, agree at last September's Council to a small increase in Spain's hake quota for the last quarter of 1978 in recognition of the fact that negotiations had effectively been concluded. It was also agreed that Spanish vessels could fish for sardine in area VIII, which is mainly French waters, and for tuna. In addition, certain restrictions were imposed on the areas in which Spanish fishing for hake could take place. These changes in respect 10 of species and areas were incorporated in the two Orders before the Committee which relate to Spain, dealing with designation and licensing respectively. I might also mention that the opportunity was taken to make a small change in the areas in which Swedish vessels were permitted to fish for blue whiting and ling. The change confines Swedish fishing in area VI to waters North of 56°30'North, the area where Swedish vessels have fished in the past. This is again in accordance with the 1978 agreement between Sweden and the Community.

I appreciate that hon. Members have on occasion expressed dissatisfaction with the number and frequency of amending orders which we have had to lay in respect of third country fishing. This has, of course, resulted partly from the uncertainty within the Community caused by the lack of an agreed common fisheries policy. That point has been made many times.

Mr. Wall
: May I make a suggestion? As I understand it, these four orders are virtually package deals agreed with the EEC. Would it be possible in future, in an explanatory note, to say whether the order is an EEC order or is agreed by the EEC? It does on the last order, but not on the others. It would be helpful to the Committee if that were done in future.

Mr. Bishop
: The orders all reflect EEC arrangements with the countries concerned. The agreements with Norway and the Faroes are reciprocal, and the United Kingdom benefits directly. The agreement with Sweden is reciprocal and mainly benefits the Danes and Germans, who fish in Swedish waters. The agreement with Spain is non-reciprocal.

Mr. Wall
: Could this be stated on the explanatory note of the order in future?

Mr. Bishop
: I do not know about that, but I am stating it now.

This situation has resulted partly from the uncertainty in the Community caused by the lack of an agreed common fisheries policy. The Commission is at present engaged in negotiating quota agreements for 1979 with the Faroes, Spain and Sweden and has already reached agreement with Norway. I hope that arrangements can be finalised earlier this year than they were in 1978. It would then be our intention to produce consolidated orders incorporating any necessary changes to existing provisions. Meanwhile the Council JPEG 11 has agreed interim arrangements permitting reciprocal fishing to continue for the first two months of 1979. The Fishing Boats (Faroe Islands) Designation (No. 4) Order extends the period in which Faroese vessels may fish in our waters to 28 February. The designation orders relating to Norway, Sweden and Spain are not subject to a specific time limit and therefore do not require extension.

I hope that the Committee will approve the orders.
