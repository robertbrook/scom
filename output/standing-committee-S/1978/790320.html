10.30 a.m.

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Law Officers' Department (Mr. Arthur Davidson): I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the Legal Aid (Financial Conditions) Regulations 1979.

The Chairman: I think that it would be for the convenience of the Committee if we also take the following: Legal Advice and Assistance (Financial Condition (No. 2) Regulations 1979.

Mr. Davidson: It is good to be able to come to a Committee with some good news, and I do have some good news today.

Mr. Ian Percival: The election date?

Mr. Davidson: No. It is good news of a different sort.

These regulations are part of the package of improvements in the financial conditions for civil legal aid and advice and assistance generally which, as the Committee will know, were announced by my noble Friend, the Lord Chancellor, in November last year, and of which he gave details in another place when moving the Second Reading of the Legal Aid Bill.

The regulations increase the lower disposable income limit—that is, the limit above which legal aid ceases to be free— from a disposable income of £815, which it is now, to a disposable income of £1,500. The term "disposable" means the income which is left after deductions have been made for tax, rent or mortgage, other necessary expenses such as travelling to work, and allowances for dependants.

The regulations also increase the upper income limit, above which one ceases to be eligible for legal aid, from a disposable income of £2,600, which it is now, to £3,600. The lower capital limit—the Col. 4 amount an applicant is entitled to keep —is increased from the present limit of £365 to £1,200, and the upper limit, above which an applicant will not normally be entitled to legal aid, is increased from the present figure of £1,700 to £2,500.

The Legal Advice and Assistance (Financial Conditions) (No. 2) Regulations relate to legal advice and assistance under what is commonly known as the green form scheme, and they increase the eligibility limits for legal advice and assistance from a disposable income of £52 a week, which it is now, to £75 a week, and from a disposable capital of £365 to £600. Other regulations, which do not require an affirmative resolution, increase the lower free disposable income limit from the present limit of £25 to £35 a week, and make consequential amendments to the scale of contributions.

In assessing a person's disposable income, allowances are made for dependants, which for this purpose include a spouse. At present, these allowances correspond to supplementary benefit rates, but further regulations, which again do not require an affirmative resolution, will increase these allowances for legal aid and legal advice and assistance so that they will be 50 per cent, above the appropriate supplementary benefit rates for the particular dependant. This will be of considerable help to families. In fact, families will benefit most of all from this package.

The opportunity has also been taken to simplify the regulations relating to the assessment of resources by incorporating some allowances in the scale and adjusting others. The simplification should in many cases remove the necessity for interviews at legal aid assessment offices. Anyone who has dealt with legal aid will know that these interviews are long, laborious and costly.

Time and again, in Committee and in debates on legal aid, estimates have been made about the proportion of the population that is at present eligible for legal aid. It is difficult to make accurate assessments because the limits relate to disposable income, which varies according to a person's commitments and family.

However, one thing is clear—namely, that far fewer people are now within the scope of the scheme than when it was first introduced in 1950. The increases which are made by these regulations, and Image Col. 5 the others to which I have referred, will, I am happy to say, recover that lost ground. As a result of the regulations, and of the Bill which is now before another place, to which I shall refer briefly later, it is hoped that over 70 per cent, of the population will be eligible for legal aid, and that over 30 per cent, of households consisting of two parents and two children will not be required to pay a contribution.

I turn briefly to contributions towards the cost of civil legal aid. As I said earlier, a Bill is at present before another place that will enable the Lord Chancellor to reduce the maximum contribution payable by persons whose disposable income is above the free limit. This is an important move because there is little point in granting people legal aid if they cannot afford to pay the contribution. It is hoped that the Bill will come before this House at the earliest possible opportunity.

Mr. Percival: If the hon. Gentleman is leaving the question of proportions, did I understand him to say that, after these changes are made, 70 per cent, of the population would be eligible for legal aid? If that is what he said, has that been worked out on a basis comparable to the previous estimates of proportion that we have had, or on some new basis? I do not remember at any time hearing that the proportion was as high as 70 per cent, or anything like it. I thought that 40 per cent, was the highest figure of which I had heard.

How do the limits, about which he has already told the Committee, compare, after allowing for changes in the value of money, with the limits when the scheme was first introduced and with the limits that existed in 1974? We need those figures, as well as the proportions, to see what substantial difference the regulations are making. If the hon. Gentleman can give us those figures before he sits down, we can deal with them when we come to speak.

Mr. Edward Lyons: Will the Minister confirm that, when the legal aid scheme first began, more than 70 per cent, of the population were eligible, and not fewer?

Mr. Davidson: I am grateful to the hon. and learned Member for Southport Col. 6 (Mr. Percival) for giving me a little notice so that I can give him the best answer available. I can assure him that the figures that I gave are accurate. Indeed, my noble Friend the Lord Chancellor quoted the same figure in the other place. Perhaps more than 70 per cent, of the population will be eligible for legal aid. Perhaps later I shall be able to give my hon. and learned Friend the Member for Bradford, West (Mr. Lyons) an answer to his questions.

In conclusion, I think the Committee will agree that these regulations, together with the others to which I have referred, and the Legal Aid Bill, constitute the most significant and important improvement in the legal aid scheme since it was introduced. I am sure that it will be welcomed by the Committee. For a long time it has been a source of irritation and concern that such a high percentage of people, many of whom are on fairly modest incomes, were ineligible for legal aid. These regulations will recitfy that.

There is no doubt that the position of litigants will be better after these regulations come into effect than it was before. This is a step which should have been taken long ago. For a long time there has been a very big gap. Many people have found it impossible to engage in litigation, even if they had a good claim,—and, if they were defending a claim brought against them, it was extremely difficult—because they were above the limit prescribed by the regulations. They were, therefore, theoretically able to pay for their own representation. But in fact they were not in a position to do so.

That is why we have been pressing for some time—and not only from this side; there has been pressure and resolute promises from other quarters as well—for a revision of these limits. We have always been put off with the answer, "Yes we agree that it should be done, but we cannot afford it". It is a little surprising that that having been the argument for so long, that this should be the moment chosen to do it.

It would probably be considered ungenerous of me if I were to say that it is very unlikely that the Government will have to find the money to pay for it. More than likely, by the time these regulations become effective, and the burden falls upon the Exchequer, there will be another Chancellor of the Exchequer and another Government which will be responsible for finding the money. Although it is not every burden that will be bequeathed to a Conservative Government by the Labour Government which will meet with our ready approval, this is one which—if we were placed in that position—we would readily accept, because we think it does no more than remedy an injustice that has existed for far too long.

Image Col. 9 I find a little surprising the figure of £6 million. I should perhaps have made that statement at the outset to give the Minister more time to deal with it. If he cannot do so, will he please let us have some more information about it?

With those qualifications, I welcome the regulations on behalf of the Opposition. We shall be glad to see them come into operation.
