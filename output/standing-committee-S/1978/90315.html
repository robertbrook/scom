Land Registration (Scotland) Bill [Lords]

10.30 a.m.

The Lord Advocate (Mr. Ronald King Murray)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the Land Registration (Scotland) Bill [ Lords] in relation to the principle of the Bill.

The Bill provides for the setting up of a Land Register of Scotland, on which will be recorded all land transactions as they take place. The new register will thus progressively supersede the existing register of sasines in which titles to land are presently recorded. The main provisions implement proposals in the report of the Henry Committee on Registration of Title to Land in Scotland, which was published in 1969.—Cmnd. 4137.

The register of sasines has existed in Scotland as a public register of title to land since 1617. This was one of the first land registers in the world, and was public from the outset. It has stood up to the test of time remarkably well. But, depending as it does on an examination by solicitors of the whole series of title deeds on each occasion on which a property changes hands in order to ensure that the transferee obtains a valid title, it has plainly become far too cumbersome a system to continue to cope with the pace and scope of modern property transactions. It could be kept alive only by the deployment of steadily increasing numbers of staff in the department of the registers, as well as in the private firms of professional searchers. Even as matters stand, delays in obtaining a completed title can frequently be lengthy, and in the longer term could only worsen if other measures were not set in train.

The basic features of the new system of registration for which the Bill provides are twofold. First, when an application for registration is made for the first time, the Keeper of the Registers will carry out a thorough and once-for-all examination of all previous title deeds to the property. Further examination of a title, once registered, will thus be rendered unnecessary on any future occasion on which the property changes hands.

2 Second, a title once entered on the land register will be guaranteed by the Keeper and any title holder will be indemnified against loss arising from any error in the register.

The new system of guaranteed title by registration, once in operation, will thus eliminate the present lengthy examination of titles. The system of public indemnity already applies in England and Wales in relation to the land registry system already partly in operation there. On the basis of experience in England and Wales, the number of occasions on which, as a result of error in the registers, this indemnity likely to be called upon is likely to be very small indeed.

(Glasgow, Maryhill)
: Does that mean that home buyers in Scotland can in future look forward to reduced conveyancing costs because the State will be incurring many of the risks involved in undertaking the indemnity?

The Lord Advocate
: I think that in the long term the Bill does present a reasonable prospect of that. [ Laughter.] I put the matter rather guardedly, because hon. A major change in machinery of the kind for which the Bill provides cannot be brought into force throughout Scotland overnight and will require to be phased over a period of years. Additional areas will be brought within the scope of the new register year by year until all nine areas into which the country is administratively divided for land registration purposes are embraced within it. It will, of course, be many years—perhaps 25 to 30—before every single property recorded in the present register of sasines is transferred to the new register and the sasines register can formally be wound up.

It may be helpful if I say a few words about the main contents of the Bill. Part I provides for the setting up of the new land register and contains detailed provisions governing the processes of registration.

Clause 1 sets up the new public register and provides for its control and management by the Keeper of the Registers for Scotland.

Clause 2 describes the land transactions to be registered and provides, subject to certain exceptions, that registration shall be the only means of creating a real right in land. That is, in a way, the principle which underpins the Bill—the concept of title by registration.

Clause 3 specifies the supporting information required with any application for registration, the main feature being the requirement as to the description of the land in question. In future, this will have to be in a form sufficient to enable the Keeper to identify it by reference to Ordnance Survey maps. The eventual certificate of title issued by the Keeper will be supported with an appropriate map section on which the boundaries of the property in question are delineated. I pause to say that that should be a great advantage to the consumer—those who buy and sell land.

: But the point my hon. Friend makes is important. Unfortunately, he is raising a number of technicalities which would require me to look rather quickly at different parts of the Bill, so I decline his invitation to answer at this point.

The remaining clauses in Part I are essentially procedural. Clause 6(1)( a) contains an important new provision. When land extending to two hectares, that is 4.942 acres, is being registered and the proprietor is a feuar or long leaseholder, the Keeper must disclose this in the title sheet concerned. This will be a useful provision adding greater precision to the register as a source of information about the extend of individual ownership.

Part II of the Bill relates to indemnity arising through loss due to error in the new register. Clauses 12 and 13 specify the circumstances governing entitlement, and clause 14 contains provisions for safeguarding the Crown's rights in the foreshore.

Clauses 15 to 19, forming Part III, pro- JPEG 5 vide for various simplifications in the forms of property deeds. Most of these clauses apply to the register of sasines as well as the land register. My hon. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Garscadden (Mr. Dewar) may find some of the treasure he is seeking in those clauses.

Clauses 20 to 30, comprising Part IV of the Bill, are in the main procedural and consequential. Clause 20, however, makes provision to assist a special class of tenants, numerically very small, known as tenants-at-will, to acquire the landlord's interest in the land in question and to have it recorded in the register. Tenancies-at-will are a form of unwritten tenancy, most commonly to be found in the north-east counties. In the absence of any written title, the occupants have hitherto been unable to record title, and have thus often been inhibited from carrying out improvements in their property through inability to offer security against a building society mortgage. The change proposed will, I feel sure, be warmly welcomed. Strictly speaking, these people have held an uncomfortable tenure midway between tenancy and true ownership of land.

(Aberdeenshire, West)

The Lord Advocate
: I think that is the case.

I do not feel that I need detain the Committee longer with detailed explanation. While much of the Bill necessarily deals with what may appear to be legal and technical matter, essentially the Bill is concerned to effect a highly desirable modernisation and streamlining of the processes of registration of title to land in Scotland, and by means of the system of indemnity against error in the land register to introduce a degree of protection to the individual citizen hitherto lacking.

The Bill has already received a warm welcome within the legal profession, since in the long run it will bring about an altogether speedier and surer process of registration of obvious benefit to all concerned in property conveyancing.

But the matter goes well beyond the convenience of the legal profession. The more modern processes of registration should themselves in due course, once the 6 new system is fully in operation, bring direct benefit to the public because of the scope which they afford for reduction in relative costs, as compared with those under the present system were it to continue unchanged. And the Law Society—here I revert to a point put to me earlier by my hon. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Maryhill (Mr. Craigen)—accepts that in due course there should be beneficial effects on conveyancing costs.

I am confident that this useful measure, providing for a necessary modernising of land registration procedures, will be generally welcomed in Scotland, and I confidently commend it to the Committee.
