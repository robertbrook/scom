NORWAY POUT (PROHIBITION OF FISHING) (No. 3) (VARIATION) ORDER 1978

10.30 a.m.

Sir John Gilmour
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the Norway Pout (Prohibition of Fishing) (No. 3) (Variation) Order 1978 .(S.I. No. 1379).

The fact that the Opposition have put down a Prayer against the order in no way means that we disagree with it, but in the present state of the fishing industry we need every opportunity there is to press the Government to give us as much information as possible about the progress of the negotiations concerning the common fisheries policy. This debate will also be an opportunity for the Minister to give information to the Committee about what has been the helpful effect of the Norway Pout prohibition order and further information about why the variation runs only for the winter months and returns to the old limits in the summer months.

We know that the result of the last meeting of the agricultural and fishery Ministers did not lead to success. We now want to know from the Government whether they intend to return to some of the other measures that were discussed before but postponed. I feel that if the postponed measures are brought forward again, this may help to put pressure on the other members of the EEC to come to a final agreement.

The matter is of some importance because the House rises for the Christmas recess at the end of next week, and we could easily be faced with a situation in which the Government came to a decision some time between now and 31st December—as they say they wish to reach conclusions before the end of the year—and we had no opportunity of discussing the matter or voicing our opinions in the House. Therefore it is very important that we should get the maximum amount of information from the 4 Minister today and I hope that it will be helpful information.

10.33 a.m.

Mr. David Mudd
: I wish to make a brief intervention following the remarks of my hon. Friend the Member for Fife, East (Sir J. Gilmour). It seems that the orders have now been effectively in operation for one and a half seasons, and I hope that the Minister will be able to give us some idea of the nature of stock monitoring that has been carried out during that period, the information that has been gained, and the Ministry's forward projections of the effects of these measures on stock growth during the next four years.

10.34 a.m.

Mr. Douglas Henderson
: I am very grateful for being called by you, Mrs. Butler, although I am not on the list of Members attending the Committee. I have one or two questions that I should like to put to the Minister in relation to the order, particularly regarding by-catch. If I have the Minister's ear, he might consider the question of how effectively this order is being enforced at present.

It was reported to me at the weekend by skippers from my constituency that the Danes were still landing considerable quantities of pout at Esbjerg and landing it in such a way that while the top of the catch landed is pout, underneath there is a considerable quantity of immature fish concealed by the pout. Unless there are more effective methods of making sure that these orders are being carried out, I begin to wonder whether we are going through a pantomime in the Committee and in the House in approving orders of this kind.

I should like to hear from the Minister whether the Government have had any further thoughts on the control of vessels. As he will know, when our vessels go into Norwegian waters they are required to report their position to the Norwegian fishery authorities. They are required to inform them when they move to another fishing position. They are required to inform the Norwegian authorities when they finally leave the waters. This makes the surveillance of foreigns boats in Norwegian waters much easier to control than the system we have, which is of Nimrod surveillance generally. Our fishermen ask why they have to report their position JPEG 5 in Norwegian waters when foreign boats coming in to our waters are under no such obligation.

The Minister must soon make a statement on that. If there is to be some quota regime as a result of the renegotiation of the common fisheries policy, it is essential that the question of enforcement and fairness is uppermost in the way in which it is implemented. I see no reason why the Government cannot, as of now, institute a system whereby every foreign boat coming in to our waters is required to report its position and state when it starts to fish and when it finishes. Until such a system exists, fishermen will not have much faith in any orders that are passed.

My second question—I have raised this with the Minister before—is whether it is possible, as we are all partners, so-called, in the European Economic Community, for us to have the right to be at Esbjerg and the other ports to check what is being landed. Our fishermen tell me that they would have no objection if Danish fishery officers were at Peterhead or Aberdeen or any of our ports to check the validity of our declared catch. Is there any reason why we cannot post fisheries officers at the other ports in the Community, either on a random basis or on a regular basis depending on the requirement, so that we can do an independent check on whether these various orders are being carried out or whether a form of cheating is going on? Fishermen are convinced that, despite the pout box order, other vessels are still cheating.

If there is to be any confidence at all in any agreement negotiated, that agreement will not be worth the paper on which it is written unless there is adequate and proper enforcement. To date, we have not had a clear indication from the Government about the methods they are proposing for enforcement.

The Under-Secretary, the hon. Member for Glasgow, Provan (Mr. Brown) made a strong statement in the Statutory Instruments Committee on Wednesday, 22nd November to the effect that the Government had improved the negotiating atmosphere in Brussels and that there was absolutely no doubt about their determination to get a good agreement. His right hon. Friend the Minister of Agriculture. Fisheries and Food made a 6 similar statement to the House on 28th November.

What I cannot understand and what I hope the Minister will explain today—I do not think that his right hon. Friend explained it sufficiently—is why the proposals put forward, which the British fishing industry regard as the absolute limit to which we could go, were summarily rejected and yet the Minister can say that he is still confident of getting an agreement by the end of the year? That suggests to me that the only way that that can happen is by a further dilution of demand and by a further weakening in our position. We should welcome the Minister's reassurance on that this morning.

10.40 a.m.

Mr. Alick Buchanan-Smith
: The hon. Member for Aberdeenshire, East (Mr. Henderson) referred to the daily situation as something of a pantomime. It is significant that a leader in the Fishing News of 24th November 1978 referred to it as "Danish Fairy Tales". That gives some indication of feeling about the way the Danes are treating some regulations and orders which we pass in this House, and it is right that we should bear that in mind today.

The leader in the Fishing News related to a visit by representatives of the Scottish fishing industry to Danish ports to see at first hand what was happening in the Danish fishing industry and to get their reactions to the pout box and the conservations measures which we had introduced. One of the most frightening aspects for the fishing industry is the way in which the Danes have diverted their efforts—to that extent it demonstrates the effectiveness of an order such as this—to other areas with absolutely no thought towards conservation. The leader says that the Danes are

"bringing in Baltic herring which size-wise ought to still be in nappies."

That is one of the worries. In these present negotiations and in consequence of the orders that we are passing, the Danes, and others of our Common Market partners, are simply not showing the same concern towards conservation as the British fisherman. To some extent, that indicates the difficulties that the Government have had in their negotiations.

JPEG 7 The Government must show that from the kind of conservation measures that we pass our fishermen are currently suffering as much as anyone else. Our fishermen are prepared to make the sacrifice which they are making. But it is particularly galling when they go to a country such as Denmark and they see that the Danes are sacrificing in one area and exploiting a species in another area as a consequence. It makes us wonder to what extent they are genuinely seeking conservation, and it makes us appreciate the enormity of the Government's task.

The second point I want to make also refers to the Danes and follows on what was said by the hon. Member for Aberdeenshire, East (Mr. Henderson). It concerns control. I do not want to follow him precisely on the point he made, though I shall be interested to hear the answer to that question.

There is very real concern in the fishing industry about the effect of some of these controls. Let me give an analogy in relation to mackerel fishing, which is another example where, having passed a measure such as this, the Government must carry through the supervision. The hon. Member for Aberdeenshire, East mentioned what the Norwegians are doing and the regulations to which they are subjecting our fishermen.

On the mackerel side there is grave concern, particularly amongst the smaller fishing boats, in that the larger boats—the purse seiners—are going straight alongside the factory vessels and discharging. As I understand the position, the Government simply accept the word of the skippers of these vessels and the factory ship—normally a foreign vessel—about the amount of the catch. The smaller vessels, which are not landing on to a factory ship, are landing on the British quayside. They are subjected to actual visual control by our own British fishing inspectors and can get away with nothing. Those landing on to the factory vessels are not subjected to visual control. This is a complaint that was made in relation to Ullapool. I understand that my hon. Friend the Member for Falmouth and Camborne (Mr. Mudd) will add to what I have said. There is an exactly similar complaint concerning fishermen in that area in that those who are landing from inshore boats are being subjected to far 8 more stringent controls than some of the bigger vessels. It is alleged—I put it no higher than that—that even amongst our own fishermen there are some who are exploiting and going beyond the quota because the Government are not exercising the correct degree of supervision.

I want to make two points in relation to this. I am concerned, first, that if there is any example of our vessels—I do not care what kind of vessels they are—breaching the regulations, we have no right to criticise the Danes in the way I did earlier. I understood that our fishermen were being well supervised, and I hope that the Government can give us some assurance that the supervision is being carried out effectively and properly, otherwise it weakens our whole case in negotiations with our Common Market partners.

The second point I would make is the simple one of fairness. It is terribly important that in our supervision we are fair not only between one nation and another but between different sections of our own fishing industry. I should be very concerned if our smaller vessels, who find it much more difficult to make a living, were subjected to more stringent supervision than some of the larger vessels simply because of the way the larger vessels operate.

I hope that we can have some reassurance from the Minister on the degree of supervision.

Finally, I want to refer to the point made by my hon. Friend the Member for Fife, East (Sir J. Gilmour). I know that negotiations are taking place at present and that the Government do not have the control in their own hands on when a decision will be made. But I have a slightly hollow feeling in the pit of my stomach, as I know many fishermen have, that a decision may be reached in the middle of December after the House has risen and when it will not be returning until the middle of January. There is great concern that whatever the Government may agree will not be subjected to the parliamentary scrutiny and questioning that such a momentous decision deserves.

If a decision is reached in December, or if it looks as though a decision will be reached when Parliament is not sitting, I hope that the Government will delay endorsing that agreement until they can JPEG 9 come back to the House and obtain the endorsement or otherwise of the House. Only in that way can we reassure the industry that we are genuinely and fully looking after their interests.
