DRAFT AVIATION SECURITY FUND (AMENDMENT) REGULATIONS 1979

10.30 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for Trade (Mr. Clinton Davis)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft Aviation Security Fund (Amendment) Regulations 1979.

The regulations amend the Aviation Security Fund Regulations 1978, which implemented in detail sections 1 and 2 of the Civil Aviation Act 1978.

Hon. Members will recall that section 1 of that Act sets up the Aviation Security Fund, out of which the Secretary of State may make payments reimbursing the costs of security measures falling to be made under the Protection of Aircraft Act 1973 and the Policing of Airports Act 1974. Section 2 empowers the Secretary of State to make regulations requiring aerodrome authorities to make contributions to the Fund.

When we debated the Bill some time ago, considerable emphasis was placed on the need for widespread consultation, which I naturally accepted. Indeed, I gave various commitments on that, and I believe that I have faithfully met those commitments. The consultation process with the industry has been helpful. It has been a continuing process, and it will go on. In regard to these amending regulations, my officials have consulted very widely with the industry.

I was anxious that Members of Parliament should also be kept fully in the picture. In order to achieve that objective, I sent copies of a consultation paper prepared by my officials to the right hon. Member for Orkney and Shetland (Mr. Grimond) and to the hon. Members for Chingford (Mr. Tebbit) and Tiverton (Mr. Maxwell-Hyslop), all of whom had been on the Committee, and also to my hon. Friend the Member for Feltham and Heston (Mr. Kerr). I offered to dis- 4 cuss the proposed amendments with them if they wished. In fact, there have been no further discussions. When I knew who the members of this Committee were, I sent them the consultation paper, which I had also put in the Library of the House.

I add in this connection that my officials have, of course, had consultations with representatives of the air transport industry about the consultation document and its contents. I have been guided in laying these amendment regulations by the results of those consultations. The Committee may find it helpful if I deal briefly with the proposed amendments.

The first two amendments and the fifth amendment are required simply to bring up to date the Aviation Security Fund Regulations 1978. The first two refer to the definition of an aerodrome. We have to keep the regulations as up to date as possible and the amendment of the two dates referred to achieves that objective.

The third amendment is the substantial one. It amends the existing sum of 80p to the new sum of 85p. The forecast outturn of expenditure from the Aviation Security Fund in the current year is just over £19 million, which is some £275,000 higher than the figure estimated when the levy rate for the current year was decided. That, too, of course, featured significantly in our debates. The estimated expenditure for next year is just over £24 million.

I expect that the fund will show a surplus of £1,115,000 in the current year. That is due to a higher growth rate than was originally predicted. There has been a substantial increase in the number of passengers carried during that period. This surplus has obviously, and I think justifiably, been taken into account in arriving at the levy rate of 85p for next year.

In deciding to propose the fourth change to the 1978 regulations, I was impressed by the arguments of operators of helicopters who were concerned about the apparent unfairness of the levy arrangements to them. The security measures which apply to oil rig helicopters emanating from the Protection of Aircraft Act 1973 are very small in relation to the general security arrangements required by the oil industry and the Department of Energy.

JPEG 5 Helicopter operators are faced with substantial expenditure on security in that respect, and enjoy little or no reimbursement from the Aviation Security Fund in respect of measures recommended by my Department. Other representatives of the industry were sympathetic, and agreed that the weight limit of 5 tonnes for the exemption of passengers arriving in small aircraft in regulation 2E of the 1978 regulations should be raised to 10 tonnes to overcome this problem.

The fifth change is necessary to remove the words which were put into the 1978 regulations merely to allow retrospection.

During the discussions in Committee last year, the hon. Member for Tiverton (Mr. Maxwell-Hyslop) was concerned about the effect of those regulations on certain transit passengers. My officials discussed this point fully with the representatives of the industry, who included representatives of the airports, the airlines, the travel trade and the airline users, and they were unanimous in their view that no amendment should be made. I wrote to the hon. Member setting out the arguments put forward and offering to discuss the issue with him if he wished. He has not indicated that he wishes to discuss them, and I assume from that that he is now satisfied.

I am pleased to say that the regulations currently in force have worked out very well indeed. Both I and my officials have had the fullest co-operation from the industry, with the result that we have been able to resolve a number of problems which emerged during the course of the year, including insurance cover for aerodrome authorities against liability which might arise from security measures, the planned introduction of x-rays on a widespread basis at our principal airports.

I am grateful indeed for the help which the industry has given on the estimating of expenditure and traffic growth and for its co-operation in ensuring that the fund arrangements have worked out so satisfactorily. Indeed, we have even been able to reduce the extra staff involved by one, which is a 50 per cent, drop in our original estimate.

I am sorry that this process has to go on at all, but the need for searching is a continuing need, regrettably, and perhaps one day the hopes that were expressed by 6 the noble lord, Lord Trefgarne, in a debate in the House of Lords yesterday, will be achieved, namely, that searching will not be rendered necessary. But I fear that that is in the far distant future.

DRAFT BRITISH AEROSPACE (DESIGN, DEVELOPMENT AND PRODUCTION OF CIVIL AIRCRAFT) (PAYMENTS) ORDER 1978.

10.58 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for Industry (Mr. Les Huckfield)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft British Aerospace (Design, Development and Production of Civil Aircraft) (Payments) Order 1978.

JPEG 13 This morning we are to consider an important step in British Aerospace's entry into Airbus Industrie. The Committee is invited to consider a draft order under section 45 of the Aircraft and Shipbuilding Industries Act 1977. The order specifies that my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State may make available up to £50 million assistance to British Aerospace under section 45. This is, of course, the maximum amount provided for in the section. The House will be invited in due course to approve a supplementary estimate for the payment of the £50 million. This is the first time use is to be made of section 45.

For the guidance of the Committee I wish to say a few words about the structure of Airbus Industrie. It is a consortium of airframe manufacturers from four European countries, including the United Kingdom, acting together in a risk-sharing partnership to supply the central Airbus organisation with airframe components. These components are assembled in Blagnac, near Toulouse, into Airbuses. The sales of the Airbus are directed from the central organisation, and this also acts as a single contact point for airline companies for service requirements, spares and so on.

Up to 1979 the full members of the Airbus Industrie organisation were Aerospatiale of France, with a 47·9 per cent, share, Deutsche Airbus of Germany, also with a 47·9 per cent, share, and CASA of Spain, with a much smaller 4·2 per cent, share. These shares defined the relative financial obligations of the members with regard to Airbus Industrie's costs. The operations of Airbus Industrie are controlled through a supervisory board. British Aerospace was previously represented on this board, but only as an observer.

As hon. Members will be aware, these companies are not the only manufacturers involved in the Airbus. The Dutch-German manufacturer, Fokker-VFW, is also involved as producer of control surfaces on the wings. Another major participating manufacturer is, of course, the American General Electric Company, which produces the CF6-50 engine for the Airbus. But these are not full partners.

Currently, the Airbus Industrie organisation has only one product—the Air- 14 bus A300. Further developments of that Airbus are in progress. However, the most important future product is the new smaller A310 Airbus, of around 230 seats. This is also to be available in two range variants.

At the end of last year there were over 180 orders and options for different versions of the A300, and 1978 was a particularly good year for the Airbus. At the Farnborough air show last year Airbus Industrie reported that it had sold 37 per cent, of all wide-bodied aircraft sold up to that time in 1978. For the future the total market requirement for these categories of aircraft, that is medium range wide body aircraft, could be as much as 1,500 units by 1990, and we expect the Airbus family to make substantial inroads into this sector.

It was the prospect of the new A310 Airbus that led to the recent proposals for the change of status of British Aerospace from a sub-contractor to a full partner.

As I am sure hon. Members will understand, there were detailed and complex matters concerning manufacture and financing of the Airbus components which needed to be looked at, together with the role and status of British Aerospace as a partner in the Airbus Industrie organisation. These were matters for the industrialists—that is, British Aerospace, Aerospatiale and Deutsche Airbus. A agreement was reached last August, subject to the approval of the Governments concerned. In due course the agreement was signed on 29 November last.

Hon. Members will realise, I am sure, that the details of this agreement are essentially a matter for British Aerospace. The main points in the agreement are as follows. First, it provides for British Aerospace to become a full member of Airbus Industries from 1 January 1979, with a 20 per cent, share and appropriate representation on the supervisory board. Secondly, the agreement provides for changes in the voting arrangements of Airbus Industrie so that major decisions require more than an 80 per cent, majority. Thirdly, there is to be continued participation by British Aerospace in the A300 programme and a substantial share in the A310 and other future programmes. Fourthly—there has been much discusison on this—along with the other parties to the agreement, British JPEG 15 Aerospace has agreed not to participate in competing aircraft programmes without the agreement of the other parties.

The full voting rights that I have mentioned apply for British Aerospace immediately to the A310 project and to questions common to the A300 programme and the A310 programme. However, for a transitional period up to the delivery of the 150th A300 aircraft, or at the very latest by 1 August 1981, the present 75 per cent, majority rule will continue to apply. But it will apply only to questions specifically related to the development of the A300 M2/B4 programme. Additionally, it should be noted that this 75 per cent, rule will apply during the transitional phase only to those situations in which the A300 is in competition with aircraft fitted with British engines.

The French and German partners have indicated that if British Airways should place an order with Airbus Industries, the "80 per cent, rule" would immediately take full effect.

It should be realised that all of these decisions are taken as part of a consultative process, with a lot of discussion and mutual agreement among the partners and sub-contractors. We feel that this is the most sensible way for an industrial concern such as Airbus Industrie to proceed.

Governments are, of course, involved in all this. This stems from the financial backing provided by European Governments regarding the discharge of the manufacturers' obligations to Airbus Industrie—for example, concerning the development and production expenditure associated with the new aircraft.

With British Aerospace's entry into Airbus Industrie, the United Kingdom Government is now a party to these arrangements and is represented on the intergovernmental committee. The document setting out the principles of this co-operation between the Governments is available in the Library of the House.

Hon. Members on both sides of the Committee will realise that the entry of British Aerospace into Airbus Industrie is a very significant step forward for the aircraft industry in Europe. Opposition Members will know even better than I that the giants of the aircraft industry are 16 currently on the other side of the Atlantic. They have 90 per cent, of the Western world's market. I believe that Europe is capable of winning a fair share of this market for her products. The United Kingdom aerospace industry is certainly the largest in Europe. I believe that by joining with our European colleagues, we shall form one of the largest and most effective industrial groups in the civil airliner market to take us into the 1980s and 1990s.

Most immediately, for British Aerospace, the agreement reached will enable it to confirm its position regarding the production of A300 wings and, just as importantly, achieve a substantial share of the new A310 Airbus. For this, the workers in British Aerospace have already paid tribute. It means, of course, that the current employment of 2,000 or so on the Airbus work will be expanded. There will also be an opportunity for British equipment suppliers. I see no reason why they, too, should not be just as successful.

I believe that British Aerospace has a soundly-based financial record. Its first set of accounts show increased sales, increased exports and increased profits reaching £65 million in 1977. The order book since British Aerospace came into being has increased by around £1,000 million and is still rising. The Government are determined that British Aerospace's position among the world's leaders shall be maintained. We are therefore ready and willing to make available the funds necessary for the heavy investment to which British Aerospace is committed as a result of joining Airbus Industrie.

British Aerospace's membership of Airbus Industrie will require additional investment to finance work already in hand on the A300 programme and for work on the new A310 Airbus. British Aerospace estimates that by 1983 it will need to invest a further £250 million at 1978 prices. We believe that this is not an opportunity we can afford to let slip through our fingers. That is why we decided to agree to British Aerospace's request for assistance in order to improve the immediate return on its investment.

If the order is approved, my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State proposes to make available £50 million under section JPEG 17 45 to British Aerospace in connection with its membership of Airbus Industrie. The money will be paid when the necessary Supplementary Estimate has been approved.

British Aerospace is satisfied that, taking account of this assistance and of the wider commercial and industrial benefits, the prospective return on its own much more substantial investment in Airbus Industrie is adequate. It is intended that the money should be paid initially as a grant, but this will be subject to two important conditions. First, the dividends which British Aerospace pay on public dividend capital may be increased if membership of Airbus Industrie proves to be sufficiently remunerative. British Aerospace has already accepted this. Secondly, my right hon. Friend will have the right, after consulting the corporation, to convert the £50 million assistance wholly or partly into loan, or public dividend capital, or a mixture of the two.

Hon. Members can be assured that through our providing the money in this way the necessary financial resources for joining Airbus Industrie are thus available to the corporation. At the same time, the Government will be able to maintain, through the setting of appropriate financial targets, the necessary degree of financial discipline over the whole field of British Aerospace's operations.

We believe that to do this for British Aerospace will help to consolidate the airframe side of the European aircraft industry, and that it is not only in the national interest but is consistent with financial objectives if we assist by providing £50 million in this way. There is no doubt that the conditions necessary for the payment to be made under section 45 are met and can be met.

It is intended to make the money available to British Aerospace before the end of the current fiscal year. To enable this to be done the corporation's cash limit which was previously set for 1978–79 has been changed. The new limit is £62 million, compared with the original limit of £12 million.

When British Aerospace's cash limit was set, the Government did not, of course, know what new aircraft projects, if any, the corporation would be undertaking during the year. The Public Ex- 18 penditure White Paper published in January last year explained that British Aerospace's forecasts projected the requirements for external finance of the vesting companies based on a consolidation of their current business.
