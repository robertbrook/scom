legislation DRAFT EUROPEAN COMMUNITIES (SERVICES OF LAWYERS) ORDER 1978

10.30 a.m.

The Solicitor-General (Mr. Peter Archer): I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft European Communities (Services of Lawyers) Order 1978.

The Chairman: I am advised that one does not have to gain to have a financial interest. I read law, but I have not practised.

The Solicitor-General: In view of that comment, I should declare an interest in the light of certain eventualities which may or may not take place.

The purpose of the order is to implement our obligations under the European Communities Council Directive No. 77/249 which the House discussed on 12th November 1976. It relates to the provision of services on a temporary basis in one member State by lawyers qualified in another.

Under article 59 of the Treaty of Rome member States have committed themselves to liberalising services within the Community. The directive is a limited instance of that commitment. It is concerned not with mutual recognition of qualifications, with lawyers establishing themselves in practice in other States, or with the right of establishment, but with visits for specific business.

Broadly, the directive requires each member State to recognise as lawyers entitled to practise law within their territories certain categories of lawyers from other States. For that purpose, each State is required to modify those rules and practices of its national law which reserve the provision of certain legal services to people holding certain professional qualifications.

When we discussed the directive in 1976 there was general agreement that any relaxation of restrictions among lawyers might give rise to problems which do not Col. 4 always arise in relation to most other professions. The human anatomy is similar in the United Kingdom or in Germany, so a doctor qualified in one country may be permitted to practise in another subject only to allowing for differences in structure of the medical profession. But legal systems are different, so a lawyer qualified under one legal system may have no expertise in another. The right hon. and learned Member for Hertfordshire, East (Sir D. Walker-Smith) in the debate on the directive, said that in a sense the practice of law in the United Kingdom and the practice of law in Germany were two separate professions.

The purpose of the directive is to liberalise restrictions as far as possible. The intention is that, where necessary or appropriate, members of the public should have access to advice from or representation by lawyers whose principal expertise is in a different legal system, and that lawyers should have the opportunity of exercising their talents outside the territorial jurisdiction of their own courts.

The order implements those provisions in relation to lawyers from other member States practising in the United Kingdom. Other States are making similar provisions.

Articles 5 and 8 of the order enact the relaxation. Article 5 provides:

"No enactment or rule of law or practice shall prevent an EEC lawyer from providing any service in relation to any proceedings, whether civil or criminal"

in England, Wales or Scotland, subject to certain conditions. Broadly, one can say that deals with what solicitors know as contentious business.

Article 8 makes similar provision in relation to the drawing up or preparing of certain classes of document. Therefore, one can say that refers to non-contentious business, although only a very limited sector of it.

The removal of restrictions in article 5 is subject to a number of limitations. First, it is subject to the provision that the visiting lawyer shall be instructed by, and act in conjunction with a lawyer who is entitled to practice before the court in question and who could properly provide that service. The home lawyer with whom he would be acting obviously would have a high degree of control. If he found that the visiting lawyer was not conducting Image Col. 5 the matter properly, he could withdraw from the case and the visiting lawyer could not proceed unless he found another home lawyer.

Article 6 ensures that it shall be clear in what capacity the visiting lawyer is acting. For example, in England he cannot act both as a barrister and a solicitor, and in Scotland in the equivalent capacities, and he cannot alternate between the two capacities in the same proceedings.

Under article 5, hon. Members will notice that an EEC lawyer is not prevented from appearing

"by reason only that he is not an advocate, barrister or solicitor".

But he may be prevented from appearing for any other proper reason—for example, incompetence or unfamiliarity with the English language.

Article 8—the non-contentious business provision—is of limited effect in any event because, even now, only a limited number of advisory and drafting services are reserved to lawyers qualified here. Such matters normally require expert knowledge of domestic law. Article 8, is, of course, limited to the preparation of documents about interests in personal estate.

Article 9 provides that the EEC lawyer is not to be entitled to draft documents relating to interests in land or to the estates of deceased persons.

Those are the liberalising provisions and their qualifications.

Article 7 prohibits an EEC lawyer in salaried employment from providing services on behalf of his employer, when acting with an advocate or barrister, which that advocate or barrister could not properly provide if he were simply employed.

Article 10 makes legal aid available in cases involving representation or advice by an EEC lawyer where appropriate, but I cannot believe that is likely to be appropriate in a wide sector of cases.

Article 2 relates to the definition of the expression "EEC lawyer". It does not include all types of lawyers in the Community. It is confined to those with rights of audience or representation in their respective member States. For example, it does not include the French conseil juridique whose capacity is purely advisory.

Col. 6 The article is drafted to give a particular designation to that part of the United Kingdom to which it applies.

Articles 12 to 17 relate to the verification of the status of visiting lawyers and to disciplinary matters. Those hon. Members who participated in the earlier discussions in 1976 will remember that article 4 of the directive laid down a basic framework of professional conduct. It provided that a lawyer visiting for purposes of representation shall be subject to the rules of the host State and that a lawyer visiting for other purposes shall be subject to the rules of professional conduct of the country in which he qualified. That was not felt to be wholly sufficient. Therefore, article 4(4) of the directive subjects visiting lawyers to certain rules operative in the host country even when they are not participating in proceedings here.

This order does not seek to spell out those disciplinary matters which are applicable in the United Kingdom. In effect, it is content to leave them to the appropriate disciplinary authorities here and simply confers jurisdiction on the disciplinary authorities.

That is the scheme of the order. We are under an obligation to implement the directive before 26th March 1979. Hon. Members will not be surprised to hear that it was not felt proper to proceed by way of a designation order and regulations. The House of Lords Select Committee on the European Communities said that the appropriate way to proceed was by Order in Council under section 2(2) of the European Communities Act. The Government felt it right to do that, and to use the affirmative resolution procedure. The draft has been submitted with a memorandum to the Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments and that Committee has approved it.

The order was drafted in full consultation with the United Kingdom professional bodies. The problem is not to protect United Kingdom lawyers from competition. I believe that they are not afraid to compete with any other legal profession. Nor, in fairness, do I believe that the legal professions of other European member States are concerned to poach work in the United Kingdom. The problem was simply to ensure that the public should have access to the widest Image Col. 7 range of expertise, whilst ensuring that they should not be exposed to offers of services by those not competent to provide them. I believe that the directive as implemented in this order achieves that purpose.

10.41 a.m.

Mr. Percy Grieve: I declare an interest as I think that most of us in the Committee this morning must declare an interest, because I am a practising member of the Bar.

I welcome this order. It seems to me that it deals with the problem which we have to face under the Community regulations in a conservative manner. It provides very proper and appropriate safeguards for discipline, and it limits, in effect, the scope of the exercise of their professional expertise of those coming here who are not qualified in English law to aid those who are qualified in English law. That seems to me to put in a pithy form the essence of these regulations.

We in this country have always adopted a far more liberal approach to the exercise by foreigners in our midst of professional qualifications than any other country that I know. Certainly, for many years there have practised at the English Bar in London distinguished foreigners who have qualified and been called to the English Bar. It would not be necessary for me to recite names, but there must be well known to many hon. Members some of the distinguished Frenchmen who have been called to the English Bar and who, over the years, have fulfilled a very important role in cases of conflict of laws involving French law arising in the English courts. The same has applied to Spanish lawyers and, to my certain knowledge, to Italian lawyers.

This has been a far more liberal approach than was ever applied, until we entered the European Communities, by the other countries. For instance, in France, although one might have qualified as a French barrister, it was not possible to be called to the Paris Bar and to practise in French courts unless one was a Frenchman. Before we entered the Community, there were distinguished English lawyers in Paris practising the law, but they could practise only in an advisory capacity, whereas in London French law- Col. 8 yers, having been called to the English Bar, were able to appear in our courts.

That situation has been ended by the overall arrangements which now prevail within the Community. It is possible for a Frenchman to be called to the English Bar and to practise here, and for an Englishman to be called to the French Bar and to practise there. This order, of course, does not deal with that situation; it deals with a person who is not qualified in English law in England. I imagine that the same rules mutatis mutandis will prevail in other countries. It seems to me that there obviously has to be a limitation upon the scope of the exercise of their abilities by such persons.

On the other hand, there may well arise a real need for such services in an ever-increasingly integrated Community. I mean integrated not in any constitutional sense but in the sense in which the mere fact of the existence of the Community is likely to give rise over the years to more and more cases which have a Community law or a foreign law element. In this context, by "foreign law" I mean the law of one of the other countries of the Community.

The Solicitor-General, in his brief and cogent speech, said that, in his view, there would be little call for the legal aid provision. I am not so sure, because it seems to me that it is precisely in the realm of family law, as well as in that of commercial law, that we are likely to see the position arise in our courts—or in the drawing-up of legal instruments in this country—in which the services of somebody from another country within the Community might be needed. That might be when a marriage between an Englishman and a Frenchwoman, or an Italian woman and an Englishman, and so on, has gone on the rocks and problems have arisen that require to be dealt with in court, for example, over property and the custody of children.

In such cases, there may well be a call for legal aid. At any rate, it is provided for, and therefore, while many cases will involve substantial sums of money and legal aid would not arise, I suspect that we shall see article 10 put into operation in some cases in the Family Division involving family problems.

I welcome the order and hope that it will meet with the Committee's approval.

He would then have to instruct a United Kingdom barrister and, again assuming that the case involved the foreign element that made the attendance of the EEC lawyer necessary, the second advocate. I once had the experience of representing somebody who was on a disciplinary charge under the United States Army regulations in Germany. It was the very interesting experience of finding myself dealing with a case before a completely different tribunal. The defending officer allocated to the case had been used to having the function of acting both as the investigator and as the advocate. We settled very rapidly and happily into our roles; I was the instructing solicitor and he dealt with the advocacy. The consequence proved to a large extent the merits of the division of the profession. However, it seems to me unnecessary for there to have to be three lawyers in a case where matters may be relatively simple so far as the EEC element is concerned, yet which requires the assistance of an EEC lawyer, who may have been practising in and regularly appearing in British courts. It is not possible to have something falling short of qualifying as a barrister in Britain, something which would enable an EEC lawyer who was regularly appearing here to dispense with the need for a second British barrister to appear in the case, provided that—and this would be necessary, I think—a British firm of solicitors was involved in the case if it was taking place in the British courts? I do not want to see the order used, in Col. 10 effect, as a further restrictive practice for the benefit of the profession.

For those reasons, I could not possibly give my stamp of imprimatur, for what it is worth, to the order.

Mr. Ian Percival: I declare my interest as a lawyer. I also declare my interest in the wide implications of the order for the lay client. I was glad that the right hon. and learned Solicitor-General stressed that matter. We are concerned not with a carving up exercise, but with ensuring that the lay client receives competent advice and representation.

People, even in our own profession, sometimes forget that the reserved services about which we are talking are reserved to people by qualification, the qualification being designed to ensure that they are competent. That is fundamental both to our idea of qualifications and to the proposition that certain tasks are reserved to people who hold such qualifications. It is quite wrong to talk about this matter as though it were a carving up exercise. It is nothing of the kind. I think that the Solicitor-General would agree that most of those who practise in the professions are just Col. 14 as keen as any layman to ensure that we adhere to the right approach in considering this matter.

I was happy to know that throughout the preparation of the agreement on the directive and the preparation of this order there has been very close co-operation and consultation with the two branches of the profession and that they are reasonably content with what has emerged.

I was also glad that the Solicitor-General made the point so firmly that, when considering the lawyers of Europe, we must recognise that we are talking about different professions in the different countries. After all, what is a profession? It is a body of people who have a particular qualification, that qualification making them competent to give advice and representation. The legal systems of Europe are so different that those qualifications must of necessity be different. Therefore, by definition, they are not the same professions. They are all lawyers, because they are all concerned with the law of their respective countries, but that is the only way in which they can be said to be the same.

It is important to recognise that an underlying assumption is made in the directive from which the order springs—namely, that the "activities of lawyers" are so similar in all EEC countries that a lawyer in one country should be entitled to be "recognised" for "the purpose of pursuing the activities of lawyers " in another country. Those words come from the directive.

For the reasons I have indicated, I believe that that is not just a false assumption but a very dangerous one. It is based, I think, on the belief that a lawyer is a lawyer is a lawyer, just as a doctor is a doctor is a doctor. But it is a complete fallacy. A hernia is a hernia is a hernia and requires the same kind of expertise and technology wherever it is suffered and wherever it is treated. Likewise with engineers, a bridge is a bridge is a bridge wherever it is being built and requires the same kind of expertise and technology.

I am not saying that the expertise or technology is identical, but the problem is the same. But the same does not apply to lawyers. The problem with which a lawyer is faced is not by any Image Col. 15 manner of means the same, unless one puts it in the very general terms that he has to advise his client on what the law is and what his rights are under that law. That is common to all of us, but it is the only thing that is.

One need only draw attention to the enormous difference between those countries in which the system of law is what lawyers know as civil law and those in which it is a common law system. They are totally different. On the criminal side, there is the inquisitorial system in most European countries and ours. They are quite different, and they require quite different training and competence.

If one approaches the matter as I think one ought to, and as I think most lawyers are entitled to approach it—that when we are talking about qualification we are talking about competence—it becomes quite obvious that competence in one country, entitling a person to be one of those people to whom services are reserved in that country, need not necessarily be any sort of qualification to entitle him to do it in another country. Therefore, if one is thinking, as one ought to be thinking, in terms of a comparable competence, it must be linked to qualification.

The weakness of this order is that that qualification does not come into it at all in that sense. There is no reference to a lawyer being properly qualified to do what he is entitled to do in another country. That is a great weakness. I beheve that the Government themselves argued for those propositions. If so, I commend them for doing so by saying gently that I am sorry that they did not stick to them. I say it gently because I appreciate that one cannot always have all that one wants in this world. I think the belief is that the dangers envisaged are in fact covered by a different method here, by the provision that there must be a qualified lawyer acting together with the one who comes to appear in a court in this country.

I am not at all clear how that will actually work out. It is a startling proposition that a Rechtsanwalt, which I think is the German equivalent of a barrister, should stand up in an English court and endeavour to present a case. If any Rechtsanwalt should think that I am being rude to them, let me put it the Col. 16 other way round and say that it is no less startling a proposition that any one of us here should stand up in a German court and try to present a case according to German law. It may be that the very absurdity of such a proposition will deter people from doing it, so that in fact, for very practical reasons, we shall have what is envisaged here, that if it is thought necessary to bring in a German, French or Italian lawyer in conjunction with an English lawyer, the English lawyer will take the lead.

I think the Government would have liked it to be worded so that the lawyer from the other country should be under the control of the lawyer of the host country. I certainly agree with that concept. It was felt that it was not necessary to insist on the word "control", and for practical reasons that is probably right. The practical reasons would produce the same situations if that word was included. I hope so, because unless this provision has the effect that we all expect and hope it will have, there will be a mess, and that does nobody any good. Ridiculous situations arising in the courts do nobody any good. From top to bottom, it does the layman, the system and the lawyers no good.

With those reservations, we accept that this was the best that could be done. We hope that it will work, and that a close watch will be kept so that if anything appears to be going wrong we can take early steps to correct it.

Referring to the point raised by the hon. I agree with the Government on article 6, and one should, at all events at this stage, make clear that the situation I believe he envisaged cannot arise.
