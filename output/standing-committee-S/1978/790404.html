Sir John Gilmour: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the Fishing Boats (Faroe Islands) Designation Order 1979 (S.I. No. 256).

10.41 a.m.

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. E. S. Bishop): The order before the Committee today is very narrow, and I am sure you, Miss Fookes, would draw my attention to order if I were to go beyond its bounds. It relates to interim arrangements for fishing by Faroese vessels in United Kingdom waters. It is precisely the same in form as the Fishing Boats (Faroe Islands) Designation (No. 4) Order 1978. The sole difference is in the duration of the order.

The latter order was discussed in Standing Committee on 24 January 1979. As I explained to the Committee at that time, the intention of the order was to give effect to a Community agreement that arrangements for reciprocal fishing with third countries should be rolled forward for two months from the end of 1978. Thus, the Faroese were allowed to fish in United Kingdom waters until 28 February 1979 on the same basis as they had fished in 1978 but taking account of the revised total allowable catches. In the case of Norway, Sweden and Spain, the 1978 designation orders had not been subject to a specific time limit and therefore did not require extension.

We had hoped at that stage that fishing agreements with third countries for Col. 4 1979 might be finalised sufficiently early for us to be able to lay consolidated orders before the House by the beginning of March. In the event, this assessment proved too optimistic, and the Council of Fisheries Ministers meeting in Brussels on 19 and 20 February agreed that 1978 arrangements should be rolled forward for a further month until the end of March. The 1979 designation order therefore has the sole purpose of substituting the expiry, date of 31 March for that of 28 February.

Having dealt with the interim situation, it may be of some assistance to hon. Members if I now say a few words about the EEC—Faroese agreement for 1979 which will supersede the measures in force during the first three months of this year. Negotiations between the Commission and the Faroese were completed at the end of February. The outcome was disappointing in a number of respects. In particular, there has been a further reduction in Community quotas of cod and haddock, the species in which the United Kingdom is most interested. Only 5,000 tonnes of these species will be available as against 7,000 tonnes last year. But almost all of this will be allocated to the United Kingdom. The reduction in the amount of saithe, a species in which we also have a substantial interest, is even more severe.

Despite these reductions the Government would be reluctant to forfeit the available fishing opportunities off Faroes, which offer access to relatively scarce white fish resources, unless they had to be purchased at too great a cost. The price for our access to Faroese waters has been the offer of reciprocal opportunities for Faroese fishermen in waters under the jurisdiction of the EEC member States. It is important to note, however, that a significant proportion of the most valuable quotas are fished in Danish waters and the majority of the quotas offered in United Kingdom waters are of low-value industrial species.

The most valuable offer from waters around the United Kingdom is of mackerel. The Faroese will be able to fish 10,000 tonnes in the North Sea and 30,000 tonnes off the West of Scotland. This represents a significant quantity of an increasingly important species, but again it is important to bear in mind that, if Faroese fishing were to cease, not all the Image Col. 5 mackerel the Faroese might otherwise have taken would be taken by British fishermen. The French and German fishermen, who would lose substantial fishing opportunites at Faroe, might divert to fishing this stock more heavily.

That is an important point, that this is to our advantage. It is in the interest of both the EEC in general and the United Kingdom to relieve pressure on the waters round our coasts by seeking opportunities for catching white fish in distant waters. In this context the agreement with the Faroes has a contribution to make even though it is not as advantageous as we would have wished.

After those few words of explanation I will deal with any questions which arise.
