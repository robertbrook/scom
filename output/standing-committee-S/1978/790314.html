GRANTS FOR GUARANTEES OF BANK LOANS (EXTENSION OF PERIOD) ORDER 1979

10.30 a.m.

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. E. S. Bishop)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the Grants for Guarantees of Bank Loans (Extension of Period Order 1979.

I do not think that I need to detain the Committee long on this order, although it is important. The order was made on 25 January 1979, and a copy was laid before the House on 6 February.

The effect of the order which is very limited, is to empower the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food to pay grant in respect of a guarantee for a bank loan to an agricultural or horticultural business given during the five years up to 31 March 1984. The Minister's existing powers relate to guarantees given up to 31 March 1979.

Hon. Members may like to be reminded that the original scheme for Government-supported loan guarantees was introduced in 1964 and was confined to horticultural businesses. It was extended to agriculture in 1965, at first under the Appropriation Acts, the whole being consolidated in the Agriculture Act 1967. That Act provides for the qualifying period to be extended for periods of up to five years by orders made by the Minister, subject to the approval of the Treasury. The orders do not, however, come into effect unless they are approved by this House.

The arrangements apply to all parts of the United Kingdom. They are operated through the Agricultural Credit Corporation, which gives guarantees to those farmers, growers and co-operatives which, although creditworthy, do not have enough security to obtain bank loans in the normal way. For its services, the corporation charges a fee of up to 2½ per cent, a year of the sum under guarantee.

The question of Government grant arises only if the borrower fails to repay 4 the bank loan and the bank claims on the guarantor under the guarantee.

The rate of grant is regularly reviewed. At present the Government have agreed to meet a maximum of 85 per cent, of any loan in default. The bank bears 5 per cent, of the default and the ACC bears the remaining 10 per cent.

Loans totalling £5½ million for well over 300 farmers are at present being guaranteed under these arrangements. Although these loans represent only a very small proportion of the total bank lending to agriculture—now over £1,900 million— the guarantee facility is important for those who use it.

It is encouraging to note that whilst on average about 75 new guarantees have been given in each of the past four years, about 50 farmers each year who have had a guarantee for a period have been able to dispense with it and obtain loans in the usual way. Since the scheme started the total number of guarantees which have been given is 688, covering a sum of £9 million. There have been only 42 cases of default, leading to total grant payments of £262,000.

Loan guarantees for co-operatives were of only minor significance in the early years of the scheme, but an arrangement for marketing groups, introduced in 1972, has proved popular. Under it, on the recommendation of the Central Council for Agricultural and Horticultural Cooperation, the Ministry pays the ACC's fees in approved cases. Over £1 million of guarantees have been given under this arrangement since it was introduced.

Finally, I should mention that there is a link between the order and the Farm and Horticulture Development Regulations. Under Directive 72/159/EEC, loan guarantees are one of the forms of assistance which must be provided for farmers with approved development plans. The United Kingdom implements this provision through the present loan guarantee arrangements, whose continuation depends on the approval of the order. I commend the order for the Committee's favourable consideration.

Mr. Jerry Wiggin

It is a grant only when there is a default, JPEG 5 and I think that the Minister of State will agree that the record is exceptional in this matter.

The original scheme was set up some 15 years ago under the Agriculture and Horticulture Act 1964. It was subsequently extended to include agricultural schemes under the Agriculture Act 1967

I have a quite important question to ask the Minister. Since the passage of the Agriculture Act 1967 the Ministry has exercised a right which it may or may not have to contain the discretion of the Agricultural Credit Corporation as to the items on which it allows its facilities to be exercised. For example, in 1972 the Ministry came to an agreement with the ACC that it would not grant-aid processing plant which might or might not be on a farm. This did not mean that it could not grant-aid a potato store, but it could not grant-aid—I should say " could not guarantee the loan on," for I am falling into the trap of using the very words which I said we should not be using—the processing plant for washing, preparing and packing the same potatoes.

I do not question the wisdom of that decision, which was taken a long time ago and which has been accepted, I think, by the industry and by the corporation. But I should like to know the statutory basis on which such directions are made.

Since that time the Brussels Commission has seen fit to interpret this innocent arrangement as a grant in aid. I do not see it in those terms, but Brussels has, and as a result the ACC is no longer allowed to lend on the purchase of dairy cows or, in general terms, pigs and poultry. Therefore, its sphere of operations is rapidly becoming constrained. We want to know how far this is likely to go and whether it is a matter on which the House should be consulted.

The Minister will be aware that under the price review recommendations currently placed by the Commission before the Council of Ministers, and subject to negotiations and the approval of the European Parliament and others, the future of capital grant aid in Europe is in question. As the original proposals were written, capital grants will cease to exist forthwith. I am certain that the Government will not be prepared to accept that, and will fight to see that it does not happen. Furthermore, I am sure that they will fight to see that any counter-proposals are not discriminatory—levelled, for example, 6 solely at the larger British farmer. But the ACC must be concerned about this matter. In many cases it administers its schemes in conjunction with the farm and horticultural schemes. Therefore, any proposal to abolish the farm and horticultural schemes would have an enormous effect on the ACC.

It would be right to pay some tribute to the efficiency of the Agricultural Credit Corporation, the board of which is chaired by my former colleague, Mr. Anthony Stodart. I think that everyone agrees that it has been doing an exceptionally good job. The Minister has told us that 333 individuals are currently borrowing some £5.6 million, and no fewer than 19 cooperatives are obtaining guarantees of some £900,000, making £6½ million in all. The total administrative staff are five. There is a lesson there about allowing free enterprise—which is really what it is— to operate such schemes.

I had a somewhat lower figure than the Minister for the total default over 15 years, but I bow to his superior knowledge and accept that £260,000 from a mere 42 cases over 15 years is a very low price to pay for the provision of some £6 million of credit, which must be earning more in six months for those who benefit by this than has been lost in 15 long years. Under the Act the Ministry is allowed to provide up to £240,000 in any one year, and yet it has scarcely needed that sum over the whole period of the existence of the ACC.

The average sum is currently guaranteed to the tune of about £17,000 per individual, so the schemes are quite substantial. I gather that the average is now rising to £20,000 or more. One can therefore say that this exercise has undoubtedly been enormously successful.

I have a philosophical thoughts as to whether, in view of the exceptional record, the joint stock banks are truly doing their full job. Why should such an organisation be needed to find a sum of over £6 million, when there is a marvellous default record and presumably a good return? If one is paying 2½ per cent., even if one can perhaps get a bit back off one's overdraft rate from the bank as a result of the credit guarantee, this shows that, given the expertise available in the agricultural world, and I strongly suspect, in the whole small business world, the way in which the banks exercise their discretion is not always as free and competitive as I should like.

JPEG 7 I should expect to be ruled out of order, Mr. Wells, if I were to go too much further, but why should this matter be limited to agriculture? There seems to be a lesson here to be learnt in other spheres. I shall not pursue that line of thought, because I see that you are starting to frown.

In the wake of the Crown Agents affair, the Treasury is properly considering all the Government's open-ended liabilities. There is a liability here of up to £240,000 a year. It is right and proper that the Treasury should examine that liability. But I hope that before the Government seek to interfere in any way further with the ACC they will not only bear in mind its exceptional record over a very long period but will take into account those who are responsible for operating the scheme and leave it to them. There is no doubt that they can be trusted.

The Opposition strongly recommend the Government to leave the ACC alone and let it get on with its task, as it has been doing so successfully. I am glad to say that it will be doing so for at least the next five years as a result of the passing of this order.
