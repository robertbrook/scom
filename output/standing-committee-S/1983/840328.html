legislation RATES (REGIONAL RATE) ORDER (NORTHERN IRELAND) 1984

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Adam Butler): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Rates (Regional Rate) Order (Northern Ireland) 1984.

The order was made by the Department of Finance and Personnel for Northern Ireland on 27 February 1984 and laid before the House on 6 March. It is short and uncomplicated. It has the dual purpose of fixing the regional rate for Northern Ireland for the year beginning 1 April 1984 and also the amount by which that rate is to be reduced in respect of domestic hereditaments. That would usually constitute the totality of my opening speech in a debate such as this, but it may be of use if I give a little background and explanation to help the Committee in its consideration of the matter.

The total rate liability of ratepayers in Northern Ireland is made up of two components. First, there is a district rate, which represents the contribution towards those services provided by the 26 local authorities, such as parks and cemeteries, recreation, cleansing and sanitation. Secondly, there is a regional rate, representing the contribution towards the cost of services now administered by central Government, but which before local government reorganisation in 1973 were administered by local authorities. That includes such matters as roads, housing, education, planning, health, water and sewerage and personal social services and child care.

When responsibility for the major local authority services was taken over by central Government in 1973 considerations of operational and administrative efficiency dictated that they be integrated with existing services already under central Government control. It therefore became difficult—and within the conventions of central Government accounting, unnecessary—to separate expenditure on the individual services, which before 1973 had been rate-borne. Yet it was demonstrably right and proper that ratepayers in the Province continued to contribute towards the cost of those services in the same way as ratepayers in every other part of the United Kingdom.

The Government were at all times seized of the necessity to ensure that determination of the regional rate took proper account of special circumstances affecting the Province, did justice to Northern Ireland ratepayers and yet did not cause an excessively high share of the cost of the services to fall upon the generality of United Kingdom taxpayers. But, understandably, dissatisfaction about the initial mode of fixing the regional rate was often given expression by elected representatives, ratepayers' interest groups and individuals. This dissatisfaction arose not so much from informed judgment that levels were too high as perhaps Col. 4 from a lack of clarity about the basis for the annual determination.

There were several attempts at reform over the years, but these did not lead to radical change, chiefly because of the complexity of the matter and the apparent elusiveness of an objective, quantitative method that would satisfy all requirements. Nevertheless officials of my Department persevered in their work on the problem, in which they had the helpful and constructive advice and participation of elected representatives from the Association of Local Authorities in Northern Ireland, and in January 1983 published a consultation document proposing a new method for fixing the regional rate. Copies of that document were placed in the Library of the House in January 1983 and copies were also sent individually to hon. Members representing Northern Ireland constituencies.

Essential features of the new method—which was used for the first time to fix the regional rate for 1983–84—are: first, that it establishes a clear relationship between estimated expenditure on "rate relevant" services and the fixing of the regional rate; secondly, that it provides for the calculation of notional rate support grant, by analogy with practice in other parts of the United Kingdom, to decide how much of rate relevant expenditure should be regarded as met by the taxpayer rather than the ratepayer. This takes account, among other things, of special features such as concentration of social problems and other components of disadvantage particular to Northern Ireland; thirdly, that the new method takes account of particular factors which make invalid the direct comparison of certain services in Northern Ireland with the analogous services in England and Wales. A good example is the police services in the Province. It would clearly be unfair to attribute to the ratepayer the whole of that burden because of the special security circumstances. Therefore the majority of that expenditure is set aside when calculating the regional rate. Finally, it provides a basis for fixing the regional rate that is more readily understood and transparent. Elected representatives and ratepayers who wish are now able to see for themselves the basis of the charges they are asked to bear and judge for themselves the fairness of the determination.

This is the second time that this new methodology has been applied in respect of the year to come. The level of the overall Northern Ireland rate increase, that is of the regional and district rates combined is determined by the level of rate relevant expenditure in Northern Ireland and by the level of rate support grant for 1984–85. The decisions on public expenditure on rate relevant services were made in the context of the 1983 public expenditure survey, and details were announced by my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Northern Ireland in December 1983. The level of notional rate support grant at 84.3 per cent is by a considerable margin the highest of any region of the United Kingdom. The rate support grant levels in England, Scotland and Wales are 51.9 per cent, 60.2 per cent and 69.1 per cent respectively. The figure of 84.3 per cent illustrates very clearly the special circumstances and special needs of Northern Ireland.

The result of the calculation based on the 1984–85 expenditure and on rate support grant of 84.3 per cent is an increase of 6.7 per cent in the overall Northern Ireland rate. The relative contribution to the overall Northern Ireland poundage of the district and regional rates is Image Col. 5 determined by the size of the average district rate. When the increase in the average district rate is below the increase in overall Northern Ireland rate, as happened this year, the regional rate increase is automatically higher than the increase in the overall average Northern Ireland rate. For 1984–85 the regional rate will increase by 7.8 per cent and this is provided for in the statutory rule being considered by the Committee.

Domestic ratepayers benefit from domestic rate aid grant which reduces the poundage payable by domestic ratepayers, to a level similar to that in Great Britain. For 1984–85 this grant has been maintained at 12p in the pound, and provision has been made for this in the statutory rule.

In a debate on an appropriation order in March 1983, the right hon. Member for Down, South (Mr. Powell) gave, I am glad to say, a broad welcome to the new system, but he expressed a desire that an explanatory document should accompany each rate demand. We have not lost sight of that proposal, but there are significant difficulties because of the short time available to determine the regional rate after all the necessary data have become available. I cannot make any specific announcement about this today, but I am considering the proposal further with my colleague, the Under-Secretary with responsibility for the environment, and I shall inform the right hon. Gentleman and any other interested hon. Member of the outcome. It is reasonable that we should see how far we can explain to individual ratepayers how the system works. I am not sure whether we can go as far as drawing attention to particular figures, as would be the case in Great Britain, because we are talking about notional rather than specific amounts. Nevertheless, I shall take the matter forward with my hon. Friend and see how far we can go.

I have given some detail of the new method of determining the regional rate. It will have the benefit of relating directly to local authority spending incurred in Northern Ireland, and of ensuring through the definition of rate-relevant expenditure and the application of the rate support grant formula, that Northern Ireland's special needs are taken fully into account. It is fair to both the Northern Ireland ratepayer and to the United Kingdom taxpayer, who both make an appropriate contribution towards the financing of local authority type services in Northern Ireland.

I shall sum up briefly and, I hope, even more clearly. What now happens is that, as far as possible, Northern Ireland is treated in the same way as any other region of the United Kingdom in determining what the rate should be. Accordingly, I commend the order to the Committee.

Mr. Derek Fatchett (Leeds, Central): I thank the Minister for his explanation of the order. As I stand here, I feel a certain sense of irony, because I vividly recall the 1979 general election when the Minister and I stood on opposing platforms. At that time, I hoped that the Minister would not be here to offer such a full and useful explanation to us this morning.

My comments will be brief. There is no doubt that the Labour party is committed to a policy of public spending in Northern Ireland and throughout the economy in general. A lot of work needs to be done in housing, social services and the Health Service. Therefore, although my Col. 6 comments will be specific and will recognise the special needs of Northern Ireland, they will also be general because the Labour party is committed to an economic policy that stresses the importance of public spending. We derive some satisfaction from that fact that the Secretary of State for Northern Ireland seems more committed to our economic philosophy than his colleagues in other Departments.

I should like the Minister to reply to three brief topics. First, why has the rate level been set above the rate of inflation? The Chancellor of the Exchequer's Budget statement referred to a rate of inflation of about 4 or 5 per cent, but the Northern Ireland ratepayer is expected to meet an increase of 6.7 per cent. In other words, the rate increase in Northern Ireland will be greater than the general level of inflation.

My second question may offer an explanation to my first: why are the Government shifting the burden away from central Government to the Northern Ireland ratepayer? The figures provided by the Minister shows that the ratepayer's contribution increases from 15.18 per cent in 1983–84 to 15.67 per cent in 1984–85. In other words, the burden has been shifted from central Government to the Northern Ireland ratepayer, and that may be why the increase in the rates is above the general level of inflation.

The shift towards the ratepayer is worrying, because we have seen it in England. When the Government took office in 1979, the general level of rate support in England was about 60 per cent. Now that figure is 51.9 per cent. Is it the Government's intention that there will be a similar reduction in Northern Ireland, and is it part of the Government's overall economic policy to shift the burden away from central Government on to ratepayers?

There is a warning for the ratepayers of Northern Ireland from the experience of those in England. We have had many arguments in the Chamber about the increase in rates in England and the major reason for that increase was the reduction in Government support to ratepayers. If the same process is taking place in Northern Ireland, ratepayers there can look forward to no increase in services but a great increase in their rate burden. Will the Minister say not just why the shift has taken place but whether it is part of a long-term shift away from central Government support towards casting the burden on the ratepayer?

Finally, will the Minister comment on what the shift in burden and the inrease in rates mean for industry in Northern Ireland? One often hears from the Conservative Benches that the increase in rate burden is generally unattractive to industry, that it leads to a loss of jobs and that it means that industry will not have the confidence to invest in new jobs, machinery or factories. Will the increase in rates and the shift in the burden away from central Government on to the taxpayer lead to a reduction in industrial confidence and to a loss of jobs? Conservative Members are keen on that argument in other parts of the United Kingdom. Does the Minister believe that it also applies to Northern Ireland?

Those are my three specific questions. I welcome this opportunity, however brief, to debate rates in Northern Ireland. The matter is important because we are talking about substantial expenditure and a substantial burden on families and ratepayers. I believe that Parliament should have the opportunity of making that expenditure Image Col. 7 accountable, and by debate we can exercise that responsibility.

10.47 am

Mr. J. Enoch Powell (Down, South): How delightful it is, Mr. Leadbitter, to be able to come upstairs from the tumultuous and contentious debates in the Chamber on the subject of rates in order, in quiet and calm under your chairmanship, to be able to discuss rates.

I was grateful to the Minister for agreeing to make a preliminary statement which was in many respects helpful to the understanding of the order, although I thought that perhaps he went beyond the permitted limits of understatement when he described it as uncomplicated and said that the method by which the rate was assessed was transparent. Apparently the Minister attaches different sense to the terms "uncomplicated" and "transparent" from those which probably most hon. Members do.

The Minister's last remarks rather took away the edge of the first request that I intended to address to him, which was that we should have a rather better explanatory memorandum for such an order than that which appears on the verso. Admittedly it would be awkward and perhaps unusual if the explanatory note exceeded the order in length, but I think that this is one of those cases where that anomaly would be permissible.

I appreciate the Minister's assurance that methods of offering an explanation intelligible to the ratepayer are still being considered between himself and his colleagues, but I should have thought that for the purposes of the House considering this order, annex .1 to the paper submitted to the Northern Ireland Assembly showing the heads of calculation would have been appropriate as an explanatory memorandum, and I hope that that may be considered in subsequent years. Certainly I found that annex extremely helpful in re-working the calculations and in arriving at one or two of the ponts that I want to put to the Minister.

Mr. Butler: In those remarks, am I right in thinking that the right hon. Gentleman in concentrating solely on a better and fuller explanation to accompany the rates order itself? Would he have preferred to see an explanatory note that was somewhat longer and clearer than what has been provided for the purposes of this debate?

Mr. Powell: There are two issues. One is the enlightenment of hon. Members, and the other is the enlightenment of ratepayers. I appreciate that it is to the latter that the Minister and his colleague are addressing themselves—and very necessary and desirable that is. I was saying, as we are considering the order itself, that I feel we could have been helped more by a more extensive explanatory memorandum, and I hope that that hint may be taken.

However, that being said, one cannot but agree that the new method being applied now for, I think, the second year following the consultative document of January 1983, is less opaque than the old method and that in some sense it attempts to relate the rate burden to expenditure upon services which in whole or part coincide with rate-borne or rate-related services in England and Wales.

Nevertheless, there still remain obscurities, and I hope Col. 8 that the Minister will be able to take the opportunity to clear up some of them. One is the mystery—at least it is a mystery to me—of how the percentage figure applied to rates-related expenditure, in this case 15.67 per cent, is arrived at. I think that that percentage was 14 per cent when the original calculations were made on the basis of the year 1981–82, and it was a lower figure in the current year when the new method was actually applied. Of course, this percentage is crucial. It purports to represent the ratio of rate to rate support grant in carrying the rates-related expenditures.

However, I think that it would be helpful if the means by which that exact percentage was calculated could be displayed and perhaps briefly explained by the Minister. He said that it represented for Northern Ireland a higher rate support grant than that available in England and Wales but that still left us not understanding how the particular precise figure of 84.33 per cent. for the notional rate support grant in Northern Ireland was arrived at and why it was different from the percentage in previous years.

I come to a second point of difficulty and, it may be, of inequity. The Minister explained very candidly that as for the purpose of these calculations we put together district council and the Northern Ireland Department's expenditure as one total, if the demands made by the district councils are less, the regional rate increases to make up for any restraint on the part of the district councils. On the face of it, it seems that if the district councils in Northern Ireland had complied with the main wishes of the Prime Minister and made every possible endeavour to keep their rate demands down in the coming year, the ratepayers would simply have been penalised by an automatic corresponding increase to fill the vacuum on the part of the regional rate. That may be implicit in the method of calculation, but it is a manifest inequity which I think will not be conducive to due economy and restraint on the part of the district councils in administering those relatively humble matters which are still left within their control.

The third point that I would ask the Minister to deal with is the treatment of interest costs. For the purpose of these calculations, a sum is added to rate-related expenditure to represent what would have been the cost of interest on capital expenditures if the interest had been paid on the money borrowed to meet those expenditures. As those expenditures are met out of current Government funds, it is thought equitable that a notional rate of interest should be imposed upon Northern Ireland ratepayers. If I have followed that argument correctly, I beg leave to differ from it. What is happening is quite different. The Government are not borrowing the amounts which they spend in Northern Ireland upon capital projects.

Of course if the Minister says, "Yes, we are, because we arbitrarily allocate part of the public sector borrowing requirement to the Government's capital expenditure in Northern Ireland," let him have joy of that argument. But if he chooses to use that argument, let me warn him that we shall claim that, at the most, even on that argument, only that percentage of the CGBR in respect of Northern Ireland capital expenditure which the CGBR bears to total public expenditure in the United Kingdom as a whole should be laid to the charge of the Northern Ireland ratepayer through these calculations.

The ratepayer is being charged for interest which the Image Col. 9 Government are not paying. Consequently, the calculation is being shifted to the disadvantage of the Northern Ireland ratepayer. I am not sure whether I can see the Minister anxious to hop off his perch. I see that I was under a misapprehension, and I can well understand his readiness to retain the relative safety of the telegraph line, but perhaps he would count this as one of the matters that he would be good enough to clear up either when he replies to the debate or, if it is too difficult, later on.

The de-rating of domestic premises is taken into account in the fixing of the rate in the calculations to arrive at the figures in the order. Would the Minister explain what happens about industrial de-rating? That may partly answer the question asked by the hon. Member for Leeds, Central (Mr. Fatchett). As I understand it, industrial premises in Northern Ireland are totally de-rated as one of the Government's measures to assist the economy of the Province. I am sure that it has been made clear that no part of the burden of that de-rating falls on Northern Ireland ratepayers. However, I cannot understand how that is achieved when industrial de-rating does not appear to have been taken into account in the calculation, although domestic de-rating has been.

Finally, I come inevitably to the major subject of the fundamental impossibility, which all our endeavours go to illustrate, of running rate-borne services in the absence of the elected representatives of the ratepayers. I shall preface what I have to say on the subject by referring to a point made by the hon. Member for Leeds, Central, who asked why the rates continued to rise in Northern Ireland faster than the rate of inflation.

Part of the answer may lie in the Minister's answer to my inquiry about why the assumed percentage representing rate support grant has diminished between next year and this year. But surely the main reason for the phenomenon to which the hon. Gentleman drew attention is that public rate-related expenditure in Northern Ireland has grown faster than the rate of inflation. If that were so it would, on these calculations, automatically produce an increased rate.

In principle, I do not think that ratepayers in Northern Ireland would be disposed to quarrel if that were the significance of the increase in their rates and it corresponded with a greater effort of public expenditure in Northern Ireland than accorded with the rate of inflation. But they would still be justified in contrasting their lot with that of ratepayers in the rest of the Kingdom. They would say, "Satisfactory or not as this increase in public expenditure may be, it is an increase in public expenditure which owes nothing to our own decisions or those of our elected representatives, and we are therefore paying a tax which does not arise out of the decisions of those whom, as ratepayers, we elect to control and to explain expenditure."

The Government are here confronting the consequences of one of their most paradoxical attitudes. They persistently state—this was in the Queen's Speech at the beginning of this Parliament—that their heartfelt desire is to give the people of Northern Ireland more control over their own affairs. There could be no more obvious or simple method of doing that than to entrust more services to the administration of the elected representatives of the ratepayers.

It may be that it is a giant step that cannot be taken at once to shift rate-related expenditure as shown in this analysis from the control of Government and the Col. 10 responsibility of this House to the control of elected bodies in the Province and therefore of the representatives of the ratepayers. But because all cannot be done at once, that can be no justification for doing nothing so resolutely year after year. I have to put on record on behalf of my colleagues and myself that at least part of the rate-related expenditure which underlies the calculations ought to be in the course of being transferred and ought to be perceived by the people of Northern Ireland as being transferred to the administration and the responsbility of those whom they elect locally.

There is no substitute—so the Government have discovered—for the rating system if we are to have local government, but we in Northern Ireland turn the point of that proposition against the Government themselves and say that there is no ultimate justification for rating without local government. The two hang together, and the divorce between the two, however it arose historically—I know that it did not arise out of a decision of this House—in Northern Ireland is ultimately unjustifiable and ultimately results in an unintelligible burden being placed upon the ratepayer.

So this morning we are doing two things. First, as parliamentary representatives of Northern Ireland ratepayers, we are trying to render more intelligible to ourselves, and thus indirectly to them, the method in which these arbitrary calculations are made. Secondly, we are inviting the Government to address themselves to a number of inequities which I have tried to define and which still appear to exist, despite the improvement in method introduced a couple of years ago.

I hope that the Minister will take the constitutional portion of my speech in the spirit in which it was meant, and that he will be able to deal fully and accurately, as is his custom in such debates, with the difficulties that some of us have encountered.

11.5 am

Mr. William Ross (Londonderry, East): I listened with care to what the Minister said this morning. I have rarely heard, either in Committee or on the Floor of the House, a speech which so clearly exposed the absurdity of our present local government structure. The people of Northern Ireland elect an elaborate structure of local government, whose main functions are then calmly bypassed when it comes to the major concerns of local authorities on this side of the Irish sea, but the Minister has done the Committee a service by exposing that absurdity. Like my right hon. Friend, I hope that the Government will now start trying to improve the set-up.

The ratepayers of Northern Ireland are being taxed, but are unable to decide how their money should be spent. It is all very well to say that the roads authorities, the housing executives, the education authorities and all the rest are open to consultation, and that officials sometimes come along to talk to local councils about their plans for the coming year. That is a very far cry from the elected representatives determining priorities for expenditure.

We, as Members of Parliament, so often find ourselves doing what could—and most certainly should—be the job of county councillors and local councillors in Northern Ireland, and burdening Ministers with local problems. Decisions are being made at ministerial level that a middle-tier level of local government would make Image Col. 11 not only more quickly and easily but with far more sensitivity. There would be much more support from those on the ground, who would be able to put their point of view to a councillor with a detailed knowledge of the subject under consideration. At present, it is impossible for a Member of Parliament—with duties in the House and outside—to have a detailed knowledge of what is, in my case, a very large geographical area. We do our best, but we cannot help feeling that our constituents suffer from the lack of a proper system of local government.

I listened with interest to the Minister's explanation of how the regional rate was decided, and how the total of regional plus local rate was calculated. I shall read his speech with care, and I think that all Northern Ireland Members should give it their consideration.

I am not yet sure what is the Government's policy on the regional rate. In 1976–77, the regional rate was 48p in the pound. It is 93.33p for the coming year. That is rather below the overall level of inflation for that period, which can only mean that expenditure on local government services in Northern Ireland is being met in some other way. I do not think that the Minister will say that total expenditure over that period has dropped very much, but what one can say is that the local councillors have run a very tight ship in most local councils in regard to local government expenditure.

For the coming year my own council—I could have checked the other councils in my constituency if I had known the reason that the Minister would give this morning—has a 1.6 per cent increase in the local rate. The Minister told us this morning that, when the average local council rate increase is below a certain figure, all that happens is that the saving is shifted over to the regional rate, and the regional rate plus the local rates on average reach a certain percentage figure, which, as my right hon. Friend the Member for Down, South (Mr. Powell) said, is well above the current level of inflation.

If one looks at this from the point of view of my local Limavady council, one discovers that inevitably, because it has held its local rate down, it must be subsidising other councils, because apparently it is reckoned on the average local council increase. If that is not so, I shall be glad to hear it, and so will my local councillors. If it is as I have expressed it, one must expect that in future years those councillors who have deliberately been running a very tight ship will have the incentive to do so taken away. It is a very serious matter that, when a council tries to make a saving and to hold the rate down for the benefit of its own people, it then finds that it has been wasting its time because the Government will get their pound of flesh in any case through the regional rate. It is quite unfair and it is something that the Government must take a careful look at, because it will store up an enormous number of problems for the Minister once the local councillors discover what is going on.

I cannot understand either what the Government's long-term policy is in regard to the regional rate. Do they intend to increase the overall rate burden purely in line with inflation or have they another intention? Perhaps we could be told exactly what their policy is in that respect. Real increases in expenditure do not take place uniformly in the different councils in Northern Ireland. Each council has different expenditure and different peaks and troughs from year to year. We should like people in the Col. 12 councils to have a clear understanding of what is going on and where savings can be made.

I am sure that the Minister will recall that in the Northern Ireland appropriation debate I raised what I saw as an inequity in the Coleraine council area because of the vast number of tourists who visit that area each summer. The Under-Secretary of State for Northern Ireland said that the tourists brought a lot of money into areas such as Coleraine as well—as costing money in additional services which had to be provided. Tourists may come and spend money in such tourist-related areas, but the difficulty is that the money they spend does not go into the pockets of the rates office but into the pockets of the shopkeepers and other retailers in the area. Given the weather in Northern Ireland, the tourist season there is at most only three months long each year, and the shopkeepers naturally have to make hay while the sun shines. But for the rest of the year the tourist trade does not exist and retailers in that area depend on the local population.

The Government cannot get away by saying in such a case, when the population increases by 50 per cent for two or three months of the year, that it is just their misfortune that they have to provide extra services for tourists for that short period. There is a case to be answered. A good case has been made by the Coleraine council—especially by the council officers—regarding extra help for the area in the provision of those services.

I hope that the Government will go back and take a careful look at the problem to see what can be done to help, because the local rate in Coleraine is rather higher than in Limavady, and I think that that can largely be attributed to the costs of providing services and so on for the tourist industry.

I have always had the problem of the inequity created by a regional rate and a local rate in my mind. The valuation officers in one local council district show equity, I am certain, to all ratepayers regarding the valuation of their premises. This is an old chestnut with me; it has been raised before, and I have never been satisfied with the answers which I have received.

The problem arises because the regional rate is taken over Northern Ireland as a whole, on the poundages in each individual council area. But the valuation officers who do the valuations in Belfast are not the same officers who do them in Londonderry, Armagh, Strabane or wherever; different people are involved. While officers in the local areas show equity within their area in regard to the valuation levels, it canot be true that the valuation levels for similar premises are the same throughout the Province. But everyone pays the same regional rate.

There is a problem in that, which can probably only be resolved in the long-term by two methods: first, by having very careful, close interaction between the valuation officers in the different areas and, secondly, by having a system of local government for Northern Ireland where those matters may be aired and the views in the various areas may be fed into the central body, so that measures can be taken to alleviate inequity and the difficulties of ratepayers throughout Northern Ireland.

I finish as I began by saying that a lot of the difficulties—in the rating structure and of convincing people in Northern Ireland that the rates are properly spent and that rate expenditure is held down as much as possible—arise from the fact that the folk there cannot Image Col. 13 see a direct link between the rates which they are asked to pay, especially at regional level, and the expenditure of those rates on the services which the electorate demands.
