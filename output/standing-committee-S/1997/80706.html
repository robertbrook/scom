Points of Order

10.30 am

The Chairman
: Good morning everyone. The Secretary of State would like to raise a point of order.

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Donald Dewar)
: On a point of order, Mrs. Michie. Today is the 10th anniversary of the Piper Alpha tragedy and I think that the Committee would wish me to say one or two words about it. That devastating event was the worst ever catastrophe in the North sea oil industry. There has been progress since then: Lord Cullen's recommendations have been implemented by the industry and the Government have introduced new health and safety legislation. However, today's anniversary and the recent loss of life in Dundee remind us that danger is ever present, and of the debt that we continue to owe our offshore work force as it secures vital energy resources for the United Kingdom. We must always remember the sacrifice of those who died and the trauma and sadness that live on for the survivors and the relatives.

Mr. James Wallace
(Orkney and Shetland)
: Further to that point of order and the Secretary of State's tribute, Mrs. Michie. For all of us, the moment when we learned of the terrifying scale of the Piper Alpha disaster is for ever imprinted on our memories. However, our memories cannot be compared with those of people who remember the loss of a loved one, or a work colleague, or who still bear the physical and mental scars of that tragic night. Even after 10 years, words are 2 inadequate to convey comfort and condolence to those who still mourn and suffer.

As the Secretary of State said, we owe a tremendous debt to those who win resources from the North sea. Indeed, we were reminded of that again at the weekend with the death, in Dundee, of two people on an oil construction platform. For many years, our country—and my constituency—has benefited from the oil that was piped to the Flotta terminal from the Piper Alpha field. Possibly the greatest tribute that Members of Parliament could pay to those who died is to ensure that lessons are identified and learnt; that there is no complacent backsliding; and that the safety regime, which is required by law, matches the risks and dangers that are encountered by those who work offshore. We must ensure not only that laws are made, but that they are regularly monitored and enforced.

Mr. Alex Salmond
(Banff and Buchan)
: Further to that point of order, Mrs. Michie. I wish to associate the Scottish National party with what has been said about Piper Alpha. It is entirely appropriate for us to remember that anniversary and to express our condolences, because we are responsible for the legislation that protects those who work offshore.

I should like to draw the Committee's attention to the positive response given by the Leader of the House to a question last Thursday that called for a parliamentary debate about the progress of the offshore safety regime, in the light of the Cullen recommendations. That debate should also examine the further legislative changes that could be made, especially in the corporate sector, to offer additional protection to those who risk their lives in the dangerous environment of the North sea.

The Chairman
: Although those were not strictly points of order, I believe that everyone felt that the Committee should commemorate this important anniversary.

We now move on to oral questions. I remind the Committee that this item of business will end in three quarters of an hour from now.

EU Funds (Reform)

2.

Mr. Archy Kirkwood
(Roxburgh and Berwickshire)
: What representations he has had on the 4 proposed reform of EU regional and structural funds; and if he will make a statement.[47533]

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Donald Dewar)
: Ministerial colleagues and I have received representations on EU regional and structural funds from the hon. and learned Member for Orkney and Shetland (Mr. Wallace) and seven leaders or councillors. I will ensure that their concerns inform the long and complex negotiations on Structural Fund reform that lie ahead.

Mr. Kirkwood
: Does the Secretary of State acknowledge that following the successful visit to south-east Scotland by the Minister of State last week, it is absolutely necessary for the local economy to be underpinned by European structural funds after the current 5b programme runs out? I am sure that the Minister will have briefed the Secretary of State about the fragile state of the economy in the borders.

The Minister made the helpful commitment that the borders would be given a reallocation from any unused EC structural funds. Such funds must be deployed either by the end of next year, or, at the latest, early in the next century. When will the Government be able to tell us what reallocation might be available to the borders economy, so that people can plan under these difficult circumstances?

Mr. Dewar
: Of course I acknowledge the hon. Gentleman's point. The Minister of State visited the borders, especially Hawick, because the Government appreciated the situation there. I have had many representations on the matter from people in the borders. I accept that structural funds are of genuine importance. At present, the borders is covered by objective 5b, which is comparatively modest, but has been of some help. Since 1994, about £23 million has come from that allocation. We have made it clear that unused funds can be redirected to the borders and that will be done. We are considering that matter.

Mrs. Margaret Ewing
(Moray)
: Is the Secretary of State confident that the change in criteria will ensure that Scotland will still receive structural funds? There have been substantial changes in the boundaries that apply in England and Wales—for example, Wales has been split into west and east Wales rather than north and south Wales. Can he reassure the Committee that the changes will not be to the detriment of Scotland?

Mr. Dewar
: Obviously, we are at an early stage of the negotiations. At present, 85 per cent. of the population of Scotland is covered by one of the strands of the structural funds. It is well known that the Commission intends to reduce that coverage, not specifically in Scotland but throughout the European Union.

We have made it clear that we want a fair system, so we shall argue strongly the case both for existing areas and for a proper place for Scotland in the restructured structural patterns when the seven bands are reduced to three. I do not want to take a hard and fast line, other than to give a total undertaking that we will use our best endeavours to get the fairest possible deal, whether for the borders, the highlands and islands or for older JPEG 5 industrial areas that suffer from the stress of the rundown of their economic structures.

The Labour Government want to go forward in Scotland; the SNP will use every opportunity to wreck the new relationship.

We must also be careful about the Lord Advocate's role as independent prosecutor. The way forward now is JPEG 7 to build unity around the issue at the United Nations. The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Calum Macdonald)
: I should like to congratulate the hon. Member for Moray on securing the Adjournment debate today, and I thank her for the manner in which she presented her case. I also thank the other hon. Members for their contributions. I shall try to answer some of the specific points that were raised, but I should first provide the assurance that the Government take flood prevention seriously. We fully appreciate the distress that flooding can cause and the damage that it can do to people's homes and businesses.

Several towns and villages in north-east Scotland, Elgin in particular, were unexpectedly affected by flooding almost exactly a year ago. Before then, as recently as 1994 and 1995, Perth, Paisley, Kirkintilloch and other areas had been severely affected as well.

I was pleased to be able to meet members of East Dunbartonshire council in April to discuss several matters, including flood prevention in Kirkintilloch. Before that, Ministers had met with members of Moray council in the aftermath of the 1997 flood, and with members of Renfrewshire council, to discuss the proposed prevention schemes there. We look forward to receiving formal proposals for flood prevention measures for all the most affected areas. I was happy to hear that a scheme was about to be proposed to the Scottish Office.

It is probably true that the general public does not view flooding as a priority for central Government or local government—that is, until it affects particular areas and people directly. It can then have a devastating impact on local communities, which can bring about a marked change in perceptions. Local authorities have a crucial role to play in seeking to prevent or mitigate flooding in Scotland. Councils' responsibilities on flooding were extended by legislative changes in 1997. The previous Government brought in the Flood Prevention and Land Drainage (Scotland) Bill, which was warmly welcomed in principle and benefited from cross-party support. The Bill was given Royal Assent just before the election, and we have taken it through to implementation. Indeed, one of the early duties performed by my colleague Lord Sewel was to sign the commencement order that brought the legislation into force.

Among other things, the new Act translates into duties some of the permissive powers that councils had under the previous legislation. Councils are now required to take certain measures. For example, they must assess water courses in their area to find out whether they are likely to cause flooding of nonagricultural land. They must maintain water courses that appear to be likely to cause such flooding. They must also prepare and publish reports on the measures that they have taken and should take to prevent or mitigate flooding. The reports should specify all occurrences of flooding on non-agricultural land.

It is not possible to provide communities with total protection from flooding. It is perhaps worth JPEG 53 remembering that the primary responsibility for the protection of land and of property from natural hazards such as flooding lies with the owner. That said, the new duties should help to reduce the number of flooding events and the suffering that they cause.

Hon. Members will know that in November last year, councils were required to produce their first reports about flooding in their areas. I am pleased to say that a good number of very useful reports were prepared. In the main, councils have clearly taken their new duties very seriously and are seeking to provide solutions to difficult problems.

I shall now turn to some of the specific examples raised. The hon. Member for Moray referred to the report produced by Moray council. I was pleased to hear that Moray council has taken a partnership approach to flooding problems, which has involved the Grampian emergency planning unit in a co-ordinating role. I understand that an emergency flood warning and response plan has been prepared in conjunction with Grampian police, the fire brigade, the Scottish Environment Protection Agency and the North of Scotland Water Authority. That can only be good news for the local communities that might be affected.

I also understand that Moray council has set up a flood prevention working party that will consider all the council's responsibilities and will plan to prepare strategies for prevention, monitoring and development control. Again, that must be the right way to take matters forward. Indeed, I have been told that the council has appointed consultants to progress the work needed in the four areas most affected by last July's floods.

Development control has important implications. National planning policy guideline no. 7 covers planning and flooding. Its purpose is to ensure that flood risk is taken into account in the planning of all developments and that measures are taken to reduce that risk. I am aware that Moray council's flood report fully recognises that requirement and that developers are now being asked to undertake hydrological studies as part of the planning process.

The main power granted by flood prevention legislation is for councils to promote flood prevention schemes of the sort that the hon. Member for Moray described. The cost of such schemes has major implications for councils' spending plans. I understand that the figures given by the hon. lady are correct, and that in the case of Moray council, expenditure of up to £9 million could be involved. Clearly, the council's present capital allocation would mean that it would be unable to meet such a large single commitment without significant pressure on other priority areas in council spending. That general issue may affect other councils, and it is being considered in the comprehensive spending review, in discussion with the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities. I cannot pre-empt the findings of that review, but I am pleased to say that, in the near future, I hope to be able to advise some councils of extra resources for planned flood prevention measures.

More generally, the fact remains that spending priorities in capital programmes are a matter for the councils concerned. However, if a scheme has 54 progressed and has been confirmed by the Secretary of State, Government grant is provided at a rate of 50 per cent. of eligible expenditure. That grant can include the costs of consultants' preliminary studies and design costs as well as construction costs. As the hon. Lady said, the statutory process between identification of the need for a scheme and its construction can take a considerable amount of time. Scottish Office officials encourage councils and their consultants to discuss their proposals at as early a stage as possible, because they must be acceptable to the Secretary of State. I therefore invite the hon. Member for Moray to remind Moray council of that in her forthcoming discussions. I am sure that a number of schemes will result from that process. I shall return later to a couple of the hon. Lady's other questions.

I shall deal first with the matters that were raised by the hon. Members for Perth and for Paisley, South. The Perth scheme, at approximately £25 million, with funding already in place, is the biggest of its type in Scotland. The Scottish office is awaiting details of the council's design costs for consideration of grant. I am also aware that Renfrewshire council has identified flood prevention as one of its key priorities; and that a scheme for Paisley is under consideration and is shortly to be submitted to me. In fact, I approved an additional capital allocation of £1 million in January of this year to allow Renfrewshire council to proceed immediately with flood prevention works.

The 1997 Act does not change the power to make flood prevention schemes, but it does encourage councils to focus on areas of flood risk and to identify what needs to be done. That should ensure that the resources that are available for flood prevention measures are used in a planned manner and to the best effect.

The hon. Member for Moray asked about the six-month period. There is no time scale laid down in the legislation, but statutory schemes must follow a sequence of proper procedures. Grant is only available when the scheme has progressed to the appropriate stage. However, I could consider making an allocation available once preliminary work has started. As I have said, design fees are also eligible for grant. I would welcome early discussions with Moray council once it has decided which option to take forward. Land compensation costs could also be included in the overall package.

I hope that the hon. Lady, and other hon. Members, will acknowledge that the Government have responded quickly and positively to flooding problems. I assure her that we shall continue to do so. I hope that she will also acknowledge that the duties and powers in the legislation are sufficient to allow councils to act to help prevent flooding.
