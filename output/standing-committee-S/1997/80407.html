Health in Scotland

10.30 am

The Chairman
: Good morning everybody. We begin with the substantive motion for the adjournment of the Committee on health in Scotland. This sitting is due to finish at 1 o'clock and I ask hon. Members for brief contributions to enable me to call as many speakers as possible.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Sam Galbraith)
: I beg to move, That the Committee do now adjourn.

Since the general election the Government have made some of the most significant changes in the health service for a considerable time. Our aim is to develop a health service with effective policies that will take it into the new millennium, when it will play an enhanced role in transforming the health of our nation.

The Government have done much, but because I wish to speak for no more than 15 minutes I shall deal with only a few areas. I shall also emphasise the specific Scottish nature of what we have done.

Let me start with expenditure. In our manifesto, we pledged to increase it in real terms year on year. Next year a total of £4.625 billion will be available for health expenditure in Scotland. That is a cash increase of 5.7 per cent. and an increase in real terms of 2.7 per cent. over inflation; it is nearly £250 million more for the health service. We shall spend £898 per person on health in Scotland this y ear; that is almost £900 for each woman, man and child. That, let me emphasise, is 22 per cent. more than will be spent in England. We promised real-terms increases and we have delivered them.

I was interested in the remarks attributed to the hon. Member for Tweeddale, Ettrick and Lauderdale (Mr. Moore), who will speak for the Liberal Democrat party. He said that despite the spin the Government have to date provided only 2.3 per cent. extra for 1998–99. I suggest that he revisits those figures. The additional money that we have put into the health service is equivalent to the rise of 1p in the pound in income tax that was promised by his party. I have spent his 1p on income tax.

The Minister for Education and Industry, Scottish Office, my hon. Friend the Member for Cunninghame, North (Mr. Wilson) has also spent that 1p on income tax on education. He spent it twice on welfare to work. The Government have stretched one penny a long way.

Dr. Norman A. Godman
(Greenock and Inverclyde)
: Can I assume from my hon. Friend's remarks that there will be an increase of 2.7 per cent. in the mental illness specific grant this year? That is only a fraction of the amount that he has just referred to, but, as he knows, that grant assists people with head injuries, in a sensible and practical way.

2

Mr. Galbraith
: The mental illness specific grant is for a wide range of provisions within the community, and some of those deal with head injuries. I can assure my hon. Friend that that funding is guaranteed for at least another three years.

We have increased expenditure in real terms by 2.7 per cent. and we have continued the attack on wasteful bureaucracy, to redirect resources to patient care. We have announced the abolition of the inefficient, divisive, internal market. As a result of those changes, over the next three to four years, another £100 million will be redirected from bureaucracy into patient care. We have promised increased resources and we have delivered them.

The White Paper, "Designed to Care", and the replacement of the internal market are at the heart of what we are doing. The overall objective is a modern, patient-centred health service that will improve the reliability, effectiveness and responsiveness of patient care.

A key part of the modernisation of the health service is a simpler structure of trusts. From April next year we will establish primary care trusts across Scotland. They will be unique to Scotland and offer a new way to strengthen links between general practice and other parts of the NHS. The primary care trusts will emphasise team working, not competition. Nurses, general practitioners, psychiatrists, midwives, therapists, pharmacists and others will all contribute their expertise and skills. Bringing together the primary and community health services will help to overcome some of the unnecessary barriers that interfere with effective patient care.

Mrs. Margaret Ewing
(Moray)
: As the Minister suggested in the relevant document that there would be no blueprint for the establishment of primary care trusts and acute trusts, will he consider the possibility that individual areas might want a combination?

Mr. Galbraith
: The hon. Lady is right; there is no blueprint. We suffered in the past from the imposition of a blueprint throughout the country. With respect to the number of trusts—which is what the hon. Lady is talking about, although that issue is linked to types also—we shall wait for consultation. I hope to deal with that briefly, but I guarantee that there is no blueprint for the matters that I have mentioned.

The primary care trusts will have strong links with secondary care. Each health board will establish a joint investment fund, which will be another unique feature of the Scottish health service. Through that mechanism the new trusts will work with the hospital sector to redesign care from the patient's point of view. General practitioners, community nurses and other community professionals will all have a key part to play in the management of the trusts. That fact will support the development of cost-effective and clinically effective services that will be responsive to needs.

General practitioners and other primary care staff will also come together as local health care cooperatives which will be managed by them and charged to design and deliver local community services.

Trust reconfiguration is another aspect of the changes, and the question asked by the hon. Member for JPEG 3 Moray (Mrs. Ewing) relates to that. As well as a change in the nature of trusts there will also be a reduction in their number. Each health board is currently consulting publicly on the trusts to operate in its area. The proposals taken together suggest that there will be between 25 and 29 trusts in Scotland, in place of the 46 hospital and community trusts at present. That is a significant reduction in trust numbers—and will result in significant reductions in bureaucracy. Money will be saved and redirected from bureaucracy to patient care. That will be a marked improvement on the current position.

Subject to the outcome of public consultation, therefore, I expect to be able to take decisions about the new configurations in the summer. I expect the new trust configurations to be up and running from 1 April 1999.

Mr. John Swinney
(North Tayside)
: Before the Minister moves on from his point about public consultation, will he comment on the concern that is felt, certainly in my constituency, that loss of trust status will lead to the concentration of health services—particularly with respect to the threat to Stracathro hospital, in which the hon. Member for West Aberdeenshire and Kincardine (Sir R. Smith) and my hon. Friend the Member for Angus (Mr. Welsh) share an interest with me. It is thought that the concentration of health services will inevitably lead to cities such as Dundee providing more health care and the loss of health care in the peripheral areas of the health board regions. Will the Minister assure us that in the consultation process the need for local acute hospital services will be protected?

Mr. Galbraith
: It is important to understand that the trust configurations and numbers are a management system. The clinical provision of services is a separate issue, being dealt with in the acute strategy review. Of course, a conflict in clinical services has been highlighted, between concentrating on critical mass to ensure that everyone gets the best possible treatment, and convenience. It is important to achieve the right balance. There will be no blueprint for that, but, although it is necessary to concentrate some services to ensure the best possible treatment, the Government are committed to local services and community hospitals.

We always opposed the changes that took place in the 1980s. As a mark of our commitment to community hospitals, we have already given the green light to two new hospitals in the short time that we have been in office. One will be in Uist and the other in Ayrshire. therefore, our commitment to community hospitals and local care is clear.

Reducing the time people wait for treatment is one of the main priorities I have set for the NHS in Scotland. As we know, there has been a reversal of the downward trend in the past few months. I have already publicly expressed concern about that.

I am determined to drive down both waiting lists and waiting times, for the benefit of patients. My right hon. Friend the Chancellor of the Exchequer announced on Tuesday 17 March that additional funds would be 4 allocated to the national health service in Scotland including £44.5 million, which will be specifically aimed at tackling waiting lists.

That substantial frest injection of funds demonstrates a continuing commitment to driving down waiting lists and ensuring that they stay down.

Mr. Tam Dalyell
(Linlithgow)
: Can my hon. Friend tell us anything about waiting lists for elective surgery? They cause considerable anxiety.

Mr. Galbraith
: Waiting lists have increased from 84,000 to over 86,000 in the past few months. I intend to reduce those lists. That is why we have put £44.5 million extra into solving the problem.

£20 million has already been allocated to health boards this month, so that they can draw up plans for tackling waiting lists in their areas. The first priority will be to tackle the number of people waiting for inpatient and day case treatment, but I also expect them to look at out-patient referrals and waiting times.

I do not wish simply to throw money at the problem—that has been tried in the past and it has failed. For that reason, a further £20 million will be distributed later this year to assist actions that will influence more strategic change in service delivery, and achieve long-term reductions in waiting lists.

In order to drive forward this work, I have appointed a high level professional and clinical support group under the chairmanship of Tom Divers, general manager of Lanarkshire health board. He will review the plans to be submitted by health boards, pursue particular problem areas, monitor the progress of health boards and trusts in reducing waiting lists, and identify good practice.

My aim, and my promise, is that by April 1999, waiting lists will be shorter than those we inherited, and that we will maintain the downward trend.

I have so far concentrated on acute and primary care. The needs of community care priority groups are equally important. Among those mental health is a key priority for the Government and the NHS.

"The Framework for Mental Health Services in Scotland", which we published last September, is entirely consistent with our broad aims for all NHS services. They should all promote: patient centred care; care services that respond to individually assessed needs, and the importance of partnership planning and shared working.

The framework will help staff in the health, housing and social work agencies to develop a partnership approach to providing care and integrated services for people with mental health problems. That is one of the themes of our policy.

The response to date has been positive, with each health board developing a comprehensive mental health strategy in line with the framework's principles, which will be progressively implemented for the benefit of patients.

I have so far talked about treating people. In the last minute or so, I want to examine the big picture. Sadly, although health is improving in Scotland, it is doing so at a slower pace than in many other countries. No Government with a social conscience could tolerate JPEG 5 such a state of affairs. If cultural attitudes and lifestyle have a bearing on health, so do the circumstances in which people live. Poverty and unemployment, poor housing and a bad environment are all inextricably linked to health.

We need a fresh start with no more nannying and no more blame attached to people. That new start should build on an accepted fact denied by previous governments—that poverty is the cause of much ill health. If we do not tackle poverty, we shall not tackle ill health.

We therefore need a public health strategy that addresses the root causes of our health problems—deprivation, poverty, unemployment, poor housing and inadequate education. That is why we have promised to improve public health in Scotland with new initiatives and preventive medicine, which is exactly what we have done.

We have launched a Green Paper, which proposes action at three levels. First and most importantly, we must deal with life circumstances. Improving them by giving people jobs is probably the most important health measure that we could take. It would give people a future, a stake in society, the determination to look after themselves and the economic means to make the necessary choices. Together with a job, a good education and a safe and attractive environment in which to work are also important.

The second is to improve people's lifestyles—not by nannying individuals but by providing them with information to ensure that they are not brought under undue commercial pressures. As long as they have a job, they have a future and the economic means to make the appropriate lifestyle choices. Thirdly, we shall take action on a number of health priorities such as coronary heart disease, strokes and cancer. Consultation on the Green Paper finishes on 30 April and it will be followed by a White Paper later in the year.

I know that there is much to be done to revitalise the health service and it is our strong intention to revitalise it. I am confident that the range of initiatives that we have announced and implemented will provide a firm foundation to achieve our common aim—a health service that will provide people with what they need, when they want it and be of the highest possible quality.

Mr. Michael Moore
(Tweeddale, Ettrick and Lauderdale)
: A great deal of the time and effort of the House of Commons over the past year has been spent on Scottish constitutional matters to the exclusion of virtually everything else. Worth while and stimulating as the many hours in Committee on the Floor of the House have been, the opportunity to talk about something else will be welcome to many hon. Members.

As we recover from detailed scrutiny of part IV and schedule 5 of the Scotland Bill, a debate on health offers a reality check. I am sure that I am not alone in thinking that the state of the health service is of greater concern to people in Scotland than the minutiae of reserving xenotransplantation or the subject matter of the Ordnance Survey Act 1841, important though they are.

6 The debate on health has moved on in the past year. It is symbolic that this Committee is a Tory-free zone because it was the Tories attitude to the health service that made them spectacularly unelectable across Scotland at the last election. The Government deserve credit for acting swiftly to abolish the disastrous internal market and to reverse many of the worst aspects of the national health service under the Tories.

Few will miss the dogma and the corrosive ethos of competition which undermined the foundations on which the NHS was built after the war. Nobody will miss general practice fundholding, which created a new class structure in the NHS as a two-tier service emerged. Few tears will be shed for the ludicrous arrangements for hospital contracting—with administrators and doctors scuttling round hospitals after one another all year, trying to sign contracts just in time for the process to start all over again.

In section 1 of the White Paper, the Government restated the historic ideals of the health service as

"free at the point of use, funded through general taxation and available to all on the basis of need."

 It is surely an indictment of the Tories' legacy that there is a danger that we might have forgotten that. While the Government have begun to tackle the corrosion left behind by the Conservatives, a serious part of their legacy has barely been touched. I am sure that it pains the Minister greatly, but the issue of core funding in the NHS will not go away.

At an earlier Grand Committee Question Time the Liberal Democrats were accused of being churlish when we dared to take issue with the announcement of extra cash for winter bed pressures. The Minister sought to correct me earlier and said that the Chancellor announced a £4.5 million budget. I think that it was £44.5 million. We would like to give them the credit for that. We are a sensitive lot and are deeply wounded when we are accused of being churlish.

We have consistently supported such extra money as the Government have been able to find, particularly as during the election the plan was to keep within Tory spending limits. We are always getting our election pledges thrown back at us and that is one policy reversal, however modest, that we are quite keen to support.

The problem remains that demands on the health service are such that piecemeal, crisis funding cannot begin to tackle the underlying difficulties it faces. I know that the Minister is unlikely to take my word for it but perhaps he will acknowledge the comments of one of new Labour's closest friends. Writing in his own newspaper two weeks ago the editor of The Observer highlighted the issue. He said:

"well directed reforms take place against a growing and unaddressed financial squeeze … the NHS needs three per cent. growth in spending in real terms just to stand still … The truth is that the NHS is in a profound and intensifying crisis."

 Those are Will Hutton's words not mine.

Yes, the Government have put more money into the NHS but they have not yet begun to get to grips with the "profound and intensifying crisis". If the Minister seriously believes that the extra cash he has wrung from the Treasury is adequate, maybe he can answer a few simple questions. Why have the Labour Government continued the discredited Tory practice of staging JPEG 7 nurses pay awards with the pay review body's award of 3.8 per cent. consequently reduced in value to 2.6 per cent?

Why in its 1997 report did the Institute for Employment Studies find that a third of nurses in Scotland fear that they may be made redundant? Is that why only one in four nurses would recommend nursing as a career? What future is there for the NHS when investment in medical equipment is now almost ancient folklore rather than current practice? The situation is just not sustainable and we will wait to see exactly how much of the waiting lists money announced recently does not disappear down the deficit drain.

Creative political thought is, of course, going into finding ways of providing the investment needed for the NHS in the 21st century. Under the Tories we had the private finance initiative. That has now been rebranded as public-private partnerships. The Minister was right to be sceptical about the suitability of PFI for the health service although he has been partial in his scepticism. The PFI was unsuitable for the Western General in Edinburgh but it is fine for the new Edinburgh Royal Infirmary. Why is that?

There need be no ideological hang-ups about private finance being invested alongside taxpayers' money in public sector projects. The world would be a simpler place if the Treasury would just lighten up about public money being used legitimately for investment in bricks and mortar, and other things besides. However, there are genuine concerns about the constraints which the PFI may place on hospitals and the public purse. The PFI contracts are for the long term and the parties are being asked to sign up to contracts over a 25-year period. How can anyone know what the nature of provision will be in 10 years' time, never mind in 25 years' time.

At the Royal infirmary the British Medical Association is already sceptical about the assumptions for current health delivery built into the PFI plans. Who will be in control? While the clinical services will remain the preserve of the NHS, the facilities management and other services will be in private hands. Will other hospitals in Lothian and the Borders be forced to adapt the referral patterns and activity to suit the contracted levels at the new royal infirmary? A huge slug of money being pre-committed 25 years ahead will be unavailable for spending elsewhere. Many questions about the PFI remain unanswered. Perhaps the Minister will answer them today.

If there is no serious prospect of the Treasury recognising the Scottish national health service's investment needs, a much more honest debate about rationing will be necessary. It is a concept which all Governments are reluctant to discuss in polite company. But in a world of deficits and underfunding the subject cannot be ducked, although the White Paper has had a go. It states:

"It is sometimes argued that the NHS cannot cope with the pressures it now faces and the challenges posed by the pace of change. The Government reject that view."

 So that is that.

8 The White Paper introduces the idea of capping general practitioners' drugs budgets. The Scottish Office is re-examining the Scottish health authorities revenue equalisation formula as it tries to introduce a new basis for re-allocating funding for Scotland. Many parts of rural Scotland fear that there is a simple way of re-allocating a capped budget m favour of the cities. With growing demand for new technologies, an aging population, and inconsistency in treatment throughout the country an increasing feature of the NHS in Scotland, who will own up to the reality of rationing in the service?

The White Paper goes in greater detail into plans for re-organising the NHS. The Liberal Democrats are supportive of the abolition of the internal market. A new ethos of partnership is upon us and it deserves to succeed. Apparently trust chairmen are already being invited to attend health board meetings. But there are still some grounds for nervousness. Will the people who were appointed secretly by the previous regime without any accountability or openness simply be reshuffled and given new tasks? Can the broad sweep of individuals who presided over the in-fighting of the internal market be relied upon to introduce the new user-friendly patient-driven NHS? Old instincts die hard. An announcement that the Minister intends to institute an open basis for the appointment of trustees and executive directors would be welcome reassurance.

Many of the trusts will differ greatly from existing bodies. Will outside candidates be allowed to apply for the new posts, or will the names of only a cadre of approved candidates for local selection go forward from the management executive? That point is important. Local communities must be ably to feel that they have some influence over the new NHS arrangements, otherwise the new regime will be seen as remote and out of touch as the old one.

Health boards have embarked on a consultation exercise to test local opinion on the future shape of local health services. Consultation is welcome, but is it for real? In Fife, Lord Ewing has already given his view of the single option under consideration there. In the Lothians, only one and a half choices have been offered. In the Borders, the consultation paper proposes only one option—to rejig the current two trusts. In the past, I supported the two-trust structure. But there has been vigorous debate about it in the Borders. Not everyone is convinced that a two-trust format is the sensible way forward but no alternative is being offered. Without those options, does a public debate have meaning? What chance is there for an alternative view to prevail? Those questions arise—despite what the Minister has said—even before the chief medical officer has concluded his acute services review. Surely the shape of the NHS, in both management and other terms, must take account of the way in which services will be provided.

There is still much scepticism about the running of the NHS. It is vital that the staff, and users, of the NHS do not regard consultation as merely a matter of going through the motions. If they do, the new start for the NHS will be dead on arrival. That should not happen. There is much to commend in the White Paper. The Green Paper allows for serious debate about improving JPEG 9 health care in Scotland. But people must believe in the changes and in the future of the NHS, and until they know who will run it and where the money will come from, the jury is still out.

10.59 am

Dr. Lynda Clark
(Edinburgh, Pentlands)
: I am pleased to be able to speak in the debate, which I want to extend to discuss the special needs of old people. That is not a coded reference to constitutional change in the other place.

Old people, especially those who are frail and vulnerable, are dependent upon the national health service. They were led to expect that the health service would take care of them in ill health until their death, but in some respects, because of poor co-ordination and lack of planning, the NHS fails old people. Resources are sometimes wasted because of structural divisions and funding problems between sectors, such as the acute hospitals sector, primary care and community care. There is a lack of research and of proper costing. More must be done to ensure that best practice is promoted across Scotland.

I am sure that the Minister is considering these issues, and I welcome the White Paper, which is an important first step in designing a service from the patient's viewpoint to deliver high-quality, clinically effective care, quickly and reliably, in the most appropriate setting. Unfortunately, that does not work for some old people. I shall give an example of the way in which some patients at present are not receiving the kind of service that the Minister would want.

I shall highlight three areas: first, admission to hospital, secondly, length of stay and, thirdly, discharge. First, a recent study of emergency and medical admissions in Lothian, showed that 35 per cent. of those admitted were over 65 years old. The same study showed that concerns about diagnostic uncertainty and patient dependency were a material factor in general practitioners' decisions to seek patients' admission to hospital. Another important influence on the admission decision was the patient's poor domestic and social circumstances. Old people should not have to suffer the upset of admission to hospital because of the lack of alternative diagnostic and support facilities in the community.

Procedures need to be set up to advise and support GPs to discourage them from seeking admission to an acute bed merely for want of expert advice or better community alternatives. There are alternatives, which I hope discussion about the White Paper will encourage. I want one-stop diagnostic day centres, improvements in communication links between GPs, experts and hospitals, and centres in the community that can provide emergency back-up care, such as overnight nursing, to help to keep patients at home in appropriate circumstances.

It is sad, but it is sometimes easier for a person to make contact with a local vet about a dog than it is for a GP to make contact with an expert geriatrician for necessary advice about the state of an old person's health.

The second issue is the length of stay. Once older people are admitted to acute care in hospitals, it is 10 unacceptable that they should be left there for weeks because of staff constraints on assessment, or the lack of alternative funding for home disability aid or care in the community.

The cost of providing a hoist, or some other adaptation in the home, is far less than the cost of a hospital bed. The issue of funding services for people who in the past might have received hospital care, needs to be addressed. I look forward to the report on the issue of the royal commission, chaired by Sir Stewart Sutherland, which, as the Minister knows, is of great concern to many old people.

The third issue is discharge. We must consider the provision of post-acute care and develop and evaluate a strategy across the country. I commend to the Minister the early supported discharge scheme for elderly trauma patients, which has been developed by the Royal Infirmary of Edinburgh trust. That has worked well in the Princess Margaret Rose hospital in my constituency, where I hold my surgery. It shows that with proper planning and organisation, the length of stay in acute care can be reduced, with successful rehabilitation at home. It has proved popular with patients and their families; readmission rates have been low and savings in the acute sector have outweighed modest resources implications for community care.

Very good work is being done in Lothian on these issues and I commend to the Minister a recent report of a working group, chaired by Colin Currie and Janet Kells. Interesting and valuable work is being done throughout Scotland on many of these matters and I invite the Minister to give priority to old people, to draw together existing research work and to give new direction to initiatives in the health service that will ensure that the national health service provides an even better service for old people, based on equity, need and compassion.

Bearing in mind that so many old people end up in nursing homes, I draw the Minister's attention to the need for reform of the legislative structure that regulates nursing homes. Legislation in Scotland is based on the Nursing Homes Registration (Scotland) Act 1938 and the scheme is far out of date. The legislation was never designed to deal with the present situation and I ask the Minister to consider a fundamental review of the legislative scheme that governs the inspection, control and registration of nursing homes, and early reform of it. I commend him for all the hard work which is shown in the White Paper.
