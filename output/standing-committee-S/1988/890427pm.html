clause Clause 30
title COMPENSATION IN RESPECT OF DENOMINATIONAL SCHOOLS

4.30 pm

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mrs. Maria Fyfe (Glasgow, Maryhill): I have several questions for the Minister. As so often happens on the Bill, matters are not as simple as they may appear. We can draw a comparison on the issue of compensation for the loss of a denominational school. The Government intend similar compensation arrangements for opted-out schools as for schools under education authority control, when the schools no longer wish to be denominational.

How would the compensation arrangements be carried out? Section 22(4)(i) of the Education (Scotland) Act 1980 states that compensation would be paid to the trustees or their successors in the case of a school run by an education authority. Undoubtedly the same persons would be paid compensation in the case of an opted-out school.

Under section 22(1)(a) and (b) of the 1980 Act, further questions arise. Paragraph (a) opens up possibilities that we had not even considered under clause 29. Previously we had only talked in terms of selling the land, but alternatively the land could be let for, say, a yearly rental or there could be an excamb. As I recall from local authority days, that means an exchange of one property for another, so that a bargain is struck on the basis of each part of the deal being of similar value. I hope that the Minister will be able to say whether the same arrangements would apply to those possibilities. If the use of the site was discontinued or a change of site made, we should like to be certain that the same arrangements would apply.

Section 22(4) of the 1980 Act states that if an education authority says that a school is no longer needed after 10 or more years have elapsed from the time of transfer, the authority may so represent to the Secretary of State. If he agrees, the school can be discontinued. Would the same apply to an opted-out school? Would representations have to be made to the Secretary of State? Would discontinuance depend on his agreement?

I said earlier that parents who were running an opted-out school might not even carry out that requirement. If they suddenly washed their hands of the school, I am not sure what, if anything, the Col. 1120 Secretary of State could do about it. If they did not make the proper representations and he did not agree that they could discontinue the school, what could he do about it if they said, "We shall do it, anyway."?

Such questions arise when one is dealing not with an education authority but with a voluntary body that has disagreements about major issues of policy or how to run the school. Who knows what might occur in such circumstances? It would be more difficult for the Secretary of State to exert his authority and enforce his will on a group of parents than on education authorities. Officials in education authorities would wish to act responsibly and would feel that public contumely might arise if they failed to do so. They would pay political penalties, but a group of parents whose children had completed their education might feel that they had nothing to lose by behaving in a less responsible manner.

Section 22(4)(i) of the 1980 Act states that the education authority should pay

"such compensation (if any) in respect of the school or other property so transferred as may be agreed, or as may be determined, failing agreement, by an arbiter appointed by the Secretary of State upon the application of either party".

Again, that raises serious questions. If the school and its land are sold, it is easy to see where the money for compensation will come from, but let us suppose that it is not sold. If the school merely changed its characteristics—there is no cash value in that—where will the compensation money come from? If the school were let to another group or an exchange were made—excamb is the legal word—where is the compensation to come from? Those are not merely technical matters, because those to whom compensation is due would want to be certain that there was a reasonable hope of receiving compensation.

Furthermore, we are discussing not only the value of land and buildings, but people's expectations that a school will continue as it has been. What sum can compensate for the loss of a school that has been of benefit to its pupils? Parents may feel that there has been a curious loss to the community's interests, particularly those of a particular religion. That loss is more than the cash value of the school. The parents and children involved may be put to great inconvenience if the nature of the school is changed. They may need to undertake longer journeys to find similar education. The possibilities that are not readily understood by reading clause 30 must be explored.

I hope that the Minister has thought about the possibilities, so that he can give the Committee a satisfactory reply.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Michael Forsyth) : I hope that the hon. Member for Glasgow, Maryhill (Mrs. Fyfe) will forgive me if I do not venture into matters that we shall discuss later in respect of the transfer of property and compensation.

Clause 30 is straightforward and simple. Where an education authority originally received a denominational school on transfer from trustees, the trustees are entitled to compensation from the Image Col. 1121 education authority, under the 1980 Act, should that school be closed or lose its denominational character.

The clause provides that where such a school becomes self governing and subsequently closes or loses its denominational status, the trustees shall be entitled to compensation from the board of management on the same basis as they would have been had the school remained under education authority management. So the purpose of the clause is no more than to guarantee the rights of trustees in relation to a denominational school. I do not foresee any difficulties.

Subsequent clauses cover how property is dealt with and the rules that apply when it is disposed of, or closed, or any other change of status. No doubt we can consider those matters when we come to the relevant stand part debates.

Mr. Forsyth: I am sure that the hon. Lady will recognise that the board of management is under a duty. My right hon. and learned Friend the Secretary of State has powers to make grants. That is a good test. It is a question not just of absolute cost per head, but of whether the cost is reasonable in all the circumstances of the school. That depends on the nature of the school and where it is situated, not simply on the number of pupils on the roll. In a remote rural area, a primary school with a handful of pupils may be the most economic way of providing for those pupils. The Secretary of State will not allow a school to become self governing unless he considers that it is likely to be viable in the longer term, to be able to attract a reasonable number of pupils and to continue to provide for them at reasonable cost. If the school's role were to go into decline, that could result in it being considered eventually too small to be economic for the purposes of the clause. Those are the types of circumstances in which a responsible education authority considers a school as a candidate for closure, and the same types of criteria would apply for self-governing schools. The amendment is not appropriate. I do not know whether I can help Opposition Members further, except to outline how I believe that we shall consider the question of viability. The hon. Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden (Mr. Galbraith) was anxious about that. The test is whether

"the number of pupils in attendance is too small for efficient and suitable instruction to be provided at reasonable cost."

We shall have to look at numbers and costs and consider how the per pupil costs for that school compare with those of similar size in similar locations. We shall look also, not only at the numbers on the roll, but at projections of numbers provided by the board of management and we shall have to take a judgment about whether they were soundly based. In doing so, we might very well wish to cross-refer with the education authority, which generally speaking will have access to comprehensive data and will also be able to tell us about its own provision and planned provision in the same area.

We shall also have to look at the curriculum and the general range of education provision at the school. Again, we shall look at whether that curriculum was adequate in all circumstances. It stands to reason that, for instance, a small rural secondary school will not be able to provide the same range of curricular choice—at any rate at any reasonable cost—as a large Col. 1130 urban secondary, and we should not expect it to do so.

We must also look at the standard of attainment, in public examinations or according to other relevant measures available. Finally we have to assess what, if any, capital expenditure would be likely to be required at the school in the near future. title COMMISSIONERS FOR SCHOOL ASSETS

Mr McLeish: I beg to move amendment No. 253, in page 62, line 27, leave out "a" and insert "an independent".

This is a simple amendment and I await the Minister's response to it.

Mr. Forsyth: The commissioner for school assets will have to undertake a task of a highly specialised nature. It will require a good deal of knowledge of conveyancing practice; for instance, and may involve raising legal proceedings from time to time. Almost inevitably the commissioner will have to be legally qualified, or will have to employ professionals to assist him in his duties. Indeed, it is highly probable that the appointment would be of a law or accountancy firm. The equivalent function in England is to be performed by the education assets board, a body created under the Education Reform Act 1988. Instead of setting up a separate body, we thought it best to provide that for each case there should be an ad hoc appointment, preferably of someone local to the area.

I acknowledge the reasons why the hon. Member for Fife, Central (Mr McLeish) suggests that we should insert the word "independent". A similar amendment was suggested earlier in relation to the body prescribed to conduct the ballots. The difficulty is that it is not acceptable on drafting grounds because it begs the question, independent of whom? I assure the hon. Gentleman that the spirit of the amendment will be reflected in practice because of the nature of the job. I ask him not to press the amendment.

Mr. McLeish: I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.
