New Clause 7
EXTENSION OF FUNCTIONS OF COMMISSION FOR LOCAL AUTHORITY ACCOUNTS IN SCOTLAND

"(1) if a governing body so requests, the Commission for Local Authority Accounts in Scotland ('the Commission') may—

  1. promote or undertake studies designed to improve the economy, the efficiency, or the effectiveness, of the management or operations of that body;
  2. in relation to that body's accounts in respect of any financial year—
    1. give advice as to the appointment of suitable persons; or
    2. arrange for members of the Commission's staff acceptable to that body,
to audit those accounts.

(2) For the purposes of this section 'governing body' means—

  1. the board of management of a self-governing school; or
  2. a college council established under section 48 of this Act; or
  3. the board of directors of a company formed by an education authority by virtue of section 59 of this Act.

(3) Where the Commission provides services to a governing body under subsection (1) above they shall charge that governing body such fees as will enable the Commission to recover the whole cost of providing those services.

(4) The provisions of section 97(3) of the 1973 Act (power of the Secretary of State to give directions to the Commission in relation to the discharge of their functions) shall apply in relation to the functions conferred on the Commission by subsection (1) of this section as they apply in relation to the functions conferred on the Commission by subsection (2) of that section.".—[ Mr. Michael Forsyth.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Michael Forsyth)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The new clause would require the Commission for Local Authority Accounts in Scotland, on the request of the governing body of a self-governing school, a college council or a corporate college, to promote or undertake studies designed to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of the management of such a body, or to give advice regarding the appointment of persons to audit the governing body's accounts, or to arrange for staff of the commission to do such auditing. It would enable the commission to become involved in those areas: indeed, the commission has requested it.

The new clause would also provide that where the commission provided a governing body of any of the bodies mentioned with services, it would be entitled to charge fees that would cover the full cost of providing those services.

The Secretary of State's power of direction is to extend to the new functions given to the commission under the new clause. It is a purely technical requirement.

New Clause 8
DUTY OF EDUCATION AUTHORITY TO PROVIDE INFORMATION

"The education authority shall, on the incorporation date or as soon as practicable thereafter, provide the board of management of a self-governing school with all the information held by that authority in respect of that school including, without prejudice to the generality of the foregoing, information in respect of—

  1. the administration of the school;
  2. the fabric of the school;
  3. staff transferred from the employment of the authority to the employment of the board of management by virtue of section 21 of this Act; and
  4. the pupils in attendance at the school.".— [Mr. Michael Forsyth.]

Brought up and read the First time.

Mr. Forsyth
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The purpose of this new clause is to ensure that the education authority provides the board of management of a self-governing school with all the information that the authority holds as managers of the school. The board of management will be taking over the school as a going concern, and it follows that it ought to inherit all the relevant records that it will need as the new managers of the school.

Even without the new clause, most of the relevant information would pass to the board of management, as under clause 32 all moveable property relating to the school is to transfer to the school's board of mangement. Any written records relating to the school would count as moveable property and would therefore transfer. However, there is no property in information as such, and nowadays it is likely that some of the relevant records may be stored among many others unrelated to the school in question on a hard disk in the education authority's computer. The purpose of the new clause is to put it beyond doubt that any information relating to the school, in whatever form, should transfer to the board of management.

It has been suggested that confidential records relating to individual pupils or members of staff ought not to pass to the board of management. I shall deal with staff records first. When someone leaves one employer and goes to another, a new contract is made and it is up to the new employer to satisfy himself that he has the information that he requires about the person whom he is taking on. The new employer may request information from the former employer, but he does not have a right to demand it. JPEG 1473 But we are not dealing with that here. The Bill provides specifically that the whole contract of employment transfers to the new employer just as if it had originally been made by the employee and the board of management. The board of management has no choice about that, any more than the individual member of staff: initially those on the board are going to have to take over the people who are already at the school, whether they like it or not. They will need basic records, in any case, for payroll purposes. But they are also entitled to receive information such as disciplinary records. Let us suppose that a member of staff had been disciplined by the education authority, and had perhaps received a final written warning. It would be wrong to entitle him to start again with a completely clean slate simply because the school had changed hands.

Similar considerations arise in the case of pupil records. The new board of management will take over the education authority's responsibility for managing the school and keeping it efficient. It is right, therefore, that any records that the education authority holds in its capacity as an education authority should be passed to the board of management. I commend the new clause to the Committee.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause read a Second time, and added to the Bill.

New Clause 9
FURTHER AMENDMENT OF 1980 ACT IN RESPECT OF RECORDED CHILDREN

" .—(1) In section 60(2) of the 1980 Act (powers and duties of education authority as regards children and young persons with special educational needs)—

  1. in paragraph (a)(i), after the word 'age' there shall be added the words 'and are not children in respect of whom the authority is under a duty by virtue of sub-paragraph (ii) of paragraph (b) below';
  2. in paragraph (b), the words 'are of school age' shall be subparagraph (i) and after that sub-paragraph there shall be added the word '; or' and the following sub-paragraph—
  3. '(ii) have not attained school age but, being at least two years of age, have come to the attention of the authority as having, or appearing to have, special educational needs,'.

(2) In section 61(1) of that Act (examination and assessment of children and young persons), in paragraph (b), for the words 'who is of school age' there shall be substituted the words 'in respect of whom the authority is under a duty under section 60(2) of this Act.'."— [Mr. Michael Forsyth.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Forsyth
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The new clause makes an important change to the provisions for the assessment for the recording of children with profound, severe or complex special educational needs. It reflects the increasing awareness of the benefit of identifying children with impairments before they reach school age. The effectiveness of early treatment of physical handicap 1474 was brought forceably home to me when I visited the Peto institute in Budapest. By accpepting the very youngest children, it was, in many cases, able to help cerebral palsied children walk and do other things much sooner than would normally be expected. Lowering the mandatory age of recording will encourage authorities in Scotland to identify children who may benefit from help with their special educational needs early in life, and to consider how they intend to provide that help.

It is estimated that 70 children per annum will need to be assessed in future and have a record of needs opened on their behalf. Not every record will have immediate consequences for provision. Many will be opened to enable the authorities to monitor the position and take action at the appropriate time. When we debated clause 65, the hon. Member for Moray (Mrs. Ewing), mentioned the work of the research team at Edinburgh university. She will be aware that it has reported that in almost half the number of records opened by authorities, the process took between six months and two years. If assessment does not begin until the age of five, as is the case now, very valuable time is lost and proper school placement decisions may be delayed. That will not happen in future. Of course, some new receords will be opened for pre-school children, which will reveal the need for provision. At the moment there are 85 records for children under five which have been opened because the authority recognised the need for early intervention. The proposals will increase the number of cases where the need for early provision is shown, but it is difficult to say how many that will be or the amount of resources involved. The extra cost will not, however, be significant when viewed against the background of over 6,000 current records and expenditure of £8.5 million. Provision for the special educational needs of children with records is made through the revenue support grant settlement. This is a package; it is not broken up into component parts. As I told the committee at our previous sitting, it has increased dramatically in real terms under this Government, by 75 per cent. over and above inflation in over eight years. I am confident that the aims of the new clause can be accommodated within that total.

Paragraph (b) amends section 60 (2) (b) of the 1980 act to place on education authorities a duty, where appropriate, to open a record of needs for children of between two and four years who have come to the authorities' attention as having or appearing to have special educational needs. That brings the Scottish legislation into line with that for England and Wales, where statements of needs must be opened from the age of two and is an example of anglicisation, which will be widely welcomed throughout Scotland.

Paragraph (a) is consequential, to ensure that authorities may continue to have power where they wish to open a record of needs for a child who is not yet two. I commend the new clause to the Committee.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause read a Second time, and added to the Bill.

This new clause is very important. People in Scotland should be made aware of all available vacancies. Authorities should also have the widest possible choice of eligible qualified candidates from within the Scottish education system, for principal teachers or posts above that.

Members of the Committee have probably received the letter from the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities about the matter. COSLA seems to have a clear view on it, but its evidence shows a divided view. There is no question but that authorities such as Grampian, Orkney, Shetland and the Western Isles advertise their posts nationally. The practice is different in Strathclyde. Tayside also advertises nationally.

COSLA says that it opposes compulsory advertising. It says:

"Practice differs throughout Scotland because circumstances differ. Authorities feel they must be free to exercise discretion in responding to individual circumstances in their own areas, from the requirement for Gaelic speakers in the Western Isles".

The majority of Gaelic speakers live in the Glasgow area, so that argument is a bit thin.

COSLA also argues that

"In larger authorities, additional staff would be required to cope with the volume of work."

That is not so. In commercial organisations where one department is responsible for staff advertising and recruitment, it is easy to cope with additional numbers. To say that additional staff would be required is a typical bureaucratic local authority response to what is only a little additional advertising. Advertising space is already being booked, so the only extra work is to put out the adverts to a few more newspapers. I, therefore, do not accept COSLA's argument.

We have already said that teachers should be properly appraised to help them in their career. Those same good teachers should be given the opportunity, through advertising, to be made aware of the available promotion posts that will give them a proper career structure. It is irrelevant whether they live in Glasgow, the Western Isles or Tayside. If individuals are prepared to move, to take a chance with career prospects and apply for promoted posts, that can only be good for them, for schools, for children and for the education system in Scotland. The more people have moved around and seen of the wide world, the better for the important sphere of education. I hope that my hon. Friend the Minister will be able at least to accept the principles of new clause 2.
