The Parliamentary Under-Secretary for Scotland (Mr. Michael Forsyth)
: I thought that it would be helpful to intervene now and respond to the amendment because there may be a misunderstanding about it. Under section 6 of the Employment Protection Act 1975 there exists a code of practice, made by the Advisory Conciliation and Arbitration Service and issued by order of the Secretary of State for Employment, covering disciplinary practice and procedures in employment. Paragraph 10 of that code specifies in considerable detail the requirements of a disciplinary procedure. It specifies, for example, that disciplinary procedures should be in writing, should specify to whom they apply and should provide for matters to be dealt with quickly. They should also indicate the disciplinary actions which may be taken, provide for individuals to be informed of the complaints against them and be given an opportunity to state their case before decisions are reached. The code also specifies that disciplinary procedures should give individuals the right to be accompanied by a union representative or by a fellow employee of their choice, which is the gist of this amendment.

The code is a code of good practice which any employer ought to follow—

As employers, the board of management of a self-governing school will be subject to the same legal provisions as any other employer, no more and no less. I accept that what lies beyond this amendment is reasonable, but it is not necessary to insert it in the Bill. If we were to do so we might as well insert the entire AC AS code of practice as something which should be embodied in every school's scheme of Government. That, too, would be unnecessary. In particular, it would not give the staff of the school any greater measure of real protection than they enjoy under existing legislation. The Secretary of State could not, for instance, order the reinstatement of a member of staff. We really have to trust these bodies to be able to make decisions in the real world: they are boards of management, not puppets.

That is not to say, however, that a newly established board will be left completely alone to JPEG 417 discover basic facts about employment law for itself. Member for Fife, Central (Mr. McLeish) said that we ought to make sure that one of the points we draw to the ttention of boards of management is the existence of this code of practice and its significance for them. We shall do that. But there is no necessity, nor indeed would it serve any useful purpose, to incorporate the detail in the Bill.

I hope that that helps the Committee, as it shows that what lies behind the amendment is covered by existing employment law. I hope that that will enable the hon. Gentleman to withdraw the amendment so that we can proceed with further consideration of the Bill.
