Mr. McAllion
: I am sure that the amendment will be accepted once the Minister has heard the arguments. The amendment would mean that before the Secretary of State either made or varied an order setting out a scheme of government for an opted-out school, he would have to consult the head teacher, the appropriate teacher representatives, the parents and the education authority. Such wide consultation is vital, especially if we are to take seriously the Government's claim that the measure will devolve power and control from the centre back to the localities where it belongs.

Under clause 2, after a successful ballot to opt out, a school will have to make a formal proposal setting out the future arrangements for running the school, the broad policies on admissions, the curriculum, preparation for public examinations and other matters. It should also identify individuals willing to serve as co-opted members on the new board of management. That formal proposal is the scheme of government set out in clause 2 and will include the articles of constitution and articles of management, which are important documents.

The articles of constitution will deal with the makeup of the board of management. Clause 3 and schedule 1 specify the detail to be included in the articles of constitution. For example, the board of management will have to have the same number of parent members and staff members as the school board had, and will have to include the head teacher. Appointed members will be specified in the articles of constitution. That will give those framing the articles scope to change the nature of the board of management. Clause 3 provides that the number of parent members must be greater than the number of appointed members, but must not constitute an overall majority. Other than that, it does not specify the number of appointed members that can be included in the articles of constitution.

The school board of a school with fewer than 500 pupils currently has four parent members, one staff member, a head teacher member and one co-opted member, but under the articles of constitution it could have two co-opted members. Instead of a school JPEG 127 board of seven, there would be a board of management of eight. Similarly, a school with between 500 and 1,000 pupils now has a school board of five parent members, two staff members and two co-opted members. Under the Bill, the board could have two additional appointed members, so instead of nine members it would have 11.

Schools with between 1,000 and 1,500 pupils currently have school boards of six parent members, two staff members and three co-opted members. The articles of constitution will allow for five appointed members. Similarly, a school with over 1,500 pupils could have a board with six instead of three co-opted members.

These important changes could be made by whomever frames the articles of constitution. The school with over 1,500 pupils could have a school board with 16 members instead of the present 13. Everybody should be consulted about the choice of those three additional members. As I understand it, the only restriction on the number of appointed members in clause 3 and schedule 1 is that it must be less than the number of parent members.

Mr. McAllion
: I am grateful to the Minister for saying that he is not unsympathetic to the amendment.

My point is that it is important to have wide consultation before a decision is made about the additional appointed members to the board of management. For example, the Minister's preference for business people running schools could be reflected in the articles of constitution, without appropriate consultation with everyone involved in the school. To get to the stage of framing the articles of constitution, there need be only a resolution from the previous school board, passed by a vote of four to three, or seven to six, or a request from 10 per cent, of parents that an election be held, followed by a simple majority. Therefore, the articles of constitution could be framed on the basis of the support of only a minority of parents and against the wishes of teachers and staff in the school. Before the articles of constitution are framed and before decisions are taken on issues such as the number of appointed members to the new board of management, it is important to consult head teachers, staff organisations and so on.

Clause 3 and schedule 1 define most of the terms within which the articles of constitution can be drawn and deal with issues such as vacancies, resignations, dismissals and eligibility. However, it leaves some scope regarding the term of office for those framing the articles of constitution. Schedule 1(4) states that the term of office must be between five and seven years, but it can vary according to who is on the 128 board. Therefore, whoever frames the articles of constitution may decide that one member on the board of management can have a term of office of five years, that another member can have a term of six years, and that yet another member can have a term of seven years. There should be wide consultation with all those who may be affected before that important decision is made.

There is scope in other areas, for those who frame the articles of constitution to make significant changes to the way in which the school is run. For example, within the articles of constitution it is possible to specify that those who are not members of the board of management can serve on its committees. But, before decisions are made about which non-members of the board of management can take part in its committee structure, there should be wide consultation with the people affected.

The articles of constitution specify the delegation of functions to various committees. Again, the decisions affecting that important matter should not be taken lightly or made by only a few people. For example, the board of management will have the right to deal with land and property, the investment portfolio and fund-raising. A board of management may decide to set up a sub-committee with delegated powers to deal with those areas and that only businessmen will be appointed to that sub-committee. Such a decision would be offensive to the vast majority of people affected by the school and there should be prior consultation with staff, parents, teachers and the education authority.

The board of management must deal with contractual arrangements for staff and teachers. Within the articles of constitution, it may delegate that function to a sub-committee on which staff representatives or head teachers are not allowed to sit. That is another vital area that should be open to public scrutiny and debate before any such decisions are made.

Similarly, regarding a quorum, the articles of constitution will state how many members of the board of management must be present before a decision can be valid. Once again, that affects everyone in the school and the decision should not be made only by those framing the articles of constitution, whether the Secretary of State or members of the previous school board. The articles of constitution can set down the arrangements for election to future boards of management, and such a decision should not be taken without consultation. It would be in order for a board of management to pay its members for attendance and carrying out the board's duties. That could cause great public controversy and lead to boards of management being taken over by small groups of elites who rewarded themselves by paying themselves for the work that they did on the board of management. Those issues cannot be decided without consulting the parents, children and staff who will be affected.

10.45 am

The articles of management will include a description of the school as it is when it considers opted-out JPEG 129 status. That description will have to encompass admissions and exclusions policies, the appeals procedure, disciplinary matters, staff dismissals and so on. All those issues are vital and must be debated openly by all those involved before they are included in the description and written into the articles of management.

The Minister will no doubt say that any school that opts out must have the same admissions policy as when it was under education authority control. However, under clause 28 a school could decide to change its admissions policy and that change would have to be written into the articles of management under the scheme of government. There should be wide consultation about such a changeover.

If a board of management decides to change the characteristics of a school by basing the admissions policy on ability and examination performance, that is bound to have an effect on all the other schools in that area. The board should be obliged to consult the education authority because the schools for which the education authority will continue to be responsible will experience the impact of the opted-out school's decision.

There is a similar problem with exclusions. Those who frame the new articles of management may decide to exclude anyone who poses a discipline problem within the school. Opted-out schools could turn their back on the problems of discipline by expelling troublemakers and dumping them on the education authority, which would have to handle indiscipline alone. That problem has existed in Scottish and English schools for many a long year and will not disappear in the near future. It should be dealt with as a whole within an education authority area. There are some imaginative schemes to deal with disruptive schoolchildren, but they require co-operation among schools. That cannot happen under the Bill, which will allow opted-out schools to walk away from the problem of indiscipline.

Civil rights are also involved because the appeals procedure will be written into the articles of management. It is important that parents know what rights they and their children have under the terms of expulsion, if expulsion is written into the articles of management.

The terms within which the board of management will deal with staff dismissal will also be set out in the articles of management. A group of people must not be allowed to decide, with the Secretary of State for Scotland, how to deal with staff dismissal without involving the representatives of teachers' organisations. It would be ludicrous for the Minister to allow schemes of government for opted-out schools to go ahead without wide consultation with head teachers, parents, teachers' organisations and education authorities. It is simple common sense that if the Minister seriously wishes to listen to local people and give power back to local areas, he must fully involve them in the vital decisions that may be included in the scheme of government set out in clause 2. I hope that the Minister will accept the amendment.
