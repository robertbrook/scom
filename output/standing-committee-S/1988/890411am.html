Mr. John McAllion (Dundee, East): I beg to move amendment No. 93, in page 5, line 32, after "charges", insert "at cost".

First, may I welcome you back, Mr. Martin? Contrary to press reports, we are all delighted to see you in your normal place in the Committee.

The amendment deals with the powers of self-governing schools to charge fees in respect of such education and facilities as are provided other than as part of school education. The Minister has already defined such further education and facilities. On 4 April, in response to my hon. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Maryhill (Mrs. Fyfe), the Minister said:

"We start from the standpoint that self-governing schools should…be able to provide a range of facilities, such as further education, voluntary instruction courses, social, recreational and cultural activities, physical education and training, organised leisure-time activities, and so on." [Official Report, First Scottish Standing Committee, 4 April 1989; c. 309.]

That definition corresponds closely to what the Scottish Parent-Teacher Council, which is responsible for the amendment refers to as adult and community education. The definition also corresponds closely to the concerns which the SPTC and many other bodies have expressed at the prospect of charging fees for this kind of education.

We had a long debate on 4 April about how such educational provision should be funded. When pressed, the Minister conceded that public funding could be made available to self-governing schools to help them establish further education classes and other activities. He said first that funding could come via the education authorities, probably through some kind of franchising system such as McDonald's and Wimpy are already involved in, to provide services to the Scottish people. No doubt if we had Conservative education authorities in Scotland, we would have franchises a la Wimpy and McDonald's. But fortunately that is not the case, so we can confidently assume that no education authority will be granting a franchise to self-governing schools to provide FE classes on its behalf.

Col. 466 The Minister also said that public funding could be made via specific allocations from the Government for specific projects. But he was very unspecific about the kind of projects for which he would authorise central funding to help establish FE classes in self-governing schools. He said that he was in favour of dancing classes being provided and he even defended FE classes on knitting as being possibly worth while. When the hon. Member for Inverness, Nairn and Lochaber (Sir R. Johnston) suggested German classes, the Minister looked positively enthusiastic. There is nothing wrong with the Minister being enthusiastic about knitting and dancing, although some people might be a little concerned at the prospect of someone with such extreme Right-wing views showing an interest in German. Apart from that, I am certain that no one would object to these types of classes being provided in any kind of school.

The Minister went on to say that the public funding could come from money allocated to the schools under the Bill, part of which they could use to establish FE classes. He cited Strathclyde as an example of an education authority which already used the money it was given for school education to help to establish such classes. No doubt the Minister would argue that there is nothing wrong with that, and that education authority schools are in the same position as self-governing schools. To that extent, he would be right. I think that the Minister describes what he is trying to achieve as breaking the monopoly on further education which education authorities currently enjoy.

Education authorities and self-governing schools will have similar powers to charge fees under the Education (Scotland) Act 1980. Under that Act, education authorities can charge fees for adequate facilities provided for voluntary further education in social, cultural and recreational activities, physical education and training. Those activities correspond almost exactly with the Minister's definition of the education in which self-governing schools may become involved.

Problems emerge, however, when we consider how powers may be applied differently by self-governing schools and education authorities. The Scottish Parent-Teacher Council points out that at present adult and community education is provided either free of charge or at cost by most education authorities. But what would self-governing schools do?

In fact, self-governing schools would provide courses in the same way as any other private organisation would—at prices that the market would tolerate and that enabled them to make a profit. They might be a nice little earner for self-governing schools.

The Scottish Parent-Teacher Council and many other bodies foresee a major problem, in that opportunities for individual learners may suffer. Yuppies may try to buy further education that they require. For example, if they want to make their continental holidays absolutely brill they may buy an FE course in modern languages at a self-governing school. If they have the money, they can afford it. But unemployed, single parents may require not only a bridging course to enable them to enter training or employment, but creche facilities and subsidised transport. No market-dominated system would meet the needs of those people. The market will always decide in favour of yuppies who are able to buy into courses, and against the unemployed and single-parent families who require a public subsidy to obtain access to courses.

The SPTC also points out that if self-governing schools become involved in profitable courses, there will be a danger that such courses will assume more importance than the provision of the school education which is the schools' original purpose. We should not lose sight of the primary aim of a school. The market place can lose sight of it; as we know to our cost, it lost sight of it in the past. Legislation was required to interfere with the free market and to prevent children from being employed in factories and mines and in cleaning chimneys. The Government had to intervene to protect the interests of children. The purpose of my amendment is to prevent the market from dominating the provision of further education classes. It will ensure that when classes are provided outwith the education Col. 468 authority, they will be furnished on the basis of cost, not profit.

The SPTC briefing argues that education involves more than what happens between nine and four o'clock to individuals between the ages of five and 16 or 18. Education is a life-long process which cannot be confined to the formal timetable. What happens outside the school may be as important for a rounded education as what takes place in class. The SPTC cited sports clubs, school trips and visits as examples of activities for which it would be reasonable to charge the children involved in them. However, the SPTC also pointed out that the charges should not be fixed at a level at which the organisers of the activity could make a profit. It would be inimical to the traditions of Scottish education if a self-governing school charged fees for visits to museums or other institutions in order to make a profit with which to subsidise normal education. I hope that the Committee will fiercely resist such a move.

"ensure that adequate funds are made available for schools so that the children of this county are not denied the immense educational value of visits to Blenheim Palace and all other historic sites."
