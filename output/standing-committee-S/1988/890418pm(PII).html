Mr. Forsyth
: The amendments are about the Secretary of State consulting the education authority 804 so that he is aware of its views and can take full account of them prior to any decision on proposals for a self-governing school. Under clause 18, those will be the initial proposals for self-governing status, but clause 28 deals with any subsequent proposals for changes in the characteristics of a self-governing school and clause 29 with proposals for discontinuing a school.

The three Government amendments stem from an undertaking that I gave to the hon. Member for Dundee, East (Mr. McAllion) when we were considering clause 1 on Thursday 16 March. I accepted that when the Secretary of State proposed to allow a school to become self-governing he should always have available the detailed views of the Education Authority.

When proposals for self-governing status are submitted, clause 16(2) requires the school board to forward a copy to the education authority. The authority will already know of the school's intention and should have no difficulty in letting the Secretary of State have a full statement of its views within the period of two months now allowed by clause 17(3). However, there is power in clause 18(2) for the Secretary of State to amend or modify the proposals submitted by a school board. That might be to deal with a technical defect in the school board's proposals or to meet some point of substance raised by representations on the original proposals. In any event, the Secretary of State at present need consult only the school board on such modification of the published proposals. Amendment No. 78 will ensure that the education authority is also consulted in such circumstances. It will therefore have every opportunity to let the Secretary of State know its views on the final version of the proposals under consideration.

Amendments Nos. 82 and 83 deal similarly with parallel circumstances: in relation to clause 28, proposals for changes in characteristics of a school, and clause 29, proposals for discontinuing a school.

I commend the three amendments to the Committee.

When the Secretary of State is empowered under statutory provisions to take decisions which affect the rights of others, it is established law that he must do so reasonably. In other words, he must not act in an arbitrary manner and must base any decisions that he takes on relevant, and not irrelevant, considerations. In order to demonstrate that he has acted in accordance with the law, the Secretary of State will be obliged to provide a statement of his reasons for approving or rejecting proposals for a school to become self governing. His reasons will be communicated to the school board concerned, which is the body having a direct interest in the decision in question. The amendments are not necessary and it would be undesirable, accordingly, to add such provisions to the statute book. The hon. Gentleman may therefore wish to withdraw his amendment.
