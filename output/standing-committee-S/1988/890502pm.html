Mr. Michael Forsyth
: This is a probing amendment to elicit what will happen to members of staff if a school is wound up. I understand the concern of the hon. Member for Dumbarton (Mr. McFall) about staff in a self-governing school that is wound up, should such an event ever take place.

Amendment No. 286, if implemented, would not improve the position of staff on the winding-up of such a school. In the event of a self-governing school being wound up, the board of management would cease to exist, so the staff of the school would also cease to have an employer. They would, in effect, be dismissed whether or not they had received notice. At present, clause 44(2)(k) provides that a winding-up order made by the Secretary of State may require a board of management to give notice to its employees of the effective date of their dismissal. The measure is designed to protect the interests of staff by giving them clear notice of when their current employment inevitably would end, through the dissolution of the board of management. Amendment No. 286 would remove that protection.

Provided that the education authority is able to offer suitable alternative employment from the point at which the staff cease to be employed at the board of management, their continuity of employment is guaranteed. One purpose of the provision is to ensure that, as far as possible, the date of dismissal is suitable from the point of view of continuity; that is to say, that the board does not dismiss staff prematurely in anticipation of the wind-up. Much will depend on 1188 the circumstances in which a self-governing school is wound up, as to the re-employment of the school's staff—this is the issue that interests the hon. Member for Dumbarton. If the school has ceased to be economic because of the small number of pupils in attendance, some redundancies may be inevitable. If, however, a school were to be wound up for managerial reasons, and were to revert to the education authority, it would, of course, be open to that authority to re-employ the school staff. For those circumstances in which staff transfer from the employment of a board of management to that of an education authority, I intend that the continuity of employment of such staff, for the purposes of potential redundancy entitlement, should be preserved. At present, that is the case where staff are in the successive employment of more than one education authority. The legal provision which produces that result is the Redundancy Payments (Local Government) (Modification) Order 1983, which I know the hon. Gentleman will have studied as carefully as I have.

I intend to extend that order to include employment with a board of management, as well as with more than one education authority, as being relevant for the purposes of continuity of employment and any redundancy entitlement which could arise some time after staff have transferred from the employment of a board of management to that of an education authority. The amendment of the 1983 order that I have proposed would also facilitate flexibility in staff movement between education authorities and boards of management in all circumstances, as well as in the case of a school being wound up.

Clause 44(2)(k) is a provision for the protection of the interests of staff in a school that is about to be wound up and, accordingly, I am of the view that amendment No. 286, which would delete that provision and its protection, should be withdrawn. However, it was a helpful probing amendment because it enabled me to make the position clear. I hope that my answer reassures the hon. Member for Dumbarton and that, on that basis, he will withdraw the amendment.
