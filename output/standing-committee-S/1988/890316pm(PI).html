Mr. Henry McLeish

The amendments would inject balance into the clause. People are worried about the erosion of local democracy, local accountability and local involvement. I do not want to go into detail on those issues because it would be more appropriate to discuss them in the clause stand part debate.

Opposition Members and educational interests in Scotland are worried because the Government are giving themselves powers to maintain self-governing schools. We shall identify the large number of powers that the Secretary of State is taking upon himself in the Bill. It would be useful for Parliament to have some control over the Executive. The reasons are obvious.

Scottish education has been a partnership between local education authorities, the Secretary of State and many educational interests. This major clause places a 46 duty on the Secretary of State to maintain self-governing schools. In the past few years the power of Parliament has been eroded on a host of matters. The Government's educational reforms arouse suspicion in Scotland. It would be a small concession to allow Parliament to exercise democratic accountability on behalf of the electorate and have some say in this matter. In the past 10 years the Government have ridden roughshod over many of Parliament's attempts to control the Executive. The Scottish Office has been involved in problems of constitutional arrangements and parliamentary legislation.

We hope that the Government will respond positively to the amendment. They should explain why this measure is necessary when there is no demand for parents, local authorities, teaching unions, the Scottish Consumer Council or the Scottish Parent-Teacher Council, who have been supportive in the past.

We want to reinforce a sense of local identity. It is appalling hypocrisy for the Government to suggest that by these 47 clauses on opting-out in part I of the Bill they are providing parental choice instead of political control. How can the Secretary of State assume powers and then devolve them back, both financially and administratively, to parents and pupils in an opted-out school? That is the crucial element that we are trying to thread through the fabric of the Committee stage, but points made here may be repeated on the stand part debate. We look forward to hearing the Minister's response.
