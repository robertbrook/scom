Mr. Henry McLeish
(Fife, Central)
: I beg to move amendment No. 36, in page 7, line 31, leave out "twelve months" and insert "four years".

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to take amendment No. 35, in page 7, leave out lines 40 to 44.

Mr. McLeish
: This is a reasonably important amendment in which we seek to give some stability to a school which has had a ballot in relation to self-governing status. If another ballot could be held 12 months after, it could be disturbing and lead to instability, which would be bad for the teaching and for the general running of the school. We seek to prevent an annual attempt to change the organisation of the school.

Several issues emerge. A ballot could mean that every aspect of the school would be disrupted for a period. Teachers, parents and the pupils' education must be considered, as there will be instability during the ballot. Consequently, a lot of energy would be expended on the ballot process—energy which would be better earmarked for the school's proper purpose of educating children. To repeat such a process annually would not be in children's interests.

We must consider the potential conflicts that would arise in a ballot that had been fiercely contested. They would be unsettling for the whole school, so a one-year lapse between ballots would not be healthy.

I should like to know why 12 months was chosen, rather than a longer or shorter period. Is it likely that circumstances could dramatically change in the school, in the catchment area or in the way in which a local authority deals with a school in such a period? What would happen when a ballot had taken place in which there had been a narrow majority in favour of rejecting self-governing status?

Our amendment would give stability to a school, which is essentially concerned with education, and ensure that groups which felt dissatisfied with the result of the ballot could periodically seek to have the majority against opting out overturned.

In support of my comments, I shall quote to the Committee the justification for the amendments by the Scottish Parent Teacher Council. It states:

"When a proposal is made to ballot parents on self-governing status, the school will enter a period of uncertainty and apprehension, until a decision is reached by the Secretary of State, if the result of the ballot was in favour. This is unavoidable. If the result of the ballot was against the school becoming self-governing, a year is much too soon to allow a school to be thrown into that uncertainty again. Moreover, the composition of parents is unlikely to change significantly in one year and the ballot result is unlikely to be different from the previous one. The provision for an annual ballot of parents would be an unnecessary use of public resources, both in terms of finance and manpower."

The SPTC concludes:

"It could trivialise the procedure."

This is a succinct complement to our comments about uncertainty and seeking to give stability to a school. Although we believe that the educational debate is of necessity about politics—it is about resources, priorities and the judgments that have to be made about pupils and parents—we would not welcome the politicisation of schools. It is in that spirit that we hope that a period of relative stability would always exist after a decision had been taken one way or another.

This is the general thinking behind our modest amendment. What constitutes a reasonable period of time is always a matter of judgment. The Government have specified 12 months; in our judgment, that is too short a period. Four years has been suggested as a period of relative stability, but that may not be appropriate either. The debate should focus on the principle of whether it is in the long or short-term interests of a school and, more JPEG 603 importantly, the pupils who are looking for an education, to have the opportunity to hold continual ballots.

My final point concerns the length of time that a child is at school. A primary school may hold a fiercely contested ballot which shows only a small majority against self-governing status. Children are at primary school between the ages of five and 11 or 12. It would be a tragedy if, over a period of two or three years, the school was continually holding ballots on opting out, with the same arguments recurring and the possibility of interference in the ballot procedure, which we shall discuss later when we consider the excellent amendments tabled by the hon. Member for Eastwood (Mr. Stewart).

A ballot would cause uncertainty in a school, but if the debate were made more complex and intense by a widening of the battle nationally or regionally to involve the Government, organisations such as the grant-maintained trusts in England and local—I am not exonerating anyone—that would not be healthy for education in the school.
