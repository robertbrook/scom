Mr. McLeish
: I welcome you back to the Committee, Mr. Martin.

We were stunned by the Conservative criticism that we often discuss clauses without tabling amendments. Therefore, to formalise the situation, we have tabled some amendments so that we may debate some of the issues involved now, and also on stand part later.

Our two amendments to clause 42 are probing amendments. We are concerned about "stopping the clock" when self-governing status is sought by a school board. In earlier debates we expressed our concern about staffing at the outset of the procedure to create a self-governing school. If the clock stops, there are transfer and movement problems and life is made very difficult for both the school and the education authority. Clause 42 is about the removal of property from a school by the education authority. In some respects, the clause is straightforward. However, there is the matter of the procedure that would be involved, which has been covered under clauses 33 to 41. We are concerned that that is written into statute. If the legislation exists, there is always a temptation to use it for some minor areas of property within a school. That may not be in the best interests of harmony between the school and the education authority at a particularly tense and difficult time.

There is the added problem that the Government are intent on making it as difficult as possible for the education authority to do anything once the procedure has started. I understand the Government's concern about what the education authority may get up to in relation to both staff and property, but a balance must be struck, as the education of children cannot stop just because a school is seeking self-governing status. Clearly, an 1148 element of movement of staff and property is essential.

We are worried about what might happen if a piece of property—for example, a temporary hut—is used as temporary accommodation in a school. That facility, provided by the education authority, may be in school X one day and then removed to school Y two or three months later. It would be absurd if there were a debate about whether that should be the property of the education authority or should remain the property—at least at that stage—of the self-governing school. All those minor headaches would arise, and the best way to resolve them would be through discussion between the two parties.

The Bill suggests that as soon as self-governing status is sought, war is declared between the two groups. That may or may not be the case. If a school is opting out because of potential closure, or after a period of bitter wrangling with the education authority, that may sow the seeds of trouble and discontent. However, I am assuming that other feelings will prevail and that the school and the education authority will have to consider the matter sensibly.

The key issue that seems to be missing from debates in Committee and elsewhere is what happens to the children. The baseline for the Opposition—I am sure that Conservative Members share our view—is quality of education. Throughout the first part of the Bill, which deals with self-governing schools, there is the danger that discontent, irritation and trouble could be fomented, partly because of the nature of the school and partly because of the pressures that are now being enshrined in legislation.

We are worried about the disruptive nature of the procedure for schools seeking to become self-governing. This provision is one more sign of potential problems.

These clauses are not technical clauses that should be passed on the nod and left to the Minister and civil servants to sort out. Subsection (1) refers to

"an education authority who have received a notice under section 13(6)".

1150 That is a written notice of the intention of a school board to ballot parents with a view to obtaining self-governing status. Under the clause, when an authority receives that notice it shall not

"until one of the conditions specified in subsection (2) of section 38 of this Act is satisfied … remove or withdraw from the premises of that school any moveable property which is owned or held by that authority wholly or partly for the purposes of that school."

The three conditions outlined are, first, the prior consent of the school board. It is unlikely that the school board will consent to giving up valuable property that it holds on behalf of the education authority until the procedure is completed. The second condition is that the result of the ballot shows that the school does not have a simple majority in favour of self-governing status, which could take weeks or months. The third condition is that the proposal for self-governing status is rejected by the Secretary of State for Scotland, which may or may not be likely.

So the clause amounts to a freeze on the recovery of any equipment by the education authority from a school once that school has shown its intention to ballot its parents on self-governing status. A series of questions remains to be answered. My hon. Friend the Member for Fife, Central (Mr. McLeish) referred to temporary huts. That is important. Several schools in Dundee have been repeatedly subjected to fire— raising as a result of which classrooms have been destroyed. The education authority holds a stock of huts to provide temporary accommodation until the insurance claims enable it to do up the classrooms. Those temporary huts belong to the education authority, not to particular schools. A school board could try to gain possession of those huts because the ballot of parents might take place when the huts are on the school premises.
