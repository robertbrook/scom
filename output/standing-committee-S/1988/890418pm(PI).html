How does one define ability and aptitude as opposed to the general notion of what people in Scotland would regard as admission on academic criteria? Our previous debate was interesting, and, to take it further, I obtained a document prepared in July 1986 for the Scottish Education Department by Ruth Jonathan. "Giftedness and Education" is one of the few reports that has been commissioned by any organisation in the United Kingdom to look more closely at what giftedness and education mean in terms of policy and achievement. Until it was produced, what little literature had emerged had come from the United States. The report discusses how an education authority or Government could best examine the distinction between general ability and aptitude and more specific selection by academic criteria. It is a complex report, but parts of it are germane to the Committee's difficulties of definition. It says:

"The term 'gifted' is used to indicate any child who is outstanding in either a general or specific ability, in a relatively broad or narrow field of endeavour... Where generally recognised tests exist as (say) in the case of 'intelligence', then giftedness would be defined by test scores. Where no recognised tests exist it can be assumed that the subjective opinions of originality and imagination displayed would be the criteria we have in mind."

That highlights, perhaps more academically, one of our previous debates. We argue against the idea that giftedness may be interpreted by reference to recognised tests of intelligence so that test scores will determine whether a child goes to one school or another.

The report then quotes the Marland report of 1972 to the United States Congress, which defined giftedness as follows:

"Gifted and talented children are those identified by professionally qualified persons (and) who by virtue of outstanding abilities are capable of high performance. These are children who require differentiated educational programs and services beyond those normally provided by the regular school programme in order to realise their contribution to self and society.


Children capable of high performance include those with demonstrated achievement and/or potential ability in any of the following areas: (a) general intellectual ability, (b) specific academic aptitude, (c) creative or productive thinking, (d) leadership ability, (e) visual and performing arts, (f) psychomotor ability."

738 That provides a useful analytical basis for the Scottish Office and the Minister to look more closely at how to define aptitude and ability to assuage the Opposition's fears that this is an attempt to bring in by the back door what we would call crude academic selection but what others might call selection on intelligence.
