My hon. Friends will wish to contribute to the debate in more detail. I wish to address myself to, and justify, the specific amendments.

Amendment No. 152 amends clause 21, which empowers the Secretary of State to designate any visiting employee irrespective of the number of hours spent in the school. For example, a peripatetic music teacher might spend one morning in the school out of a whole working week spent in other schools managed by the authority. It is important to secure such a teacher's continued service to the school, but it is surely wrong to place him or her under the jurisdiction of a board of management rather than the education authority.

The clause tackles the issue in the wrong way. It would be better to provide that the education authority must continue to provide the services of part-time staff rather than empower the Secretary of State to compel the transfer of employment on the basis of only a few hours' work.

Amendment No. 152 is straightforward and it balances the interests of continuity of education in the self-governing school, the host education authority, and the employees. It is right to argue for protection of employees during a period of turmoil, and we might return to that issue later.

The object of amendment No. 64 is to provide freedom, protection and flexibility for employees after the opting-out ballot is under way—a crucial period for school and staff. The amendment expresses the Opposition's anxiety that employees might be transferred against their own interests and aspirations to a school with very different characteristics from that in which they were employed before the incorporation date. An education authority is obviously different from a board of management of a self-governing school.

Career prospects will emerge as an important issue. Such prospects—and I emphasise the word "may"—be much better under the authority than under the board of management. An employee should be able to reflect on prospects in order to make a judgment about them. The Opposition are also worried that a teacher might object to working for an employer whose approach to education is different from that of the authority. Our anxiety about that is linked to clause 28, which allows the characteristics of the school to be changed through a ballot. A member of staff involved in a transfer may face uncertainty two or three years later as a result of clause 28, when, before, the characteristics of the school were, to all intents and purposes, similar to those that he enjoyed under the education authority.

812 The argument is about natural justice for the employee and educational efficiency. An individual should have the right to remain within the education authority sector. We are talking about two sets of circumstances—the school in which the teacher finds himself, and his status and contract with the education authority. The best balance could be struck by allowing an employee to have the best of all worlds, where he can make a judgment about the relative merits of career prospects either in a self-governing school or under an education authority. That is not an unreasonable request to be made on behalf of an employee who makes a valuable contribution. It has nothing to do with national or local politics or the internal politics of a school.

I have painted a picture in which an employee could be adversely affected by a school's move to self-governing status, but clearly the opposite may happen. However, the balance of our judgment is that certain factors may reduce the quality of long-term career prospects and conditions of service if an employee moves to a self-governing school.

Amendments Nos. 137 and 138 try to tackle the question of transfer. At present there are contractual obligations in Scotland between employees and the employing authority—the education authority. Those obligations, which have evolved over many years, try to strike a balance between educational efficiency, the children's interests and the proper safeguarding of employees' rights. We are concerned about the freeze on staffing provided for in clause 23, which could last for at least three months and possibly much longer. It seems to be an encroachment on individual freedom for a system to freeze the movement of staff at a school, or adversely to affect the movement of staff within an authority, on the assumption that that school is remaining in the education authority sector.

There are pluses and minuses to the issue. We have talked about the positive aspects, but circumstances may arise in which the dismissal of a teacher is actively considered by the parties providing education. We have touched on safeguards for teachers, but we must be more specific. If employees are drawn into the freezing of movement and transfer and end up being employed by an opted-out school, that constitutes a major threat to their status.

We sincerely hope that the Government will be flexible in considering this clause, linked to clause 23. If not, teaching and non-teaching staff will be justifiably worried about their future. It should be left to the Secretary of State to discuss the issue further with the education authority, the teaching unions and people involved in education before a judgment is made.

If the Government are unwilling to accept the amendments, we ask for further consultation. We have tried to thread that characteristic through our debates in Committee, but there has been very little consultation. However, if the Bill becomes law, there may be activity on that front. When dealing with enormous numbers of staff in potentially sensitive situations, it is sensible for consultation to be held before legislation is drafted and therefore difficult to change, rather than to alienate support and generate JPEG 813 fear among employees in Scottish education, by passing legislation that provides no scope whatever for consultation, especially in regard to the freeze process.
