Mr. Henry McLeish
(Fife, Central)
: I beg to move amendment No. 208, in page 43, line 40, at end insert—

"(1A) Schemes for the appraisal of teaching staff must be part of a coherent and negotiated programme of staff development.".

There is a danger, in discussing this amendment, that we may stray into the clause stand part debate, so I shall be brief.

The main issue is to ensure that any discussion of teacher appraisal is seen against the background of staff development. In the aftermath of the teachers' dispute, discussions ensued in the Scottish joint negotiating committee and then appeared in Government circulars. In Scotland, there has been a debate about staff development, and most of the teaching unions and education authorities accept that it is a crucial part of a modern, progressive education system.

The emphasis in the consultation paper issued by the Scottish Education Department in February is slightly worrying because, although appraisal is seen as part of staff development, there is an increasing stress on the importance of appraising teacher performance. The amendment will ensure that teacher appraisal forms part of a wider staff development programme; be that in the Scottish Education Department, the education authorities or the teaching unions.

The stand part debate will hinge on the fact that teacher appraisal can be approached in two ways. If it is conceived as part of staff development, most people will accept that some form of appraisal is necessary, especially if it identifies the strengths and weaknesses of an individual teacher. Appropriate resourcing and teacher support will then be given by the education authorities if that is agreed by individual staff members and the teaching unions. However, staff appraisal could be conceived in a way that would make Scottish teachers and education authorities apprehensive. That would occur if staff appraisal was seen in isolation from the wider staff development issues. In that process, appraisal could lead to consequences that would not be in the interest of staff members or the children whom they teach. The distinction between the two approaches will be made clearer during the stand part debate.

The amendment will ensure that everyone understands that the appraisal of teachers is only one JPEG 1379 element of a wider staff development programme. On the stand part debate we shall also discuss the fundamental distinction between dealing with issues at a local level through discussions between teachers, their representatives and the education authorities, and the imposition of national principles and regulations, which would lead to uniformity throughout Scotland and which would not be in the long-term interests of education.
