Self-Governing Schools etc. (Scotland) Bill

10.30 am

The Chairman
: Order. There are two honourable Members present who are not entitled to be on this Committee. They know of the powers that the House has given me. I ask them to leave. [Interruption.] Order. The honourable Members are not permitted to speak. [ Interruption]. Order.

Motion made, and Question proposed,

That during the procedings on the Self-Governing Schools etc. (Scotland) Bill, the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock and at half-past Four o'clock.— [Mr. Michael Forsyth.]

Mr. Henry McLeish
(Fife, Central)
: We shall oppose the sittings motion and, in doing so, we shall take the opportunity to discuss what most Scots regard as a uniquely objectionable Bill. The sittings motion is about the allocation of time and we shall try to impress upon the Government the view that the way in which they have handled the Bill so far is, politically, a disgrace. We shall illustrate that and identify what has gone on in the past 18 months under the supervision of the Under-Secretary, the honourable Member for Stirling (Mr. Forsyth).

Activities this week have shown that there is deep anger in Scotland about the Bill. We have dissociated ourselves from what we regard as the student antics of the Scottish National party, but I appreciate its frustration, as I understand that of my honourable Friends. Never in the past 30 to 40 years has a Bill been developed in such a shabby manner and never before in the history of Scottish education since the war has an Under-Secretary of State been at war with education interests, including teachers, parents, the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities and the Churches. After 18 months of neglect by the Government, we now have, as I have stated, a uniquely objectionable Bill.

It is far too easy for the Opposition to indulge in the type of protest to which I referred. However, there is a more meaningful way of demonstrating to Scots and honourable Members the odious nature of the proposals. That is what we intend to do this morning and throughout the stages of the Bill. We warn the Government that, although we have modest expectations about amending the Bill, the Committee stage is only part of a much wider campaign throughout Scotland. It is a friendless Bill and opting-out is poison to Scottish education. Every conceivable interest stands organised to fight it. But that is of no consequence to the Under-Secretary of State. He is on auto-pilot. He is not listening. His ideology has set him on a course 8 where he needs his close political hard-Right colleagues on the Committee.

There is something even more significant about our attack: we will not give up the fight against the Bill, even when it becomes an Act. We will ensure that from the Orkneys to the Border every voter is aware of the utter contempt of Scottish education shown by the Scottish Office and, more particularly, by the Under-Secretary.

It is sad that, unlike under Rab Butler, whom I quoted on Second Reading, we now have a dramatic deterioration of political standards. A system that has been built up over decades of painstaking work by many organisations is now to be dismantled at the whim of a Minister.

I should have thought that the Secretary of State for Scotland would be present this morning. The access that the Under-Secretary has to the Prime Minister is the corrosive influence on Scottish education. The Secretary of State should take control of his Minister's activities.

The Chairman

Mr. McLeish

The sittings motion is about timing. I want to demonstrate that the way in which the Government are handling the sittings motion merely heaps insult upon injury.

The Bill requires more time because Scottish education has been betrayed. I want to put on the record why the Bill and the Committee are a disgrace. On 27 May 1987, The Scotsman said that at a Conservative news conference in Edinburgh, both the Scottish chairman, Lord Goold, and the Minister responsible had confirmed that the Government would be pleased to see opting-out allowed in English schools, but not in Scotland.

Secondly, on BBC Radio Scotland on 9 April 1988, the Prime Minister was asked whether she would confirm that opting out of local authority control would go ahead in Scotland. She replied:

"No, I cannot confirm that it will definitely go ahead. The first thing we had to do was to see that every school has a school board."

 Can the Under-Secretary say how many school boards have been established in Scotland?

The right honourable Lady went on to say that the Government would have to take further soundings to see whether sufficient number of people

"would like to have opting out as an option."

 Nearer home, the Under-Secretary of State said in the Scottish Grand Committee on 21 March 1988:

"We have always made it clear that if there is evidence of real and substantial demand for opting out, we would not stand in the way."— [Official Report, Scottish Grand Committee, 21 March 1988; c.37].

 JPEG 9 Finally, when debating the School Boards (Scotland) Bill, the Secretary of State for Scotland said on 12 April 1988:

"I have always made clear, in the election period and since, that given that we do not have a history of school boards or governors in Scotland of the kind that we are now creating, the first stage must be their creation. As my honourable Friend the Under-Secretary and others have said, we must then gauge whether there is a demand for a further power."— [Official Report, 12 April 1988; Vol. 131, c.34–5.]

 The Government's behaviour is a political disgrace. Lord Goold, the Prime Minister, the Secretary of State for Scotland and the Under-Secretary are all on record as saying to the Scottish people that there would not be a Bill for opting-out until school boards had been established. Does the Under-Secretary want to intervene now to tell us why he has betrayed the trust of Scots on this issue? That is why we need more time.
