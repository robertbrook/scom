Clause 59
POWER OF EDUCATION AUTHORITIES TO FORM COMPANIES TO MANAGE COLLEGES OF FURTHER EDUCATION

Mr. Sam Galbraith
(Strathkelvin and Bearsden)
: I beg to move amendment No. 269, in page 40, line 23, at end insert

"provided that such matters do not embrace activities which are detrimental to the provision of further education.".

This clause is about the power of education authorities to form companies to manage colleges of further education—a provision to which we are not opposed.

When a company is so formed and starts to set up committees, there is the danger of a conflict—intentional or otherwise—in that the generation of income and profit becomes an end in itself rather than an adjunct to the good conduct of, and for the benefit of, the college or institution. That is why we 1260 should like the amendment inserted at the end of the clause.

Opposition Members are not opposed to the principles involved, but we should like to put a belt and braces on the provision to ensure that it is not detrimental, although the number of times that it would be detrimental are no doubt limited.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Michael Forsyth)
: I welcome you to our proceedings, Miss Boothroyd.

The amendment is helpful. The Educational Institute of Scotland, which was responsible for its genesis, fears that some companies set up to manage further education colleges may engage in non-educational activities, which could overwhelm their further education role.

However, there is a difficulty with the drafting of the amendment. It is difficult to get the correct balance of wording to allow scope for activity while not producing a definition that may be the subject of endless argument about what is in the interests of the college. If the hon. Member for Strathkelvin and Bearsden (Mr. Galbraith) will seek to withdraw the amendment, I shall consider whether we can find a form of words and return to the matter on Report. I cannot give the hon. Gentleman an absolute guarantee, but we shall try to meet his requirements.

Mr. Edward Leigh
(Gainsborough and Horncastle)

Mr. Forsyth

There would be difficulties if the education authority did not agree to corporate status. The clause, therefore, provides for that.

Mr. Forsyth

Against us are the Minister backed up by his civil servants and Conservative Back Benches replete with hon. Members who have been in the House for 10 years. They read the Bill line by line, suggest amendments and consider others to see whether they should add their name to them. Therefore, I am amazed that none of them picked up this problem. They have not even added their names to the amendment. Greatly experienced Conservative Members pore over the Bill, burn the midnight oil and do not go out at night to the pub. They read every line, take out Webster's dictionary to check the meanings and then consult their lawyers, but they do not see the problem with this clause. Therefore, in a spirit of co-operation, the Opposition have tabled the amendment to help out the Government.

Having seen the amendment, the Minister will realise that the problem with clause 61 is that subsection (3) is unclear. The Bill mentions further education, but it means education authority education. Indeed, clause 69 states:

"'colleges of further education' means an educational establishment, under the management of an education authority, for the provision of any form of further education".

That is the definition in the Bill, but clause 61(3)(a) refers to

"any group of teaching staff employed in or in connection with the provision of further education in Scotland".

1266 That seems to mean education authority provision.

The SJNC, which the clause will abolish, was set up under the Education (Scotland) Act 1981, which amended the Education (Scotland) Act 1980. The SJNC refers to the central institutions and colleges of education as well as education authority colleges. Clause 61(2Xb) deals with

"a settlement formulated under section 94(1 Kb) of the 1980 Act".

Therefore, we assume that the clause covers the negotiating rights in both central institutions and the colleges of education. We do not necessarily agree with the abolition of the SJNC—we shall come to that later—but we believe that the clause intends to cover all the institutions. However, clause 61 (3) refers to further education and the matter is not clear.

The amendment is designed to clarify the position and help the experienced Government and their Back Benches who pore over such things but for some reason have not picked up the problem. That is done in a spirit of wishing to help because we do not want endless, tedious disputes in the courts when the Bill is enacted. Mr. McAllion

The unilateral abolition is strange because, although the idea for the committee sprang from the 1974 Houghton report, it was established by the same Conservative Government through the Education (Scotland) Act 1980 and later through the Education (Scotland) Act 1981.

This morning, several Conservative Members spoke about 10 glorious years of Conservative rule. No doubt they were all present at a London hotel yesterday and paid £35 each for the doubtful privilege of listening to the Prime Minister reminiscing and drinking wine that one of them described as fit for fluid for a car battery. It is obvious that the Government have made mistakes during those 10 glorious years as they are going back on something that they set up. They are trying to abolish the Scottish joint negotiating committee. What are their reasons for abolishing a committee that they set up to facilitate national wage agreements and agreements on conditions of work for people in the further education sector?

The Secretary of State gave some of the reasons. I note that he is absent again although he claimed in a debate last night that he was a leading member of the Committee. Perhaps he thinks that today is Wednesday because he thought that yesterday was Thursday. He does not know what day it is so perhaps he thinks that the Committee is not sitting today because he believes it is Wednesday. Almost a year ago he was asked about the abolition of the SJNC. He said:

"In the Government's view the 40-strong statutory committee has proved a cumbersome and ineffective forum for negotiating the pay and conditions of academic staff in the local authority further education colleges and in the centrally-funded colleges.

If we examine the first reason, we could read into the words "cumbersome and ineffective" the fact that the Government believe that the SJNC is not hard enough on people working in the public education sector. It has allowed them to earn too decent wages and secure too decent conditions of employment.

There are two distinct sectors in further education. The Secretary of State said that they had different funding sources and different conditions of service, and that the pressures facing management were different. He was referring to the further education colleges run by education authorities and to the central institutions and colleges of education, which have their own governing bodies and are funded directly by the Scottish Education Department. He tried to explain further why it was inappropriate to allow the SJNC to continue to negotiate for both sectors. He said:

"progress has been disappointing … from time to time in recent years we have received representations from both management and staff interests about the unsatisfactory nature of the present arrangements."— [Official Report, 24 June 1988; Vol. 1454; c. 749.]

When the Minister replies to the clause stand part debate, I hope that he will say what those representations were.

We have received the amendments tabled by the trade unions representing the staff of the SJNC, which 1270 suggested that the whole clause should be deleted. Far from making representations to the Minister that the staff are unhappy with the SJNC, the union says that the SJNC, which was constituted in January 1982,

"has operated effectively, using negotiating sub-committees where necessary for local authority and for centrally-funded colleges."

That is not what the Secretary of State suggested in his parliamentary answer. The union continued:

"The staff value its statutory protection for salaries and conditions of service. It has never provoked the prolonged industrial conflicts experienced in the schools and the universities."

"The staff have demonstrated a flexible approach to the problems posed by the separate funding arrangements for the different college sectors"

by the creation of two separate negotiating subcommittees. It winds up by saying:

"The committee was prompted by the Houghton Report of 1974 which stressed the need to ensure that salaries in the different sectors maintained a relationship which reflected similarities of work. This remains a sound objective. The SJNC (FE) has been effective in pursuing it."

That is the staff side, yet the Secretary of State says in a parliamentary answer that he has had representations from the staff about the unsatisfactory nature of the present arrangements. The two do not gel, and the Minister must explain to the Committee who is telling the truth. Is the staff union telling the truth when it says that the SJNC has been doing a good job, or is the Secretary of State for Scotland telling the truth when he says that the staff have approached him to say that they are dissatisfied with how the SJNC has been doing its job?

If the Government intend to abolish the SJNC, with what will they replace it? Several references have been made to the Government's consultation document, issued in 1987, which sets out their intentions. The section headed "Responsibilities as to Staffing" refers to the delegation of staffing matters to the college councils and says:

"Such delegation of staffing matters would not affect national agreements currently in force on pay and conditions for college staff, but subject to such agreements, the Government expects that councils would be free to determine the numbers and grading of college staff without reference to the education authority, within the discipline of their approved budgets. The staff would, however, remain as employees of the education authority."

So the consultation document says that staff will remain as the employees of the education authority and that their wages and working conditions will remain subject to the national agreements arrived at through the SJNC. That was the Government's intention when they issued the consultation document in 1987.

"My officials stand ready to assist the various parties in devising alternative arrangements which better reflect the needs of management and staff at all levels in the two sectors."— [Official Report, 24 June 1988; Vol 135, c. 750.]

So the Secretary of State said that his officials would help staff and management to make alternative arrangements to replace the statutory provision of the SJNC. However, the staff and management disagree. The management want the SJNC to be abolished and the staff want it to be retained. I should have thought that if the Government are to play a role, it should be to find agreement between employers and staff, not to take the side of the employers alone and then only in part.

Mr. McAllion

But that is not what the Minister said when he first spoke in this stand part debate. He said that the Government do not intend to make proposals. Employers will pay whatever they can afford. He said nothing about national agreements, but said that what the education authority can afford to pay staff would depend on its policy.

Mrs. Fyfe
: Does my hon. Friend agree that subsection (3)(a) negates what the Minister has just said? It is easy to foresee local groups breaking away from any agreements that might be reached.

Mr. McAllion
: My hon. Friend is right. The Bill refers to

"any group of teaching staff'.

There could be an equivalent Professional Association of Teachers for the FE sector. The Minister's eyes light up with delight and he thinks, "Great. A non-striking body to support the Conservative party." He would love to see such bodies defy whatever national agreements might be reached by COSLA and the staff.

The Minister and his Back Benchers intend corporate colleges to emerge in Scotland. They do not have to listen to whatever is agreed between COSLA and the staff, but can go their own way. The Minister said that it will be up to the employers to pay whatever they can afford. If a corporate college can afford to pay its staff more, it should do so. If a corporate college cannot afford to pay more, it should pay less.

The Minister wants to have it COSLA's way, the Secretary of State for Scotland's way and his own way, with a completely free market. He cannot have it three ways: he can have it only one way. Either there are national agreements that take the place of the SJNC, or there are no national agreements. I conclude from the Minister's comments that there will be no national agreements. The education authorities and their employees can get together, by all means, to try and form a body that will arrive at a national agreement for them, but they will be undermined and undercut by the freedom given to any group of teachers and any college to break away.

I remind the Minister of the 19 institutions in England that are being looked at, to see whether they are fit to look after their own affairs. Where corporate colleges are given the freedom to make their own arrangements with their own staff, they will immediately attack the employment in the college, and attack the contracts, reducing wages and affecting working conditions. That is what the clause is about, and that is why we shall vote against it.
