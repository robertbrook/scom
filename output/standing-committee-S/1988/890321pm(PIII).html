On the financial side, our commitment is to ensure that a self-governing school is no worse off, or no better off, than if it were still in the control of the education authority. Furthermore, the education authority has a continuing and direct role to play in special needs provision.

Under section 4 of the Education (Scotland) Act 1980 the regional psychological service has a duty to all children living in its area, irrespective of the school that they attend. If a child has recorded special needs, the education authority has a continuing involvement in assessment and placing decisions. Often, in the case of such children, decisions about significant expenditure to provide specialist facilities at their local school will have to be made. Where the most appropriate school for a child is self-governing, the education authority, in discharging its duty to provide for children with special needs, would have to consider whether to put the necessary additional resources into that school or to oblige the child to travel to a second education authority school. Children's performance at school must be affected if they do not have their own room or books at home, do not get to bed until 2 or 3 o'clock in the morning or do not have breakfast. Those factors are reflected in academic results.

I recently spoke to teachers at a school in one of the worst parts of Dundee. They were very concerned about how the Bill would deal with teacher performance. They wanted to know whether their performance would be judged by the school's academic record. That school could not have a good academic record in the present circumstances because of the area. The Minister would say that it was a poor school, but nothing could be further from the truth. It has less trouble than any other school in Dundee because it tries to provide the education and environment appropriate to its pupils. The pupils like attending the school and the teachers like teaching them. Everyone gets something out of it. The pupils might not do as well in exams as pupils in other schools, but that is determined by factors beyond the school's control. If the Minister is serious about improving performance in Scottish schools, he should tackle those factors, such as unemployment, poverty and bad housing. Instead, he sits back and encourages unemployment, poverty and bad housing and seeks to blame teachers and Opposition Members.

This is a bad Bill and a bad clause. The only people who will vote for it are those whose political career depends on it—Conservative Back Benchers who do not understand it and Conservative Front Benchers whose ministerial position depends on it. If the honourable Tories had been here tonight, they would have voted with us. That is a sign of the times for the Conservative party in Scotland.
