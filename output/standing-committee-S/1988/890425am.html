Mr. Henry McLeish (Fife, Central)
: The Opposition want to spend time on the stand part debate because the Committee worked through the amendments quickly last Thursday.

It is, however, difficult to know where to start, because of an article in The Scotsman, which is entitled, "Rector launches bitter attack on Thatcherite 'barbarism'", and is about Scottish education. Because the article has caused such a furore, perhaps the Committee should examine the assessment of achievement programme in mathematics in Scotland. I hope that the Minister will reply to the criticisms of that programme, and of the Government and Scottish Office role in it. Being charitable, the Opposition do not wish to delay the proceedings by discussing such important matters now, but we shall return to them later. They are relevant to clause 25, which is about finance, and to clause 28, which is about the ability of opted-out schools to change their characteristics.

Finance is important. Few people in Scotland accept the Government's proposition that the financing of education authority schools and opted-out schools will be even-handed. It is feared that the complex financial structure, which the Government will have to deploy to tackle the problem, will give the Secretary of State for Scotland and his Ministers unlimited power to manipulate the finances of opted-out schools in order to make them at least superficially attractive. The complexity of the financial arrangements provides opportunities for exploitation in favour of opted-out schools. A set of guidelines, published in England and Wales after the Education Reform Act 1988, tries to tackle the financial arrangements required for grant-maintained status. I hope that the Committee will refer to that during this debate.

There are three types of grant that the Government can give to grant-aided schools: capital grants, recurrent grants and special purpose grants. Recurrent grants are ostensibly based on a working out of finance between the education authority and the Government. The education authority should, 864 therefore, be no worse off, and the grant-aided school, no better off. There are, however, major weaknesses in the structure of recurrent grants, which allow the Government to provide extra assistance to grant-aided schools.

It is clear from the English financial guidelines that grant-aided schools will be able to deploy two other means of supplementing their capital grants. They can sell assets, and although they should apply to the Secretary of State in order to do that, they have an edge on education authority schools in terms of property and land, which can be sold to invest in the capital equipment of the school. The English guidelines suggest that grant-aided schools will be able to raise funds from other sources for capital programmes.

Paragraph 43 of the financial arrangements guidelines for grant-maintained schools, laid down by the Department of Education and Science states:

"Governing bodies may also wish to finance capital expenditure for which they are not successful in obtaining capital grant from within their AMG or from funds raised from other sources. They will be free to do so, but the Secretary of State's approval may be required where the capital project would have the effect of increasing the school's entitlement to annual maintenance grant."

Built into the English regulations, therefore, are ways for grant-aided schools to steal a march on the education authority by raising funds from other sources or by the disposal of assets.

Extraordinary suggestions can be found in the special purpose grant proposals in the financial guidelines for England. Those suggestions might enable opted-out schools to use the special purpose grant to boost the finances of the grant-aided sector, and to embark on staff changes unavailable to the education authority.

The allocation of special services and the working out of the financial arrangements for apportioning them to grant-aided schools are fraught with difficulties. If a portion of the special advisory services were to be lost to such a school, the education authority's efficiency and its ability to provide the same level of services would be impaired. Moreover, at a time when the poll tax will bite because of the gearing effect, education authorities could face a dilemma if the financial arrangements are not worked out even-handedly.

The Opposition's cynicism derives from discussions with a variety of organisations that fear that control of financial arrangements gives the Government not only an economic, but a major political tool with which to attack and undermine the education authority sector. Cynicism also arises from awareness of the real agenda of the new Right of the Conservative party with regard to both the Bill and the Education Reform Act in England.

What is the real agenda? Opting-out is a political joke in Scotland. It is difficult to find anybody who will engage in a serious conversation about the issue. That is regrettable. Underlying opting-out is the concept of "privatisation by stealth" as described by Dr Jack Demaine, a senior lecturer in the department of education at Loughborough university, in an ACE report. He believes that such privatisation is the hallmark of the new Right's education policy.
