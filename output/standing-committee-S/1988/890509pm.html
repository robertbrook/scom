Mr. McFall
: We support the use of tests in schools. The Minister may look surprised, but we have always supported the use of tests for their proper purpose of diagnostic assessment. Tests are necessary to give teachers and parents a better understanding of the needs and progress of an individual child. They also give teachers an idea of the quality of their teaching and of the materials and methods that they use.

The Government's intended introduction of national testing at primary 4 and primary 7 will not do that. It will separate pupils in order of rank. Results will be published and comparisons between schools will be made. It will be a return to the old qualy system, where young people sat a qualifying exam at 11 years of age and were then separated into different schools. The Government regard that as a supposed golden age, but in Scotland it was a golden age when 80 per cent. of pupils failed and only 20 per cent. passed. The Government's proposed national testing scheme will be even more divisive. Pupils will be labelled as failures at the ripe old age of nine, when any form of selection automatically implies a form of rejection.

The Government's proposals on national testing have attracted more criticism than any other in the Bill. National testing will undermine the equality 1324 of opportunity that has been developed in Scottish primary and secondary schools over the years. We said that the School Boards (Scotland) Bill would result in a political battleground for each school. In the same way, national testing will pit school against school and teacher against teacher. That is the fundamental reason why we oppose it.

There were many responses to the Government's proposals on curriculum and assessment of November 1982. The Scottish Consultative Council on the Curriculum, which advises the Secretary of State for Scotland, gave its response, as did local authorities. I presume that Strathclyde, which has figured in all our debates so far, also responded. "Whereas testing in primary schools is universal and part of a teacher's responsibility, and whereas diagnostic testing is to be welcomed as being in the good interests of the child, the development of any system of standardised national testing which is normative will inevitably lead to a change in attitude to tests by pupils, teachers and parents, that is likely to be detrimental to the interests of the child."

What is the educational justification for the testing?

The Scotsman stated in one of its leaders that there was no genuine attempt to reach a consensus about a programme of improvement in the Bill. Those remarks are especially appropriate to national testing.
