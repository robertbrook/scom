Clause 22
FURTHER PROVISION AS REGARDS TEACHERS IN SELF-GOVERNING SCHOOLS.

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 70, in page 13, line 19, leave out subsection (1) and insert—

'(1) Only registered teachers shall be employed, or continue to be employed, as teachers in self-governing schools.'.

4.30 pm

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.— [Mr. McAllion.]

The Chairman
: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking the following amendments:

No. 55, in page 13, line 19, leave out 'may' and insert 'shall'.

No. 56, in page 13, line 21, leave out

'subject to such exception or may from time to time be prescribed'.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Michael Forsyth)
: I was about to enlighten the hon. Member for Moray (Mrs. Ewing) as to the relevant terms of section 90 of the Education (Scotland) Act 1980. Subsection (1) reads:

"The Secretary of State may"—

I repeat "may" because that wording is reflected in clause 22—

"in regulations under section 2 or 74(1) of this Act prescribe that only registered teachers shall be employed or continue to be employed as teachers by education authorities in the educational establishments to which the regulations apply, subject to such exceptions as may from time to time be so prescribed."

Hon. Members will recall the familiar ring of that final phrase from clause 22. In other words, we are merely applying to self-governing schools the same provisions and policies that operate in the maintained sector.

The regulations prescribe that every teacher employed by an education authority shall be a registered teacher. They also provide that where an education authority is unable to find a person holding qualifications appropriate to the specific post that it is seeking to fill, it may either, with the approval of the Secretary of State, appoint permanently a registered teacher who does not hold the precise qualifications appropriate to that post, or it may make a temporary appointment of a registered teacher and continue the search for someone with the appropriate qualifications. In all cases, however, the person appointed must be a registered teacher. Our intention is to make regulations in precisely those terms for self-governing schools.

Mr. McLeish
: These important amendments are to a clause that deals with the financial structure of self-governing schools, the consequent relationship with education authority provision and the financial set-up involved.

Two general issues have worried many organisations, especially local authorities, since the introduction of the consultative paper on the Bill. The first is the funding that would be provided to a self-governing school. In discussions in the Committee and elsewhere, assurances have been made that self-governing schools should be no better and no worse off in any financial year than they would have been had they remained within the education authority sector. It is important to debate that generally when we reach clause stand part. These are essentially probing amendments to seek further clarification, guidance and assurance on what that will mean in practice.

The financial arrangements for England and Wales, which were the subject of a Department of Education and Science paper, raise wider anxieties about how the funding is determined and how self-governing and education authority schools face up to the practical implications of that. We are worried about that general issue.

Our second main worry is specifically about the services that education authorities provide at present. Their delivery and financial effectiveness may be fragmented by several schools seeking self-governing status in a particular authority. Specialist advice is available on many areas of the curriculum. Providing a certain sum of money for specialist services in an education authority where schools opt out involves a basic financial problem. If a proportion of funding is given to newly established self-governing schools, despite having lost the cash to cover those specialist services, the authority may be left with a commitment to and staffing for those services that cannot be changed merely because expenditure is earmarked for the opted-out schools. The amendments try to deal with those broad and with more specific areas.

Amendment No. 210 is a simple probing amendment. It seeks to discover whether there is a genuine commitment to ensuring that the funding remains much the same. On the face of it, that could be the intention and reality of clause 25. It covers recurrent grant, capital grants and special purpose grants. Local government expenditure is immensely complex, as those with local government experience know. The uncertainty about this issue is the result of that complexity. Although we may have political differences on many issues, we must ensure that the local authority is not at a significant disadvantage after the mechanics and practicalities of the formula are worked out. Therefore, amendment No. 210 seeks to examine the general question of funding and to obtain on record the Minister's comments on the balance between the new sector and the existing education authority sector.

I mentioned that some services are, in terms of economy of scale, cost-effective for education authorities to provide, whether the authorities are small, or large like Strathclyde. That emerges in amendment No. 211. But there is a fundamental problem with the allocation of the wide range of services. The Member indentified many of them in his response to the hon. Member for Eastwood during our discussions on clause 24. We are worried that, unless the Scottish Office and the Government get it right, we may be 852 helping the opted-out sector. I am sure that the Minister does not want the education authorities to be undermined by the range of their provisions.

Earlier I gave a specific example of specialist services. Let us now take the example of a maths adviser in my own authority of Fife. If we use the simple concept of removing 10 per cent, of funding from the equation because some schools opt out, the authority will be faced with the dilemma of funding that person with reduced income, or changing the structure to dispense with him, or to amalgamate or rationalise in that area. That is an extreme example of education authority concerns.
