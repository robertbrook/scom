MARRIAGE (SCOTLAND) BILL. [Lords.]

title OFFICIAL REPORT.
clause NEW CLAUSE.
title —(Authority for proclaiming intended marriages, etc.)

The following churches shall be and hereby are authorised to proclaim intended marriages and to issue banns of intended marriages to members or adherents of their respective churches, and to make such regulations for this purpose, and for the issue of all necessary certificates, provided such regulations are submitted to and approved by the Registrar General of Scotland:

  • The Baptist Church;
  • The Catholic Church;
  • The Congregational Church;
  • The Episcopal Church in Scotland;
  • The Free Church in Scotland;
  • The Free Presbyterian Church;
  • The Jewish Church;
  • The Methodist Church;
  • The Reformed Presbyterian Church of Scotland;
  • The United Free Church of Scotland; The United Original Secession Church.

Mr. Robert Gibson: I beg to move, "That the Clause be read a Second time."

The purpose of the Clause is to extend the authority for proclaiming intended marriage. Hon. Members will recall that, according to the present law of Scotland, proclamation of marriage banns may be made only in the Church of Scotland. But the Morison Committee felt that that was a slur on the other churches in Scotland, because ministers of other churches in Scotland had the right, at any time and in any place, to perform the marriage ceremony. They had that right in common with ministers of the Church of Scotland. That is the one concession that is made in the Report, but the Bill does not give effect to that concession. It was the first and foremost finding of the Col. 1596 Morison Committee and, accordingly, it may well be taken as the consideration, or the price for the violent changes that are sought to be imported into the marriage law of Scotland by the Bill. It may be that the price is not a very big one; that it is in fact of the dimensions of thirty pieces of silver. Yet the Government are not prepared to pay even that miserable price for the great betrayal of the principle of the Scottish law of marriage which is contained in the Bill. That is a shocking state of affairs. I do not wish to recapitulate anything that I said at the last meeting, because what I said then is being pondered over in Scotland—and with very grave misgivings. The Tory Press, after our last meeting, was whimpering very loudly because the Government are again being found out in a betrayal of principle.

In effect, this question of banns is a very small matter, and I cannot understand the Government not honouring this unanimous and foremost finding of the Morison Committee. If there is any virtue at all in banns, surely proclamation in the church where the parties are known is much more efficacious than proclamation in a church where neither of the parties is known. That is particularly the case in the large centres of population. Unfortunately, we have reached a stage when church-going is not so common as it used to be. Even at best, the proclamation of banns is not an effective method of bringing to the notice of the population of Scotland the fact that two residents in Scotland intend to contract marriage. But if there is virtue in banns, they ought to be proclaimed in the church where the parties are known. That conclusion is borne out by the facts with regard to bigamous marriages contracted in 1937. In that year 54 bigamous marriages were brought within the notice of the Courts, resulting in 58 persons being charged with bigamy. Of these 54 marriages, 25 were regular marriages performed by ministers of religion. Of these 25 marriages, 12 were preceded by proclamation of banns. Accordingly, the purpose of the proclamation of banns failed, in that it did not produce an objection to these persons being married on the ground that one or other was already married to somebody else, and so was not in a position to contract marriage according to the law of Scot- Image Col. 1597 land or any other Christian country, where monogamy is the rule.

That being the position, the effectiveness of the banns would, in my submission, very clearly be increased if they were made applicable in the church to which one or other of the parties belonged, or with which they were connected. The proclamation would then be made in the presence of the people who knew the parties with some degree of intimacy. If the Government are not prepared to honour this foremost condition in the Morison report, they are striking a very serious blow at the whole principle of banns, and a very serious position will arise on the Report stage, because it may be necessary then to move to exclude banns altogether, as a pre-requisite of marriage in Scotland. Accordingly I move this Motion with very deep confidence.

I should be very sorry indeed to appear to give an opening to any controversy between various churches and denominations in Scotland, but I am bound to say I have a good deal of sympathy with the view that, as a method of publication, the publication of banns is a little bit obsolete, and for that reason, if for no other, I am a little surprised at anxiety being expressed from any quarter that this method should be more used than it is. After all, it belongs to the same theory of publication as that of which we have relics in Scots law, whereby requirements are made in old Statutes that certain facts are to be published from the Market Cross in Edinburgh, and from the pier and shore at Leith, and things of that kind. The publication of banns does nobody any harm. It is a thing to which obviously many people attach great sentimental value because I notice from the figures that the hon. and learned Gentleman got in answer to his question, that in 1937, out of a total of about 33,000 marriages, no less than 25,000 were preceded by proclamation of banns as against 7,600 by publication of notice. We have to accept the position that the people of Scotland obviously attach value to this method, obsolete and historical as it may be. The parish church is selected as the place of publication not because it is the institution of religion for the Church of Scotland but because it is the recognised historic, local, centre of each parish, and because it is, as far as I am aware, the only church in Scotland organised on a territorial basis. So much for the old arguments.
