Mrs. Hart

I concede that what the hon. and learned Gentleman is trying to do is to make a drafting Amendment which he thinks will improve the Bill, but I think he has overlooked what the other matters might be, and why it is important to distinguish these from those in subsections (1) and (2). It would cover other matters relating to professional training such as, for example, in-service training, the whole idea of refresher courses, courses which provide for in-service training, for example a Brunton-type course, new methods of primary teaching, and so on. They would not easily be covered under subsections (1) and (2), which relate rather to people entering teaching and the kind of training which is needed for them.

There are all kinds of examples where in-service training is becoming more and more important. For example, courses in the use of television, and courses in all those things where newer methods are devised. If education is to keep up to date, it is necessary that there should be 56 an opportunity for teachers already in service to have courses provided for them. It is intended that this "other" in subsection (3) will give the Council the opportunity to consider and make recommendations on this kind of thing. If "other" were left out, this could not be covered.

Mr. Forbes Hendry
: I beg to move, in page 2, line 8, to leave out from "matters" to the end of the line.

The Clause is characteristic of the Government's attitude towards the Council. We are promised a Teaching Council to give control of the profession to the profession itself, but, although that is done with one hand, with the other hand the Government take away all the advantage. We all welcomed Lord Wheatley's Report as giving the profession the status it deserved. On this subject of teacher supply Lord Wheatley made no reservations in his recommendations. These recommendations are very short and are stated on page 48 of the Report.

The Government, for some reason best known to themselves and about which we have not had an explanation, propose to remove all the teeth of those recommendations by refusing to allow the Council to discuss questions of remuneration and conditions of service, matters which are the whole crux of the case in the supply of teachers. Of course there are other considerations, such as training, but, when we get down to brass tacks, to consider the primary cause of the shortage of teachers, we are inevitably driven to the subject of remuneration and conditions of service.

There is no doubt that there is nobody better qualified to discuss supply than the teachers themselves. The Secretary of State and local authorities may have a material interest in a contrary direction and may decide, having regard to the national economy, that the amount of 64 money available is limited, but in considering how to spend available resources, if they are wise, the Government will rely on the best possible advice they can get, and in this case that is from the profession itself.

Here we have a short Clause which puts on the Council a duty to make to the Secretary of State from time to time recommendations on such matters relating to the supply of teachers as it thinks fit, or as may be referred to it by the Secretary of State. That is precisely what Lord Wheatley advised. But then, for some reason, the essence of the recommendation is removed by the restriction that the Council must not deal with matters of remuneration and conditions of service. This makes the Clause utterly meaningless.

The hon. Lady may say that there is other machinery for this purpose, the Joint Council. That is true, but the Joint Council is a negotiating body, and both sides of it, professional and Government, would welcome such impartial advice as they would get from the new Council. The new Council is to include all sections of the profession, which is not true of the unions. Unfortunately, in that respect the profession is divided. It is painfully obvious to those outside it that there is very little unanimity in the profession on these matters and that the branches of the profession quarrel among themselves.

However, here we are to have a professional body of the very highest standing and representing all branches from university teachers downwards and able to consider these matters in a detached way and able to offer considered advice not only to the Government and the local authorities, but to the teachers' unions themselves. If the Clause is to have any meaning, this reservation must be removed.

The hon. Lady may have good explanations which are not obvious to me, but I ask her to consider what I have said very carefully, for any explanation of a provision running contrary to the recommendations of the Wheatley Report must be very cogent if it is to be accepted.
