I welcome this opportunity to debate agriculture because since I first entered the House of Commons the only main debate that we have had on agriculture during the hours of daylight has been on the Price Review in which very few Scottish Members had the opportunity of putting forward the Scottish viewpoint. All other discussions of Scottish agriculture so far have taken place in the hours of darkness, late at night on Prayers when most reasonable people were already in bed. It is right, therefore, that at least today we can discuss agriculture in the light of day and not in darkness. It is also gratifying that we have gracing us with his presence this morning the Secretary of State for Scotland. I hope that in these hours of light he will do nothing to obscure our discussions.

In taking part in debates in the Scottish Committees and in the House I have noticed the general critical attitude often shown by hon. Members opposite towards agriculture. This betrays failure to appreciate the rôle which agriculture has to play in the economy of this country. This is a time when balance of payments difficulties are looming very large, and it is well to remind ourselves of the part that agriculture plays in saving imports. With output running at a level twice what it was before the war and 60 per cent. higher than in 1945, the import bill is very much smaller than it otherwise might be. So much for what we have achieved. More important is the scope which still remains for the further saving of imports. It has been shown that our imports of temperate foodstuffs at present run at a level of £1,000 million a year, a sum equivalent in value to about three months of exports. This demonstrates the tremendous scope still for import saving on the part of agriculture.

This makes one wonder whether the Government are sincere in their promises JPEG 115 to encourage import-saving industries. If they are sincere, then surely agriculture deserves more support than it was given in the recent Price Review. Milk production, for example, is a section of the industry in which producers are giving up all the time. They are giving up in Scotland at the rate of about one a day. Over the last two years the production of butter has fallen by about 40,000 tons and, as the Chairman of the Milk Marketing Board pointed out in Glasgow last week, this represents an extra charge of £15 million to £20 million on our import bill. This demonstrates how dairy farmers deserve more encouragement and a fairer share of the markets in dairy products.

This is a question not only of saving imports but also of whether in future these imports will be available from overseas. There is no sign of the serious world food situation letting up and it is likely that in years ahead food supplies will be less available. The Secretary of the British Association drew attention in London yesterday to this fact and said that as far as he can see ahead it is unlikely that there will be any relaxing of this world situation. It is not only a matter of more mouths to feed but of the change in the pattern of world trade. This is shown clearly in meat consumption, which in Europe is rising fast. As it rises so there is less meat available to come into Britain from our traditional suppliers—the Argentine, Australia and New Zealand.

Consumption of meat is rising in South America. This means that there is less meat available there for export to this country and to Europe. Exactly the same applies to Australia and New Zealand where producers are finding greatly increased outlets for their products in industrial Japan. This means that all over the world less meat is available and that what comes to us comes at much higher prices. The increase in dairy production throughout the world has now stopped and surplus stocks of dairy produce are now virtually eliminated. As this happens, prices on the world market begin to rise.

I am not surprised that as a result of our cheap food policy our beef is at present being diverted from Britain to the Continent. As a consequence, the British 116 housewife has to pay more for the meat which she gets. It is all very well for the housewife and the meat trade to grumble, but what is happening is inevitable in the light of the world supply situation. We cannot, ostrichlike, hide our heads in the sand and think that the world supply situation does not affect us. Thank goodness for this situation, even if prices rise in the shops, as long as it brings home to the Government and the consumer that there is a real case for the increased production of beef in this country, including of course Scotland.

Furthermore, the present situation means that we have to export more manufactures from Britain to pay for the same amount of food and that the terms of trade move against us as an exporting country. As this happens, so more urgent becomes our task in British agriculture in replacing imports of food from overseas.

It is well to remember the particular situation of British agriculture when compared with other industries. This is in contrast to the suggestion which some people make that farming enjoys a specially favoured position. It is well to remember that British farmers produce in direct competition with suppliers overseas. They do not have the benefit of tariff protection such as manufacturing industry has—for example, the 25 per cent. tariff which the motor car manufacturers enjoy. In addition, the cost of agriculture to the British taxpayer has been falling since 1961 in real terms. The level last year was the lowest in real terms since 1955/56. At the same time, we should bear in mind that consumer prices in Britain are the lowest of any country in Western Europe.

The Government are showing their eagerness to pin prices and keep them down and are exhorting industry to absorb rising costs. Prices in food production are the lowest and the most stable of all prices, and agriculture is the one industry which each year at the Price Review is forced, whether it likes it or not, to absorb £25 million of rising costs. If all industries followed the example of agriculture, there would be no need for a Prices and Incomes Board. In these circumstances the Government should be more generous and should not hold down and restrict agricultural production.

JPEG 117 It is against this background that farmers in Scotland regarded the Price Review of last February. It is not just a question of prices being inadequate to cover costs and to give a reasonable income, important though that is. It is not just a question of incomes in the farming community lagging behind incomes elsewhere, a fact which cannot be disputed. It is much more a question of agriculture not being given a fair chance to play its part in the economy of the country.

There is, however, a much more dangerous threat still looming up. One of the conditions of the 1947 Act was that agricultural prices should be consistent with an adequate return on capital. This is important because if they are not we shall not get the investment necessary to give us technological improvement and the increased productivity which is so necessary for the future prosperity of the industry.

It is worth our while to remember the very good record which agriculture has in increasing productivity, a record which if equalled by any other section of British industry would solve many of our present difficulties. Growth in agriculture has been particularly fast. Anything which hinders this growth is to be very much deprecated. In paragraph 3 of the 1965 Price Review White Paper the Government said:

"The Government consider therefore that a primary objective of future agricultural policy must be to encourage this growth in productivity through:—

  1. the maintenance of stability in the industry;
  2. the further progressive introduction of new technological improvements …".

This pious hope expressed in the White Paper will go completely unfulfilled unless farmers receive a better return on capital than they have at present prices.

In this respect I would draw the attention of the Secretary of State to an article in Scottish Agricultural Economics on the return on capital in Scottish agriculture. I hope that the right hon. Gentleman has read it. If not I recommend him to do so, and he would do well to pass it on to his right hon. Friend the Minister of Agriculture. This article shows that the return on fixed capital, buildings and land has been running over recent years 118 at as low a level as 2 per cent. It also shows that the return on stock and machinery, including the return on management, has been running in recent years at a level of only between 6½ per cent. and 9 per cent. That figure is not only the return on capital but also includes an element of return on management. If we compare the return on capital in industry, even including the nationalised industries, it will make us realise how often there must be a positive disincentive to further investment in agriculture.

Bank Rate which we had in recent months and the depreciation of investment allowances under the Finance Bill, we must regard the Government's attitude as paying only lip service to these aims of technological improvement. By their own actions, the Government are putting a brake on investment and on innovation and on new development in Scottish agriculture. A clear example of this can be seen in what is happening in the machinery trade. Anyone who talks to machinery dealers, as I have in recent months, will be told that, over the past six months, sales of new machinery have been declining and old machinery has not been replaced at the same rate as in recent years. This is but one manifestation of the Government's policy to which attention should be paid.

Where, now, are the Government's preelection promises of cheaper capital? All we have in the Price Review is an extension of credit under the Agriculture and Horticulture Acts, but no special terms such as were promised before last October. Where are their plans to improve the structure of agriculture? We heard plenty about that before last October. All we have now is the farm business recording scheme. Perhaps the Secretary of State will tell us a little more about that. We know very little about it as it affects Scotland, how it is likely to develop, what effects he hopes it will have, and, in particular, whether Scotland is to participate in one of the pilot schemes.

In capital assistance to agriculture, one scheme of particular benefit to Scotland has been the Farm Improvement Scheme. Anyone, whether he works in the agricultural community or not, must be aware, as he goes through the countryside, of the JPEG 119 tremendous change which has taken place in the modernisation of farm buildings. One can see this from the roadside and get a good impression, but if one sees what is happening inside the buildings one realises what a tremendous benefit the scheme has been to Scottish farmers. It has led to a much quicker uptake of new management techniques, new types of buildings and so on. But there is still a slight question mark hanging over the scheme. It is due to expire in two years' time, and it would be interesting to hear from the Secretary of State whether the scheme is expected to continue or not.

The provision of more capital could be of particular help in the hill and upland areas. These areas are a vital part of the whole agricultural economy, being a source of store stock in store lambs and store cattle, yet this is a sector of agriculture in which capital is very often at its shortest. There is a case here for extending the Farm Improvement Scheme to give a rate slightly higher than the one-third for capital improvements in these areas.

I have always thought that the Hill Farming Act and the Livestock Rearing Act, which gave a higher rate of capital assistance to these areas were of great benefit, but the scheme under these Acts was discontinued two years ago because, I understand, not enough people were making use of it. I suggest that the reason why the scheme was not being used so much before was that, in order to qualify under it, a farmer had to prepare a comprehensive scheme covering the whole farm. He was not able to do single improvements, one at a time; any improvements had to be part of a whole scheme. Although a scheme could be spread over a period of years, it was often too daunting for the individual farmer and beyond his capital resources. This, I believe, was the reason why the scheme was not more used, and there are strong grounds for extending the Farm Improvement Scheme to a higher rate of assistance in these areas. It could make our upland farms very much more viable than they are at present.

Another very profitable sector for investment, and one which is largely the responsibility of the Government, is the fighting of disease. Livestock farming in 120 Scotland is by far the greatest single sector of our agriculture, yet, despite its value, over the country as a whole very nearly 10 per cent. of our gross output—very nearly £100 million, it is estimated—is lost every year through the ravages of disease. I pay tribute to the great work which has been done and is still being done in Scotland in our research organisations and institutes and also by our private and public veterinary services, but there is a tremendous amount still to be done.

I single out for particular attention preventive medicine. Scotland blazed the trail in the eradication of tuberculosis. We are now, I hope, leading the way in the eradication of swine fever. I want the Secretary of State to tell us how the swine fever scheme is going. The Highland Show a fortnight ago—almost a blueprint of what happened last year as well—raised a lot of concern among pig keepers in Scotland about the success of the swine fever eradication scheme.

Another direction for a big extension of preventive medicine lies in the eradication of brucellosis. We have already discussed this in debates in the House, and we should like a more positive assurance from the Secretary of State that the Government are taking the matter seriously. The cost of the disease in dairying is known fairly well, but the cost to our beef herds is not known nearly so well. When this aspect of the matter is coupled to the grave danger of brucellosis to public health, one sees at once that this is a matter to which the Government ought to pay close attention.

Now, the question of marketing. In passing, I mention the situation as regards potatoes. I welcome the Government's proposal announced last week to co-operate with the Potato Marketing Board and to give it powers to intervene slightly earlier in the potato market. This was something which we on this side recommended last winter and on which the Potato Marketing Board itself exerted a lot of pressure. Yet the Minister of Agriculture stubbornly refused to do anything then, with the result that prices were depressed even further and the final cost of the buying programme was pushed up much higher than the level at which it might have been had the Government permitted the Potato JPEG 121 Marketing Board to intervene more quickly. I am glad that the Government have at last learnt their lesson in this respect. Nevertheless, even when the Potato Marketing Board does intervene, prices in Scotland, particularly in the north of Scotland, are very often £3 or £4 per ton or even larger sums below the average for the rest of the country, and producers in these areas suffer a great disability in relation to producers elsewhere when prices are depressed. I want the Government to look again at the question whether the Potato Marketing Board could be permitted to intervene on a regional basis when prices fall below the average in the region, not just over the country as a whole.

The greatest question mark at the moment hangs over the Government's plans for a meat authority. Over the past weeks and months we have been awaiting an announcement, yet we are still no nearer to its actually being made. As we wait, so the uncertainty grows. One can hardly open a paper now but read of some section of the industry or the retail or wholesale trade trying to guess at what is happening. I hope that the Government will not delay in announcing their proposals for the meat commission.

I hope that, when they do make their announcement, it will be plain that the Secretary of State has made sure that Scotland's special position is absolutely clear. The position of the industry in Scotland is not the same as it is throughout the rest of the country. Scotland's interests tend to be different. We are a net exporter of meat. Another factor which must not be forgotten is that for producers in Scotland quality is far more important than it is for producers in England. Two years ago, the Government removed the quality element from the price guarantee scheme. In my personal opinion, this was to the detriment of many Scottish producers, and I hope that in their proposals for a meat authority and for meat marketing the Government will not forget Scotland's special position as a producer of quality meat, whether beef, bacon or lamb.

In what I have said I have tried to show that the Government have not made a good start in their handling of agriculture. In the way they handled the Agricultural Price Review they gave 122 farmers very little confidence in the future and in how they would handle the whole question of meat marketing. They have failed to understand the background against which farmers have to work. They have not appreciated the balance of payments situation and how agriculture can help it. They have not appreciated the world food shortage and the changing patterns of world trade.

I conclude with a quotation:

"The purchase prices of a number of agricultural products have not covered the expenditures incurred in their production and the numerous reorganisations have engendered an atmosphere of nervousness and fuss. Agriculture has been set great aims which have not been backed up by the necessary economic measures, and from the centre there have gone out all kinds of stereotyped instructions in which local experience and the natural and economic conditions have been ignored".

This quotation could well have come from some critic of the Government's agricultural policy since last October. In fact, it did not. It was said by the First Secretary of the Soviet Communist Party about Soviet agriculture. I trust that our Government will adopt the same attitude of self-criticism. If they do, it will be to the benefit of agriculture and to the benefit of Scottish farmers, and it will certainly make our task even easier.

Reference has been made to the effect of mechanisation on the growing of potatoes. A study is being made of this and of the spread of gangrene in potatoes, and the extent to which this and other diseases may be caused by greater reliance on mechanical lifting. Dr. Elizabeth Gray, in Aberdeen, has been making a considerable and important study of this matter. Not only is it a question of the mechanical effects, but weather comes into this and the lightness or otherwise of the soil. This is important, because considerable loss has been experienced in the storage of potatoes.
