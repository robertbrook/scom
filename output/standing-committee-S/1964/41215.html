Clause 4.
(PROTECTION FOR PERSONS

COMPLYING WITH CONDITIONS.)

Amendment moved [10th December], in page 5, line 38, leave out subsection (2).— [Mr. Hendry.]

Mr. Forbes Hendry
: The Committee will be glad to hear that the speech on which I embarked at the end of our last Sitting will not last long. As I was saying, however, it seems desirable that a standard should be laid down for purification of effluents entering a stream. This largely depends upon the river concerned. The standard might vary from one river to another. Indeed, it might vary from one part of a river to another. A great deal of money could be wasted in carrying purification too far. It is desirable that some facility should exist for prescribing the standard of purification required. That is precisely what Section 25 (1, a) of the 1951 Act does. I do not know whether that 1951 provision has proved to be effective, but it might have useful application in the future.

The Under-Secretary of State probably knows that a great deal of work has been done over the years on water pollution and purification by the Department of Scientific and Industrial Research and that research is still going on. It might well be that the standards pre- 106 scribed would require modification from time to time and, possibly, as good a way of doing this as any other is by means of byelaws. I therefore ask the Under-Secretary to tell the Committee whether he does not, on reflection, consider that that would be a useful provision to maintain.

It has been put to me after study of the scientific documents that probably a useful standard to set from time to time would be, say, the reduction of the polluting load to one-quarter less than that which on calculation would just be small enough to avoid nuisance. That seems to me to be a fitting criterion for purification. Perhaps the hon. Gentleman will let me know whether he considers that a byelaw might not be the best way to achieve that.

Mr. Gordon Campbell
: I, too, should like the Under-Secretary to cover this point. I realise that the power to make byelaws may not have been much used and that that may be one of the reasons, perhaps the most important reason, the hon. Gentleman will give. I should, however, be grateful if he could tell us whether this means that it has been decided not to be worth while to try to prescribe standards. The byelaws were supposed to attempt to prescribe standards, and I wonder whether, in practice, it was found to be too difficult to undertake this task.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Dr. J. Dickson Mabon)
: The answer is very much "Yes". The task of trying to prescribe standards, and, furthermore, to observe them, particularly when new discharges came into being, has proved to be difficult. It is true that no byelaws have been made either in England or in Scotland under this provision. The legislators, no doubt, thought it wise, the administrators no doubt helped them along, and Parliament was, no doubt, coaxed into agreeing to it, but the technicians and the authorities concerned could not formulate sufficient byelaws to put them into operation. That is the short answer, that in practice it has not proved workable. Therefore, it is thought undesirable that we should continue this reserve right for the boards. I am sorry that I do not think that we should accept the Amendment.

Mr. G. Campbell
: I beg to move, in page 5, line 45, to leave out from "Act" to "and" in line 46.

The object of this Amendment is to inquire why the conditions to be satisfied before a discharge is made or an outlet is brought into use should not be included among the conditions which from time to time can be reviewed and altered. It may be that a condition which was made at the time when an application for a new discharge was submitted under Section 28 of the 1951 Act has become out of date. It could be due to modernisation or to a major change in processing by an industrial concern. It seems strange that the conditions which were laid down for a new discharge or altered outlet, which is how I understand the words in parenthesis, in the period from 1951 to 1964 before the Bill becomes an Act should not be included amongst those which can be reviewed or varied under the Clause.

To give examples, it is possible that a new discharge for which permission was given, say, 10 years ago could, 11 years later, be reconsidered by an industrial firm. That firm may have found a means of improving the purification processes; it could have installed a new purification plant. It might, therefore, want to produce a greater volume of effluent than the maximum which was laid down as one of the conditions. In view of the improved purification, that might be entirely acceptable. Alternatively, it could be that because of a new process the temperature would be much higher than originally but without any harmful effect.

It might be that such a change, and the desire of the industrial undertaking which I have used as an example, would constitute a new discharge. That would mean that the whole process of Section 28 of the 1951 Act would have to be gone through again. That may be the inter- 108 pretation which the Under-Secretary will give, that because the conditions are to be altered the discharge would become a new discharge requiring a new application under the 1951 Act. In that case, is it really necessary that this should be done? Is it really necessary that it should, in these circumstances, automatically become a new discharge and have to be treated under the 1951 Act? Would it not be simpler to be included in the Clause as a condition which could be reviewed and altered? If that is not so, I should be grateful for an explanation why it should be necessary to go back to the 1951 Act in each case of alteration of the condition for a new discharge—that is to say, something which has been brought into use between 1951 and 1965.
