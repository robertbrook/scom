The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. William Ross)
: I beg to move:

That the Chairman do now report to the House, that the Housing (Amendment) (Scotland) Bill has been considered in relation to the principle of the Bill.

 I am sorry that the hon. Member for Edinburgh, South (Mr. Clark Hutchison) was put to inconvenience because the printers missed out a nought. I am sure that if he had turned over the page, and departed from the Explanatory and Financial Memorandum and read the Bill itself, he would have been put quite at ease as to the purpose of the Bill.

It is a very simple and straightforward Bill. It deals with the single purpose of raising by £10 million the aggregate amount of advances which may be made from the Consolidated Fund to the Scottish Special Housing Association for the purpose of building houses in Scotland. The S.S.H.A. is a wholly Exchequer-financed body. It relies on these advances for all its capital expenditure. The amount of work which it can undertake is dependent on the extent to which it can draw advances from the Consolidated Fund. This is the reason for the Bill, to raise by £10 million the advances open to it.

A statutory limit to the advances was first made in 1955 when the limit of £75 million was placed on its finances by Section 16 of the Requisitioned Houses and Housing (Amendment) Act, 1955. In 1957 this limit was raised to £100 million by Section 23 of the Housing and Town Development (Scotland) Act—of sacred memory. In 1962 this was raised still further to £110 million by Section 18 of the Housing (Scotland) Act of that year. The position is that up to the financial year 1964-65 the actual advances which have been made to the Association have reached about £101-4 million. By the end of March the amount committed was about £106 million. It stands to reason that if we go on approving houses in respect of the S.S.H.A., it will mean that by the end of the year we shall be dangerously near the actual limit of £110 million. We are, therefore, seeking Parliamentary authority for raising the limit by an additional £10 million.

I do not think that anyone would want to see the forward planning of the Scottish Special Housing Association inhibited JPEG 7 in any way, or its building work curtailed. The Association has been and is a valuable instrument for house building in Scotland in particular circumstances. It was started in 1937 before the last war at a time when unemployment was pretty bad in Scotland, and it was an instrument placed at the disposal of the Commissioner for the Scottish Special Areas—Scotland was then a distressed area—for the purposes of enabling houses to be built; for slum clearance purposes; and to deal with overcrowding in outlying areas where it was difficult to get contractors. It was part of the economic build-up of the area.

The war came before the Association had really got going but by that time it had built about 1,714 houses. After the war, when there was an urgent need for getting on speedily with the building of houses, it was taken over by the Secretary of State for Scotland and since that time has been at the disposal of the Secretary of State in respect of house building. The amount of work it has done since 1937 may be judged by the fact that it is now the owner of 60,000 houses. Outside Glasgow, it is the largest owner of houses in Scotland.

In order to ensure that its work shall go on, we wish to secure that it shall be uninhibited by the closeness of the financial limit by the end of the year. Since the Housing and Town Development (Scotland) Act, 1957, the activities of the Association have been steadily curtailed. There was a basic change of task. It was given the special job, under that Act, of dealing with the Glasgow overspill, building in the reception areas, or arranging for building, for the families over-spilled from Glasgow into those areas. Secondly, it could give direct assistance to local authorities who needed houses built urgently when the financial burden would have been construed as being unreasonable in respect of their rates.

Overspill building has not gone as well as was originally hoped, but nearly 6,000 houses have been provided—5,887—and 3,172 were built by the S.S.H.A. on its own behalf. The Association not only builds on its own behalf but is the agent of local authorities and builds for local authorities as well. It has done valuable work in relation to site preparation and layout design. This can be seen in some 8 of the towns which have been handling the overspill developments, such as Irvine, Whitburn and Kirkintilloch. Other areas have enjoyed the benefit of the experience built up by the Association, which has been an advantage not only to those areas but to Scotland as a whole.

Since 1964, and under the Housing Act of that year, the Association was given yet another task, which, to a certain extent, it had been doing, of making provision for key workers. It was given the statutory task of building to meet the urgent needs of industry in respect of housing for workers brought into areas for key purposes. It demonstrated what could be done at Linwood and Bathgate where nearly 2,000 houses were built pretty quickly to meet an urgent need. It is doing the same thing in relation to the pulp mill at Fort William at present, where it is undertaking the building of 150 houses to meet the need there.

Hon. Members will appreciate how essential is the Association where there is a need for building houses in an area which, from its own point of view and its own local authority needs, has not been able to build up the kind of organisation required. Here we have an organisation which can be switched from one part of Scotland to another to do these special jobs. I think it is something in relation to what we want to see in Scotland spreading out from the centre, and this organisation will prove a valuable instrument in that respect. Of the 60,000 houses which the Association owns it manages two-thirds and acts as agent in respect of local authorities in the areas.

Apart from this work it has from the start had another function, which was to experiment in relation to the use of building materials. It has carried out, and has developed over quite a number of years, the "no fines" system of concrete building. It is working on industrial methods in relation to multistorey flats for an experimental block in Glasgow. The application of these industrialised methods to two-storey construction has not gone so well, but in the Dumbarton area the Association is putting up some experimental blocks on the basis of development which has been furthered south of the Border by the JPEG 9 Ministry of Housing Development Group. We are trying it out to see whether it could be an advantage to us in our search for speedy and easy building methods in Scotland.

The financial effect of the Bill—I am sure that this will be of interest to the hon. Member for Edinburgh, South—is that the capital expenditure authorised by the Bill is covered by advances from the Consolidated Fund and does not directly affect the Scottish Housing Vote. It is possible for there to be an indirect effect and this was the point about the missing nought. It is referred to in the Explanatory and Financial Memorandum. It is impossible to estimate precisely what this will amount to because it is not possible to forecast at this stage the precise rate of expenditure of the additional £10 million.

The additional charge on the housing Vote arises under two heads. In the first place, all the houses which the Association builds attract subsidy under Section 1 of the 1962 Act at a standard rate of £42 per annum. The Association also receives the additional subsidies under the 1962 Act where these are appropriate, for example, for multistorey flats and for schemes involving special precautions because of subsidence and the like. In the second place the Association receives an additional payment from the housing Vote under Section 19 of the 1962 Act. This is to make good the deficit on its operations after taking into account the income from the rents and subsidies and the outgoings on repairs, management and loan charges. The payment is, therefore, the counterpart of the rate contribution made by a local authority to its housing revenue account and has to be met by the Exchequer, since the Association has no rating resources. The amount of this additional payment is even more difficult to estimate than the subsidies. As the Memorandum says, the ultimate burden on the housing Vote to cover both these elements might be of the order of £500,000.

I hope that the Committee will appreciate that although this is a simple and straightforward Bill it is, from the point of view of Scotland, an important Bill. It ensures that, probably for at least two to two-and-a-half years, building by the Association will carry on at its present 10 rate. I sincerely hope that it will increase. The fact that we had to come earlier to the House than was envisaged when the last Act went through in 1962 for a further extension of the limit shows the extent to which the S.S.H.A. has been used to meet urgent housing needs in Scotland.

What we want to build up in Scotland is a stock of houses into which people can move so that labour will not be held immobile. We need a stock of rented houses. I am not against even the Scottish Special Housing Association, if it has the capacity, building houses for sale in the right places.
