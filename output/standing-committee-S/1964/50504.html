The Minister of State for Scotland (Mr. George Willis)
: It is clear from the speeches that the Amendment moved by my hon. Friend the Member for Western Isles (Mr. Malcolmn MacMillan) covers very important matters of wide concern. No one questions the desirability of widespread development of shelter belts to benefit livestock and crops in exposed areas and to contribute to the amenities of the area. Again, no one questions the desirability of getting the best possible system of communication, including piers 518 and jetties, to benefit the coastal areas of the Highlands. No one would question the importance of the regeneration of considerable areas of rough moorland.

I say, without reserve, that these operations and activities will be within the power of the Highlands Development Board which will assist in their operation or undertake operations, where necessary. The Board will not supersede or override statutory or other bodies which have the power, ability and willingness to carry out these developments, but it will be able to review the position and to submit specific proposals for developments or improvements in existing facilities. If necessary, the Board will be able effectively to fill in gaps in present arrangements.

About the delays, one of the objectives in establishing the Board is to secure effective co-ordination in the preparation of suitable proposals and to shorten the whole process of development.

In connection with shelter belts there are inducements in existing schemes of grants for the planting of shelter belts. In particular, grants of 50 per cent. are available under the Crofters' Agricultural Grants Scheme and of 33⅓ per cent. under the Farm Improvement Scheme. In addition, small woodland grants are given, through the Forestry Commission, at £22 4s. per acre, for quite small areas of shelter planting. Finally, the Forestry Commission has made available to crofters in the Outer Isles small trees, surplus to its requirements, for planting either for amenity or shelter purposes. These have been given free of charge. I mention these facilities because there is a growing consciousness among crofters of the advantages of tree planting on their grazings, whether in small shelter belts of blocks or on a more extensive scale.
