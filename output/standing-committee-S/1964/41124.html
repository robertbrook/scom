RIVERS (PREVENTION OF POLLUTION) (SCOTLAND) BILL

OFFICIAL REPORT

Resolved,

That if the proceedings on the Rivers (Prevention of Pollution) (Scotland) Bill are not completed at this day's Sitting, the Committee do meet on Thursday at half-past Ten o'clock.— [Mr. Willis.]

10.30 a.m.

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Mr. George Willis)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Rivers (Prevention of Pollution) (Scotland) Bill has been considered in relation to the principle of the Bill.

In the ancient Burgh of Musselburgh, which I have the privilege of representing in the House of Commons, older men like to talk nostalgically of the salmon they used to catch in the River Esk. The story goes around of how the last salmon rather foolishly entered the river and made its way painfully up to the Telford Bridge and finally, in disgust at what it met in the river, gave itself up to a boy with a bent pin just before it got to the old Roman Bridge.

I have no doubt that similar stories are told about other rivers all over Scotland. This is part of the folklore of Scotland which has grown up as a result of the increasing extent to which poisonous and polluting matter has been poured into our rivers and estuaries during the past few years. Certainly many of what were once fast flowing, sparkling streams and rivers have become little more than smelly, dirty water channels, and yet in Scotland we are fortunate in that we still possess a great heritage of inland and tidal waters which are virtually as nature made them. It is our job to keep them so and to try to rid the others of the pollution from which they suffer.

The first general attempt to do this was made by the Labour Government, I am glad to say, in the Rivers (Prevention of Pollution) (Scotland) Act, 1951. In that Act, a duty was placed on the Secre- 6 tary of State for Scotland to promote the cleanliness of the rivers and other inland waters and tidal waters of Scotland. It required him to appoint a Scottish River Purification Advisory Committee and it empowered him to appoint river purification boards. Under the Act, nine such boards have been established. There have also been set up river purification authorities which, north and west of the Caledonian Canal, consist of the county councils and the large burghs.

The Act gave a certain number of powers to the boards and authorities, but its main powers were contained in Sections 22 and 28. In Section 22, the use of streams for disposing of polluting matter was prohibited. Under Section 28, the consent of the boards had to be obtained when any new or altered outlet for the discharge of trade or sewage effluent was made into a stream. It is mainly these powers which have enabled the boards and the authorities to do the work which they have done since they were set up.

Section 29 of that Act authorised the Secretary of State to apply the provisions of the Act to tidal waters if an application for an order was made by a river purification authority or other interested person, and the Secretary of State was also required to make orders for the Firth of Forth and the Firth of Clyde as from the appointed day. The order in respect of the Firth of Forth came into operation on 1st August, 1960. A draft order in respect of the Clyde was published in January, 1962. A public inquiry into the objections to that order was held in September, 1963, and the report of the inquiry was received last July. So far, nothing further has been done about that order. An order has also been made in respect of the Solway Firth.

As a general result of the operation of the Act, there has been some overall improvement in the condition of Scottish rivers, but the pattern is varied. In some rivers the improvement has been notable. In others, the improvement has been barely noticeable, while in yet others there has been a deterioration.

This picture may be a little disappointing, but it is true to say that, but for the work of the Advisory Committee and the boards and authorities, it would have been a great deal worse.

JPEG 7 I am sure that the whole Committee would wish me to express our appreciation of the work done by the boards and of the manner in which it has been done. I mention the manner because, by and large, the results achieved have come about through the co-operation of the boards and authorities with local authorities, industry and agriculture rather than by resort to the penal provisions of the 1951 Act.

Mr. John Rankin
: Would my hon. Friend tell us of the rivers where improvement has been shown and those where no improvement has been shown so far?

Mr. Willis
: I cannot give my hon. Friend a detailed list, but I am sure that he can call some to mind himself. I know that the River Esk in my own division, while not all that it should be, has nevertheless been considerably improved. That is one example from my constituency and I am sure that other hon. Members can apply what I have said to the rivers in their constituencies.

In spite of what has been done and as a result of what has been done, it has become evident that the boards and the authorities require to have their powers extended, and the Bill seeks to do that. The 1951 Act applied the full system of control of polluting discharges by a consent procedure to new or altered discharges after the application of the Act to particular areas. It was an offence under Section 22 to make any discharge of poisonous, noxious or polluting matter into a stream, but a defence was provided if proof were given that there was no reasonable alternative to discharge into a stream and that all reasonably practicable steps were being taken to prevent the matter from being unnecessarily poisonous or polluting. In this the legislation for Scotland was broadly similar to that enacted for England and Wales.

Experience has shown that there would be an advantage in extending the consent procedure to discharges which existed prior to the application of the 1951 Act. This extension of control has been enacted for England and Wales. The Advisory Committee has been urging that similar provisions should be made for Scotland at the earliest possible opportunity, and in this it has had 8 the support of many river purification authorities. The Advisory Committee includes representatives not only of the local authorities, but of industry, agriculture and fisheries, and there has been general agreement on the principle of bringing pre-1951 Act discharges under control by a consent procedure on the lines of the English and Welsh legislation of 1961.

Clause 1 of the Bill applies the consent procedure and powers to pre-1951 Act discharges. Under the Bill, it would be an offence to contravene these requirements and the maximum fine would be £500. This represents an increase over the penalties of the 1951 Act, but the increase mainly reflects the changed value of money and makes the maximum fine rather more realistic in today's terms. I emphasise that we expect that it will still be the aim of river purification authorities wherever possible to enlist the effective co-operation of those who are responsible for the discharges. As in the 1951 Act, it is intended to allow a reasonable period before bringing fully into force this important extension of control, and Clause 1(11) provides that this period shall not be less than 14 months after the enactment.

Mr. William Baxter
: Can my hon. Friend elaborate a little about how the Clause may affect industries such as the paper industry which is very important to Scotland?

Mr. Willis
: The paper industry will have to obtain consent for its pre-1951 discharges of effluent.

Mr. Baxter
: But the difficulty about the paper industry even asking for permission to put the effluent into the river is that there are no known means whereby water used by the industry can be economically purified, so that the industry will be in some difficulty about meeting the requirements of the Bill. The Bill may have a crippling effect on the industry and I should like my hon. Friend to devote a few minutes of his speech to that aspect of the matter.

Mr. Willis
: I expect to say something about it, but the answer is that the river purification authority will take those factors into consideration when giving consent. All these factors can be JPEG 9 considered and the result of the consideration will form part of the terms of the consent. If an industry is not satisfied, it will have a right of appeal to the Secretary of State, as in the past.

Clause 2 provides that applicants for the consent of the river purification authority should have freedom against prosecution in respect of discharges while their applications are being dealt with in the circumstances envisaged by Clause 1(11) where applications are made before the appointed day for bringing Clause 1 into operation.

Clause 3 ensures that river purification authorities will have the proper information needed by them in considering applications for consent.

10.45 a.m.

Clause 4 is the corollary of widening the scope of control by a consent procedure, and it provides that those discharges that are made in accordance with consents and the conditions attached to consents under this Bill or the 1951 Act shall not constitute offences against other statutes dealing with pollution. These are specified in subsection (2) of Clause 2.

Section 25 (1, a) of the principal Act, which is repealed by this Clause, provided for byelaws being made by river purification authorities prescribing standards for the purpose of determining when matter is to be treated as poisonous, noxious or polluting. Circumstances and conditions, however, are so varied that it has been found both in Scotland and in England and Wales that it is technically impracticable to prescribe such standards for general application. This means, in fact, that the river purification authorities have to deal with each particular case on its merits.

Clause 5 is intended to provide a reasonable basis for the effective operation of the consent procedure. It is important that someone who is complying with the terms of a consent should have the assurance that these terms will not be varied at short notice without his agreement. After all, the conditions imposed by a river purification authority may have entailed modifications of plant to produce an effluent of suitable standard. But it is equally important that river purification authorities should be able to put the position under review in the light of changing conditions in 10 the waters for which they are responsible. The period set down in the Clause is not less than two years, which seems to be fair to all concerned and leaves a suitable discretion to river purification authorities.

Clause 6 provides for appeals to the Secretary of State—and I think this is point in which my hon. Friend the Member for West Stirlingshire (Mr. W. Baxter) was interested—on lines similar to those of the Spray Irrigation (Scotland) Act, 1964. These provisions take into account the considerations that have governed the attitude of the Scottish Committee of the Council on Tribunals. These apply particularly in the requirement—which actually is in Clause (1)4—that where the river purification authority refuses consent, or grants consent subject to conditions, it shall communicate in writing to the applicant its decision and the reasons therefor; and also in the requirement that the Secretary of State shall afford a hearing to the parties concerned in an appeal. The appeal provisions will also apply to appeals under the principal Act.

Mr. Baxter

If, perchance, the decision of the purification authority was of such a nature that it necessitated an appeal to the Secretary of State for Scotland, would the Secretary of State for Scotland have power, after listening to the appeal, to vary the contents of the Act?

Sir Myer Galpern

Mr. Willis

Clause 7 brings into line with this Bill the relevant provisions of Section 28 of the principal Act governing new or altered discharges, particularly in regard to the maximum fines which the Bill establishes.

Clause 8 relates to tidal waters, and I should say here that the recommendation of the Scottish River Purification Advisory Committee in regard to wider powers also covered the position of control of tidal waters. In the case of England and Wales, the Clean Rivers (Estuaries and Tidal Waters) Act, 1960 applied the consent procedure to new or altered discharges to the tidal waters specified in the Schedule to that Act as they applied already to inland waters under the England and Wales Act of 1951. In the case of Scottish tidal waters, Section 29 of the 1951 Act made provision for control of such waters by Order, with special provision for the Firth of Clyde and the Firth of Forth, 12 such control being limited to new or altered discharges.

Clause 8(2) will apply control over new or altered discharges to all those tidal waters, as mentioned in the Third Schedule, in which development leading to any significant change in their present condition is thought to be likely. This is on a similar footing to the provisions for England and Wales. This provision will obviate the need for river purification authorities or other interested persons to apply for individual tidal waters Orders for the purpose of controlling new or altered discharges.

Tidal waters Orders, however, have already been made under Section 29 of the Principal Act in respect of the Firth of Forth and the Solway Firth, and subsection (1) of the Clause provides that, in those two cases, the control shall also extend to existing discharges, as it does under Clauses 1 to 3 to inland waters.

Clause 9 brings the maximum fines for offences under Section 22 of the principal Act into line with those now imposed under the Bill. The Clause also repeals Section 22(3) of the principal Act, under which it is a defence to charge under subsection (1, a) of that Section to show that it was not reasonably practicable to dispose of the effluent in any other way. This is no longer necessary, because questions of practicability will be considered in relation to the application for consent—

Mr. Hector Hughes
: What happens to industrial concerns that have hitherto put effluents into a river and are now to be controlled by Clauses 8 and 9? They may thereby be put to a great deal of expense—and, indeed, industrial loss—by being debarred from discharging effluents which, hitherto, they have discharged, and the discharge of which is necessary for the purposes of their industrial concerns.

Mr. Willis
: That is a question similar to that asked by my hon. Friend the Member for West Stirlingshire, and the answer is exactly the same. If the discharge existed before 1951, the concerns will now have to obtain consent for that discharge. In obtaining that consent, discussions will take place, and arrangements will be made, where possible, to try to meet the difficulties that may result. I take it that, as a result of the discussions JPEG 13 that take place, suitable arrangements will be found possible in most cases. If not, and if the person affected feels that he has just cause to complain, he will have the right to appeal to the Secretary of State.

Clause 10 is rather lengthy in its treatment of the subject of taking samples of effluents, but it is vital in the interests of all concerned that there should be a clear code governing the taking of samples. As subsection (1) provides, these samples will be presumed, until the contrary is shown in any legal proceedings, to be samples of what is passing to the controlled waters.

Clause 11 restricts the disclosure of information acquired for the purposes of the Bill, and is designed to safeguard the interests of persons who are providing information and samples of effluent to the river purification authorities. A sample of effluent could well give a great deal of valuable information about technical processes that have been developed by a firm. However, there is nothing in the Clause which would in any way inhibit the proper exercise of the functions of the purification authorites nor, of course, of the courts.

Clause 12 is a straightforward means of ensuring that certain specific powers and provisions of the 1951 Act will be available in the application and enforcement of the further control provided under the Bill.

Clause 13 is formal, apart from the provision of subsection (2), which reflects a small divergence of Scottish practice from that of England and Wales in regard to discharges from ships. We do not consider that the general exemption for discharges from ships allowed by the English Act should also be applied to Scottish waters.

Clause 14 relates to expenses and is in standard form, and Clause 15 is purely formal.

This Bill may seem rather technical, but because its purpose is to retain or recover some part of our national heritage—clean and sparkling rivers and estuaries—I am sure that it will command the warm support of the Committee.
