Mr. Forbes Hendry
: I beg to move, in page 9, line 33, at the end to add:

(7) In performing any functions under this Act or the principal Act in relation to tidal waters or parts of the sea (including all controlled waters), a river purification authority shall have special regard—

  1. to the interests of fisheries and,
  2. to the factors arising from the tidal nature of the waters and, in particular, to 158 additional dilution due to dispersal of the effluent by tidal action, and the varying direction of flow and salinity and any other special properties of those waters.

During the debate on Second Reading, I mentioned the curious circumstances on the River Don where the river was clean to all intents and purposes until its tidal reaches, where conditions became very bad and where a great nuisance was suffered by those living on the banks. This caused me to devote a great deal of thought and research to this matter. At this stage, I should like to express my indebtedness to the Federation of British Industries for the expert advice which it gave me on this matter.

The circumstances in a tidal part of a river may be entirely different from those in the fresh water part. The purpose of the Amendment is to underline the necessity for the river purification authorities to treat tidal waters differently from fresh waters. The Bill seems to be defective in this respect as compared with the corresponding English Act which contains a statutory provision in almost exactly the same terms as my Amendment. The English provision was introduced on Report into what was then Clause 1(3) of the 1961 Measure after considerable thought had been given to the subject.

The difficulty is that an estuary is very different from the fresh water part of a river in that there are sometimes two flows of water simultaneously, the fresh water coming down and the salt water coming in from the sea. Generally speaking, the effect is that the flows are stratified. Generally speaking, although not always, the salt water is below the fresh water so that certain parts of the river may have a comparatively fast flow of water seawards whereas in other parts of the estuary there is a reverse flow. Obviously, if a discharge is made into the reverse flow, serious effects may follow. What I want to do is to underline the necessity for the purification authorities to keep these matters in mind.

I am advised that there is a chemical reason for the nuisance which occurs in the tidal part of the River Don. It is that the sulphates in the river coming with the salt water do not absorb oxygen to the same degree as in the fresh water, so that sulphates become sulphites. This is beyond my comprehension but I am JPEG 159 told that sulphide of hydrogen stinks like anything. That is the cause of the trouble. This sort of nuisance will not be cured unless thought is given specifically to this problem.

I believe that in the course of their operations the river authorities will keep these matters in mind, but in considering the Scottish legislation it is proper for this Committee to follow the English precedent which gives river boards in England and Wales some guidance about the carrying out of their duties in estuaries.

The purpose of the Amendment is to avoid the difficulties that arise when two pieces of legislation overlap one another. I am, of course, referring to the Oil in Navigable Waters Act, 1955, as amended by the Act of 1963. The important point is that this legislation was passed in order to give effect to the International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea by Oil. It gives the masters of ships certain special defences to proceedings taken against them, and these were laid down and agreed internationally.

JPEG 171 These defences would not be available under the 1951 Act, the Act for the prevention of pollution in Scotland, as amended by the present Bill. This would be tantamount to flouting internationally-agreed rules. In the short time in which the Government have been in office, they have not exactly endeared themselves to our foreign friends in Europe or other parts of the world, and I think that denial of this Amendment would give them further cause for annoyance.

In the past the Scottish Office, as far as I can discover from my researches, has expressed the view that the 1955 Oil in Navigable Waters Act did not limit the scope of operation of the 1951 Act. This, as the Committee no doubt remembers, originally applied only to streams and was extended to tidal waters only by orders made by the Secretary of State under Section 29. In view of the terms of the Convention, which was the basis for the 1955 Act, it is submitted that the defences which are internationally agreed should not be left to evaluation by the courts, as would be the case under the 1951 legislation, as amended or extended by the orders.

The International Convention for the Prevention of the Pollution of the Sea was held in London in 1954, and Article III, which is subject to Article IV, lays down the requirement that ships are not to discharge or allow oil to escape into the sea. Article IV provides that Article III shall not apply to certain discharges, including:

(a) the discharge of oil or of an oily mixture from a ship for the purpose of securing the safety of the ship, preventing damage to the ship or cargo, or saving life at sea; or


(b) the escape of oil, or of an oily mixture, resulting from damage to the ship or unavoidable leakage, if all reasonable precautions have been taken after the occurrence of the damage or discovery of the leakage, for the purpose of preventing or minimising the escape."

These safeguards for shipping have been internationally agreed and would be overruled by this present legislation. I think that the Under-Secretary of State would agree that shipping is in a slightly different position from that of other organisations and bodies which it is intended to bring within the scope of this Bill. I think they are in a peculiar position, and deserve to be treated under the 1955 Oil in Navigable Waters Act as 172 Amended by the 1963 Act, because this is designed specifically to meet their particular situation.
