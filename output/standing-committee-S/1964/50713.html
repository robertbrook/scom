Mr. Ian MacArthur
: I am very happy to open this debate, because it concerns the future of those great stretches of Scotland which lie outside the highly industrialised central belt. Three-quarters of our population and most of our Members of Parliament are contained within that central belt, a narrow strip of country. But most of Scotland lies outside, and it is in these remoter places that much of the natural wealth of our country is to be found and certainly most of the beauty and some of the finest surviving traditions which are associated throughout the world with Scotland.

I want to open my argument by reminding the Committee of the way in which these four regional studies were started. They were part of a sequence of development which embraced the Local Employment Acts, the associated special Measures for the establishment of the strip mill and the pulp mills, and the Budgetary concessions of 1963 which were designed, and very successfully so, to spur on the industrial development of Scotland, and in particular the establishment of new industries. Taken together, these policies of the Conservative Government provided a pattern which surpassed any programme for regional development in the Western World. They were followed by the Central Scotland Plan, which concentrated large public investment in this critical part of Scotland, and that in turn was followed by the four regional studies which set out to research in depth into the economic state of the rest of Scotland. Taken together, 218 the Central Scotland Plan and the four regional studies cover the whole of our country.

This was the first time in our history that an economic enterprise of this magnitude had been undertaken. Much of this was done in the face of constant and denigrating criticism by the Opposition of the day, who appeared to be incapable of understanding the industrial revolution which was taking place in Scotland and which was being guided and encouraged by the Government and by public-spirited individuals and bodies in Scotland. To their credit, the official spokesmen of the then Opposition did not assault the concept of the four regional studies. Because of this, I hope that we may be able to conduct this debate in our usual spirit of parliamentary calm.

These four studies were designed to provide in themselves a comprehensive plan for the development of the whole of Scotland outside the central belt. They covered the North-East, the Highlands, the Borders, and the South-West. In some cases they overlapped, and deliberately so, because the problems of one region are often shared by the region next door. I was particularly glad that the Highland study included those parts of the Highlands which lie outside the narrow Scottish Office definition, which until then had been restricted by the accident of history and county boundary to the seven crofting counties. My own constituency, Perth and East Perthshire, is covered by both the Highland and the North-East studies, and that decision conformed to the characteristics of the area.

I was very glad that when the right hon. Member for Kilmarnock (Mr. Ross) became Secretary of State he decided to continue these studies. This showed an appreciation of Conservative policy which he had previously lacked. It was a positive move, and a welcome one, particularly so as his Government have done precious little for Scotland since taking office. But I must be fair. They have provided some advance factories, but all of them concentrated in the central belt. They have introduced their Highlands and Islands Development Bill which, it was stated in the balmy pre-Election days, would provide employment for everyone in the Highlands and everyone who wanted to return there. This was a JPEG 219 splendid vision, backed by £150,000 rising to £1 million, and a complete paucity of ideas about what in fact will be done to meet the Highland problem.

They have a forestry programme, so small that I will spare the right hon. Gentleman's feelings and not mention it. If any of my hon. Friends want further details, they can obtain them from the Forestry Commission's headquarters, which are to be enlarged, not in Perth, but in Savile Row, W.1, where the trees of Green Park can be seen from the roof.

What a contrast all this is with the heady promises which were made at the Election. What splendid prospect did the Minister of State—I am sorry that he is not here at the moment—hold out for us in his Election broadsheet—the unleashing of a

"dynamic to get Scotland going again".

The electors of Edinburgh, East, unleashed the dynamic of the hon. Gentleman the Minister of State. JPEG 223 There are two levels of depopulation which I hope the studies have covered. One is the movement away from the glens and the remoter parts of our country, continuing at a slower pace the long, sad history of the vanishing people. In modern times this has reflected the falling away in agricultural employment and in the economic reality of life in a predominantly industrial nation. I do not believe that it will ever be possible to bring about a dramatic reversal in this trend and to repopulate the glens as they used to be, but our regional policies should be designed to arrest the trend by encouraging wider opportunities based on forestry and on other forms of employment related to the natural resources of the countryside—canning, tourism, craft industries and the like.

Small enterprises of this kind may employ only a handful of people, but relatively small developments can make the world of difference in remote places. There have been advances of this sort and I hope that more will be encouraged, perhaps by making it easier for the enterprising little man to have access to advice and inducement.

The other level of depopulation affects some of our country towns where the nature of local employment is inadequate or insufficient to hold the population. Here again I do not believe that we can ever hold things just as we should like them to be and provide local employment suitable for every boy leaving school and every young man entering his profession or trade. Indeed, it would be economically foolish to make the attempt. There must be a constant movement of people from one place to another, seeking new opportunity and blazing new trails. But let the movement be contained more within our own country so that the high road to England is less travelled than it used to be.

There has been a significant improvement in these last years. The industrial face of Scotland has changed, with old industries giving way to the new. In mid-1963–64 the increase in industrial employment in Scotland was greater proportionately than in Britain as a whole. My right hon. Friend the Member for Argyll (Mr. Noble) deserves much of the credit for this break-through, and 224 the growth engendered by the regional policies of these last years will continue for a long time yet.

It was right to concentrate Government help, and the taxpayers' money, in the areas of relatively high unemployment and to move on from there to the acceptance of the principle of growth points, which is such an important feature of the Central Scotland plan. Perhaps the next stage should be to apply the growth points principle more widely in Scotland, although on a much smaller scale. It may be that we are now approaching the time when the actual level of unemployment should no longer be the main criterion in determining where Exchequer help should be available.

Perhaps I can illustrate my point by using a constituency example. Perth is a prosperous city by most standards, and hon. Members must take it from me that it is the finest place in all the world. If there is nowhere quite like it, there are other country towns in Scotland which may share its problem. In a place like Perth the margin between prosperity and decline can be narrow. Employment there was growing until recently but latterly the employment opportunities in the city have been falling away. I shall not detain the Committee with the details but a drop of 700 people in employment is a serious matter for a town of this size. The level of unemployment is low but this conceals some measure of migration partly to the south, partly to other places in Scotland, and of course mostly among young people.

I expect that other hon. Members representing country constituencies share this experience of a disturbing falling away of employment opportunity in these remoter places. It is easy to exaggerate it, but it is there, and the problem will grow unless it is more closely met. This is why I suggest that before long consideration should be given to extending the growth area principle more widely. I have in mind that some of the suitably placed towns in Scotland, outside the central belt, should be designated for limited development, perhaps with an employment target recommended by the regional studies and towards which financial and tax inducements would be directed. These towns and smaller JPEG 225 burghs would act as trigger points dispersed throughout the country. Their limited development might well turn the trend of depopulation and help us to spread more widely the growing prosperity which has come to Scotland and which our people have richly earned.

The scope of the regional studies, which was revealed last year, shows that questions such as these were to be reviewed. The right hon. Gentleman has disappointed us often enough. I hope that he will give us reason to praise him this morning by announcing that he will publish these four regional studies.
