[Mr. JAMES MCINNES in the Chair]

EDUCATION (SCOTLAND) BILL

OFFICIAL REPORT

10.30 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mrs. Judith Hart)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Education (Scotland) Bill has been considered in relation to the principle of the Bill.

Its purpose is to amend the provisions of the Education (Scotland) Act, 1962, which authorise the making of regulations for the introduction of a pension scheme for the widows and dependants of teachers. The main effect of this amendment will be to allow the regulations to provide for the payment of temporary pensions in cases where a teacher dies in the early years of service leaving a widow with children.

As hon. Members know, it has not been possible until now for a sufficient measure of agreement to be reached about an acceptable basis for a widows' and dependants' scheme. I think that we all agree that the absence of such a scheme is a most serious deficiency in the conditions of service of the teaching profession. For many years the teachers were prepared to accept only a scheme paid for by equal contributions from employers and employees. The local authorities, on the other hand, took the view that widows' pensions should be provided within the existing cost of the main superannuation scheme by reducing other benefits.

This latter arrangement was, of course, the one followed in 1953, when a pension scheme was introduced for the widows of local government employees. It has always been recognised, therefore, that it would be very difficult for the local authorities to accept greater obligations for one particular group of their employees—the teachers—than for the rest of their staff.

Happily, the deadlock on the provision of a widows' scheme for teachers was broken in 1962 by the Working 6 Party on Pensions for Teachers' Widows. The Report of that Working Party outlined the basis for a contributory scheme which, although not a shared cost one in the sense I have just mentioned, would be comparable in cost to the teachers with the shared cost scheme in the Civil Service and, in some respects, more advantageous in the benefits it provided. This solution depended on the teachers' additional contributions for widows' pensions attracting Income Tax relief and the cost of the administration of the scheme being borne by employers. This would make the net cost of the scheme to the teachers about the same as that paid by civil servants, with whom the teachers had consistently compared themselves in this matter.

Under the legislation of that time, however, the only way of meeting the cost of pensions for teachers' widows and dependants was by the reduction of the existing superannuation benefits. In order to allow for the introduction of a scheme on the financial basis suggested by the Working Party the Education (Scotland) Act was amended in 1963. At the same time it was suggested to the teachers and the local authority associations—who readily agreed—that a working group of teachers, local authority and Departmental officials should be established to formulate the details of a scheme on the basis suggested by the Working Party. This, of course, would be without prejudice to the holding of the formal consultations which my right hon. Friend must have with them under the Act before a widows' and dependants' scheme could be introduced.

The working out of a detailed scheme was a most complex task which demanded much patience and skill, but the scheme produced by the Working Group is, in the view of my right hon. Friend, as suitable as anything that can be devised within the framework outlined by the Working Party.

I would like to take this opportunity of expressing on behalf of my right hon. Friend his appreciation to the members of the Working Party and of the Working Group for giving us the benefit of their time and experience and for providing the possibility of a solution to what has, until now, proved to be an intractable problem. My right JPEG 7 hon. Friend takes particular pleasure—which, I am sure, will be shared by the Committee—that this solution should have been pioneered in Scotland. The Committee may like to know that since the Scottish Working Party Report was published, a Working Party in England and Wales has been preparing, for consideration by the bodies concerned, a scheme of pensions for the widows and dependants of teachers in England and Wales on a similar basis to the Scottish one.

At this stage I should perhaps explain that, after the Working Group had completed its task by preparing a scheme, my right hon. Friend formally consulted the representatives of the local authorities and teachers about the introduction of the Working Group's scheme, as he is required to do under the Education (Scotland) Act.

He has now had the views of the various bodies concerned. I am glad to say that the local authority associations and the E.I.S. favour the introduction of the proposed scheme. The local authority associations have also told my right hon. Friend that they will accept their share of the administrative costs of the scheme and he is very grateful to them for their willingness to co-operate in this way.

I am sorry to say, however, that the Scottish Schoolmasters Association and the Scottish Secondary Teachers' Association take different attitudes to the scheme from that of the E.I.S. The S.S.A. make two points: first, that steps should be taken to help older teachers to participate because of the cost of covering their past service, and, secondly, that the support of serving male teachers should be determined by means of a plebiscite. The S.S.T.A., for its part, has rejected the scheme but says that if its objection is overruled, there should be a plebiscite of all male teachers before the scheme is introduced.

The question of a subsidy for existing teachers was debated on the Education (Scotland) Bill in 1963 and was also raised with my right hon. Friend by the E.I.S. at an earlier stage. The scheme is optional for teachers in service and will give individual teachers entering it discretion as to how much of their past service in excess of the proposed compul- 8 sory minimum of 10 years they wish to cover.

When a new scheme like this is introduced it is inevitable that participation should be less easy for older persons to the extent that the cost of covering service previous to its introduction must be met within the limited period of service remaining to them. It is for individual serving teachers to decide, in the light of such other insurance provision as they may have made when no public scheme was available and of their own circumstances generally, whether, and to what extent, participation in the scheme would be advantageous to them. My right hon. Friend feels, therefore, that the proposed scheme would be sufficiently flexible to meet the needs of older teachers and that one cannot accept the suggestion that there should be a special contribution from public funds to enable them to participate.

On the question of holding a plebiscite, my right hon. Friend does not think that there are good grounds for considering such a step. He has consulted the teachers' organisations in fulfilment of the statutory requirement to consult representatives of the teachers. He has weighed the views expressed by them on behalf of their members.

The largest teachers' organisation—the one which has within its membership over two-thirds of the men teachers who are in the main organisations and the one which has concerned itself most closely over a very long period with the whole problem of widows' pensions—has intimated that it is in favour of the introduction of the scheme. It has done so after the closest examination of the detailed provisions that have been worked out and after considering the practicable alternatives.

Hon. Members may have seen the report in the Scottish Educational Journal just before Christmas. I think it was clear that the E.I.S. had considered all the possibilities and was pleased to accept the scheme. This, coupled with the fact that the lack of a widows' pension scheme is a most serious omission from the conditions of service of teachers in Scotland, has convinced my right hon. Friend that he should proceed with the proposed scheme. It will afford protection to the families of teachers on conditions which the majority of teachers JPEG 9 regard as being as good as they can reasonably expect to obtain, and no individual serving teacher who thinks otherwise is under any obligation to enter the scheme. My right hon. Friend therefore proposes to take steps towards introducing a widows' and dependants' scheme by issuing the necessary draft regulations as soon as possible. He expects this to be at the beginning of next month.

Having given hon. Members an outline of the background, I come to the proposals of the very short Bill we are considering today.

As hon. Members may know, the normal pattern of widows' pensions in public service schemes is that pensions should be provided only where the person has completed 10 years' service. The Working Group, to which I have referred earlier, felt, however, that, with the trend towards earlier marriage, a 10-year qualifying period was unduly long for teachers. The provision of pensions for life for women widowed at an early age was, however, felt to be inappropriate, and it would be very expensive. Another possibility considered was the payment of a death gratuity, but it was found that there would be Inland Revenue complications about that.

After careful consideration, it seemed that the form of benefit which would provide valuable cover at relatively low cost was something along the lines of the National Insurance widowed mother's allowance. This is payable only to widows who have dependent children, and it ceases when all the children become self-supporting. These conditions of payment normally make sure that such pensions are payable only to those who really need them and that they continue only so long as there is a need for them.

The Working Group recommended that this form of benefit should be provided for widowed mothers. Since it could not be included in the proposed scheme which it had been asked to draw up, because of the qualifying period of 10 years laid down in the Education (Scotland) Act, 1962, it stressed that the necessary amending legislation should be sought at the earliest opportunity.

Clause 1 of the Bill accordingly provides for the removal of the requirement 10 in paragraph 20 of Schedule 3 to the Education (Scotland) Act that a teacher must have been in service for 10 years before a pension can be paid for his widow and other dependants. As a result of the change proposed in the Clause it will be possible for widowed mothers' allowances to be paid. This provision, I know, will be welcomed by everybody as providing a useful protection in the particularly tragic cases of teachers who die leaving young wives and dependent children.

In the draft regulations for a widows' scheme which, as I have mentioned, my right hon. Friend expects to issue at the beginning of next month, it is proposed that a widowed mother's pension should be payable where a teacher dies after at least three years' service leaving a widow and a dependent child under 19. The widowed mother's pension will range from £58 to £106 per annum, according to the number of years' service of the teacher, and the children's pensions will vary from £60 where there is only one child to £210 where there are four or more. We all recognise that these sums are modest, but, together with National Insurance benefits, they will make a real contribution towards a widow's security in those, fortunately rare, cases where a teacher dies in the prime of life.

I should also mention that the present requirement in the Act that there must have been 10 years' service before there is any entitlement to pension applies not only to any widows' and children's pension scheme but also to any scheme for other dependants. In the draft regulations which my right hon. Friend is proposing to issue there are also provisions for the payment of pensions to other dependants of teachers; for example, to a mother who is maintained by a daughter who is a teacher. As a result of the changes proposed in this Bill it will also be possible for dependants' pensions to be paid where the teacher concerned dies after having had less than 10 years' service.

I have given hon. Members an outline of the background of the Bill, an indication of its purpose, and how we intend to use the provisions for the draft regulations which we shall be issuing shortly. As will be realised from what I have said, the proposal to grant pensions JPEG 11 in respect of only three years' service is a quite new feature of public service superannuation. I can think of no better group of people than widowed mothers for whom this change should be made, and I therefore commend this small but very deserving Bill to the Committee.
