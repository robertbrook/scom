Mr. Hendry
: It is proper to point out that the Amendment in page 2, line 38, is not in my name, although I have no objection to it. During the debate on the consideration of the principle of the Bill I pointed out that, as drafted, it might cause an exceedingly large number of applications to be made to river purification authorities from a great many small discharges—in most cases quite innocuous—and that the resources of the authorities might be over-burdened by the very volume of these applications. The authorities do not have the machinery to deal with a large number of applications at one time.

I therefore suggested that the Bill would be improved if it were left to an authority to choose those discharges with which it intended to deal from time to time. It would probably start with those which it thought to be the worst, leaving the more innocuous ones to a later date, when its machinery would be capable of taking them into account.

All the Amendments are intended to make the procedure much less cumbersome and time-taking, and to ease the task of the authorities in dealing with existing discharges. The Amendments would give power to the authorities to give to persons making discharges notices requiring those persons to make application to the authorities for their consent to those discharges. In other words, an existing discharge would continue until the authority concerned decided to take JPEG 5 action to deal with it. In that event the authority would give notice to the person making the discharge, whether that person was a local authority, an industrialist, a farmer or a private individual. Until notice was given, it would be lawful for the person concerned to continue to make the discharge.

I then go on to provide that:

"Where such notice has been given it shall, subject to this Act, be unlawful for any person to continue to make the discharge to which the notice relates without the consent … of the river purification authority.…"

In other words, the authority will deal with these discharges as and when it finds it possible to do so, having regard to its machinery and organisation.

There is good precedent for a procedure of this kind. In the planning Acts Parliament had this matter very much in mind in connection with the control of advertisements. There are many advertisements throughout the country, some of which are quite innocuous but some of which are obnoxious. It was appreciated that planning authorities would not be able to deal with all these advertisements at one time, so the Acts provided that advertisements could continue to be displayed until they were challenged by the planning authority, as and when the authority chose to deal with them, when they would be taken into consideration and, if necessary, modified, or even banned.

As is proposed in my Amendments, the planning authorities would give consent for a continuation of the display of the advertisement for a fixed period, normally five years, at the end of which time there would be no need for any further procedure unless the authority again challenged the advertisement and brought it into account. That seems to me to be an analogous case, and I therefore propose that these discharges should be allowed to carry on until they are challenged by the purification authority, and that once notice is given, the discharge becomes illegal without the consent of the authority.

The other Amendments are entirely consequential upon that general thesis, and I do not propose to deal with them in detail.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Dr. J. Dickson Mabon)
: I have no doubt that the hon. Member for 6 Aberdeenshire, West (Mr. Hendry) has the best of intentions. His argument that he wants to help the authorities by making sure that they are not inundated with a great deal of work is attractive at first sight, but, in fact, this series of Amendments would stand the 1951 Act on its head and make it extraordinarily difficult to operate it effectively, certainly in connection with all that has gone before in this matter.

As it stands, the Clause would make it unlawful, on and after the appointed day, to continue to make an existing discharge of trade or sewage effluent

"being a discharge which is not controlled under Section 28 of the principal Act, without the consent under this Act of the river purification authority"

the important point being that the onus is placed on the discharger to apply to the authority for its consent. The effect of the Amendments would be to leave it to the authority to ascertain where all the existing discharges are, and then, if it wished, to serve on the dischargers notice requiring them to make applications for consent.

A number of other matters come into operation from that. If an authority had to go out and discover all the persons making a discharge, far from it being easier for the authority it would be enormously more difficult. There would be considerable delay because, as I said in the Second Reading debate, it is difficult to trace all discharges, particularly small ones, which can be even more noxious than the large ones. Not only that, but the staff work would be considerable. Furthermore, the problem of identification would be difficult, and there would be possibility of confusion between new and old discharges. This would make the task of the authorities almost superhuman.

I concede that it might seem that by having this blanket provision whereby a discharge is an offence until it is authorised, a lot of work should be involved, but there is a sufficient time lag between the Bill becoming law and its provisions becoming fully operative to allow authorities to overcome the difficulties involved. I should point out that this is what they estimate. Therefore, by proceeding in this way we feel that we should be adopting the more sensible course.

JPEG 7 On the other hand, to do as the hon. Member suggests would not only undermine the principle of the 1951 Act but would make the task almost impossible administratively. I am told that, scientifically, there is a strong case for arguing that no one could trace all these discharges. Certainly it would be enormously difficult to straighten out confusion between new and old discharges without standing the original Act on its head, as the hon. Member—no doubt inadvertently—is trying to do. I am sorry that I cannot advise the Committee to accept the Amendments, which would do considerable damage to the operation of the Bill.

"Particular industrial concerns which have investigated the possibility of establishing factory premises here are of a type which both require a fair volume of water for use in the manufacturing process and which have a corresponding volume of liquid waste for disposal. In some cases the availability of facilities for disposal may be the factor ultimately governing the choice of site".

In the next paragraph of the Report, discussing alternative ways of disposing, either into trunk sewers or sewerage plant, it states:

"The first of these alternatives has drawbacks, namely that the actual volume and strength of the waste may not be known with reasonable accuracy until the factory is in operation."

Therefore, it seems to me that if we are to encourage industry to locate itself in various parts of Scotland, while we must take reasonable steps where facilities are available to see that industrial waste is not discharged into open streams, we must also give reasonable latitude, and allow for the fact that people may not know to the nth degree—as is asked in the Clause—exactly what may be the volume of the waste. This is borne out by what was stated in the last Report of the Board, the 1963 Report, not perhaps the most up-to-date but the latest one available to me. I hope, therefore, that the Under-Secretary will be able to accept the Amendment.
