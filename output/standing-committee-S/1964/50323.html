The Chairman
: Before I call the first Amendment I would remind the Committee that there is a Financial Resolution in connection with the Bill. Copies are available in the Room. I would also like to call attention to the fact that adequate notice of Amendments should be given. As a general rule I do not intend to call manuscript Amendments.

Clause 1.—(ESTABLISHMENT OF HIGH-LAND DEVELOPMENT BOARD.)

Mr. Russell Johnston
: I beg to move, in page 1, line 5, to leave out "enabling the Highlands and Islands" and to insert:

"affording the people resident and those desirous of becoming resident in the Highlands and Islands comparable opportunities of living and advancement to those obtainable elsewhere in this nation, to enable them to develop their own communities and culture and thus".

The Clause goes on thereafter to say:

"to play a more effective part in the economic and social development of the nation, there shall be established a Highland Development Board …'.

This is an unexceptional Amendment but nevertheless one which makes an extremely important emphasis, an emphasis which we must concentrate on throughout our discussions. The vital word is the word "people". This first Amendment is to make it crystal clear that in our deliberations about the Board and any future Secretary of State who may have responsibility for it the whole object of the setting up of this Board and spending money on Highland development—and I hope that we shall spend a lot—is to transform the region into a productive and thriving community 4 in which men and women of ability can earn as much as they can elsewhere and can envisage a considerable economic future.

On Second Reading the hon. Gentleman the Member for Caithness and Sutherland (Mr. George Y. Mackie) put it this way:

"My point is that in the Highlands we have to produce conditions in which the good pupils of Golspie High School, which has the highest percentage of university entrants in Britain, can feel that they can stay here, develop their own country and thrive. At present they do not believe that. They believe that the only road to prosperity is the highway to England or elsewhere. That is a situation that has continued for a long time in the Highlands."—[OFFICIAL REPORT, 16th March 1965; Vol. 708, c. 1138.]

It is a situation which has existed too long. I went to Portree High School in Skye and I know how few of those who were with me at that time are now in the Highland area and how many of them would have liked to have had the opportunity to be there.

Consequently, we must all make the basic assumption that there must be a sacrifice on the part of the well-off southern areas. This means a better planned distribution of population and industry and in the long term, therefore, a proper integration of what are loosely described as the remote areas. Is the Bill to hold out any real hope to the peripheral areas? I shall be extremely disappointed if it does not, and so will the Member for the Western Isles (Mr. Malcolm MacMillan). In the short time that I have been a Member of the House—and I am well aware of my shortcomings in that respect—one of the most frustrating parts of my work has been to try and obtain basic conditions of living for the people of the remote areas—things like electricity, water, roads and wireless communications. I have been disappointed to a degree by the attitude of the present Government, which I hoped would be considerably more humane than the last, if only on pure Socialistic theory, to the remote areas. The previous Government took decision on strict commercial assessments rather than any social consideration.

The Clause says:

"For the purpose of enabling the Highlands and Islands to play a more effective part in the economic and social development of the nation…."

JPEG 5 It is frequently said that the Highlands are the playground of the few, and there is much truth in this. There is an equally widely canvassed view that, instead of being the playground of the few, the Highlands should be the playground of the many. It is from this view that I deeply dissent. I want a vision of a new Hebrides, not a new Butlin's.

It should be clearly written into the Bill that the object of the exercise is to provide conditions for people to live and work and advance themselves in the area. There is nothing to prevent any future Secretary of State from saying that here you have a vast area which is half of Scotland and the best way in which it could play the most effective part in the development of the nation is to afford relaxation with national parks and tourist facilities to the uttermost limit for industrial workers of the south. We would then still have a situation in which the able pupils from Golspie High School would have to go south to find opportunities in technology and scientific work and, to a degree, work in the professions. I am certainly not against tourism and there are great opportunities for its further expansion and extension. However, I do not want to see a situation arising in which the Highlands are simply made the playground of the many. This should be emphasised at the beginning.

I was disturbed by a letter I received from the Scottish Landowners Federation. This interesting letter more or less said that the Bill was splendid, provided that it was not implemented. It said that the Federation had had discussions with the Minister and urged upon him to be careful and cautious about embarking on schemes of agricultural reclamation. I have a Question on this subject for Written Answer—I am not sure whether it is in order for me to mention this. However, unless the Minister and the Government are to be concerned with agricultural reclamation and industrial growth then the Bill will have failed from the point of view of the people who live and work in the Highland area.

A Liberal Party publication on the High-lands has said this already and I do not think that I could say it any better. The publication—"Highland Development" is the name of it—says precisely what I and the Liberal Party think should be done: 6

"… despite the large sums which are being spent on the Highlands"—

and there are large sums being spent—

"the great majority of the best pupils from the Highland schools, when they go south to universities and colleges, never come back. There are people who declare that what is our loss is the country's gain, and people speak with pride of the achievement of the Highlander in agriculture and industry and administration in the more fertile land of the South, and in the multi-storied offices of our great cities. What we wish particularly to emphasise is that this is not the case. The benefit to the country as a whole would be much richer if these young people had scope for their talents nearer home. It is also most misleading to claim that the remedy is largely in their hands but that they lack the energy or the will to do anything about it."

This is an argument which frequently comes from this side of the Committee, but not my part of it. The publication goes on:

"To their great credit, and often to their personal disadvantage, some do stay and contribute richly to the community which they love. But it is quite unrealistic to expect any young man with a family, or with ambition, to sacrifice the chance of betterment in a job in the south for the sake of patriotic attachment to his homeland. He may want to stay, but if faced with the prospect of superior conditions and promotion in Birmingham or London, he will go. And though his departure may benefit him in the short term, we assert that in the long-term it is detrimental to the national welfare, and delays still further the construction of the kind of Scotland and indeed Britain, we as Liberals, would like to see."

I am concerned that any future Secretary of State may say that the only proper use which can be made of the Highlands is to develop them for tourism. That I do not want to see this at all. It was during the Second Reading debate that a great deal will depend on the Secretary of State. Secretaries of State are neither infallible nor liable to go on for ever. We are liable to have a different Secretary of State, whether of the same party or another. In these circumstances it seems to me essential to write substantially into the Bill our intentions on in-industrial and agricultural development in the Highlands.

Mr. Malcolm MacMillan
: I am sure that I am speaking for every Member on JPEG 9 this side of the Committee and most, if not all, hon. Members opposite, when I say how much we sympathise with the Amendment of the hon. Member for Inverness (Mr. Russell Johnston), partly because his speech rings a lot of familiar bells which have been clanging around our ears and the ears of many others in this Committee for many years. The fact that he comes newly to these Highland and Island problems—in a Parliamentary sense only, for I know that he has been interested in them for many years—makes it none the less refreshing to have his contributions and his new opinions on the subject. I do not think that there is on this subject a sharp party line, within the Committee, at all events—I shall not talk about the very different actions of the political parties represented opposite when they are in the Highlands. But I do not think that the hon. Member will draw much fire here against this Amendment and against his views.

However, I would say that, ultimately, even the Bill itself is not so important as the intentions of the Government and of those who are to make it work. This is what is fundamental. Herein lies the hope and, as the hon. Gentleman rightly said, the danger. It is not so much what the Bill says as what is meant by the Bill. I believe that it was Chesterton who said that it was not so much what the word meant as what one meant by the word; and that applies here.

In what the hon. Gentleman said it was implicit that only a Minister and Government full of good will towards the Highlands would introduce a Bill like this. On the other hand, if there were, later, to come along a succession of less helpful Secretaries of State to give the general directives for which the Bill provides, directives which were of the wrong character, the whole purpose and intention of the present Government could be frustrated. That might well happen in later years and the hon. Gentleman was quite right to have regard to it. This is an important consideration to have in mind and it becomes important to have things written into the Bill to tie Ministers down to at least the minimum expectations of performance.

However, for the purpose of the general discussion of the provisions of the Bill in Committee, the Amendment, while an acceptable hors d'oeuvres, is 10 not necessarily a substantial part of the meal, because it is surely already accepted that the Bill deals with people. Surely it is the concern for people which has made the Labour Party consider the whole question of how to develop the Highlands and Islands. Over the years we in the Labour Party have repeatedly fought on this issue and we have advocated greater provision, more generous provision, more effective provision for developing the resources of the Highlands and Islands and raising the standard of life of the people of the Highland and Island communities—not only by developing growth points—and always with the aim of retaining the population's present distribution and helping the small communities which will die if they are not developed. This case has been put year after year over the decades. I have, therefore, every sympathy with the Liberal Party spokesman's argument in this respect. I am sure that it is correct and I know that the hon. Gentleman has put his case with a sincerity which I would not dream of inpugning.

What is meant by the Bill—and I am sure that the hon. Member will agree—is that we want to give the Highland and Island communities a better chance first to survive, secondly, to develop and, thirdly, to contribute more to the general wealth of the United Kingdom. This is stated in the Explanatory and Financial Memorandum and in this Clause. This purpose is solidly in the Bill and it is most important that it should be stated and defined there in this positive and contributing sense, because the Bill assumes a future viable Highlands and Islands. It is implicit in Clause 1 from the very start that the Government's intention is that there will be a Highlands and Islands area which will develop so that its resources will be used to the full so that it will contribute in a bigger and bigger way to the general wealth and welfare of the country as a whole.

I know that there are those who take the view that in many respects the contributions have been a one-way traffic. But that is not so. Although great amounts of money are being spent in the Highlands and Islands, as elsewhere throughout the country, when one talks about giving the crofters—that is the wrong expression—investing in crofting, JPEG 11 we must not forget that we invest vast sums in agriculture generally. Indeed, farming in the home counties and in other prosperous areas benefits from every £100 so spent much more than the agriculturists in the Highlands and Islands, where high freight and transport costs and other factors militate against the crofters and marginal farmers in the full enjoyment of what should be equal benefit.

When we hear about the cost of agricultural subsidies for the Highlands and Islands, we should not assume that we enjoy a favourable differential. We have reason to complain against the very adverse differential. Every £100 of assistance which is given to crofting agriculture in the Highlands and Islands is worth a great deal less by the time it reaches the crofters than it is worth in the shires of England or in the more prosperous southern areas of Scotland, so that in the end the crofter gets far less assistance from the nation per acre than any other section of the agricultural community.

Not only that, he has to apply it to the most marginal land, to the least productive land which will produce the most frugal harvest; so that, again, he is up against this difficulty of being wrongly attacked for the amount of subsidies which he gets, while yet receiving far less in far more difficult conditions than any other section of the agricultural community in Great Britain. I have often thought that instead of criticising the crofters who suffer from this adverse differential which is based on an accident of nature, remoteness, geography and the poor quality of the soil, we should be thinking in terms of the need for a positive differential, with special aid being applied to areas which need far greater assistance—areas where there is so much marginal and unproductive land. I know that there are many farmers who would agree with this sort of differential and although I admit that it would be difficult to apply, there is a powerful case for it.

I do not want to fall into the arguments of the hon. Member for Perth and East Perthshire (Mr. MacArthur) who was castigated for talking about tourist lavatories in that wind blown area of Glenshee. No doubt the more wind blown 12 the area is, the more necessary it is to have lavatories, although Highlanders like others can no doubt find means and places around Glenshee to get over the problems of absence of less public lavatories. On the other hand, I do not want to disparage the contributions of a specialist on the subject if he wants to discuss that particular aspect of Highland reconstruction.

I would emphasise to the hon. Member for Inverness that the intention of the Bill is what matters. He has already said that in his mind there is no doubt that the Government's intentions in the Bill are to deal with the solution of the problems of our people in the Highlands and Islands. I agree with him that there are dangers of finding a sort of two-class attitude, an attitude which can even be found within the Highlands themselves with inferior service to the local people. It is here that there can arise certain dangers from an over emphasis on the tourist industry. I will give an example to show what can easily happen. A county councillor, a minister, in North Uist who was going across to the Islands a few months ago. He was on one of MacBrayne's new ferry steamers and he wanted a meal. He was asked if he was a member of MacBrayne's tour, a group of tourists. When he said that he was not, he was told that he would have to wait until the tourists were fed. This is the sort of fearfully snooty and blatant attitude which can be found on the part of MacBrayne's who used to have on their steamers going to and from Stornaway a little brass plate which referred to "passengers and other incumbrances".

There is a danger in the over emphasis on tourism—however important in itself. There is gross exaggeration about possible earnings for ordinary crofters and others from seasonal tourism. The retired Chairman of the Crofters Commission fell into this error when he began often at the expense of the dignity of local people; and there is always a danger of undermining that dignity if tourism does not develop along the right lines. The Highlanders do want to be wholly dependent upon tourism. It is not the natural industry of the Highlands. The Highlanders may be naturally hospitable but that does not mean that they are natural waiters—although they have been waiters for far too long in another sense.

JPEG 13 Tourism at the very best is only seasonal and is dependent upon climate and other vicissitudes which are completely outside the scope of planning and beyond the control of the individuals concerned. It can supply never more than a seasonal income, a very small supplementary income, and there is a danger that whole families become involved, husband and wife and children, to tote up four-figure incomes from tourism. After all, we have, in most cases, to divide these incomes by two, three and four. Whole families are often involved. Even in the most prosperous and accessible areas in the Highlands and Islands, tourism is an industry that must be well organised. But it has to be seasonal—climate and weather dictate this—and it can only provide for ordinary people a rather small additional income. It will not solve the general problem of unemployment and depopulation that exists in these parts.
