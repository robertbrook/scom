title ARTS AND AMENITIES IN SCOTLAND

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mrs. Judith Hart): I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the matter of Arts and Amenities in Scotland.

It is four years since the Committee last discussed the arts. This is the first debate of its kind under the present Government, and it is a subject in which I have particular responsibilities and a very personal interest. Therefore, I welcome this morning's debate, particularly because it follows the publication last February of the Government's White Paper, "A Policy for the Arts: the First Steps". It is a White Paper which has been received with thankfulness and even a degree of enthusiasm by those who live and work in the world with which it is concerned, not as any kind of final answer, but as a first measure of our concern that the arts should flourish and should be enjoyed.

The important thing was, and I think that this has been generally recognised, that among all the pressures of all the problems that faced the Government on assuming office, the White Paper was prepared and published by them and that at a time of acute financial difficulties we have made more money available for the arts. Before I develop this point, I shall say a word about Ministerial responsibilities, because there is, I believe, some confusion about the arrangements in Scotland and the Committee might wish to have the position clarified.

My hon. Friend the Joint Under-Secretary of State to the Department of Education and Science is co-ordinating the carrying out of Government policy over the whole field of the arts. Her Col. 6 appointment and the specific remit that she has been given by my right hon. Friend the Prime Minister have been warmly welcomed. Men and women working in the wide field of the arts now know that there is within the Government a Minister charged with the task of initiating policy to sustain the arts and to create a context within which they can flourish.

The Secretary of State for Scotland continues, however, to be responsible for his Ministerial functions in relation to the Scottish national institutions and the Arts Council and its Scottish Committee, and he has taken over responsibility from the Treasury for Ministerial functions concerning the National Library of Scotland. I have immediate responsibility under the Secretary of State for the discharge of these functions, and we have arranged that the Department through which all this will be done will be the Scottish Education Department instead of the old division between the Treasury, the Scottish Home and Health Department and the Scottish Education Department. For the first time, therefore, one Department is dealing with all the Scottish national institutions, with the Secretary of State's interest in the Arts Council and its Scottish Committee, and providing the link with local authorities on the arts.

Having clarified that, I turn in more detail to the White Paper. It contains, amongst many other things, the figures which show how the grant to the Arts Council was substantially increased in 1965–66. The Committee may wish to know the figures affecting the Scottish Committee of the Arts Council, because much of what is said this morning will be linked with efforts that the Scottish Committee of the Arts Council is making in Scotland.

In the debate in July, 1961, the Government spokesman took credit for the rise in the grant to the Scottish Committee of the Arts Council from £82,000 in 1955-56 to £123,000 in 1961-62. This year, the figure is £255,000. To put it another way, the amount has more than doubled since the last debate, whereas the previous comparison, which was thought by the last Government to be a fairly good one, showed a rise of only 50 per cent, on much smaller figures.

Image Col. 7 I shall give just one example of what this increase means. Within the budget of £123,000 in 1961–62 the Scottish National Orchestra received £38,000. This year, the orchestra will receive £62,500, a very big jump. There is a great deal in the White Paper which we could spend a lot of time discussing, but time is short and I do not intend to speak at great length.

I should like at this stage to refer only to the building fund which is now available for local authorities. It is remarkable that until this year the Arts Council was never able to set aside money to assist efforts by local authorities to house the arts. The total figure which we can afford to offer at present is small—it is £¼million in 1965–66—but it is intended to be a starting point. Up to now, there has been nothing at all.

Before anyone asks me what share of this total Scotland is likely to receive, let me say that this is, in the first instance, up to the Scottish local authorities. As I commented at the time the White Paper was published, the Scottish Committee of the Arts Council cannot stake out any Scottish claims in London unless Scottish local authorities come forward with firm and well-prepared proposals. I am sure that Scotland will get a fair share of the building fund if the local authorities concerned act with a fair amount of speed and a good deal of decision. A number of schemes are already under consideration by the Scottish Committee of the Arts Council, but the initiative lies with local authorities and it is now up to them.

That was why we decided to follow up the White Paper with Scottish Education Department circular No. 589 addressed specifically to local authorities. Hon. Members will by now have read it. It paid tribute to what is already being done by many local authorities, but it indicated—at least, I hope it did—how much has still to be done. It is certainly true that authorities in general are coming nowhere near the rating limit of 44/5d. which they are entitled to spend under the Local Government Act, 1948.

I appreciate, as does everybody else, the heavy rating burdens that have to be faced and all the competing demands within the areas of local authorities, but I emphasise that this is a sphere in which Col. 8 a relatively modest outlay can bring a quite disproportionately large return in terms of human happiness and enjoyment. Indeed, most of the suggestions in the White Paper would involve very little expense.

There has not been in Scotland a survey comparable to that which was carried out in England and Wales by the Institute of Municipal Entertainment, which was outlined in the last Annual Report of the Arts Council. It showed how much, or how little, local authorities in England and Wales had spent on the arts and on what they called entertainment since the 1948 Act allowed them to spend a proportion of the rates on the arts. The only thing which we can do in Scotland is to form an estimate, and our estimate is that local authorities on average—I emphasise "on average", because, obviously, this conceals wide variations—seem to be spending less than 1d. of the rates on the arts. There is, therefore, a good way to go.

I have said that most of the suggestions in the White Paper would involve comparatively little expense. The circular was intended to be a fairly practical guide. Some Of the schemes in it would cost virtually nothing and others very little. The point is that a small contribution by local authorities could make a large difference to, for example, a group of amateur musicians or to small dramatic societies.

It might be said that the circular is concerned really with two problems. The first is to improve and strengthen what already exists, and the second is to create new opportunities. I should like to look briefly at both of these and will take the new opportunities first.

Mrs. Hart: I have touched upon the Highlands, and perhaps I can escape from them somewhat speedily to return to an even greater need, and that is the needs of communities, the towns and the villages, of our industrial areas. They are closer to the main centres of population but they have no share in the artistic riches which the cities can offer. It is not going too far to say that they produce our wealth but get very little return from the civilisation which it has created.

I should like very much to see the development of three possibilities. The first is the arts centre. I emphasise that I am talking about professionalism in the arts and not about amateur activities, which thrive and prosper in many of the industrial villages and towns. I am talking about opportunities for people in communities outside the cities in our industrial areas to hear the best that the professionals can offer in music, theatre, and so on. I should like to see arts centres in cities and towns, places where actors can play and musicians can be heard, where paintings can be seen, poetry can be read and people can come to meet and talk and have coffee as well as be an audience, and where they can find familiar things as well as what may be unfamiliar new riches.

During the last few months, I have talked to many of the men and women who are deeply involved in the theatre and in music in Scotland. I find a very wide agreement among them that to the extent that there is a barrier between the arts and the people, it is a barrier of unfamiliarity and inhibition rather than any kind of disenchantment. After all, there is a wide gap—we should recognise this—between those who have grown up in a home which took for granted the fact that it would have Brahms on its Image Col. 11 record player, that the children would be taken to the theatre or the ballet as a holiday treat or to visit the galleries and museums for fun, and those whose first experience of many of these joys comes at school. For the latter, there is a perfectly natural hesitation in going to concerts, to plays, ballet or opera which the world of the arts must recognise and, I think, does recognise.

What we have to consider is how best we can attract and encourage those for whom these things are a little unfamiliar to come into the world of the arts. There are ways which may be tried. Folk music, good jazz, the young generation of poets, and theatre such as Brecht, Pinter and Wesker may well be far more attractive to the new generation than the traditional drawing-room comedy and bedroom farce which has been the habitual enjoyment of a generation which has grown up knowing the theatre.

What seems to me to matter above all is that whatever is offered should be the best of its kind, with no concession to lower standards. The young seem to have a natural flair for discrimination between the first-rate and anything less than first-rate. There is certainly no concession of any kind in seeking to provide what is best of its kind and likely to be enjoyed.

The second possibility to be fostered is the encouragement of visits to performances by parties from outlying towns and villages. The Arts Council can make special arrangements for cheaper tickets and travel—for example, visits organised by trade unions, factories, clubs and organisations of all kinds. The third possibility is to take the arts to people by organising tours of companies and artists, perhaps to the point where we have almost a mobile arts centre going to a town or a village for an evening or two of music, theatre or painting, everything which can normally be found only in the city.

I am glad that the Scottish Committee of the Arts Council has taken up suggestions of mine on these lines, and I hope that we shall see practical results within the next year. They follow up, of course, the White Paper's suggestion that: Col. 12

I am very anxious that the expertise which they build up should be available to those who, although perhaps not concerned with creating full centres for the arts, are, nevertheless, about to build community centres or youth wings of community centres, which may include facilities for the performance or practice of the arts. It would be a great pity, when that kind of scheme is coming forward, if the details of it were not such as made it possible for it to be used for a number of other purposes in the arts. We are always available, as I am myself, for anyone who seeks this kind of help.
