Clause 11.—(POWER TO OBTAIN INFORMATION.)

Amendment proposed [12th May], In page 7, line 20, after "Board", to insert:

"shall prepare a list of all lands over 500 acres in extent owned in the Highlands and Islands and of the names of the owners of such lands, showing the rateable value and the acreage owned in each case, and further"— [Mr. Rankin.]

Question again proposed.

10.30 a.m.

Mr. William Hamilton
: Yesterday I made what I hoped were non-party and non-controversial points. I do not wish to detain the Committee for too long this morning.

In the Second Reading debate I referred to the 1873 survey, and the Secretary of State indicated later how inaccurate the results of that survey probably are now. No one knows for certain how inaccurate they are. Even landowners do not seem to know exactly how much land they own. The right hon. Member for Argyll (Mr. Noble), in a discussion we had a few weeks ago, admitted that. He may recall a conversation that we had with a B.B.C. man, who was obviously very knowledgeable about the position in the Highlands and who, in the course of the conversation, stated that he had conducted a survey in Ross-shire to find out who exactly owned what. He indicated that he could find without difficulty who owned 85 per cent. of the land, but could not find out, no matter how he tried, who owned the other 15 per cent.

This may or may not be a general problem in the Highlands. If it is, I cannot but feel that the activities of the proposed Highland Board will be thwarted or at least retarded by lack of knowledge of who owns the land.

Perhaps I might quote from one or two very interestng articles in The Observer 710 Weekend Review of 21st March, 1965. The article is headed "Who Owns Britain?" and it begins with the quotation, "God gave the land to the people". Somebody else has been busy since then. I want to give the land back to the people. My Amendment is intended as a first step in that direction. The writer of the article in to which I am referring, Mr. Mervyn Jones, quotes a letter which he said he had received from Sir Donald Cameron of Lochiel, saying:

"At a very rough guess I would say that my estate today contains about 130,000 acres.… I have not for some years made an estimate of the acreage. However, I should think that the above figure is not very far out."

This example shows how difficult this subject is, and it is not explained by the records. It is not at all clear how much the individual landowners own. With my customary diligence I asked the Library staff of the House to go into this question. I subsequently received from a Member of the Research Department this letter:

"I mentioned in my letter of 1st July that I had written to the Scottish Landowners' Federation in connection with your inquiry. I have received a reply which, as you will see from the copy enclosed, is a 'nil' return. The Scottish National Trust were more helpful and I also enclose a copy of the Factor's letter."

From this it appears that the biggest owners are the Scottish authorities, including the Forestry Commission, the Agriculture Department and other Scottish authorities.

The letter from the National Trust could not be less forthcoming than that of the Scottish Landowners' Federation. Dated 9th July, 1964, the National Trust for Scotland says in its letter:

"We thank you for your letter of 7th July inquiring about the largest landowners in Scotland. We presume you refer to acreage rather than to rental. We naturally do not know the size of other estates in Scotland, but would imagine that the largest landowner in Scotland is probably the Secretary of State through the lands administered by the Forestry Commission or by the Department of Agriculture. Our own acreage spread over some 36 properties aggregates about 70,800 acres … Among the largest landowners must be: The Duke of Buccleuch, the Countess of Seafield, A. C. Farquharson of Invercauld, Westminster Estates in Sutherland."

This is all the information one gets from the National Trust.

When I sought to find out who the landowners were who voted in the JPEG 711 Burmah Oil debate in the House of Lords I just could not get the information. There is no record on which to base that inquiry at all.

Figures are bandied about concerning the extent of the acreage owned by certain people. I have seen figures for the Duke of Buccleuch giving 220,000 acres, for the Countess of Seafield 213,000 acres, for Lord Lovat 190,000 acres, and for Westminster Estates in Sutherland 98,000 acres. I do not know whether the figures are accurate, but it is clear, whatever the acreage, that it is important to find out who the landlowners are in the Highlands.

My hon. Friend the Member for Western Isles (Mr. Malcolm MacMillan) will confirm that undercurrents of opposition are developing towards preventing the proposed Highland Board from doing what we all hope it will be able to do. I would quote from an article in the Inverness Courier of 24th July last. It was written before the General Election and before the Highland Development Bill had been presented. It was in correspondence, following my quoting an article in the Second Reading debate. The correspondence quoted the writer of the original article in the Inverness Courier of 24th July last, in which he said:

"Signs have not been wanting recently that some owners of great sporting estates, alien and indigenous alike, are marshalling their forces quietly, but nevertheless determinedly and insidiously, in order to ensure that the vast areas which they own by virtue of wealth or accident of birth will be kept as private preserves for thier own enjoyment, irrespective of what the national interest may require or demand."

I do not know what the politics of the Inverness Courier are. I am pretty sure that they are not Left; they may be Radical or Liberal, which is the worst of all possible politics. Nevertheless, that is rather a significant quotation, coming before the General Election and before the details of the Bill were announced.

The same writer quoted the Highland Herald of 30th July as follows:

"It is certainly not Socialists or Liberals who are objecting to the further development of hydro-electric schemes; who own vast deer forests; who erect signs in lonely places prohibiting camping, caravanning and fishing. These are high-ranking Tory pursuits. We know of one powerful landlord whose properties stretch across Scotland and at the mouth 712 of every glen are his notices warning off those who would camp or tarry awhile."

These are the people whom we want to put in their places, and who must be put in their places if the Bill is to be as effective as we wish. If development takes place on the scale that we wish and hope, there will be a quite substantial increase in land values. We saw that in Fort William and we see it, not only in the Highlands but in Fife, as a direct consequence of the Forth Road Bridge. As development goes on, there will be large unearned incomes accruing. The proposed Land Commission Bill will deal with that, I hope, but it is important for us to find out to whom these increments are accruing.

I have no wish to detain the Committee unduly. My hon. Friend the Minister of State for Scotland will no doubt argue that the point of my Amendment is covered by general powers for obtaining information, but I would ask him, if the Highland Board want to develop, say, that 15 per cent. of Ross-shire which no one knows who owns. How does it set about it if it cannot find the owners? I believe there are powers to enable the public to acquire land compulsorily when the ownership of it is not known and cannot be determined.

My final point is, if the Minister of State and the Secretary of State feel that to try to cover the Highlands with the kind of survey indicated in The Scotsman article of Tuesday, 11th May, is impracticable at this season, or would be too costly or too diversionary of the Highland Board's activity, I would ask them if they would consider the desirability of undertaking a pilot scheme in one or more counties to see what the difficulties are and what the results might be. It is for these purposes and more as a probing effort that I put down my Amendment.

It is considerable in businesses. It is borne by businesses and not by the Government. It is a very useful reverse subsidy from industry to the Government. In the Highlands there are many small businesses of one type or another 758 —for example, hotels arnd shops. If they have to provide certain information—they do not yet know what the information is; the Minister has told them nothing about it—they will have to keep far more careful records than many of them keep at present. Perhaps they should keep them already. The keeping of these more careful records will involve extra cost so that information can be provided to the Board.

It is fair to say that the powers as envisaged in the Clause will be a serious deterrent to businesses coming to the Highlands, unless the Minister is to say, as has been said outside, that these powers are being put into the Highlands at the moment only because they are to be put to the rest of the country by future Bills. If this is so, the deterrent argument falls, because industry will be saddled with this sort of nonsense wherever it is in the country. If the Minister says that these powers are to be only in the Highlands and the areas concerned, this must be a deterrent to people coming in, because of the inevitable extra cost of providing information for the Board.

There is, finally, the question of the secrecy of commercial and technical operations. The Minister says that he does not believe that this power will be used in that respect. It may well be that that is so, but every conceivable thing that the Board may want to get is clearly laid down in the Clause, in my view.
