The purpose of the Amendment would, had it been framed in a more satisfactory way, have been to bring a greater degree of precision into the criteria on which the Investigating Committee should inquire into any matter. It would, first, exclude the possibility of a reference to the Disciplinary Committee of a case of serious misconduct which did not amount to or which was dissociated from a criminal offence. There might be examples of this, but it would be undesirable that a loophole should be permitted, because there might be the possibility of misbehaviour in relation to pupils. It is essential, secondly, that the Investigating Committee should not be precluded from having regard to any matter which is brought before it. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Mary-hill (Mr. Hannan) that in Scotland all Common Law offences and many statutory offences are, at least theoretically, indictable. It is essential to remember that the Investigating Committee merely has to decide whether there is a prima facie case to go before the Disciplinary Committee. It has no other function, and the wording used in the Clause is a sufficient guide to the committee in exercising its discretion as to whether or not it should refer a case to the Disciplinary Committee. The decision to refuse registration lies with the Disciplinary Committee, subject to appeal. This seems to be a fair enough protection against any frivolous complaints being referred.

It would, therefore, not be of real help to the Investigating Committee or the Disciplinary Committee to seek to adopt a more precise definition, whether in the form of the Amendment or in any other form. Indeed, for the reasons I have explained, to seek to be more precise—to attempt in any other form to specify offences—would be to hamper the Committees in their functions which are laid on them in the Bill. It would also make it more difficult for the Investigating Committee and the Disciplinary Committee to reach their decisions on the basis of what is relevant to teaching. In Scotland there are no fewer than 29 such schools—I am grateful to the Under-Secretary of State for giving me the information which I should have found difficult to acquire otherwise—of all sorts of description, ranging in size from 37 pupils to over 1,500. There are nearly 20,000 boys and girls educated in these schools. They are in no sense snob schools but are mostly schools which have been set up by endowments. They are widely spread throughout Scotland. There are four in Aberdeen, and they range to the Marr College at Troon. They are in the big cities, and they give education to a wide range of boys and girls drawn from all social classes.

Almost invariably they are fee-paying schools, but in addition to taking fee-paying pupils they offer a large number of places in varying proportions to the local education committees. In some cases these schools are the only secondary education available in the area of an education authority.

These schools are of varying kinds. Some of them are Roman Catholic schools with a separate outlook. But they have one thing common to them all—they have individuality. Their own governors are able to lay down policy for the schools and to a large extent JPEG 303 they are able to experiment with education, not only among secondary pupils but among primary pupils, because practically all these schools take children from the age of 5. It is generally agreed by all concerned that schools of this kind have a value in the educational system of the country. The Committee of which Lord Fleming was Chairman in England and Wales stressed the importance of varied education of one 304 kind and another and said that it would be a pity if these independent schools were swallowed up into the general system because they had an important part to play.
