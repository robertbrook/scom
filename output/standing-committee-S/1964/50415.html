Because transport is embraced, on his own argument, by the words in the Bill, I would suggest that the Amendment might be perfectly reasonable in that it asks, within its job of attending to transport questions, that it should be in on the job of preparing a transport plan. I do not think there is anything objectionable in that. I do not subscribe to his view that by keeping a short line going we are going to run in competition with British Railways. I do not think that has ever been intended.

It is not suggested by the hon. Member for Inverness (Mr. Russell Johnston) that somebody ought to take over British Railways. This is not the point of this Amendment. There are cases—and the Col. 412 hon. Member gave one example—where it might be that the Board would be able to give first-rate advice and the best advice on whether, in fact, it would be better to find some money to keep a small spur railway line going than to construct new miles of extremely expensive road. Road construction is expensive. There is no doubt that if one studies the economics of constructing a road over some of the areas where it was proposed not long ago to withdraw the railway services, the cost would run into many millions of pounds.

That situation may well arise at any time, and it might confront the Board. Therefore, it is far better that the Board should be in at the beginning in the preparation of a plan. After all, the Amendment states, as regards the recommendation from the Highland Transport Board, that anything the Board itself will do will be in consultation with the Secretary of State, the Minister of Transport and, I assume, the local authorities and other corporate bodies, to whom we referred earlier today. It would be extraordinary if the Board were left out of the making of the plan. It cannot be in the mind of my hon. Friend that the Highland Development Board should not be taking part in the preparation of transport plans for the Highlands and Islands area.

If I remember rightly, it was written very firmly into the Labour Party's own policy that each of the regional boards should, in fact, undertake a complete survey of the transport needs of their area and prepare integrated transport schemes. I do not imagine for one minute that it was intended that the Highlands and Islands Development Board should have been excluded from that intention and that programme. Therefore, it would still remain a priority duty as well as a power of the Highlands Development Board, as one of these regional boards, to assist, as it says in the party policy documents, in the preparation of an integrated transport programme for its area. That is perfectly clear and, as the Minister says, we are concerned with keeping Election promises and fulfilling our part of the programme.

It will, in fact, almost automatically be a duty of the Development Board to Image Col. 413 prepare a plan for an integrated transport system, suitable to its own area within national transport planning. I know that on the Election platform I am committed to that. I am committed to it because it was an essential part of Labour Party policy and, therefore, of Government policy as well.

We have still to act, for example, upon the Jack Report and the Kilbrandon Report on rural buses. Two Ministers of Transport have been sitting on these two eggs. So far we have still only taken samples from different areas—none of them, I may say, in the Highlands and Islands. Therefore, planning rural bus services is a job which still has to be done, and done by the most suitable body. Presumably, it will now be done in the Highland and Island area after the Highland Transport Board has passed out of existence, by the next body in line with responsibility, which is the Highland Development Authority.

Transport is an inescapable problem in the Highlands. Road, rail, sea and air transport are all at different stages of development and all working at varying levels of efficiency and inefficiency. We Col. 414 have British Railways running one part of transport in the area. We have MacBraynes operating a considerable part of the sea and bus routes with an involvement of public money aid, at the moment, of something like £500,000 a year. Yet it is under the control operationally almost completely of private directors, answerable to private shareholders, except that on the financial side there is a Government observer watching the interests of the Government's involvement.

Obviously, getting transport right for economic and other development is going to form a very large part of the work of the Highlands and Islands Development Board. I feel that this should be given emphasis, and that there is no reason in this case why the Board should not be given the duty and authority to take part in the preparation of an integrated transport plan in the Highlands, which is what we had in mind.
