There cannot be any quarrel with the underlying purpose of Amendments which seek to bring the public interest to the forefront, and certainly the public interest is what is really at the heart of the matter here. In the ordinary way, if the Secretary of State of the day had to take issue with the Council it would clearly be only on the basis that considerations of the public interest made it necessary, and absolutely necessary, for him to disagree with the Council.

The doubt which arises on the Amendments, and the reason why I fear that I must resist them, is whether it is appropriate, in a statutory provision governing the powers of a Minister of the Crown, to import a reference to the public interest in the way which the hon. Member suggests. It can well be argued that such a reference is quite unnecessary and, indeed, inappropriate. To bring in the phrase "in the public interest" does not add anything effective to the con- 204 tent of the Clause, and, of course, in all fields the actions of Ministers of the Crown are inevitably governed by considerations of the public interest. This must be so by the nature of the system of government in this country. It is, therefore, not only pointless, but somewhat gratuitous, to bring in an express reference to the public interest in a context such as this. I fear, therefore, that we could not accept this Amendment, and I hope that the hon. Gentleman, having understood why this is so, may find it possible to withdraw it.

That is the vital part of the Bill. The way in which we are prepared to give teachers a degree of self-determination with regard to the regulations dealing with qualifications for entry into their profession indicates the degree to which we are prepared to accept the real intent of the Wheatley Committee. To regulate the terms of entry into the teaching profession is precisely and exactly what the teachers want, and the fact that this Amendment is necessary shows that we are not yet providing that.

There are four ways in which we can express the relationship between the Sec- 208 retary of State and the Council. First, we can say that the Secretary of State should be given a power to accept the recommendations of the Council. In other words, if the Council recommends, the Secretary of State will accept. Secondly, we can say that the Secretary of State should be given power to accept, but also given power to veto or reject. Extending it further, we can say that he should be given power to accept or reject, and also, to a limited degree, to initiate. Lastly, we can say that he should be given power to accept, to reject, or to initiate as he wills, which is what the Bill says.

At the beginning of the debate of the consideration of the Principle of the Bill, the Secretary of State referred to what Lord Wheatley had said. He said:

"We therefore had knowledge and experience, men who … started with pre-conceived ideas about the problem, and yet every one of whom moved from those pre-conceived ideas towards general agreement.  I agree with the right hon. Wheatley did not recommend this. He did not recommend that the Secretary of State should be empowered merely to accept recommendations of the Council. He did not even recommend that the Secretary of State should merely be confined to having the power of veto. What he said was that if this Council is to be successful in any situation of crisis—and it is a situation of crisis about which we have to think—it is necessary for a strong curb to be placed on the freedom of the Secretary of State to initiate regulations at will.

I think I am correct in saying that he left in 1954, so a lot has gone in since then.

In evidence to the Select Committee was given an analysis of the kind of JPEG 217 Legislation on which it would be expected that the affirmative procedure would relate to regulations under a Measure. The first instance—and I am paraphrasing paragraph 75 of the Report of the Select Committee—is where powers were given which when exercised, would substantially affect the provisions of an Act whether by altering, increasing or changing its extent, its duration or otherwise. Such is not the nature of the Bill before the Committee. The second category was comprised of powers to impose financial charges. This is not a Bill in that category. The third consisted of cases of skeleton powers where the Act fixes the purposes and leaves a substantial amount to be dealt with by subordinate legislation. This is not that kind of Bill, either. The kind specified on the occasion of the publication of the Report I am mentioning was the Development Council Order made under the 1947 Act where one was dealing with a Measure which stated, in effect, that a Development Council Order could be set up for any industry. That does not relate to the type of Bill before the Committee, which deals with educational provisions and so on.

I have endeavoured to show that the understanding of the hon. Member for Inverness—of where it is and is not suitable to have the affirmative procedure as against the negative procedure for all the regulations emanating from a Measure—has led him to a false conclusion as to the arrangements under a Bill of this kind. On the other hand, the Wheatley recommendation on procedure provides in the event of a disagreement between a Minister of the Crown and a non-Parliamentary body for the latter body being allowed to determine what happens inside the House of Commons. In our view this is not acceptable.

Having found ourselves unable to accept the Wheatley recommendation as it stands, and having come to that conclusion on good grounds, and being faced with the choice between the negative and affirmative procedure for every regulation under the Bill, we have felt it correct to say that it should be done by the negative procedure. We know that this will give ample opportunity for any disagreement on any regulation proposed between the Secretary of State and the Teaching 218 Council to be fully explored in the same way as regulations are explored now under the negative procedure, the only substantial difference being the time scale allowed for debate.

A substantial concession has been made to the opinion among teachers; that is embodied in subsection (7), the abandonment of provisional regulations. This should wholly convince the teaching profession that the Government are very anxious indeed to meet the points which it puts on this issue—to meet its point that it would not wish the Secretary of State to have powers which would seem to by-pass the duties and responsibilities of the Teaching Council.
