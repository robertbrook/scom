Mr. T. G. D. Galbraith
: It is not often, as the hon. Member for Fife, West (Mr. William Hamilton) has just said, that he and I find ourselves in agreement with each other. On this occasion the reason perhaps is that our discussions have not been across the Floor of the House or of the Committee but have been, as it were, behind the scenes, when I always find the hon. Member very much more reasonable, if I may say so, than on other occasions.

There is no need for me to tell the Committee that when the Bill was first introduced I did not like it at all. The last time a similar Bill was introduced JPEG 5 by the hon. Gentleman who is now the Minister of State, who, I am glad to see, is with us, I spoke against it and I am glad to say that on a vote it was defeated. However, we must recognise that the political scene has changed since then. There is now a Socialist Government. I do not think they are likely to last very long, but there they are, and they are opposed to the existence of these ex officio officers. That is a fact which we have to take into account. But if the political scene has changed the Bill, as it now will emerge with the Amendments being proposed this morning, is a very different Bill from that of the Minister of State, and which the House, in the last Parliament, rejected so decisively. He may be Minister of State but he has shown that he is a less skilful politician than his hon. Friend the Member for Fife, West. It may be that in any future reconstruction of the Government—if there is time for any—this point will be noted.

I apologise to the Committee if I am taking rather longer than did the hon. Member for Fife, West, but it is necessary for me to explain to some of our supporters outside the Committee exactly how we are where we are this morning. The Bill began, as the old one did, as a Measure proposing to abolish out and out the right of ex officio officers to sit in town councils, although they have sat there for hundreds of years, doing very valuable work. After the Bill was published the hon. Member for Fife, West, and I had some discussions, and he amended it to allow the officers to sit in the Committees and in the council. Originally they were going to be abolished altogether.

I thought this was an improvement, but I still felt that the Bill was unduly severe and unnecessarily restrictive for the hon. Member's purposes, which I entirely understood, even though I did not necessarily agree with them, so he and I had some further discussions and, as he has indicated, we agreed that if he would introduce the present Amendment to allow voting in the committees we on this side would no longer block the Bill, as we had the right to do, and as we had the power to do because of the position of the Bill in the Ballot.

The effect of the further Amendment will be that the ex officio officers will be 6 able to take part in the day to day affairs of the corporations, and to speak on any issue and vote in the committees, but they will not be able to vote in the council as a whole. That is the price that has had to be paid for the continued membership of these ex officio officers in the various corporations to which they are attached.

Taking into account the political situation—a Socialist Government opposed to these ex officio officers altogether, and a Conservative Opposition able to prevent any action at the present time—I think this is a very fair compromise from the point of view of both sides of the Committee.

The Government side would like to abolish these officers, root and branch, as they have said many times, but, to prevent them now, at this moment, from having the power to influence decisions in closely-balanced corporations—and I understand there is only one of them in Scotland—the Government side are willing to accept some other features which they do not really like, such as the continued existence of the officers in the councils although with diminished powers. In the same way we on this side would have preferred the status quo because these officers, in our view, have been doing very valuable work for a great number of years, and in an era when so many business men live outside city boundaries contribute greatly to the prosperity of various cities as deans of guild or deacon conveners or conveners of trades, and represent the business element. And represent them democratically, I should point out, because in Glasgow the number of voters who vote for them is more than 10,000. The ex officio officers bring a form of experience and advice which is valuable to a thriving community, and which should be preserved, if possible.

Feeling like this, we could have blocked the Bill outright, and prevented any change from taking place, at least in the immediate future. This course of action was canvassed, I must admit, by several, although not by a majority of my own hon. Friends. I personally, though I felt with my heart like them, with my head did not feel that that was wise, and so I got in touch with all the bodies represented. When hon. Members see the size of the pile of JPEG 7 communications which I have had I think they may feel that perhaps the Bill, instead of being called "Hamilton's Bill", might well be called "Galbraith's Bill", because I am sure that I have done more work on it than the hon. Gentleman has. But merit is not always rewarded as it should be.

At any rate, I got in touch with all these bodies and they all agreed rapidly, as one of them put it, that half a loaf is better than no bread at all and that the compromise should be accepted, although, naturally, it was not something they wanted to do. They thought it would be better to accept the compromise than run the risk that on some future occasion the hon. Member for Fife, West might be more fortunate and higher up the Ballot, and, having been thwarted this time, would be influenced to retaliate by sweeping these ex officio officers away altogether.

The bodies affected were encouraged to take this view because the right hon. Gentleman the Secretary of State for Scotland told me in a letter that if the hon. Gentleman's Bill got through and on to the Statute Book it was not his intention to pursue the matter any further; in fact, these ex officio officers would be taken out of the arena of political controversy altogether in the future—though that is something they never would be—and would be able to continue to perform the bulk of the work which they have been doing so well for so long. Looking at the matter from the point of view of both sides of this Committee I think this is a reasonable compromise.

Final authority rests with the democratically elected representatives. This is something for which the Labour Party has fought for a great number of years. If the Bill goes through, as amended, they will have achieved that, and that is the real object of the operation from their point of view.

From the point of view of the ex officio officers, who only reach their offices after long years of voluntary service of great benefit to the community, they will still be able to give almost as much if not as much service in the future as they have done in the past. The value of their traditions and public-spirited service will not be lost to the community, and 8 they will continue to form an integral part of the councils and of their work. They have had to sacrifice something. What they sacrifice is their vote in the full corporation. They have shown that they are willing to make this sacrifice in order to be able to continue and to preserve the right to serve the community as they have done for so many years.

Therefore we on both sides of this Committee, without too much argument, should set our seal upon the compromise which will give nobody exactly what he wants but gives everybody something which he believes to be of great value.

Mr. Anthony Stodart
: As one of those representing the City of Edinburgh, whose special interest has, perhaps, been naturally greater than that of certain of the other three cities, it is not improper or unreasonable that I should say that although this Bill does not go as far as I would have liked, it represents a considerable triumph for what I believe to be common sense. I am thinking not only in terms of political expediency, but the whole approach to this matter on each side of the Committee.

I do not think anyone would deny the importance of the work done by the dean of guilds and his court. This is not under dispute. He and his court decide on matters concerning such things as the erecting of all buildings, ensuring they are built to the proper specification, and protecting the rights of those who are to live there, the formation of streets and new highways, and so on. It is, therefore, 10 of great importance for the efficient carrying out of this job that there should be, as dean, somebody who has technical knowledge of what must be a highly technical subject. One cannot guarantee that one will get such a man from among the elected members of the town councils and local authorities of any of the cities.

One could find an expert in the matter from among the officials of the town councils but I think an extremely important feature of the dean of guild Court is the complete independence of the local authority and in such a case that would be lost. Having taken part in certain matters when the Corporation and the City of Edinburgh have been in dispute with the dean of guild Court, I think that were the dean of guild Court to lose that independence by having an official of the Corporation presiding over it it would be a retrograde step. We are, therefore, continuing to make it possible to introduce into the civil affairs of these four cities someone who is an expert and who will be an independent dean of guild.

The only matter which has been in dispute is how great a part he should play in civic affairs. One might ask, should he be allowed to play as much part as the aldermen do in England? Aldermen are not elected by the ratepayers, but by the councils, and the only limitation I have been able to find on aldermen in England is that they cannot take part in the election of other aldermen. Otherwise they vote as freely as any democratically elected councillor. That would not be an altogether unreasonable analogy here. That is why, particularly where one has what might be broadly described as a needle situation, in which the votes are very close, I do not believe that the votes of non-elected persons ought to be decisive in matters outside their own sphere of interest. It is surely right that the deans of guild should vote in the committees on which they sit where their experience counts, such as the planning and highways committees.

The only point at which I fall out on this Bill is that I think there is a very good case for these non-representatives being permitted, not only to report the conclusions and the views of their specialist committees to the council, but having reported that the committee feels JPEG 11 strongly about a certain point they should be allowed to cast their votes in the council on the matters within their sphere of influence but not on other matters. Had that been the final situation I would have regarded this as having met all the views I hold of this matter. As my hon. Friend has said, when one gets sharply defined views held on each side of the committee it would be too much to expect one should get all one's views conceded 100 per cent. The view I hold, thanks to the exchange of opinions which have taken place in considerable amity, is that we have reached a conclusion which is fair, democratic, and will meet the wishes of the great majority of those who are affected.

CRIMINAL PROCEDURE (SCOTLAND) BILL

Clause 1.—(ADMISSIONS AND AGREEMENTS AS TO EVIDENCE IN SOLEMN PROCEDURE.)

Question proposed, That the Clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Wylie
: May I shortly explain the purpose of the Clause? It is to enable parties to proceedings on indictment in Scotland to agree facts and documents which are not in dispute. The present law on this matter on indictment is governed by the provisions of Section 20 of the Administration of Justice (Scotland) Act, 1933, as amended by Section 12 of the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act, 1938. Those Sections make it possible in proceedings on indictment for parties to agree documents. Section 20(1) of the 1933 Act provides:

"In any trial on indictment it shall not be necessary for the prosecutor or the accused to prove any documents which are admitted by the other party, and copies of any documents may, where the parties so agree, be accepted as equivalent to the originals, provided that no such admission or agreement by the accused shall be accepted, except in a case in which he has legal assistance in his defence."

That is as far as the law of procedure goes as respects proceedings on indictment. It covers only agreement as to documents. It does not cover agreement as to facts which are not in dispute.

For some time in summary procedure this has been operated effectively in terms of Section 36 of the Summary Jurisdiction (Scotland) Act, 1954, which provides that in summary procedure both documents which are not in dispute and facts which are not in dispute can be agreed by the parties, provided always—this is an essential safeguard in all these cases—that the accused is legally represented. Section 36(1) of the 1954 Act provides:

"It shall not be necessary in any summary prosecution, for either party to lead proof of any fact which is admitted by the opposite party, or to prove any documents the terms and application of which are not in dispute, and copies of any documents may, by agreement of the parties, be accepted as equivalent to the originals: Provided that this subsection shall not apply unless the accused has legal assistance in his defence."

26 For a number of years it has been possible in summary procedure for parties—the prosecutor on the one hand, and the accused through his legal representative, on the other hand,—to narrow down the issue which is in dispute between the parties by agreeing that such matters as are in dispute shall be dealt with in this way.

In proceedings on indictment a great deal of time is taken up by the prosecutor proving facts which at the moment cannot be agreed but which may not be in dispute. This is aggravated by the two-witness rule, the requirement of the law of evidence in Scotland that there must be corroboration of any material fact. The effect of this is that in many cases a great deal of time is taken up proving facts which are not in dispute. A great deal of public money is expended on bringing witnesses, sometimes from all over the country, and a great deal of the time of jurors as well as of the court is taken up in proving these matters.

The purpose of the Clause is to make it possible for the prosecutor and the accused through his legal representative to agree facts which are not in dispute. The Clause in effect re-enacts the provisions of the Administration of Justice (Scotland) Act with regard to documents. The purpose is so that the whole law on this matter of agreement in solemn procedure can be found in the one piece of legislation and so that it will not be necessary for anyone concerned to go first to this Measure and then to the 1933 Act, as amended by the 1938 Act. In other words, this is a consolidating Measure, to the extent that it introduces a new principle in solemn procedure, namely, proceedings on indictment, whereby facts not in dispute can be agreed, and it consolidates the existing law with regard to documents.

Before I close my submissions on this matter, may I make some observations on drafting? I must accept responsibility for the drafting of the Clause. When I studied it, it seemed to me that there were provisions here which could be improved. I would be grateful if the Minister of State would in due course help me through the Parliamentary draftsmen. The last part of subsection (1) reads:

"and a copy of any document may, where they so agree, be accepted as equivalent to the original document".

JPEG 27 That is, in effect, repeated at the end of subsection (3). Subject to such advice as the Minister of State may give on this matter, I should have thought that it is not necessary to have both provisions in the Bill. It may be possible to dispense with one of them. I should have thought that it would be best to delete the first provision.

It has also occurred to me that it might be wiser to include in the Bill the additional provision added by the 1938 Act to Section 20 of the Administration of Justice (Scotland) Act, 1933. The provision which was added to the 1933 Act reads:

"(3) Any reference in this section to proof or admission of a document shall include a reference to proof or admission as to the person by whom a document was written, executed, or received, and as to the time and place of such writing, execution, or receipt."

This is a provision which it might be advisable to include in this consolidating Measure. I should be grateful if the Minister of State would consider this matter.

I have had my attention drawn through an article in the Scots Law Times to the fact that difficulties may arise if an accused through his legal representative agrees a joint minute in this way and then subsequently dispenses with the services of his legal advisers and continues the case on his own behalf. I can see that a difficulty could conceivably arise. If a joint minute had been agreed in this way and then, after the prosecutor had opened his case, the accused dispensed with his legal advisers and sought to withdraw the minute, difficulties might well arise. On the other hand, this has operated perfectly satisfactorily in summary procedure where the same problems could arise. Perhaps it is not necessary to introduce a safeguarding provision of that character. I mention it as an aspect of the Bill which may be the subject of criticism. It may at a later stage be necessary to consider this and, if necessary, put in amending provisions. I have no desire to take up the time of the Committee further on this Clause this morning.

Clause 4.—(INTEREST ON CLIENTS' MONEY.)

Question proposed, That the Clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. T. Steele
: This is the most important Clause in this Measure, and perhaps I will be forgiven if I put on the record its actual provisions. It deals with interest on clients' money. The object of the Clause is to resolve the problems created by the decision in Brown v. the Commissioners' of Inland Revenue. That case affirmed the principle for a fiduciary relationship existing between solicitor and client and said that in consequence a solicitor might not, without full knowledge and free assent of his clients, obtain any pecuniary benefits from him other than proper charges and disbursements. The point of that case was that it applied that principle to clients' money in the hands of the solicitor which the solicitor puts on deposit in his own name for a number of clients generally and which in consequence earned interest. It was held that the solicitor must account for that interest, irrespective of the difficulties of apportionment.

It should be made abundantly clear that the solicitors profession completely JPEG 35 accept the decision in Brown. There is not the slightest desire on the part of the Law Society in Scotland, or on the part of the legal practitioners to repeal the decision in that case. On the contrary it is the foundation of the practice of a solicitor that this relationship exists and should be maintained. The Clause to which the assent of Parliament is now sought is designed, not to repeal this case, but in some respect to put additional obligations on to solicitors and to define the proper way in which the principles of the case should be applied in certain circumstances.

As it stands today the position is that a solicitor who receives clients' moneys is under obligation to put them in a separate bank account, apart from his own moneys. There is no general obligation on the solicitor to put clients' moneys on deposit or to earn interest upon them, but if he does he must account for it.

The difficulty in the practical application of this principle relates to sums which are too small to be put on deposit, or sums which are held for too short a period to justify their being put on deposit. A solicitor will have in his clients' accounts a large number of small sums in respect of rents, small debts collected, maintenance payments or income of a client. It would be ridiculous to suppose that there could be any possibility of that money being put on deposit, earning interest which should be accounted for to the client. There is the other extreme, of sums which are large enough in themselves to be justifiably to put on deposit but which are being held for too short a period. A typical example is in a conveyancing practice where a large number of conveyancing matters are always going on, such as the buying and selling of houses of £3,000 or £4,000 in value. The solicitor will have these sums coming in and will be paying them out again within a few days.

There are two elements in this matter which have to be taken into account—the size of the amount and the period over which it will be wanted. The problem arises in this way. Although these sums, whether by reason of their amount or by reason of the period during which they are held, cannot properly or reasonably be put on deposit in the name or 36 for behoof of individual clients it is possible for a solicitor, prudently to look at the sum total of his clients' accounts and say, "I think I could safely put a fifth, a quarter or a third of this money on deposit for a period of time and earn interest upon it." The difficulty, in practice, of applying the decision in Brown's case is that it is impossible to calculate, what, if any, part of the interest earned could be attributable to the small sums to which reference has been made. It is impractical to do the arithmetic and work it out in that way. Therefore if a solicitor is to comply strictly with the full principle of Brown's case there is only one things he can do and that is not to put the money on deposit at all.

In that case, nothing is earned, the clients gain nothing and the solicitor loses a certain amount of interest. The only party who gains is the bank, which will have the money on current account without paying any interest at all. It can probably be said that it was certainly not the intention of the other place, in laying down the very right and proper kind of principle established in Brown's case that the client should get nothing, that the solicitor should lose and that the bank should gain.

The remedy proposed for this situation is that where it is fair the client's money shall be put on deposit in the name of, or for behoof of the client. A legal obligation to put the money on deposit on this respect will be created in the right and proper case, where it is fair to the client. It is also proposed that if the solicitor does not put the money on deposit, in a case where he should have done so, then he shall still be bound to account to the client for that amount, with the interest which would have been earned if he had put the money on deposit.

I have referred to two factors, the amount of any particular sum and the period during which it is held. Each of these is relevant in coming to a conclusion as to whether, in fairness to the client, the money should or should not be put on deposit. Broadly speaking, rough justice might be done if it were said it would be unnecessary to put the money on deposit or to account for the interest if the sum were less than £500 or the period over which it was JPEG 37 held were less than eight weeks or two months.

Neither of these factors can be regarded as absolute. In some cases where the sum would be less than £500, say £450, it might be available for six months, and clearly that money ought to be put on deposit in the name or for behoof of the individual client. The solution proposed is that above the limit of £500 or two months there shall be an absolution obligation to put the money on deposit in the name or for behoof of the individual client. Below those limits there is an obligation to put the money on deposit where it is fair and right for the solicitor to do so in the interests of the client. The means whereby it is proposed to carry that principle into effect is this Clause, coupled with rules which the Law Society of Scotland would make under the Clause. The rules made by the Law Society must be approved by the Lord President of the Court of Session before they come into operation, and I have the authority of the Law Society to say that a rule has been drafted to deal with the matter and the Lord President of the Court of Session has seen and approved it.
