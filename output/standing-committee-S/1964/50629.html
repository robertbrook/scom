Mr. Edward M. Taylor
: I have the privilege of replying to what I think will be the shortest and least controversial speech that we have heard from the Under-Secretary of State this Session. This is the first Estimates debate I have attended and it is also the first occasion upon which I have been asked to open a debate. It may, therefore, be prudent for me to apologise in advance to the Under-Secretary for any apparent breach in the Front Bench etiquette. I can think of no better words to do this than those used to me by a very burly centre forward who came from Greenock, in a football match some years ago. After up-ending me rather viciously in a football match he comforted me with the words, "I am so sorry; it is not intentional, except when I mean it."

This debate is concerned with the vital and human problem of local government finance and the rates burden. We have the advantage that many Members on this Committee have had the recent experience of serving as members of local government bodies. It must be apparent to everyone that the massive post-war increase in local spending and the new circumstances created by revaluation in 1961 have made finance the most acute 6 and serious problem of local government today.

In the last nine years we have had a dramatic advance in education and housing production and in almost every single field of local government activity the advances have involved an increase of about 100 per cent. in the total local government spending in Scotland. In less than a decade the amount gathered in rates has increased from less than £60 million to over £111 million. Under the revaluation of 1961 the average share of the domestic ratepayer declined from 60 per cent. to about 52½ per cent. This change was by no means equal throughout the country, and the element of hardship did not decrease, despite this apparent reduction. The burden of the rates at present is a massive one.

To take the cities alone, in Glasgow at the present time the average man, woman and child carries a rate burden, direct or indirect, of £27 3s. 1ld. This means about £100 for the average family which they pay on the house they live in or in the prices in the shops or through the firms and factories in which they work. In Edinburgh the figure is £23 9s. which is substantially below Glasgow for no apparent reason. In Aberdeen the figure is £24 7s. and in Dundee £22 5s. I think we can gauge the hardship involved by these figures when we consider the amounts of rates in arrears. The most recent figure for Glasgow was 2·37 per cent. in arrears, which means that at the last count there were over £650,000 arrears of rates in the City of Glasgow. Glasgow was by no means the county with the highest percentage: the highest I can find is Ross and Cromarty where there are around 4 per cent. arrears of rates. These figures are from the Rating Review, and they show clearly that the rating burden involves a great deal of hardship because many people simply cannot afford to pay.

Industry and commerce also bear a mighty load of rates and I hope the Secretary of State will look carefully at all the available information before he seeks to take an easy way out of this problem by abolishing derating next year. One Scottish company already paying more than £1½ million in rates recently did a survey of comparable firms in England and Scotland. It had the professional JPEG 7 assistance of one of the local assessors and it found that the basis of assessment with the Scottish and English firms was approximately the same, with Scotland perhaps a little higher, but it found the actual rate poundage burden in England among these firms was less than half that in Scotland which means that all things remaining equal, and they certainly will not be exactly equal, when derating goes it will mean that for every £1 in rates paid in England the Scottish firms will pay approximately £2 10s. There are many firms affected in the same way. Some of the Scottish shipyards have to allow more than £10,000 in the cost of one ship for the burden of rates.

Faced with this hardship the previous Government appointed the Allen Committee to look into the incidence of the rates burden. Now we have this Report we see that one of the principal findings is that in Scotland ratepayers are having a pretty rough time. In paragraph 215 of the Report we have:

"The average rate payment in Scotland is greater than in England and Wales as a whole for all income groups, and very considerably so."

Later, in paragraph 217, this is put in a clearer and more specific way, when the Report states that in Scotland rates comprise about 50 per cent. of housing costs in every income group compared with between 28 per cent. or 30 per cent. in England and Wales as a whole. While we were waiting for this Report the previous Administration passed the Rating (Interim Relief) Act to give local authorities the power to remit rates in cases of hardship. In Scotland local authorities have had this power for a long time, and for that reason provision was made for an additional £1 million in the Scottish General Grants.

Everyone appreciated this could only be a stopgap measure because it in no way solved the fundamental problems of the rating system in so far as it affected Scotland. That is why the previous Administration set in motion the review of the relationship between rates and grants. Local authorities in Scotland were very hopeful that something useful would come out of this review because they appreciated it was a feasible proposition for a Government which had reduced general taxation so greatly in these 13 8 good years, in terms of transferring part of the burden from the ratepayer to the national Exchequer. Income Tax was brought down from 9s. 6d. to 7s. 9d., the Purchase Tax maximum rate from 100 per cent. to 25 per cent. and the general burden of taxation on the national income brought from over 30 per cent. to less than 25 per cent. Local authorities could, therefore, regard this transferring of part of the burden from the ratepayer to the Exchequer as something feasible.

How can we possibly think of such a possibility from a Government who in seven months have increased the burden of general taxation so greatly? Unfortunately, and perhaps tragically, we did not have the opportunity of carrying out these proposals. As is only too obvious from the composition of this Committee a new Government were elected. If nothing else confirms this, it is abundantly clear, from the simple issue of local government finance, that this present Government were elected reluctantly and with a threadbare majority on the basis of irresponsible and wild promises which have since been flagrantly and cynically broken.

There must have been many members of my party, many of our traditional supporters, who were tempted to jump on the Socialist bandwagon by the clear and specific promises which were given to give early relief from problems of rating in Scotland. Their manifesto, the New Britain, gave the impression that these problems would be solved with the speed of an agile antelope but instead we have had the tired tortoise steadily and deliberately stepping backwards over a period of seven months. If hon. Members opposite question this I think we should look at the extent of the commitment in the Election manifesto, the New Britain.

Page 13 of the manifesto says:

"While the reform of the rating system and an investigation of alternative methods of local government finance may take some time to accomplish, we shall seek to give early relief to ratepayers by transferring a larger part of the burden of public expenditure from local authorities to the Exchequer."

On page 14, talking about the massive leap forward in education which we were to have, it says:

"To assure the funds, Labour will restore the percentage grant and transfer the larger part of the costs of teachers' salaries from the rates to the Exchequer."

JPEG 9 On page 15:

"Labour will introduce a policy of lower interest rates for housing."

There we have three particular assurances, and we have seen no signs of their materialising.

There was the publication of Scotland's own manifesto, Signposts for Scotland, and here the message was just as clear and specific. On page 20 it said:

"The Exchequer will carry a larger share of the financial responsibility for education."


"While our long-term plans for local government finance are being worked out local authorities will be given immediate inducements both to step up the housing programmes and make greater use of their powers".

On page 18:

I note the manifesto says "immediate" and not "early." How long is early and how long is immediate? I hope the Under-Secretary of State will give us an indication of precisely what the Government mean by the words "immediate" and the word "early", and how precisely are these pledges to be honoured by a Government who have already made savage increases in general taxation.

Where is the money to be found? From the road tolls defined as indefensible in page 10 of Signposts? from the slashing of investment allowances which were going to be increased according to page 8 of the same document. Or is it from higher loan charges on housing which were going to be slashed, according to page 18? These promises cannot lightly be laughed away, because it is to them that the present Government owe their shaky existence.

It would be wrong to say that the Government have done nothing to deal with the problems of local government finance. A great deal has been done, and I think the ratepayers of Scotland will suffer the consequences within the next few months. Apart from their indifference to election pledges the Government have shown amazing ignorance as to the effects of their own policies. In the General Grant debate on 15th December the Minister of Housing suggested that rates in England and Wales would probably go up by 6 or 7 per cent. Later, in May, he admitted they would go up by more than 10 per cent. We expected better things for Scotland. We expected a more exact estimate and we had one. On 4th 10 April, at Question Time the Under-Secretary of State said that rates in Scotland would rise this year by about 4 per cent., which would add 30s. to the burden of the average ratepayer. This Government have already produced a Mr. Three Per Cent. and in my mind the Under-Secretary will always be Mr. Four Per Cent.

What has happened in the two short months since them? We have only had two authorities declare their rates for the next year and we still have the worst ones to come although there have been many grim warnings from the major cities and counties as to what is going to happen. Dunbarton County Council is making no 4 per cent. increase nor a 5 or 6 per cent., but a massive 1s. 6d. increase in rates which is more than 7 per cent. On Thursday last we had the announcement by the Burgh of Greenock, which may be known to some Members opposite. When I contacted the Scottish Office yesterday they had not been advised of the actual increase, but from information which I have no reason to doubt, because it comes from the hard-pressed ratepayers of Greenock, I understand that the rates there increased by no less than 1s. 9d. to a record 25s. This increase was not 4 per cent. but about double that amount. I am glad to have the opportunity of advising the Under-Secretary of this latest increase. The worst is still to come, and I think it is clear from figures we have that the rates increase in Scotland will be much more than 4 per cent., probably of the order of 8 per cent. When are we going to have the early relief and the immediate inducements which were promised by the Government?

What have the Government done on the capital side of local government spending? This is the most vital part of the problem. When I was a member of the Glasgow Corporation I remember hearing time and time again from the City Treasurer, now the hon. Member for Glasgow, Springburn (Mr. Buchanan), that capital spending was the real problem of local government, because this was building up trouble for the future. At present, according to the Rating Review, local authorities in Scotland have, or had last month, a capital debt of £958 million. The figure JPEG 11 must now exceed £1,000 million. Specially low interest rates for local authorities would, therefore, be of immense value, because a reduction of 1 per cent. in the average interest payments for local authorities could mean a saving of £10 million, or 2s. in the rates. This would be an enormous saving.

Of course, however, we have not had any such luck. Instead, we have had the longest ever period of 7 per cent. Bank Rate, culminating in a phoney reduction to 6 per cent., which means little if anything to local authorities. Just after the announcement of the reduction to 6 per cent., we had the latest local authority loan by the City of Swansea with a coupon of 6¾ per cent. issued below par. In the interim, local authorities have had to pay the highest price ever, round about 9 per cent., for short-term loans.

What about grants? This was the obvious way for the Government to transfer part of the burden if they really wanted to. If they meant to carry out their Election pledge of transferring part of the burden from the ratepayer to the Exchequer, the simplest and obvious way was to do it by means of the general grant. When the announcement came of what the Government had in mind for the next two years, however, we had a general grant which carried exactly the same proportion of local government spending as in previous years. At the time, the Government tried to cover this up by mentioning an extra £1·2 million which they were to give in the second year. If we investigate this, it becomes clear that it is nothing new and is no concession whatever.

In the debate on the general grant in England and Wales the hon. Member for Southall (Mr. Pargiter) made it clear that that was no concession and that it was merely taking into account what had happened time and time again in previous two-year periods, namely, that in the second year there was a substantial increase in local government spending through no fault of the local authorities. This was always compensated for by later measures introduced to increase the general grant. The Government have simply decided to make this move a 12 little earlier. We have, however, had no increase in the proportion of local government spending which is borne by the Exchequer. Where is the early relief and when are we to get it?

Apart from that, local authorities in Scotland will pay dearly for the 6d. per gallon increase in the price of petrol, and many of their vehicles now carry the extra 50 per cent. licence duty. The 33⅓ per cent. increase in postal charges will seriously worsen the already serious plight of local authorities, and an increase of 3s. 3d. in the National Insurance contributions will seriously hit local authorities like Glasgow, which has more than 46,000 employees. At that time, the City Treasurer in Glasgow estimated that the Labour Government would cost Glasgow an extra £200,000 or so, and this was before the most serious measures came forward.

Quite apart from that, the general increase in prices during these seven months, coming to just under 5 per cent., will greatly increase the burden of local authorities and must inevitably lead to a substantial increase in the rate burden. Many of the essential items in local government budgets have been substantially increased while even the new import surcharges are also having their effect. If all these things are the culmination of the early relief which we expect from the Socialists, I fear what the prospect will be when we have a major review.

What, then, should the Government do? First, they must bring an end to the senseless and foolish economic policies which are crippling enterprise in the nation and which are having a serious effect on local government finance. Secondly, they must indicate clearly and precisely how and when they will take steps, if not to redeem their election pledges, at least to make good the damage which they have caused in these seven disastrous months.

Thirdly, the Government must ensure that spendthrift local authorities are restrained from further excesses. At a time when local government finance faces a crisis in Scotland we do not expect the Secretary of State for Scotland to approve such ridiculous escapades as the recent uninvited jaunt of one of the city councillors to America to hear his city engineer make a speech. Any waste and JPEG 13 extravagance at such a time must be ruthlessly rooted out. The Secretary of State certainly has a responsibility here.

Fourthly, justice and equity must be brought into municipal housing finance. There is no question about this. This is a matter which we have pressed over and over again.

Before I present the Government's case for what we are doing, I want to sketch a little of the background to supplement the undoubted fair representation which we have had from the hon. Between 1951–52 and 1963–64, the total expenditure of local authorities, leaving aside trading undertakings such as the public transport system in the four cities, increased from £102 million to £270 million. It is true that grants more or less kept pace with that increase in expenditure, but the net result to the ratepayer was that the sum demanded by way of rates rose from £37 million to £104 million. In the financial year 1964–65 just ended, the figure is estimated to be £111 million. That is the measure of what the Opposition did when they were in power.

If we analyse the 1964–65 figures, it will be seen that the gross expenditure on rate fund services, excluding trading undertakings and the common good, was £302 million. Receipts, mainly rents, amounted to £51 million and grants to £140 million, leaving rates to bear the figure of £111 million.
