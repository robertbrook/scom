It is perfectly true that in some of our industrial developments in the Highlands, and particularly in, for example, agriculture, in forestry and in fishing operations there is very wide co-operation between the people engaged in them in passing on useful new ideas, new techniques and new methods. There is very little "I have got a new idea and I am going to stick to it myself" in this type of industry. The same cannot be said with anything like the same certainty in the industrial and commercial fields as a whole. There are specialist methods of operation which are of great economic benefit to the firms using them, and if they gave them to their competitors they would get a great deal less in return for the skill and hard work of their workers and shareholders.

The Minister of State has not given the Committee one single example to show why this type of information is wanted or what sort of information is likely to be asked for under this Clause. It is difficult for the Opposition, not knowing in the least what is in the mind of the Minister of State, or indeed of the Secretary of State, to try to show to the Committee the possible dangers which may lie in the proposals in the Clause. I have chosen three examples which may give the Committee a not unfair picture of the sort of problem which may well arise. It is simple for the Minister to say that they will not happen, but he is taking powers so that they can happen if he wants them to.

The first example is the new industry which I hope will grow very fast once it gets established in the Highlands, fish farming. This is a completely new enterprise and a number of firms are actively interested in it. They want to carry out research for growing many different sorts of fish, and some firms are thinking of putting many millions of pounds into the operation. They certainly will not want JPEG 763 to give to their competitors information about their skills and the results of their researches.

Another example is the drink industry as a whole. On Drambuie the Minister said there may be reasons why information should not be given to anyone else. Almost every firm which distills whisky or brews beer has its own special process, guarded with a great deal of care. The information should not be demanded and given to everyone.

The third industry which I have looked at from this point of view is one which is very well known to the hon. Member for the Western Isles (Mr. Malcolm MacMillan), the textile industry. I had a certain amount of interest in it in the old days. It was extremely valuable and important to know what one's competitors were doing—about colour, about design. It is no secret that one's agents in New York or Montreal, or somewhere of that sort, were watching, trying to get the best information about what one's competitors were doing. That sort of information should not be made available to the Board and to its members and to its officers, just for the sake of knowing what is going on.

When one turns to the business of hotel keepers, crofters and the smaller people, it is very unlikely that the Board will want to question them in detail about what they are doing, what their receipts are and whether they had two or four people bed and breakfasting; but the powers to do it are in the Clause.

The Committee must realise that, and must pay attention to it. I think it was during the Second Reading debate that I said that the Clause gives to the Highland Development Board and to its officers powers which the police, the Inspectors of Taxes, and the Board of Trade do not possess for any purpose, except that when a firm goes bankrupt or is involved in fraud there is, very properly, a public inquiry.

The Board will get much better co-operation, and possibly, in the long run, more accurate returns of the information it really needs, by going to the people and getting it from them willingly and cheerfully. Highlanders are not a non-co-operative race by nature. They are only non-co-operative when the Government are trying to force them to do something which they know they should not and need not do.

As I understand it and from my researches into this over the last few weeks, these powers seem to fall into two broad categories. First, a number of Statutes, some of which have been referred to, confer the power to ascertain the name and address of persons with an interest in land. That is quite common and quite understandable in matters relating to the acquisition of land and the development of land. The second category is where the statutory body is given more general powers linked to the functions of the statutory body itself. In all those cases it will be found that the main function of the statutory body is as a source of subsidy or a source of provision of grants. In all those cases, where the main function of the statutory body is to provide grants and subsidies, there is an understandable power to acquire general information on which the basis of those grants and subsidies can be calculated. It is an application of the principle of a means test, in a way, the kind of thing one gets in the Legal Aid (Scotland) Act where, before legal aid is granted, power is given to the National Assistance Board to ascertain the whole means and circumstances of the applicant.
