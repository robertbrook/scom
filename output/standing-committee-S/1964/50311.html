10.30 a.m.

Mr. N. R. Wylie: When the Committee adjourned on Tuesday I was making the point that the Disciplinary Committee set up by the Schedule is really similar to a court of law, and I made reference to the powers which the Committee will have under the Bill for recovering documents and for citing witnesses. In these circumstances it is altogether appropriate that the Committee ought to have power to make its own rules of procedure, but what I question here is whether it ought to have powers to frame its own rules of evidence, and that is the point of this Amendment.

The rules of evidence are really rules of law which have been evolved over a very long period of time with the object of achieving justice between the parties, and if we start interfering with the rules of evidence in a committee of this nature there is a risk at least that we may prejudice the right of parties appearing before the committee.

For example, it would be competent for the Committee, if it thought fit, to provide that hearsay evidence would be admissible. I do not know whether the Committee would ever dream of making a provision like that, but that is the kind of thing that could fall within the provision "the rules of evidence to be observed".

It would be highly prejudicial in my submission if the Committee exercised its powers in that way. It might also provide that the necessity according to the law of evidence for corroboration would no longer be required, and that Col. 354 the one witness rule, which is a familiar feature of the English law of evidence but which, with one or two statutory exceptions, is not a part of the law of Scotland, could be embodied in the rules of evidence set up by this Committee. This is the kind of thing which should not be done, and the Committee should not even have power to make decisions of that nature.

It is quite true that under paragraph 2(3) of the Schedule there is provision for the Lord President of the Court of Session approving any rules made under this Schedule, and of course there is a safeguard there, because I am quite certain that no Lord President of the Court of Session would ever confirm rules with regard to evidence which conflicted with the law of evidence which is so important in the interests of justice between the parties. To that extent it seems to me that the whole provision here—"the rules of evidence to be observed"—is unnecessary, undesirable, and should certainly be deleted from paragraph 2(1) of this Schedule.

Mr. Michael Clark Hutchison: It is quite clear from the wording of the Schedule that the intention is that the proceedings of the Disciplinary Committee should as nearly as possible resemble the proceedings in a court of law. The Committee is given power to administer oaths, to call witnesses, and require the production of documents, and it is going to have an assessor who can advise on the law. For that reason I fully support the arguments of my hon. hon. and learned Friend.

I wonder whether the hon. Lady the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland has really considered this question. Evidence is a very large and complex branch of the Col. 356 law. It has been developed over the years by Statute, by case law and the interpretations of judges. It has all the time been designed to secure that an accused person, plaintiff or defendant, has a fair trial. The rules of evidence have been designed over the years so that prejudice, recriminations, lapses of memory, lies, and all the other human failings to which mankind is subject, are taken account of.

Both in England and in Scotland the rules of evidence are in my view better than those obtaining in any other system of law, and they are certainly worth following and worth very exact study. It would be total folly to allow a lay Committee to have its own rules of evidence. They would be very amateur and would undoubtedly lead to much injustice.

Under sub-paragraph (3) such rules will be subject to the approval of the Lord President of the Court of Session. What hon. Members opposite wish us to do is to delete from the Bill the reference to the rules of evidence to be observed.

The anxiety expressed has centred largely around the need to make sure that the rules of evidence to be followed by the Disciplinary Committee will in no way be of a lower standard than that of the rules of the courts of law. I support the argument that there ought not Col. 358 to be any possibility of that, but the point which hon. Members opposite have missed is that unless those words are in the Bill it will be possible for the rules of evidence adopted by the Disciplinary Committee to be of a lower standard. This is the only safeguard which will make sure that they are not. If the words are removed there will be no provision that the Lord President of the Court of Session must approve the rules of evidence adopted.

The provisions are very much common form, and are paralleled in Section 37 (1) of the Medical Act, 1956, and in Schedule 2, paragraph 3, of the Professions Supplementary to Medicine Act, 1960. It is clear that if the Council had it in mind to set up rules of evidence which in any way fell below those applied in the courts it would quickly be brought to book by the Lord President.

It is not possible to go on the basis that the rules of evidence in the courts would automatically apply in proceedings before the Disciplinary Committee unless provision is made to link those rules with the rules approved by the Lord President of the Court of Session. If the words are left out, it will not be possible for the hon. and learned Member for Pentlands (Mr. Wylie) to assume that the rules will be those of the courts; nor can it be assumed that they will be of the same standard.

We consider it essential to include in sub-paragraph (1) a reference to the rules of evidence, and I am advised that if these words were deleted we should leave room for doubt, which I am sure is the last thing that hon. Members opposite would want. Secondly, the provision has ample precedents. Thirdly, there is the safeguard that the approval of the Lord President is necessary.

There is a further point. If no provision is made in the paragraph for the rules to be made by the Council to cover the rules of evidence—this refers to subparagraph (3)—the Council could go on the basis that it had complete discretion. It could argue that it could deal with this matter quite separately, and not according to the rules to be made by it under the paragraph. For example, it could apply as it thought fit what the hon. and learned Member referred to as the laws of evidence, without the safeguard of the Image Col. 359 approval of those rules by the Lord President.

I hope that I have said enough to indicate that—taking the objective of hon. Members opposite as being one with which the whole Committee would agree, and as one which is entirely admirable and which was in our minds when we drafted the Bill—the only way to provide the necessary safeguards is to leave these words in the Bill, so that there is no discretion on the part of the Council, and so that the Lord President can ensure that the rules made by it shall not be of a lower standard than that of those operating in the courts. What I have said answers the points raised as to hearsay evidence, the evidence of husbands and wives, and so on. All these points will be safeguarded if the words are allowed to remain in the Schedule.
