That was a Conservative Minister's reply to the hon. Gentleman. It is our reply too, for the reason that we are dealing with an area which, for reasons of geography and history, has thrown up a society with its own traditions—I was going to say "peculiarities"—and its own method of farming.

In regard to crofting, the area has become generally recognised for 40 or 50 years as the seven Highland counties. It was so recognised in the Congested Districts Act, 1897, in the Highlands and Islands Medical Services Act, as one to which special remedies could be applied, in the Highlands and Islands Shipping Services Act and in the two Crofters Acts. All this was for the very good reason that the area is generally known and recognised and has easily definable boundaries 56 which cover the areas which we generally mean by "Highlands and Islands".

Some of the problems of Arran might be similar to those of the seven crofting counties, and there is nothing in the Bill to prevent the Board, or, for that matter the Scottish Economic Planning Council or the Secretary of State, from deciding that the best way of dealing with those problems is by an order and by including the area within the provisions of the Bill. Such provision is already made in the Clause where it says,

"and such other areas in Scotland as, having regard to their character and proximity to the said counties, the Secretary of State may, by order made by statutory instrument, from time to time designate."

There is nothing to prevent that from being done if the Board, the Scottish Economic Planning Council or the Secretary of State can be persuaded that this is the way to deal with the problems of the area.

We feel that we should set up the Board to deal with the area which has been generally recognised in legislation in the past as the Highlands and Islands, and that we should give the Board a chance to find its feet and to get going. Then, in the light of its progress and experience, we can begin to see whether it would be desirable to include other areas.

The greater part of the argument of the hon. Gentleman was ill-founded and did not have a great deal to do with this point.
