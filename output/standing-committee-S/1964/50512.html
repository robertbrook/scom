Mr. MacMillan: I recognise that the hon. Member for Fife, West (Mr. William Hamilton) also has an Amendment but he had to leave the Committee temporarily just now and I want meanwhile to say a few words in support of the Amendment in my name and that of my hon. Friend who has moved it.

I think both sides of the Committee will recognise that it is impossible to escape from contact with the landlord in connection with the work of the Highland and Islands Development Board. Therefore, the landlord assumes an importance here which, of course, he has in reality in the area, quite apart from his place in the Bill. But with the Bill he now becomes a person of even greater importance in the context of all Highland development which is likely to take place or to be attempted under the new Bill and the new Board. So many of the Board's functions will require consultation with and the co-operation of the landlords and there will have to be so many references to the landlords in the course of the Board's normal work in the Highlands and Islands that it is extremely important that the landlords should be known. I think there should be no dispute about that anywhere in this Committee.

Therefore, for the convenience of the Board in carrying out its functions and for the convenience of everybody concerned, including the owners, it is better that all the landlords should be known in advance rather than that they should have to be approached one by one as problems arise and all the necessary research be undertaken in the midst of the job of dealing with every individual case where such facts are required. I do not imagine there will be any vital objection of principle in any of the parties to this information being obtained and being Col. 702 systematically recorded. I do not think there have been any objections from the landlords themselves so far to this Amendment; we have had no communications about it and I do not think there is anything on public record against something of this kind being done. Indeed, to some extent these estates are listed now in the many local valuation rolls; although it is not done systematically in the form of a register for the whole region, which would be far more convenient for reference for everybody concerned and especially for the Highlands and Islands Development Board. In the valuation rolls in each of the separate counties and burghs, information of a kind, on the subjects locally is available; but there has always been difficulty in getting information of a wider character, including acreages, in particular.

To a large extent the task suggested in our Amendment is a matter of collation of information which is already known in this inadequate form and scattered way. In the 'seventies of the last century it was not regarded as too onerous a task to prepare a register of estates, with acreages and some description of their current use. Moreover, under the Crofters Acts, a croft register now exists, so that any crofter who has to be approached is already registered. The acreage of each croft is known, the tenant is known, and generally speaking the proprietor is consequentially known as well. So there is no breach of any principle or precedent or any lack of precedent in our request. There is a clear precedent for it in part in the valuation rolls of the counties and burghs. Secondly, the crofts themselves are already registered, and indirectly the proprietors are registered too because the crofts are registered. Finally, there was the 1874 register of estates, now badly out of date.

So if objections of principle were not raised by the landlords in the 'seventies of the last century why should the landlords of today object to the register being brought up to date after this long lapse of years? I am sure they will recognise the fact that with the coming of the Highlands and Islands Development Board it is more important and worth while to have a register of this kind in 1965 than it was in the 'seventies of the Image Col. 703 last century. I cannot feel there will be any serious objection to this Amendment.

Mr. MacMillan: There are several answers and, admittedly, several doubts on that point still remaining; but I will give only two. One is that a request was made to the Government at different times over the past several years for a register of all lands and it was rejected out of hand. As to acreage, this 500-acre figure is essentially a compromise, I quite agree, but I am glad to have been asked by a Liberal why we do not go the whole hog, since they usually ask us in this party why we are going too far and not compromising.

The other point is that for a start we should be happy if we could have a list even of lands over 500 acres. Additionally, so many crofters are in fact the owner-occupiers now that it would not be a difficult task to extend the list well below that 500-acre minimum acreage. Many crofters have taken occupation since it became possible to enjoy the normal croft tenants' grants and other assistance, formerly only for tenants, in recent times. So it should be fairly easy in practice to extend the information and record to cover the lower acreages because the croft register exists and the owner-crofters are known.

However, the main point is that we have not got an up-to-date register of land ownership. As I have said we shall be involving landlords at many stages of the Highlands and Islands Development Board's work and the Board will have to go to them. First of all, therefore, the Board must be able to identify them and know with whom they are dealing; and it is better to have all this knowledge available in a systematic record in advance than to start hunting out identity of each landlord, and the other facts about each area of land on every separate occasion when this need arises. And since we have had no objections at all to this record, being made from any landlords' organizations Col. 704 of any kind outside or from any individual landlord I cannot imagine that the more progressive landlords to whom we are accustomed to address ourselves on the other side of this Committee will have any objection either. And since there was a register in the 70's of the last century I cannot imagine any landowner opposite wanting to be thought more secretive or reactionary in these respects than those landlords of the last century.

I, therefore, think that the arguments are very strong that such a register would not only assist and serve the administrative convenience and tidyness of the Board's work but would also be of use to the landlords themselves. Some time earlier this morning we were talking about entry by the Board's officials upon lands and talking about 24 hours' notice and seven days' notice to occupiers or owners. I am sure that hon. Member's opposite, including any landowners among them, would want to save the landlords and the Board's time by having this information we are asking for made up and ready for the convenience of the landlords and the Boards. If a landlord has to be consulted and to be informed quickly about some scheme, involving his estate or himself, it is much better that he should be easily known and identified and that general information and statistics about his lands kept in an up-to-date record so that he need not be bothered and questioned about this by the Board. This register would have permanent value and would probably be kept up-to-date in the same way as the croft register from year to year, or at intervals of three years or five years. In any event, it would be a tremendous convenience to the Board itself and would cause no inconvenience to the landlords. I do not think anyone would ask for such confidential information as whether the landlord pays his income tax or super tax—or, how much. The necessary information on rating and valuation is already in the valuation rolls and I do not think anyone would object to such a useful document as this register of lands which is all we are suggesting.

That is all I want to say and I hope my argument has been reasonable as well as comprehensive. I would appeal to all hon. Gentlemen to assist by supporting the idea of such a useful public record which could inconvenience no one.
