Clause 1.—(THE REGISTRAR GENERAL.)

The Chairman
: I propose, for the convenience of the Committee, that we take, with the first Amendment, Amendment No. 2, in page 1, line 17, leave out "without prejudice to that generality".

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mrs. Judith Hart)
: I beg to move, in page 1, line 17, to leave out "without prejudice to that generality" and to insert "subject to that general duty".

We have a number of Amendments, and so have hon. Gentlemen opposite. Some of the Opposition Amendments have drawn our attention to points in the Bill that needed examination. We are very grateful to hon. Gentlemen opposite for that, even though we have put down Amendments which are slightly different.

It will be clear if one looks at the subsection which is affected by the Amendment now before the Committee, that if one left out altogether the words "without prejudice to that generality" without putting something in their place one would not quite cover all the contingencies which it is necessary to cover. The "generality" referred to—I agree that it is a rather awkward word—embraces every function conferred upon the Registrar General by any enactment.

4 The effect of the inclusion of the phrase is that the Registrar General is not to avoid, negative or prejudice the exercise of his other statutory functions. In other words, in the exercise of his power in the latter part of this subsection he is still subject to the general duty set out in the first part of the subsection. In this context, the phrase might be best translated as "subject to that general duty". If the words were entirely left out, the subsection might be read as conferring unlimited discretion upon the Registrar General.

The question is to get the right sort of safeguarding phrase. The one used in the Bill is most commonly used in narrating a particular power already covered by the preceding generality, but here we confer an additional power. On further consideration, "subject to that general duty" seems advisable.

Mr. Anthony Stodart
: I have no intention of crossing swords with the Under-Secretary or of breaking the spirit of amiability with which the proceedings have opened. I am grateful to her for what she has said about our object in putting down Amendments. The present Amendment is typical, in that it is to simplify the Bill. I must confess I have a suspicion, despite the hon. Lady's persuasive arguments, that the phrase we are proposing to take out is draftsman's legal jargon. I am not convinced that there is a great deal behind the words, even those which the hon. Lady suggests should be substituted. They are better than the words which are in the Bill. I would think it not impossible to strike a blow for brevity and to leave out the words altogether, but I shall not press our Amendment on this occasion.

Amendment agreed to.

Mr. Stodart

This is a matter of no great substance, but I am all for using in legislation words which one uses oneself. "Utility" is not a word which many of us use JPEG 5 in our everyday conversation, whereas the phrase we suggest, "service and efficiency" covers what the word "utility" implies.

Mrs. Hart
: I can well understand the motives which inspire the hon. Gentleman, but in fact there is a difficulty here. It is a simple one and I think he will appreciate it. Other Acts empower the Registrar-General to do various things, but they do not empower a person acting on his behalf, so the effect of this Amendment would be to provide in this Bill something which is not provided for in other Acts of Parliament which also empower the Registrar-General—for example, the Census Acts and the Marriage Acts. I agree the object would seem to be simple, namely to shorten the Clause, but the effect would in fact be different. If the Amendment were accepted the functions of the Deputy would be in some doubt, whereas subsection (3) makes these functions absolutely clear.

Under the Amendment which the hon. Gentleman proposes the Registrar General would have to issue some authorisation to his Deputy to act on his behalf, and the Amendment does not make it clear whether the Deputy is to have a general power so to act or whether he is to have a whole series of specific powers. The Clause makes it JPEG 9 clear that the power to act is a general one, and we think there is advantage in saying so in the Clause itself. Also, to be logical one would have to amend in a similar way the Census Acts and the Marriage Acts and all the other Statutes which involve the duties of the Registrar General, and giving him the power to appoint somebody to act on his behalf.

For this reason we prefer the text of the Clause. In fact the effort made by the hon. Gentleman to shorten it would on this occasion seriously change the sense.
