Mr. William Baxter: It is not often that I am the first speaker. All the congratulations that need to be expressed were expressed at our last meeting, so may I be permitted to skip that part of the programme, other than to say that I to some extent envy and congratulate the Opposition on having the privilege and pleasure of opposing Measures which come before the Committee from time to time. That is sometimes a much more delightful exercise than being on this side and trying to put Measures through the Committee. Having said that I do not wish to weary the Committee by further congratulating anybody, nevertheless, I associate myself with the remarks which have been made.

There is one group of people who were very much criticised on Tuesday and who, in my opinion, ought to be congratulated rather than criticised. I refer to members of river purification boards. The Members of this Committee do not appreciate the excellent work that those people have done in trying to bring about that co-ordination of local authorities which was absolutely essential, nor do hon. Members appreciate the difficulty of the task which was placed upon the shoulders of those people in setting up a completely new organisation for this purpose. They had to get offices, officials, engineers' reports, and so forth. That took a considerable time. I speak with some personal knowledge, having been one of the founder members, together with the former Member for Glasgow, Pollok, Sir John George, Col. 54 of the Forth River Purification Board. I recognise the amount of work those boards did. If any blame has to be placed upon anyone's shoulders for the state of rivers today, it certainly cannot be placed upon the shoulders of members of river purification boards.

I am inclined, like my hon. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Govan (Mr. Rankin), to apportion the blame in the opposite way. The Government and the Secretary of State at that time took far too long to set up the river purification boards which were permissive under the 1951 Act. If the boards had been put into operation when the Act was passed or when the Conservative Government then came into being, I am sure that many of the problems confronting us today would not be there. The members of river purification boards deserve some credit for the work they have done.

Rivers cannot be cleared without altering the method of approach. The method of approach in the past was that every small local authority was called upon to provide sewage purification works for its own domestic sewage. This approach gives rise to duplication of work. The hon. Member for Aberdeenshire, West (Mr. Hendry) said that at present there is a considerable scarcity of qualified engineers. That to some extent adds to the problem.

I should like to know from the Under-Secretary how many schemes have been submitted for his approval by local authorities for the alteration or provision of new sewage purification works. How long did it take the previous Secretary of State to decide whether those works could proceed and the amount of grant to be made available to local authorities? With that information we shall have at least some indication of what the attitude of the former Secretaries of State has been in the past.

I believe that something more should be done than is proposed in the Bill. River purification boards should be set up in a different manner. They should become more or less independent powers, not the co-ordinating power envisaged in previous Acts and in the Bill. If the problem is to be solved, there must be some overriding authority so as to bring about the co-ordination of sewage purification works which is necessary and also bring industrial effluent into the works Image Col. 55 provided by the co-ordinating authority. I cannot see how it will be able to solve the problem unless that is done.

One of the great problems confronting local authorities and river purification boards at present is how to get co-ordination between various local authorities. I can illustrate this very well in relation to the River Forth. My right hon. Friend the Member for East Stirlingshire (Mr. Woodburn) complained about certain effluent flowing into the tidal waters of the Forth from the upper reaches of the River Carron and the River Forth, and so on. This problem is due primarily to the fact that there is no power to compel co-operation between local authorities. On the Carron there is Denny. One comes down to Bonny-bridge. There is the whole of the Bonny-bridge area. Then one comes down to Falkirk, Larbert, and Stenhousemuir, all along the river. They are all more or less separate authorities, but a degree of co-operation certainly exists. On the River Forth one goes up to the Bridge of Allan. One then comes down to Stirling, Alloa and Bannockburn. Then one comes right down to Grangemouth. Each local authority naturally considers the problem from its own point of view. This will continue under the Bill. This is a fundamental mistake when trying to legislate for the purification of rivers.

There is a problem in relation to the domestic effluent going into rivers which has not been solved. It can be solved to a certain extent, but it is being added to all the time. I refer to the soap products of the various soap manufacturers. This in itself causes a very great problem in river purification.

The problem of industrial effluent is almost insurmountable for the paper industry. A great deal of money and effort have been expended on trying to get the effluent from paper mills down to reasonable proportions, but it is difficult to evolve a system by which the soda used in the purification and washing of paper can be wholly extracted. This is a problem of some magnitude to an area such as Denny. The paper mills there were established many years ago. The hon. Member for Glasgow, Hillhead (Mr. Galbraith) suggested that the sewerage pipe from the paper mill of an industrial unit should be taken three miles Col. 56 out into the tidal waters of the river, but that would involve a considerable cost, a cost which could not be borne by the industry. It would mean the stoppage of this industry which is very important to Stirlingshire and the surrounding area of Scotland.

I speak with some experience, because before I entered the House we in the local authority considered this problem very carefully in co-operation with the paper manufacturers of Denny. We found that to take a pipe from the Denny paperworks down to the tidal waters of the Forth, which is not going three miles out into the sea, would at that time have cost £1¼ million. That was not the only problem confronting the local authority and the paper industry. Once one started piping water which had gone through the process of purifying the paper it would have left the Carron absolutely dry. The water which flows in a river such as the Carron is compensation water coming from the Carron Reservoir. That reservoir, as the former Secretary of State must know, plays a very important part in the development of the new town of Cumbernauld. Regard must be paid to the many circumstances and difficulties inherent in trying to operate a Bill such as this.

It would be far better for Scotland as a whole if some contamination still took place in certain of our rivers, if this kept in being the industry upon which we all so much depend. One must be reasonable and considerate when trying to introduce a Measure such as this. That is why I concur with the point of view which has been expressed about Clause 1(4), regarding the words in the previous Act that "consent shall not be unreasonably withheld". That should not be omitted from the contents of the Bill. I do not think that what was said by the Government spokesman provides a satisfactory answer. I counsel the Government to reconsider this problem. It is easy merely to put into operation the strict letter of the law. I want to know whether the Secretary of State, to whom appeals can be made, has power to alter the law, because I am sure that the opinion of every hon. Member is that the law is made to be upheld by members of the Government and by members of the public. Therefore, the law as we lay it down would have to be interpreted by any Secretary of State and he would Image Col. 57 not have room to manœeuvre if the provision were omitted from the Bill.

I am not happy about the Measure. I do not think that this is the proper way to try to solve this very difficult problem for Scotland. It has not solved it in the past. I do not think that it will solve it in the future. The only way to solve the problem is to have powers vested in a co-ordinating body such as a rivers purification board for the catchment area of, say, the Forth. Such a board should have full executive powers to make decisions on sewage purification both domestically and industrially.

The hon. Member for North Angus and Nearns (Mr. Buchanan-Smith) referred to a very important section of the community, the farming community. Farmers may have some difficulty in meeting the requirements of the Bill. We must have regard to these difficulties, not only as farmers but as members of the community. None of us knows enough to be able to say that the nature of some of our fertilisers is such that, after filtering through the soil, they will do no harm to man or beast.

We use fertilisers extensively, and, if we did not, the productivity of our farms would be greatly retarded. So we are between the devil and the deep sea. We use the fertilisers and they ultimately percolate through the soil into the rivers and streams, bringing about a contamination which no one at this stage can definitely say has or has not a detrimental effect upon fish. Therefore, I counsel the Government to be most careful about what they propose in the Bill in this regard.
