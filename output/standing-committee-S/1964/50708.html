It will be within the recollection of the Committee that on 17th March and 19th March, 1964, this Committee discussed, at some length, the contents of the Brunton Report, which is very relevant to our discussion this morning. It is not my intention to follow his example, much as I respect his views in other matters. I do not intend to introduce party politics into this discussion at all, because I believe that the introduction of party politics into educational matters makes very little contribution to educational progress.

In the view of myself, and most people, the record of the past years has been one of steady progress. In support of that, I would instance the comment made in the impartial and soberly-worded report of the Scottish Education Committee for 1964. This has a very definite effect upon the setting for the 166 raising of the school-leaving age. In the year 1964 there were major developments in Scottish education which contributed towards that setting. If I may stray for a brief sentence outside the limits of this discussion, the major development was the introduction of a new university in Stirling, in my home town. I hope that I may, on behalf of the whole Committee, add our very best wishes to the newly-appointed principal, Professor Cottrell. I know that the hon. Member for Stirling and Falkirk Burghs (Mr. Malcolm MacPherson) would wish to subscribe to these good wishes and we in turn would like to send him our good wishes.

The new university, the establishment of a General Teaching Council, and the announcement of the forthcoming raising of the school leaving age, to be effective in 1970, were probably the three most important advances in Scottish education for a long time. We have to consider this morning what the immediate requirements are for the raising of the school-leaving age. We must first of all have adequate buildings. Secondly, the curricula should be planned to ensure that the maximum value from the extra year is gained by both the pupil and the nation, because the pupil's contribution is ultimately one which the nation can ill afford to lose. There is, finally, the problem of teacher shortage, and the consideration of such measures as have already been taken, or may be required, in order to get a greater number of teachers.

Most of us who are educational enthusiasts to some extent look forward to progress, such as the raising of the school-leaving age, and accept it because it is necessary and important. There are, I think, some doubts in the country as a whole as to the wisdom of this step. Criticism will be made, either now or at a later stage, by teachers upon whom a heavy burden will be thrown, as shortages will continue, and by pupils to whom the lure of a wage packet is very great, despite the fact that the wage packet may remain for many years at the same level. Parents also do not always appreciate, as much as we should like them to, the advantages to be gained from their children remaining at school. We cannot dismiss a hungry labour market which is already in existence in many parts of England, and which we sincerely JPEG 167 hope will be in existence in Scotland in the very near future.

The Brunton Report places a very wise emphasis on the need for raising the school-leaving age. I will quote from paragraph 21 on page 12 of that Report which says:

"No matter how well the schools may perform their task, boys and girls who leave school at the age of 15 are not fully-developed, intellectually, emotionally, socially or physically; nor can their further development be safely left to chance … It is important that their education should continue on a broad front."

The argument leading up to that is, in my view, very cogent and wise. The success of implementing the raising of the school-leaving age depends upon the fulfilment of the requirements which I have instanced. There is first the question of adequate buildings. It is true that the S.E.D. 1964 Report speaks of the good progress in this respect. A total of £43 million has been spent on Scottish schools last year, and this is a very considerable sum of money. This sum has increased steadily over the years. The result has been that we have more than half the children in Scotland today studying in schools built during the post-war period. That is a tremendous achievement and one which is frequently under-estimated, but it is not enough because there is an additional point in relation to school buildings.

If additional education is to be successful, then it is important to remember that classes must be smaller and certainly not larger. We are committed to a policy whereby we are going to keep a greater number of children a further year at school. I had personal experience of the last raising of the school age, at a local authority level, where, despite a very remarkable degree of foresight and effort on the part of the director of education and others, the difficulties were very great indeed. I see some of those difficulties only too clearly, but I do not want to go into them in detail at this stage. I hope that such preparations as we are now making will avoid at least a certain number of such difficulties. In our building programme we should remember the need for smaller classes and work towards that end. This is something which is often left till last on a long list of so-called priorities.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mrs. Judith Hart)
: First, I congratulate the hon. Lady the Member for Renfrew, East (Miss Harvie Anderson). We have all been delighted to see her on the Front Bench this morning. I hope that we shall have other opportunities of doing so. I welcomed the tone of her speech, up to the last few words, and gladly respond to it. I shall try hard not to respond to the tone in her last paragraph, although I was ready, in case she should emerge with a highly party political speech. I am glad that she has not, because this is a subject on which there is a great deal to say. We can have party politics, too, but first let us discuss what is being done and what needs to be done. I very much welcome the opportunity of giving what will, in effect, be a sort of stocktaking report. I shall have something to say about most of the points which the hon. Lady raised.

First, I propose to say something about the general approach to the problems involved in raising the school-leaving age. Then I want to deal with an aspect of fundamental concern—the problem of the supply of teachers—and then to look at the contents of education in the secondary period and the reorganisation of secondary education. Lastly, I shall deal with the problems of accommodation which will be involved in meeting the needs of the children who will be staying on in 1970.

It has always been part of our thinking that the period of compulsory education should be extended to the age of 16 at the earliest practicable date. We have always regarded it as right that our young people should have the fullest opportunity of taking their secondary education as far as possible. We could not be satisfied with a system in which a secondary education lasting four years or more was the right only of a relatively small number of children, often—though I emphasise not always—socially selected, 174 in the sense that in our society we tend to find that there is a close relationship between opportunity and social and economic background. We have always believed that an extension of the period of compulsory education was very much in the country's interest. Clearly it is. Every step forward our country has ever taken technologically and socially has been marked by an extension of the period of compulsory education.

I do not know that I would necessarily say that 1970–71 is the ideal time to set for this change. Obviously there will be a lot of difficulties. But, unless a date is set for a reform of this kind, it is all too easy to allow difficulties to postpone action. So we are basing our planning, clearly, on the raising of the age in the session 1970–71. I would emphasise one thing, and that is that we need to keep the change in the leaving age in perspective, and we need to see it in terms that are not too dramatic, because children very often tend to stay on at school beyond the present leaving age, and we expect this to develop further.

In 1955, over the country as a whole, only 10·3 per cent. of children attending public schools who three years earlier were aged 13 were still at school. By 1964, that proportion had risen to 19·4 per cent.—almost double. But although the effect of this trend by 1970 will be to make the raising of the leaving age a somewhat less clear-cut and dramatic change than might have been the case, it is obvious that we are here dealing with a fundamental issue, and it is one which presents a number of major difficulties.

As a Government, it is our responsibility to put the education authorities in a position, so far as it is humanly possible, to meet the 1970 situation. The responsibilities they have are very heavy, in gearing up the schools to provide the right kinds of courses in secondary education, in making more school accommodation available, and in trying to bring about what the hon. Lady the Member for Renfrew, East referred to as the most effective deployment of the teaching staffs that they have. The whole problem must be approached as one demanding the very fullest co-operative effort, and in this I believe that the teachers' organisations have a very substantial part to play. The JPEG 175 education service has some five years in which to carry its preparations forward. This may seen a fairly long period, but it is quite clear that it is going to allow little enough time for all that will have to be done, and if we are to be successful, all those who have some part of the responsibility for the education service will have to be ready to adopt an approach which is both flexible and imaginative, and we shall need to use all our resources of ingenuity in making sure we make the best use of accommodation and facilities.

It was estimated last year that in Scotland as a whole the number of pupils who, but for the raising of the leaving age, would have ceased full-time education and for whom, therefore, additional facilities would have to be provided by 1970–71 would be about 52,000. This was based on the expectation that the age group that is first involved would total some 82,000, that some 20,000 would in any case be remaining at school because they wanted to, and that about 10,000 would be moving into full-time further education at the age of 15. Over and above these, there would be an increasing number of pupils who, as a result of the raising of the leaving age, would stay on at school voluntarily beyond the age of 16.

Education authorities were asked a year ago to make a study of the additional number of pupils for whom they would need to provide, and in April of this year most of the authorities had submitted their estimates of the numbers concerned. Obviously the estimates are slightly provisional at this stage but, on the whole, they bear out the estimates made last year, except perhaps that for a variety of reasons the use that we can expect to be made of further education may be rather less than was thought last year. There are a number of demands in other directions on the facilities of further education.

Having sketched in the background and our general approach, I turn now to the problem of teacher supply. What is the position likely to be in 1970–71, when the leaving age is raised? My right hon. Friend and I have given a good many figures bearing on this on previous occasions, but it is important that the position should be clearly understood. In October last it was estimated by the 176 education authorities that they needed some 3,600 teachers to fill vacancies, to replace uncertificated teachers and retired teachers over the age of 70. On the forecasts made by the Robbins Committee concerning the increasing numbers likely to be coming into higher education, the position is likely to improve over the next few years. But, with the raising of the leaving age in 1970–71, we are likely to be short of some 4,000 to 5,000 teachers in that year. In other words, we can expect to have a shortage rather greater than the one we have this session.

These figures are on the basis of the maximum class sizes as they are at present prescribed. As a Government, we do not regard it is exceptional that we should for any indefinite period postpone a reduction of these prescribed maxima. This will mean that, as soon as possible, we want even more teachers to reduce the class size, as well as raising the school leaving age in 1970–71. Obviously we shall have to be governed by how successful we are in attracting teachers.

There is no one single solution to the problem of teacher supply. What we have to do is to take every practicable step to stimulate recruitment to teaching still further. Let us look at this, as it were, in categories. The number of graduates admitted to teacher training in recent years has been more than 1,000 a year. We shall try to do even better than this. But, although the numbers of graduates entering teacher training are likely to increase as higher education expands, it is doubtful whether we can reasonably expect a significant increase in the proportion of the total output of graduates who come into teaching. As the hon. Lady herself pointed out, at the moment a little over 50 per cent. of the output of the Scottish universities in arts and pure science already come into teaching. We have to recognise that there are a number of other legitimate claims in industry and other professions with which we have to reckon.

The simple fact is that not every one wants to teach, and we just have to accept that that is so. But we have been stepping up our efforts to persuade far more graduates to choose teaching. Our teams have been in the universities, alongside industrial firms, doing a very persuasive job. In so far as it is possible to attract more graduates into teaching, JPEG 177 we shall spare no effort to do so, but we should not imagine that these efforts will result in a situation which will make any appreciable contribution to solving the teacher shortage in 1970–71.
