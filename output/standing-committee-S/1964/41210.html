Mr. Hendry: I am obliged, Mr. Steele, for your suggestion to discuss these Amendments together. That is not only for the convenience of the Committee, but essential for a proper understanding of the intention of the various Amendments. At first glance, they look like drafting Amendments, but, as I shall show, they are much more than that and are of considerable substance. As they would bring about considerable amendment of the principal Act as well as of the Bill I hope I may be forgiven if I describe the penalty provisions of the principal Act, otherwise it is almost impossible to understand what I mean.

Col. 54 Under the principal Act of 1951, there are roughly three classes of offence: offences against Section 22, offences under Section 28 and a third type of offence against byelaws. The question of offences against byelaws is not relevant to my argument and I propose to say no more about it.

There is, however, a great difference in quality between offences under Section 22 and those under Section 28. The former are what I describe as being in the nature of continuing offences. The principal Act refers, for example, to a person who

"causes or knowingly permits to enter a stream any poisonous, noxious or polluting matter".

That is something which a person might not only do, but continue to do for a considerable time. Obviously, if a person does it for a considerable time after being warned about the offence, he is aggravating his original offence. It seems right and proper that in such circumstances the penalty should become greater the longer the offence is committed.

On the other hand, an offence under Section 28 is a once and for all offence and consists of bringing a new effluent into operation. Section 28 of the principal Act commences by stating:

"Subject to this section, no person shall without the consent of the river purification authority (which consent shall not be unreasonably withheld) bring into use any new or altered outlet for the discharge of trade or sewage effluent to a stream".

As hon. Members will appreciate, something can be brought into operation only once. It might be done again at a subsequent date, but it is not a continuing offence. It is a once and for all offence and once a person has been convicted of it, he has tholed his assize and cannot be charged with repeating it. It is not a continuing offence like offences under Section 22.

In the Bill we find that a new offence is committed under Clause 1. It is declared to be unlawful after a certain date

"to make a discharge of trade or sewage effluent to a stream".

This is not a new event; something which has been in effect for some time is being continued. It may be continued for some years. This is in the nature of Image Col. 55 a continuing offence analogous to offences under Section 22 of the principal Act. So that if a person has a discharge of old standing and he is refused consent by the river purification authority to continue that discharge he is aggravating the offence the longer he carries on doing it. Thus, this is not a once and for all offence such as an offence under Section 28 of the principal Act.

Whichever Minister is responsible has treated an offence under the Bill in exactly the same way as an offence under Section 28 of the original Act. The proposal of subsection (7) is simple and straightforward. It states that

"A person who contravenes subsection (1) of this section shall be liable—(a) on conviction on indictment, to a fine not exceeding £500, or (b) on summary conviction, to a fine not exceeding £100."

There it stops. The Clause contains no provision for an increased penalty on continuation of the offence which would form an aggravation of the offence and which would appear to deserve an increased penalty the longer it continues.

If a person commits an offence under the Clause and has been convicted and fined £500, or whatever is the appropriate figure, he has tholed his assize and it seems that he can go on gaily committing the offence without fear of further prosecution. It seems to me that that is quite wrong and that the Bill would be much strengthened if this offence were not dealt with in this way but declared to be an offence under Section 22 of the principal Act. That is the effect of the first of my Amendments. The Under-Secretary of State will, I think, agree that it would strengthen the Bill, because it would strengthen the hands of the river purification authority in dealing with an offender who was refractory and continued the offence.

My proposal, therefore, is that such an offence should not be dealt with as a single offence but should be brought within Section 22 of the principal Act, so that if the person committing the offence continues to do so he will come under the continuing penalty provisions of Section 22 of the principal Act and the longer he does it, the greater will be the penalty.

I do not quarrel with the amount of penalty which is proposed. A serious Col. 56 offence might be committed under the Bill and in certain circumstances a serious penalty would be appropriate. Indeed, in corresponding English legislation there is no limit to the fine which may be imposed. I do not agree with that, and I think that the Under-Secretary is right to stipulate an upper limit. I have no quarrel with his fixing it at £500 for a serious offence. In case anyone has doubt about the appropriateness of that amount, I point out that the penalty of £500 would apply only in a case sufficiently serious to be tried on indictment. There must be a tremendous variety in the seriousness of these offences from, say, effluent from a simple domestic septic tank, which would be a comparatively minor offence, to a heinous offence which might call for the extreme penalty of £500.

Where I quarrel with the Under-Secretary is the method by which he amends the previous legislation. I have pointed out that under the principal Act two separate types of offence are dealt with under Sections 22 and 28. In producing the Bill, however, the hon. Gentleman has made it extremely difficult, not only for lawyers but for river purification authorities, to see what the law is. He does not make it clear at first glance that he is materially increasing the penalties for offences under the original Act. I have no quarrel with subsection (7) of the Clause, in which the penalty is increased to £500, but my immediate reaction was whether the penalty for an offence under the principal Act was being correspondingly increased. It took me a long time to find out, because the penalties under the original Act are being increased in two places.

First, in Clause 7(1) the Under-Secretary proposes to increase the penalty under Section 28 of the principal Act to exactly the same level as in Clause 1(7). I then wondered what the hon. Gentleman was doing about Section 22 of the principal Act and, again, I had to go to great trouble to find out. It took me a long time to discover that he is dealing with Section 22 in Clause 9(1). From the viewpoint of everybody who administers the Bill, whether a river purification authority, a lawyer or a judge, I do not regard it as fair that a person should be Image Col. 57 burdened with the necessity of looking at different Clauses of the same Measure to find exactly the same thing applying to previous legislation.

I suggest to the Under-Secretary that it would be convenient, not only to himself, but to all concerned, if he could bring all these increases in penalty into one Clause in the Bill, so that anybody reading it could see the penalty position at a glance without searching through the whole of a fairly major, long and difficult Bill. For that reason, my Amendments suggest the striking out of Clause 7(1) and Clause 9(1) and the incorporation of those two subsections in Clause 1(7) in the way I have suggested. If this were done, the Bill would be much clearer. I am not criticising the drafting. I am simply making a constructive suggestion to make the Bill much more convenient to all concerned.

One other point in the various Amendments which is rather difficult to follow is my substitution of a series of figures for the figures in the principal Act. My reason for this is that the continuing penalties in the principal Act have not been increased in the same proportion as the original penalties. If the penalties for the original offence are being increased from, say, £200 to £500, in my opinion it would be right and proper that the continuing penalties—for aggravation of offences—should be increased in approximately the same proportion. I have discussed this with the Under-Secretary and I believe that he appreciates the point.

Having explained the Amendments, I suggest that, complicated though they seem, they would be for the convenience of those who administer the Bill and would strengthen the hands of river purification authorities in putting the Bill into operation.
