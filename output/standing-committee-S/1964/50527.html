[Mr. JAMES MCINNES in the Chair]

REGISTRATION OF BIRTHS,

DEATHS AND MARRIAGES

(SCOTLAND) BILL [Lords]

OFFICIAL REPORT

10.30 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mrs. Judith Hart)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Registration of Births, Deaths and Marriages (Scotland) Bill [Lords] has been considered in relation to the principle of the Bill.

 Perhaps I might begin by sketching in briefly the history of earlier legislation on this subject. Until 1855, as the Committee will know, Scotland relied for its information about births, marriages and deaths entirely upon the Church. For many centuries, it was the Church's records which were the only ones available. Compulsory registration by civil registrar began in Scotland on 1st January, 1855. Before that, registers had been kept by the Church for many centuries.

The Registration, of Births, Deaths and Marriages (Scotland) Act, 1854, is still the principal Act. It established for the first time compulsory registration by a civil registrar. When it was introduced by Lord Elcho on 29th March, 1854, he said that it would be unnecessary for him to point out how essential it was that

"a country, claiming to rank among the civilised nations of the globe, should be in possession of an accurate register of the births, deaths and marriages of its people".

 He explained that the Government had brought in the Bill in response to representations from various bodies in Scotland in favour of better service to the public and of improved statistics for scientific purposes. He concluded that

"while the rich have their title deeds, their parchments and their sculptured monuments, there was literally no record of the poor man's birth or death except the parish register, which might not inaptly be called the charter of the poor man".

 6 He trusted, therefore, that the Bill would be accepted, as it was, without a dissenting voice.

This 1854 Act established a Registrar General's Office in Edinburgh and a body of local registrars appointed by parish councils and placed under the supervision of the sheriff in certain matters. The Act laid down in considerable detail how the work of registration was to be carried out but also provided for the discharge of the duties of registrars being prescribed in fuller detail by regulations. Ever since then, this has been the Act governing all these matters, and it has stood the test of time remarkably well. But there are now six amending Acts and numerous amending provisions in other Acts. The registration law in Scotland is thus now in need of modernisation. Not only is some of its language old-fashioned and some of its provisions difficult to understand; some of its procedures and requirements are out of date or are expressed in terms which prevent desirable improvements being made both in general efficiency and in services to the public.

This Bill, is, in effect, a Measure of law reform, much more than a consolidation Bill. It is a rewriting of all that is worth keeping in the present law, together with improvements inserted at convenient places, to meet current and future needs. Hon. Members who have in the past quite rightly expressed themselves as wanting legislation which said what it meant and which did not refer to many other Acts of Parliament have what they want before them in this Bill. It is an extremely clear piece of legislation. It rewrites in modern language the old legislation which we want to keep, and it inserts the new improvements which we want to make.

It began as long ago as the nineteen-twenties when, I am told, the distinguished Registrar General of the day, Dr. Crawford Dunlop, opened a file for suggestions for improving the Registration (Scotland) Acts, and since then suggestions have been accumulated by his successors in the light of experience over the years. The present Registrar General received authority from the Secretary of State four years ago to undertake a comprehensive review of the registration law, JPEG 7 in consultation with interested outside bodies, and the present Bill is the result.

It would be unusual to find that complete agreement with the bodies consulted had been achieved at every point, but I am happy to assure the Committee that there has been a very considerable measure of agreement on the main proposals now embodied in the Bill, and I am confident that it will be generally welcomed. It has come to the House of Commons from another place, where it received lengthy and careful scrutiny, and numerous Amendments both of drafting and of substance were made, without the need for a Division. The Bill is thus one to which a good deal of hard thinking has already been given. But registration affects every one of us individually at some time or another, and there may be other suggestions coming from this Committee.

What we want to do is to give the Registrar-General, the local authorities, and also the public, a new code which defines the functions of the local registrars and the Registrar-General clearly, but not so tightly that details cannot be adjusted to changing circumstances from time to time without the need for a new Bill. I say this because we are anxious that the basis of the Bill should be understood.

I should add that there is no corresponding English Bill, as the English Registration Acts were consolidated in 1953 and further changes have to proceed in England by way of amending legislation. The two countries differ in this, but I am assured that the contents of this Scottish Bill will not in any way cause embarrassment in England.

I come now to the main aspects of the Bill itself. It has five Parts. Part I is concerned with the organisation of the registration service, centrally and locally. There will continue to be a Registrar-General with his headquarters in the New Register House in Edinburgh, and a network of local registrars, the functions of all being laid down in the Bill. The Bill makes a few changes in the central organisation and rather more in the local organisation, but leans to the side of caution in both.

Under the original Act of 1854 the control of the local registration service was shared by the Registrar-General, the 8 parish council and the sheriff. The broad distribution of control is that the local authorities appoint the local registrars and their staffs and arrange for their accommodation and remuneration, the sheriffs having certain administrative duties in regard to the dismissal of registrars and the determination of the registration districts and their boundaries. The local registrars carry out their registration duties, however, under the Statutes and Regulations, supplemented by instructions or advice from the Registrar-General.

The object of the Bill is to simplify and clarify these arrangements. County councils and councils of large burghs have replaced parish councils for purposes of the local registration service; there is no longer a need for the sheriffs to exercise the administrative functions given to them under the 1854 Act. What is now proposed is that the Registrar General and the present local authorities will share control of the local registration service. The local registration authorities will appoint the registrars and their staffs and will determine their remuneration and conditions of service. The functions of the registrars will continue to be laid down by statute but, in exercising these functions they will be subject to such instructions or directions as the Registrar General may give. The local registration authorities will also have power to fix the hours of business of the registration offices and, after consultation with the Registrar General and subject to the approval of the Secretary of State, to alter the boundaries of registration districts. Any proposed changes will be advertised and there will be machinery for the consideration of objections. These arrangements have been incorporated in the Bill after consultation with the local authority associations, the sheriffs and the Association of Registrars of Scotland.

Part I also contains several provisions which are of special and urgent importance. Because of the division of administrative responsibility under the existing Acts, it is not entirely clear who the employers of the registrars are, and this has caused difficulties for the Association of Registrars from time to time. A definite statement on this is much overdue, and Clause 7 of the Bill makes clear that the registrar is to be deemed to be an employee of the local registration authority by whom he is appointed.

JPEG 9 Then, both the Registrar-General and the local registrars are having accommodation problems for their registers, which have grown in bulk with the passage of time. The annual intake of registers in the New Register House needs 110 linear feet of shelving and the shelves will be full probably within five years. It is proposed to microfilm the registers as they come in from the local registrars, deposit the originals in a proper repository elsewhere, and retain the microfilms for search and extract. These microfilms will take up only three linear feet annually instead of 110. The Registrar General cannot make these necessary and sensible changes under the existing law, and the new Bill will give him the power he needs.

The Bill also gives local registration authorities power to set up repositories for the custody of the older registers held by the local registrars in order to relieve storage difficulties in the local registration offices. At present, the local registrar has no power to deposit his registers elsewhere than in his own office. The great majority of the 900 registrars are part-time officers, and many of them carry out their duties in their own homes. The practical problems are obvious.

Part II of the Bill, comprising Clauses 13 to 21, sets out the provisions for registration of the birth of children born in Scotland. The existing provisions have, generally speaking, been retained, but there are several new features, and I should like, if I may, to elaborate on some of these because they are important and will, I believe, be greatly welcomed throughout Scotland and, in some cases, by Scots men and women in other parts of the world.

When the Act of 1854 was prepared, it was the usual custom for a mother to have her child at home, and, as a consequence, the provision under that Act by which registration was made in the district in which the birth occurred was a very convenient arrangement, because in almost every case registration was made in the district in which the mother resided. Nowadays the majority of births take place in maternity hospitals, some of which serve a wide area, and it frequently happens that a parent is put to the inconvenience of having to make a special journey—sometimes quite a long one—from his home to the 10 registration office for the district in which the maternity hospital is situated in order to have the birth registered.

Under Clause 13, a more convenient provision has been made. In the case of any birth occurring in Scotland and where the mother resides in Scotland, registration may be made either in the district in which the birth occurred or in the district in which the mother resides, whichever is more convenient. Thus, if a woman resident in Haddington has her baby in an Edinburgh hospital or anywhere in Edinburgh, the birth may be registered in either Haddington or Edinburgh.

: The hon. Lady is speaking of my constituency. It is certainly so, but in the event of a woman living in Berwickshire having her child in Berwick-on-Tweed, does the same apply?

Mrs. Hart
: As the right hon. Gentleman will appreciate, that is a very complex question and, if I may, I should like to deal with it either later this morning or at a further stage. It is a somewhat involved difficulty. In fact, the short anwer, is "No", but a "No" without the reasons is rather inadequate. I want to give the reasons too. For those who have a baby in a place different from the place where they live in Scotland, the Bill will apply generally as I have explained.

I now turn to Clause 15, which deals with foundling children. The main object of this Clause is to place responsibility for providing particulars of foundling children firmly on the shoulders of the local children's officer, who is the best person to obtain the necessary information. The provision in this Clause which the Committee will like most, I think, is that which enables a foundling child to be registered in the same way as any other. Under existing arrangements, the birth entry of a foundling does not show the date of birth but it shows instead that the child was found on a certain date. It is proposed that, in future, if a medical certificate showing the approximate date of birth is produced at the time of registration, that date may be entered as its date of birth. The Committee will appreciate what a difference this will make for foundling children who, at the moment, have a different sort JPEG 11 of birth certificate with a rather different type of entry. Now like everybody else they will have a birthday and a birth certificate which does not differentiate them in any way from other children.

10.45 a.m.

One of the objects of Clause 17, which deals with delayed or neglected registrations, is to meet the difficulties of adults who find that their births have never been registered at all. It is a very, very rare event indeed for a birth to escape registration nowadays, but fifty years ago this did occasionally happen, and there are people in Scotland today who are unable to get a birth extract because no one is available to supply the particulars concerning their birth. Under Clause 17 they will be enabled to act as their own informants, provided the Registrar General is satisfied that the correct particulars concerning the birth are available.

The object of Clause 18 is to improve the facilities for. recording the father's name in the case of an illegitimate child. Under the present Acts the name of the person acknowledging paternity of an illegitimate child cannot be entered in the register unless he attends personally with the mother and signs the register with her. This sometimes means that although a father wishes his child to be registered in his surname this cannot be done because of his inability to attend at the Registrar's Office. He may, for example, be a Service man serving overseas. In future it will be sufficient if the mother attends on her own, and produces a statutory declaration made by the person acknowledging paternity, and at the same time herself makes a declaration before the registrar. It is also provided that the father's name may be entered at a later stage if he makes a statutory declaration acknowledging paternity within 12 months of the date of birth of the child the Committee again will appreciate a valuable little reform.

Clause 19 provides for the issue, free of charge, to each informant of a birth an abbreviated certificate of the birth, containing particulars of name, surname, sex, date of birth and place of birth. The short form of birth certificate was first introduced under the Registration of Births, Etc. (Amendment) Act, 1934. Its 12 two chief attractions were its cheapness, and its omission of particulars concerning parentage, an omission which saved considerable embarrassment in the case of illegitimate children. Because of these attractions the abbreviated certificate has become quite popular, and it is now accepted for almost every purpose. There is still, however, a lingering suspicion that anyone producing the short certificate has something to conceal, and consequently the main object of the certificate, which was to help illegitimate persons, has been only partly successful.

It is now proposed to issue the abbreviated certificate free of charge instead of the full extract issued at present, and it is hoped that as a result of the short form will soon come to be accepted as the normal type of certificate to be produced by everybody. Full extracts will, of course, still be obtainable on payment of the appropriate fee. This will help the objects behind this amendment to the Act.

Part III of the Bill comprises Clauses 22 to 28 and relates to the registration of deaths. I have already mentioned that a new system for deaths, similar to that in relation to births, is proposed. At the present time registration of a death must be effected in the district in which the death occurred, and this arrangement frequently involves bereaved relatives in considerable inconvenience at a time when all should be done to spare them from trouble of any kind. Clause 22, therefore, provides that registration may be made either in the district in which the death occurred, or in which the deceased person had his usual residence.

Apart from this, there is little that is new in Part III, but I should mention that Clause 24 provides that different forms of certificate of cause of death may be prescribed for persons of different ages, and that in the case of a child under one year of age such forms may provide for the giving of particulars of the condition of the mother before the death of the child.

The certificate of cause of death is an essential feature in death registration, and on it is based the compilation of medical statistics used on a wide scale nationally and internationally. The provision whereby different forms of certificate may be prescribed will enable JPEG 13 more information to be obtained concerning infant deaths and will permit much more detailed research to be carried out in this field, a most important one in the study of the factors involved in perinatal and maternal mortality. It is in fact allowing information to be provided in this particular kind of death which medical researchers are anxious to have.

Part IV deals with the registration of marriages, but of ecclesiastical marriages only. It is not among the objects of the present Bill to make any change in the law of marriage or the preliminaries to marriage. The registration of a civil marriage contracted before an authorised registrar will continue in accordance with the provisions of the Marriage (Scotland) Act, 1939. The registration of ecclesiastical marriages, however, happens to be provided for in the Registration (Scotland) Act, 1854, as amended from time to time. It would be inconvenient to leave any part of the 1854 Act outstanding—we want to repeal it all—and we have, therefore, consolidated all the existing provisions for the registration of ecclesiastical marriages with minor amendments in Part IV of the present Bill.

Part V deals with a variety of matters and I shall touch only on those that are new and of special interest. First of all, Clauses 32 to 41 rewrite the existing provisions for preserving, checking and indexing registers, for searching indexes, and for the issue of extracts from the registers—but setting out the procedures with sufficient flexibility to allow scope for new technical processes now and in the foreseeable future.

As will be seen, use is to be made of photographic and other methods or reproduction. At the moment, when going to a registrar's office in Scotland to register an event it is necessary to wait until he has transcribed the particulars into two registers, and then both have to be signed. The reason for this is that he has to send one copy to the Registrar General and retain the other one for use in his own office. Both are principal copies. Under the Bill, however, only one register will be completed when the informant calls. A duplicate will be made later, either by the registrar himself or, when suitable arrangements are made, by the Registrar General by means 14 of photographic or other methods. One result of this will be that informants will not have so long to wait at registration offices and the flow of work at busy periods in the large offices will be speeded up. The Registrar General will also be given powers to use photographic or other methods of reproduction when issuing extracts where this is possible and desirable. If a photographich extract is issued it will be authenticated in the same way as one written by hand or reproduced by typewriter.

Clause 42 simplifies the procedure for the correction of errors.

Clause 43 contains new proposals for recording changes of name and surname already made in accordance with the common law of Scotland and in use for at least two years. There is nothing in Scotland exactly corresponding to the deed, poll procedure in England and Wales, and for many years the Registrar General has received requests for a convenient method of recording such changes. It occasionally happens, for example, that a young man or a young woman is applying for entry into higher education or employment and is faced with the fact that he has been brought up with a Christian name or a surname different from that in his birth registration. At present it is possible to get documentary evidence of a change of this kind by means of a statutory declaration or a deed recorded in the Books of Council and Session or to obtain recognition of the change from the Lyon King of Arms. What people want, however, is to have a change recorded in a birth extract rather than in a separate document, and this is what the Bill will provide.

The original entry in the register will not be altered, but when an extract is obtained the change or changes would be shown in addition to the original name or surname.

I should add that this is not an entirely new departure. The 1854 Act provided that a name given in baptism after registration in another name may be registered; and also that a name changed without baptism might be registered where the parents or guardians did not recognise baptism. This applies, however, only to a change of name and not to a change of surname; but it is a useful provision and is continued in the Bill.

JPEG 15 Then there are three Clauses, Clauses 45 to 47, which deal with the old parish registers. There are 4,000 volumes of these in the custody of the Registrar General in the New Register House. The oldest register, for the parish of Errol in Perthshire, dates back to 1553. These old registers up to the year 1854 were all gathered in during the latter half of last century. This was a statutory requirement of great wisdom, for otherwise many might have been lost or damaged. They form one of our national treasures, and are in regular use every day in the Library in the New Register House for genealogical and biographical research in association with the statutory registers since 1855.

It is designed to bring up to date the system of keeping records which are quite essential, and it is vital that these records are kept with complete accuracy and as proficiently as possible.
