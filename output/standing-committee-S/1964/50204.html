Question again proposed.

Mr. Norman Buchan: As I was saying before we were interrupted, we must stress the importance of adult education as a means of providing an adult intake into the teaching profession, because the time taken to create a teacher is not three or four but 16 or 17 years. We are in a vicious circle, because we do not have enough teachers to produce enough of the right kind of pupils, and because we are not producing enough of the right kind of pupils, we are not producing enough teachers. This is the vicious circle which we have to break.

There are one or two other factors to be kept in mind. My hon. Friend the Member for the Western Isles (Mr. Malcolm MacMillan) said that we could not compete with industry. But we have to compete with industry. One of the ways of doing that is to establish a council such as this to increase the status of teaching and act as a counter draw to the attraction of other professions, crafts or jobs.

One of the anxieties expressed is that the existence of such a Council, with a tendency to demand increased standards, will tend to discourage an intake, but I believe that it works the other way and that when demands for standards are made, the so-called pool increases. One of my criticisms of the colleges is that they do not make sufficient demands on their students.

Another anxiety has been that there is to be a majority of teachers on the Disciplinary Committee. On this committee above all we need a majority of Col. 60 teachers if the committee is to be the reverse of tyrannical, because before that committee will come teachers accused of one offence or another, and if there is a majority of teachers on the committee, that will tend to have a cushioning rather than a tyrannical effect. This is the necessary purpose, rather than any restrictive practice. I take the view that rather than having too many teachers on the Council, we have far too few practising teachers, and this is a situation which might be improved when we consider the Bill in Committee.

Six members of the Council are to be nominated by the Secretary of State for Scotland. This may be the means of reaching some kind of agreement with the Churches, particularly the two Churches which have written to hon. Members. The Secretary of State may consider having consultations with the Churches before proceeding with his nominations.

In this connection, I should like to raise the claim of yet another group of people, the trade unionists of Scotland. One of the problems which we shall be facing following the Brunton Report is the attitude of the trade unions to mobility of labour and so on. The Secretary of State should at least consult the S.T.U.C., for almost every trade unionist is also a parent whose children are in State schools, and trade union representation would be a valuable addition to the Council.

Perhaps the weakest part of the Bill is to be found in its powers. The Bill lacks teeth. The Bill does not say that a teacher must be registered before he is allowed to teach. This is something which we shall have to consider very closely in Committee, because, unless the Bill has teeth, there will still be the danger of unqualified and uncertificated teaching continuing. On the other hand, the very existence of the Council may make that unnecessary.

In the background to these discussions there is always the concept that uncertificated teachers are somehow fully qualified people who have accidentally not gained certification. No one would for a moment expect a hospital to say that while it did not have enough doctors, at least it had many nursing auxiliaries. Nursing auxiliaries are useful, and I should like there to be an extension of Image Col. 61 comparable auxiliaries in schools, but just as nursing auxiliaries are no substitute for doctors, so are uncertificated teachers no answer to the shortage of qualified teachers. In the same way, for a man to have a good bedside manner is not sufficient qualification for him to take out somebody's appendix.

We are here dealing not with brutal matters like the removal of an appendix, nor even with the craft which is involved in dealing with metals—and there is plenty of craft in turning and shaping metals—but with something infinitely more precious, a child. Of course, teaching is a job, and also a craft, and we should not be put off by the apparent hard-boiled cynicism sometimes expressed by teachers. A man who has taught in classes of 50 pupils for many years is not only doing a job, but showing a great deal of devotion, and I have found this devotion among many people.

We are told that we cannot legislate for status, that status is something which comes by nature. Of course we cannot legislate for personal qualities, but status is a structural and social thing and we can legislate about the structure of society. That is the final argument for the Bill.

Despite its weaknesses, particularly its lack of teeth, the Bill is a step forward. The very existence of the Council will bring about an increase in status for teachers, and that will help recruitment. The Bill will help to remind the teachers that their function is social, that Parliament has been concerned with their function in society, and the teachers will respond. What we need is not a devalued but an enhanced profession, and ultimately the child will benefit from that, and it is with the child that we must always be finally concerned.

May I express the personal opinion that every child is infinitely educable? We are nowhere near the beginning of an understanding of human development. In the same way, I believe that every M.P. is infinitely educable and perhaps we can learn in our discussions here today. I have not lost faith in the teachers of Scotland. In fact, my faith in them has been increased. The amount of devotion which I have seen among teachers in Scotland, sometimes in terrible conditions, is to be found Col. 62 only among those who care for and love children. I have not lost faith in M.P.s, by the same analogy, despite the scenes that we saw in the House on Tuesday. In new and old schools, I have seen teachers working devotedly for the children in their care.

I welcome the Bill and I ask those hon. Members who are worried about certain aspects of it to appreciate that this is only a small start, but that it is taking us once more to the point where education has the position it once held—right at the centre of our way of life. Only through education can Scotland develop and only with the full-blooded and warmhearted co-operation of the teachers of Scotland can we once more see a rejuvenation of our Scottish way of life. With the Bill, Scottish teachers will be assisted to play a full part in that rejuvenation.

Mr. Alasdair Mackenzie: I welcome the Bill for a variety of reasons. It will enhance the status of the teaching profession by giving teachers more responsibility in matters of particular interest to them, something which has been lacking for a long time. The Bill will also have the effect of making the profession more attractive, and that will help in the recruiting drive, which is urgent in view of the raising of the school leaving age to 16.

It is of crucial importance that this extra year should be used for the benefit of pupils. The Council will have to give serious consideration to the use of this extra year if it is not to become burdensome and discouraging for the less bright type of pupil—I am thinking particularly of boys. Unless they are taught subjects which they can assimilate during that extra year, their time will be largely wasted.

The subject of entry and the granting of certificates to teachers is very important. While the academic attainments of teachers in general are equal to those of other professions, such as medicine and the ministry of the Church, that does not mean that all teachers are equally capable of imparting their knowledge. This is the important point. There is an impression that all teachers who take a degree or a certificate or gain a diploma are equally proficient at teaching, but anyone with experience of educational administration will know that that is far from the case. Some uncertificated Image Col. 63 teachers have the ability to teach, and it is a pity that in days gone by many of them have been lost to the profession. I know many uncertificated teachers who spent twenty years doing a wonderful job. Mr. A. Woodburn: I should like to congratulate the Secretary of State on introducing this Bill and on the manner in which he introduced it. He stated a most important consideration which is apt to be forgotten in all educational debates, that education does not exist for the teachers or for the local authorities; it exists for the children. In recent times this has tended to be forgotten.

It looks to me as if everybody these days goes about with a chip on his shoulder complaining about not having enough wages or salaries; that nobody is getting good enough conditions, not the teachers, the doctors or anybody else. Nobody seems to think about the patients or the children whom they are supposed to deal with. I think a great deal of this is an exaggeration. Teachers today are working in conditions which are better than ever existed in our country before. The local authorities are building palaces for them to work in, far better than Members of Parliament get to work in. The facilities are really excellent, and, while there are still many old schools and many bad conditions, the progress which has been made is simply wonderful.

I deplore teachers calling stinking fish on their profession. The profession is proud of itself. It does great work. I agree that the devotion which teachers give to their job is far more in some cases than they are paid for. They do not all watch the clock till it is time to go home, and even after they go home they work in order to make themselves proficient. Many do nothing but live for the profession. I am sure that they get far more enjoyment out of it than the people who are always watching the clock and wondering how soon Image Col. 69 they can quit. Therefore, I hope that a little less of this depreciation of the teaching profession will go into the speeches of teachers and other people, and that it will be recognised as one of the finest professions, and one of the most enjoyable, in the world.

My wife was a teacher in times when they had a right to grumble. In the early days of the E.I.S., when somebody moved that they should ask for a rise, some of the women teachers said, "What would we do with the extra money?". That is never asked today. In those days they thought that it would be a strain on the local authorities, and they had such a public spirit that they nearly refused to accept it. In my own experience, during the 1931 election, when the Government were proposing cuts for teachers, I had a deputation from teachers. They were not objecting to the cuts. They recognised that these would be necessary. They came to see me only to see if I could do anything to ensure that the cuts were fairly distributed. There was not the slightest objection to the cuts.

Teachers do not always think about the £ s. d. of the matter. They believe in the conditions. My wife recommends young girls to go into the teaching profession, as it is one of the most enjoyable and pleasant jobs in this land. I think that if more people had that spirit there would be a better recruitment of teachers. Even the teachers in this Committee do not look so bad as a result of their experience. They are qualified, intelligent and active and they have not been exhausted by their profession.

When we were putting through the Teachers (Superannuation) Bill an actuary was listening. I was surprised at the remark made—I think by the noble Lady the Member for Aberdeen, South (Lady Tweedsmuir)—to the effect that the average age when teachers die was about 82 for men and about 85 for women. Yet we are normally told that between the ages of 60 and 65, a man collapses and is carried off to the cemetery. If that is the case, I said to the actuary, it must mean that some teachers are living over the age of 100. "That is true," he said. "There are quite a number." So we shall not get rid of the Col. 70 teachers for a long time yet; they will survive.

I know teachers today who are 75 years of age, who are splendid teachers and could still work. The local authorities ought to come looking for them, because they are such good teachers. Therefore, when we talk about needing more teachers, we should not ignore the great reserve of teachers who have retired, not because they wanted to, but because they had to. Some of them are working even though they do not get extra wages and even lose their pension. I think that it is a deterrent to drawing on this source if a teacher who goes out to work not only loses his pension but is involved with all the expenses of travel and other expenses in going to work. It reduces the amount which they get for working to a level below what their salary would be if they stayed at home. This does not make sense to many people. But this is the obvious first source for recruitment of teachers.

I believe that the status of teachers should be recognised and I think that if this Council is going to do any good in that respect it is to be welcomed, but we should not exaggerate the part which this plays in the minds of the average teachers. They are practical people. While it may be a good thing in itself it will not solve the problem of finding extra teachers. The first reserve, as I said, is the teachers who are trained, who have more experience than any new teacher, and they should be drawn upon. There may be other reserves. Other reserves certainly must come from outside the teaching profession. The Secretary of State said it took five or six years to make a teacher, but the problem for this Council, I believe, is what is to be done in the immediate circumstances, before the five or six years are up, when more teachers need to be recruited.

It is no good the teachers insisting that they must have graduates, because if they are not there it is a case of "taking the breeks off a Hieland man" to ask for more graduate teachers. The competition for graduates is so great that there is no sense in thinking that one can win teachers by an auction sale. When I had the responsibility of the Secretary of State, we had one or two professions wanting their professions to be made graduate professions. I refused. I said, "What Image Col. 71 you want is a rise of wages. That is why you want this graduate status. You want higher status to get a higher income. But there are not enough graduates to start spreading them over another half-dozen professions." We must resist this while being sure that the people we appoint can do the job. Many people, such as those in the probation service, are doing an excellent job without necessarily being graduates at all. Therefore, we must resist this idea that the only people who can do any work for the community are the graduates. There are not enough, and there never can be enough. The standard is now so high that fewer and fewer people are able to get into universities and fewer and fewer people are able to be graduates. Principal Appleton of Edinburgh University, in his report on the subject, pointed out that this continual raising of standards is a restrictive practice, restricting the field from which one can draw. This may be very good from the point of view of status, but one does not necessarily raise the number of people who are able to qualify as graduates.

If one cannot get graduates to fill the jobs one has to fill them in some other way and this is the immediate problem to which this Council should devote its attention to, and not, as the hon. Member for Paisley (Mr. John Robertson) said, be tempted to develop it along the lines of restrictive practices in order to make a monopoly of the job and make a scarcity value for teachers. We want well qualified teachers but we do not want a scarcity value.

The problem, therefore, will only be affected by this Council to the extent that the Council tackle the problem in this way. I hope that it gives practical advice to the Secretary of State and not idealistic advice about high-falutin' qualifications which a large number of people could never obtain. Even the universities are apt to load on a whole lot of somewhat unnecessary subjects in order to elevate their status. This puts the hoop through which people must jump to get a job even higher.

They sometimes found that the honours graduate was so satisfied with what he had achieved that he did not keep himself up to date in his own subjects.

I mention this to show that this matter can be over-exaggerated; that we can stick labels on people unnecessarily. I know of one education authority which had to sack an honours graduate—a graduate who, incidentally, had three degrees—because he could not keep discipline in the classroom. Discipline may be as important in getting the job of teaching done as the academic side of it. Many qualities go to make a good teacher, not necessarily only university qualifications.

We all admit that the trained teacher is the best one and I agree that it is desirable to obtain teachers who are certificated and who have gone through the college of education. Even so, the college of education is building up its prestige and insists that only those who have gone through its courses can really teach. The person who has been qualified to teach in England, having taken a university Image Col. 73 course in teaching, is not qualified to teach in Scotland because he has not qualified at the college of education. This matter needs rationalising and, for this reason, the college of education should be given university status or something of that kind so that training in it will rank as graduation for the task being learned there. We should train people for teaching and not try to train them to do everything, and then try to turn them into good teachers afterwards.

What happens if we cannot obtain the highest quality of teacher? It may be that we need not insist on the highest quality for every job that must be done in the teaching profession. It may be, as my hon. Friend the Member for Renfrew, West (Mr. Buchan) said, that there is a place for auxiliaries in the profession so that teachers may be relieved of a lot of work that could be done by, for example, clerkesses. This might leave the teacher more time in which to undertake the difficult job of teaching.

If, when all this is done, we still cannot get the number of teachers we need, we must then make the best use of those we have got. In this connection, I congratulate the Glasgow Education Authority for going ahead with the use of scientific methods of teaching. At least seven years ago I pointed out that while we educate people in science and teach science, we do not always use it to the best advantage. We should learn to use it to a greater degree whenever there is a scarcity of labour in any profession. America, for example, was built up because it could not get all the labour it required. Machinery had to be used. If we cannot obtain the labour we need, we must do likewise.

If we are to multiply the number of teachers we now have in our schools, but if we are unable to do so through recruiting, we shall have to use science by way of closed circuit television, film script lectures by the best teachers, and so on. Methods of doing this are available, but insufficient use is made of them. It is at least thirty years since I first learned that the best way of teaching economics was to put the matter on the blackboard, while the worst way was to scribble in chalk on the blackboard in the middle of a lecture. If this information is on films, slides and other Col. 74 mechanical aids the work can be done with the least amount of labour and, at the same time, the best use of skilled labour can be made.

If in Glasgow—and I understand that in Fife experiments are also being conducted into these methods of teaching—and in Edinburgh a success can be made of closed circuit television teaching and other scientific means of learning, it means that the best teachers can expound their subjects to the largest number of people. There is no reason why, in the ordinary school, a teacher should not explain the subject just as well to 600 students as to 60. If a lecture is given to a university class of a couple of hundred students, they all hear it at the same time, just as well as if they were in classes of 60. Having explained the subject or given a lecture, the teacher can then go on to the task of teaching, for the tutorial work is a completely separate concept.

I urge the Secretary of State urgently to consider the whole question of the modernisation of teaching. I am sure that this applies to universities as well as ordinary schools. Incidentally, I understand that in some cases university students find it easier to obtain the notes on certain lectures from previous students in order to know exactly what the professor has said. All these things should be brought up to date and, if our universities and schools are to teach science properly, they should get down to the planned use of it.

I have a great respect for teachers. I know and have known a great many of them and I respect the work they do.
