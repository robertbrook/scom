SCOTTISH GRAND COMMITTEE
Tuesday, 2nd February, 1965

[Mr. JOHN BREWIS in the Chair]

TEACHING COUNCIL (SCOTLAND) BILL

OFFICIAL REPORT

10.30 a.m.

Resolved,

That during the proceedings on the Teaching Council (Scotland) Bill the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock.— [Mr. Ross.]

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. William Ross)
: I beg to move.

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Teaching Council (Scotland) Bill has been considered in relation to the principle of the Bill.

 The Bill makes a considerable—indeed, a major—change concerning one of the most important aspects of Scottish education: that of the entry into teaching, and the regulations in relation to the education and training of entrants, which at present is controlled by the Secretary of State and which, after the passing of the Bill, will in great measure pass to the teaching profession through the establishment of a Teaching Council. This is a far-reaching change. It is not merely a device to give status to Scottish teachers, as some people have suggested, but is one which recognises the status of teachers and their ability to accept new responsibilities in regard to their profession.

I should like to pay tribute to the work of the Wheatley Committee and to Lord Wheatley himself, whose Report is the basis of the Bill. Lord Wheatley has said that the Committee was in some respects an unusual one. Apart from the three independent members, each of whom, I am sure, made a valuable contribution, the membership was made up of the interests most closely concerned with education. We therefore had knowledge and experience, men who, as Lord Wheatley says in his Report, started with preconceived ideas about the problem, and yet every one of whom moved from those preconceived ideas towards general agreement. The degree of unanimity 6 which was reached, bearing in mind the nature of the body, is a tribute in itself to their realism and to Lord Wheatley in guiding the Committee.

I have always thought, and I have made no great secret of the fact, that the Wheatley Report merited action by the Government, and I have made it my business to bring forward the Bill as quickly as possible to give effect to the Committee's recommendations. It is nearly two years since the Wheatley Report was laid before the House in May, 1963, and the Committee's work started earlier than that. I propose to say something about the context in which the Wheatley Committee was appointed and about the role of the teaching profession in the shaping of educational policy. Then I should like to look at the situation confronting us today in teacher supply, which undoubtedly is one of our most serious problems and one which underlies the Bill. Then I should like to deal with the Bill itself.

Many of us, on both sides, will recall the circumstances in May, 1961, which led to the setting up of the Wheatley Committee. In that month, we had a debate in this Committee Room. Teaching in those days was in a ferment. Many of us remember it as Members of Parliament. There are some who remember it as teachers actively involved in the discussions of that time. I think that the morale of the teaching profession was never lower. Those in the profession felt that they had been forgotten and had lost status Everything culminated in and boiled up over two special problems: that of salaries at that time, and that of a feeling that dilution would be thrust upon the profession. The result was that we had a strike in Glasgow. The feelings of many of the people in Glasgow at that time were shared elsewhere in Scotland.

It was in those circumstances and to get out of that difficulty that the then Secretary of State, now Viscount Muirshiel, suggested to the teachers that there should be a review of the outstanding problems. That led to the establishment of committees and subcommittees to look into these problems, and from that we have had quite good results in the way of legislation. The one big thing which came out, however, was the establishment of the Wheatley JPEG 7 Committee, from which eventually came the Wheatley Report. Although I pay tribute to the former Government for what they did concerning some of the other problems, I have always regretted that they allowed all these months, spreading into years, to pass without taking action on what I thought was the central and most important part—the Wheatley Report.

On the face of things, the question of reviewing the existing arrangements for granting certificates to teachers would appear to be a purely technical matter. It is far from it, because it really means the whole problem of control of entry to teaching and the extent to which the profession itself can properly be given specific responsibilities in that regard.

The Wheatley Committee not only did a thorough job, but it did a remarkable job, because it had to assess and make suggestions concerning the balance of interest between what might be called the professional interest and the public interest as represented by the Secretary of State. This careful assessment of legitimate professional interests and responsibilities of education authorities and the Secretary of State for the education service underlies the carefully calculated balance in the composition of the Teaching Council which the Committee recommended and which is enshrined in Clause 1 and in Schedule 1 of the Bill.

I want the Committee to bear in mind, particularly when we discuss the details of Clause 1 and the composition of the Council, that we are sticking fairly closely to what was laid down and carefully thought out by the Wheatley Committee concerning the interests already existing and the changes which we are making. In particular, the Wheatley Committee was at pains to acknowledge the statutory responsibilities of the Secretary of State. Whatever else can be done and whatever happens it cannot be gainsaid that the responsibility to the public and to Parliament remains statutorily in the hands of the Secretary of State. That being so, he must retain in any changes power to exercise that responsibility. This is fundamental and, I think, is recognised by the teaching profession. Certainly it was recognised by the Wheatley Committee. The responsibilities to Parliament of myself or of any 8 other Secretary of State are such that he must have the last word in such matters as the regulation of entry to teaching. How that is exercised is an entirely different matter. I am satisfied that we can build up trust, confidence and responsibility with the teachers to be able to give them this considerable measure of self-government. The point which I wish to make at this stage is that the Government's view is that the Committee's Report, resting as it does on an informed and balanced view of the realities of the situation, offers a sound and practical basis on which to build a new system for control of entry to the profession.

It should be remembered that this is applicable only to Scotland. It is not the first time that Scotland has pioneered in education, and I hope that it will not be the last. It is a matter to our credit—and I feel that I speak for the whole Committee—that we are prepared to make this change, far reaching as it is, and give a unique place to the teaching profession in Scotland. It is for this reason that we come forward with the Bill, embodying practically all the specific recommendations of the Wheatley Committee.

In paragraph 57 of its Report, the Wheatley Committee stated:

"…we think that the time has now come when a further advance should be made in the process which has been taking place in recent years of giving the teaching profession an increased measure of responsibility in matters which are of prime concern to it."

 I think that is right. That is why we are setting up the Teaching Council.

There has been in recent years a gradual change in the process under which teachers as a body have come to play a greater part in shaping educational policy and participating in educational decisions. As one who has been a teacher, I greatly welcome this. As Secretary of State, I think it is essential to ordered progress in education that we should work in harmony and co-operation.

The teachers have, for example, played a valuable part in the work of the Advisory Council. That is a body which will disappear with the growth and development of the new Teaching Council. In more recent times they have participated in a great many committees and working parties dealing with specific problems, and there has been a greater recognition of the need to bring teachers into consultation on educational policy, not only at a JPEG 9 national level, but also at a local level. Teachers—and this is where they differ from those south of the Border—have played a considerable part in the actual governance of our colleges of education. In fact, they have representatives there. This is a change and an advance which we made some time ago as between the two countries. In addition, teachers directly elected by their colleagues to those bodies will, I hope, continue to play the same kind of part within the new Council. The party opposite, to its credit, played its part in this development, but I am disappointed that it stopped short of the one big decision to legislate for the Wheatley Report.

When the Teaching Council comes to be set up—and I hope that we shall be able to proceed with this as rapidly as possible—the timetable, as I see it, is that we shall get the Bill through by the summer, we shall be able to set up the Council by September, it should be in a position to start exercising its authority in January, 1966, and take over my responsibilities thereafter. In about April I will hand over those of my responsibilities that I am allowing to lapse. At that time, the Scottish Council should go and the new Teaching Council should then be in full control. I hope we can accept this as reasonable progress towards its establishment and bringing it into operation.

With the new Council in being, the teachers in Scotland will have what can fairly be called a unique measure of self-government. As the profession itself acknowledges, this involves new responsibilities as well as new powers. Power without responsibility is tyranny, and the last place we want tyranny is in Scottish education. There may be cynics, as I have said, who think that this is merely a device for giving status to teachers. In my opinion, that is a misplaced view of what has been happening and of the coming together which has taken place between those responsible for educational policy.

I have every reason to think that Scottish teachers will welcome their new responsibilities and will act responsibly and with a full realisation of the future needs of the education service and of our country and of the paramount needs of our children. It is not the Government who matter, it is not the teachers who 10 matter, in education: it is the children who matter. If we lose sight of the needs of our children in modern education, we shall not get any of our problems right and we shall all lose the respect of the public. Status depends eventually on the public, not on what we think of ourselves, but on what the public think of us and whether we are meeting our responsibilities. There are great opportunities in all this for the teaching profession, and, as I have said in my last sentence or two, there are challenges.

This is basic to what we are doing here. I do not want to stray too far from the theme of the Bill, but it would be quite unrealistic if I did not say something about this problem. We had a very good debate on the subject last night, and I am sorry that quite a number of hon. Members were not there. Gentleman the five minutes he wanted, but he should have taken his opportunity when it was there. However, that is by the way.

I do not want to give too many figures now—many of them were given last night—but the fact is that, in October last, the education authorities estimated that they needed 3,600 teachers to fill vacancies, to enable them to get rid of uncertificated teachers and to give the kind of teaching coverage which we regard as minimum. That is the shortage just now. If we do nothing about anything else, this shortage will disappear by 1970. But, of course, the previous Government decided to raise the school-leaving age in 1970–71, and this will call for more teachers. It means that all pupils will stay on till 16 instead of leaving at 15 as they can do now, and the immediate consequence will be a shortage of teachers in 1970 of about 4,000. This will be without doing anything at all to reduce the size of classes, and the size of classes in Scotland is higher than the permitted maximum in England. Therefore, even though we overcome the one problem and get the extra 4,000 teachers in 1970, in order to solve the problem of the size of classes to the extent of giving us parity with England and Wales, we shall need another 3,000 teachers by 1975, and if we wanted to reduce the size of classes to 30 the shortage would be about 10,000.

That is the extent of the problem, and I was disappointed in the right hon. Gentleman the Member for Argyll (Mr. Noble) last night because, while my hon. Friend was speaking, he sat there laughing. It was his Government who decided to raise the school-leaving age, and he, as Secretary of State, ought to have known that it takes at least three to five years to make a teacher. This is why time is not on our side. We cannot wave a wand and make thousands of teachers appear. The right hon. Gentleman should have been ready at that time with ways and means of meeting the problem as it came. Let us not, therefore, think that setting up a Teaching Council is all we need to do. We cannot allow even a year to pass without pressing ahead, 12 and I hope that there will be general recognition of this by everyone concerned.

I have been speaking about numbers of teachers, but it is not really with the number of teachers that we can properly begin. Those who will go to swell the schools in 1970–71 will be the pupils who today are leaving at 15, and the pupils who are leaving today at 15 are pretty dissatisfied with the education which we are giving them in their secondary courses now. Therefore, in considering the kind of education we want, we have to relate our thoughts to the kind of teacher we want. The kind of teacher we want must be related to the qualifications of teachers. Here we come to the real problem before us of working out exactly what is needed in relation to the kind of teachers, the number of teachers and the kind of education which we must give. All these considerations are closely knit together. We must see to it that we have the right kind of teacher to meet the needs of the pupils, not just the needs of the schools. It is important, for example, to have more men teachers in the primary schools in the interest of stability in staffing and the continuity of teaching.

Mr. John Rankin
: Will my right hon. Friend agree also that the question of the kind of teacher and the kind of pupil is intimately related to the question of the school buildings in which they will be housed?

Mr. Ross
: Yes, this is true also. All these things are related, and the question of the kind of buildings is related to the kind of education that one gives.

But I am thinking particularly of the imperative need to devise courses of secondary education for pupils who are not academic but who still deserve the best in education that we can give them, an education which will interest them. I am speaking of the pupils who used to be called junior secondary material, a term which is rather misleading and which would be best forgotten.

The Brunton Working Party Report has given a major lead in this direction, and the welcome it has received and the response it has evoked from education authorities and teachers give grounds for thinking that we can expect to make real headway in secondary education, not least by getting far more varied, more interesting and more life-related—call JPEG 13 them vocational if you like—courses. With the raising of the school-leaving age in the offing, the need for effective progress in this direction is all the greater.

We can make headway only if we have the right kinds of teachers in the right numbers. There have been suggestions—we canvassed them last night, and my hon. Friend spoke about the review which is taking place—for short-term measures, bringing back more retired teachers, having special recruitment arrangements, getting back more married women teachers into the profession, and so on. There have been suggestions that we should pay more. I wish that this were the solution, but I do not think that it is as easy as that. We are concerned here with competing demands for a limited number of qualified people, and, fundamentally, what we must do is increase as much as we possibly can the pool of people on which we can draw, trying to ensure that teaching continues to secure its proportion—and, if possible, an increased proportion—of those who are capable and qualified for the profession.

All these suggestions are reasonable in themselves, but we must recognise that, even at best, we might still be left with a considerable shortage. Therefore, although I do not exclude action on these short-term measures, as my hon. Friend said last night, a realistic attitude towards the long-term measures must remain.

However successful we may be in trying to seek a solution in this way, any additional recruitment which might be obtained will not solve our 1970 problem. So it is against this background that we must consider whether new sources of recruitment can be found and what kind of teachers we really need. We must examine how far industrial or commercial experience is of value to teachers of this kind. This is a field which we can explore and from which we may be able to obtain qualified and capable people in order to deal with the new problem in education.

We have to consider very carefully whether we can find new sources of recruitment. A great deal of thought is being given to this question now. I have met the teachers myself on more than one occasion, and I have no wish to offer solutions just now because they are matters which must be worked out, but there are proposals now under discussion 14 in the Scottish Council for the Training of Teachers and with the teachers' associations. We have not reached the point of decision, but, remembering the time scale, we must realise that this is not something which can be just put off, with the idea that we can let the Teaching Council deal with it. We cannot afford to waste a year. Indeed, in my view, we cannot afford to waste a month if we are to be in a position realistically to deal with the situation in 1970.

I realise that suggestions for change in the training system are liable to give rise to misgivings in some quarters. In bringing forward the Bill, we are, for our part, giving an earnest of our intention to entrust the teaching profession with a substantial measure of responsibility, and we feel justified, therefore, in asking the teachers to take a realistic and dispassionate view of the situation, as they did when they embarked on their deliberations on the Wheatley Committee.

A few words now about the Bill itself. An important task of the Council will be to establish a register of teachers to take the place of the existing arrangements under which teachers are certificated by the Secretary of State. Even more important in some ways will be the Council's functions as regards entry to teaching. The question of registration is covered by Clauses 6 and 8. The main Clauses establishing the Council and its functions are Clauses 1 and 2, together with Schedule 1.

The Teaching Council will consist of 44 members, 21 of them directly elected by the teachers. There will be four teachers elected from among themselves by the principals of the colleges of education. This will give 25 teachers. There will be 13 members from the education authorities and educational institutions, higher education establishments and the like, and six appointed by the Secretary of State.

I know that hon. Members have been receiving letters about this, and so have I. But if we acceded to all the requests of people who have said that they have a special interest, it would be a Council not of 44 members but of about 78. We have to bear this in mind, and this is why I think that we were right in sticking fairly closely to Wheatley.

The functions of the Council will be to review the standards for entry into the JPEG 15 teaching profession and to make recommendations to the Secretary of State as regards the incorporation of these standards in regulations under Clause 7 of the Bill. Another function, under Clause 3, will be to advise the Secretary of State about teacher supply. This is a function exercised at present by the Scottish Council. As regards teacher supply, we specifically say that this function will not cover salaries and the like because such questions are already dealt with by the Scottish Joint Council.

Under Clause 5, the Council will keep itself informed about the work of the colleges of education, but not to the extent of interfering in the day-to-day work and routine of the colleges. Clauses 6 and 8, as I have said, deal with the responsibility for establishing and maintaining a system of registration of teachers. At present, it is the Secretary of State who grants the certificate, what we in the profession used to call the parchment, to the teacher. In future, this will be a matter for the Council in relation to registration.
