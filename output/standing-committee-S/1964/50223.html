At the end of that morning we were going over much of the same ground in debating, That Clause 3 stand part of the Bill. A number of questions were put to me about the scope of the Clause and of the Council's duties. It may help the Committee if I say a little more at this stage. It is quite clear to me that we should not contemplate a situation in which the Teaching Council, further to its duty under the Clause of considering and making recommendations on matters relating to the supply of teachers, had some duty to make recommendations on matters of remuneration and conditions of service. This would be an impossible position because there would then be a function on the part of the Scottish Joint Council under the 1962 Act to put forward salary recommendations to the Secretary of State as a result of negotiations in that Council. We must clearly safeguard the position of the Scottish Joint Council. We must also avoid exposing the Secretary of State to statutory recommendations on salary 102 matters from two quite distinct bodies, on the one hand the General Teaching Council discharging its duty to make recommendations to him as to the supply of teachers, and on the other hand the Scottish Joint Council exercising its function of putting forward salary proposals as the outcome of the salary negotiations between the two sides of the Council. That is why the duty of the Teaching Council under the Clause as it appears in the Bill is deliberately so defined as to make it clear that the duty in respect of matters relating to the supply of teachers does not extend to remuneration and conditions fo service.

What we are concerned with in this Clause, as, indeed, its wording makes quite clear, is a duty upon the Council. The Council, in considering matters of supply, whether in general or in relation to some particular sector of the work of the schools or further education, will no doubt seek to break down the problem engaging it as a means of analysing what is involved and of establishing what measures it might recommend should be taken. A number of hon. Members argued that it was unrealistic to expect the Council in looking at supply to ignore economic considerations. It was in the light of what they said that I took the point that it would be open to the Council, if its analysis of a particular supply problem suggested that this aspect might be critical, to make it known to the Secretary of State that this was the Council's opinion. The Council would know that this aspect was not one which it would make the subject of a recommendation to the Secretary of State, but it would be open to the Council to take the matter on the basis I have suggested. It would not, however, be a duty for the Council to do so, nor would the Secretary of State be bound in any way to have regard to any general observation or expression of opinion of this kind. This answers the point put by the right hon. Member for Argyll (Mr. Noble) and the noble Lady the Member for Aberdeen, South (Lady Tweedsmuir).
