OFFICIAL REPORT

Tuesday, 25th March, 1969

[Sir BARNETT JANNER in the Chair]

AGE OF MAJORITY (SCOTLAND) BILL [ Lords]

10.30 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Bruce Millan)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the Age of Majority (Scotland) Bill [ Lords] in relation to the principle of the Bill.

This Bill is modest, and its purpose is reasonably clear—to lower to 18 the age at which a person attains majority or full age and ceases to be a minor under Scottish law. The Bill is complementary to two other Bills now before Parliament, the Representation of the People Bill, which lowers the voting age to 18, and Part I of the Family Law Reform Bill, an English and Welsh Measure only, which implements the main recommendations of the Latey Committee by lowering the age of majority in England and Wales to 18 and removing from young people between 18 and 21 the substantial disabilities from which a minor suffers under English law.

I would remind the Committee of the general attitude which the Latey Committee took to the question of lowering the age. It was a Committee dealing only with England and Wales, but some of its general points have relevance to Scotland as well. In paragraph 132, for example, it said:

"Young people today, as the old never tire of remarking, are not what they were. They are largely literate and educated; they are far better off financially and far more independent of their parents; they are taught to think and enquire for themselves and mostly do so; and their experience of life is wider. The question is not whether this is a good thing or a bad thing, but what are we to do about it. This Committee is convinced that we must ensure that the young go out into the world as fully prepared for their adult responsibilities as possible, and that in giving them adult status at 18 we are doing no more than recognising the simple facts."

I would agree very much with those sentiments.

The Committee will, of course, be aware that, under Scottish law, the minor—that is, a boy between 14 and 21 or a girl 6 between 12 and 21—has always enjoyed a measure of freedom which has been denied to the minor in England and Wales. He has the legal capacity to enter into binding contracts, he can acquire and dispose of property, both moveable and heritable, and can make a will dealing with both. He can marry without parental consent, provided that he is 16, and the procedure by which a young person can be made a ward of court—a controversial matter in England sometimes—is unknown to Scottish law.

Apart from these rights, the minor in Scotland is afforded a special measure of protection from the courts: if he is foolish enough to enter into a contract which is seriously to his detriment, he can apply to have it reduced and can challenge a contract entered into during his minority at any time up to the expiry of the period known as the quadriennium utile, that is, the four years immediately following the attainment of his majority. That provision is retained in the new circumstances. It can thus be said that, under existing Scottish law, a minor in some senses has the best of both worlds. On the one hand he has virtually full legal capacity, and on the other he has this special protection of being able to have a contract reduced in certain circumstances up to four years from the date of his majority.

Therefore, we started in Scotland from a background quite different from that which the Latey Committee considered in England and Wales. Nevertheless, it seemed to the Secretary of State that, in the light of the Latey Committee, it was desirable to reappraise the general position of the age of majority in Scotland. Therefore, after the Government decided to implement the Latey recommendations in England and Wales, something which was announced last April, the Secretary of State consulted a number of organisations in Scotland which might be expected to have a view on the social as well as the legal implications of any change in the law.

I should tell the Committee that neither the Faculty of Advocates nor the Law Society of Scotland saw any real need for legislation on the subject. Both emphasised the benefits enjoyed by young persons in Scotland under the existing law, but they recognised that there were considerations pointing the other way and made some suggestions which have been taken into account in drafting the Bill.

JPEG 7 The other organisations which were consulted, however, which included the Convention of Royal Burghs, the Counties of Cities Association, and the Association of County Councils, among the local authority associations, the S.T.U.C., the N.F.U., the Scottish Landowners' Federation, and one or two others, favoured lowering the age of majority in Scotland at the same time as in England and Wales. After considering the various comments the Secretary of State decided that the balance of advantage lay with making the change in Scotland. The decision to legislate was announced last November in reply to a Question by my hon. Friend the Member for Caithness and Sutherland (Mr. Maclennan).

Before explaining the provisions of the Bill, it might be helpful to summarise the arguments which led my right hon. Friend to conclude that the balance of advantage lay with introducing the Bill, despite the rather different legal circumstances of minors in Scotland at present.

In the first place, whatever the legal rights of minors, young people in Scotland are as much entitled to claim the status of majority at 18 as their contemporaries in England and Wales. This is an important point because, unless we make this change, of course we should be in the anomalous position of not recognising the age of 18 in Scotland, when it was being recognised in England and Wales, as the age of maturity at which young people's ability to manage their own affairs responsibly is fully recognised by law. There would be considerable resentment in Scotland if we did not make this change. Even if there are not the same formal disabilities in law in Scotland as south of the Border, that is, if anything, a reason for no longer retaining the formal vestiges of minority until the age of 21.

Secondly, there are practical considerations pointing in the direction of the same age of majority both sides of the Border. Considerable anomalies would arise if the ages were different. For example, a young person of 18 in Scotland would be debarred from office in a trade union or friendly society but not in England and that would be indefensible. The statutory provisions listed in Schedule 1 show that similar difficulties would arise over age limits prescribed in other Acts which apply to Great Britain as a whole. 8 Finally, there are indications that young people in Scotland may in practice suffer from some at least of the disabilities with which the Latey Committee was concerned.

For example, in the memorandum on Scottish Law which it submitted to the Committee and which is reproduced in Appendix 5 of the Report, the Scottish Law Commission says, with reference to a minor's right to enter into contracts:

"Generally … in matters of importance and, in particular, in matters relating to heritable property, parties are reluctant to deal with a minor unless he or she has a curator who has consented to the transaction."

That is the practical situation and there are disabilities there.

Commenting on the same point, the Law Society of Scotland has said that, at the moment, a minor has great difficulty in buying a dwellinghouse and obtaining a building society or other loan on its security. If the age of majority were reduced, the need to obtain a curator's consent would be resolved for those aged from 18 to 21, although we know of other difficulties for young people purchasing property and obtaining a building society loan, particularly at present, for reasons upon which I should be delighted to expand, but not here this morning. The legal disabilities between 18 and 21 can at least be largely resolved by this change.

These were some of the general arguments which led us to the conclusion that the Bill should be introduced. Its sole purpose is to lower to 18 the age at which a person attains majority and ceases to be a minor under Scottish law. It alters the existing law only to the extent that that is necessary in consequence of the lowering of the age. It does not affect the many other features of the law of minority in Scotland which are at present under review by the Scottish Law Commission. Nor does it alter the marriage law of Scotland, which is being examined by a Committee under Lord Kilbrandon.

Clause 1 (1) provides in general terms that, in future, a person shall attain majority at 18 instead of 21, that persons who are already 18 but not yet 21 on the date on which the Bill is brought into operation shall attain majority on that date, and that references to such expressions as "full age" and "minority" as they appear in statutory provisions or deeds executed after the commencement of the Act shall be construed accordingly. However, the construction of deeds JPEG 9 executed before the Bill comes into force is not affected. It would clearly be wrong to alter the effect and meaning of such deeds retrospectively by Act of Parliament.

Clause 1(2) provides that the change in the age of majority will apply for the purposes of any rule of law. One effect is that the quadriennium utile, the period of four years after the attainment of majority during which a person can challenge a contract which he has entered into while a minor, will continue, but will in future run from 18 to 22 rather than from 21 to 25.

There are some statutory provisions which contain explicit reference to the age of 21, and where these limits are clearly related to the age at which a person attains majority under the common law, we propose to substitute the age of 18. Schedule 1 contains a list of the enactments and Regulations.

There are references, no doubt, of similar kind in the large body of Local Acts, but it is virtually impossible to trace all those similar references, so, instead of providing for them explicitly, in Clause 1 (5) we provide that the Secretary of State shall be empowered to amend by Statutory Instrument any such provision which is brought to his attention and which it would clearly be appropriate to amend under this legislation.

10.45 a.m.

I make quite clear what the Bill does not cover. There are many other references to age limits in the Statute Book—for example, in the criminal law. These references have been fixed with a variety of considerations in mind. They are not affected by the Bill unless they appear in Schedule 1. Schedule 1 seeks to change the age limits and is clearly related to the age at which a person attains majority under the common law. Other age limits, perhaps decided upon for other reasons, are not affected by the Bill and do not appear in Schedule 1. Similarly, it is desired to except certain enactments from the application of the new rules of construction which are introduced by Clause 1(2). These enactments, which are limited but which include the law relating to Income Tax and taxation generally, the law about voting, and also the Regency Acts, are listed in Schedule 2 of the Bill. The two Schedules are similar in content to the 10 corresponding Schedules of the Family Law Reform Bill for England, but hon. Members will have noticed that Schedule 1 contains a number of pre-Union enactments of the Scottish Parliament, some of which prescribe an age limit of 25 rather than 21.

In the past some significance was attached to the age at which the quadriennium utile expired, that is to say, the age of 25. The present law on this point has given rise in the past to a certain amount of confusion because in certain contexts the expression "full age" has been taken to refer to the attainment of the age of 25 rather than the age of 21. We feel that we ought now to remove that kind of confusion.

I should like to draw attention to Clause 2(3) which concerns the making of the commencement order. There is a similar provision in the Family Law Reform Bill. For the reason that it is desirable to maintain a similar age of majority on both sides of the Border, it would be the Government's intention to synchronise the reduction in the age of majority in the two countries. In other words, this Bill would come into operation at the same time as the corresponding provisions of the Family Law Reform Bill in England.

As I said at the outset, this is a modest Bill which does not make substantial change in the Scottish position, but it is a Measure which, on balance, we feel to be most desirable. I hope that my explanation has been reasonably clear, and I commend the Bill to the Committee.

Mr. N. R. Wylie
: The concluding remarks of the Under-Secretary of State correctly sum up the significance of this small Measure. Even the Scottish Grand Committee will have its work cut out to make a two-day debate on this Bill. I could be wrong. One never knows the resources of this Committee. The provisions of the Bill, in effect, duplicate the provisions of Clause 1 of the Family Law Reform Bill together with the associated schedules. Indeed, it is the kind of provision which could easily have formed part of a law reform Bill such as we had a few months ago.

The need for this kind of change, and the reason for seeking such a change in Scotland, is the inadequacy of the Common Law of England to deal with the status and standing of young people under 21. The very terminology of the law of England in this context is totally inadequate. In England there is reference to the term "infants". The situation is neatly summed up in the Latey Committee Report which says at the top of page 42:

"The young, and all sensible people connected with them, find it quite ludricrous that legally speaking a married man with three children and a car can be referred to as 'infant'."

That sums up the total inadequacy of the English Common Law on this matter.

I am grateful that the Under-Secretary of State pointed out that the problems which have initiated this change in England and Wales do not arise in Scotland. Indeed, it has been said by some eminent authorities that perhaps the Scottish common law has gone too far the other way and gives too much responsibility to a young person under 21. I do not share that view, but would underline what the Minister has said, that the common law of Scotland differentiates between pupils on the one hand and minors on the other—minors being young boys between 14 and 21. and young girls between 12 and 21, giving to the latter a wide field of legal capacity.

The hon. Gentleman mentioned all the important aspects of the minor's legal standing in Scotland. It to some extent depends upon whether the minor has a curator or not. His curator is normally his father. It is interesting to note that the Guardianship of Infants Act did not make 12 the mother the curatrix of a minor child. If the father dies the child may be left without a curator, and sometimes the court appoints one. If he has a curator, who may be his father or a person appointed by the court, provided that he acts with the consent and concurrence of his curator, he has the widest possible contractual powers, with the one exception that he cannot gratuitously dispose of his heritable property. One of the shortcomings of the English Common Law compared with the law in Scotland is that in Scotland the minor child has wide testamentary capacity. It was the Succession (Scotland) Act, 1964, which conferred upon him testamentary capacity to dispose of his heritable property. One must remember, when assessing the legal capacity of a minor in Scotland, that even if he has a father alive who is his curator at law, if he sets out on his own path in life then he stands on his own. He then has full legal capacity notwithstanding the fact that he is under 21. Marriage has always been treated in Scotland in a special way and is still substantially in advance of the position in England, and will be so even after the Family Law Reform Bill has been enacted. From the age of 16 a minor child in Scotland is free to marry without parental consent. This is a principle derived from the Canon Law basis of Scottish marriage law; it regarded marriage as a Sacrament personal to both spouses.

These are features of the position of the minor child in Scotland which put him in a much stronger and more responsible position than is the case with the minor child in England.

If I as a lawyer were asked to say whether the Bill is necessary in Scotland I should be bound to share the view expressed by the Faculty of Advocates and The Law Society that it makes so little difference that we could well do without it. In one sense it contracts the position of the minor. It deprives him of the privilege which he enjoys during the period of the quadrietmium utile in regard to contracts effected between the ages of 18 and 21. When the Bill becomes law, during the period of three years he will not be in the preferred position which the common law at present grants him. He will still be able to reduce contracts entered into before the age of 18 during the four years following 18, but the net effect is that that three years will not be covered as at present they are covered by the common law.

JPEG 13 I am bound to agree with the Under-Secretary of State that times and people have changed. I have always taken the view that the law ought to recognise changes. There is nothing sacrosanct about 21 as the age of majority, except that it has been the age of majority for about a thousand years. There is no peculiar significance, apart from that, to be attached to the age of 21. During the later Roman period it was 25, during the early Middle Ages it was 15, and only later did it rise to 21 in the case of those holding what was known as knight tenure or knight service. Historically, the age of majority is tied up with capacity for military service. One has only to look back to the war years to realise that, if it is linked traditionally and historically with military service, then that liability for military service arises at the age of 18, and equally the age of majority should be linked with it.

The real reasons for justifying this change are as follows. People today are more mature than they were. They are physically mature at an earlier age than they were. Although emotional maturity does not necessarily follow physical maturity, physical maturity is something which one has to recognise. Economic changes have given a wider freedom to people under 21. People of 18 and upwards are certainly earning more money, and one of our social problems at the moment arises from the fact that a youth of 18, in many cases, probably earns about as much as his father earns. The effect of this on family and social discipline is liable to be unfortunate, but again it must be recognised. If people of that age are earning at such a level to make them economically independent of the household in which they have been brought up, and independent of their parents, one must recognise the facts. Young people marry earlier than was the case some years ago. The marriage statistics show a steady rise in the number of people who marry under the age of 18, both youths and girls. It is interesting, although not strictly relevant to the Bill, that roughly one in six of girls who marry under 21 are under 18, but in regard to boys the figure is roughly one in 15; it is a small proportion. I am glad to see that no attempt is being made to alter the age of marriage in this respect.

The age group to whom we are now giving the status of the adult, as has been pointed out by a number of hon. Members on several occasions, are precisely the age group in which the incidence of violence is highest. But it is important to remember that although, quite rightly, one hears a lot about the problems of violence and young people we must not ignore the fact that the vast majority of young people in Scotland today are thoroughly responsible, thoroughly sensible, law abiding citizens. I sometimes wish that the Press would give as much publicity to the positive contributions of young people as it does to the less desirable contributions for which a very small minority are responsible.

This is a small Bill. It is of very little legal significance, but I think that, in a sense, it is symbolic. One could describe it as an act of faith by our generation, reflecting our confidence in and respect for the young people of today. I am confident that that confidence is not misplaced. For these reasons, and in that context, I recommend my hon. Friends—indeed the whole Committee—to give this Bill the fullest support it could have.

Mrs. Winifred Ewing

Mr. Donald Dewar
: I do not think anyone could say that this Bill has had 16 what might be described as a controversial progress so far. I have no intention of disrupting the somewhat comfortable apathy which has surrounded it. I saw with some amazement that it passed through all stages in another place in a very short time. The debates took up just over six columns of Hansard. That would seem to have set some sort of record, I would have thought.

Obviously, no one will oppose the Bill, although one or two of us may be slightly puzzled about why it has been brought in. I have said in the past, and I think most people would agree, that there is no particular merit in having uniformity of the law of England and of Scotland, at least on the technical level. I accept, however, and I have also argued, that in other circumstances, if we are talking about the social basis of law, prima facie there is a case for saying that there should be uniformity on either side of the Border. It would be regrettable if any great gulf existed between the laws of England and Scotland in that important field. But I am not convinced that a very strong case has been made by my hon. Friend for the Bill.

I have been a little—not alarmed, for that is far too over-strong a word—surprised at some of the doubts which seem to have emerged in explanations of why the Bill is necessary. In another place the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland, posing the question whether legal capacity to enter into binding contracts should be reduced from 21 to 18, said that:

I believe that young people today have as much ability, seriouspurpose, energy and talent as they have ever had. Let us not be misled by television or Press coverage relating to a small minority who deliberately seek publicity and give a different impression.

Last weekend I visited a large firm in Glasgow which for many years has operated a sound and successful training scheme. I saw there many apprentices at work, and I had discussions with them and with their instructors. Their instructors told me privately that the large majority of these young men were concentrating hard in a responsible way upon their work and upon their future. The five years' apprenticeship which had previously been the rule had been reduced, by agreement with the trade unions and others, to three years. But at the end of the three years, I was told, those young men are better trained and have more knowledge and skill than their predecessors. In other fields, too, young people in their late teens are accepting responsibility for their affairs and their future and are giving service to others.

I deplore criticism of young people based simply on the behaviour of a few, whether that behaviour involves violence, idleness or drugs. I find that most young people are working hard to equip themselves for life and at the same time are enjoying such leisure as they get.

Young people today will question what they are told. They are not prepared to accept dogma or convention. They wish to know the reason why. A logical explanation, coupled with advice, will be taken seriously and, in all probability, will be accepted by young people. It is not enough to be dogmatic or to tell them that they must do something simply because it has always been done. This is no reason to detract from parental responsibility at an earlier stage. I do not believe that this Bill, or similar measures, will give parents an excuse to opt out. Parental influence and responsibility are more important today than ever. In the early days simple discipline is required, but later, in their teens, young people expect explanations and advice.

Hon. Members have mentioned the matter of hooliganism, but this is certainly 34 not confined to young people. If the reasons for public order are explained, as well as the eventual effects upon the community and upon the young person himself, hooliganism will die out. Drugs are a cause of great anxiety to parents, but if it is explained that the taking of drugs involves the serious risk of being led on to really dangerous drugs, this will have much more effect in stopping young people from experimenting with preparations which may be represented by some people to be no worse than alcohol.

The Bill suggests minor changes in a sensible direction. What is important is our general attitude to young people in Scotland and the sort of encouragement which we give them to create a worthwhile future in Scotland. This is of immense importance not only to all of us in this Committee but to all age groups in Scotland.
