"So we are talking about an asset which is no longer an asset".—[OFFICIAL REPORT, First Scottish Standing Committee, 10th June, 1969; c. 156.]

I was replying to the hon. Member for Glasgow, Hillhead (Mr. Galbraith), but not referring to him personally. I was taking of the position of owner-occupiers and others who would be involved in these supplementary payments.

The Government's basic contention, which must be recognised as an important point of principle, is expressed in paragraphs 21 and 22 of the White Paper. Special consideration should be given to certain owner-occupiers because they have often committed their life savings to a property at a time of housing shortage. Some of these young couples get rehoused by a local authority, but they lose completely the purchase price of the tenement single end, or room and kitchen. If they were to get an appreciable supplementary payment, it might often be the 158 deposit on buying a house and I am sure that the hon. Gentleman would favour that.

But would it not be right and proper to extend this provision to commercial undertakings, and landlords are engaged in a business. As the hon. Member for Hillhead reminds me constantly, houses to rent ought to be run as a business and recognised as a business, not a bad business, but a good business providing a service. That being so, it would be an unusual principle in British law to seek to compensate the owner of a business for the deterioration of an asset.

In 1955, the Conservative Government introduced a special supplement in England for this limited purpose, but, for reasons which escape me, this was not done in Scotland. The best thing done in Scotland in this respect was in 1959 when the Conservative Government, again with this restriction to an owner-occupier, said that the minimum they would give would be the gross annual value of his house. Those of us who recollect the days before the 1961 revaluation will remember how small were valuations of houses at that time. They represented nothing like the purchase price even of a single end or a room and kitchen.

I put it to the hon. Gentleman that we cannot extend the provision in the Bill. His Government would not do so and I do not see why the present Government should take any further steps along that road.
