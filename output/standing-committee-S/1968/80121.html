The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Dr. J. Dickson Mabon): I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the Town and Country Planning (Scotland) Bill [Lords] in relation to the principle of the Bill.

May I preface my remarks by welcoming you to our proceedings today, Sir Beresford. As a fellow Scot we know that you will take a keen interest in what we are debating, both now and later, and I am certain that, knowing Scotsmen, you will make sure that we do not get involved in too many heated arguments.

It is a particular pleasure to me to introduce this Bill. My first love as a Minister was housing. I very soon found that housing programmes were doomed to frustration unless land use planning had been effectively done well in advance. With the passage of time I have found myself more and more deeply involved in land use matters all over Scotland and more and more convinced of the importance of us all asking the right land use questions at the right timeâ€”and if the question is addressed to me, of producing good answers based on systematic analysis.

This Bill is long and complex. Its main purpose, however, is simple: to enable the public to take a constructive part in the planning debate at each stage, while at the same time providing for local planning authorities and Ministers to proceed systematically from the general to the particular in development planning, and to distinguish strategy from tactics. The many detailed provisions Col. 6 tidying up existing procedures are subsidiary to this.

Before I go on to the specific provisions I think I should deal with two general points. An article in The Scotsman some time ago complained that this Bill followed too closely its English equivalent without enough regard for special Scottish conditions.

Mr. T. G. D. Galbraith: Hear, hear.

Dr. Dickson Mabon: Some hon. Members may sympathise with this view, as does the hon. Member for Glasgow, Hillhead (Mr. Galbraith). We must, of course, have a separate Scottish Bill. The previous administration in which the hon. Gentleman was an Under-Secretary of State for Scotland in 1959 were so indiscreet as to foist upon Scotland a Great Britain Bill, but were compelled to re-enact it in Scottish form, so that Scottish practitioners could conveniently handle and understand it. But there has never been a significant difference in planning legislation between England and Scotland. Administrations of both complexions since the war have, when legislating on planning, produced almost identical Measures for the two countries.

If there have been arguments during the long period since 1947 to the effect that the system set up by the Act of that year was wrong for Scotland, rather than merely becoming out of date for Great Britain, I have not heard them. On the other hand, if it has made sense during the last 21 years to have basically the same planning system operating in both Scotland and England, I am not clear what circumstances have arisen recently to suggest that the two countries should have quite different planning systems.

The truth is that essentially the same machinery is likely to be good for efficient planning in both Scotland and England unless we propose either to give radically different responsibilities to local planning authorities in one country as against another, or to vary the extent to which, and the manner in which, development is controlled in the two countries. I presume no one is going to suggest either of these things. I agree that there is scope for difference in detail. Hence a separate Scottish Bill. There will be room enough for debate about Image Col. 7 detail in Committee. The right approach is for use to join with our English colleagues in conceiving major changes. This was done in this case.

This Bill has its origin in a Report entitled "The Future of Development Plans" made by the Planning Advisory Group as long ago as 1965. The Group was drawn from both sides of the Borderâ€”of its 20 members four were from Scotlandâ€”and its Report was as relevant to Scotland as to England and did, in fact, arouse considerable interest in Scotland. I must acknowledge that, though the Group reported under the aegis of this Government, it was set up by the previous Administration, who presumably envisaged that its ultimate result would be closely similar legislation for Scotland and England.

We have wasted no time. We gave a reasonable period for consideration of the P.A.G. report, then from 1966 had it discussed exhaustively by a Joint Working Party drawn from the Scottish Development Department and the local authority Associations, and this led, amongst other things, to a general conference of local authorities in Edinburgh early in 1967. Perhaps I might be forgiven for remarking that on the occasion of that conference I referred to the need for legislation and predicted that new legislation would take about two years to prepare.

It has been argued that it is wrong to introduce a new planning system significantly altering the distribution of responsibility between the Secretary of State and local government in advance of local government reorganisation. This is referred to in an article in The Glasgow Herald this morning.

The Government do not agree. It is no secret that the introduction of the new development plan system envisaged in this Bill will be gradual. I do agree that it will very probably not be completed till local government reorganisation takes place. But should this time be lost? This Bill is adaptable to a variety of local government structures. There are certainly authorities already existing who could usefully apply its provisions, and there is no merit in holding up for several years new style plans in areas where they can Col. 8 be effectively prepared soon, and are urgently needed. Among other things, this will give us valuable experience of the administrative working of the new system, before the start of the massive task of local government reorganisation. If anyone is interested in a practical example, I can think of no better one than the meetings which we have had in Lanarkshire, and the decisions announced on Friday by myself on behalf of the Government in relation to them.

I apologise for this rather long introduction, but I felt it was necessary to set the Bill in its background. I turn now to the detailed content of the Bill.

Part I, Clauses 1 to 14, is, of course, the most important part. It embodies the fundamental change proposed in the planning system, namely, the replacement of the old style development plan by two new types of plan. Those are, first, a structure plan, which will consist of a written statement with appropriate illustrations, and which will set forth policy and proposals; and second, local plans, which will roughly resemble current development plans in form.

Only the structure plan will normally require to be submitted to the Secretary of State for his approval. Local plans will, after new processes of public inquiry into objections on the same lines as obtains at present, be adopted by the local planning authority without reference to the Secretary of State unless that is specially called for.

Some concern has been expressed about the principle of giving of power to local authorities to make and adopt their own plans. It has been thought that this is tantamount to making the planning authority judge and jury in its own cause. To some extent this difficulty is unavoidable in policy matters. Sometimes the Secretary of State finds himself in this position. It may be argued that that happens only rarely and is inevitable, and that there is no need to add instances of public authorities judging their own case by putting local planning authorities in this position when the Secretary of State is available to act as appellate authority. This argument has to be put in the context of up-to-date views on the distribution of responsibility between the Secretary of State and local government and Image Col. 9 on the delays, sometimes very considerable and to the detriment of the local public, experienced in the operating of the present planning machinery.

We cannot expect to maintain the repute and value of local authority work, or to achieve expeditious planning if the Secretary of State is to be involved in every element of every development plan no matter how minute. Let us remember that local planning authorities will not be operating in a vacuum. They can prepare local plans only within the framework of a structure plan which will have been approved by the Secretary of State, and if there are signs that a particular local authority is disregarding that structure plan in framing its local plans, then the Secretary of State can call in these local plans for his own attention.

Further, the Bill contains ample, and, indeed, substantially more, effective safeguards for the rights of the public than exist under the present dispensation. In particular, the important provisions in Clauses 3 and 7, which require publicity to be given to plans at an early stage and enable requirements to be laid down to ensure the participation of the public in the formation of development plans, will ensure that public opinion can be brought to bear on plans from the earliest possible stage. Under the new system there will be no place for the preparation of plans behind closed doors, leading to situations in which either a virtual fait accompli must be accepted, or major rearguard actions have to be fought with all that means in terms of expense and delay.

And if, after all this, representations made against a plan are not withdrawn there must be a public inquiry held by a person either appointed by the Secretary of State or appointed in accordance with requirements laid down by the Secretary of State. As a final safeguard it will always be open to the Secretary of State to call in a local plan for his own decision if for any reasons at all he thinks it expedient to do so.

Part II of the Bill deals with the enforcement of planning control. The existing provision for enforcement in Scotland allows an unauthorised use of land to be made the subject of enforcement action at any time up to two years Col. 10 after its discovery. After the two years have elapsed it becomes immune. For a variety of reasons this provision has not worked very successfully, and what is proposed in the Bill is that in future this immunity will be abolished except in a limited number of special cases. In these exceptional cases the immunity will be gained not two years after discovery but four years after inception of the use; this we think is a more practical form of control.

Other charges in this Part of the Bill include the institution of certificates of established use to deal with cases of possible uncertainty created by the fact that enforcement will now be possible for an indefinite period; the replacement of the sheriff by the Secretary of State as appellate authority for enforcement; and provision for the issue of what are called "stop notices" to enable the local authority to require constructional work to cease on unauthorised development so as to prevent the offence from being made more difficult to rectify while the issues are being threshed out.

Part III of the Bill provides new machinery for dealing with planning appeals. In brief, the Secretary of State is empowered to delegate the right of deciding appeals of classes which are to be specified by him to persons he appoints for this purpose. This is not a power that we foresee any need to use at an early date in Scotland. The number of appeals decisions averages only about 200 a year, and it has been found convenient and practical to deal with this small volume with the help of a panel of part-time reporters, whose work is, I think, highly respected.

The delegation envisaged in the Bill would require the creation in Scotland of a full-time trained body of inspectors such as already exists in England. At present we do not see a justification for this, but it is desirable to provide the power of delegation so that, if there is a sharp increase in the numbers of planning appeals of a routine character, there will be available a means of dealing with these expeditiously.

Part IV of the Bill is concerned with land transactions. The main point here is that the designation of land in development plans as being subject to compulsory acquisition is abolished. This Image Col. 11 particular provision has never proved of real value. In practice it has merely acted as an extra piece of administration added to the already complex procedures attending upon compulsory purchase. The planning purposes for which land may be compulsorily acquired are restated in Clause 28 to accord with the new form and objectives of development plans.

This Part of the Bill also makes good an undertaking given to local authorities at the time of the Land Commission Act, 1967, by extending to them the general vesting declaration procedure available to the Land Commission. It also adjusts the provisions for the purchase of land blighted by planning proposals to take account of the new development plan system.

Part V of the Bill makes new provision for the protection and preservation of buildings of special architectural or historic interest. The main change contemplated is that in future the listing of buildings will have the effect of requiring express consent for the demolition, alteration or extension of the buildings in question. It is as if a blanket building preservation order were being applied to all listed buildings, and perhaps one or two hon. Members may be satisfied with that proposal.

Another valuable provision in this Part of the Billâ€”in Clause 52â€”is the power to restrict compensation payable on the compulsory acquisition of a listed building if it can be shown that the building has been deliberately allowed to fall into disrepair because the owner had his eye on the redevlopment of the site.

I should also mention a change which we are proposing in the procedure for statutory listing of buildings. At present it is a requirement that the lists should be recorded in the Register of Sasines. This requirement has seriously hampered progress and it is being repealed in Schedule 10 to the Bill. In future the listing will be effected through lists deposited with local planning authorities. Col. 12 Part VI of the Bill includes a large number of miscellaneous and desirable provisions on which I need not, perhaps, dwell for too long.

Clauses 60 to 62 deal with the appointment of Planning Inquiry Commissions to enable proposed developments of exceptional importance or presenting very special and unusual features to be considered more exhaustively than is possible under the present system.

Clause 63 authorises local planning authorities to delegate certain of their functions to their officials. This provision results from a management study of development control which revealed that many planning decisions were substantively reached by officials, without harm, and that a lot of time could be saved by recognising this. The degree to which delegation is given and the conditions to which it is subject are in the hands of the local authority itself and the Clause includes ample safeguards against abuse.

Clauses 64 to 67 impose on all existing and future planning permission a five-year time limit causing the permission to expire if it has not been used. The purpose of this is to avoid the build-up of unused planning permission which can falsify the whole development plan picture and act as a deterrent to granting permission for development proposals.

Clause 68 abolishes the payment of compensation in many circumstances for statutory undertakers if their development of land is refused or subjected to conditions.

Clause 75 calls for the posting of notices on land in the case of planning applications for those types of development which experience has shown to be the most likely to be controversial.

It enjoys, I believe, the general support of local authority and professional planning opinion in Scotland. In mentioning that I would not wish to suggest that we are by any means opposed to suggestions for its improvement. On the contrary, the Government have themselves Amendments in mind, some of them in the light of representations received since the Scottish Bill was published, and we shall keep Image Col. 13 an open mind towards any further suggestions for its improvement that may be made.

Friends and hon. Gentlemen opposite may make. As I say, we shall keep an open mind towards any further suggestions for the improvement of the Bill that may be made either now or in Committee. This is essentially another modernisation Measure from a great modernising Government. I ask the Committee this morning to approve its Second Reading.
