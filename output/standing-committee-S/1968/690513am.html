Clause 38
RESTRICTION ON EXERCISE BY PUBLIC AUTHORITIES OF POWER OF DISPOSING OF LAND

Question proposed [ 8th May], That the Clause stand part of the Bill.

Question again proposed.

Mr. Gordon Campbell
: At the end of the last meeting I was trying to find out what were the intentions of the Government and to what extent this Clause would mean that approval would be needed from the Secretary of State for the sale of council houses. The Minister will now have had an opportunity of considering carefully the point which I was raising.

I can summarise it in this way for the Minister, by putting three questions to him. First, does he agree that at present there are situations in which local authorities can quite correctly sell houses without seeking the approval of the Secretary of State—for example, where such houses are new, have not been approved for housing needs, and have not been subsidised?

Secondly, does this Clause now alter that situation and require the local authority to ask for the Secretary of State's approval whenever any house is proposed to be sold?

Thirdly, if that is the effect of the Clause, why are the Government seeking now to cover these houses for which at present there is no need for the Secretary 440 of State's permission to be given because they have not attracted subsidy and have not been used for housing needs?

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Dr. J. Dickson Mabon)
: I should like to follow the hon. Gentleman in his three questions. The first question is in relation to houses built without subsidy or the promise of a subsidy—I think that distinction is important, too—and whether they can be sold without the consent of the Secretary of State. The answer is "yes". It has always been the case and this Clause does not seek to change that. Any house on which a subsidy has been paid is within this Clause and within previous legislation. This applies also to houses where there has been a promise of payment, that is to say, even before the first payment of subsidy has been made, the Secretary of State has the right of certain powers, which are tightened up in this Clause, over sanction to sell.

So the answer to these questions is that if there is a house which has been built by a local authority without subsidy or promise of subsidy—and in the case of the example given about the house in this circumstance and which was replaced because of road improvements—the answer would be that no sanction would be needed. Does this Clause alter the situation regarding these houses? The answer is "No". It alters only the situation regarding houses subsidised or having been promised a subsidy. Therefore, the third question does not arise.

Mr. G. Campbell
: I am grateful that the Minister has now given an explanation in answer to the points which I raised at the last meeting. I shall not open up the whole question of sale of council houses in appropriate circumstances. I made a fleeting reference to that at our last meeting. I am glad that the Minister has made it clear that the position is not changed where local authorities at present can sell without asking the permission of the Secretary of State.

I am sorry that he should think it necessary to stipulate that the Secretary of State's approval should be sought where there is a promise of a subsidy and even where no subsidy is to be used. There clearly could be a case where when a house was first being built the local authority thought that it would be for JPEG 441 housing needs, but then decided that it would not be required for housing needs, and did not ask for a subsidy for it at all; it seems unnecessary to cover that case.

It is satisfactory to find that the Government are not trying to alter the position entirely so that the permission of the Secretary of State has to be asked in every case. We believe that it should not be necessary for local authorities to have to go to the Secretary of State all the time on these matters, but the Minister's clarification shows that he is not trying to be doctrinaire in this case.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause ordered to stand part of the Bill.
