EDUCATION (SCOTLAND) BILL

Clause 11
HANDICAPPED CHILDREN

Question proposed [ This day], That the Clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

4.0 p.m.

Question again proposed.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Bruce Millan)
: I had spoken about teacher supply in special education and had dealt with points which hon. Gentlemen had made on that aspect which are relevant to the Clause. Perhaps I could now say something about accommodation. In the first instance, if we are thinking of accommodation for children at special schools—that is, the over-fives—then, generally speaking, we can say that the position in Scotland is satisfactory. There may be areas in which the accommodation is hardly adequate. There may be cases in which the buildings are not as good as they could be. The same kind of criticism, of course, might apply to ordinary schools. But, in terms of the total number of places available, I think that the situation is reasonably satisfactory. The figures were given in answer to the hon. Member for Dumfries (Mr. Monro) on Monday of this week.

These places, of course, may be at special schools in separate establishments, and they may be in special classes in other schools. The position varies from one area to another, partly depending on the number of children involved in the education authority area. In areas in which the numbers are small, one very often finds attachment to an ordinary school; in other areas, one finds special accommodation separate from the ordinary school provision. Generally 530 speaking, we can say that the position is satisfactory in respect of day schools.

It is very difficult to make a general statement about residential accommodation. I would certainly not accept that the general position is unsatisfactory, but, of course, there are difficulties both with particular types of disability, which may affect only small numbers of children, and with areas where, because of the sparsity of the population, the numbers of children likely to be involved in special education as a whole are fairly small. In these areas there is sometimes a difficulty in providing adequate provision without residential accommodation, and in residential accommodation there is sometimes a difficulty in getting sufficient children involved by combining areas, for example, to build up a viable school.

Looking at the situation of the country as a whole, and taking account of the grant-aided special schools, the position is certainly improving. I do not feel that the position, generally speaking, can be described as unsatisfactory.

None of these problems is easy, but the provision is certainly improving and is much better than it was a few years ago. However, taking Scotland as a whole, we still have a long way to go before we can feel satisfied that we are making adequate provision for all the children in these various categories.

I turn from the children who are within the authority of the education authority to the children who may be reported to the local authority as a social work authority, as it will be under the Act. The number who are reported as unsuitable for either education or training has been running at an average of about 170 per year over the last few years. At present the local health authorities and the voluntary bodies between them provide places in day centres for about 330 children. That is not adequate provision by any standards, but it is no simple proposition to relate that figure to the figure which I gave earlier of the number of children who are reported annually.

To begin with, some of the children, unfortunately, go into hospital and at present are not provided for by the local health authority. Some of them are so badly handicapped that it is difficult to make any kind of provision for them outside hospital. In some cases one must take account of the wishes of parents who may not necessarily want the particular day centre which is provided by the local health authority. It is not simply a question of relating the two figures. But we can say that this is a field in which, under, I hope, the stimulus of the social work departments which will be established later this year, additional provision ought to be made. I hope that that will happen.

I am sorry that I cannot make any kind of general pronouncement which would apply all over Scotland. The position varies from one education authority to another, and the way in which authorities meet the problems which they have at present varies not only according to their enthusiasm for meeting these problems and their willingness to meet 532 them, but also according to the circumstances of their area. What may prove to be right and workable in scattered rural areas, for example, would not be right and workable in a bigger urban area.

Having made all these qualifications, I will sum up by saying that the position is not yet completely satisfactory but has certainly improved. I expect that the implementation of this Clause and the extended powers of ascertainment which are given to local education authorities will make the position rather better, and more quickly better, than is being achieved at present.

A further point concerns special education for the under-fives. It is possible for education authorities, under existing powers, to provide nursery education for the under-fives. The extent to which that ought to be provided depends a good deal on the kind of child with which one is dealing, but if one takes the deaf child, or the partially deaf child, for example, and particularly the wholly deaf child, one recognises that it is important to get nursery education going as quickly as possible. In relation to the total number of deaf children a large proportion are catered for before the age of five—at the age of three and the age of four. That is an example of the authorities already, under present legislation, providing under-five special nursery education, and I expect that provision again to be improved by the new powers which we are giving to authorities under the Clause.
