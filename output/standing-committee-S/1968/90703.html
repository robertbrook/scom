When we are talking about staffing standards it is as well to be clear about the present position. Sometimes the impression is given that virtually every primary class in Scotland has about 45 pupils. This is very far from the truth. The average size of primary classes in Image Col. 63 Scotland at January, 1968, was just under 33 pupils, and if one leaves out the smaller schools with four teachers or fewer and looks only at the large ones where 90 per cent. of our pupils are educated the average size of class was then just under 35, so that the overall position was that not all classes were around the 45 mark. Just as there are geographical discrepancies in secondary education, the same applies in primary education, and the problem of classes with more than 45 pupils is largely confined to one geographical area and to the authorities of Glasgow and Lanarkshire, which have two-thirds of the primary classes over 40. As I see it, the first step is to try to ensure by every possible means at our disposal that as many as possible of the additional teachers becoming available go to the areas where they are most needed, that is to say, where over-sized classes are most prevalent.

Until the present time we have been planning new schools on the basis of a maximum class size of 45. The authorities generally, I know, are anxious to reduce the size of classes, and many have for years been organising their schools on a basis of classes considerably smaller than the statutory maximum, where they have been able to. We have now decided to ask all education authorities, unless there are exceptional circumstances—and these are likely to be geographical rather than staffing difficulties—to plan new schools on the assumption of a maximum class size of 40. The maximum class size of 45 will no longer be used for the planning of new schools, and many local authorities may continue to plan on a class size of less than 40. This is a considerable step forward. It only applies to new building, and the problem there is comparatively simple.

But now we come to the existing schools and what is required to bring the class size from a maximum of 45 down to 40. Then we have something which is much more complicated to deal with. In some cases this may involve reorganising catchment areas; in some cases it may be necessary to extend the schools by adding Col. 64 some classrooms; and in other cases it may even be necessary to build entirely new schools. If one has a single-stream primary school with classes of around 45 it may not be possible to bring them down to 40 in a simple way because there is no accommodation available and one has to look at the additional accommodation and the readjustment of zoning areas to enable one to do that. The problem in existing schools is a good deal more complicated, and we have to consider providing new schools built to a standard of classes of 40 pupils and whether we should alter the Regulations covering the maximum class size in schools generally by reducing them from 45 to 40.

This is an attractive proposal, but I am not sure that it is realistic to carry it out at this time for the reason I have mentioned, of what is likely to happen at the schools.
