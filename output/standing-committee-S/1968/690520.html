Mr. Buchanan-Smith
: Both are probing Amendments. We should like the Government to give us some indication of how they see these subsections working. Under subsection (1) a duty is laid upon a local authority to close, demolish, or bring up to standard those houses which are now not up to a tolerable standard as defined in Clause 2.

Subsection (2), to which my first Amendment relates, says that in carrying out their duty the local authority must, within such period as is reasonable, take account of the need for alternative accommodation. On Second Reading many Members expressed concern about the physical and human nature of the problem of housing people displaced while their own houses were being brought up to standard. It happens obviously where a house is demolished, but not so obviously where a house is to be improved.

Many instances have been given, for example in tenements, where in altering three houses in order to make two, not only has one to make sure that during the period of alteration and improvement the tenants in all three houses are found accommodation, but that after reconstruction the family of the third house is found accommodation elsewhere. Obviously, where there is demolition, or a family is displaced, accommodation of a more permanent nature has to be found. Where a local authority is concerned, it has an obligation to find accommodation for such people through its housing list.

Dr. Mabon
: The noble Lord the Member for Edinburgh, North (Earl of Dalkeith) asked me what was preventing an even greater number of houses being built than at the present time, when we have record figures. One wants to look at that matter in terms of four serious factors. Land is an additional factor, but that is a matter of planning, and the rest.

The first factor is money. While the subsidies have been improved considerably, there are still strains on the Exchequer and on local authorities to finance these programmes, though not as substantial as was the case some time ago. But I think that we are getting near the right figure for the allocation of money for expenditure on housing that we have authorised so far. Some may say, "Not enough". If so, I am glad: I would prefer that to people saying "Too much". One public expenditure which we ought not to cut is housing. That is exemplified by this discussion.

The second factor is manpower. To some extent it is true that we cannot build houses in the old style with the present labour force. Therefore, we have adopted industrialised building. To date, 33 per cent., or even a higher percentage of all new houses approved by the Department are built by industrialised methods. That is not only an economy of labour but can be an economy of time.

The third factor is materials. We have largely overcome difficulties here, although from time to time we do have shortages of this item and that item. That is a matter of organisation.

The fourth factor is method. Within method, I would argue that there must be sense of purpose. Even a good authority with a good sense of purpose cannot achieve all that it wants to achieve if it has bad methods. Conversely, too, the best organised authority which does not have a sense of purpose will not be able to achieve a high programme.

I make this last point particularly, because if we took away subsection (2), we would make a nonsense of subsection (1). That would not apply in every case, I quite agree, but subsection (1) would be given a meaning of comprehensiveness and immediacy that in many cases would be absurd. It would mean that whenever we did a survey we would promptly have to rehouse everybody in houses that were to be demolished, or switch them out into 12 temporary accommodation in order to improve the accommodation. Gentleman's constituency as to show that there are rural areas or comparatively rural areas where these problems are quite serious.
