Mr. Gordon Campbell: This morning we are to consider a subject which is of continuing concern in Scotland, and rightly so; that is, the subject of crime and punishment. I am one of those who are especially concerned about this, not only in relation to crimes of violence which have received a considerable amount of publicity in recent years, but also in relation to the other kinds of crimes and to their total in Scotland. Like others, I have been much disturbed by the numbers of crimes of all kinds which have been carried out without the detection, and, indeed, without the conviction of the culprits. In the last week we have seen reports in the Press indicating the welcome news, which I hope the Minister will be able to confirm today, that the total number of crimes known in Scotland in 1968 was rather less than in the previous year. We greet this information with considerable relief, but, in view of the unfortunate increases in the numbers of crimes in previous years, we can only say that we are starting to contain the situation. I should like to put a point to the Government with regard to the use—I hope the increasing use—of equipment and aids by the police to help them detect crime and bring criminals to justice. I put a Question to the Government in recent weeks, asking them what use is being made in Scotland of closed circuit television. We all know that this has had a deterrent effect in places like large self-service stores where there may have been temptations to some to serve themselves without making the required payment. Television has had considerable success there. But my point is really a wider one. If we can augment the ordinary activities of the police by aids which, in the technological age, should be becoming increasingly available, we can give the potential criminal a feeling that he does not know when he is being watched or recorded. Thus there can be a psychological effect, because such a person is not merely watching the policeman on his beat; he does not know what else is being used to record, photograph or in other ways detect what he is doing. I am not suggesting that the citizen's privacy should be invaded. Such devices should be used only in public places or in places where somebody should not be; for example, in the vaults of a bank at midnight, or somewhere like that. But I believe that more can be done, and that the results can be psychological as much as practical. The admissibility of such evidence in courts is another matter which will have to be investigated at the same time, but I do not believe that there can be great difficulties in this technological age. How can we encourage and reinforce this trend? We tend to think of the police as crime investigators and detectors. I also like to think of them as part of the machinery which prevents crime from being committed. They can only be part of the machinery. Another part of it is the public themselves, and success in crime prevention must depend on cooperation between police and the public. The struggle is not one of the police versus the criminal but of the community versus the criminal. A good deal of crime is caused by the negligence of the citizens. The police know that if we took better precautions the crime would not have occurred. We have in each force a crime prevention officer who pays regular visits to particularly vulnerable premises and advises occupiers how to make them more secure, and all forces undertake propaganda aimed at making the community more security conscious.

There have been two fresh initiatives in this field that are worth special mention.

Col. 208 The first is the development in the four cities and other areas of a system of crime prevention panels consisting of representatives of local voluntary and business organisations with an interest in security and crime prevention. We had local campaigns in Dundee, Glasgow and Lanarkshire, and the Scottish Chief Constables, with the assistance of the Scottish Information Office, will be mounting a national crime prevention campaign in the autumn of this year. These are all important developments which will contribute to public cooperation with the police.

There is another important aspect of police crime prevention—the presence of the police. The new systems of policing will contribute to crime prevention in more than one way. They make the most effective use of manpower by emphasising the police presence in the area in which they are operating, and I believe will continue to promote better relations with the public. They give an impressively quick response to incidents and they improve the flow of information in the police service itself.
