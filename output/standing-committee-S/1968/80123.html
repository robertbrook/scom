TOWN AND COUNTRY PLANNING (SCOTLAND) BILL [Lords]

Question proposed [21st January],

Question again proposed.

The Chairman: I am grateful to hon. Members for their co-operation at our last Sitting in keeping their contributions so brief and to the point. I have quite a long list of would-be speakers today, so I hope that there will be the same spirit of Scottish reasonableness again.

Mr. Alex Eadie: When we rose on Tuesday I was making some comment on the proposed procedure in Clause 21, Part III of the Bill. My point was that one can sympathise with the Scottish Development Department's methods to relieve itself of the burden of dealing with decisions on appeal. This is usually between the date of submission of an appeal and the date fixed for the inquiry, which is normally a period of two or three months. Thereafter, several more months elapse before the final decision is issued by the Department to the appellant and to the local planning authority.

I do not know whether it is the Department's intention to restrict appeals to cases involving minor development only. Even if that is the first intention, I think that, once started, the practice will be to pass more and more to these independent reporters. Nevertheless, it appears to be much better to adhere to the present practice where all reporters' recommendations are submitted to the central office of the Department, where they can be considered in relation to national, regional and area policies and any known future proposals, and also related to previous decisions on appeals concerning identical or very similar types of development. Col. 54 With the proposed procedure, the independent reporter is not likely to be able to evaluate these factors, with which the Department's officials are familiar in their day-to-day work. The reporters may well reach decisions which conflict with these other factors. Furthermore, it will be more difficult to achieve uniformity than at present if the reporters reach different decisions on dealing with appeals relating to identical or similar proposed developments. It may well happen that a reporter would uphold an appeal for one area, and that another reporter would dismiss an appeal on very similar circumstances for another area, thereby resulting in unfairness to individual appellants.

I want to comment on Clause 77, which deals with the local register of planning applications. The present procedure under Section 12(5) of the Town and Country Planning (Scotland) Act, 1947, is that every local planning authority has to keep a register of applications for planning permission as described in paragraph 12 of the Town and Country Planning (General Development) (Scotland) Order, 1950.

The register has to contain particulars of the application including the name and address of the applicant, the date of receipt by the local planning authority, a brief description of the development, the decision of the planning authority, and details of any appeal and final decision. Section 12(5) does not authorise anyone other than the applicant to see the application form and plans submitted with the application form, but only the relative entry in the register.

The proposed procedure under Clause 77 is for provision to be made by a development order of the Secretary of State for the register of planning applications to be kept in two or more parts, each part containing such information as may be prescribed by the order. The order may also make provision

"(a) for a specified part of the register to contain copies of applications and any plans or drawings submitted therewith".

It is difficult to envisage any type of register of which copies of the plans and drawings are to form part. Many of the plans received with applications are far too unwieldy in size to form part of any such register and, furthermore, in many cases the application is accompanied by a large roll of anything from 15 to 25 Image Col. 55 plans, making it impossible to keep them in a pocket in the register or keep them in any handy form with the register.

In those circumstances, should not the present type of register be maintained? If it is intended that a member of the public, a local elector, or any prescribed class of person entitled to see the plans, should call at the planning department, the application form and plans can be exhibited to him over a public counter in the planning department. It would be preferable for these plans to be shown in the planning department so that questions on technical matters can be answered. In the case of Midlothian, the procedure is to pass an application from the County Clerk's office, after recording in the register, to the planning department. Then it goes to the County Surveyor's department and then to the County Engineer's department before being returned to the County Clerk's office, after which it is submitted to the first available meeting of the Planning Committee or Planning Sub-Committee.

The proposal to make plans available for public inspection involves the taking up of considerable time in the planning department, with people calling in to see plans, asking questions, and presumably also taking tracings if they so desire.

Another point has been suggested by a member of the planning department's staff for Midlothian, namely, that, where an application is current and available for inspection by the general public or even by adjoining owners, it is possible that another architect can ask to see the plans and then appropriate the design for his own use. This is a matter of considerable concern to firms of private architects. Naturally they prefer to safeguard their designs until the proposed building has been completed. In this connection, it is true that architects for adjoining owners are entitled to see detailed plans submitted with a petition for a building warrant to the building authority in the counties and to the Dean of Guild Courts in cities and burghs, but notice of such applications is limited to the adjoining owners, which restricts the number of people and, consequently, architects who are made aware of the petition.

I have the honour to represent a constituency containing one of the most superb examples of the artistic genius of man in laying out cities. I therefore consider it one of my duties, in concert with others, to act as a guardian of the priceless heritage of Edinburgh in the New Town. Thanks to the controversy the years before last over the ring road, many of the Image Col. 59 good citizens of Edinburgh have become active in taking a greater interest in their environment. Many more groups, street associations and others are being formed and more activities developed in existing societies, the Georgian Society and the like, and this is very healthy. If nothing else worthwhile came out of the ring road proposal, at least many people were woken up to take the right kind of notice.

One sad feature of past planning procedures is that, although classification of buildings by listing has gone ahead with commendable speed and meticulous care, to the extent that almost anything worthwhile has been provisionally listed, there has been an almost complete lack of logical follow-up with preservation orders where they are needed. My satisfaction at hearing the Minister of State construe Part V as a "blanket preservation order" provision was very great, but we will have to probe hard in Committee to satisfy ourselves that this is so. The reason I say that is that listing of buildings up to now has been mainly provisional, and statutory listing is likely to take a considerable time, because of the intricacies of Scottish law. It may be twenty years or more before all the buildings we want to see statutorily listed are in fact so listed. I am not certain that Part V will cover buildings which are only provisionally listed. If not, I still have many anxieties about the Bill's achievement in this respect.

Provisional listing by itself is absurd because the owner is not necessarily informed that his house is "listed", and so does not know that he has to apply for permission to change or demolish it. Some formula must be found so that owners at least are notified of provisional listing.

I sometimes wonder whether we take enough interest in the practice of other countries. France has similar problems in preservation and we could learn more from the mistakes and successes of other countries. About a year ago, I sent the Minister of State an interesting pamphlet produced by the French Embassy about the preservation of historic monuments and I hope that he has been able to incorporate some of its suggestions in the Bill.

Col. 60 I appeal to all those societies and bodies concerned with historic buildings to speak up now if they have any practical suggestions for improving the Bill. Past efforts to save individual buildings have often come too late, but we now have a chance to produce meaningful machinery for preservation, and this is the time to get it right. I therefore implore all those bodies based in my constituency or concerned with the New Town of Edinburgh to study this Part of the Bill with the greatest care and let us have their views as soon as possible. If we miss this chance, we may have to wait very many years before there is another opportunity to produce the right legislation, by which time many of the buildings which we want to preserve will be gone.

The acid test of whether the Bill will succeed is whether it will encourage local authorities to do the right things more speedily and efficiently, while at the same time safeguarding individual rights and freedoms. To the extent that these two divergent aims are reconcilable, the Committee stage will present us with a formidable challenge.

The Bill is good in that it is well intentioned. We all sympathise with what it is trying to do. However, in planning law there is always a paradox. On the one hand, one tries to streamline procedures and speed up cumbersome machinery and, on the other, one needs to ensure that individual rights are not infringed and that essential safeguards are not eliminated in the interests of administrative expediency. The White Paper eloquently laid out that paradox, which is not easy to escape.

He had tried to lodge his objection by way of what one might call the "layman's common sense" letter, but had been told that this would not do. He was left with a week in which to find a lawyer who would hurry together formal objections, all 30 copies, which would get him into the field when the decision was ultimately taken. We should consider whether the Bill will simplify the procedures and thus help ordinary persons to make their views heard and get their objections, which may not be on a major scale but are important to the individuals concerned, through to those ultimately taking the decisions.
