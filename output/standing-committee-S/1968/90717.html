Mr. Alick Buchanan-Smith
: I hope that in this rather over-heated and feverish atmosphere of London at present at least in our debate this morning we may find some refreshment in discussing tourism, although we ourselves may not be able to enjoy what it offers us. The only reflection I can make is that if this good weather continues it will probably do more for the tourist industry in Scotland than all the speeches in Scottish Grand Committee. One hopes that the good weather continues for Glasgow Trades Holiday starting this weekend.

This morning I wish to discuss the tourist industry and tourism in general not only in its context as one of our major earners of foreign exchange and also the vital power it has for economic development in certain areas of Scotland, both of which things are undoubtedly true. I want also to look at it particularly in relation to the countryside as a whole. I begin by putting forward two sets of facts which I think will help us in trying to put our discussions on tourism and the countryside into proper perspective.

It is a very sobering thought, when we look at the figures for population towards the end of this century, to realise that, at the moment, the United Kingdom has a population of about 55½ million and it is projected that by the end of the century that will go up to something over 68 million, an increase in this crowded island of 13 million. Scotland, with a population of about 5.2 million at present, is expected to have by 1988, the latest date for which official estimates have been made, a population of 5.5 million. Scotland is sharing in the growth of population, but 250 what also concerns me is that when one looks at the map of Scotland one sees in particular areas very great increases in population. In the Tayside area, for example, it is projected that the population will rise by over 75,000, in Greater Glasgow it will rise by 78,000 and in Greater Edinburgh by 150,000 over present population figures.

The second fact which we also have to remember is that against this background of rising population we are living at a time when much of our land and open spaces are being lost for development purposes. In Scotland at the moment over 5,000 acres a year are being swallowed up for development purposes.

These two facts, the increase in population and the amount of open space being swallowed up every year for development, demonstrate two things. They demonstrate that we have to try to develop opportunities for recreation and tourism, not only for tourists who come to Scotland from outside Britain but also for our own expanding population. This is one of the most important facts to bear in mind. We must also remember that this is a population with an increasing amount of leisure.

We must remember that in this congested island open spaces are no longer unlimited. It is utterly essential that we should keep a balance, between urban or industrial development and the countryside; because in our open spaces and our scenic beauty there is no doubt that Scotland has a priceless asset which we must nurture and not squander. We have to make absolutely certain—here I refer to debates we have had recently in the House—that industrial development, which we all want for Scotland, is properly sited, not piecemeal or on ribbon development lines. Nowhere is this more important than in the Clyde Estuary.

In this context we look forward to hearing from the Minister of State that the Countryside Commission will have a very useful and positive planning function. Already, in the Report of the Scottish Development Department we have read something of the work the Commission has done in the general task of preparing and agreeing maps for Scotland designating countryside areas. I hope that its work will go beyond that general work and that the Commission will also participate in the specific work of planning, particularly where industrial and urban development JPEG 251 is spilling over into countryside areas. The Countryside Commission must be brought in not only by local planning authorities but by all those Government bodies and other organisations concerned with development in the countryside.

I mention in passing a very unfortunate omission recently by the War Department in relation to extension of the ranges on the Pentland Hills just outside Edinburgh next door to a very heavily populated area. Only at a late stage was the Countryside Commission brought in. It should have been brought in at a very early stage in all planning decisions which affect population and recreation of our people in Scotland.

I find it very difficult to judge the progress of the Commission, and I certainly reserve judgment on it, because we have not yet had the first annual report and it would not be fair to judge what it is doing without knowing more about it. We appreciate reasons for financial stringency of which the Commission is a victim, but when we look at the Estimates we see in Appendix IV that of the budget for the Countryside Commission administration expenses take up £65,000 of the estimated cost against £20,000 for its positive work of research development projects and grants and loans. There may be reasons in these early stages why such a heavy proportion of its expenditure is weighted in the direction of administration. It is important that the administration should be well founded, but I hope looking ahead that this body will be given the financial resources sufficient to do the job which Parliament has given to it and to carry out projects of a positive nature.

A particular example where its work would be especially useful is in relation to the provision of caravan and camping sites. This is one part of financial help which local authorities are able to give through general local authority finances. This provision is one matter in which more positive action is needed. This is not covered in any way by the Development of Tourism Bill. That is why it is important that we should have a central body in Scotland to push this matter forward. Particular reference is made in the Annual Report of the Highland and Islands Development Board to it. The section of the Report on tourism is an outstanding piece of imaginative thinking for positive action. I recommend hon. Members to 252 read that section if they have not already done so. A particular point which the Highlands and Islands Development Board makes in its Report is the vital need to provide more caravan and camping sites and at the same time to bring them up to a very much higher standard. Particularly in country areas where there is scope for developing this kind of tourist accommodation the Countryside Commission is in a unique position, among all tourist bodies in Scotland, to help in providing finance and guidance to this form of development.

The second area where I think there is tremendous scope for development is in the Deeside area. Here I refer to the excellent report produced by Professor Walton and his team at Aberdeen university entitled "The Royal Grampian Country", which must be one of the most comprehensive reports on a specific area of Scotland in relation to tourism ever produced. In other words, we must not only provide to potential tourists a much wider choice of area to which to go, which will automatically increase the volume of tourists coming to Scotland, but within those areas we must give those who do come a much wider choice of activity throughout the year. This in itself will help those who are working in tourism much better to take advantage of what is offered to them.

If we are to widen our market for tourism with wider areas and wider activities, one of the things of which we must make absolutely certain is that there is adequate access to the tourist areas. I believe that in our road programme and what we do to develop and improve roads in various areas we can provide the biggest single boost to the development of tourism. The authors of "The Royal Grampian Country" carried out a survey in that area of Scotland which showed that 93 per cent. of the visitors came by car. This shows how important roads are in bringing people to a potential tourist area. In an area such as Deeside I am sure that the hon. Member for Aberdeenshire, West will agree that the roads are of good standard in relation to other areas of 256 Scotland. Of those making adverse comments, 77 per cent. complained about the roads. This should be a guide to us. The more we can do to assist the development of roads in the areas the more we shall open up the areas to tourism and the more we shall help tourism in those areas. We must go beyond the improvement of existing roads. We must have a development of new roads and new routes, as has been done on the west coast of Scotland in the Glenfeshie to Geldie road.

I turn now to the way in which regional and local tourist associations might be encouraged to develop. The functions of a regional organisation might best be described as the organisation of information services for visitors, the "servicing" of tourists by providing, for example, booking services at information centres, the development of the image of a particular area with the literature required for this purpose and the identification to the Central Board of the potential and requirements of a particular area. Local organisations should essentially be the continuance of the tradition which we have in Scotland of active and lively "resort" tourist bodies which in future might be encouraged to concentrate their efforts on the arrangement of events and activities for tourists to take part in and the provision of information about "what's on" and where to stay in the locality.

Working out of our individual parts in this exercise is very important. Promotional publications would best be planned and financed at regional level, to avoid wasteful use of resources in unnecessarily competitive locally based campaigns.
