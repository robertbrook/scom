Mr. N. R. Wylie: This Clause, like Clause 2, is one of the more significant Clauses in the Bill, and, as I understand it, it is intended to replace Section 3 of the 1947 Act. Section 3 of the 1947 Act combined the corresponding provisions of the first two Clauses of this Bill in so far as it imposed a statutory duty on local planning authorities firstly to carry out a survey of their district, and then, within three years after the appointed day or such later period as permitted, to prepare and submit a development plan.

Now these two obligations are repeated in the Bill, with the exception that at this stage of the Bill we are concerned with a structure plan, and much of what can be said on Clause 1 is likely to overlap with what is bound to be raised on Clause 2. I should like to know if there is any peculiar significance in the splitting up of the obligation to carry out a survey and the obligation to prepare and submit a structure plan as it is set out in this Bill, in contrast, as I have indicated, to the provisions of the 1947 Act.

We are anxious to co-operate as much as we possibly can with this Bill, as was clearly indicated at Second Reading, but much of the Bill is going to require detailed and careful explanation. Provided we get these explanations we are going to co-operate as much as we can.

The Committee ought to remember that Clause 102 provides for the coming into operation of this Bill on an appointed day, and there may be different days appointed for different Clauses—Sections of the Act, as it will be. Clause 102 reads as follows:

"This Act shall come into operation on a day appointed by an order made by statutory instrument by the Secretary of State …"

So for a start the Bill itself is not going to come into operation when it receives the Royal Assent. The whole thing is going to be governed by the Statutory Instrument to which I have just referred. The Clause goes on:

"… and different days may be appointed under this section for different purposes …"

Presumably that means that the Government have in mind bringing certain Clauses of this Bill into operation before others are brought into operation. The Clause goes on:

"… and, in particular, different days may be so appointed for the coming into operation of the same provision in different areas."

Image Col. 5 That rather suggests not only that different Clauses are to be brought in at different times but that it is envisaged that there will be a geographical phasing of the application of the legislation, and that it is contemplated bringing the legislation in for the purposes of certain areas while delaying its application to other areas.

I would like to know at this stage whether the fact that the obligation to carry out a survey is embodied in one Clause, and the statutory duty to prepare and submit a structure plan is embodied in another Clause, means that it is contemplated that Clause 1 is likely to be brought into effect before Clause 2?

Secondly, we would like to know, is it envisaged that even in the obligation of Clause 1—that is the statutory duty to carry out the survey—that that is an obligation which is to be applied to certain local planning authorities at the outset and not to others? I think this is important, because, as was conceded by the Minister at Second Reading, and indeed by the Government at an earlier stage in another place, it appears to be recognised that not all local planning authorities as at present constituted would be able to carry out their obligations under Clause 1, let alone the much more detailed and complicated obligations under Clause 2.

What worries us about Clause 1 is that the criteria which have to be taken into account in the survey, the things which have to be looked at in the preparation of the survey, are complex, and that most local planning authorities do not at present have the expertise adequately to fulfil that function. This, of course, is where a discussion on Clause 1 tends to overlap with the discussion on Clause 2, because the same problem arises there, only to a greater extent. I think we ought to know at the beginning what the Government's intentions are in this direction.

Is it envisaged that the obligation to promote a survey of the district will be imposed only on the major planning authorities in Scotland? Or, alternatively, is it envisaged that it is to be imposed on all planning authorities, but that the smaller authorities are going to be expected to get together on an ad hoc basis for the purpose of preparing this Col. 6 survey? Unless they do so, I do not see how they could possibly meet the requirements of this Clause.

In subsection (4) of this Clause there is a statutory duty on the local planning authority, for the purpose of discharging its functions under this Clause, of

"examining and keeping under review any matters relating to the district of another such authority"

and there is a duty to consult with the other authority. I have run up against difficulties in the past in regard to much more limited statutory obligations for consultation between local authorities. The statutory obligations for local authorities to consult with adjoining authorities only arises where there is perimeter development in the one which is likely to effect existing land uses in the other. If one runs up against difficulties in practice with that very limited obligation for consultation, I am slightly concerned about how subsection (4) is to operate when there is a wide duty of consultation between local authorities, and how that is going to be interpreted. These are not matters which we felt it was possible to submit to Amendment, but the very wide terms of subsection (4) do carry with them the kind of latent difficulties to which I have referred.

I would just remind the Committee that, whatever may be said about how it is intended that this Clause and this legislation generally is to work, we are dealing here in Clause 1 with obligations imposed on local planning authorities, and local planning authorities mean the local planning authorities set out in Section 2 of the 1947 Act. Indeed, it is the problem of setting up regional bodies to deal with what are really regional responsibilities and problems, both in Clause 1 and in Clause 2, which we feel is one of the more serious shortcomings of this legislation as a whole.

These are some preliminary observations on Clause 1 about which I think the Committee would wish to have the Minister's views.
