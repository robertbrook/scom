SCOTTISH GRAND COMMITTEE

OFFICIAL REPORT

Tuesday, 18th March, 1969

[Mr. JOHN ROBERTSON in the Chair]

HOUSING (SCOTLAND) BILL

10.30 a.m.

Resolved,

That during the proceedings on the Housing (Scotland) Bill the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock.—[ Mr. Ross.]

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. William Ross)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the Housing (Scotland) Bill in relation to the principle of the Bill.

In asking the Committee to consider the principle of this Bill I must emphasise that it ought to be judged as part only of the framework of housing policy for Scotland. The complete framework has been built up by a series of Acts over the last half century. Fortunately, it has been made the easier to understand by the consolidation which we carried out in the Housing (Scotland) Act, 1966, and the Housing (Financial Provisions) (Scotland) Act, 1968. But it is only a framework. The job of Government and Parliament is to get the framework right. We have to look to the local authorities, the building industry and the varied clients of the industry to carry the policy into effect.

I imagine that few of us, on either side of the Committee, regard the present structure as perfect. It suffers, more than anything else, from imbalance, imbalance in many ways. For example, although we have just completed another record year for new housebuilding, and although it is gratifying that a total of 41,989 houses have been completed and within that total the private sector has achieved a new record of 8,720, we cannot be content so long as Scotland is able to build only one house for owner-occupation for every four built by the public sector.

Then there is an imbalance in some parts of the country in relation to slum clearance. Over the country as a whole we have been achieving some quite good results. We have closed or demolished in the last four years 68,800. In the four 6 years before that the number was 50,500, but the total is not large enough in relation to the problem which has to be dealt with. Some local authorities are, frankly, not allocating nearly enough of the houses they provide to rehousing those living in slum conditions. And some, while building houses on the outskirts and rehousing slum dwellers, are not doing as much as they could in actually dealing with the slums. There are two aspects to slum clearance: one is to rehouse the occupants of unsatisfactory houses; the other is to clear the site and rebuild.

Finally, there is an imbalance between new and old. Admittedly we have in Scotland a great many houses which are so far gone that nothing will do but to replace them, probably about a quarter of a million. But the very size of that problem has sometimes blinded us to the large number of houses which are falling in standard at a time when standards are rising, but which it is still not too late to save. It will take a tremendous effort to replace all the worst houses within a reasonable time, and since the second-grade properties will have to remain in use for some years to come—I think the number is about 200,000 in this second grade—it is only common sense to divert part of our resources to saving them from degenerating into slums. If anyone would like to know how we have fallen in this respect I have the Report here which was published in 1945. My right hon. Friend the Member for Clackmannan and East Stirlingshire (Mr. Woodburn) will remember it. Many of the figures used in that White Paper over 24 years ago are still being used today.

When we took office in 1964 the obvious need was to achieve a substantial increase in the number of new houses being built. It was also clear that insufficient information was available about the nature of the housing problem. So we resuscitated the Scottish Housing Advisory Committee—which started work in 1944—which the party opposite had allowed to lapse. The Committee already has produced three valuable Reports.

The first of these Reports was published in January, 1967, and was called "Scotland's Older Houses". It had been prepared by a sub-committee under the chairmanship of Mr. J. B. Cullingworth. Let me pay a sincere tribute to the fine work which that sub-committee did. Of course, we are all acquainted with the JPEG 7 problem of Scotland's slums, but the Report gave a new emphasis to it.

Some people discovered for the first time that there was a terrible problem here. When we have a problem of this size, concentrated within particular areas, there is an appalling task which faces us of fathers and mothers trying to bring up families in appalling conditions. It is a credit to them that so many fine families have been reared in these conditions. Let us not forget the neglect and dilapidation—and the common staircases which are a disgrace to present-day Scotland and all we claim for it.

Legislation alone can never cure the problem of bad housing in Scotland. That task requires the co-operation of the local authorities, housing associations, owner-occupiers, landlords, tenants and, not least, the building industry. Naturally, following the publication of the Report, we had consultations with a wide range of interests, and from these emerged the proposals outlined in the White Paper "The Older Houses in Scotland: A Plan for Action" which was published last summer. Since then there have followed further consultations on the legislation required. In this connection I want to mention particularly the help which we have received from a working party of officials nominated by the local authority associations.

Let us not underestimate the task they have. We give some figures in paragraphs 2 and 3 of the White Paper. The subcommittee considered that the condition of almost 300,000 of the older houses was so bad that they ought to be replaced quickly and drew attention to the need for rehabilitation and improvement of at least 200,000 more. This means that the number of older houses for which effective action needs to be taken or planned in future is 30 per cent. of Scotland's total.

How is it to be done? The local authorities must take the lead in driving forward in their districts the policy put out in the White Paper. They must see that houses which are worth preserving, whether they are privately owned or in public ownership, are repaired and improved, and that those which have come to the end of their useful lives are demolished and replaced. This can mean their buying up houses and doing the work themselves. But it does not necessarily mean only that; I emphasise that the local authorities must give every 8 possible assistance and encouragement to private owners to do the work themselves.

The first need will be for the local authorities to make proper surveys of their districts. On the basis of these surveys we would expect that the district would be divided up into areas, which may often be quite small—perhaps just one or two streets—but could sometimes be larger.

To help them in this, we propose to give a manual of guidance, supplementing what is in the Bill. The Bill itself lays down a new minimum standard of tolerability. A house which does not attain that standard needs to be improved or to be taken out of use. Some houses will only barely fail to meet the standard, while others will be well below it, and we shall suggest ways by which the local authorities can work out which houses, or areas of houses, should be scheduled for improvement and which for demolition.

Mr. Ross
: Between 1950 and 1960 the average per year of conversions and improvement was 2,700. I am glad to say that in 1968 alone we managed to convert and improve 13,500. Before the hon. Member picks me up, even before I have fallen down, he should examine himself to see if what he did was any better. In case there are more interruptions, I have loads of figures here.

For example, an owner may be very willing to carry out improvements, but have a difficulty about where the present tenants are to live while the work is done; and the sensible and most satisfactory solution may be for the local authority to rehouse them, on the understanding that the improved houses will be let to the original tenants or to others nominated by the local authority.

I turn now to some of the details of the Bill. This is a distinctively Scottish Bill. We have different problems in Scotland and, therefore, the Bill differs from the English Bill.

Part I of the Bill deals with houses which do not meet the tolerable standard. Unlike the present provisions about unfitness, which simply list a number of factors which have to be taken into account, but can be ignored, the new standard lists a number of tests, worded as positively and precisely as possible.

The tests include for the first time some objective items—for example, a sink with a supply of both hot and cold water, and the exclusive use of a w.c. The important point to notice is that if the house fails to meet any single one of the tests it fails to meet the standard and, as I have already said, needs to be brought up to that standard or taken out of use.

It is, of course, not practicable to require every house which does not meet the new standard to be dealt with immediately. In a number of areas as much as one-third of the housing stock will not reach the standard. So the Bill gives local authorities powers to act in relation to the houses which fall below the standard, and in Clause 1 places on them a duty to secure that houses that do not reach the standard are dealt with as soon as possible.

In place of the complex powers in Parts III and IV of the 1966 Act, which deal with "clearance areas" and "improvement areas", Clauses 4 and 5 enable local authorities to define areas in which most of the houses do not meet the tolerable standard and which they think 10 suitable for treatment, either by demolition or improvement.

I have already said we would expect them wherever possible to persuade the owners, but, where that is not possible, the authority may proceed to purchase land in the defined area and demolish the buildings or improve the houses, as the case may be. In other words, we give powers for the local authority to take initiative.

Clauses 6 to 15 develop the basic procedure for what will now be called housing treatment areas.

I am sorry to have to inflict a new definition upon the Committee. I hope no one will shorten it and call it "h.t.a" or anything like that.

I should draw the Committee's attention to Clause 16, which has been included to meet wishes expressed to us by local authorities. The Clause will give the authorities the power, once a housing treatment area has been defined, to apply a special scheme of occupancy control to it. It will be entirely discretionary: there will be no obligations on any local authority to adopt it, either generally or in relation to particular areas. The control would not affect the sale of the houses, but it would be an offence for anyone either to occupy a house which is subject to a control order, or to let or otherwise allow another person to occupy such a house, without the consent of the local authority.

A major obstacle to faster progress with slum clearance has been the basis of compensation payable to the owner-occupier of an unfit house when it is acquired compulsorily. Clauses 18 to 23 provide that in the circumstances there defined—mainly owner-occupation of the house for two years or more—the owner of a house which is acquired or vacated as not meeting the tolerable standard is to be entitled to a supplementary payment, equal to the amount by which site value, the normal basis of compensation for an unfit house, falls short of its market value. This payment should have the effect of reducing objections from people whose major worry is the amount of compensation, and should help to make faster progress possible.

For houses which do not qualify for these new payments Clause 22 provides for JPEG 11 an increase in the amount of the "well-maintained" payments. These arrangements will apply to houses which have been purchased or vacated on or after 1st August, 1968. This is an order to avoid unfairness and uncertainty arising between the date of publication of the White Paper and the date when the Bill becomes an Act.

Part II of the Bill introduces new powers for local authorities to deal with houses which are in disrepair. These are designed to' deal with the kind of problem which arose in Glasgow and elsewhere following the gale of last January. A number of authorities, including Glasgow Corporation, felt inhibited by the form of their existing powers from stepping in to deal with urgent repairs to privately owned property. I remember the Secretary of State being urged to handle this like a military operation. No Secretary of State or authority could deal with this situation within the statutory powers. We felt improvements were required in the statutory powers and we are taking steps to make them. The new powers in the Bill will enable a local authority to take action at an earlier stage before deterioration has gone too far.

Part III of the Bill makes some important alterations to the existing arrangements for improvement grants. Private owners will still have to find half the cost of improvements, but we are proposing very substantial increases in the grant maxima which, in conjunction with the other provisions in the Bill, should encourage much wider use of these grants. The standard grant maximum is raised from £155 to £200. The discretionary grant maximum is raised from £500 to £1,200 and the maximum eligible cost for local authority schemes is raised from £1,400 to £2,500. The Bill also gives a wider discretion in relation to the condition and life required of the house after improvement and redefines "improvement" so as to include some works of repair which previously were excluded. Altogether there is a far wider range of direction now available to local authorities and to private owners in respect of bringing property up to this tolerable standard.

Regarding Part IV, which deals with rents, an important issue stressed by the Cullingworth Report was the need to give landlords who improve their houses an adequate return in the form of increased 12 rent. Part IV of the Bill does this by providing for the rents of houses which are improved to a fully satisfactory state to be fixed under the fair rent machinery of the Rent Act, 1965, instead of being related to former rents plus a fixed percentage of expenditure.

The local authority will first have to certify that the improvements to be carried out will bring the house up to a satisfactory standard, and the actual fixing of the rent will then be a matter for the rent officer subject to appeal to the Rent Assessment Committee.

To safeguard individual tenants Schedule 4 to the Bill provides for the phasing of any rent increases which result from this provision over a period of years: three years if the house is already on a regulated tenancy, five years if it is on a controlled tenancy.

For reasons of equity we have thought it right in the Bill to deal also with the rents of houses which are already at the satisfactory standard, without the need for further improvement. In these cases, however, Clause 51 provides a delaying mechanism. This is to avoid an overload of the rent officer organisation immediately after the Bill comes into force.

I sincerely hope this will be used. We will have the improvements. If we do, it may well be there will be an overloading of the Rent Officer and the Tribunal machinery. We want to avoid that before bringing in this further stage. We think priority must be given to providing a stimulus for the improvement of houses which are not yet up to standard.

Finally in the context of improvement, I draw attention to Clauses 58 and 59 which introduce an entirely new specific grant to local authorities to help them carry out works to improve the environment of houses; for instance, by creating play spaces for children, planting trees or providing parking spaces. The Cullingworth Committee was very concerned about the lack of any provision of this kind, and anybody who is familiar with the neglect and squalor that often occur in the backlands of tenements will appreciate the need for it. The expenditure which will attract grant is such as the Secretary of State may approve, subject to a maximum of £100 per house in the area. The Bill is drafted to enable the Secretary of State to pay the grant in support of the proposals by a local authority to carry out works on the JPEG 13 environment in any predominantly residential area. This is something on which we could spend a great deal of money, for there is much to be done, and should in itself have a great effect on the outlook of an area both from the point of view of people who pass through it and, much more important, the people who actually live there. In present economic circumstances progress is bound to be slower than many would like. In the first place we shall be looking for environmental improvements to accompany house improvements and to make them more worth while.

Naturally, I have been able to deal only with the major points in the Bill. I would emphasise that the Bill is only part of an overall policy to deal with our older houses, but I believe it can be the starting point for a major campaign to sweep away the evil housing legacy which has been with us for far too long. It seeks to face up to the real problems, the problems of the owner, the problems of the occupier, the problems of finance, and the problems of resources.

I commend it warmly to the Committee.

During debates on other legislation affecting house repairs, including the passage of the 1954 Act, we had long discussions on what disrepair really meant. There were instances of people living in large houses, but whose families had grown up and gone away, so that they were over-housed; whereas there were also people living in, inadequate tenement accommodation, who could and would have made mutual exchanges.
