Mr. Bruce-Gardyne: I am grateful, Mr. Mallalieu. That will save time and will possibly avoid confusion, although the Amendments are not on exactly the same point.

I want, first, Mr. Mallalieu, to express my own thanks to you for arriving this morning. I do not know what would have happened to my Amendments had you not arrived. I am delighted to be able to argue them under your Chairmanship and not to have to face the prospect of having to be here so early on another Tuesday morning.

The Amendments require a little background explanation to make their purpose clear to the Committee. The first is designed to require the Government to return to Parliament before the proposed new interim limit of £700 million on the borrowing powers of the South of Scotland Electricity Board and the North of Scotland Hydro-Electric Board is reached. The second Amendment is designed to require the Boards to borrow for their requirements, within the limits proposed in the Bill, exclusively by stock issues.

For reasons which I shall explain, it would not be my intention, though I cannot speak for my hon. Friends, to press the second Amendment to a Division. Whether I press the first Amendment to a Division will depend on the Minister's reply.

Broadly speaking, the purpose of both Amendments is the same. It is to limit the access of the Boards to the Government's printing press. At Second Reading I expressed the hope that Mr. Goode of the I.M.F. would not have his attention drawn to the Bill during his visit to London last week. In the event I cannot help wondering whether his attention was not drawn to the Bill because I note with interest that he decided that his regular visits of security, of which last week's was supposed to be the last, should be continued into next year. I cannot help wondering if he was not motivated in this very understandable decision by a scrutiny of the Bill.

If Mr. Goode's attention was drawn to the Bill and to the accompanying Memorandum it is hardly a matter of surprise that he should have felt it necessary to continue and extend the probationary Image Col. 5 period to which Britain is at present subject. He would have read in paragraph 3 of the Memorandum—Cmnd. 3806—that the Government had to come back to the House to seek new borrowing limits for the two Boards, because the Boards had obtained smaller contributions than forecast from internal resources. He would further have read, if he referred to the debate on the Electricity (Borrowing Powers) (Scotland) Order, that the main reason why there was the shortfall in ploughback was that Ministers had intervened and frozen prices. If Mr. Goode had got thus far in his examination, he might further have reflected that if the Bill went through as proposed by the Government, and without Amendment, there would be nothing to prevent Ministers from using their power of the printing press under the Bill to freeze prices in the electricity industry in Scotland till another £250 million had been run through. Finally, Mr. Goode might reflect that this contrasted a little oddly with the Chancellor's promise to limit growth of the money supply, and in particular with the intention expressed by the Chancellor in the Budget and since to curtail private consumption by keeping the advance of earnings behind the advance of prices.

It is for these reasons that my Amendments are designed, to use one of the nautical metaphors so beloved of successive Chancellors, to keep us more nearly on course. That broadly is the background against which I ask the Committee to consider these Amendments.

"The Committee have not considered these problems in detail. They appreciate that the industries"—

that is, the nationalised industries—

"have not, in recent years, experienced difficulty in raising from the Exchequer, at favourable rates, the volume of capital they require for their approved investment."

Alas, we cannot discuss that proposition in this Committee. For that reason I argue that, as we can do nothing under the terms of this Bill to rectify the situation, I would not wish to press that Amendment to a Division. It is designed for a perfect world and we live in an imperfect world.
