When one is considering the problems of the Highlands and when one is concerned, as we all are, with increasing activity and development in the Highlands, it becomes clear that transport is a vital factor. That applies not only to transport within the area but also to communications with the rest of Scotland and with the rest of Britain. Inside the area one finds physical barriers of hills and water which produce problems which are not met in many other parts of Britain.

As an example of the importance of communications with the rest of Britain I shall take the A.9 road. For those in the Committee who visit the Highlands or live nearby it is certainly striking that many of the bodies and persons concerned with the development and welfare of the Highlands generally are especially concerned that the road leading north to their area should be improved as fast as possible. The point is that this is concerned with expenditure outwith the Highland area. They are recommending that money should be spent on a road outwith their area, because of the importance of speedy communications with the south. This is important to those who are considering setting up industry or engaging in other activities in the Highland area, particularly the north and north-west Highlands, because they are concerned at the time which it will take a lorry to move north and south, to and from a new site.

The establishment of this Group is a step of the greatest significance and importance for the future development of Highland transport.

As hon. Members will be aware, the Transport Act transferred to the new Group the whole of the interests in Scottish road, passenger, and sea transport services formerly owned by the British Railways Board and by the Transport Holding Company. In Highland terms, this includes the full ownership and control of Highland Omnibuses Limited, and of the other two Scottish Bus Group companies Alexanders (Northern) Limited and Alexanders (Midland) Limited, which operate services in the Highland area, and of the Kyle to Kyleakin ferry. In addition, the Group acquired at the outset the 50 per cent. interest in MacBrayne's shipping and bus services formerly owned by the Transport Holding Company. They have, therefore, felt that advantage would lie in the Group acquiring the complete ownership and control of MacBrayne's through the purchase of the 50 per cent. shareholding owned by Coast Lines Limited.

JPEG 163 This will help us enormously to get on with the modernisation and rationalisation of the fleet owned by the Scottish Transport Group. One fact which is already clear and to which the Scottish Transport Group attaches great importance is that the old dependence on shipping services to the Islands on general-purpose ships is changing, and giving way to a need for new roll-on roll-off ferries capable of dealing with the increasing volume of private cars and goods transported by road.
