[Miss HARVIE ANDERSON in the Chair]

ELECTRICITY (SCOTLAND) BILL

10.32 a.m.

Resolved,

That if the proceedings on the Electricity (Scotland) Bill are not completed at this day's Sitting, the Committee do meet on Thursday at half-past Ten o'clock.—[ Dr. Dickson Mabon.]

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Dr. J. Dickson Mabon)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the Electricity (Scotland) Bill in relation to the principle of the Bill.

The main purpose of the Bill is simple: it is to raise the limit on the amount which the Scottish Electricity Boards may borrow, from £580 million to £800 million. It is, I think, a truism to say that future economic growth in Scotland depends on the expansion of our power resources. The generation of electricity is universally recognised as a vital prerequisite of industrial expansion and the improvement of living standards. No one is likely to argue, either, against the necessity of long-term planning of electricity needs on a national Scottish basis. Many of the new industries now moving into Scotland will require greatly increased capacity.

Electricity demand in Scotland has been accelerating at a higher rate than in England and Wales. During the past ten years the Scottish demand has shown an annual growth rate of about 8½ cent. Sales of electricity are expected almost to double during the seven years up to 1975. This reflects the underlying buoyancy in the Scottish economy. The lion's share of the expenditure is needed for new generating capacity. So although the sums involved are large, so are the operations they are required to finance.

Electricity is now available to almost everyone in the south of Scotland and to 96 per cent. in the north. On average, Scottish electricity consumers pay less for their electricity than consumers in England and Wales.

6 With power stations taking five to seven years to design and build, correct load forecasting is essential. The Scottish Boards have had a very good record in this respect, but correct load forecasting is not enough. It is also essential that the right decision as to size and type of station should be made, so that the system as a whole will be reinforced in the most efficient way, having regard to the characteristics of the expected load and its geographical distribution.

Before going on to elaborate on our requirements may I briefly describe the process of decision-making. Each spring the Boards submit investment programmes for five years ahead. These are subject to a very searching inter-Departmental examination in the light of which the Secretary of State approves programmes for the forthcoming year. The amounts which then have to be borrowed for the investment programmes are closely controlled by the Scottish Development Department and the Treasury in accordance with statutory requirements.

Investment appraisal is carried out in accordance with the principles set out in the White Paper Cmnd. 3437, using the Test Discount Rate recommended by the Government.

Planning of new generating capacity for' Scotland as a whole is undertaken by a Joint Planning Committee of the two Boards. All possible methods of meeting future increases in demand are considered. A careful study is made of the various "mixes" of plant which might be suitable.

At this crucial stage a computer is employed to simulate the working of the system taking each of the most suitable alternatives in turn. The total system costs are then calculated for each alternative for each year, discounted to the base year and compared with each other. This gives the Planning Committee a clear picture of the effect on Scottish system costs of each alternative, with all other stations fitting into their proper place in the system and adopting the most appropriate load factor.

The new power stations now being built in Scotland are visible proof of an intensive programme of investment. A large slice of the borrowing authorised JPEG 7 by the Bill will be required for the completion of two large projects.

At Longannet Point in south-west Fife the giant new coal burning station of the South Board is rapidly taking shape. This plant, with an eventual capacity of 2,304 megawatts, will be fuelled directly by the Fife coalfields. It will generate its first capacity next year and will work up to full output by 1971. It will operate at high thermal efficiency and will use up to 5 million tons of coal a year after 1971 and provide employment for up to 10,000 miners.

It is interesting to note the value of the project to the construction and engineering industries. The value of contracts placed is £90 million, of which Scotland's share amounts to well over half.

Another sidelight is that the older Kincardine Power Station near Longannet reached, last year, the highest thermal efficiency of any coal burning station in Britain.

On the other side of the country, work is in progress on Hunterston B, which will add 1,250 megawatts to the South Board's capacity by 1973. This new station, incorporating the latest improvements in world nuclear research, will produce almost four times as much electricity as Hunterston A. The work force on this project will build up to 2,000,

But the Boards are already considering needs in the years after these two great stations have been commissioned. With a continuing increase in demand to be met, both the North and South Boards are planning additional stations in the mid and late 1970s.

The first instalment proposed is a 300 megawatt pumped storage scheme at Foyers. After that the Boards see the need for a further large station in the west of Scotland where the heaviest demand is concentrated. The proposal for Foyers is particularly interesting in the silver jubilee year of the North of Scotland Hydro-Electric Board. It was at Foyers, after all, in 1896, that Lord Kelvin built the first major hydroelectric station in Britain. The tragedy of the Highlands is that so few further stations were pioneered in the next half century. Between 1921 and 1941 six major Highland power schemes were rejected by Parliament.

8 It was, of course, our late beloved Tom Johnston who was largely instrumental in passing the famous Hydro-Electric Development Act, 1943. This was not only an Act of Parliament; it was an Act of faith which was, for a small country, as dramatic as the establishment of the Tennessee Valley Authority ten years earlier in the United States. I doubt if the present "take off" of the Highlands under the Highlands and Islands Development Board could have happened but for the work of the Board, which has brought electricity to 96 per cent. of the Highlands and helped provide the infrastructure for modern industry in modern communities.

In the last 25 years technological breakthroughs have pioneered new forms of power. The proposal is that the Foyers scheme, like the successful new station at Cruachan, would use off-peak electricity at time of low demand to pump water uphill from a low level to a high level reservoir and generate its own electricity at peak periods by running the water down again. Schemes of this kind provide a means of storing electricity and, in effect, allow us to get more intensive use out of thermal or nuclear plants already installed elsewhere. In other words, Foyers would cause more coal to be burnt.

Less dramatically, the work has continued of bringing electricity to remote islands, of serving a North Board area which covers a quarter of the land mass of Britain. Extensions are in progress in the Shetland Islands, the Outer Hebrides and in several of the remoter mainland areas. The Board has in operation 76 miles of submarine cable serving 23 islands, apart from islands which generate their own power.

Let me turn to the financial criteria. The extent to which money has to be borrowed to meet the capital requirements is dependent, not only on the scale of investment, but also on the financial performance of the Boards. Let me repeat that, on average, Scottish electricity consumers as a whole pay less for their electricity than consumers in England and Wales. If that position is to be maintained without excessive borrowing, considerable efforts will be necessary to keep down costs.

Dr. Mabon
: I should like to contemplate all these questions and answer them comprehensively at the end of the debate, because, if I am tempted into any more diversions, important as they are, I shall never end this speech, and I want to get back to the essential point about prices.

Prices are important in many ways, but they depend on techniques and efforts which the Boards must make to keep down costs, and therefore prices. The industry in Scotland pioneered in Great Britain the off-peak use of electricity, and this is to a large extent the reason why we have this marginal difference in prices. This has had a material effect on daily and weekly demand curves—that is to say, the off-peak use of electricity—and has produced considerable benefit in reducing both investment in new capacity and running costs. The JPEG 11 scope for further off-peak development is becoming more limited.

The Scottish Boards are now concentrating on the improvement of productivity and efficiency. They are already able to report significant savings as a result of their efforts. The South Board was singled out by the National Board for Prices and Incomes for its work in introducing and developing yardsticks for the assessment of individual performance. Work studies and incentive payment schemes have been introduced, and computers are being employed to an increasing extent. The services of management consultants have been used where appropriate. This is viewed by the Boards as a continuing process which is expected to increase the productivity of labour, measured in terms of units sold, by about 40 per cent. in the next four years.

I come, now, to another important provision in the Bill, and hon. Members may be interested in this, although at first sight it seems small. I am referring to the change proposed in the statutory requirements governing the authentication of the Boards' seals. I shall return to other general matters when I have dealt with this. At present two signatures are required, that of the Chairman or another authorised member of the Board, and that of the secretary or another authorised person. It is proposed in future to require only the signature of the secretary or other authorised person. With an organisation of the size of these Boards, and bearing in mind the many transactions which will require sealing, hon. Members will readily appreciate that the requirement of a signature by a Board member as well as that of an official of a Board can be unnecessarily restrictive. I understand from the Boards that it has in fact become unduly burdensome. Care must be taken in relaxing protective requirements of this nature, but what we have proposed is in line with modern practice, and was accepted by Parliament in recent legislation governing the Transport Boards and the Air Corporation.

The estimates on which the proposed new limit in the Bill is based are explained in the White Paper on the Bill in the Boards' brochure "Plans for the future, 1968–1975", copies of which are 12 available to hon. Members, but it might be helpful if I explain the position briefly.

The last occasion on which the House considered the borrowing powers of the Scottish Electricity Boards was the debate on the Electricity (Borrowing Powers) (Scotland) Order, 1966, which raised the borrowing limit of the Scottish Boards from £500 million to £580 million. The hon. Member for Moray and Nairn (Mr. Gordon Campbell) will remember that I said then that the £580 million was expected to last till March, 1969, and that this would give the House an opportunity of reviewing the position within about two years. This estimate has proved to be very near the mark. It is two years since we debated that Order. At 31st March, 1969, the total borrowings to count against the limit are expected to be £583 million, and this debate is taking place almost precisely two years after we gave the figures in the Borrowing Order debate, saying that we would get pretty well near that figure by the spring of 1969.

The new limit of £800 million is expected to last till 1975, but the Bill provides an interim limit of £700 million beyond which the Boards will not have power to borrow unless the Secretary of State for Scotland, as two years ago, makes an order which must receive the approval of the House of Commons. The House will, therefore, have an opportunity to consider the Boards' activities before the limit of £700 million can be passed. The estimate is that that point is likely to be reached in 1972.

The White Paper on the Bill shows, in paragraph 8, that at 31st March, 1968, total borrowings had reached £542 million, and estimates the further capital requirements of the Scottish Electricity Boards by 31st March, 1975, at £506 million. The allocation of this estimate between the two Boards and its detailed make-up between generation, transmission, and distribution expenditure and working capital are again set out in Table 3 of the Boards' brochure.

Of the total requirement of £506 million, the Boards expect to meet £300 million from internal resources, leaving the balance of £206 million to come from borrowing. This is the extent of the new borrowing envisaged, but, in addition, provision has been included for about JPEG 13 £37 million of re-borrowing to repay loans which are at present outside the borrowing limit.
