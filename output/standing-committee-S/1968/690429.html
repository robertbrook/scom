TOWN AND COUNTRY PLANNING (SCOTLAND) BILL [ Lords]

Question again proposed.

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Dr. J. Dickson Mabon)
: I was finishing a particularly cogent and well-argued case in reply to the Amendments which were so ably proposed by the hon. and learned Member for Edinburgh, Pentlands (Mr. Wylie) at our last Sitting, and I was going on to indicate the third safeguard. I was arguing that the judge and jury argument is basically misconceived, because, in the context of development, planning local authorities are acting in the public interest. They have no interest to grind and they should be trusted to act responsibly.

But I accept that in certain cases it can be alleged, perhaps with some degree of belief, that it is not quite true of a particular authority. That is why I should like to impress on the Committee the fact that, while we are anxious to see this process of public participation developing properly and being really effective the Secretary of State will, nevertheless, want to watch this dialogue at all stages. He will have the power, if he is not satisfied that it is being adequately achieved, to direct that local authority not to take further steps for the adoption of the plan till it has taken further specified participation action. So that, seen against these fears, I would say that it would be a mistake to seek to make this big difference in planning legislation, taking us back to the 1947 system rather than forward to the system 246 which should be in operation in the 'seventies.

I accept the hon. and learned Gentleman's point, that with the reform of local government we will be adapting many of the fundamental Statutes of local government, of which this will be one, and I also accept, therefore, that we will look at this matter afresh at that time, but for the present I feel that it would be quite wrong if we in Scotland failed to make this essential form of devolution. The days of democratic centralism are over, and because of the sheer volume and nature of work we have to devolve more and more on the local authorities. I hope that the Committee will reject these Amendments.

Mr. T. G. D. Galbraith
: This Amendment is probably the most important that has been moved so far, and it may well be the most important in the whole Bill, because the Minister is seeking to take away from the individual who is aggrieved the right to appeal to an outside authority. I am very sorry to have to say to the Minister, who often speaks with great conviction, that I did not have the feeling either this morning or last week that he was speaking with conviction. He was doing something which he has often accused me of doing—reading an official brief. The arguments against giving the local authority the last word were expressed extremely clearly by my hon. and learned Friend. It is absolutely contrary to natural justice. I wish the Minister would listen instead of looking at his notes, because I want him to answer these points.

He has not even begun to try to answer the point as to why natural justice should be interfered with. There has been no attempt to answer that point. I should like to envisage him in the situation where he wishes to carry out some development, but the local authority says, "No", because it has some other plan. This is a body which has an interest but which is judge in its own case. Think how utterly frustrated the Minister would feel. But if he could find some other body to whom he could appeal, such as the Secretary of State, he would be much more satisfied. It is part of natural justice and that is what our whole system in this country is based upon. Furthermore, the local authority is involved in local things and JPEG 247 is much closer to them than the Secretary of State.

The hon. Gentleman said that the local authorities are not necessarily officials; they are elected representatives. I quite agree that some are elected representatives, but they become more closely involved when decisions are taken. It is not an impartial view which is taken when there is an appeal to the local authority which has set up a plan. It is merely the same people walking three times around the table and coming to the same answer as they came to at the beginning. It seems to me to be utterly and absolutely crazy to introduce this change when the existing system—and I am talking only about where there is appeal to the Secretary of State—has the trust of the people in Scotland. Basically, even when an answer goes against them, they trust it, and feel that matters are looked at in an impartial way. But that is never going to happen when the last word rests with the local authority which has originally taken the decision.

There is another point with which the 'Minister did not deal and which he sketched over in a most lamentable way. He never referred to the views of the local authorities. He knows perfectly well that this is something about which we feel strongly, although I want to stress that there is absolutely nothing party political in our objection here, he made no reference to what local authorities said.

He did not deal with Glasgow, which is the largest city in Scotland, and yet it is specifically declared, in a letter sent to me and to other Members of Parliament, and, I imagine, to the Secretary of State, on 14th March, that

"Because the structure plan is inevitably vague, and because there is no appeal at the local stage, individuals who feel that they may be aggrieved will appeal, perhaps unnecessarily, at the structure stage."

Here is the major local authority in Scotland asking that the existing system with regard to appeal to the Secretary of State should be maintained.

Why does Glasgow ask for that? Local authorities usually like to have more power. Has it not occurred to the Minister that it must be something very strange that makes local authorities ask not to have powers? I think it is because, from a practical point of view, 248 they see difficulties, and also because they are genuinely worried that they should be party and judge in their own case.

The Minister of State said, quite rightly, that planning was not carried out in ivory towers and that it was most necessary to get participation. I agree with all of that, but it very often happens that the views of the planners do not accord with the views of the citizens. For example, there is the Stansted case. In my own constituency there is the problem of the Great Western Road. Fortunately, it will be possible to take this to the Secretary of State, but if the widening of the Great Western Road by Glasgow Corporation were objected to by my constituency, and after the inquiry was held the report were made to the Glasgow Corporation which came back with the same answer as before, there would be no satisfaction at all. That argument does not apply in this case, since there is going to be a public inquiry, as land is being compulsorily acquired, but the next stage will be that the Minister will say, if land is compulsorily acquired because of a local plan, "We do not need to have a public inquiry with appeal to the Secretary of State. The local authority is able to do that." That is the next logical step from what he is proposing now.

The Secretary of State is used to acting in a quasi-judicial capacity. It is not at all easy to step outside what one feels one would like to see happen, and to look at it in a quasi-judicial capacity. Local authorities have absolutely no experience of doing this, but the Secretary of State has plenty of experience and, on the whole, he has done it to the satisfaction of the public in Scotland.

The Minister has not answered any of these points and he has not even put up a good case of his own. He has not explained why it is necessary to have this change. I have had the advantage, or possibly the disadvantage, of serving not only in the Scottish Office, and of carrying out the functions which the Minister carried out before he was elevated to his present position, but in an English Department. I know that the position is utterly different there and that there is a tremendous pressure of work coming up to an English Ministry from local authorities, which it really cannot properly discharge. It gets swamped. Therefore, I accept that it may be necessary JPEG 249 in England to depart from the ideal of an appeal to an outside body. But I do not accept that that applies in Scotland. The Minister simply did not go into this. Why does Scotland have to be treated the same as England? I do not like introducing this argument when the whole of Scotland is upset by the irrelevant activities of the hon. Member for Hamilton (Mrs. Ewing) and her friends. It is, nevertheless, perfectly true in this instance, that the situation in England is utterly different from that in Scotland, and there is no reason at all why the machinery in Scotland should follow something that is necessary and second-rate in England, as I think the Minister will admit, and not have an appeal to an outside body.

The Minister said that this is a great change. We are going to have a structure plan—and we accept that—and we are going to have a local plan with a lot of discussion and participation, to use the word of the moment—though what the deuce it means I do not know. All right, have a structure plan, have participation, and have all these things that the Minister wants. Then there is the local inquiry and the reporter is appointed. Nobody is objecting to that. Then the reporter makes his report just as he does today. But the Minister wants that report to go back to the local authority while I want it to go to the Secretary of State. That is the only difference between us. I merely want the report of the reporter to go to the Secretary of State rather than to the local authority against which the individual is appealing, and I cannot for the life of me see why that should not work in Scotland. The Minister has not brought forward one iota of argument to justify this change, and I should like him to say why it is necessary.

"the attempt to process all these detailed plans through a centralised procedure, including provision for objections (of which there may be hundreds or even thousands on a single plan) and public local inquiry, has inevitably led to very serious delays which tend to undermine public confidence in the system."

It goes on to refer to a delay of two or three years in some cases or even longer. In that context a change had to be made. That is not satisfactory.

That was a problem with which our colleagues in England and Wales were faced but it is not a problem in Scotland. If the Minister is right about this great JPEG 251 dialogue of which we have heard so much, as he put it,

"… to reduce the number of objections by persuasion and by a proper dialogue between both sides …"—[OFFICIAL REPORT, First Scottish Standing Committee, 24th April, 1969; c. 241.]

I hope that there will be less pressure at the centre after this Bill becomes law than there is at the moment. If there is no real problem at the moment the basic assumptions of this Bill tend to suggest that there will be less of a problem in the future. In this context the question of relieving pressure at the centre by delegating to local authorities the ultimate decision in objections to a plan which they have put forward is not justified either on the facts as we know them today or on reasonable anticipation for the future. In our view, it is wrong that this change should be made. It puts local planning authorities in an invidious position.

I was interested in what my hon. Friend the Member for Hillhead (Mr. Galbraith) said about the Glasgow Corporation. Local authorities will not want this power. Having promoted a plan, having insisted on the validity of that plan at a public inquiry, the local authority will have the ultimate duty to decide whether to modify or change it in any way. This is one of the crucial issues of policy in this Bill. It is not a party political matter. The Committee should be careful before invoking such a major change in policy which has no justification, and which, on the face of it, appears to run contrary to the principles of natural justice.
