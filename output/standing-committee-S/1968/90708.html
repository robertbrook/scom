Mr. John Brewis
: We have several times recently debated the National Health Service on the Floor of the House. A few days ago we debated the future financing of the Service, and about a month or so before that there was a debate on the hospital building programme in Scotland. It would be tedious for me to repeat the points made in those debates.

I would compliment the Secretary of State on the Health and Welfare Services Report, both for its contents and for the way in which he has managed to publish it in time for this year's debate. The Report gives the figures for the cost of the National Health Service in Scotland. In 1967–68 the gross cost was £177 million, an increase of about 11 per cent. over the previous year, representing a rise in real terms of 61 per cent. compared with 10 years ago. These percentages are far higher than the rise in the Gross National Product; yet the shortage of staff, buildings and equipment is as acute as ever, and forms the central dilemma of the Health Service.

Nowhere is the shortage of funds more acute than in the hospital service. Last Friday, at the meeting in Aberdeen of the British Medical Association, Mr. Walpole Lewin, the Chairman of the B.M.A. Central Committee for Hospital Services, said that £500 million was needed immediately to prevent Britain's hospital service from crumbling. Scotland's share would be about £50 million, which is more than three times as much as we are now spending. Recently the building of the Royal Hospital for Sick Children in 102 Glasgow did crumble, and it had to be replaced at short notice.

The problem of regional hospital boards in allocating priorities is obviously agonising. You, Mr. Robertson, are fortunate in Paisley in that a new general hospital is being built, but other areas such as South Ayrshire, the Borders, Dumfries and Motherwell have had to be put back in the queue.

There must be great difficulties, too, in deciding whether the money is to go to support glamorous advances like in transplant surgery or to improve the very old accommodation for the mentally subnormal. I am glad that neither is being entirely neglected. There is a new transplant unit at the Western General in Edinburgh, although the finance for it came from the Nuffield Foundation, and the first new mental institution for thirty years has been built at Lynebank, Dunfermline.

Will the Secretary of State be more specific about shortages of staff in the hospital service than he was in answer to a Parliamentary Question last week? It is satisfactory to know that the number of medical staff has increased, although there were slightly fewer doctors in the general practitioner service. The increase in nursing staff seems to be mostly in part-time staff, and in the less well-qualified enrolled and pupil nurses. The number of three-year trained registered nurses was less at 30th September, 1968, than in 1965; yet in those three years there were never less than 6,000 student nurses in training. So it seems that the wastage rate among nurses is alarming. We would like to be reassured that the staffing position in mental hospitals in Scotland is better than in England, as I believe to be the case.

I now turn to the reorganisation of the Health Service which the Secretary of State put forward last December in a Green Paper. As hon. Members know, the Health Service is organised on a tripartite basis.

First, there are the hospital and specialist services grouped around 400 hospitals administered in a two-tier system by five regional hospital boards and 76 area management boards. The proportion of the finance of the National Health Service which goes to the hospital service is 62 per cent., and the majority of the medical practitioners are in this service.

Secondly, there are the general practi- JPEG 103 tioner services, which include medical, dental, ophthalmic and pharmaceutical services, and they are run by 25 local executive committees. The finance for both hospital and general practitioner services comes overwhelmingly from Government funds.

Thirdly, there is a wide range of local authority health services which are administered by 56 counties and large burghs in Scotland. The divisions between these services seem to some extent to be arbitrary. For example, maternity services come under the hospital service and are also provided by the local authorities, and the same applies to geriatric services.

Where there is overlapping there is probably also wastage of resources and some inefficiency, although it is a tribute to those who work in the services that the co-operation between the three parts is as good as it is. Each service tends to plan its future development without proper reference to the others. The family doctor increasingly needs the technical aids which are available only in the hospital service. Here I think the provision of more health centres might help. If they are attached to the local hospital they can bring together not only the family doctor service and the specialist staff but also the local services controlled by the medical officer of health. Clearly, advantages could be gained by a unified Health Service administration, provided that there is no domination of one branch over the others.

How this object is to be secured is rather more difficult. The Green Paper suggests a single-tier administration of 10 to 15 area boards to replace the 162 various authorities I have already mentioned. This is a drastic reduction. If one glances through the Health and Welfare Services Report one is struck by the variety of services which it indexes. Each of the 10 or 15 area boards would inevitably become a considerable bureaucracy. I would think of necessity it would have to be split up into various divisions for administration.

The Green Paper states that if there were more than 15 boards their areas would be too small for proper planning, but if there were fewer than 10 they would be too remote from the point of view of the Health Service workers and also their patients. I am sure that the latter point is true, because the regional hospital boards at present are incredibly remote both to 104 doctors and to the public, although the management boards are better known. Local control helps to humanise the administration of the Health Service, and one must never forget the value of local voluntary effort. For example, I cannot imagine how the Health Service run from a central board in Aberdeen would be acceptable in the Orkneys or the Shetland Islands. I think, therefore, that remoteness of control would be a step backwards.

This is also true of the local authority services. When we were last year considering the Social Work Bill we decided that local welfare services should remain the function of the local authority. The health services and the welfare services are closely entwined. The Green Paper now suggests that local authority health services should be detached from the administration of the welfare services and placed under an area board. Presumably, their present controller, the medical officer of health, would become an official of the area board, which, in rural areas, might be as far as 70 miles away. This, too, seems to be a retrograde step when one remembers that local authorities need health advice on many questions which come under their aegis. I am thinking particularly of the work of the sanitary inspector, who deals with food hygiene, insanitary premises, rodent control, and so on. Such new divisions in responsibility in existing local authority services might leave the edges more ragged than those between the present divisions.

We should make no firm decisions till the Wheatley Commission on Local Government Reorganisation has reported. It would be highly desirable if the new health boards were to coincide with the new local authority boundaries, but one can only speculate on what the Wheatley report will recommend. I would think it unlikely that it would recommend as many as 10 or 15 top-tier authorities for Scotland. If it recommended, say, only five top-tier authorities, it would be wise to keep the present two-tier administration of the health services, with the regional boards corresponding to the top tier and the local authority and management boards corresponding to the second tier. There would then still be a reduction in the number of management boards. This view in favour of a two-tier system of administration is supported by some remarks which were made by Mr. Farqu-harson-Lang, who is Chairman of the JPEG 105 North-East Regional Hospital Board. He thinks that a one-tier system of area health boards would be unworkable, and that hospital service boundaries should be the same as local authority boundaries. This might mean regional health boards and local health committees, supposing that Wheatley recommends a two-tier system. The present views of the right hon. Gentleman the Secretary of State for Social Services seem rather similar, since his speech in Norwich a few months ago. Surely, where distances are comparatively greater, as they are in Scotland than in England, arguments about remote control are correspondingly more forceful when applied to Scotland.

I turn now to one or two separate points. There is considerable anxiety at present about abortions, and such anxiety centres mainly in London, but I do not think anyone can remain indifferent to the pathetic queues of miserable, frightened girls who are waiting in certain clinics. We obviously cannot stop the sex explosion, nor do we want to encourage promiscuity, but surely more extensive provision should be made for family planning. I understand that until the Secretary of State makes an implementation order the local authorities cannot use the powers to provide clinics and family planning advice which was given to him under the Health Service and Public Health Act, 1968. Why has there been delay in this matter?

Following the publication of these proposals last December, over 130 sets of comments have been submitted by interested organisations and individuals. There was a considerable amount of consultation before we published the Paper. Some of these comments have been outstandingly constructive and helpful. The overwhelming weight of opinion accepts that reorganisation of the structure of the Service is desirable, and that this should take the form of introducing, in place of the existing tripartite system, a unitary structure under which one authority in each area will be responsible for providing all the health services.

Most of the organisations which take this view consider that the new authority should be an area board established ad hoc for the purpose; but the Association of County Councils in Scotland has expressed a very strong view that the whole of the Health Service should pass into the control of local government. By no means all local authorities share this view. Many would regard it as sufficient if a proportion of the members of area health boards were drawn from the members of local authorities within the area.

Some other aspects of the composition of boards also give rise to differing views. For example, there is a division between those who want a single-tier structure with some 10, 15 or even 20 area boards reporting directly to the central depart- JPEG 107 ment and those, mainly in the hospital service, who believe that there would be advantage in having a few regional boards under each of which there would be a number of local or district boards.

I am giving careful consideration to all these comments and taking careful note of what the hon. Gentleman said this morning in the light of the general acceptance of the principle of unification. Before reaching any conclusion, I shall want to take into account the recommendations of the Royal Commission on Local Government. The hon. Gentleman was right to emphasise that particular point.

These will be of direct relevance, for even if we have ad hoc boards the boundaries for health purposes should march with the boundaries for social work and other related services as far as possible. The precise links between health and a number of major local government functions will be extremely important.

Equally important is our intention to continue to conduct the review of the structure of the health services in the same way as we have done hitherto, not by the issue of cut and dried solutions but by giving the widest possible scope for all concerned to express their views on the merits or defects of particular proposals. We do not make the naive assumption that we can please everyone, but neither shall we make the error of ignoring the wealth of experience and common sense to be found throughout the Health Service.

The hon. Gentleman spoke about hospital building. I am the first to agree that we would all like to do more, but there are certain limitations. My hon. Friend the Under-Secretary of State took the opportunity in an Adjournment Debate on 23rd May to make a progress report on hospital building. I shall, therefore, make only relatively brief reference to that subject.

Of course, we should like to see more hospitals being built and there are many urgent tasks still to be done, but the statistics and the new buildings to be seen on the ground leave no doubt that we have greatly accelerated the pace of the programme. There is no doubt that we would have been further forward if we had started earlier. We are now in a period of sustained advance. Perhaps the Committee will forgive me if I quote 108 the essential figures again. Between 1948 and 1962 we produced in Scotland an average of about 230 beds per year. From 1962 to 1965 the rate was 570 beds a year. Over the four years from the beginning of 1965 to 1968 the rate was almost 1,000 a year. In 1968 alone schemes providing 1,370 beds were completed—a rate of more than five times that achieved in the years from 1948 to 1962.

These new beds have already made possible considerable improvements in several important areas of the service, and there are further big developments in progress at an advanced stage of planning. Since 1965, for example, against a total of about 8,000 beds, almost 1,100 beds have been provided for geriatrics in new or substantially converted buildings. Schemes to provide a further 105 beds are now in progress, and about 700 beds will be provided in schemes now being planned. This shows the priority we have given to the geriatric service. Similarly high priority has been given to providing new mental hospital and mental deficiency beds. The hon. Gentleman mentioned the one at Lynebank, Dunfermline, which has recently been opened. We seek to improve conditions and to make available additional beds in areas of shortage in mental deficiency provision. We have provided over 1,300 new beds in these categories, and nearly 300 more are in schemes expected to complete shortly. On the maternity side, 516 beds are under construction, of which 400 are expected to be completed in the current financial year. This is a considerable story of expansion along carefully selected priority lines.

In all, schemes in progress at present will provide about 2,750 beds. As the grosser deficiencies in long-stay accommodation begin to be overtaken, we are seeing a change of emphasis in the programme. About 1,870 of the beds now under construction are acute beds; that is, about two-thirds of the beds at present under construction, whereas in the completions since 1965 about two-thirds were long-stay beds. As the district general hospital programme gets under way, a higher proportion of the programme will be devoted to acute beds, including short-stay geriatric and psychiatric beds at general hospitals.

On the important subject of value for money in hospital building, some indication was given of what we were doing in the debate on 23rd May. I should like to make JPEG 109 special reference here to our report on the evaluation of the Falkirk Ward, S.S.H.D. Hospital Design in the Note No. 4, and to the section in it entitled "Standards and Costs." This Section makes an analysis of the cost of the ward unit, taking each of its special design features in turn, costing it and discussing its advantages. A particular point about the Falkirk Ward, and indeed about our hospital planning in general, is the emphasis it gives to the provision of good quality accommodation which will be versatile in use. This is our main insurance against the changing needs and patterns of use not only in ward units but also in outpatient departments, health centres, maternity units and units for dealing with accidents and emergencies. Suitability for multiple uses in the patient-care areas means a higher intensity of use and thus enables fewer rooms to suffice for a given work load. Detailed studies have shown that it is practical to design many areas of accommodation in this way.

I now turn to the subject of revenue expenditure and the demands made upon it. Since 1964 the amount of money we have been able to devote to hospital revenue expenditure has increased each year by never less than 2½ per cent.—indeed from 1966 by some 3½ per cent. annually in real terms. The cumulative effects of this have been substantial. For example, a 1 per cent. growth in hospital revenue in 1964 meant an addition of £660,000 while in the present year it means an addition of £1,070,000. In total figures the hospital revenue allocation has increased over the last five years from about £71 million to nearly £111 million, an increase of over 55 per cent.

Despite the very real advances that have been made, it is, of course, true that the pressures of current financial restraints are present in varying degrees throughout every sector of the public services. In that sense the hospital service is not unique. Medical advances, however, are such that there has probably never been a time when the Service has not been under financial pressure, but the fact is that during the last year or two a very considerable number of developments, with major financial implications, have been brought to the attention of the hospital service.

There have, for example, been a number of reports making recommendations about the need for development in 110 various parts of the service, including the Royal Commission on Medical Education, the Salmon Committee on Senior Nurse Staffing Structure, the Zuckerman Committee on Hospital Scientific Services, and the Tunbridge Committee on the Health of Hospital Staff. At the same time negotiations with the medical profession have shown the need for increased expenditure to make it possible to extend study-leave facilities and to permit minimum time off duty; and medical developments have led to a considerable increase in expenditure on drugs, sterile supplies and X-ray laboratory services.

It is not just a question of the building of hospitals but a matter of what goes into it in the form of all the new mechanical needs and technical needs of the hospital. The cost is ever a challenge. Even before all these desirable elements can be considered, the cost of staffing and running the new buildings provided from our expanding capital programme has to be met. This amounts to a considerable sum each year, especially when a new hospital is being opened. In the current year nearly half, 45 per cent., of the total increase is required for this purpose. This means that the additional revenue available in any particular year is always stretched to meet the extra demands on it, and any desirable developments, therefore, take rather longer to introduce than we would like.
