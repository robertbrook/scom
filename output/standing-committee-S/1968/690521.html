NATIONAL MOD (SCOTLAND)

Clause 1
CONTRIBUTIONS TOWARDS EXPENSES OF THE NATIONAL MOD

10.30 a.m.

Question proposed, That the Clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Russell Johnston
: Clause 1 contains the nub of the Bill. As I explained in seeking leave to introduce the Measure on 2nd April, the whole purpose of the Bill is permissive. It is designed to allow district councils which wish to do so to make contributions to the funds of the National Mod, even if the National Mod is not held within their areas. This is a power which they do not at present have, although both town and county councils have it.

I do not think that it is necessary for me to expand this point at length since I covered it in the speech I made on 2nd April. There is no doubt that district councils within the Highland area where Gaelic is spoken wish to have this power. I have a letter from the District Councils' Association expressing support for the Bill and, as the Minister will know, some district councils have already attempted to make contributions in this way but have discovered it to be outwith their power to do so. I need not say more about the provision. The point is simple, the intention is permissive, and I hope that the Clause will commend itself to the Committee.

Mr. Peter Doig
: I am not inclined to support the Clause because I regard it as a waste of public money. We continually hear from hon. Gentlemen opposite demands for reduced public expenditure, yet the same people who clamour for less Government and local authority spending go on to make speeches calling for increased public expenditure of all kinds.

4 For example, I have in mind the speech made by the hon. Member for North Angus and Mearns (Mr. Buchanan-Smith) in Stonehaven last year in which he said:

"Every pound of public expenditure is a pound less in your pocket."

In the area in which he was speaking, substantial grants are provided out of public money, and these benefit the town greatly.

Today we are considering another proposal from hon. Gentlemen opposite which will increase public expenditure, yet not a voice is raised from the benches opposite against this suggestion. Frankly, I cannot understand their attitude. We are asked to agree that local authorities be allowed to spend public money not for the benefit of the people in their areas but for the benefit of those living in other areas, some of which may be so distant from the former that not even 1 per cent. of their ratepayers will be able to attend these festivities.

We are told that the purpose of the Bill is partly cultural, but I suspect that it is simply another method of boosting what is virtually a dying language. How many people in Scotland speak Gaelic? What purpose does the language serve? Can something be expressed in this language which cannot be expressed in English? If people enjoy singing in Gaelic, cannot they enjoy singing in English? Why not allow this language to die of its own free will, as it is dying? I expressed similar views on the Floor of the House not long ago in relation to the Welsh language.

I cannot understand how those who are opposed to public expenditure can be willing to authorise it for something of such little value as this. After all, it is difficult enough in the world today to get people to understand one another. One of the obstacles to there being better understanding among nations is the lack of knowledge of our various languages. If certain languages are dying of their own free will we should do the sensible thing and allow them to die. Instead, however, we find that hon. Gentlemen opposite, who clamour for less public expenditure, want to maintain, sustain and boost a language which would otherwise die.

The Bill is an unworthy reason for increasing public expenditure, particularly since I can think of many ways in which our precious public money could be put to better use. Frankly, I am astonished to hear a group of hon. Gentlemen who have JPEG 5 consistently criticised public expenditure now trying to justify the spending of public money for a flimsy reason such as this. I therefore am unable to support the Clause.

I doubt whether more than a tiny percentage of the people in my constituency would be in favour of spending the ratepayers' money on a proposal of this kind. We have a so-called Highland Society in Dundee; I suspect that it has only a few members. Occasionally I receive letters from the Society, but figures are never quoted—this, in itself, usually indicates that the numbers are small. I have no doubt that if its membership were large it would be shouting the fact from the housetops.

I cannot believe that at a time of financial stringency we should agree to a proposal of this kind. At all times we must make the best possible use of all money, especially public money. We must be selective in deciding how the nation's money is spent and I cannot support a suggestion that it be spent in the way proposed.

Mr. Ian MacArthur
: I had not intended to speak this morning, but having heard the speech of the hon. Member for Dundee, West (Mr. Doig), which I can only regard as appalling, I am bound to rise in support of the Bill, upon which I congratulate the hon. Member for Inverness (Mr. Russell Johnston), particularly since it is much better than some of the Measures which we have seen emanating from the Liberal Party in recent years.

The hon. Member for Dundee, West was right to say that Gaelic is a declining language. It is regrettable that fewer people speak it every year. He was also right to say that interest in this ancient tongue is a minority one. However, it is a minority interest which has a large following outside the ranks of native Gaelic speakers. I was impressed, when the Mod was held in Perth some time ago, by the large number of people from Perth and elsewhere who listened to a presentation of songs and music from this ancient Highland culture, who enjoyed the experience and who, I believe, benefitted culturally from it.

The hon. Gentleman regards this ancient tongue, this heritage, as clumsy. I regard it as something of rare and special value which we should try to preserve. I do not suggest that we should impose the speaking of Gaelic on people, because that 6 would be nonsense. However, when we have, as we must have, a special regard for the survival of this tongue—after all, we teach it in our schools—we should help the continuation of a folk tradition in every way we can. The Mod is one way of doing it.

It dismays me that the view of the hon. Member for Dundee, West seems to be that because this language and this annual celebration of it does not happen to interest him those whom it does interest should have no rights in the matter. He went on to say that this was a waste of public funds, and he expressed surprise that those of us who look for Governmental economies should support the spending of a sum of money in this way, however marginal that sum. That suggestion came strangely from the hon. Gentleman, who accepts and applauds the vast annual wastage of money through, for example, the Land Commission, increases in taxation which have amounted to £l¾ million a day since the 1964 General Election, the imposition of £12½ million in S.E.T. in Scotland as a result of the last Budget and many other Government measures. The tragedy of the vast increase in public expenditure under Labour is that so much of it is being devoted to extravagant and totally unnecessary, indeed harmful, purposes.

We spend some money on museums, opera and the arts generally. If it is right that we should do that, it is also right that a small part of this cultural expenditure should be devoted to the National Mod. I believe that we should do everything possible to encourage the interest which a great many people in Scotland, outside Dundee apparently, have in this ancient language, which many of us love dearly.

In many respects public expenditure is advantageous. Indeed, we get better value in many instances from money spent publicly than we do from money spent privately. For example, if we individually had to pay dustmen—we used to call them scaffies—to have our garbage removed as well as for our water and so on, these services would cost us a lot more than they do at present.

Indeed, considering how little we do on this score, I sometimes wonder how it is possible for us to have any people still living in Scotland. In many cases we measure the harshness, the bareness, of life in many Scottish towns. I welcome this proposal. I should like to see it taken up on a much larger scale. Although the Bill must be permissive, the more successful it becomes and the more money that is spent on this—spent money is not necessarily wasted; it depends on what is got for it—the better.

There is so much in our past that we ought not to lose. It is a great pity that many Scottish youngsters grow up never knowing Scottish songs. I am not talking merely of Gaelic songs. We boast, for example, of Robert Burns, yet how few of our youngsters could properly go through a song of Robert Burns—word and tune perfect? How few could render that great piece, Tam O'Shanter? Mr. Michael Noble
: I want to keep the Committee only a moment or two, and I would probably have been even shorter if the hon. Member for Dundee, West (Mr. Doig) had not spoken, I find myself, as I have occasionally but not always in the past, in almost complete agreement with the hon. Member for Motherwell (Mr. Lawson), basically for these reasons. This small but important Bill will give district councils which exist in Gaelic speaking areas, and indeed, I would hope, one or two district councils in the lowland area, too, if they so wish, the power to spend a small part—it is inevitably a very small part—of their ratepayers' money to support something which I believe is, if not central to Scottish history and tradition, at any rate an important part of it.

I do not accept the view of the hon. Member for Dundee, West that Gaelic is of its own accord a dying language. It has certainly become very much less important in the life of most Gaelic-speaking areas than it was in the past. In my constituency, in Argyll, thirty years ago perhaps 65 per cent or 70 per cent of the people understood or spoke Gaelic. Today it is a much smaller percentage, but it is still an important part of their lives, and many of the young people are learning the language and learning it with enjoyment.

I do not believe that this in any way detracts from their learning as children. Children in countries which have more difficult languages—for example, Jugoslavia and Norway—have to learn three or four languages to enable themselves easily to be understood as they travel round the world. We in Britain are luckier than some peoples, because most people understand English in some form and we can JPEG 9 get by more easily, but it is an important part of the training of children's minds to learn different languages, and I see no harm but great benefit flowing from Scottish children learning two or three languages, of which Gaelic could be one.

I find it difficult to be entirely enthusiastic about the prospects of attracting tourists to Scotland, in particular to the Highlands, if all that we can present to those who come to our very beautiful part of the world is—though certainly different, and imposing and wonderful—scenery, and if in the evenings the only thing they can do is to go to cafes and listen to rock and roll or pop music; in that case they might just as well be in any other part of the world. It is only by keeping the tradition of Gaelic, and so on, that it is possible to put on, as happens in many parts of the Highlands, a ceilidh in the evening—often quite small, but of great interest and attraction to tourists. It is different; it is something they perhaps cannot hear anywhere else, and it is in itself an attraction.

The Bill would give district councils, as the hon. Member for Inverness (Mr. Russell Johnston), who is to be congratulated on bringing it forward, says, the power to contribute something towards what is part of our national tradition. I think this is right. I do not want to expand on the question whether public money will be spent or wasted. One hopes very much—I think all of us do—that as the Mod becomes more fixed in the minds of Scottish people as a whole it will in the long term attract enough support from amongst its enthusiastic supporters not to require a great deal of assistance from district councils or, indeed, from anybody else. However, at this time it needs support to assist it in what it is trying to do. I support the hon. Member for Inverness. This is a small amount of money. It will not be wasted. I hope very much that the Government, too, will support this small but important Bill.

The Gaelic language may not be so widespread throughout the Highland areas as it was in former times, but it is still cherished by an important minority; and in the constituency of my hon. Friend the Member for the Western Isles (Mr. Malcolm MacMillan), who is by some quirk of 10 Parliamentary fate excluded from this Committee, it remains the principal language.

The records throughout the half of Scotland which is the Highlands are for the most part in the Gaelic language. I fear that, if this language were to languish, all that part of Scottish history would be lost. I believe that interest in the Gaelic culture is reviving in the Highlands. This is greatly to be welcomed. Because of the spread of a uniform culture throughout Western Europe we are in danger of losing our distinctiveness, the special contribution which we have made and which we can make to the civilisation of Western Europe.

The Gaelic language has its parallels in many countries in Western Europe in the sense that it is a minority language understood by few, but cherished by few. The Basque language, the Friesian language and a number of other minority languages in Germany, are still protected, preserved and understood by people in those countries. It would be a serious loss if the Gaelic language were altogether to fall into desuetude.

The purpose of this modest but important Bill is the entirely laudable one of enabling communities which have an interest in the language and culture of Gaeldom to support the National Mod. Like the right hon. Member for Argyll (Mr. Noble) I imagine that it is indeed probable—and that may be better—that in time this event will be at least self-supporting, and possibly, through its successful operation, may even enable the profits to be spread more widely in fostering the language and the culture.

This contention by my hon. Friend does not stand up to scrutiny, because, for the most part, there are very few people who speak Gaelic only. At the last census of the language taken in my constituency there was only one person who could understand Gaelic only. If we allow the Gaelic language to die, we shall, on the contrary, create a problem of com- JPEG 11 munication. We shall create a problem of communication with our own history, and we shall find it less easy to understand.

My hon. Friend the Member for Dundee, West asks, cannot those who are enthusiastic devotees of and singers in Gaelic learn to enjoy singing in English? Of course they can.

My place this morning would have been better taken by my hon. Friend the Member for the Western Isles who is himself a Gaelic speaker. Once again, I express my regret at his absence, but congratulate the hon. Member for Inverness, and express the hope that his Bill will be given a speedy passage through the House.

Mr. John P. Mackintosh

Friend the Member for Caithness and Sutherland (Mr. Maclennan)—the historical and traditional reasons—but also because this is not merely looking backwards into an antiquated past. The preservation and encouragement of the language keeps a type of thought, a pattern of mind, and a type of society going which I believe exist now and are vital for the area. My own interest, and that of many people in Scotland, in reviving the Highlands is not merely to make the Highlands an extension of Glasgow or of Edinburgh. Our object is to restore the life, tradition and vitality of a particular culture and outlook in the Highlands which we know and admire and which, in part, are kept going by an outlook enshrined in the language I cannot say too strongly how much people's patterns of thought, contact with their past, outlook and philosophy, from which all of us in Scotland, and indeed the whole world, have benefited, depend on the vitality of this language.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Bruce Millan)
: On behalf of the Government, I welcome the Clause. It has a fairly narrow effect, but an important one in itself, and an important effect in outlining our support for the Gaelic language, which has been the gist of the speeches we have heard in support of the Clause.

The Clause removes an anomaly. In many parts of Scotland district councils are much more interested than town and county councils in helping the National Mod, but they have been prevented from doing so by our existing legislation, whereas town and county councils have been able to get special dispensation from the Secretary of State under Section 339 of the Local Government (Scotland) Act, 1947, to make contributions towards the National Mod. Where county councils or town councils have made applications to the Secretary of State under that Section, authority has been granted. Town and county councils will also be able to make contributions to the Mod under the provisions of the Clause.

Section 339 of the 1947 Act has not applied to district councils, and, therefore, 14 they have been completely excluded from making contributions to the National Mod, unless it happened to take place in their own areas or was convenient for them and they were able to go through the rather elaborate procedure, which did not work at all, to enable them to make a contribution.

The Clause gives the district councils, in particular, the opportunity to make contributions to the National Mod whether or not the Mod is being held within their own areas, and without having to obtain the consent of the authority for the area in which the Mod is being held. In that respect the Bill is very useful, and I congratulate the hon. Member for Inverness on introducing it.

The amount of money involved is likely to be quite small, because the Mod is pretty self-supporting.
