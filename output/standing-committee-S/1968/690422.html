Mr. Wylie
: This proposition has been canvassed in more general terms at an earlier stage of our proceedings. This Amendment is an attempt to ascertain with a little more definition than we were favoured with before exactly what are the Government's intentions in regard to the co-ordination of planning authorities, in the exercise of their duties in respect of structure plans, and the Countryside Commission for Scotland.

Under the 1967 Act the Countryside Commission, in a sense, has fairly wide planning functions. The kind of things which the Countryside Commission has to take into account are planning considerations in many instances. It has certain statutory functions specifically in regard to planning matters. These are set out in Section 8 of the Countryside (Scotland) Act. Subsection (1) of that Section provides:

"The Commission may, and if so requested by the Secretary of State or any local planning authority shall, advise the Secretary of State or, as the case may be, the authority, in relation to any matter arising under the Planning Acts which affects land in the countryside."

146 The Countryside Commission has discretionary powers to give advice either to the Secretary of State or local planning authorities, and a statutory obligation to give that advice if it is requested either by the Secretary of State or by a local planning authority. It is perfectly clear that when dealing with structure plans there will be an overlapping of functions, because the structure plan in a county area will very likely—indeed, almost inevitably—impinge on the jurisdiction and responsibilities of the Commission.

The Secretary of State has to designate certain areas as countryside areas before the Commission comes into play. We would all be interested to know if the Minister of State could give us some idea of the extent to which the Secretary of State has proceeded with the responsibilities imposed on him under that Act. I imagine that, by this time, considerable progress has been made by the Secretary of State in designating areas for the purposes of the 1967 Act.

If it be the case, as it seems to be, that there is an overlapping of functions here, and in the structure plan field, it is difficult to envisage circumstances in which the Secretary of State and/or the local planning authority would not wish to consult the Countryside Commission. There may be a case for writing into the legislation an obligation, rather than leaving it to the looser terms of the 1967 Act.

What happens if the local planning authority does not choose to consult the Countryside Commission? There is no statutory obligation on it at the moment to do so. This Amendment does not seek to make it so, but at some stage there should be a statutory provision for consultation, and if that stage is not to be an early stage, when the matter is in the hands of the planning authority, one would expect it to be appropriate for such consultation to take place when the structure plan is being considered by the Secretary of State. That precisely is what the Amendment seeks. Under the 1967 Act it is not obligatory on the Secretary of State to consult the Countryside Commission, but he has a discretion to do so. If he does so, of course the Commission has a statutory duty to comply and to give him the advice he seeks. The Amendment seeks to say that when the Secretary of State is considering a JPEG 147 structure plan submitted to him or re-submitted he must consult the Countryside Commission before taking a decision. I think he would almost inevitably do that in any case, but it would be better to face the fact that in effect he will have to do this. This should be recognised in the legislation.
