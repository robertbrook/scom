SCOTTISH GRAND COMMITTEE

OFFICIAL REPORT

Tuesday, 26th November, 1968

[Sir MYER GALPERN in the Chair]

AGRICULTURE (SPRING TRAPS) (SCOTLAND) BILL

10.30 a.m.

Resolved,

That if the proceedings on the Agriculture (Spring Traps) (Scotland) Bill are not completed at this day's Sitting the Committee do meet on Thursday at half-past Ten o'clock.—[ Mr. Buchan.]

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Norman Buchan)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the Agriculture (Spring Traps) (Scotland) Bill in relation to the principle of the Bill.

My right hon. Friend told the House in reply to a Question on 6th December last that he proposed to introduce legislation to enable him to ban the use of the gin trap in Scotland. This is the purpose of the Bill. It has been a matter of serious concern to many people that the use of this cruel method of capture is still legal in Scotland—even though only against foxes and otters—a full ten years after it has been prohibited altogether in England and Wales.

As long ago as 1951, the Committee which had been appointed to inquire into Cruelty to Wild Animals—under the Chairmanship of Mr. Scott Henderson—reported that it had received more representations about the cruelty caused by the gin trap than on any other matter, and it concluded that the gin trap was a "diabolical instrument" which caused an incalculable amount of suffering, and that its sale and use in this country should be banned by law within a short period of time. That was 17 years ago.

When, however, certain of the recommendations in the Report were given effect to in the Pests Act, 1954, it was decided that it was desirable to make provision for allowing the continued use of the gin trap in Scotland, and the Agriculture (Scotland) Act, 1948, was amended so that, while generally prohibiting the use of spring traps which had not been approved by the Secretary of State, it 6 also gave a specific power to the Secretary of State to authorise the use of unapproved spring traps, either generally or on conditions related to the animals which might be caught by them or the circumstances or areas in which they might be set. It was under this power that the Secretary of State, in 1954, made the Order known as the Agriculture (Spring Traps) (Scotland) Order, 1954, approving generally the use of unapproved traps against wild animals throughout Scotland.

Within the next four years humane alternative traps were developed and approved for use against rabbits, hare and vermin, but not against foxes and otters, and the 1954 Order was replaced by the Agriculture (Spring Traps) (Scotland) Order, 1958, which allowed the use of unapproved traps against foxes and otters only. It is this Order which allows the use of the gin trap today.

It is clear that in 1954 it was hoped that it would be possible to get rid of unapproved traps in Scotland within the near future, and this hope was fulfilled within four years' time for all the animals concerned other than foxes and otters. The Act had provided that if a humane alternative were found, the Secretary of State might withdraw any authority given to use unapproved traps, and he was acting under this power in 1958, when their use was limited to foxes and otters.

Since 1958, the Humane Traps Panel, which was set up in 1954 to advise the Agricultural Ministers on the approval of new traps, has been concentrating its attention on trying to find a suitable alternative to the gin trap for use in Scotland, and in 1964 the Panel was reconstituted on a primarily Scottish basis.

In spite of its assiduous application to the task of trying to find a humane alternative to the gin trap, it has so far been unsuccessful, and, although it is still hoped that it may be possible to find an alternative within the four-year period specified in the Bill, we have felt it becoming more and more indefensible to continue the use of the gin trap year after year in the hope, so far disappointed, that a satisfactory alternative trap can be found.

This is not to say that we despair of such a trap ever being found, but it seems to the Government that it is not right JPEG 7 to continue this excessively cruel method of capture when there are other ways of taking and getting rid of foxes. While none of them may individually be a complete answer, it should, we feel, be possible to use these other means, perhaps in combination, to contain the problem. It may be that the final solution will be found through increased knowledge in directions quite different from those being examined by the Humane Traps Panel and not by the use of traps at all. I do not think that I should be drawn into making forecasts about these possibilities and what discoveries may be made or what techniques may be devised. My Department, together with the Ministry of Agriculture, is keeping in close touch with relevant research work. It is reasonable to hope that, with advancing knowledge methods may be found of controlling the fox which will, in the long run, be much more satisfactory than the gin trap or any other method known today.

We realise that in some areas, although not everywhere in Scotland, those responsible for controlling foxes depend very heavily on the gin. It is because of this that we have decided, with some reluctance, and in the face of considerable pressure for immediate abolition, that the ban should not take effect till 1st April, 1973—that will presumably be for a period of about four years after the Act is passed.

In devising our proposals, we have kept in close touch with the representatives of the interests most likely to be affected by this proposal—the organisations interested in the prevention of cruelty to animals, representatives of the farmers and landowners and of the freshwater fisheries interests—and have also taken the views of the Humane Traps Panel.

While, not unexpectedly, those most concerned with cruelty to animals would prefer an immediate ban and those who suffer most from the activities of foxes would prefer to see action delayed as long as possible, it is fair to say that most have accepted the period of four years as reasonable notice to be given to enable farmers and others with responsibilities in this matter to consider what will be the best alternative form of control.

Mr. John Brewis
: What action are the Government taking to discover an alternative to the gin trap?

Mr. Buchan
: We may explore that question later. There are experiments in the field of reproduction inhibition and so on which are closely connected with the problem, and we are keeping a careful watch on them. It may be that an alternative method can be found.

The Humane Traps Panel has agreed that four years will give it a reasonable time to continue its efforts to find a humane and satisfactory alternative. Was it not Samuel Johnson who said that there was nothing like an approaching execution to concentrate a man's mind wonderfully? It may be that the time limit will help give a stimulus to the work being done on this matter. Four years was the period of notice given in England and Wales by the Pests Act, 1954, which provided for the use of unapproved spring traps becoming illegal in 1958.

I should, however, direct the Committee's attention to the provisions in Clause 2 for varying the appointed day from 1st April, 1973. As so often happens in such Clauses, the provision looks rather formidable, but it is not really so. It seemed to us only right that when specifying a date as far ahead as April, 1973, we should, at the same time, take JPEG 9 power to vary this date if circumstances should alter. We are accordingly providing that the Secretary of State may advance or defer the date by an order which would be subject to negative resolution. Hon. Members would, therefore, have an opportunity to object to any proposal and to discuss it if they so wished.

Certain limitations are, however, laid down in the Bill.

Mr. Archie Manuel
: Very strong ones.

Mr. Buchan
: The date cannot be advanced to earlier than two years from the passing of the Act, so there will be at any rate a minimum of two years given to those interests concerned with fox control. Therefore, even if this power is used those who rely on the gin at present will have a period of at least two years from the passing of the Act to try to find alternative measures.

Mr. Manuel
: Does my hon. Friend agree that the proviso of the two-year period is valueless by 1971, because it would take two years before the order could be operated after that? Therefore, it would only be of value up to 1971; it would still be 1973 before the order would be operative.

Mr. Buchan
: An appointed day could be brought forward, but it could not be brought forward to nearer than two years from the passing of the Act. There would be at least those two years before it became operative.

If we were to defer the appointed day, this could only be done for one year at a time. We wish to give proper warning to those involved with a very serious problem. There must be one year's notice, at least, of the intention to change the appointed day; whether it is to be advanced or deferred there will be one year's notice. If it is to be advanced, the order must be made at least a year before the date to which it is to be advanced. If it is to be deferred, it follows that it must be laid two years before the new appointed day, because there must be a deferment for one year and notice of one year. Therefore, in the case of deferment there will always be two years from the decision to defer to the date when it becomes operative. This sounds complicated, but I hope that I have made it clearer. As the appointed 10 day cannot be more than one year after the date which is being deferred, there will be one year's notice that the appointed day is to be altered.

I hope that the Committee will agree that, taken together, these provisions provide a reasonable flexibility while meeting the requirement of giving adequate notice to those affected and providing timeous opportunity for consideration of any proposed change.

Having said that, I should make it clear that it is my view that this ban should become operative on 1st April, 1973. We have no intention of using the varying powers in Clause 2.

I also remind the Committee that as the present provisions of the Agriculture (Scotland) Act, 1948, remain on the Statute Book till the appointed day, that Act could be brought into operation at once to ban the gin trap if a humane alternative trap had been found and was available in reasonable quantities. If those provisions were taken care of and a suitable alternative found, we could use the provisons of the 1948 Act to bring in the ban immediately. In such a case, all that would be necessary would be to make an order withdrawing the present authority to use unapproved spring traps on foxes and otters. The provision in the Bill removing entirely the basic authority to give such power would come into force on the appointed day. It could be considered whether in those circumstances there was a good reason for advancing the appointed day.

I recognise this, but the time has come to set a limit date for banning the gin trap, and if that date can reasonably be earlier than 1973, so much the better, but I think I should make clear that the Government's view is that, unless there is a drastic change in circumstances, there should be no question of postponing beyond that year the abolition of these excessively cruel and horrible machines known as gin traps.

May I make two comments about the letters which I, and others, have received? It strikes me that the correspondents are under two impressions: first, that because the gin trap was abolished in England ten years ago, we in Scotland have been guilty of gross inhumanity in not abolishing it as well; and secondly, that Scotland is the only country which retains this admittedly cruel and crude instrument. Gentleman told us, an Order sanctioned the continued use of the gin against foxes and otters.
