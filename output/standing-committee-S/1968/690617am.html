The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Dr. J. Dickson Mabon)

I am very grateful to the hon. Gentleman for making it plain that this is not a general attack on Treasury control and for limiting his argument in this context to the method of fixing the interest rate as being unduly cumbersome. The reference to the Treasury in the Bill follows the wording of Section 12(3) of the 1966 Act which we are replacing. I have considered this point, and I agree that it is not essential to use these words.

The Amendment as drafted would leave the fixing of the interest rate with the Secretary of State. That could be justified on the argument that it was desirable that the rate should be fixed 208 by someone who did not have a direct financial interest, but it might be simpler to allow—as Glasgow Corporation perhaps intended—the local authority to fix the rate itself, subject perhaps to a power for the Secretary of State to prescribe a maximum. There is a precedent for such an arrangement in Section 56(3) of the Water (Scotland) Act, 1946—although no order by the Secretary of State has ever been made under that act.

If the hon. Gentleman withdraws his Amendment, I shall be inclined to consider what is the best course and to put down a suitable Government Amendment on Report.

Clause 31

Dr. Mabon
: Both Edinburgh and Glasgow have local Act powers relating to the repair of houses. It is not our intention to interfere with either of these. There may be other local Act powers which will no longer be appropriate in the light of the provisions of the Bill. What we are doing here is to vary local legislation in order to make it consistent with what we are trying to achieve throughout the whole of Scotland.

In other words, if Edinburgh has an Act stronger than the general provision, then the strength of its local Act will remain. If Glasgow, on the other hand, has a weaker Act, then it will be made stronger by this provision. Others, which have no provisions of this kind, will be brought up to the national minimum. This is what the Clause does. It does not detract from the stronger power in any other local provision.

Dr. Mabon
: We have the power to vary local Orders or Acts in accordance with national requirements. An example is the Caravan Sites and Control of Development Act, 1960. We had some JPEG 221 discussion about that when we discussed the Town and Country Planning (Scotland) Bill. In that we have a provision which allows us to vary local Acts as long as they are varied in a principal Statute, of which this is undoubtedly one. That would be a precedent for this kind of activity. The hon. Member asks, is this the right place so to do? The answer is "Yes".

I have also been advised of another precedent for what we are doing—Section 30 of the Building (Scotland) Act, 1959. If we wish to vary Orders or Acts of a local character we can do so through that Section in order to bring about what is, in effect, a national standard. I agree that this would be taking over these powers, but this is the right way of doing it and I have no reason to believe from Edinburgh that they are in any way distressed about it.

Dr. Mabon
: I have never known a drafting Amendment to bear such heavy arguments. We are taking this power so that we shall avoid two problems. One is the legal complications of duplicate provisions. Therefore, one may wish to repeal certain provisions in order to avoid this.
