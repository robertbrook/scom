We have had the replacement of profits tax by corporation tax.

The detailed calculations made in the report, as such, are not in step with the present-day position. But the landlord is basically in the same position. He is still unable to set off, as an expense, either expenditure on improvements or expenditure on accumulated repairs, if the deterioration which made them necessary occurred before he acquired the property. This means that when a landlord borrows money to meet his share of the cost of a grant-aided improvement or of accumulated repairs associated with an improvement, the proportion of his rent income, which is set aside for capital repayments, counts as profit and is subject to income tax and 264 corporation tax. The landlord in calculating his prospective return, therefore, adds the tax to his other expenditure and so requires a substantially higher rent return before undertaking the work than would otherwise be the case.

Dr. Mabon
: I beg to move Amendment No, 91, in page 58, line 20, column 3, leave out 'subsection (3)' and insert 'subsections (3) and (4)'.

This Amendment repeals subsection (4) of Section 180 of the 1966 Act. As at present drafted, the repeal extends only to subsection (3). Subsection (4) requires that, in relation to clearance area procedure, the medical officer of health should consult the sanitary inspector before making an official representation. With the repeal of the clearance area provisions and the substitution of housing treatment area provisions, which do not rest on official representation, subsection (4) is, of course, no longer necessary.

Amendment agreed to.

: I beg to move Amendment No. 93, in page 59, line 43, column 3, at end insert:

In section 54 the words from 'other' to '33(1) of this Act'.
