Mr. Hendry: As I was about to explain, the methods by which a local authority may borrow are, for the most part, set forth in Section 260 of the Local Government (Scotland) Act, 1947. Generally speaking, that Act lays down five methods of borrowing.

The first method is by means of a document called a mortgage. Here, I must give a special explanation to the hon. Member for Edinburgh, East (Mr. Willis), whom I misled previously by saying that the word "mortgage" had no meaning in Scotland. It has a meaning in Scotland when applied to a method of local authority borrowing whereby a local authority, by means of a very cumbersome deed, puts a charge upon its rates and revenues. That is a very old-fashioned type of deed, and it is also a very expensive one. The hon. Member for Central Ayrshire (Mr. Manuel) as already mentioned the large fees which have been customarily incurred by local authorities when borrowing from the Public Works Loan Board, which has insisted on getting mortgages on loans to local authorities. The type of bond envisaged in this Bill will very largely take the Col. 474 place of these mortgages, which are now old-fashioned, cumbersome and expensive.

The next three methods by which local authorities may borrow are overdrafts, cash credit accounts, and temporary loans or deposit receipts. These are temporary borrowings which are extremely useful, but as they are not really suitable for long-term borrowings I need say no more about them now.

The fifth method envisaged by the 1947 Act is the issue of stock. That is a convenient method for big cities and the larger counties, but it is not really practicable for the small burghs—or even some of the smaller large burghs—because, generally speaking, it is not practicable to issue stock on the market in sums of less than about £3 million at any one time, and that is only practicable for a very large authority.

There are several other ways in which borrowing can be done, but they are mostly internal methods, such as setting up capital funds of the type envisaged by the Bill, or borrowing from internal sources, or borrowing in the rather irregular but very ingenious way mentioned by the hon. Member for Central Ayrshire—the setting up of municipal banks. These, however, are not practical means of getting the big sums of money which local authorities need on a long-term basis.

It has been found by local authorities in Scotland that the issue of housing bonds is a very convenient way of raising money. These bonds are issued for varying periods—generally five years, but sometimes more—and they have the great advantage of being renewable without the issue of a new bond. When the period runs out, the local authority simply puts a minute on the bond in order to renew it. This has the great advantage to the local authority of a saving of Stamp Duty. Stamp Duty is payable to the Treasury on the issue of a new bond, but this is avoided by renewal.

It is also a very convenient way of transferring a debt from one person to another. For instance, if the holder of the bond dies it is very easy to transfer it to the relatives, or even to sell the bond, just by means of a letter to the Image Col. 475 local authority. That does not carry Stamp Duty either, whereas a transfer of stock involves a rather heavy Stamp Duty. My proposal is that the same type of bond should be widened to include all the activities of a local authority.

Having given mat introduction, it would be proper for me now to deal with the Schedule, which has been particularly mentioned by the hon. Member for Central Ayrshire, who has considerable experience of these matters. Paragraph 1 calls for very little comment at all, as it simply deals with the terms on which the bond may be issued. Paragraph 1(a) states that the bonds shall

"bear interest at such rate as the local authority may determine at the time of the issue of the bonds".

and (b) states that the bonds may be issued in denominations of £5 and multiples of that sum. I do not envisage that a local authority will issue many bonds of such low denomination as £5, but I think that in the case of small burghs, in particular, it would be a convenience for them to be able to issue a bond for as little as that for the purpose of small savings. My experience of small burgh borrowings is that a lot of people of modest means are only too willing to lend money in comparatively small sums to the local authority, because they then know that the money is invested locally in a safe security in which they have trust. I think that the small denomination will be quite useful.

Paragraph 1(c) provides that the bonds shall be isued for periods of not less than a year. That is necessary, because there are already provisions for temporary borrowing, and what I have in mind for this bond is not short-period borrowing but long-period borrowing, which is essential if a local authority is looking ahead in a wise way. Paragraph 2 provides that the period for which any local authority bonds are issued may be extended for a further period. That arrangement has been found extremely useful because, as I say, it saves Stamp Duty on the issue of new bonds, and has been found generally useful in the case of housing bonds. Paragraph 3 contains the Stamp Duty provision which is exactly the same as that applying to housing bonds, and has proved the means of a convenient saving to local authorities which are issuing Col. 476 bonds for fairly long periods. It simplifies the method of transfer, and also saves Stamp Duty.

Paragraph 4 contains an interesting provision which has been mentioned by the hon. Member for Central Ayrshire. It states:

"Local authority bonds may be issued by a local authority at such price as the authority may from time to time determine, but not, without the consent of the Secretary of State, at a discount."

The reason the central Government's consent must be necessary in the case of a bond being issued at a discount is that one automatically gives the lender an Income Tax advantage at the end of the period. If a bond of £100 is issued at £98, the effect is that the lender obtains £2 free of Income Tax at the end of the period. The advantage of issuing a bond at a discount is that one gets away from rates of interest, but in municipal bonds it is not necessary as a rule to issue at a discount.

I think that hon. Members will agree that, if bonds are to be issued at a discount, with an Income Tax advantage to the lender, then the consent of the central Government, whether in the form of the Secretary of State or of the Treasury, is a very right and proper thing to have.
