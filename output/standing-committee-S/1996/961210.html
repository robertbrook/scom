New Clause 10
WAITING TIMES BETWEEN CRIMINAL CHARGE AND BEGINNING OF TRIAL

—(1) The Secretary of State shall lay before Parliament not later than 30th June in each year, a report listing, for criminal cases disposed of in—

  1. the district courts;
  2. the sheriff courts; and
  3. the High Court,
respectively, the average number of days which elapsed between the charge being preferred and the beginning of the trial.

(2) A report under subsection (1) above shall include breakdowns of the periods between the charge being preferred and the beginning of the trial by reference to the category of offence, on such basis as the Secretary of State may determine.'.—[ Mr. Davidson.]

Mr. Davidson
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The purpose of the new clause is to ensure that the Secretary that the Secretary of State produces statistics showing the length of delays between the time when a charge is preferred and the beginning of a trial. By that I mean when a charge is laid by the police which informs an individual that he or she is being charged, rather than any step in the legal process. I am conscious that there is scope for ambiguity, which will be cleared up by the Minister 432 when he accepts the new clause in principle and reintroduces it on Report.

The new clause would speed up the system. The Government have based the entire Bill on a false premise. Their argument is that tougher sentences will serve as a deterrent. I believe that a greater deterrent to criminal behaviour is the certainty of being speedily punished. Substantial delays in Scotland between the charging of a person and their being brought to court undermine the credibility of the entire system. A large number of people in my area have been charged with offences but have not appeared in court. The perception spreads throughout the community that they are almost immune from prosecution or action being taken against them, which undermines the credibility and morale of the police and certainly undermines the willingness of people to come forward and provide evidence against those who are charged.

I want to refer in particular to correspondence that I received recently from Govan housing association, a local organisation which provides housing through the housing association movement in an extremely difficult part of my constituency. It said in its letter:

"I'm not sure if you can help, we have a problem. The attached correspondence makes the problem clear. Members, I'm sure, would willingly meet the costs, if the police and courts were capable of producing sustainable results. Unfortunately that is not the case, which is a matter of real concern.


Some parts of Govan are developing into no-go and difficult-to-let areas, which results in further lost income, accelerating the spiral of decline. Your assistance in arriving at a conclusion to this problem would be appreciated."

Of course, it is not my assistance that is needed but that of the Minister.

The housing association goes on to spell out in considerable detail how, over the past few months and years, forced entry by the police to premises in the area while carrying out raids and making arrests has meant that it has been out by hundreds if not thousands of pounds because it has been left to carry the financial burden of that. It said in its letter that it would be quite happy to carry that burden if it believed that anything would happen as a result.

The association gave me a list of addresses, some of which have been raided by the police several times, incurring costs in each case—£436.67 in one case and £328.39 in another, as well as £314.33 due to several break-ins by the police and several sets of repairs. The letter states:

"The Association has in the past accepted and absorbed all of the costs arising from lost rental, forced entries…in the hope that benefits would accrue to the community at large. The operation from the Associations point of view is, or could be, an exercise in futility."

New Clause 12
HARASSMENT


  1. `—(1) In this section, "harassment" means engaging in a course of conduct whereby a person—
    1. follows, loiters near, observes or approaches another person;
    2. telephones or contacts by electronic means or otherwise another person in such a way as to be reasonably likely to cause that other person alarm, distress or fear for his safety or for that of one or more other persons; and "harass" shall be construed accordingly.
  2. A person who harasses another person is guilty of an offence.
  3. A person guilt of an offence under this section may be sentenced on conviction or indictment to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 5 years or a fine or both, or on summary conviction to imprisonment to a term not exceeding 6 months or a fine not exceeding level 3 on the standard scale or both.
  4. A constable may arrest without warrant a person whom he has reasonable cause to suspect to have committed or to be about to commit an offence of harassment.
  5. It shall be a defence to charge under this section if the person charged proves that the accused did not know and had no reasonable cause to believe that his behaviour was likely to cause alarm, distress or fear for personal safety under subsection (1) above.'.—[ Mrs. Fyfe.]

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: We believe that a new statutory criminal offence of 'harassment' is not necessary in Scotland. Any conduct that is likely to cause alarm, annoyance or upset will already be criminal as the common law offence of breach of the peace.

Breach of the peace is a broad and flexible offence. It is not necessary for the victim to have suffered harm or for the accused to have intended that result. As a common law offence, the maximum penalty is life imprisonment for a conviction on indictment in the High Court. Therefore, no changes are required to Scottish criminal law in this regard.

JPEG 439 However, we recognise that there are difficulties for victims of stalkers to gain adequate protection from the civil courts. Member.

Members of the Committee will be aware that the Protection from Harassment Bill was published on 5 December. It will create in Scotland a new legal right to be free from harassment. It will enable a victim of harassment to apply to the court for a non-harassment order, breach of which would be a criminal offence, attracting up to five years' imprisonment.
