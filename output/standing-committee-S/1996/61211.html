Public Expenditure in Scotland

10.30 am

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Michael Forsyth)
: I beg to move, That the Committee do now adjourn.

I am extremely grateful for the further opportunity to debate the public expenditure plans for the first three years of the next Government. The hon. Member for Hamilton (Mr. Robertson) may scoff but that is what they are: they set out the public expenditure plans for the period that will be in the charge of the next Government.

Despite the hon. Gentleman's huffing and puffing, there is agreement across the Floor of the Committee between Conservative and Labour Members about the Budget strategy. The Labour party does not want higher public borrowing and it opposes tax increases at a United Kingdom level. We are extremely grateful for Labour's support for the tax cuts that the Chancellor announced in the Budget.

The Labour party agrees that we must reduce the public sector borrowing requirement and that we should reduce the proportion of Government expenditure to below 40 per cent. of gross domestic product. The Labour party appears to be committed to the current block and formula arrangements for funding Scotland although, of course, it would put them at risk with its tartan tax-raising Parliament.

Let us be clear: the disagreement between us is only about how the existing pot is to be divided up. I exempt from my remarks the hon. Members for Orkney and Shetland (Mr. Wallace) and for Banff and Buchan (Mr. S almond), whose position is different. They would impose additional taxation and would increase the size of the pot. As for the official Opposition, however, the dispute between us is how the money should be divided up.

On Monday, we spelt out our priorities. To meet them we had to make reductions in some aspects of spending. We are honest and open about that: we had to make tough choices and select our priorities. Our baseline is falling in real terms. Under a Labour Government, the same baseline would be falling in real terms—a Scottish Parliament, of course, would threaten further that baseline.

We have made education, law and order, health, industry and enterprise our priorities. That means a tight settlement for local government; reducing expenditure on trunk roads; getting councils to rely more on the private finance initiative for capital; and a reduction in housing investment, unless local authorities make progress with the task of stock transfers. Unless the policies that we pioneered so effectively are embraced, there will be less 2 scope to continue the massive expansion of housing investment in Scotland under a Conservative Government—nearly £12 billion has been invested in improving housing and providing vastly new opportunities for people to opt for home ownership.

Much has been made by some elements about the outcome for the housing programme, but that must be put into perspective. My contribution from the block to local authority investment will remain unchanged for the second year running. Last year, outstanding housing debt was £.9 billion or £6,166 for every council house. This year it is £4.1 billion or £6,521 for every council house: an ever-increasing burden that will fall on the rent payer and, ultimately, the taxpayer, unless action is taken to stem the growth of debt. Will the hon. Member for Hamilton tackle that problem, or will he allow it to continue? Of course there are investment needs. We shall provide £540 million—more than half a billion pounds—over the next three years. Taken together with receipts, the total at local authorities, disposal should be a great deal more than £700 million. That will provide a base to attract further investment from the private sector.

I was delighted to read in today's edition of The Scotsman, in a report about the Scottish Grand Committee, that the hon. Member for Edinburgh, Leith (Mr. Chisholm) had not agreed to Shelter's proposals because the party was not committing itself to specific figures on housing for its first Scottish Office budget. The Labour party says that it is concerned about housing, and I believe it expects to be in office in a few months' time—some time after May—but it is absolutely disgraceful that it cannot say what it will do about housing. It is not fit to be the Opposition, never mind in government.

The very nature of maritime transport obviously involves an element of risk, and the provision of an emergency towing vessel is not necessarily a panacea to all possible exigencies. However, it is possible with forethought and planning to reduce that risk.

Captain Belton's report identified three areas deemed to be of highest priority: first, the Dover strait; secondly, the Minhes; and, thirdly, the south west Approaches. The two other areas identified, but with a significantly lower risk factor were, first, the Northern Isles—Shetland and Orkney—and, secondly, the north channel; that is, Northern Ireland and west Scotland.

The risk analysis applied to the above areas demonstrated that Shetland attracts a ranking of fifth in importance. That evolves primarily from low—traffic densities, which is apparent in comparison with that of the Dover strait. The risk is lessened considerably by the availability and capability of local harbour tugs in both Orkney and Shetland—two in excess of 40 tonnes bollard pull and three in excess of 50 tonnes. A considerable amount of offshore explanatory and production work is undertaken west of Shetland, where BP will be maintaining a purpose-built emergency response vessel, in addition to the normal offshore towing and supply vessels that service the oil industry. Captain Belton's report stated that those resources would provide a reasonable degree of cover to the northern isles.

In addition, Captain Belton investigated the processes adopted in other European countries and, in particular, the type, design, size and power requirement for emergency towing vessels, which the United Kingdom might wish to deploy. The Coastguard Agency decided that further evaluation of the effectiveness of emergency towing vessels was required before any long-term commitment should be made to the considerable Government expenditure involved. Emergency towing vessels are a very expensive option, and we have to be absolutely sure that proper value for money is demonstrably achieved. It would cost more than £2 million a year to provide a JPEG 47 tug, year round, in Shetland. Nonetheless, the Government are exploring very seriously the matter of a possible joint venture in Shetland.

Furthermore, the Coastguard Agency decided that, although arguments can be presented to establish emergency towing vessels on an all-year-round basis, a degree of common sense had to be applied to the number of vessels, their precise locations and the period of charter. As the vessels are of a preventive nature and, generally speaking, weather conditions can be expected to be worse in winter, the Government decided that emergency towing vessels should be established only in the three primary designated areas and, furthermore, only for the winter period. That takes into account the needs of the entire United Kingdom. The Coastguard Agency has been in correspondence with commercial enterprises in Shetland to consider possible joint arrangements in the provision of emergency towing, and how these might be of benefit. The discussion will continue, but without commitment at this stage.

For a trial period, therefore, the Government have undertaken to charter emergency towing vessels, with associated salvage expertise, to assess their effectiveness in the three defined and chosen locations. All coastguard rescue centres maintain an up-to-date database of all local tugs and their availability and capabilities.
