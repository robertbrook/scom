TESTING OF PRISONERS FOR ALCOHOL

Amendment proposed [ This day]: No. 259, in page 33, line 7, after "any", insert "convicted".—[ Mr. McAllion.]

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman
: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking the following amendments:

No. 260, in page 33, line 8, leave out second "who" and insert "whom".

No. 262, in page 33, line 13, at end insert "convicted".

No. 254, in page 33, line 16, at end insert—

"(3) The Scottish Prison Service shall be required to ensure that all prisoners with a problem of alcohol abuse have access to an alcohol education programme during their time in custody.".

No. 255, in page 33, line 17, at end insert—

"'Alcohol Education Programme' means a programme of instruction and counselling in relation to the abuse of alcohol which is approved in regulations by the Secretary of State for Scotland.".

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton)
: It is contrary to good order or discipline for any prisoner to be under the influence of alcohol. It is also against prison rules for a prisoner to be in possession of alcohol. Those points are just as relevant to prisoners on remand as to convicted prisoners. The need to secure the security and personal safety of inmates applies equally to the unconvicted.

No disciplinary action would be taken against any prisoner for consuming alcohol before being remanded in custody. Prison staff would be provided with separate guidance on the time taken for alcohol to be eliminated from the body. A remand prisoner would not be charged with a breach of discipline unless it was clear that the alcohol had been consumed while he was in the custody of the Scottish Prison Service. On that basis I ask the hon. Gentleman to withdraw amendments Nos. 259 and 262. I am grateful for amendment No. 260, which I invite the Committee to accept.

Amendment No. 254, which I shall consider along with amendment No. 255, is technically defective. The Scottish Prison Service is not a separate legal entity from the Secretary of State and it is he who has legal responsibility for prisoners. More importantly, however, the effect of the proposed amendments would be to oblige the service to follow a model that would be 322 neither appropriate nor cost effective. I therefore ask the hon. Members in whose names the amendments appear to withdraw them.

Mr. John McAllion
(Dundee, East)
: I am grateful to the Minister for conceding that the grammar was wrong in the clause and for explaining that it will apply to remand prisoners and convicted prisoners equally. The amendments were intended to clarify those points, and the Minister has now provided clarification, which we accept.

I am not happy about the Minister's comments on amendments Nos. 254 and 255, except to the extent that there is perhaps a technical defect, in that the reference to the Scottish Prison Service should have been to the Secretary of State for Scotland. However, that is a technicality with which the Minister could deal if he supported the principle of the amendment. Perhaps the Minister could expand on and explain his view that it is not appropriate or cost effective to try, by means of educational programmes, to deal with alcohol abuse in prison. One would hope that after release people would begin to lead good, orderly lives. I should have thought that education programmes could help them to do that.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: The sentence planning approach which the Scottish Prison Service has adopted targets prisoners' needs in relation to reoffending. Prisoners are encouraged—and challenged—to confront their offending behaviour. In most cases, prisoners with problems of addiction and alcohol abuse will be able to associate those problems with their offending behaviour. Scarce resources should be targeted on those prisoners whose criminal behaviour is directly related to patterns of abuse or addiction—which is not the same as all prisoners with problems of alcohol abuse.

The Scottish Prison Service adopts a multidisciplinary approach to alcohol abuse that involves health care and education specialists working collaboratively with residential officers. Alcoholics Anonymous is also involved in most prisons, working with prisoners in groups or providing one-to-one counselling as necessary. The approach suggested by the amendments is too restrictive. An educational model of instruction and counselling would not allow the prison service sufficient scope to respond to the needs of individual prisoners.

By providing access to an alcohol education programme for all prisoners with an alcohol-related problem, rather than for those whose criminal behaviour was directly related to their addiction, the amendments would divert resources from where they were most effective.

Mr. McAllion
: I am saddened, but not surprised, that the Minister believes that an alcohol education programme involving instruction and counselling about the abuse of alcohol is not appropriate or cost effective for inmates of Scottish prisons. However, given the current balance of the Committee, I shall not force the amendment to a vote. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.
