Mr. McFall
: Amendment No. 92 refers to hospital direction orders. We tabled the amendments because social workers and other professionals whom we consulted do not see the need for the provision on hospital direction orders because they already exist. The provision could lead to people being punished for being ill when the offence was committed. Implicit in the statements in the Bill concerning genuine illness is a statement of disbelief. We want to probe the Government on the reason for the hospital orders.

Under amendment No. 92 a hospital direction could be made only if the court was satisfied that a hospital would admit the patient. A hospital may be able, but unwilling, to admit a patient. It may be unwilling to spare a bed for a patient who, when better, will be sent back to prison. There is an anomaly in the provision: if someone is sent back to prison, he may suffer a relapse. A patient with a personality disorder may not be suitable for a hospital direction or may be difficult to manage; the court should make a hospital direction only if it is satisfied that there will be a hospital place available for the patient. Hospitals may be unwilling to accept patients under hospital directions, which could have a disastrous effect on the relationship between patients and hospital doctors. If the doctor and patient know that when the patient is better he will go back to prison, where is the incentive to work together to improve the patient's health?

Mr. McFall
: He is a prisoner of the Scottish Office.

During our previous sitting I told the Minister that last Saturday I visited a patient who had been admitted to Carstairs under a hospital order. I know the patient and his family well and he is getting on very well in that environment. If a threat was held over him that he could go to prison, it would have a disastrous effect on him and he might relapse to the extent of being even more ill than when he was admitted to hospital. I have known about that example for some time—it was poignant to speak to that young man last Saturday and to realise that we would be discussing the very issues that affect him and other patients in Carstairs.

The Government must explain why they have introduced the provision. It follows the Reed committee recommendations in England and Wales, but those recommendations do not include a punitive element. The punitive element in the Government's proposals will be far from beneficial—indeed, it will be detrimental.

Doctors may be reluctant to take patients from the courts in such circumstances. They may want to hold on to a patient for longer than medically necessary if they JPEG 193 believe that imprisonment would lead to a deterioration in the patient's mental health. The proposed change would put an extremely unfair burden on psychiatric services. The court should make such an order only if it is satisfied that the hospital will agree.

Amendment No. 93 provides that the hospital should be prepared to admit the patient within seven days of the hospital direction. If the patient is so seriously mentally ill that he falls within the criteria laid down by section 17(1) of the Mental Health (Scotland) Act 1984—that is, if his health or safety, or that of other people, are at risk—he needs to go to hospital and should not have to wait for 28 days. Section 22 of the 1984 Act provides that, when the sheriff in a civil admission decides that the patient should go to hospital, that should happen within seven days.

The effect of amendment Nos. 97 and 98 would be to ensure that a patient is kept temporarily in another psychiatric hospital—not in a police cell or residential care unit—until he is transferred to the hospital named in a direction. The current wording of clause 5 means that a person may not go to hospital until 28 days after the court has made a direction. Our recommendation is to reduce that period to seven days, but the question arises as to where the patient should go in the meantime, as he will have been found by the court to be seriously mentally disordered. He should not be held in residential care, in a private home or hostel or a police cell—all of which the current wording of the clause would allow.

I am unaware of any residential care facilities in Scotland that would be available to keep a seriously mentally ill person in the conditions of security required under a hospital direction. Owing to the Government's failure to conduct a review of services for mentally disordered offenders in Scotland—we have been crying out for one for many years—there are no suitable medium-secure units.

It would be beneficial to delete the provision that residential care could be an option for the court. The court should not be able to require the person to be kept anywhere other than in a hospital. If a person is not kept safe in hospital, there may be a serious risk of suicide or self-harm, particularly because the sentence—the hospital direction—is so devastating.

The aim of amendment No. 100 would be specifically to spell out that a member of staff at the hospital takes the offender to hospital. It is to be hoped that that would generally happen immediately after the court hearing—one of the doctors who has given evidence could do it. Although the court could direct that to happen under the general provision in the clause, it is preferable to spell it out in detail. The most appropriate person to take the sick person to hospital would be a staff member of that hospital, not a police officer or social worker. Local authorities are concerned that, for the first time, they will be used in that capacity to take an individual from court to the hospital. They feel that that is totally inappropriate and out of keeping with their professional role.

Amendment No. 101 would give effect to a hospital direction lasting for seven days. If the patient were not 194 transferred within seven days, the direction would lapse and the prison sentence would come into effect. If the patient is so ill that he needs to go to hospital before he can start his prison sentence, he should go as soon as possible. Ideally, that should be as soon as the court has made the hospital direction. Entering temporary accommodation could be extremely traumatic for a person in mental distress.

Amendment No. 102 is a subsidiary amendment to amendment No. 101. Amendment No. 103 would ensure that, if the Secretary of State wanted to move the patient to another hospital, he must first consult the management of the new hospital—the doctors who are providing the report for the court, the mental health officer who reported to the court and anyone else appropriate. The Secretary of State cannot direct that the patient must go to the state hospital. If a hospital direction has been imposed by the court, it would be unacceptable if the Secretary of State were able to direct that the patient must go to a different hospital from that considered appropriate by the court. Although the clause envisages that that would occur only in exceptional circumstances, it is more likely to occur if 28 days are allowed to elapse between when the hospital direction is made and when the prisoner goes to hospital. That is another reason the Government should accept amendment No. 102, which would change the period to seven days: emergencies would be less likely to arise.

The amendment would ensure that the patient was not unduly prejudiced. The Secretary of State should carry out a full investigation before he changes the hospital named in the direction and it would be inappropriate for him to name the state hospital if the court did not believe that the offender required the degree of security provided there. If, at a later stage, the patient needs to go to the state hospital, the doctors can arrange it and the patient would have the right of appeal. So, although we welcome the fact that the Government have considered the Reed committee recommendations for England and Wales, we think the matter has been handled inappropriately and that hospital order directions should not include a punitive element.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: Clause 11 increases the maximum period of imprisonment which certain courts can impose. The measure will provide the courts with increased flexibility in dealing with the range of cases coming before them. The current maximum periods are three years for sheriff and jury trials. In sheriff summary courts, the maximum is three months, although where second and subsequent offences inferring personal violence or dishonest appropriation of property are concerned, the courts may impose a maximum of six months. In the district court, the maximum is 60 days.

The Government make no apology for keeping the sentencing powers of the courts under review. Hon. Members may recall that the sentencing powers of the sheriff solemn court were increased in 1988 from two to three years. in 1994, we consulted again on whether there was a need to increase the sentencing powers of the courts primarily as a means of relieving pressure on those courts. It was concluded at that time that, in the absence of a system of sentencing guidelines, it would be better to concentrate instead on seeking to have more cases tried in the sheriff solemn court. But matters have changed since then. Business has increased in the High Court and the sheriff solemn courts and the number of remits for sentence from the sheriff solemn court to the High Court has increased. Crucially though, the High Court now has express statutory powers to issue sentencing guidelines.

Therefore, clause 11 provides for the maximum period of imprisonment which certain courts can impose to be increased to five years in sheriff and jury trials and six months in sheriff summary, with up to 12 months for relevant second and subsequent offences. There would be no change in the powers of the lay justices in the district court but, as at present, the powers of stipendiary magistrates who are professional judges would continue to mirror those of the sheriff summary court.

The proposed increases in the sentencing powers of the sheriff and district courts will benefit the operation of those courts and the criminal justice system generally. That greater flexibility should help to relieve pressure on the system—the point made by the hon. Member for Glasgow, Govan (Mr. Davidson) in earlier debates and considered as desirable. It would also enable the sheriff courts to determine the most appropriate sentence for the cases before them without the need to remit to the High Court for sentence.

Increasing the sentencing powers of the courts is not a back-door means of incresing sentences imposed on individual offenders. It is simply a practical step that responds to evidence that the courts could function better with extended powers. More serious cases can be tried locally in the sheriff court, which could result in such cases being dealt with more speedily and improve the efficiency of the criminal justice system. Where the offender is aggrieved about the level of sentence imposed on him, he may appeal. That would enable the Appeal Court to express any general view about the adequacy of the sentence in relation to the offence involved.

224 It has been suggested that the extension of summary powers restricts the present rights of the accused to be tried by jury where the offender faces serious charges. There is no inherent right in Scots law for an accused to be tried by jury. But in any case, sheriffs and stipendiary magistrates already have powers in summary cases to impose sentences of imprisonment of 12 months for certain drugs offences, of nine months for cases of police assault and resisting arrest and six months in a wide range of statutory offences and certain repeat, common law offences. Both are therefore already used to dealing with serious offenders within the remit of their present summary powers.

We believe that the changes will benefit the operation of the courts. Of themselves, they should not lead to significant increases in sentences. Rather, they will allow better distribution of business among the courts enabling them to determine the most appropriate sentence for the cases before them without the need to remit to the High Court for sentence.
