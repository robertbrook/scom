Scotland and Defence

10.30 am

The Secretary of State for Defence (Mr. Michael Portillo)
: I beg to move, That the Committee do now adjourn.

It is a great pleasure to be in the constituency of the hon. and learned Member for Fife, North—East (Mr. Campbell), in the country in which my mother was born, raised and educated and in what my grandfather referred to without exception as the Kingdom of Fife.

Any Secretary of State for Defence is likely to be a frequent visitor to Scotland and I have enjoyed my excursions to Coulport and Faslane, the Army's headquarters the headquarters of 52 Lowland Brigade in Edinburgh, and to the Tornadoes and search and rescue helicopters at RAF Lossiemouth. Further afield, I have visited the Kings Own Scottish Borderers and the Highlanders on deployment in Northern Ireland, and also HMS Edinburgh in the Arabian Gulf.

Scottish units and heroes have played a distinguished role when they have fought for Britain. Throughout the cold war, Scotland was at the front line in defending the United Kingdom from attack from the air or by submarine and it was home to the nations's nuclear deterrent. I want to mention some of that history and to talk about Scotland's role in our national defence in modern times.

Admiral Sir Bertram Ramsey improvised the Dunkirk evacuation in 1940 and commanded the D-day and Normandy landings in June 1944. Seaman George Mackenzie Sampson was the first Royal Naval reserve rating to be awarded the Victoria cross, for his actions on the beaches of Gallipoli. The Scots Guards, who provide a visible Scottish presence and protect the Queen when they are on public duties, fought bravely in the Falklands in 1982 and provided reinforcements for the Gulf in 1991. The Royal Scots is the oldest regiment in the British Army and it has fought in the service of the Crown of the United Kingdom since 1678. It was at Aboukir and Alexdrandia in 1801, Waterloo in 1815 and it fought bravely in Europe in the second world war and bravely again in the Gulf war. Piper Laidlaw of the Kings Own Scottish Borderers was awarded the Victoria cross at the battle of Loos during the first world war, when he played his regimental march, "Blue Bonnets O'er the Border", as his regiment stormed the German positions; he continued to do so until his wounds meant that he was unable to play any longer.

We remember the valiant acts of the 602 (City of Glasgow) and 603 (City of Edinburgh) squadrons during the second world war—602 squadron was at one time commanded by the father of the Minister of State, my right hon. Friend the Member for Edinburgh, West 2 (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton). During the war, those squadrons intercepted the first German bombing raid on British territory and shot down the first Luftwaffe aircraft to hit British soil.

This year marks the 60th anniversary of the first Spitfire aircraft. I am delighted to announce that a Spitfire with strong Scottish connections will be returned home for display to the public. The aircraft, which is known as LA198, was for a time the gate guardian at RAF Leuchars; before that, from 1947 to 1951, it served with 602 (City of Glasgow) squadron. The squadron no longer exists, but the members of 602 Museum Association have long wished for LA198 to be returned so that it could be displayed to the public in Glasgow. The Spitfire needs to be restored. Subject to the necessary parliamentary approvals, LA198 will, I hope, return from storage in England "back to base" in Glasgow before long.

Mr. Portillo

Scottish units and personnel have served in the defence of the United Kingdom for many years. We are a United Kingdom—the Union gives us strength and weight and the ability to wield real influence and do good around the world. That influence is a force for reason in Europe.

The Union also gives us security against any external threat to these islands; security as a highly effective member of NATO; and security for the wider global interests of Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland and England alike.

Scotland is vital to the United Kingdom's integrated defence. Our armed forces and the fabric of our defence structure are totally integrated across the United Kingdom. Each element is an inseparable part of the greater whole. The collapse of the Soviet Union was a triumph for liberal-democratic values, but the parallel collapse of its integrated defence structure offers a stark lesson for those who, against all logic, advocate separatism here, with all that that entails—less security; waste and duplication; uncertainty and confusion; and disastrous consequences for the defence industry.

We must preserve Scotland's vital place in the United Kingdom's integrated defence. The first element of that is the nuclear deterrent. Our operationally independent deterrent provides the ultimate guarantee of the United Kingdom's national security and makes a vital contribution to NATO's war prevention strategy.

Faslane is the Royal Navy's main submarine operating base and home to our force of modern Vanguard submarines—there are two at present and the third, HMS Vigilant, is due to arrive for sea trials soon. The Trident missile defence system will provide the United Kingdom's strategic deterrent and, from 1998, its sub-strategic capability. That system is ably supported by the armaments depot at Coulport, where dedicated service men and women work hard to JPEG 3 maintain the deterrent. In recent years, both bases have benefited from enormous investment in the latest specialised infrastructure, systems and engineering support.

The royal dockyard at Rosyth is another important national asset. Negotiations with Babcock have been concluded and we intend to sign the sale contract shortly, subject only to acceptable final contract terms and conditions. That agreement represents good value for taxpayers and will generate savings of £100 million over 10 years. It will give Babcock and its employees the prospect of a secure, long-term future for the dockyard. To assist in that, the Government have allocated a significant programme of ship refit work to the yard that will last well into the next decade. The forthcoming refits of, among others, the carriers HMS Invincible and HMS Ark Royal and the nuclear-powered submarines HMS Sceptre and HMS Spartan will take place at Rosyth.

I am pleased to make it clear that negotiations with Rosyth 2000 will shortly reach a conclusion and the consortium is preparing to take over the base. I want to pay tribute to Mr. Negus, the industrial convenor of the trade unions of Rosyth, whom I met this morning, and his colleagues, who played a significant part in the negotiations.

Ten regiments of the British Army are recruited in Scotland. Scottish regiments make a major contribution wherever the British Army serves. The Royal Highland Fusiliers recently took part in a brigade-level exercise in Poland—the first ever on that scale in a former Warsaw pact country. A company from the Argyll and Sutherland Highlanders has just returned from a tour in the Falkland islands. The Scottish military tradition is reflected in the depth and strength of relationships between the Army and the people of Scotland. The Army is committed to Scotland and to investment here. We shall demonstrate that with the launch of the Army personnel centre in Glasgow on 2 December. That organisation will be responsible for the career management and administration of the life of every serving member of the Army and the Territorial Army, and will look after more than 500,000 service men and women.

Civilians working for the Ministry of Defence are also strongly represented in Scotland, with 10,000 employees. The Defence Evaluation and Research Agency has six major sites in Scotland. Most of the United Kingdom's current generation of laser-guided weapons and bombs were developed at the trials range at West Freugh in Kirkudbrightshire. It is also home to a state-of-the-art satellite ground station, the first in the world to be awarded a blue ribbon by RADARSAT International of Canada for its work on processing images and data for use in civil projects. We support nearly 100 defence related projects in Scottish universities, worth several hundred million pounds.

The defence industry is also well represented in Scotland. The Government are committed to armed forces that are resourced and equipped to undertake the tasks that we ask of them. The Government have increased efficiency and eliminated waste, which has allowed us to make more investment in the latest and most cost-effective equipment. It enables our industry to compete effectively, both in the United Kingdom and overseas and ensures its involvement at the frontiers of technology.

The Edinburgh—based GEC Marconi Avionics is currently making excellent progress on the ECR90 radar for the Eurofighter, one of the most advanced radar projects in Europe.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: I pay tribute to the Scottish Law Commission for the excellence of its reports, especially in this instance. They were produced after extensive consultation following publication of the commission's discussion paper No. 97 entitled "Contract Law: Extrinsic Evidence, Supersession and the Actio Quanti Minoris"—the action of the lesser amount.

In its final report, the commission reviewed certain aspects of contract law which gave cause for disquiet, and recommended that three rules which had unfair and invidious effect should be abolished. The consultation process revealed virtually unanimous support for the reform.

Although the measures in the Bill are of a technical legal nature, and will be of most interest to the legal profession, they are no less important for that. The reform of the rules will benefit those who enter into contracts, especially those associated with the purchase of houses. The present rules often lead to manifest injustice and have produced confusing and conflicting case law.

The three rules to be abolished are as follows. First, where a contract appears to have been embodied entirely in writing, extrinsic evidence is not admissible for the purpose of proving additional terms. This is known as the extrinsic evidence rule. Secondly, contract terms agreed between the buyer and seller of a house, usually in the missive of sale, are superseded by the subsequent conveyance of the property unless steps are taken to contract out of the rule. This is known as the supersession rule. Thirdly, a buyer of heritable property cannot, while retaining the property, recover damages for breach of contract by the seller. This is known as the rule against the actio quanti minoris, or the bar on damages when property is retained.

I shall explain briefly how the Bill's provisions will reform the three areas of law. Clause 1 will enable evidence to be led to prove contractual terms which are additional to those in a written document, unless the written contract is expressed in manner which would exclude any such additional terms. The Law Commission was unable to find any justification for retaining the old rule which prevents a party from proving a valid and subsisting term of a contract simply because it does not form part of a written document.

40 The old rule is replaced by a rebuttable presumption that all the agreed terms of a contract are contained in the contract document. Clause 1 relates only to matters of proof and evidence, not to substantive law. The fact that additional terms can be proved does not mean that they are formally valid or properly constituted. The clause therefore includes a saving for existing statutory provision regarding the constitution, or formalities of execution, of a contract. [Interruption.]

The hon. Member for Orkney and Shetland (Mr. Wallace) asked which case was referred to. It was the Winston v. Patrick case, and the rule was known by the same name. Clause 2 removes the existing rule that contractual matters agreed between parties are entirely superseded, regardless of the parties' intentions, by a formal deed to implement the agreement even where the deed does not, in fact, implement all the agreed terms. This rule has been criticised because often it frustrates the intention of the parties. For example, a condition may be agreed in the missive of sale of a property as to the condition of a central heating system. At present it is not certain that such an agreement could be enforceable following delivery of the disposition conveying the property. The Law Commission considers that while there cannot be an objection to a rule that a conveyance supersedes a contract in so far as it implements it, this supersession should not extend to terms not implemented by the conveyance. Clause 2 stipulates that terms that are unimplemented or otherwise unfulfilled by a conveyance are not superseded by it. Clause 2 continues to allow parties to contract into the supersession route if they desire to do so for their own purposes.

Clause 3 abolishes the general rule that a buyer of certain types of property who claims that there has been a breach of contract by the seller is not entitled to retain the property and claim damages based on a diminution in the value of property. It is clear that the main application of this rule relates to house sales: its effect is that a buyer seeking to claim for a breach of contract by a seller is obliged to reconvey the house to the seller before being able to raise the action. Clause 3 will allow a buyer of a house or business to claim damages for breach of contract by the seller without being obliged to return the property.

The Bill's provisions, as I explained, are technical and non-controversial. There are no party political or European implications, and the Bill will not even result in an increase in public expenditure. It merely represents much-needed clarification of the law of Scotland. I strongly commend it to the Committee.

Under the common fisheries policy, we are guaranteed a certain proportion of fish, irrespective of whether we catch them in Scottish waters or elsewhere. We have to find the solution to the problems of fish stocks in the North sea by working together with the other nations fishing there to ensure that our common heritage is safeguarded.
