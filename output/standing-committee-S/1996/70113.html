Administration of Justice (Expenditure)

10.30 am

1.

Ms Roseanna Cunningham
: To ask the Secretary of State for Scotland what assessment he has made of the impact of the recently announced Government expenditure proposals on the administration of justice in Scotland; and if he will make a statement.

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Michael Forsyth)
: The Government's plans allow for 187 extra operational police officers, £2 million more for running the sheriff and supreme courts and enough money for the Prison Service to cope with increased prisoner numbers. I am satisfied that those increases will ensure the efficient administration of justice throughout Scotland.

Ms Cunningham
: Will the right hon. Gentleman join me in praising the staff of the Scottish Prison Service, in particular for the skill and bravery they have shown over the Christmas and new year period when dealing with a spate of incidents? For example, in my constituency there has been a rooftop protest, a hostage-taking and, now, an attempted suicide bid.

Does the right hon. Gentleman accept that there is a developing crisis in our prisons and that the time is ripe for a wholesale review of the financing and working of our prison system, especially in view of the expected increase in prisoner numbers?

Mr. Forsyth
: The Government's policy is to ensure that there are sufficient prison places available to meet the disposals of the courts. We are making provision for a new prison, which will be funded under the private finance initiative. The chief executive of the Prison Service has made it clear that he believes that the service is performing well. It has consistently met all of its targets each year—and each year those targets have been made tougher.

I agree with the hon. Lady that the staff of the Prison Service are to be congratulated on the difficult task that they carry out, without our prison system, of looking after some of the most difficult people in our country. They will continue to have the Government's support, both in resources and through the policies that are being implemented throughout the Scottish Prison Service with considerable success.

Mr. Davidson
: Does the right hon. Gentleman accept that the Government's expenditure proposals will not reduce the enormous delay between people being charged by the police and their being brought to court? Is he aware 2 that there are many cases in my constituency of people awaiting trial for 12 to 18 months? Is he further aware that the Minister of State has refused to accept a Labour proposal for the publication of league tables for courts and for offences, which would speed up the administration of justice? There are too many delays and the Government are not doing enough to remedy the problem.

Mr. Forsyth
: My right hon. and learned Friend the Minister of State has certainly not rejected such a proposal. I am delighted to hear that the hon. Gentleman is a convert to—or, at least, has abandoned his party's opposition to—the publication of information about the performance of the courts. Of course, we already provide a degree of information, not all of which is made public. I plan to ensure that people are aware not just of the sentencing policies in different courts, where there is a wide variation, but of the performance of the courts.

I agree with the hon. Gentleman that we need to ensure that the administration of justice is speeded up. Provisions that have been set out in the Crime and Punishment (Scotland) Bill and in previous legislation are designed precisely to achieve that. I look forward to seeing the hon. Gentleman supporting the Government in the Lobby on Wednesday for the Third Reading of the current Bill.

Mr. O'Neill
: Does the right hon. Gentleman recognise that Glenochill prison probably has the greatest concentration of long-term, difficult prisoners? Does he appreciate the success last week—and it was a success—of the staff and the negotiating teams in ending the hostage problem? Did not Mr. Frizell's breathtaking complacency in his interview on the radio yesterday show that the Government are wholly unaware of the difficulties that will arise from the likely reduction in the remission and parole arrangements if the Crime and Punishment (Scotland) Bill becomes law? Does not he realise that the position in Scottish prisons will only get worse if long-term, difficult prisoners have no prospect of sufficient reward for behaving well and trying to rehabilitate themselves in the hope of gaining freedom as quickly as possible?

Mr. Forsyth
: There is a difference between me and the hon. Gentleman. The Government believe that our fundamental duty is the protection of the public and that we must therefore organise the prison system in a way that ensures that. The hon. Gentleman is wrong to caricature our proposal to allow remission to be limited to one sixth as not recognising for good behaviour. On the contrary, we are changing a system that allows people automatic release at the half-way point of their sentences to one where they have to serve five sixths of their sentences and will get the one sixth remission only if they have demonstrated good behaviour and a commitment to taking part in training and other courses that may help them to deal with their offending behaviour or dependencies on drugs or alcohol. That seem to me to be the right way forward. The hon. Gentleman appears to be rather out of sorts with even the hon. Member for Blackburn (Mr. Straw) on this matter.

The hon. and learned Gentleman is right to suggest that the CFP is not fulfilling its objectives, which is why the Government are committed to a root-and-branch reform of it.

Community Care (Quality)

5.

Ms Rachel Squire
: To ask the Secretary of State for Scotland what recent assessment he has made of the quality of community care services.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: The quality of community care services in Scotland is monitored on an on-going basis.

Ms Squire
: Does the Minister agree that the Government's policies on community care are, far from promoting quality, leading to a two-tier service, based on the ability to pay, and are driven by market mechanisms? Does he further agree that the cuts forced on local government will remove choice and flexibility, lead to job losses and place an even greater burden on Scotland's 700,000 parents?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: Local authorities have £662 million in 1996–97 to meet their community care responsibilities; for 1997–98, the grant-aided expenditure allocation includes an additional allowance of £48.4 million for community care. The inspection of Tayside showed quite conclusively that the three authorities could achieve long-term savings of between £3 million and £6 million if they made greater use of the private sector in order to achieve comparable quality. I am happy to involve the community care implementation unit in any local discussions, and for it to give guidance; but if local authorities pursue greater value for money, they will find that they can achieve a great deal more than at present.

Mr. Bill Walker
: Does my right hon. and learned Friend realise that the inspection on Tayside of community care demonstrated clearly that savings of up to £200 a week per person could be achieved by transferring people from public sector nursing homes into the private sector? Is it not the case that, throughout Scotland, many millions of pounds could be transferred in that way? Many more people could thus be catered for; and there would be no need for the so-called reductions and cuts. The local authorities have the power to make decisions; they should be enablers, not providers.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: My honourable Friend is right. Many more people would be treated. We believe that £20 million more could be used to provide services of comparable quality. Given the substantial increase in community care funding of £48.4 million, three should be scope to increase front-line services, provided that councils fund pay increases from efficiency savings and look for the most effective way to deliver services.

Mr. Chisholm
: Does the Minister accept that the two main reasons for the current bed crisis in Lothian and elsewhere are the loss of almost 2,000 acute hospital beds in Scotland since the general election and bed blocking, arising from the inability of most social work departments to fund nursing home places either in the public or the private sectors? Does not the Minister realise that the worst local government settlement for years and the worst housing cuts ever will have devastating effects on the JPEG 7 health of the people of Scotland because of bed blocking, the underfunding of community care and more people becoming ill in winter from living in cold, damp homes?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: We have recently allocated an extra £15 million for health boards to help to reduce pressures which are likely to arise during the winter, including reducing waiting lists and easing bed blocking. I have not the slightest doubt that the most effective use has been made of those extra resources, which my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State has allocated recently.

On a general point, if local authorities were to collect all the unpaid local government tax that is owed, they would be in a very much stronger position.
