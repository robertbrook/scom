Mr. John McFall
(Dumbarton)
: I shall address the points raised by a number of amendments, commencing with amendment No. 126. I am aware that the issue of early release will be extensively debated in relation to clause 28. However, the amendment raises matters that affect the process of early release.

We have received representations from the Association of Directors of Social Work, the Scottish Association for the Care and Resettlement of Offenders and the Law Society of Scotland on this issue. Amendment No. 126 would establish a Scottish prisoners early release panel to examine the issue of early release because we have grave doubts about whether the process outlined in the Bill will be practical or fair.

The Bill would implement a change from the independent external parole board system to a prison-based system, but can such a prison-based system operate effectively without creating any additional tensions in the system? Scottish prisons are already overcrowded; the prison population numbers about 6,000. If a prison-based system were introduced, allowing 12 days' early release in any two-month period—half of that for prisoners taking part in education, counselling and pre-release programmes, and half for good behaviour—it could result in significant tensions as prison staff will carry out the assessments. That will demand a degree of objectivity, which may not be possible at all times. The consistency of judgment that will be required of what the Bill describes as a "prescribed person" will be severe. But there is no doubt that judgments will vary considerably, depending on the personnel involved, their knowledge of the inmate and their relationship with him. It will be impossible to persuade prisoners that such a system is fair.

268 The proposal is also unfair on prison staff, who currently act in a certain capacity and have a specific relationship with prisoners. If we give them another role, in which prison staff have what prisoners will perceive as extra power over them, in terms of the number of early release days that prisoners can accumulate, that will lead to tension in prisons. In addition, it would require an enormous administrative system to be set up. The scale of the system, the objectivity of assessments and the intervals at which assessments will be repeated will increase bureaucracy.

Furthermore, how will the system be linked to prison programmes and to programmes run by outside agencies? At present, the system has to be evaluated in a systematic way. That is a good system, which we are about to throw overboard in haste.

The Law Society of Scotland has also commented on the idea of the assessment being carried out by an independent person. It suggested that the person who will grant early release days to prisoners should be independent.

It is important to set such a proposal within the framework of independent adjudication—that will ensure that prison discipline will not be affected adversely. It is inappropriate for a prisoner's early release to be determined by individuals who are not independent of the prison system.

Insufficient time has passed since the implementation of the Prisoners and Criminal Proceedings (Scotland) Act 1993—which we debated earlier and which came into effect on 31 October 1993—to enable us to evaluate whether that Act deals with the matter effectively. A negative impact on prisoner behaviour is a real possibility. The Government must resolve that matter before the clause is accepted.

Amendment No. 128 states:

"'Prescribed minimum standard' means an absence of misconduct report".

The Scottish Association for the Care and Resettlement of Offenders discussed the proposal with us. It said that good behaviour should be the minimum standard required of all prisoners. The absence of a misconduct report should be all that can reasonably be expected of most prisoners, especially in view of the patchy availability of programmes, education schemes and work in the prison estate. The ability of the Scottish prison service to offer all prisoners the opportunity to participate in programmes that are designed to address offending behaviour is questionable because of the high number of prisoners in Scottish prisons. Any extension of the programmes that currently exist would require significant additional resources. However, in view of Tuesday's Budget and the block grant in Scotland, it is not clear that the necessary resources will be available. However, we shall discuss that matter later in the Bill.

The Government's consultation paper, "Making the Punishment Fit the Crime", states that a prisoner's behaviour should be measured over a reasonable period such that it is demonstrated that his behaviour is sufficiently "good to merit reward". However, considerable difficulties attach to such a measurement. JPEG 269 Requiring prisoners to participate in programmes that are meant to correct offending behaviour might mean that prisoners participate in them for the wrong reasons, thereby wasting limited and valuable resources. For that approach to be effective, it must be linked to the continuation of opportunities in the community, but those linkages are currently inadequate. Her Majesty's chief inspector of prisons in Scotland, Clive Fairweather, has already criticised the absence of follow-on programmes for sex offenders on release, and similar difficulties exist in relation to the programmes for drug abusers. The Government are introducing mandatory drug testing in prisons, but the follow-on programmes do not exist, which means that valuable time and resources are wasted and that work is being dissipated.

New clause 6 fleshes out what the functions of the Scottish prisoners early release panel should be. It states that that panel should determine

"appeals about the decisions of local visiting committees to withhold early release days."

It also states that the panel should consist of

"a chairman … and … not fewer than 6 nor more than 9 other members, appointed by the Secretary of State."

It goes on:

"In making appointments, the Secretary of State shall have regard to the desirability of securing that members of the panel have expertise or knowledge of … community safety … resettlement and rehabilitation of offenders; and … re-offending and recidivism."

It is important that the new clause deals with recidivism. Resources are wasted, especially in the age range between 16 and 25, where there is a horrendous rate of recidivism: it is currently around 80 per cent. Those young people are not affected by prison programmes. They are more dangerous when they leave prison than when they went in, which represents a real danger to public safety.

Grave concerns have been expressed about the fact that the processes in the Bill are not objective and about whether a prison-based system will be effective. With that in mind, it is important to have an independent forum—the Scottish prisoners early release panel would provide such a focus.

The practicalities of the Government's proposals are enormous. For example, to assess the eligibility for release of an estimated 40,000 prisoners is a huge challenge. To give responsibility to visiting committees for the initial assessment in respect of early release would be more efficient and would save money too. The proposed panel would hear subsequent appeals against the visiting committee's decisions. That would streamline the system.

What additional resources will be made available to the voluntary sector to enable the provision of an effective aftercare service? That is where the system falls down time and again. At present, every prisoner has the right to request voluntary aftercare from the social work department. Some local authorities fulfil that responsibility by funding independent agencies such as SACRO to provide certain services such as information, assistance and daytime activities. If the 270 number of prisoners increases significantly, organisations will be extremely unlikely to be able to discharge that responsibility unless an increase in resources is achieved. We need to take seriously the role of agencies involved in resettlement if we are to reduce offending and recidivism and cut down on the revolving door of the prison system and the cycle of offending.

The amendments are tabled, first, for simplicity, secondly, for public safety and, thirdly, for the welfare of those within the prison system and, more importantly, of those in society.
