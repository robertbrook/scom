The hon. Member for Dumbarton wanted to know exactly what we mean by a life sentence. In an automatic life sentence, the judge sets the relevant part of the sentence, which could be six, 10, 12 or 15 years; he sets the minimum time to be spent in custody depending on the circumstances and the gravity of the case. That minimum time is fixed unless it is successfully appealed against. Release after the relevant part of the sentence is a matter for the parole board; if, for example, it is thought that the circumstances of the crime are not of the most serious nature and the judge gives a shorter relevant period, the question of release on life licence comes up before the parole board after that period has elapsed. There is no remission on the minimum period, or relevant part, as set by the judge.

The hon. Gentleman asked if life means life. A life sentence means being under sentence for life so that, after release, the offender is on life licence and subject to recall. The key issue and purpose of clause 1—clause 2 deals with a separate subject—is to protect the public from serious offenders. The provisions would have applied in the Maguire case and would have given the public additional protection, as they will do as soon as they are enforced. That rules out virtually all cases involving children, to which the hon. Member for Dundee, East referred, although if children were involved in extremely serious cases they could come before the High Court.
