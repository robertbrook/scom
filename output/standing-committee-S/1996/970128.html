Clause 1
FUNCTIONS AND POWERS OF OMBUDSMAN

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: I welcome the hon. and learned Member for Fife, North-East (Mr. Campbell) to the Committee. On Second Reading, he said that the Government could not afford him, but I am glad that the call of duty has been sufficiently strong to persuade him to attend.

Mr. Menzies Campbell
(Fife, North-East)
: I thought that it was my duty to be benevolent towards the Minister because there seems to be some dissent in his family about which party should be supported at the next election.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: We are delighted that, after years of coaxing, the hon. and learned Gentleman has at last joined the Committee. We look forward to his contributions.

The Law Society was concerned that clause 1(5) might in some circumstances place on it a duty to provide documents that it did not have and had no powers to obtain, or which it did have but which were confidential. Amendments Nos. 3 to 5 put matters beyond doubt and ensure that the Law Society is required to provide only information that is within its knowledge or documents that are within its possession 4 or control. The amendments also make it absolutely clear that the duty to provide documents supersedes any other duties of confidentiality to which the professional organisation or practitioner might be subject. That addresses Law Society concerns that it might in some circumstances leave itself open to challenge if it passed confidential documents to the ombudsman. Under the amendments, the provision of the documents to the ombudsman could not be legally challenged on the ground of breach of confidentiality. The amendments are clearly in the interests of the person who makes the original complaint and who wishes to see a full and complete investigation of its handling.

To cover the small number of cases in which a relevant document is not in the professional organisation's possession or control, amendment No. 6 gives the ombudsman the power to go directly to the practitioner. The practitioner would be required to provide the ombudsman with the information or document, if he had it, notwithstanding any duty of confidentiality that would otherwise apply. The amendments are designed to ensure that the ombudsman can obtain all the available information that he considers he needs for his investigation.

Dr. Norman A. Godman
(Greenock and Port Glasgow)
: The Minister knows that I received my early training in a shipyard, not in the law faculty at Glasgow. How does the Minister define "confidential documents"? Would such documents include medical or hospital notes and, if so, can he confirm that the parliamentary ombudsman has to seek the permission of the complainer before such confidential documents are handed over?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: Obviously, there can be no question of asking medical practitioners to break the Hippocratic oath and we would not want to do that. I assure the hon. Gentleman that the ombudsman can ask only for documents that are relevant to the investigations and to the professional organisation involved.

It might help the Committee if I give one or two assurances that the Law Society sought on the amendments. The first is that the amendments give the ombudsman the power to require only the production of such information or documents as are relevant to the investigation of the professional organisation's handling of a complaint. The ombudsman does not investigate the original complaint about the practitioner and would have no power to require any document relevant to that unless it had a bearing on how the professional organisation handled the complaint.The investigation is about the way that it was handled. Accordingly, the power will not interfere in any way with the legal profession's principle of self-regulation.

Secondly, to avoid any possibility of misuse of information obtained either from a professional JPEG 5 organisation or a practitioner, I give the undertaking that my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Scotland will direct the ombudsman to use only information or documents that are obtained in the exercise of his powers in connection with his statutory functions and to ensure that his staff do likewise. Those directions wi6ll be made under schedule 3 of the Law Reform (Miscellaneous Provisions) (Scotland) Act 1990. The Secretary of State's present directions are printed at the end of the Scottish legal services ombudsman's annual report, which is available to members of the Committee. I must again stress that the entitlement is to investigate the handling of complaints, not the complaints themselves.

Mr. Menzies Campbell
: The Minister's clarification of the circumstances in which documents should be produced is welcome. The fact that such a clarification was offered to us at the start of our first sitting may indicate the way that the Bill was drafted.

With regard to confidentiality, the Minister has not dealt with the matter that I raised on Second Reading in the Scottish Grand Committee. Although I appreciate that the Law Society of Scotland has expressed itself satisfied, that society is not the Parliament of the United Kingdom and it does not have the duties of scrutiny that we have.

I tabled amendments Nos. 20 and 21 to ascertain whether the Minister understands the consequences of his proposal. Essentially, the proposal is to give a power to an appointed official—however well intentioned or well qualified he or she may be—unless it is satisfied that that power is necessary and that its scope is adequate. We should never give the powers that are open ended.

The Minister sought to deal with some apprehensions by saying that the Secretary of State will give directions to the ombudsman about how he should exercise the Bill's powers. In particular, he will give directions about relevancy in relation to a complaint of mishandling that the ombudsman may be investigating. Those directions may be persuasive, but they are not binding. The ombudsman operates within the context of the Act that authorises him to proceed. With regard to relevancy, he makes the initial judgement about what is or is not relevant—indeed, according to one view, he has the final judgement on that matter. If the Government's view is that the exercise of that judgment about relevancy is to be circumscribed in some way, that should be included in the Bill. That circumscription should not be included in directions that the Secretary of State for Scotland may issue, whether that is done in relation to schedule 3 of the 1990 Act or included—perhaps for convenience—in the final pages of the ombudsman's annual report.

My amendments would protect confidential information that is in the hands of professional organisations or individual practitioners. I must make it clear that my proposal is not a protection for lawyers 6 or their professional organisations: it is for individual members of the public, about whom there may be information or documents that are in the possession either of an organisation or a practitioner and that are otherwise confidential. That might include, for example, medical records or information that is gathered under the subscription of confidentiality, if I may put it that way. The Government recognise that confidentiality is likely to be an issue in this matter—otherwise, they would not have expressly included in the Bill a provision to the effect that the power of the ombudsman overrides any duty of confidentiality that the practitioner may owe.

We should not override that duty of confidentiality. The relationship between a legal practitioner and his or her client is the only relationship that is protected by law. If, during the course of evidence in court, a legal practitioner is asked any question about his relationship with a client, he is entitled to refuse to answer it. Someone in a confessional, who has heard the confession of a criminal, does not have that protection. As a matter of policy, it is very unlikely that priests would be forced to divulge what is said to them but, as a matter of law, they do not have the protection that is afforded to the relationship between a legal practitioner and his or her client.

We are entitled to ask why the legal services ombudsman is so special that we are asked to give him a power to override a fundamental principle of the legal system, when the other ombudsmen—some might say, with equally important responsibilities—are not to be given that power.

Dr. Godman

Mr. Campbell
: I understand that to be the case. If I were aggrieved by the way in which a complaint against a legal practitioner had been handled and applied to the legal services ombudsman for redress, inviting him to investigate the handling of the complaint, by implication, it could be argued with some force that I had given up the right to confidentiality. But there are other circumstances in which a legal practitioner may hold papers that are entitled to the virtue of confidentiality, and which would be subject to the measure that the Minister proposes.

I shall give the Committee a practical example. Two people are involved, as passengers, in a road traffic accident and sue the driver of the car in which they were travelling but, for one reason or another, the action does not proceed properly and they are dissatisfied with the way in which their solicitor has conducted the case. They apply to the Law Society of Scotland to complain about that and are dissatisfied with the way in which it handles their complaint. They then apply to the ombudsman who says, "I want all the files connected with this case."

10.45 am

Of the two passengers, only one wishes to persist with his or her complaint to the ombudsman, but the papers held by the practitioner include the medical reports of both. If the person who is conjoined to the original action for damages for personal injuries decides not to proceed with the complaint against the solicitor who is thought to have handled the case inappropriately or ineffectively and his or her fellow passenger continues to pursue it, the ombudsman will ask for all the files and papers held by the practitioner. He will receive not just medical records relevant to the person persisting with the complaint but the medical records of the other passenger. The practitioner cannot say "These records are confidential between me and passenger No. 2." As the complaint of passenger No. 1 is the subject of the investigation, he cannot stand on that confidential relationship.

The medical records may contain material which a man or a woman would not want to reach public domain. It will be said that all ombudsmen, by definition, are reasonable men who will act responsibly and whose motivations are proper, and that there will be no negligence in the way in which they deal with complaints. Such assertions are always made when an extension of power is offered and people otherwise minded question to that extension.

The Bill did not properly address the issue of confidentiality. The Government are now saying that confidentiality—this is a pejorative way of putting it— 8 may simply be swept aside. Amendment No. 20 would provide that a practitioner or organisation is not obliged to provide information or documents unless that individual or organisation is entitled in law to do so—that is, when they are not bound by any principle of confidentiality. Amendment No. 21 would provide that when an issue arises between the ombudsman and the practitioner or organisation, either can go to the sheriff under the summary procedure, which is designed to be short and sharp, and ask for an order of the court to determine the issue.

The principle of confidentiality should not be easily and lightly overridden. If an issue arises between the ombudsman and the practitioner or organisation, there should be a way to resolve it. My two amendments would better protect the interests not just of those who complain to the ombudsman about the handling of their complaint by the Law Society of Scotland, but of third parties who may not have made any such complaint.

There is nothing more fundamental and important to the confidence of the public in the system of law and in the provision of legal services in Scotland than to know that their complaints will be properly handled. Member for Leith may find that in due course outraged constituents will say, "We had no part in any complaint to the ombudsman, but it seems that in the exercise of his power under the Act, he has taken possession of information relating to me which was entirely confidential." We are not dealing with those who make the complaint or with protecting the Law Society or the Faculty of Advocates or members of either of those bodies but with protecting the interests of third parties who have not made any complaint. That is the issue.

Amendment No. 20 which contains the words,

"entitled in law to do so",

is apt to deal with confidentiality as well as with any issue arising under any other statute. For example, certain statues regulate the provision or information about children. It is not possible, in certain circumstances, freely to use information in relation to children. Adoption is an example of that. Therefore, the reason for the phrase,

"but only so far as it is entitled in law to do so",

is to deal with confidentiality, which is the most likely issue to arise, as well as with the fact that there might be statutory restrictions. Other Acts might restrict the JPEG 13 ability or an individual to use information. That is why the amendment is phrased in such a way. The hon. Member for Leith may be apprehensive about amendment No. 20, but he will appreciate that amendment No. 21 provides expressly that where any question arises about the legality of the provision of information or the production of a document, either the ombudsman, the individual or the organisation can go to the sheriff, using summary procedure, and ask for an order to determine whether the document should be made available or the information provided. If the ombudsman thinks that individuals or institutions are unreasonably or illegally failing to do what they are supposed to do, there is a means to deal with the problem.

As the hon. Member for Dundee, East said—and his point is well taken—the question of relevance falls to be determined under the Bill by the ombudsman. There does not appear to be any effort to introduce a concept of reasonableness. Although the government and other hon. Members might not intend it, the legislation opens up a potentially rich seam of litigation for lawyers. Questions might arise as to whether the power provided by the Bill is being exercised in such a way as entitles it to be described as an abuse of discretion. The whole question of relevancy seems not to provide the bulwark of protection that the Government appear to think that it might. The difficulty of taking a stand on an issue where a wide discretion is afforded to an official is that the official's decision might well be the subject of challenge by way of judicial review in the Court of Session. If we are anxious to get on quickly with processing complaints—an entirely sound and reasonable point of view to take—creating circumstances in which judicial review becomes commonplace will hardly aid their swift passage.

For those reasons, when we reach the point at which we must make a decision on such matters, in accordance with the appropriate procedure, I shall test the mood of the Committee.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: I thank the hon. Member for Leith, my right hon. Friend the Member for Dumfries and my hon. Friend the Member for North Tayside for their support for the measure. The purpose is to enable members of the public to make complaints to the ombudsman and for the ombudsman to have the power to investigate them. There is a strong consumer interest. It is in the interests of the complainer for the ombudsman to have access to all the necessary documents, but the ombudsman is concerned only with how the complaint was handled, not with the actual complaint itself. The relevancy test must relate to the handling of the complaint. The ombudsman can ask only for the relevant papers.

I must tell the hon. Member for Greenock and Port Glasgow that medical records are unlikely to be relevant to how the Law Society handled the complaint. The ombudsman cannot make a blanket 14 request for documents; the request must be relevant to how the professional organisation handled the complaint. But we think that it is in the interests of the complainer that the ombudsman has all the relevant information.
