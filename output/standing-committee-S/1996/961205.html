clause Clause 53
title CONFISCATION OF ALCOHOL FROM PERSONS UNDER 18

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton): I beg to move amendment No. 249, in page 55, leave out lines 31 to 36 and insert—

'(2) Where a constable has reasonable grounds for suspecting that a person of or over the age of 18 has alcoholic liquor in his possession in a public place and that a person—

  1. has supplied such liquor to a person under the age of 18 for consumption in a public place; or
  2. intends that the liquor should be consumed in a public place by a person under the age of 18,'.

This technical amendment clarifies the policy intention that alcoholic liquor may be confiscated from a person aged 18 or over in a public place only if it has been supplied or is intended for supply by the adult to an under-age person for consumption in a public place. The amendment is intended to address the issues highlighted in evidence from southern Ireland relating to the enforcement of collecting drink from a young person who tries to pass it to somebody over the age of 18 as soon as a police officer hoves into sight.

Mrs. Maria Fyfe (Glasgow, Maryhill): The topic was discussed on Second Reading and I asked this question then, but as I did not get a satisfactory answer, perhaps the Minister can respond to it today.

When a crowd of young people are gathered in a street, they are not all likely to be under or all likely to be over the age of 18—some will be under 18 and some will be over 18. I cannot imagine how a police constable will be able to distinguish one from the other and say "You, you and you can hold on to this alcohol, but these others may not". It is important that young people under the legal age should not consume alcohol in the street or other public places, but enforcing this requirement may cause some difficulty.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: I am grateful to the hon. Lady for allowing me to explain the purpose of the amendment more fully. Its purpose is to put it beyond doubt that police powers to confiscate alcohol from an adult in a public place extend only to circumstances where they have reasonable grounds for Col. 388 suspecting that the adult has supplied—or intends to supply—the alcohol to under-age persons for consumption in public.

The provision in the clause seeks to close a significant loophole that came to light during the operation of the provisions in the 1988 Irish legislation. When police approached a mixed age group of public drinkers—including drinkers who were both under and over 18 years of age—the alcohol was always to be found only in the hands of the 18-year olds and over, and could not therefore be confiscated. But the test in the clause is "reasonable grounds"—a phrase that is also mentioned in the amendment. Clearly there may be problems of enforcement, but if the police officer has reasonable grounds he can proceed and a report is sent to the procurator fiscal.
