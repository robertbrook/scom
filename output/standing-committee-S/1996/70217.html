title Local Government Finance

10.30 am

1. Mr. Eric Clarke: To ask the Secretary of State for Scotland what assessment he has made of the benefits of giving local authorities additional funds to eliminate the backlog of people awaiting help for adaptations, extensions to their homes and placement in nursing homes. [14706]

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton): Substantial resources have been provided to local authorities to meet the needs of community care client groups. It is for local authorities to determine how those resources are deployed.

Mr. Clarke: I thank the Minister for his reply. I wrote to his right hon. Friend the Secretary of State on 22 January and I have not yet received a reply. Like many of my colleagues I have constituents coming to my surgeries to tell me of their problems in relation to the matter. People are holding beds in hospitals when they should be back in their homes with the adaptations and extensions that they require. Many others are awaiting places in the community and want to return there. I accuse the Government of transferring the responsibility from the national health service to local authorities without there being proper and adequate funding.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: The hon. Gentleman's letter will be answered as quickly as possible. I shall look into the matter.

The number of blocked beds in Midlothian is about 20. The authority and the health board are aware of the situation and the hon. Gentleman will be aware that we allocated an additional £15 million this year to help health boards deal with peaks in admissions during the winter period. The Midlothian health board's share of that was £2.1 million.

As for resources in Midlothian, the housing association grant for 1996–97 was £1.27 million and there is also a special needs capital grant. The Minister for Local Government, Housing and Urban Regeneration will soon meet Scottish Homes and that will be one of the issues that will be considered. About £750,000 has been committed to assist with the re-provisioning associated with the closure of Gogarburn and St. Joseph's hospitals.

Mr. Bill Walker: Does my right hon. and learned Friend agree that one of the problems we are facing is the fact that the huge sums of money transferred from the national health service to local authorities have not been Col. 2 spent as wisely and as well as they could be? For example, the £200 per head of population in this county and in Perthshire demonstrates clearly the difference between making use of what is available in the private sector and the local authority in its role as provider. In fact, the local authority should be an enabler, not a provider, if it cannot provide at the same price.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: There is no doubt that the inspectors' report in relation to Tayside found that the three councils, Angus, Dundee City and Perth and Kinross, could make savings of between £3 million and £6 million a year to make more use of the independent sector. That suggests that between £33 million and £66 million could be saved in Scotland, which would mean that far more people would be placed in appropriate accommodation. The report said that the standards were comparative between the public and private sectors and were of good quality.

Mr. McAllion: Why cannot the Minister understand that beds are currently being bought in national health service hospitals? Patients who have been assessed as needing nursing home care cannot be discharged to available beds in nursing homes in the community, because local authorities do not have the funds to pay for those available beds. If the Government can find £60 million for a royal yacht and £70 million to double the military cadet forces, why cannot they find the money for the national health service and for elderly people in this country?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: I shall explain to the hon. Gentleman that the funding for community care has increased enormously in recent years. The amount of extra funding for 1996–97 is £56,513,000 for Scotland—an increase of 8.5 per cent. for community care. For social work, it will mean an increase of 6.4 per cent. overall. The hon. Gentleman will be interested in the recent comparative study into local authority expenditure that is before the Committee this morning. It confirms that each person resident in Scotland receives £27 more per head than each person in England in respect of social work. The Scots are doing well in comparison with everyone else in Britain.

Mr. George Robertson: Why does not the Minister come clean and tell us that the agenda is the privatisation of residential care in Scotland, and say that the Government have no other objective in mind? At the same time, why do not he and his colleagues start telling the truth about this year's local government settlement? The so-called saving of £60 million from local government reorganisation really represents a major cost to local authorities because of the gerrymandering of local council boundaries two years ago, for which the Government were responsible. Does he not realise that, all over Scotland, people know the truth; they blame the Government for the sacking of teachers, home helps, and care assistants which is a direct result of the settlement? Last year, his right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Scotland was forced into a humiliating U-turn at the Kilmarnock meeting of the Scottish Grand Committee. Surely, in their dying days, the Government will have the decency to make yet another U-turn.

Image Col. 3

Mr. Bill Walker: On a point of order, Mr. Stewart.

The Chairman: Points of order are taken after questions.

Mr. Walker: It is a question of truth.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: The hon. Member for Hamilton (Mr. Robertson) will be aware of the statement made by the right hon. Member for Dunfermline, East (Mr. Brown)—the shadow Chancellor of the Exchequer—about his extreme reluctance to increase income tax. That statement straight away undermines the local government campaign of the hon. Member for Hamilton.

On the question of the private sector, we are arguing for more and clearer information for residents so that they can make informed choices as often as possible. We propose to publish a new directive on information which will require authorities to publish the comparative costs of residential home care for the elderly. One of the key points, which I have stressed already, is that the standards of quality are comparable between the private and the public sectors and, therefore, value-for-money issues are all the more important to ensure that funds are properly and appropriately applied.

Glasgow council tax payers and ratepayers do not want funds prioritised for such things.

title Air Passenger Duty

4. Mr. Welsh: To ask the Secretary of State for Scotland what assessment he has made of the impact on rural and island communities and the transport network at Scotland of the proposed increase in air passenger duty. [14709]

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: The Government are giving the impact of the forthcoming increase in air passenger duty, particularly for sensitive routes in Scotland, their fullest consideration.

Mr. Welsh: Why have the Government introduced this anti-Scottish airport tax, which will directly hit every commuter and raise business costs? If the Government truly want to lower taxes, why have they introduced yet another anti-Scottish, Tory tax?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: Flights between the Scottish islands, which are operated with small aircraft, are exempt. In addition, there are increased levels of revenue support, allowing the Western Isles council to offset the impact of the duty in its subsidy of the inter-island Stornaway-Benbecula route, which is operated with larger aircraft. That has ensured that that route is affected by the tax in a similar way to the inter-island routes in Orkney and Shetland.

In 1996–97, we gave a subsidy of £9.2 million to Highland and Islands Airports Limited for some 10 airports, and we also subsidised the lifeline air services between Glasgow, Tiree and Barra. We recognise the financial fragility of many air services, so we are giving the impact of the increase our political consideration.

Mr. Gallie: Does my right hon. and learned Friend agree that the Government's open skies policy has brought great advantage to Scottish air travellers in recent years with a massive array of cheap flights between Scotland and other United Kingdom cities? Will he take account of the fact that the fixed rate tax disadvantages Col. 6 some companies? Is it in his remit to suggest to my right hon. and learned Friend the Chancellor of the Exchequer that in future airport taxes be related to air fares on a percentage basis rather than a fixed rate basis?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: I will certainly pass on my hon. Friend's comments to the Chancellor of the Exchequer. Strong passenger growth has been experienced after the introduction of air passenger duty and it is expected that that growth wil continue. We also believe that the open skies policy is beneficial in bringing more tourists to Scotland.

Mrs. Liddell: Is the right hon. and learned Gentleman aware that the air passenger duty is only one part of the heavy transport burden that the people in the highlands and islands of Scotland have to carry? Will the Government consider instigating a wide-ranging study of that additional burden as it relates to air fares, ferry charges and excessively high fuel prices? Fuel prices in the highlands and islands are 11 per cent. higher than in urban Scotland, which must have a significant social and economic impact.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: We have already offered to study such issues at the Highland convention; I am grateful to the hon. Lady for her support for that recommendation, which will arise again at the convention's next meeting.

title Skoal Bandits

5. Mr. Dalyell: To ask the Secretary of State for Scotland what is his policy on Skoal Bandits. [14711]

Mr. Michael Forsyth: All oral tobacco products were banned on 1 January 1993.

Mr. Dalyell: Would it be reasonable to suggest that the Secretary of State send to Sir Gordon Downey his considered comments on the contents of pages 124 to 128 of the book "Sleaze" by David Leigh and Ed Vulliamy, which concern the delicate matter of lobbying for the tobacco industry?

The Government's position at that time was somewhat awkward because we were publicly committed to negotiating a voluntary agreement with US Tobacco on the sale of Skoal Bandits, while we were also seeking legal advice on the possibility of introducing a ban. The question before us was whether, by concluding a voluntary agreement, we would prejudice any subsequent ban and lay ourselves open to challenge. In the event, regulations to introduce the ban were laid, and we were challenged. Unfortunately, the Government were defeated in the courts. Regulations were then introduced under a European Community directive, which is why it took us until 1993 to institute the ban.

Friend the Member for Brigg and Cleethorpes lobbied a number of Ministries at the time, including the then Ministry of State, Department of Trade and Industry, Mr. Alan Clark, on which occasion he was accompanied by the hon. Member for East Kilbride (Mr. Ingram), who was concerned about the effects of job losses in his constituency. The only other representation that I received was from employees in East Kilbride who stood to lose their jobs—and subsequently did so.

Mr. McFall: The Secretary of State will know of the dishonourable campaign surrounding Skoal Bandits. The book "Sleaze" names him and his hon. Friend and asserts that the "No turning back" group was lobbying hard for those jobs. The Scottish Office gave £200,000 in grant for fewer than six jobs.

Given the Secretary of State's involvement in Scotland against Drugs and other groups, will he assert this morning that Skoal Bandits which cause mouth cancer, especially in young people, have no part in the health programme in Scotland? Mr. Forsyth: I fear that the hon. Gentleman must have prepared his question before he heard my answer to the hon. Member for Linlithgow, in which I made it clear that Skoal Bandits were banned on 1 January 1993 as part of an EC directive that applies throughout the Union. It is rather disreputable for the hon. Gentleman to attempt to make that an issue. The hon. Gentleman is obviously not aware that the grant paid to US Tobacco for it to establish itself in East Kilbride was regional development grant, which was introduced by a Labour Government, and over which Ministers had no discretion. We subsequently abolished it, so that all support is now discretionary. The hon. Gentleman ought to pay heed to the statement made by Madam Speaker a few days ago.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: No, I will not go as far as that. I entirely rule out privatisation. All services must be free at the point of delivery, but if there is strong local support, each case must, of course, be considered on its merits. The people of the north-east of Scotland will enormously benefit from the Stonehaven project, because they will get a purpose-built hospital with the best facilities much more quickly than by any other route.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Raymond S. Robertson): I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the Transfer of Crofting Estates (Scotland) Bill [Lords] in relation to the principle of the Bill.

The Bill is an enabling measure to give the Secretary of State for Scotland the power to dispose of his crofting estate to bodies that he believes are representative of the crofters on the estates. Such organisations are commonly referred to as crofting trusts. I shall refer to them as such, although members of the Committee will wish to note that there will be no requirement that the bodies be trusts in the traditional legal sense.

The Bill is the result of the crofting trusts initiative, which my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Scotland launched in the autumn of 1995, when he made it clear that he would be willing to consider transferring the Scottish Offices's crofting estate to a crofting trust. The aim was to give crofters on the estates greater control over the land and to offer them all the advantages that private ownership can bring.

In February last year, my right hon. Friend published a consultation paper entitled "Crofting Trusts", which set out his proposals in greater detail. It made it clear that the Government would be willing to consider transferring the estates at no consideration to help to ensure the viability of all the trusts; that the transfer would be on a voluntary basis; and that existing tenants who became tenants of a crofting trust would continue to enjoy the protection, rights and obligations that apply under the existing legislation. The paper also made it clear that the Government have no intention of selling the estates to other third parties. Its proposals were widely welcomed.

In drawing up the Bill, we have developed proposals which allow a flexible approach to address the differing circumstances of individual crofting communities. We know that there is no one type of trust that will address the needs of all the communities involved and we do not wish to limit unduly the options available to crofters who wish to pursue such an opportunity.

The Bill gives my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State the flexibility to transfer land to different types and sizes of trusts that might be proposed by the crofters. It would allow individual trusts to be based on part of an estate, a whole estate or aggregations of estates.

Clause 1 gives the Secretary of State a discretionary power to dispose of his crofting property and associated rights on terms to be agreed with the recipient body.

Clause 2 requires the Secretary of State to consult the Crofters Commission before disposal and that the disposal should be made only to bodies that are representative of crofting interests in the property to be disposed of and which have the promotion of the residents on the property as their primary objective.

Clause 3 provides the Secretary of State with the power to provide financial assistance towards the costs of establishing a crofting trust and to defray expenditure incurred by a trust in effecting the transfer of the property.

Col. 58 Clause 4 ensures that the Scottish Office is not required to undertake all conveyancing and related work involved in a transfer. Instead, trusts will commission that work with assistance, when that is agreed, under the provisions of Clause 3.

Clause 5 removes for all time any right of pre-emption over any property in the estate, while providing for compensation to be paid to those who have lost the right of pre-emption.

Clause 6 ensures the continuation of a right to buy, which arises from the Land Settlement (Scotland) Act 1919 and applies to tenants of non-crofting land who have been on their holdings for more than six years.

The Bill offers an important opportunity to the crofting tenants of the Secretary of State for Scotland. It offers them benefits of ownership and will allow them to make their own decisions and to address their own needs and circumstances in their own way. I commend the Bill to the Committee.
