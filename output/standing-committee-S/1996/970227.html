Mr. Robert Maclennan
(Caithness and Sutherland)
: I should like to deal with amendments Nos. 16 and 17.

4 Under clause 2, the Secretary of State could dispose of crofting interests only to a body that was

"representative of the crofting interests",

and which had

"the promotion of the interests of persons residing on such property as its primary objective."

Amendment No. 16 would substitute the term "the people" for "persons", to make it plain that the body concerned should represent the entire community in the area, and not simply a number of persons residing on the relevant type of property. The crofters will be the dominant interest on most of the Secretary of State's crofting estates. They will form the body to which the transfer might be made. It is important, however, to include the interests of non-crofters resident on the estates in the articles of association, or any other document that establishes the body. It should not represent a handful, or even a majority.

Our experience of how crofting trusts might work is limited and based especially on the Assynt crofting trust. If such trusts are to work, a consensus about the desirability of establishing a trust, its purpose and its organisation for the benefit of the community, is vital. The use of the word "persons" in clause 2(1)(b) makes it theoretically possible for the promotion of the interests of a limited number of residents on the estate to be the measure's primary objective. That is not satisfactory. The term, "the people" is comprehensive and emphasises the necessity of taking into account the interests of everyone who lives on the estate.

Amendment No. 17 is similar to amendment No. 3, which the hon. Member for Dumbarton (Mr. McFall) tabled, but is more specific. Some people might consider crofters' interests to lie in early disposal of the property. The property might be handed over to people who were not interested in developing crofting interests. The articles that set up the body might not make it sufficiently plain that the crofting trust had in mind,

"the economic and social wellbeing of the … community",

not the short-term economic interest of the majority who established the body.

The House and the Committee has, or should have long-term interests in mind. Dominance of the trust by a group of individuals who are prepared to pass on the title at the first opportunity, possibly to an absentee proprietor for a sum that is in their short-term interest, would not fulfil the Bill's objective. The purpose of the Bill should be beyond doubt or peradventure. The absence of a reference to the economic and social wellbeing of the community as opposed to the interests of the persons is unduly restrictive, and I believe it appropriate to widen what is covered by the clause.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Raymond S. Robertson)
: Amendment No. 1, tabled by the hon. Member for Dumbarton, would require any crofting trust to be representative of the local crofting community. That would extend the remit of a crofting trust in two ways. It is our understanding that the term "crofting community" would normally include the local residents in any crofting estate, together with persons such as doctors, teachers and ministers living outside the estate but forming part of the local community.

By introducing a reference to the community in the district of the property to be disposed of, the amendment would require that a crofting trust represent the interests of not only those directly involved in the estate, but those on neighbouring land which may be in private ownership or form part of the Secretary of State's crofting estate that has not been transferred to crofting trust status.

We believe that members of the wider local community, such as doctors, teachers and ministers, and others living outside the estate, should be taken into consideration when the Secretary of State considers proposals for crofting trusts. The Bill provides that the Crofters Commission shall have regard to the general interests of that crofting community when advising the Secretary of State. However, we do not believe that a crofting trust should be placed under a duty to promote the interests of those who are not resident on the land that it owns. The Bill as currently drafted would not prevent non-residents from being members of the trust, or from benefiting from its objectives. It does, however, mean that the interests of the residents would be primary, as is only proper.

Amendment No. 16 would require a crofting trust to have the promotion of the interests of the people residing on the relevant property as its primary objective. The Bill, as drafted, uses the term "persons" instead of "the people". Although, on one interpretation, the amendment could be said by implication to require the trust to ascertain collectively what the interests of "the people" were and to promote them, there is a further interpretation. I am advised that the effect of the amendment could be to require that a trust should simultaneously promote the interests of every individual on its land, even where those interests were conflicting or mutually exclusive. This would, I suggest, place a potentially impossible duty on any crofting trust. It could, in effect, give any individual a right of veto over decisions taken by a trust for the good of the crofting community as a whole.

As drafted, clause 2(1) will ensure that a crofting trust will be properly representative of crofting interests and will promote the interests of those living on the estate. Its management role will involve making decisions that are designed to promote the interests of residents, but it is inevitable that there will be occasions when trusts cannot satisfy the wishes of all tenants. All landlords at some time may face such situations.

6 Amendment No. 17 would require crofting trusts to have the purpose of maintaining and developing the economic and social well-being of the relevant crofting communities. The term "crofting community" would include doctors, teachers, ministers and others not resident on an estate but who formed part of the community. The amendment would therefore extend the remit of a crofting trust to include responsibility, in part, for people not resident on the estate.

Amendment No. 3 would require the Crofters Commission, when advising my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State on proposals for crofting trusts, to have regard to local community development in the district in which a property to be disposed of was situated. The hon. Member for Dumbarton said at the Scottish Grand Committee that if the Bill's central objective is to strengthen community development in sparsely populated areas of Scotland, then it needs to be strengthened in a number of ways. My noble Friend Lord Lindsay has made it clear that the concept of local community development lies at the heart of the Bill. However, I cannot agree that the Bill needs to be strengthened.

10.45 am

The amendment is unnecessary. The Bill as drafted already allows the Crofters Commission to take account of local community development when preparing advice to the Secretary of State. It can do this when considering the general interests of the crofting community, as it is required to do, or as any other matter which the commission considers to be relevant.

The term "local community" is also, I would suggest, uncertain. The local community could include more than just the residents on the property to be disposed of. It is quite proper that the commission should have regard to the development of a wider community, but such development, which goes beyond the land and members of a crofting trust, cannot be of such importance that the commission should be required to give it special, or preferential, attention.

The Bill will allow the commission to give due regard to local community development when framing its advice to my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State. I would therefore ask the hon. Member to withdraw his amendment.

That defies reason. The people might be regarded as having their interests protected if they were predominantly of a particular view, but unanimity would be to go against the meaning of language. It is important that it should be predominantly the interests of the people, not just persons who could be a small minority of the people.

As drafted, the Bill is seriously defective and capable of causing great difficulties in administration. It could give rise to widespread anxiety among those in a crofting community owned by the Secretary of State who may feel themselves in a minority and at risk. These people might be prepared to do anything to prevent such a development. I hope that the Minister will think again.

Mr. Macdonald
: There is a serious point here that needs to be explored in some detail. We must make clear who will run these estates, and for whom, if a transfer of ownership occurs. Transitory residents are not, of course, at the centre of our concern, but it is important to remember that non-crofting tenants have a right to be considered and represented in the proposed structure.

The Minister did not answer the point about the phrase "of persons" perhaps meaning one, two or three persons. On some estates crofting tenants are as few as that.
