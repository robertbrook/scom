Mr. James Wallace
(Orkney and Shetland)
: I beg to move, That the Committee do now adjourn.

I wish first to express, on behalf of all members of the Committee, our pleasure at meeting in Selkirk and our thanks to the Scottish Borders council for the arrangements that it has made for us today. When my parliamentary colleagues were considering a venue for our debate, we thought it fitting that it should be in the constituency of my right hon. Friend the Member for Tweeddale, Ettrick and Lauderdale (Sir D. Steel), who will soon be retiring as a Member of Parliament. I know that I speak for political friends and opponents alike when I pay tribute to the 32 years of his distinguished service, not just to the political life of Scotland, on the international and national stage, but at a constituency level, serving the people and interests of this special part of Scotland.

We chose deliberately to debate education and local government finance today. As the scenes outside the Room have shown, that subject is of great concern to people in the Scottish Borders and, indeed, throughout Scotland. We are debating it on the day after the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities published a report, which it commissioned from Pieda. The report showed that the spending restrictions this year and next 2 year could be responsible for as many as 17,000 job losses in Scotland, and the loss of about £315 million in household income.

Furthermore, we are debating the matter on the day when chief officials of local government services in Scotland felt obliged to make their views known publicly in a letter to The Scotsman. They wrote.

"We do not dispute the Government's right to determine its public spending priorities, but it is important to understand the consequences for local government services. It is equally important to be aware of the many new duties and responsibilities which have been placed on local government as a result of public demand, or new legislation. These new responsibilities must be properly funded."

It is significant that those are the views of officials with no party political axe to grind. Since Friday, I have had the pleasure of being in the Borders with my right hon. Friend the Member for Tweeddale, Ettrick and Lauderdale and my hon. Friend the Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire (Mr. Kirkwood). Apart from enjoying the air, it has given me an opportunity to experience at first hand the sense of anger and outrage felt by so many local residents about the scale of possible cuts in services, especially in education, which will be forced on the Scottish Borders council by the Government's straitjacket.

More than 500 people attended a public consultation meeting held in Galashiels on Friday night to voice their concerns about the matter. On Saturday, in the main street of Hawick, people were only too anxious to sign a petition calling on the Secretary of State to think again. It is the recognition of the value of a broad, high-quality education system, and the threat directed by the Government at the heart of that system, that are fuelling such concerns.

Such worries are not confined to the Scottish Borders. A little over two weeks ago, I visited Uyeasound primary school in Unst—the northernmost island in Britain. For the second time in two years, its future is being reviewed, even though the school roll has more than doubled during that time. The Shetland Islands council is also reviewing the future of two other primary schools, one being at Haroldswick in Unst where, reluctantly, school board members have accepted the inevitability of closure; the other is Quarff primary school, whose future is threatened as the Shetland Islands council tries to keep within its budget.

In the other half of my constituency, the chairmen of the Stromness academy and Kirkwall grammar school boards wrote to me, saying:

"Parents and teachers feel strongly that schools in Orkney have been hit hard enough over the last few years and that any further reductions in human and material resources could only lead to a drop in the current high standards maintained in our schools."

The leader of Argyll and Bute council wrote to me, saying:

"The unique combination of circumstances which affects our financial position menas that we are not discussing efficiency savings, but service cuts which will seriously affect the provision of school education, community education, social work services, libraries and sports facilities, roads and transportation, as well as the financial support which we provide extensively to groups and organisations throughout Argyll and Bute."

In Edinburgh, possible proposed savings include 44 redundancies being made in education, community centres being closed in the evenings and weekends and the scrapping of hot school meals—sometimes the only hot meal that many young children have in a day. The JPEG 3 closure of community centres cannot happen in a vacuum. Many young people go there in the evenings; if they cannot go to a community centre, we may find trouble building up on the streets.

In Dundee, cuts of £4 million in education are reported, including the doubling of music tuition fees. Those cuts, serious enough in themselves, come on top of the cuts made this year, many of which were quite well documented by the Educational Institute of Scotland in its leaflet. Indeed, the Association of Directors of Education in Scotland estimates a cut of £90 in education budgets in the current year, and it claims that early indications are that Scotland will suffer cuts of as much as £104 million in 1997–98.

The lifeblood is being sucked out of the education system in Scotland. Some of the consequences were eloquently expressed at that consultation meeting in Galashiels on Friday night. For example, any threat to physical education teaching is a threat to the teaching of a subject important to the health of young people. It is an essential part of a balanced curriculum, which helps to develop some of the skills that every adult needs.

The threat to curriculum support teachers in the expressive arts undermines the Government's stated objective, set out in the five to 14 curriculum programme, which stresses that the development of the expressive arts is not a frill but is important in developing abilities and methods of communication, and that it makes an important social and economic contribution to society. The Government's own words are now under threat. The withdrawal of curriculum support music teachers will put added pressure on classroom teachers, who may not be able to teach music on their own—and out of the window will go school concerts.

The Minister cannot point an accusing finger of profligacy at Scottish Borders council. It employs 37 per cent, fewer staff per 1,000 head of population than the Scottish average. In an answer to my right hon. Friend the Member for Tweedale, Ettrick and Lauderdale on the amount of uncollected poll tax and council tax, about which the Government are making great play. Councils such as those and Scottish Borders council are now finding that they have to pay a penalty for their previous prudence and will not get the same level of benefit as other councils.

The purpose of the debate is to try to make Ministers see how the consequences of their policy are threatening the lifeblood of our educational system and to get them to address the problem with adequate funding. There is no sign in the White Paper, "Raising the Standard", which was published on Friday, that the Government have begun to understand the gravity of the situation.

If properly done, there is much to commend teacher appraisals. But the Government complain—and I have seen quotes from the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland, the hon. Member for Aberdeen, South (Mr. Robertson), the Minister responsible for education—that the participation rate has been far too low in some parts of Scotland, so appraisal will now be put on a statutory footing. That fails to appreciate that if appraisal is to be property done, it requires the head teacher to spend quality time wit the teacher being appraised. To do that, the teacher will not be in the classroom, so support teachers will be required. Where will the money come from when local authorities are strapped for cash? The Government fail to appreciate the financial consequences of some of their proposals. How can the people of Scotland understand £800,000 being spent on a pre-election advertising campaign for nursery vouchers when some vital educational services are under threat? I do not think that they will be taken in by that sort of publicity.

What sort of priority do the Government have when they are making provision for additional assisted places for the few, while for the many the story will be larger class sizes, less breadth in the curriculum for secondary pupils, less music tuition, less equipment, fewer books, unrepaired buildings, less school transport, dearer school meals, and perhaps no hot meals? That is the scale of the problem and one which faces the many.

The Secretary of State recently told COSLA:

"There is no pot of gold under my bed."

The shadow Secretary of State was quoted as saying at a Scottish Forum for Business lunch that Labour had no "secret pot of gold" for councils. When the Prime Minister has such difficulty in getting his candidates to sing from the same hymn sheet on Europe, it is rather telling that both the Secretary of State and the shadow Secretary of State manage to sing in harmony on the subject of local government spending cuts.

To claim that there are no resources is simply not true. The Scottish block could have been increased to the tune of £180 million or more if the Chancellor had not opted to try to win a headline by cutting the standard rate of income tax by 1p. Liberal Democrats said then that it would be paid for in higher council taxes and reduced services. When we divided the House against that tax cut, the Labour party sat on its hands and forfeited any credibility in a campaign to protect vital public services.

Education is a vital service and investment in it is essential if Scotland is to be competitive in the 21st century and a generation of young Scots are to be given JPEG 7 the opportunity to fulfil their potential. We shall not compete successfully on the basis of a low-wage, low-skill work force—plenty of countries will do that better than us. Our competitive edge must come from doing what other countries cannot do better: better design, better marketing, higher added value to better innovation, taking full advantage of the revolution in communications and information technology. That all requires proper investment in education.

It is frustrating that when we should be investing in education, to equip our young people with skills to meet the challenges of the millennium, we are engaging in a debate to keep secure the fabric of what we already have. As the head teacher of Philip Haugh community school in Selkirk, Sheena Vannan, said—as reported in this week's Peeblesshire News—

"We are returning in this region to pre-war standards, when instead we should be educating children for the millennium."

At base, we have an education system in Scotland of which we can be rightly proud; it has achieved much for many generations of Scots. That is why we should build on, not destroy, it. When he came to office, the right hon. Member for Stirling (Mr. Forsyth) said that he would be a listening Secretary of State. Let him listen now—let him listen to the people to whom he talked outside and let him respond to their fears and concerns, and to the concerns of communities across Scotland. He must invest in our schools and in our young people. I ask him not to destroy the life chances of a generation of young Scots.
