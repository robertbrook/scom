Crime and Punishment (Scotland) Bill

Clause 29
PRISONERS HELD ON REMAND

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

10.30 am

Mr. John McFall
(Dumbarton)
: I wish to draw the Minister's attention to subsection (2) of the clause, which provides for the retrospective grant of early release days, determined by the extent to which adverse reports have not been made by prison staff to the officer responsible for granting early release days.

It appears that the prisoner will have no right to be notified of such reports and no right of reply. I want to probe the Minister on that point to discover whether the clause will create a two-regime system for remand prisoners and other prisoners. If that is the case, why not have just one set of early release provisions relating to all prisoners, including those on remand?

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton)
: When passing a custodial sentence, courts are required to have regard to time spent in custody on remand. Usually, this will lead to a backdating of the sentence to cover remand time. Clause 29 provides that, in such cases, the prisoner's behaviour on remand will be assessed retrospectively with a view to the award of days of early release. Matters such as the notification to the prisoner of the determination of the assessment and the prisoner's right of appeal against the determination will be dealt with in prison rules. The new section 39 of the Prisons (Scotland) Act 1989 makes provision for rules to cover these matters in relation to both clauses 28 and 29. Remand prisoners will have similar rights and responsibilities to those of sentenced prisoners. The main difference is that obviously it is not known, in the case of remand prisoners, whether they will be found guilty, and receive a backdated custodial sentence. That is why, in their case, the award of early release days has to be retrospective.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 29 ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Clause 32
SUSPENSION OF PERIOD OF EARLY RELEASE

Mr. McFall

The Chairman

Mr. McFall
: This continues points that were made earlier. The amendments would ensure that an offender was not triply punished for the commission of a single new offence. As the Bill stands, the offender would, first, be punished for the offence itself, secondly, be liable to recall to prison from the date of its commission and, thirdly, be additionally punished with custody for the period between the date of release from prison and the date of the new offence. That is retrospective punishment. In cases in which the original prison sentence was 12 months or more, there would be a fourth punishment for a single new offence because the clause will make such offenders liable to double the period of imprisonment for the new offence. Will the Minister comment on that triple punishment?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: The amendments would weaken the provisions in the Bill, which we have introduced to provide the courts with adequate sanctions to deal with released prisoners who go on to reoffend within a relatively short time of their release from prison. The amendments would shorten the period during which a court could order return to prison from the whole of the period of earned early release to just the remaining balance of that period at the point where the released prisoner committed the subsequent offence.

While I have listened to the arguments of the hon. Member for Dumbarton (Mr. McFall), I do not consider that the arguments in favour of this change are strong. I do not wish to see the courts' powers in this respect reduced, and I regard it as appropriate that a released prisoner should risk losing the whole of any period of early release if he commits a further offence in the period that follows his release.

We consider that a prisoner who has been released early from prison owes a particular obligation to the community to lead an orderly and law-abiding life. For that reason, a prisoner who fails in this way and commits a further offence, either while he is under supervision or while he is in the period of one sixth of the sentence he previously received, should be subject to the additional sanction provided by clause 32. Where the prisoner has been released early from a long sentence, he could face return to prison for an appreciable period, and that period could be in addition JPEG 315 to the period imposed for the new offence; but that reflects the breach of faith that a return to offending represents.

It can be argued that this provision may be severe. The subsequent offence may be regarded as minor, although we view even minor offences by released prisoners as a serious setback. But the answer is not to reduce the potential length of the period of return, as the amendments would. The court is not required to order the return of the prisoner if it does not think that return is merited, or it may order the return of the prisoner for a period shorter than the whole of the period of early release. There is thus ample discretion available to the court to consider the circumstances of the individual case and to make an order accordingly. It would be wrong to curtail the possible period of return, as the amendment would do, since that may prevent the court from dealing with due severity with the serious cases.

Mr. McFall
: I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

TESTING OF PRISONERS FOR ALCOHOL

Mr. McAllion
: I beg to move amendment No. 259 in page 33, line 7, after "any", insert "convicted".

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to take the following amendments:

No. 260, in page 33, line 8, leave out second "who" and insert "whom".

No. 262, in page 33, line 13, at end insert "convicted".

No. 256, in page 33, line 16, at end insert—

"(3) The Scottish Prison Service shall be required to ensure that all prisoners with a problem of alcohol abuse have access to an alcohol education programme during their time in custody.".

No. 255, in page 33, line 17, at end insert—

"'Alcohol Education Programmed' means a programme of instruction and counselling in relation to the abuse of alcohol which is approved in regualtions by the Secretary of State for Scotland.".

Mr. McAllion
: Amendment No. 259 is a probing amendment to find out whether testing for alcohol applies to all prisoners, whether they are convicted or on remand. We believe that the clause is unclear and we do not know whether it applies to all prison inmates. The amendment simply seeks clarification from the Minister.

Amendment No. 260 is a minor amendment for grammatical purposes. It has been noticed by someone in the legal fraternity that the Scottish Office and the JPEG 319 clerks who draft these Bills have made a grammatical error and the amendment is to correct this.

Amendments Nos. 254 and 255 come from the Scottish Association for the Care and Resettlement of Offenders. They state that offenders with a problem of alcohol abuse, which may also be related to their offending behaviour, should be encouraged to participate in an alcohol education programme. That would provide them with an opportunity to learn to amend their use of alcohol in the expectation that, if they reduce their alcohol use on release, they will have an improved likelihood of also reducing their offending.

SACRO recommends that such programmes should be available to prisoners, but it is essential that they are based on certain theoretical models and that they are properly evaluated. SACRO believes it to be essential that such alcohol education programmes are run by approved bodies and that they are of high quality.

That brings us back to an earlier debate in Committee where there was concern that, because .of the inadequacy of resources made available to the prison service, its programmes were not properly staffed and were run by people who were properly-qualified. In the event, prison warders have been encouraged to do overtime and to run some of those programmes—perhaps as a cost-saving measure, but that would not really apply in these circumstances.

May I also draw the Minister's attention to an example I heard of when I attended the national poverty hearing here in Westminster. A man who spoke at that hearing spent some time—perhaps four or five years—in Barlinnie prison for a very serious offence. Because of his personal circumstances and his alcohol addiction—which led to his becoming an alcoholic—he ended up offending and going to prison. In prison he was able to give up alcohol and to turn his back on it and I am sure that he was able to benefit from the programmes that were available to him then.

However, those programmes will be of benefit to no one if the Scottish Office does not radically alter the circumstances facing a prisoner when he comes out of prison. When that man eventually emerged from prison, 320 returned to Glasgow and asked for council housing he discovered that, because he was in arrears in his tenancy he was not eligible to be allocated a council house. The council had been instructed by the auditors not to allow the man a new tenancy until his arrears had been cleared. The man was then placed in the Simon community, which looks after vulnerable people at a cost to the same council of some £300 per week. It would have been much easier to have cleared the arrears and given him a council house. He could not take up further education and training while in the Simon community or he would have been thrown on to the streets because to qualify for a place there he has to be entirely dependent. [ Interruption.]

The Chairman
: Order. I have the highest regard for the Simon community and enjoy talking about it and hearing of its good works, but not while the Committee is discussing this amendment. The hon. Gentleman is straying a bit wide of the subject.

Mr. McAllion
: I was trying to speak quickly in order to get the point across. My point is simple. This is a very worthy group of amendments which I hope that the Minister will take on board, particularly those from SACRO. Even more importantly, I hope that the Minister realises that when prisoners come out of prison at the end of these programmes and try to adjust to society there can be problems. We owe them the opportunity of at least starting a new life. They do not get that now because of the crazy rules about limiting public expenditure that have been introduced by this Government over the past 17 years.
