Ms Cunningham
: Amendment No. 138, as will be immediately clear, is merely a paving amendment, so my remarks will relate to amendments Nos. 139 and 142, which are the substantive amendments in the group.

Amendment No. 139 adds a further test that the

"conviction was otherwise unsafe or unsatisfactory."

228 It introduces a third criterion for assessing whether a miscarriage of justice has occurred. The reason behind the amendment ought to be fairly clear: the appropriate test to be met in relation to appeals is contained in section 228(2) of the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1975. I think that the provision should be supplemented by allowing the Appeal Court to overturn the conviction if it considers it unsafe. It is necessary for the Appeal Court to be able to remedy miscarriages of justice whether or not they fulfil the technical requirements of section 228(2). It is wrong in principle that the Appeal Court should uphold a conviction even if it is satisfied that the conviction is unsafe based on the evidence presented to the jury at trial. The Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1975 should be amended to provide for the Appeal Court to overturn the conviction if it is considered to be unsafe in much the same way that the English system requires the English Court of Appeal to allow an appeal against conviction if it is considered unsafe.

Amendment No. 142 follows on from amendment No. 139. It adds:

"and a reasonable explanation may be without prejudice to any other explanation constituted by

  1. the evidence in fact not being available at the trial; and
  2. the evidence not being disclosed by the accused as a result of undue influence.

The amendment aims to add a definition of what constitutes a reasonable explanation of why evidence was not brought forward at the original trial.

The Appeal Court should be able to hear additional evidence that was available at the time of the trial, provided that there is a good explanation for its failure to be produced in the original case. The current statutory provisions on additional evidence are contained in section 106(3) of the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995. It is appropriate to concentrate on the words of the statute, which relate to the non-availability limit on the introduction of fresh evidence. The cases of Church and Elliott, both in 1995, are relevant. In the Elliott case, the court considered that the provision son additional evidence constituted as requirement that must be satisfied before the court could hear additional evidence. Therefore, whereas section 228 of the 1975 Act allows a review of any alleged miscarriage of justice, where that alleged miscarriage of justice relates to the non-production of additional evidence, the court is constrained by the terms of the statute to hear that evidence only if it was not heard at the trial and it was not available and could not reasonably have been made available.

I understand that the first report of the Thomson committee in 1971 suggested that additional evidence could be presented on the basis of a reasonable explanation for the failure to adduce the evidence at the trial. The provision under section 228 (2) of the 1975 Act, from which the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Act 1980 derives, was considered by the court in the JPEG 229 Elliott case. The Lord Justice-Clerk, Lord Ross, was of the view that, because Parliament did not adopt the reasonable explanation test suggested by the Thomson committee and instead provided the wording currently contained in the statute, Parliament clearly intended the more exacting test. The decision in the Elliott case reaffirmed the law as it stood prior to the Church case.

Concern has been expressed in a number of quarters that the High Court's interpretation of the additional evidence test does not meet the demands of justice. Indeed, Lord McCluskey has suggested in his book on criminal appeals that non-availability raises a simple question of fact: either the evidence as available or it was not. But one must also ask to whom it was available. Lord McCluskey concludes that availability must be considered from the appellant's standpoint, taking full account of what was practicable for him at the trial.

In all the circumstances, the view taken in the Church case-a reasonable explanation for the failure to reproduce the evidence based upon a liberal interpretation of the statute-points to the route for reform. It is clear that the policy objective pursued by the Lord Justice-General is, in that case, the correct one and that the proper test for dealing with the problem raised in the Church case would be along the lines of an excusable failure to produce the evidence at the original trial.

That explanation comprises the case for the amendments. I very much hope that the Minister will take on board those sentiments.

The intention behind clause 14 is to introduce a greater flexibility in the criteria on admissibility of 230 fresh evidence on appeal. the amendments however, would either restrict the criteria or their concerns are already covered by the provisions in the Bill.

The purpose of amendment No. 139 is not fully clear. The issue of a conviction being unsafe or unsatisfactory can be fully reviewed within the general ground of appeal, namely that there has been a miscarriage of justice.

The Sutherland committee, albeit in the context of replacing the ground of appeal of "miscarriage of justice", argued that introduction into Scots law of the formulation of a conviction being "unsafe" would give rise to problems of interpretation. It recommended that the general basis for an appeal should remain that a miscarriage of justice had occurred and that this ground should not be qualified or limited in any way. That was accepted by the Government.

The committee did, however, go on to say that its conclusion that there should be one broad, unqualified, general ground of appeal, applicable to all cases, was not incompatible with the identification of specific examples of cases which could satisfy the general ground. It thought that this could be used to settle certain points of doubt in the current law and to give a pointer to future development of the underlying principle. The additional instance that amendment No. 139 attempts to introduce is clearly not of the specific concrete type of the other two instances in the Bill and could introduce, rather than settle, doubts about the basis of appeals. I therefore ask the hon. Lady not to press the amendments.

Amendments Nos. 57 and 121 would restrict the fresh evidence that the Court of Appeal could admit to only two particular circumstances, not achieving the breadth that we believe is appropriate and necessary.

Amendment No. 142 would appear to introduce similar particular circumstances of evidence as in amendment No. 57, but would avoid restricting the fresh evidence that could be admitted by retaining the reasonable explanation test. It may be helpful if I explain the reasoning behind the reasonable explanation test.

The Sutherland committee, which recommended this test for the introduction of fresh evidence, also recommended that no attempt should be made to qualify or interpret the term "reasonable explanation". The committee believed that the ground for admission of fresh evidence should be flexible and it did not wish to try to circumscribe what might be a reasonable explanation in any particular circumstances. We, therefore, accepted this recommendation and the reasoning behind it, and believe that amendment No. 57 would not achieve the positive change that both the Sunderland committee and the Government wish to see in this area. Amendment No. 139 is unnecessary since the two particular circumstances raised by it, and also by amendment No. 57, should be readily encompassed by the wider "reasonable explanation" test. I therefore ask the hon. Members to withdraw the amendment.
