E. Coli

10.30 am

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Michael Forsyth)
: With permission, Mr Maxton, I should like to make a further statement about the E. coli food poisoning outbreak in central Scotland. Copies of the statement are available in the Members' Room.

First, I and my colleagues at the Scottish Office would like to extend our deepest sympathy to the bereaved families and to express our sincere hope that those who are currently ill will make a swift recovery. I am sure that the entire Committee will join me in those sentiments.

This is the most serious outbreak of E. coli food poisoning that we have faced in the United Kingdom. The latest information I have is that nine people have died, with 388 suspected cases, 205 confirmed cases and 50 people in hospital. The condition of 30 people is giving cause for concern. The chief medical officer has informed me that, on the basis of the data collected to date the outbreak peaked between 20 and 26 November and that the daily number of new cases has fallen consistently since that time. It is still possible that a small number of patients may yet come forward with symptoms and we must all exercise care with food preparation and hygiene. All the epidemiological evidence points to the recent outbreak having one source.

I must acknowledge the immense amount of work that has been put into the outbreak by the North Lanarkshire council through its environmental health department, the Lanarkshire health board, by the Falkirk council and the Forth Valley health board and by the hospitals, community health services and general practitioners. I visited Monklands general hospital last Monday and was impressed by the dedication and commitment shown by the staff there, which I am sure is replicated in the other centres handling the outbreak. I gave an undertaking then that Lanarkshire health board would receive an additional allocation to take account of the extra demands being placed upon it. I can tell the Committee today that the same will apply to the Forth Valley health board.

Since the outbreak began, the Scottish National Blood Transfusion Service has provided more than 600 units of frozen plasma for use in treating E. coli patients. The SNBTS has responded marvellously, as it always does, in meeting the blood supply needs of hospitals. However, this outbreak underlines the continuing need for fresh blood supplies. There is a mobile blood transfusion unit parked outside with a team of nurses. Hon. Members might like to join the public in taking the opportunity to donate blood or to sign up to be added to the donor register during our meeting this morning. By registering, people enable the service to call them in as and when needed, and this is particularly important. I am delighted 2 to be able to tell the Committee that the Opposition party leaders and myself will be setting an example after the statements have been completed. I apologise in advance for our absence from the Committee for that purpose.

The procedures followed during the food poisoning outbreak reflect a long-established view that responsibility should lie with the local authority environmental health department. This is enshrined in the Food Safety Act 1990 and its associated Great Britain-wide code of practice, and in the guidance published by the Scottish Office Department of Health. The Bill, which became the 1990 Act, had all-party support and was passed unopposed. The code of practice made under it was the subject of extensive consultation and was laid before Parliament. There should thus be no dispute in this Committee that it is right that statutory responsibility for food safety enforcement should rest with local authorities. The Secretary of State has reserve powers, which could effectively replace the local authority in handling an outbreak of this nature. At no stage in this outbreak has anyone suggested that use of those powers would be appropriate.

On the basis of information supplied to my officials, I understand the sequence of events in the present outbreak to be as follows. Information that this was indeed an E. coli outbreak became available around midday on Friday 22 November, in the light of reported cases of illness and tests on patients becoming ill over the previous few days. Investigation to trace the possible source of infection established by the end of that day that food supplied from the premises of J. Barr and Son was the probable source. Officers from the environmental health department of the council and the health board spoke to Mr. Barr that same evening.

I have been informed that those officers told Mr. Barr that they thought the infection was coming from his shop and obtained his agreement that he would withdraw from sale all cooked meats and cooked meat products with immediate effect. I also understand that they also visited early the following morning at 7am, before the shop opened, to confirm this position and they decided that it was not necessary to impose a statutory prohibition on trading. It has, however, become clear that cooked meat did leave Mr. Barr's premises that day. The circumstances in which that meat was supplied are being investigated by the police and I do not propose to risk compromising that process by commencing further.

The local authority was responsible for tracing others who might possibly be at risk of infection, through a process of identifying and interviewing Mr. Barr's customers. They have explained to my officials that this was not an easy process, not least because the shopkeeper's records made it very difficult to produce an accurate or reliable list. The availability of adequate information is something which the police are investigating; it was not until Friday 6 December that it was possible for the local authority environmental health department to complete the task of checking through all the customer records for the relevant period as far back as the beginning of November.

The set procedures require the establishment of an outbreak control team to co-ordinate all activities during the release of information. It met for the first time at 11 am on Saturday 23 November and by that afternoon, general practitioners had been alerted, the arrangements for a helpline established and a press notice issued. JPEG 3 That achieved extensive coverage, and there were, for example, 350 inquiries on the helpline the following day. Meanwhile, the health authorities and local practitioners were gearing up to cope with the expected influx of cases, with all the associated testing, interviewing and checking requirements.

I have taken time to explain these events carefully so that the Committee will understand the extent of the action taken locally. On Saturday, Sunday and Monday, Scottish Office officials spoke to the local officials involved. On Tuesday 26 November, my officials contacted the Lanarkshire and Forth Valley health boards. Prompted by information from the latter of suspected cases of eating cold meat from Scotmid stores, and by media reports that morning, my officials sought urgent information on the extent of the outbreak beyond North Lanarkshire. My officials were not able to get a response from North Lanarkshire council either by telephone-which was engaged-or by fax, but they were able to confirm with the Edinburgh environmental health department that information had been provided to a number of adjacent environmental health departments about outlets beyond North Lanarkshire. My officials therefore urgently arranged to attend the meeting in Hamilton on the evening of Tuesday 26 November, at which they were briefed on the situation.

In view of the outbreak, my officials decided that a food hazard warning notice should be issued to all environmental health departments in Scotland, in accordance with the procedures laid down in the code of practice. Its purpose was twofold: first, to alert all local authorities in Scotland to the outbreak, to ensure that if potentially contaminated products were found to be distributed outside the affected area they would know what action to take; and secondly, to confirm to enforcement authorities in the central belt that products may have been distributed to outlets in their area, so that they could investigate and, if necessary, withdraw from sale. It has since been established that Lanarkshire had contacted other food authorities directly and action was already under-way. The food hazard warning is a document that positively requires action by enforcement officers of food authorities.

My officials sent the draft by fax to officials of North Lanarkshire council who were present at the control team meeting on Wednesday 27 November and my officials were told by the council that details of the network of outlets should be described as confidential. The initiative to restrict the availability of the list of outlets appended to the food hazard warning in that way did not come from the Scottish Office, as I have already told the House and I hope that Opposition Members now accept that.

A second food hazard warning notice was issued on Thursday 28 November, asking enforcement officers to take action to remind butchers and other premises selling or processing raw and cooked meats of the need for them to implement the highest level of hygiene on their premises and to follow the published advice contained in the document, "Safer Cooked Meat Production Guidelines".

In addition, officials have met with senior representatives of the Scottish Federation of Meat Traders Associations to advise them of the details of the outbreak 4 and to encourage a campaign of good hygiene practice. The federation, in conjunction with the Meat and livestock Commission, is offering all independent butchers in Scotland a free advisory visit to assist them in identifying the points that are critical in the control of food safety.

As I announced on 29 November, I have invited Professor Pennington to lead an expert group. The expert group's remit is

"to examine the circumstances which led to the outbreak in the central belt of Scotland and to advise me on the implications for food safety and the general lessons to be learned."

I have asked Professor Pennington to let me have any priority recommendations he wishes to make by the end of this year. I have also asked the chief medical officer to prepare an advertising campaign to inform the public, shopkeepers, the catering trade and those responsible for processing food, of good practice designed to minimise the risk of food poisoning. As members of the Committee will have seen, that campaign, costing more than £600,000, has begun.

My right hon. and learned Friend the Lord Advocate announced on Thursday a public inquiry under the Fatal Accident and Sudden Deaths Inquiry (Scotland) Act 1976 to investigate the circumstances and causes of deaths caused by the outbreak. The inquiry is public, has a wide-ranging remit and is entitled to explore and determine the reasonable precautions, if any, whereby the deaths might have been avoided; the defects, if any, of any system of working which contributed to the deaths; and, any other facts that are relevant to the circumstances of the deaths. The sheriff conducting the inquiry will have extensive powers to call witnesses and order the recovery of documents. All interested parties, including the families of the deceased, will have the right to give evidence and be represented by lawyers at the inquiry.

I must emphasise that, contrary to what we have heard in recent days, a fatal accident inquiry is as powerful a means of investigating an incident such as this as any other public inquiry. It has been able to handle incidents as significant and diverse as Lockerbie and the Ibrox disaster and is commonly used in medical cases. The Committee should have every confidence that it is entirely appropriate to tackle the circumstances of this outbreak.

Any such outbreak involving illness and death is harrowing for those directly affected and their relatives and, indeed, for all those involved. It is with sadness that I must tell the Committee that, even as I speak, the number of deaths has increased by one—unfortunately, another person has died at Stobhill hospital.

I close by reminding the committee not to lose sight of the immense effort made by many dedicated people in dealing with the consequences of the outbreak and tracking down its causes. They deserve our thanks and support.

: I congratulate the hon. Member for Glasgow, Maryhill (Mrs. Fyfe) on securing the Adjournment debate. I shall endeavour to answer the points she raised. It would be useful at the outset, however, if I explain the changes that have taken place this year in the method of making urban programme allocations.

The urban regeneration policies that we are now implementing have been arrived at after extensive consultation going back to late 1993. Throughout that period, there has been overwhelming support for the policy changes from local authorities and other consulted bodies. Until this financial year, urban programme funds were allocated on the basis of individual project applications made to the Scottish Office by local authorities. That approach had a number of deficiencies. Decisions on which projects to support were taken centrally by the Scottish Office. Moreover, decisions were taken only once a year in an annual competition. The process also lacked a strategic focus at local level. Local authorities, local communities and other partner agencies such as Scottish Homes and local enterprise companies which were responsible for drafting and implementing a local regeneration strategy, could have no certainty about which projects would be selected.

In addition, the considerable effort made by community groups at local level in working out detailed project applications, submitting them to the local authority and then to the Scottish Office was often wasted since the competition was invariably significantly oversubscribed. Finally, some of the eligibility criteria for the urban programme, such as the requirement for projects to be innovative, made it unhelpfully inflexible, not necessarily addressing local needs on the ground.

JPEG 79 As well as rectifying some of those deficiencies in the urban programme, in formulating a new urban regeneration strategy we were keen to build on the success of the four experimental Scottish Office-led urban partnerships in Castlemilk in Glasgow, Ferguslie Park in Paisley, Wester Hailes in Edinburgh and Whitfield in Dundee. Since they began in 1988, the partnerships have demonstrated considerable achievements as a result of their strategic, comprehensive, multi-agency approaches to regeneration. Indeed, with the involvement of all the appropriate bodies, including the local communities and the private sector, the partnerships broke new ground and were widely admired. Their achievements include the following: unemployment has been cut in half; more than 11,000 people have been placed in jobs; the private sector has invested some £60 million; up to one in five houses are now owned by their occupiers; crime is down by 41 per cent. in Wester Hailes, 25 per cent. in Castlemilk and more than 20 per cent. in Whitfield. More children are attending school and attaining qualifications in Ferguslie Park and Castlemilk. Far more people are now satisfied with the area in which they live.

Our new policy, programme for partnership, brought together the two strands-seeking to replicate the partnership's successes more widely across Scotland and making better use of the urban programme. Under programme for partnership, we invited the main regeneration agencies to form partnerships with the private sector and representatives of their local communities to bid in competition with each other in areas to be designated priority partnership areas or PPAs where deprivation is most concentrated. In addition, we invited authorities to bid for regeneration programmes for those areas eligible for urban programme resources but outside the worst concentrations of deprivation.

Our prime intention in implementing that policy was to ensure that all the relevant public agencies, together with local communities and the private sector, focused resources on the selected areas and, by working in partnership, made the best possible use of the available resources. The urban programme funds would be allocated in a block to authorities on the basis of strategic aims agreed with the Scottish Office. That meant that decisions on which particular projects to support to achieve those aims would be devolved to the local level.

At the end of June this year, we received bids from authorities on behalf of them and their regeneration partners for urban programme funds under programme for partnership. In total, we received 29 bids for PPA status and 21 bids for regeneration programmes.
