Mr. Stewart

At the end of the debate on Thursday, the hon. Member for Linlithgow raised several questions relating to clauses 26 and 27. In my introductory remarks to clause 27—I may need to respond later at length, depending on what hon. Members say—I shall endeavour to answer those legitimate questions.

First, perhaps I may set out the purpose of the clause. Valuation matters are important; perhaps that is common ground between members of the Committee. They are also fairly technical matters and it is no criticism of hon. Members if they do not always have at their fingertips all the details of how a valuation system works in Scotland.

The clause provides for the establishment of each new authority as the valuation authority for its own area and for the transfer of existing valuation arrangements to the new authorities. The Secretary of State is empowered, by order, to specify the combination of areas for which a single assessor should be appointed and how those joint arrangements will work. That will avoid the need to increase the number of assessors from its present level of 10. Each valuation authority, or combination of valuation authorities, will be required to appoint an assessor as well as the number of depute assessors it considers necessary, to take office from 1 April 1996. Any assessor or depute assessor appointed by predecessor authorities will cease to hold office on that date.

That means that we have 10 assessors responsible for their service throughout Scotland.

I turn now to some of the points raised by the hon. Member for Linlithgow at the end of the previous sitting. He specifically asked that I should confirm that assessors would remain responsible for lands valuation and for rating and council tax purposes in Scotland. I am happy to give him that confirmation. The Bill makes the assessors' continuing responsibilities emphatically clear and we do not intend to change that.

The hon. Gentleman also aked me to clarify our intentions regarding the prescription of qualifications for assessors. I refer hon. Members to clause 27(3) which makes clear that the Secretary of State must

"prescribe the qualifications required to be possessed"

by an assessor or depute assessor. That rolls forward an existing arrangement and I hope that it will be acceptable to the Committee. We have no current intention of varying the present qualifications, which have served well the system since 1956. They consist of the appropriate level of membership of RICS, which, as the hon. Member for Linlithgow told us, has made representations to the Scottish Office.

The question of broadening the range of professional qualifications appropriate to the assessor's post was reviewed in the 1980s and at that time we did not consider any change to be appropriate. We see no reason to revisit that issue at present.

890 The hon. Member for Linlithgow also sought confirmation that we intended to use the powers in clause 27 to combine valuation authorities, through the mechanism of a joint board, for the purpose of appointing a single assessor in such a way as to retain the present number of assessors. That directly relates to the question that the hon. Gentleman asked a few moments ago.

It is our intention to use those powers to achieve groupings of authorities for the purpose of appointing assessors which will bring about the benefits that existing arrangements have given. Broadly speaking, we intend that there should not be a change in the present number of assessors—there should be 10 throughout the country. However, on considering groupings and boundaries, if there were a strong case for some slight variation, I should not feel bound to oppose that.

The combination of areas is a related, but separate question. One obvious possibility is that combinations should be made which would, in effect, allow the present regional assessors' departments to continue as if no reform were taking place. That is not the only option.

To gauge the pros and cons of different approaches, I propose to initiate as soon as practicable a broad consultation with the bodies and interests most obviously involved to determine which approach will prove most efficient and command support. We want to hear what bodies, such as the Scottish Valuation Advisory Council, the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities and RICS have to say on the matter before bringing forward particular proposals. I assure the hon. Member for Linlithgow that we do not have preconceived views about which combinations of areas will prove best, but nor is any option automatically ruled out.

Finally, the hon. Member for Linlithgow asked whether it was intended that, as now, the assessors should also act as electoral registration officers. That was the subject of an amendment to the clause which was subsequently withdrawn. That amendment would have had the effect of vesting statutorily in the assessors of the new authorities responsibility for electoral registration. That is an important point for the ordinary elector and for us when we stand in front of the electorate at elections. I shall spell out the position.

The Government do not consider that statutory vesting would have been either necessary or desirable. The current position is that assessors have traditionally and for good reasons carried out the task in Scotland. I hope that Members of the Committee will agree that it has been well done, but that is no argument for removing the flexibility which the present statutory provisions allow. The Representation of the People Act 1983 provides that it is a matter for each local authority to decide who should carry out the electoral registration function, which is so important to our democracy. We would not want to prescribe the local authorities' power of decision in such away.

The power of flexibility held by Scottish local authorities has created no problems and I do not think that the Committee would want to take such an important power away by statutory prescription. It would also be technically unsatisfactory to give local authorities a general power under section 8(3) of the 1983 Act and then JPEG 891 take it away by means of the specific duty prescribed by the amendment. Thus, while we would in practice anticipate that the new authorities would want to continue with the long-standing practice of vesting the electoral registration function in the assessors, we do not consider that there is a case for tightening the statutory flexibility that is currently available to authorities, nor has any such case been put to us by the authorities.

The clause is important and deserves attention. The questions of the hon. Member for Linlithgow have, I hope, enabled me to make a number of matters clear and to help the Committee. I commend the clause to the Committee.
