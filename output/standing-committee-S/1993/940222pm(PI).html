Local Government etc. (Scotland) Bill

Clause 8
TRANSFER OF EMPLOYEES

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 90, in page 4, line 18, at end insert—

"(1A) An order under this section in the case of any person who on 31st March 1996 is in the employment of one or more local authorities who are or include an authority which ceases to exist by virtue of Part I of this Act, shall make such provision as is necessary to ensure that, to the extent, if any, to which, by reason only of the said Part I of this Act, that person would apart from the order cease on 1 st April 1996 to be in such employment, that person is transferred on 1st April 1996 to the employment of such local authority as may be specified in or determined under the order.".— [Mr. McLeish.]

"(1A) An order under this section shall include such provision with respect to any person who is transferred by or under the order from the employment of one authority to that of another so as to secure that.

  1. so long as he continues in the employment of that other authority by virtue of the transfer and until he is served with a statement in writing referring to the order and specifying new terms and conditions of employment, he enjoys terms and conditions of employment not less favourable than those which he enjoyed immediately before the date of transfer; and

Mr. Tain Dalyell
(Linlithgow)
: I suspect that the Minister is wrong about the transfer of staff, and I am sure that he is wrong about the timetable. The purpose of amendment No. 90 is to ensure that all local authority employees on the 31 March 1996 transfer to a new authority on 1 April 1996. It incorporates the provision in section 216(2) of the Local Government (Scotland) Act 1973. Many people read our proceedings and it will make more sense if I read this out.

"An order under this section in the case of any person who on 31st March 1996 is in the employment of one or more local authorities who are or include an authority which ceases to exist by virtue of Part I of this Act, shall make such provision as is necessary to ensure that, to the extent, if any, to which, by reason only of the said Part I of this Act, that person would apart from the order cease on 1st April 520 1996 to be in such employment, that person is transferred on 1st April 1996 to the employment of such local authority as may be specified in or determined under the order."

I hope that that is clear enough for those who read our proceedings.

As amendment No. 90 is supported by the employing authority and by the trade unions and staff associations and is considered to be the best way forward in the circumstances of reorganisation, why on earth cannot the Government accept it? No convincing answer has been given to that question.

The 1973 Act set out the basic principles on the transfer of staff from the existing to the new authorities and the compensation arrangements that would be in place for those who were redundant. Are those basic principles anywhere to be found in the Bill? If not, why not? The Committee deserves to know. I reiterate that the Minister is incorrect. The general principle of transfer was set out in sections 216(2) and 216(3) of the 1973 Act. The specific requirement to transfer all staff allowed detailed advice from the staff commission. That has not been done on this occasion.

The second issue is this morning's assertion that the timing of the reorganisation is no different from the timing of the previous reorganisation. That is factually not the case.This reorganisation will take place in half the time. It is no good saying that this is simpler than in the 1970s, because many people think that, far from being simple, it is actually more difficult than in the 1970s because of the nature of modern technology and the difficulty of unpicking the regions.

Can we go over the facts? The Wheatiey commission was set up in 1965. The commission's proposals were published in 1969, and the White Paper was published in February 1971. The publication of the Local Government (Scotiand) Bill was in October 1972, and it received Royal Assent in November 1973. On 2 April 1973, the Scottish local government staff advisory committee was appointed. On 13 November 1973, the Scottish local government staff commission was appointed. On 7 May 1974, shadow elections for new regions, islands and districts were set up, and on 16 May 1975 the reorganisation of the new local authorities took place. That spans almost a decade.

What is the timetable this time? The consultative document was published in October 1992. Parliamentary colleagues will not take it amiss if I say that the consultative document did not hold a candle to the care and consultation of the Wheatiey commission. Member for Inverness, Nairn and Lochaber (Sir R. Johnston) and others went up and down the country consulting and listening properly. The repeated reaction from local authority staff was that the Wheatley commission had come to see them, had listened to them, had asked relevant questions, and had reflected on what they had said. None of that has been done this time. In Banff and Buchan and in many other places—

The position of staff is critical. The Minister was right to say that members of staff are local authorities' main resource. They provide key services to local communities. As a result, they want the Bill to contain clear commitments about job security, employment continuity and the maintenance of their living standards. Assurances of concern and good will are not enough. The issues are fundamental and need to be tackled in primary legislation. The Minister must respond by giving the guarantees that staff need. Mr. Andrew Welsh

: May I follow the questions raised by the hon. Member for Linlithgow (Mr. Dalyell) by saying that I am worried about the timetable? The Government seem to be offering an 18-month to a two-year period in which all the crucial matters will be discussed and finalised. That is not an adequate time scale, given that there was a six-year gestation in 1975 for the introduction of the new authorities. The time scale on offer now is very short.

Personnel and services will be disrupted during the transition. Authorities will operate in parallel in that period and local government officials will be stretched, but—simultaneously—conclusions will be reached about both the general principles and the details of redundancy and staff transfer.

The Minister said that there is no real difference in the intention between the Bill and the amendments. There is a big difference. Clause 8 allows orders to be made for transfer after further consideration on the tightest of timetables. Clause 9 refers to staff after transfer. The staff commission can only give advice. It is up the Secretary of State to issue orders. That process is light years away from any provision in the 1973 Act. There is a fundamental difference of approach between this Bill and the 1973 proposals. Why are such matters not in the Bill?

The Minister said that there will be no difference in the end product. If that is true, why not retain the 1973 provisions? There should be explicit safeguards for staff. Provisions in the 1973 Bill were established after a great deal of consultation. Why change them?

Given the complexity of merging staff from large units into smaller units, is the timetable adequate to tackle such matters? There is no general precedent in Scotland for such a merger. The Government are leading us into new territory. We have not received an answer to the information technology problems, which go to the heart of the way in which local authorities operate. The points about early retirement or redundancy have not been answered. The Minister has not adequately responded to points about the effects of the measure on staffing standards, the local economy and individual families. Those issues will be decided later by ministerial orders in the tightest possible timetable. The amendments could provide the answers the those problems.

How do the Government expect the 18-month to two-year period to unfold? What timetable will local authorities receive for the transition period? The answer would provide a pointer to the Government's thinking. Everyone could then decide whether the timetable was realistic. Staff matters are at the heart of the system. If the system is to work well, decisions about staff must be made correctly. The timetable is very tight. That is unfair to local government. How does the Minister believe that local authorities will cope? How does he expect them to perform within the timetable?
