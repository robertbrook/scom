He gave a very powerful speech, which I 556 recommend to Ministers. I shall quote a few passages from it. He said:

"When I took up my post as Chief Executive of Lothian Region in 1974 little did I think that nearly 20 years later I would be standing here today on the threshold of yet another radical re-organisation of Local Government in Scotland. Those of us who have survived the experience would not readily wish to have a 'repeat performance'. Mercifully my brief today does not require me to make comment on the proposals now before you but merely to respond to the question 'What did you do in the great re-organisation of 1975 Grandad'."

That is the view of the man who the Government have chosen. He asks:

"What then was the diagnosis and what were the recommended remedies?"

He refers to his concern at the

"fragmentation of administrative and support services."

He also said:

"What I am trying to convey here I think is that the lead time towards the 1975 re-organisation was long, and carried out against a background of a growing awareness for the need for change."

He contrasts that with the situation today. I shall not try your patience much longer, Sir James. Bob Peggie said at Dunblane:

"much of the experience gained by those at the top was lost at vesting time through retiral or an unwillingness to be involved in the new set up."

I think that all of us can produce anecdotal evidence of excellent local authority personnel who are just not prepared to face the hassle of yet another change, and I cannot find it in my heart to blame them. My hon. Friend the Member for Falkirk, East, who has very close links with many local authority people, knows that that is true. It is true in our area and, I suspect, throughout Scotland.

Mr. Peggie says:

"Although I could understand the rationale I personally had reason to regret the very restrictive rules laid down for the appointment of staff."

He continued:

"I make no apology for repeating that preparation time for change must be adequate."

We must ask whether what we have heard this afternoon and this evening allows enough preparation time for change. Mr. Peggie added:

"I wish to conclude by posing a question—did Wheatley and Paterson get it wrong to the extent that yet another reorganisation is necessary?"

That question more or less answers itself in the speech. He went on:

"The feeling is that the patient does not appear to have any serious symptoms of malaise."

Even so, he noted:

"The patient is back in the operating theatre, gowns and masks have been donned, and scalpels are held at the ready."

Mr. Peggie ended by saying:

"However, when the organs are moved around and the wounds stitched up, it is to be hoped that the period of recuperation will not be too lengthy and that the patient can reasonably look forward to a long, healthy and useful life."

I think that we have already discovered that the Bill's geological flaws mean that the reforms cannot last and are not worth the candle. I urge the Government to consider their appointee's considered speech at Dunblane last year.
