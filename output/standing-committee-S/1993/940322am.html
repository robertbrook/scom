That constitution might create the illusion that the River Tweed Commissioners and the decisions made about the management of the fisheries on the River Tweed, such as who pays what and who has access to what are subject to democratic control. Of course, it does not work like that.

The clause is another example of the way in which the representative commissioners—who represent angling clubs and, in this case, local authorities—are forever changing whereas the riparian owners' representatives are there for all time.

The chairman of the commissioners, and those who call the shots and make decisions about the management of the river, are, practically always, the riparian owners—the proprietors of the fishing beats on the River Tweed. Such people have never paid much attention to the case for improving public access to fishing or improving the management of the river and its fisheries in the interests of the public and, to a lesser extent, of environmentalists.

The legislation governing the river goes back hundreds of years and is long overdue for a major overhaul. The way in which the River Tweed and other Scottish rivers are managed should be changed.
