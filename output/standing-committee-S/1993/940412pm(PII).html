Mr. Welsh
: We are debating the subject of the make-up of the customers council that would reflect accurately the needs of customers of the water boards. If a body is to represent properly, it must itself be representative. The possibilities are endless. I have selected community councils and elected councillors as a shorthand way of allowing a broad cross-section of the Scottish population to participate, and allowing an extra dimension of responsibility to community councillors.

The community councils wish to extend their role. They feel that they do not have enough to do. I am sure that they would be happy to take on the extra responsibility of reflecting local needs directly to the water boards.

The reason for the addition of councillors is self explanatory. They are already elected individuals who reflect their communities' needs. Any committee worth its salt should elect its own chairman, as is usual practice in organisations with which I have been connected. It goes against my grain for chair persons to be foisted on organisations by outside bodies, although the Government seem to have taken to themselves too great power with all-too-evident relish. Clearly, the customers council should be allowed to choose its chairman. I hope that the Government will see it that way; so far they have not.

I have no quarrel with the other amendments, which would add specific representation for consumer affairs, rural areas, and disabled persons. Disabled persons have special needs which only they can reflect to the new water boards. I hope that the Government will consider those representations. I trust that my amendment would allow all those interests to be represented. Given the organisations that I have chosen, my amendments should be inclusive rather than exclusive. It is not meant to exclude groups mentioned in other amendments.

What would be most unacceptable is a so-called customers organisation which the Secretary of State controlled absolutely, appointing its chairman and members from persons who appear to him to have relevant experience. That would be another unacceptable aspect of the centralisation that the Government are imposing on the water boards and associated organisations through the Bill.

I should like input to come at long last from other nongovernmental sources—people either elected by their fellow citizens or with other well-established local 1726 credentials. The Government have cosseted the water boards taken them to one side, pushed them through in the Bill, and made sure that they are created in the Government's image. I cannot see why the Government cannot allow some decentralisation and local input into decision making for the customers council.

The amendment, and those grouped with it, make a plea to the Government to allow local input. They have centralised, and taken control of water through the Bill. If they intend a customers council to be truly representative of local and consumer interests, they should look kindly on the amendment, accept it in both its spirit and content and, at long last, perhaps concede some point to the Opposition, which they have not done so far on an issue that transcends party politics as a method of reflecting consumers needs.
