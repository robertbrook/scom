Mr. McLeish
: I am pleased to see you looking so well this morning, Mr. Martin, and bracing yourself for another enthusiastic and interesting week in Committee. This morning we start to discuss the staffing of this so-called reform of local government. Clause 8 deals with the transfer of staff to the new authorities and the first group of amendments is essentially about the unacceptable powers that the Secretary of State wishes to take upon himself to influence and manipulate that transfer.

It is important to spell out that the boundaries discussions in Committee have resulted in a near farce. Scotland will be littered with small councils that cannot possibly provide efficient and effective services. In many areas of Scotland the boundary problems are unresolved. We now want to discuss the transfer of staff but the transfer to what? We do not have a boundaries map which would allow any sensible disucssion of where staff should be transferred from and where they should be transferred to. At present we have a boundaries farce.

We have already discussed costs. It is clear to everyone but the Government that whether reorganisation costs £200 million or £720 million in terms of the burden it will place on council tax payers and business tax payers or, indeed, in terms of the cuts in services that will be needed to meet that cost, this is a Bill too far.

Today we are discussing a new crisis, one of timetabling. One year from today political parties in Scotland will be expected to start campaigning for the new so-called unitary authorities. Elections will be held on 5 April 1995. This debate is absolutely crucial because it is about 482 transferring 300,000 people from one system to another. It is about a payroll bill of nearly £3.7 billion. That transfer will take place in the few months between the establishment of a staff commission, when and if the Bill receives Royal Assent, and the end of the year.

We intend to use the amendments to illustrate that, once again, local government is likely to be pitched into a crisis not of its own making but one deliberately engendered by a Government hell-bent on making changes which the staff in local government have not signed up for. A crisis of morale already exists among those staff. They care about the services they provide. They are dedicated and often have rich skills and display professionalism. When morale is affected it can undermine service delivery. Apart from providing services, which is their bread and butter, staff are concerned about their future. Those 300,000 people cannot be dismissed lightly. Their families, aspirations, incomes, careers, conditions of service and pay levels are crucial to them. Their morale should not be undermined to such an extent that the services that we hold in high regard start to suffer.

Clause 8 and this group of amendments relate to the transfer of employees. We seek a sensible balance between the transfer of the staff to the new authorities and the ability of those new authorities to take decisions and to develop policies that are not unduly constrained by what the House may decide.

Another issue for consideration is the Secretary of State's powers. If there is one main feature of 15 years of Tory Government in Scotland, it is their desire to centralise: to centralise power in the Scottish Office and to take more powers from this House, not through primary legislation, from which they shy away, but by parliamentary orders. That is simply unacceptable to the Opposition. We believe that the main concerns, parameters and frameworks to deal with staffing should be dealt with in the Bill.
