New clause 38
COMMON GOOD FUNDS

".—(1) It shall be the duty of a local authority in administering property held by it as part of the common good to ensure that where a community council or councils exist for the whole or for a major part of the area in which such assets or property are held—

  1. the community council or councils shall be consulted regarding the administration of those assets or property; and
  2. all appropriations therefrom shall be made on the combined nomination of:—
    1. the local councillor or councillors for the electoral divisions covering the area to which the common good related prior to 16th May 1975; and that community council or councils or any one of them.
  1. Where property to which subsection (1) above applies has been transferred from an existing authority and vested in a new authority pursuant to section 15 above, any obligations of the authority under subsection 4(a) of that section shall be regarded as having been met if the duty under section (1) above has been complied with.
  2. This section shall not apply in the case of the areas covered by the councils for Aberdeen, Dundee, Edinburgh and Glasgow.
  3. For the purposes of this section—
  4. 'existing local authority' includes as joint committee and a joint board but does not, subject to any other provision of this Act, include an islands authority;
  5. 'new authority' means any of the authorities constituted under section 2 of this Act, and includes a joint board and a residuary body; and
  6. 'property' includes any records held by, or in the custody of, an existing local authority.".— [Mr. Kirkwood.]

Mr. Archy Kirkwood
(Roxburgh and Berwickshire)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

It may benefit the Committee to spend a few moments on the important question of common good funds and how the Bill provides for them.

We have had a good innings on most of the essential questions arising from the Bill. Although there may be opportunities to do so on Report and in the House of Lords, we have not spent much time examining how community councils relate to the new reorganised structure. Evidence from my constituency and elsewhere, and especially from rural Scotland, suggests that the community councils feel that they have been somewhat bypassed. I understand that the 1973 provisions are not weakened or diminished. However, there is a view, with which I have some sympathy, that the Committee has missed an opportunity to consider what further powers and extensions of responsibility—even extending powers to spend penny rates, or alignment with whatever the current provisions are for parish councils in England—could be given to community councils in 2026 Scotland. In the consultations before the Bill was presented it would have been appropriate to consider the advisory role that community councils have played and will play. But I suspect that to do so at this stage would, technically, be out of order.

I wish to focus on community councils as vehicles to reassure local communities, especially in rural Scotland. The provisions to husband resources, heritable property and the assets of the common good funds. Those have existed in Scotland for many years, and should properly be devoted to the purposes for which they were originally bequeathed.

As members of the Committee know, common good funds have existed for years—in some cases for hundreds of years. They have been well looked after and well used by local communities, especially in rural Scotland, for the benefit of the people. In 1973, concern was expressed and there were debates in the House about the potential loss of control over those assets as the 1973 reorganisation created bigger units. For example, the funds that were bequeathed for the Hawick common good—formerly in the control of the Royal Borough of Hawick—moved to the control of the district authority. In Roxburgh, the district council had its headquarters in Hawick. Therefore, control did not move geographically, but it changed in that it went into the hands of the district council that covered the whole of Roxburgh.

There are other examples, of which St. Andrews is one. My hon. and learned Friend the Member for Fife, North-East (Mr. Campbell) has corresponded with the Minister, expressing concern. In 1973, the control of the common good funds moved from St. Andrews to Cupar. That caused some anxiety at the time and there were discussions about it during the 1973 proceedings. After the present reorganisation, in north-east Fife—if the proposed boundaries for Fife remain extant—control of the St. Andrews common good fund will go not just from St. Andrews to Cupar, but from Cupar to Glenrothes, Falkirk or goodness knows where.

I impute no conspiratorial motives to the Government; I am perfectly satisfied that Ministers are doing their best. However, in those circumstances, people who are responsible for common good funds in their localities are worried that there are no guarantees that those funds will be ring-fenced for the communities for which they were designed in the first place. That merits attention.

The new clause is my best stab at providing the necessary reassurance and protection. I appreciate that it is not watertight and that it may be technically deficient. The Minister may say that it is impossible to provide the legislative framework to get the guarantees for which I am asking because that would be fiendishly difficult. The nature of community councils, the way in which they are constituted and the limits and constraints on their powers and responsibilities make it difficult to hand common good assets to them. They are more transient than are some statutory local authorities and they do not have the legal capacity to hold heritable property.

There is anxiety in south-east Scotland, in St. Andrews and in the Highlands and Islands where the new regime of unitary local authorities that the Bill creates will be much further removed from the localities that the common good funds were originally designed to serve. I am sure that the Minister will concede that those funds are a crucial part of local communities. The Hawick common JPEG 2027 good fund, for example, contributes substantially to supporting the Hawick common riding, which is an important local festival. The funds are a valuable resource which have been under the control of local people.

Fears have been expressed to me, with which I sympathise, that if the Government are not careful and not alive to the danger that control may leak away to locations far removed from the areas that the common good funds were designed to serve, there will be much anxiety and anger in the communities covered by common good funds in future. If the Minister can provide some reassurance for the people who control and are worried about common good funds in Scotland, the new clause will have served a purpose.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Allan Stewart)
: I have listened carefully to the arguments of the hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire (Mr. Kirkwood) and particularly to his point about Cupar and St. Andrews because I was born in St. Andrews and brought up in Cupar.

As the hon. Gentleman suggested, there are some technical problems with the new clause. I do not criticise him for that, but it would restrict the flexibility of the new authorities and there could be problems when community councils do not exist. However, I accept the spirit of the hon. Gentleman's proposal. I cannot accept the new clause, but I can assure the hon. Gentleman that in our guidance to the new authorities on their relations with community councils, we shall include the need for them to take full account of his points about the administration of common good funds.

Mr. Kirkwood
: That is all that I could have expected this morning. It is a useful reassurance. In addition, I hope that the Minister will make sure that officials scrutinise and monitor the future use of the new councils' assets to ensure that there is no geographical leakage outwith the areas for which the funds were designed. However, the Minister has given a useful reassurance, and on that basis, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the motion.

Motion, and clause, by leave withdrawn.
