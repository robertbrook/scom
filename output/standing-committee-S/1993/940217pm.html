The Parliamentary-Under Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Allan Stewart): When we adjourned for lunch, I was about to give the Committee information about the responses to the consultation process on the eligibility of employees to stand for election to the new authorities.

Highland regional council commented:

"In terms of eligibility of employees to stand for election the Council, while regretting that the end of the two-tier system will Col. 430 mean that Council employees will now have greater difficulty in seeking election to local authorities (it will be particularly so in the Highland area)"—

clearly, it is easier in the central belt—

"feel that the principle of an official not being able to serve on his employing authority must be adhered to. There does not appear to be scope for workable exceptions to this rule."

Those views are shared by many local authorities from different parts of the country.

It is worth focusing on the views of councils that are already unitary authorities. Shetland islands council came out with a clear and unequivocal statement. It said:

"It is the view of the Council that the present arrangements in relation to the eligibility of council employees to stand for election should remain."

In the relevant section of their consultation response, Orkney Islands Council concluded that:

"The basic conflict of interest and possible public perception of that conflict of interst are such that the rights of individual members of staff to stand as elected members cannot be justified against the reputation of local government as a whole and that therefore all members of staff should be prohibited from standing for election to the authority which employs them."

Those views are particularly significant because they were offered in the light of practical working experience of the existing rules in unitary authorities.

I acknowledge that the other side of the argument was also presented to us by some councils, most of whom argued for a change in the existing rules. They did not argue for the proposals in the amendment, but for a change in the existing rules through some form of restrictions. There was, however, no consensus over what the extent or nature of any such restrictions should be. Some councils argued for "guidelines" being established to prevent a conflict of interest. Others suggested identifying a range of "restricted" posts or "politically sensitive" posts.

On the eligibility issue, Strathclyde regional council suggested that in a single-tier structure—I hasten to add that Strathclyde is not in favour of such a structure—appropriate steps should be taken to void such a large number of people being deprived of the possibility of serving their communities as councillors. It took the position suggested by the hon. Member for Fife, Central (Mr. McLeish).

On the matter of numbers, the hon. Gentleman referred to a figure of up to 300,000. That is an estimate of the number of employees of local authorities in Scotland. The number of people who would be affected by the eligibility rules is only a tiny proportion of that figure. No one suggests that all 300,000 would wish to stand as elected members of the authorities. The numbers of those affected would be a tiny proportion of that figure, given that people, especially in the central belt, could work for one authority but live in another.

When considering the specific matter of how arrangements would be made, Strathclyde suggested that provision should be made in respect of employees up to a certain management level which would enable them to stand for election

"provided they were prepared to take unpaid leave of absence during the period when they became councillors".

The hon. Member for Fife, Central said that the Government seemed to be taking an absolute position on the matter and asked whether there was a possibility of amending it. The absolute position is far and away the Image Col. 431 easiest to maintain.
