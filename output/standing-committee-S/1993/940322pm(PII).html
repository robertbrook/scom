The amendment owes its origins to the Scottish Library Association. It is an all-party amendment. It deals with the school library services, which are also known as the education resource services. Those services are presently provided by regional councils as part of their education functions: they are not part of the public library service. Members of the Committee may ask why they exist. Libraries in schools should contain books and other educational materials required by the schools throughout the year. However, schools also need access to a larger collection to use from time to time during the year. In simple language, no school could afford to maintain all that material. To attempt to do so would entail an expense that schools could not afford. It makes economic sense to have a central source so that schools can hugely expand the facilities for their pupils.

The school library service consists of three elements: books, other educational materials such as charts, maps, illustrations and artefacts, and staff. In addition, the central services provide important training for librarians working in individual schools. They also provide vital bibliographical facilities so that staff in schools can identify the materials most suitable for the purposes in hand.

All nine regional councils in Scotland at present maintain such a service. The question arises: what is to happen to those central regional services when education becomes the responsibility of the smaller successor councils? I suggest that some of the new authorities will be too small to provide a central resource service that is remotely comparable to those which now exist. Even if they could afford to do so—some people suggest that they could not—it would take a long time to build up collections and doing so would be a wasteful duplication of existing services.

Other resources held at regional level, such as property, can be expected to be split up among the new authorities. However, the education resource services could not be split up without their value being destroyed. It would be foolish to send stocks on a subject to one successor authority and stocks on another subject to its neighbour. The same applies to staff. Professional library staff are specialists. One member of staff may be expert in back-up for children with special educational needs, another may specialise in mathematics and sciences, and another in computer work or in arts. Splitting up such teams by sending individuals to different successor authorities could destroy the purpose of the system.

The central school library services that back up libraries in individual schools are usually provided by the regional authorities as part of their educational role without involvement by the district authorities, which are responsible under the Public Libraries Consolidation (scotland) Act 1887 for public libraries. However, in Tayside, the regional council uses the district authorities' public library service to perform part of the task on an agency basis. Nevertheless, there is still a central service at regional level to supplement the facilities provided in the three districts.

In Tayside region the service contains 220,000 books and units of other educational materials. Four Col. 1398 professional staff run the service and there are 80 professional staff serving in such central services throughout the nine regions of Scotland. Consequently, the Committee must consider the likely effects on the school library services of the abolition of the regions. It is not unrealistic to suggest that they will probably vary.
