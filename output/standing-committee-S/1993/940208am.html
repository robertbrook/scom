Mr. Home Robertson
: I endorse the views of my hon. Friend the Member for Linlithgow and of the hon. Member for Kincardine and Deeside (Mr. Kynoch). The amendment would defer the implementation of part I of the Bill covering the abolition of existing regions and districts and the introduction of a new system from 1996 to 1998, and, in the case of amendment No. 19, to the year 2000. The purpose of that delay of two or four years would be to make it possible for a proper, open and independent review of the structure of Scottish local government to be undertaken in the context of the establishment of a Scottish Parliament by the next Labour Government.

Such a prudent delay would avoid all the massive costs to taxpayers and the disruption to local services and institutions that are inherent in this shoddy Bill. It would ensure that we achieved the broadest possible consensus about the future of local government and it would make it possible to create a new structure that would stand the test of time. The Bill will not stand the test of time, but it will lead to the waste of a lot of effort and energy and cause a lot of agony. It is shot through with confusion and contradictions. It has been brought before the House by a Government who have been consistently hostile to the concept of local democracy and local services.

It is worth comparing the background to the Bill with that to the Local Government (Scotland) Act 1973. The 1973 Act followed a long period of public debate and consultation during and after the deliberations of a royal commission. At that time there was consensus on the fact that something was fundamentally wrong with Scottish local government; no such consensus exists today. The Wheatley commission was established to examine the need for the reform of Scottish local government. It included members of all political parties and of none, for example: Tom Fraser, a distinguished Labour politician in Scotland; Betty Harvie Anderson, the Minister's predecessor as the Conservative Member for the former constituency of East Renfrewshire and a former Deputy Speaker of the House of Commons; the hon. Member for Inverness, Nairn and Lochaber (Sir R. Johnston) and professionals and academics who had an intimate knowledge of the provision of services and Scottish local government.

That independent royal commission spent three years considering the way forward for local government in Scotland. It undertook genuine public consultation and its overriding objective was to strengthen Scottish local government. The structure that it recommended has stood the test of time, despite the harsh environment created by a hostile Government in the past 15 years. The Government now have the benefit of good local services and genuine local accountability. In recent years, Scotland's elected local authorities have done a remarkably good job in protecting their local communities in difficult circumstances.

After the experience of the poll tax, Parliament should think twice before again inflicting rubbish legislation on Scotland. An open, independent review would have nipped the poll tax in the bud and an independent commission on local government would lead to something very different from the Bill. The amendment would allow time for such an independent review. It would also allow time for local government reform to be set in the context of a long-overdue reform of Scotland's national administration.

Scotland now probably has the most autocratic, remote and unaccountable administration of any nation in western Europe. It is hardly surprising that some people are becoming cynical about a democratic system that has allowed a succession of unaccountable Tories to govern our country with such total impunity for so long.

We will have a Scottish Parliament, because it is necessary for the good government of Scotland and because our people want it.

It is very likely that, besides taking control of the Scottish Office, and taking back some of the power that has been handed over to Tory-appointed quangos, a Scottish Parliament might also take a hand in decisions affecting some major strategic services now run by the regions.
