The amendment is, I hope, non-contentious. I see a bubble coming out of the Minister's head saying, "That's unusual for the hon. Member for Carrick, Cumnock and Doon Valley (Mr. Foulkes)".

In various places in the Bill, the civic head of an authority is described as "chairman" and the deputy head as "vice-chairman". I understand that. I have no quarrel with the Bill's draftsmen because those terms are regularly used throughout the United Kingdom to describe the people who run local authorities.

Later clauses in the Bill deal with the chairmen or chairwomen of the cities. It states that they shall be the "lord provost". Again, I have no quarrel with that. If the cities wish to call their chairmen "lord provost", I shall not object to that.

394 The Bill also provides for any authority to designate the person who chairs the authority with a title that it chooses. It would be open to individual authorities, for example Ayrshire, to call the person who chairs the authority the "provost". Similarly, in other authorities, such as West Lothian, the person chairing the authority could also be called the "provost". I do not wish to challenge that provision.

The amendment deals with the generic term for the person chairing the authority. Throughout the Bill, the generic term is "chairman". The amendments suggest that the person chairing the authority should be called the "convener" and that his or her deputy should be either the "vice-convener" or, my preference, "depute convener".

There are two reasons why I move the amendment on behalf of all my hon. Friends—I hope that it will also find favour with hon. Members in other parties. First, "convener" is a non-sexist term. It does not imply that the person chairing the authority is either a man or a woman. The word "chairman" is always open to challenge. Suggestions are made that the person should be called the "chairperson" or that appalling title "chair". "Convener" cannot be shortened. It is not open to misinterpretation and it is non-sexist. That is the first argument in its favour.

The second argument is that it is a Scottish term, one that is used regularly throughout Scotland for a person who is in charge of/chairs various bodies. The term "convener" is well recognised, understood and accepted. Perhaps the only challenge or dispute is whether its final vowel should be "e" or "o". I am impartial, agnostic and quite relaxed on that aspect.

However, I believe that the word "convener" or "convenor"—I used "convener" in the amendment—should be adopted as the generic term. It would then be open to each local authority either to adhere to that term—as I hope most would—or, under a subsequent provision, to change the term to "provost" or whatever. I hope that this reasonable, modest and sensible suggestion finds favour with the Minister.

Clause 3
ORKNEY, SHETLAND AND WESTERN ISLES

Mr. Stewart
: I take it that the clause is generally acceptable, so I shall just record briefly what it does. It makes special provision for the existing islands councils for Orkney, Shetland and the Western Isles to continue as legal entities on and after 1 April 1996. We have earlier had a useful exchange of views on that in relation to the amendment to clause 2 moved by the hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire. The position is different from that on the mainland.

The special arrangements for the islands are intended to avoid unnecessary disruption and expense in circumstances where there will in effect be little change in the operation of the new authorities. There will also be no need to have councils elected in shadow form in the islands areas, so they are excluded from the requirement to have elections to shadow authorities on 6 April 1996.

The three authorities will in future be known as councils with the names of the new council areas being Orkney Islands, Shetland Islands and Western Isles. For practical purposes, that means that the names of the councils in Orkney and Shetland will remain unchanged. In the Western Isles, however, the council will in future be known as the Western Isles Council.

The provisions in the clause are a sensible acknowledgement of the fact that as they already have single-tier authorities there are strong arguments for ensuring that the islands areas have as much continuity and as little disruption and expense as possible. I do not think that there is disagreement on that. Therefore, I commend the clause to the Committee.
