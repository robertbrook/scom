Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: The hon. Gentleman raises the matter of child abuse and other factors that are relevant to education and social work, which could have a bearing on police costs. I shall explain how the costs will be arranged.

I shall outline the arrangements for attributing costs between Lothian region and Borders region, which form an existing joint police board. I am considering an existing model. However, that model need not be used for the joint boards that the Bill establishes. Each new joint board will be able to determine the most suitable method. Consultation will take place with police authorities. The Secretary of State has to approve the amalgamation scheme and make the first one. When he does that, after consultation with COSLA, he must approve the funding methodology.

The South-Eastern Police (Amalgamation) Order 1975 provides for the combination of the police areas of Lothian region and Borders region and the administration of the combined police area by a joint police committee. 1178 Under section 19(2)(d) of the Police (Scotland) Act 1967, an amalgamation scheme provides for the payment by the constituent authorities, in such proportion as may be specified in the scheme, of the expenditure incurred by joint police committees in performing the functions delegated to them.

Under section 2 of the 1967 Act, the delegated functions are: to provide pay, allowances and expenses for police officers—and for their civilian staff—and buildings vehicles, apparatus and other equipment for the police force. A new article substituted in the amalgamation scheme in 1992 specifies that the expenditure incurred in carrying out those functions shall be apportioned between Lothian regional council and Borders regional council as the expenditure arises in providing the police service in each of the areas of the two councils. The two councils decided that that was a fairer division than the previous arrangement, which was based on the population of the two areas. Borders complained that its burden was too heavy. The costs are not assessed on population but on policing activities. In making the change, Borders followed a precedent set by the Highlands and Islands.

To apportion expenditure as it arises in the two areas, it is necessary to determine the extent of provision of police service in each area in every respect, including the issues referred to by the hon. Gentleman. The extent of provision is determined by the location of officers, civilians, buildings, vehicles or equipment in the different geographical divisions into which the Lothian and Borders force area is divided. The extent of provision in the two constituent areas is measured by the police board, in drawing up its annual expenditure budget, in accordance with article 29 of the South Eastern Police (Amalgamation) Order 1975. Methods of allocation have been agreed between Lothian and Borders regional councils.

Similarly, expenditure on staff is apportioned according to the location of the staff concerned in Lothian or Borders. Central staff costs are divided in the same proportion; expenditure on buildings and vehicles is apportioned by reference to the location of the building or vehicle concerned. Other expenditure is apportioned either on the basis of identifiable costs in the two areas or in the same proportion as identifiable staff costs.

I have described the present arrangement, which works well. It demonstrates that we can take account of the views of authorities in joint boards. As I said earlier, that does not mean that we have to apply the arrangement to the new joint boards, but the Secretary of State will take full account of the preferences of the new boards and their constituent authorities before making the initial amalgamation schemes under clause 34.

The present preferences are for the constituent council—the police authority—to bear the costs of policing undertaken in its area. If the precedents of Borders, Lothian and Highlands and Islands authorities are followed, expenditure will be based on policing, not population. I have tried to answer the hon. Gentleman as comprehensively as possible.
