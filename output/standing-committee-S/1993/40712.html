Scottish Estimates

Agriculture and Fisheries, Scotland

10.30 am

The Chairman
: I remind hon. Members that the debate is about agriculture and fishing. The supply estimates under discussion cover both.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Sir Hector Monro)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered Class XIV, votes 1 and 2.

As Mr. Maxton just said, these estimates cover agriculture and fishing. I shall start with agriculture. I am sure that all Scottish farmers wish that they were having some of this warm weather up north, where it has been raining and has been miserable for the past week.

The framework of the common agriculture policy and now of the general agreement on tariffs and trade is crucial. The Government have consistently argued for sensible reform of CAP in the interests of consumers, taxpayers, and farmers. We supported the substantial package of CAP reforms agreed by the European Community in May 1992. Those reforms shifted agricultural support away from wasteful market intervention measures towards direct payments to producers on-farm.

The Government successfully fought against the original MacSharry proposals that would have discriminated against large farms, which are much more prevalent in Scotland. The Government secured a good deal for Scottish farmers. Scottish farmers have benefited substantially from the revised and new schemes introduced since 1992, for example, arable area and headage and extensification payments.

There was particular concern among farmers over the introduction of the new integrated administration and control system—an important measure being implemented across the European Community to help combat fraud. I am pleased to say that the IACS exercise turned out to be a great success by any standards. Over 99 per cent. of ICAS forms were received by the deadline last year, including detailed information and maps for individual holdings in many areas. That was a tremendous achievement, and I commend the staff of the Scottish Office Agriculture and Fisheries Department for the immense amount of additional work that they did.

GATT provides a stable base within which British farmers can plan with more certainty. It provides a more rational framework for the future development of a more market-orientated CAP. The agreement does not take effect until the middle of 1995. It applies to the EC as a whole and as yet is specified in fairly general terms. 2 I agree with the Commission's analysis that the new GATT obligations are broadly consistent with the measures already agreed under CAP reform, notably through the agreed cuts in cereal prices. GATT reinforces the move towards a greater market orientation of agriculture. It should be seen as an opportunity of which United Kingdom and Scottish farmers are in a good position to take advantage.

My right hon. Friend the Secretary of State and I took part in a useful and constructive meeting with United Kingdom farming unions to discuss GATT on 18 April. I welcome the measured support of the farming unions for our efforts which have been considerably more positive than in some other Community countries.

Scottish agriculture, together with food and drink products dependent on farming, has some significant potential advantages in the more competitive environment that lies ahead. At the heart of that is our reputation for quality, based on an unspoilt natural environment and good farming practices. The Government are determined to build on those advantages by encouraging the improved marketing of Scottish food and drink products, at home and abroad.

I shall paraphrase some recent developments. The marketing development scheme, which will be of tremendous help to producers and processors, earmarks £1.5 million for Scotland. There is an EC scheme of grants for processing and marketing agricultural and fisheries products. Over the next six years, more than £30 million is expected to be available in Scotland towards capital grants for buildings, plants and equipment.

As many hon. Members know, I have emphasised the importance of quality in farming wherever I have been during the past two years and that supports the line taken by the National Farmers Union for Scotland over the Scottish quality farm assurance scheme. I have also pressed hard for a Scottish quality mark. Mr. James Millar has been chairing a committee which has now given its recommendations. I have commissioned the preparation of a business plan so that that can be further developed this year.

On top of that, we have a new Scottish food quality certification body which is separate from producers and will certify the quality that is required for Scottish products and will determine which products can have the mark on their packaging.

The livestock sector of Scottish agriculture is in very good heart. The 1992 reforms to the common agricul- JPEG 5 tural policy led to substantial increases in the livestock headage subsidy rates.

In addition, the developments in the market for beef and sheepmeat, in particular in the last few years, have led to buoyant market prices for cattle and sheep farmers in Scotland.

Total expenditure on the livestock subsidy schemes—the sheep annual premium scheme, the suckler cow premium scheme, the beef special premium scheme and the hill livestock compensatory allowances—has increased from £182 million for the 1992 scheme year to an estimated £218 million under the 1994 scheme year. There has been a dramatic increase in subsidies. If we consider suckler cows in particular, the subsidy—including the extensification addition—is now £93.90, which is a tremendous help to hill farmers, particularly in the less-favoured areas.

As a result, the net farm income of cattle and sheep farmers has increased significantly in the past two years. Overall, those farming primarily sheep have seen their net farm income increase by 39 per cent., those farming mainly cattle by 100 per cent. That is all extremely good news for Scottish agriculture.

The fact that there is a 20 per cent. penalty on the 3.8 per cent. overshoot will mean that there will be only some 0.7 per cent. penalty in the non-LFA this year.
