Mr. Andrew Welsh
(Angus, East)

Member for Carrick, Cumnock and Doon Valley has made a forthright speech, but I must part company with him because he has been arguing on the issue of size and ignoring the issue of proportion. Mere size does not solve every problem; the key to progress is good management, with whatever level of resources is available, through councils appropriate to, and representative of, their area.

This time I do see a solution for Clydesdale—, through cost effectiveness, service delivery and local support across all parties and sections of the community.

326 I support Clydesdale's bid to become a unitary authority. The community does not want to lose its identity in a large, remote and, to it, unwanted south Lanarkshire. Clydesdale is different in character from the remainder of the proposed council. Clydesdale has a large rural area with a sparse population and a geography and economy distinctly different from the more industrialised and heavily populated parts of Lanarkshire.

The existing district council has reflected those differences and delivered services in a style and manner totally suited to its demographic needs. In housing, its maintenance expenditure is the second highest in Scotland, yet its rents are low. Ninety-seven per cent. of Clydesdale's housing stock has full central heating and it has developed a decentralised administrative system. Clydesdale district has a national reputation for its approach to management and for its services.

It is precisely because of that community-based, sensitive service provision that I support Clydesdale as a unitary authority. The smaller population ceases to become a drawback when allied to a management and service delivery structure that provides value for money and meets exactly the needs of its communities. Instead of being a mere southern fringe of what would be a large urban-based system, Clydesdale would be at the centre, and be an authority that delivered quality services exactly suited to the needs of its client population. That type of quality service is at the heart of the matter.

Member for Carrick, Cumnock and Doon Valley referred. The majority said yes, but it is important to be aware of the question that was answered. "Are you in favour of the establishment of a unitary council for the area presently covered by Clydesdale district?"

 That positive proposal achieved majority support.

It can be said with absolute certainty that those who voted yes strongly favoured a unitary Clydesdale authority. The minority no vote could probably be further divided into several categories. Some would oppose south Lanarkshire and some would oppose JPEG 327 any reorganisation. Who can tell? What was clear beyond doubt was the majority vote of confidence for Clydesdale as one of the new unitary authorities.

It is the quality of service that is so important—a service supplied by a method appopriate to the area and communities that the council services. Size alone is not important, good management and quality service are also important.

The people of Clydesdale do not wish to be part of a large south Lanarkshire, as the Government proposed originally.
