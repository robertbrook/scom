In a sense, the clause is easily understood. It means that the successor authorities must be consulted about a huge number of important land, building and other contractual agreements.

We have consistently argued that the Bill is a Bill too far. It is unwanted, expensive and unnecessary, and the people of Scotland feel very angry about certain parts of it. The referendum, to which we cannot refer, reinforces the arguments that we have made day in and day out in Committee.

Apart from all that, there is a double burden. First, I wish to discuss the alternative date. We want to know what the Secretary of State will do with his power to specify the date on which the provisions will be implemented. I suspect that the Government's half-baked proposals have been discussed with very few people. There is more to the matter than politicians attacking the Government: that can happen any day. But will the Minister say what discussions have been held with, for example, the chambers of commerce, the Confederation of British Industry, or the Federation of Small Businesses? What discussions have taken place in the constituencies of the Conservative Members who are helping to push through this ill-conceived clause?

The answer to all of those questions is that, basically, there have been no discussions. The business community will not know the impact of the clause until they get embroiled in the mess that it makes. It is another chicken and egg question: if we did not have this crazy Bill, would we need this clause? The Government are hell-bent on getting the Bill through and so need something to stabilise the transitional process. Why is that necessary? Why should the successor authorities be able to constrain work being done, contracts being let and land being sold? It has been suggested that tensions have already appeared in the planning relationships between districts JPEG 1363 and regions. Some planning authorities may be holding up decisions because they are looking ahead to the impact of the Bill. The Government must answer the questions about consultation: what, if any, consultations have taken place, and what has been the feedback?

The second problem with the date concerns compulsory competitive tendering. The Scottish Office sent a letter dated 18 March to the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities, in which the Government linked CCT in Scotland with the timetable of the Bill. The implementation and practice of CCT are very complex. The Scottish Office letter is fairly lengthy, and sets out the conditions in the transition phase governing the extension of CCT, which the Government are considering. It also discusses CCT in relation to existing activities, and examines CCT under the Local Government Planning and Land Act 1980.

An important point is made at the end of the letter which merely confuses further the already crisis-stricken timetable drawn up by the Government. Not content with producing a tiered approach to the future of CCT, the Government state in the letter that they intend to issue guidance notes for consultation with COSLA. The letter makes it clear that three months should be given for consultation on the draft guidance, and that COSLA, at the end of that period, may decide that it is appropriate to discuss the matter further with the Scottish Office.

We are in March now. If the consultation process starts in April, will the timetable for the Bill begin to unfold? There will be some tentative views about CCT in the offing and consultation will be held in the period after the Bill leaves this House and during the time that the Government begin to think seriously about how they can implement it. That is ludicrous. If the Government took seriously the mess that they have made they would scrap CCT, or call a moratorium on it. There would be no new extensions, and no changing of the dates of existing commitments to CCT or discussion of how they might be phased in after the transition process.

For CCT to continue at a time when there is at worst a freeze and at best massive delays in contractual arrangements is a recipe for economic and social chaos in Scotland. Why have not the Government taken the sensible approach and told local authorities to put CCT aside at the end of an existing contract? It certainly has not fulfilled the Government's wild expectations about value for money and has resulted in massive numbers of excellent staff being kicked out of local authorities. Those with private sector jobs have had their wages slashed; their conditions have deteriorated and unacceptable employment conditions have been foisted upon them.

If there is a moratorium, it must continue beyond the transition period. To judge from the letter, the Government are tackling the issue, but we need a clear, unequivocal commitment to take compulsory competitive tendering off the political agenda for a considerable time.

We are probing the issue of the relevant date and suggesting alternatives because we are anxious about the impact of the proposal on the big providers in Scotland. Strathclyde and Lothian were mentioned but we could 1364 equally have mentioned the other 10 regions and the 53 district councils.

The Government must face reality for a change. When the Bill is enacted, the Secretary of State will define the relevant date in an order. From that point there will have to be discussions between existing and successor authorities. Let us take police procurement in Strathclyde, for example. At present, Strathclyde regional council can decide on orders and contracts and then get on with it, buying from the private sector and generating employment and economic activity.

Let us think ahead to when this mess of a Bill is implemented. Strathclyde will have to speak to 12 successor police authorities because the board may be in the process of being established. There will be a Strathclyde police authority, 12 successor police authorities and the embryo for a joint board. The wide world outside in Strathclyde is waiting for orders, for jobs to be created and for work that local government does well to be started. What will happen? Who will do what in that mess? The Government will say, as usual, "Well, we hope that there will be a fair degree of co-operation and good will." But as I have said many times, the livelihood of employees, councils and the business community cannot be left on a wing and a prayer, or a hope and a prayer.

The Government simply are not addressing the issues; instead, there is a half-baked proposal that will inflict enormous hardship on the contractors involved. In respect of the police example, the Government must say how it will work for the benefit of the people of Strathclyde who are the recipients of the services.

The other reason for the alternative date is that we do not trust the Government.
