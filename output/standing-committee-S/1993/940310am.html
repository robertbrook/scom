Before I discuss examples, for which the maps will be helpful, I want to be fair to the Government. A great deal of concern has been expressed about the potential dangers in the Bill and under reorganisation. I want to make it clear that I do not believe that it was ever the Government's intention that the Bill should in itself change catchment areas, nor do I believe that it was their intention to change the basis of the Education (Scotland) Act 1980 or to abolish school transport across boundaries.

It is important to realise that everyone in Scotland has feared the worst and that there has been a considerable campaign on these issues. People had little faith in the Government's good will. That says a great deal about the regard in which the Government are held. Virtually no one in Scotland was prepared to give the Government the benefit of the doubt.

The new powers that the Government are seeking in their new clauses need clarification as to whether they will lead to discrimination against non-denominational schools. Both the Roman Catholic Church and the Church of Scotland have made it clear that they want all schools to be treated equally. In those circumstances, the Government have to spell out whether they believe that the letter or the practice of the new clauses will discriminate for or against the non-denominational or the Catholic sectors. Although I accept that the Government do not intend reorganisation to have a discriminatory effect, I will be able to show that the way in which the legislation is applied will inevitably have such an effect. I shall discuss transport first. One of the Opposition's new clauses would include in legislation an undertaking that there will be no detrimental alteration to existing travel arrangements as a result of the changes and the ensuing financial arrangements. The statutory limits are two miles and three miles, yet many authorities operate limits of one mile and two miles. The present limits in Strathclyde are one mile for children attending primary school and two miles for those attending secondary school. The legal limit is two miles for the under-eights and three miles for the eights and over. Therefore, it would be entirely within the law for an eight-year-old who currently gets a bus for a mile and a yard to have to walk just under three miles in future. That is a clear deterioration.

It is unacceptable for eight-year-olds to walk up to three miles. That is not the position in many parts of the country. The Government should be prepared to accept that they must not allow any deterioration. A clear statement in the Bill would be helpful.

To the best of my knowledge, under the existing system there is no problem when school catchment areas overlap local authority catchment areas. The Minister must spell out to us—if not today, later in writing, and also in reply to the many points that I shall raise—the process for closure or change of boundaries.

I believe that there must be a change in the regulations by which school changes are made. The current legal minimum of 28 days written notice seems to be insufficient. The best practice of the vast majority of local authorities should be made statutory by an amendment to the appropriate regulations. I hope that the Minister will accept that.

It is not clear to me—and the Minister will have to spell out—how decisions are to be made in the case of school catchment areas that cross boundaries. I understand that both sets of parents and both authorities are to be consulted, but it is not clear whether both authorities will have to agree; whether one will have a veto over the other's decisions; whether the authority covering a second school that is fed by a primary school for which diversion elsewhere is proposed would be able to overrule the decision; or whether the secondary school's authority would be able to hold on to the primary pupils, even against the wishes of the authority in which that primary school is situated. All those problems will arise at some time if the new system is introduced. The Minister must make it clear whether either local authority would have a veto.

Would decisions be made by the authority covering the secondary school, that covering the primary school, or the initiating authority? If they did not agree, would the matter be left to the Minister? Would that happen only when boundaries were to be crossed or only when a request was made? Profound disagreements will sometimes arise and it should be made clear who will have the power to make decisions and in precisely what circumstances the Minister will be able to intervene.

It should be borne in mind that the measure is being introduced in the context of falling rolls and financial pressures. In those circumstances the status quo is not an option for many authorities. Population transfers and falling rolls will mean that circumstances are fluid and that changes will occur. I want the Minister to make it clear how he intends the system whereby money is to follow the pupil to operate. As he is aware, many schools that are operating at well under capacity will have higher costs per pupil than they otherwise would. It will be in a local authority's interest to keep the loading factor in its schools as high as possible, if it is being allocated an average cost per pupil. Therefore, rational local authorities will want catchments that provide them with the highest possible loading in their schools, to minimise the diseconomies. That will not necessarily be educationally beneficial to the youngsters, but is entirely rational for the local authorities, at a time of falling rolls and severe financial pressure.

The outcome could be that one authority will effectively penalise another by taking decisions about catchment areas, or decisions that will affect school loading, that will have implications for other authorities, with- 1006 out those other authorities being able to intervene. One of the existing provisions under which such consent is required is section 22D of the 1980 Act, which relates to denominational schools. Under this section, a proposal to make a significant change in relation to such a school, including any changes to the school's catchment area, must come to the Secretary of State for consent in certain circumstances.

The question of whether a proposal requires consent under this section and, if so, consideration of the proposal by the Secretary of State, involves the application of a key test—namely, whether the Secretary of State is satisfied that it would result in a significant deterioration in the distribution and provision of school education in denominational schools, compared with that in other schools managed by the education authority. Because section 22D refers to schools managed by the education authority, it is not clear that it would apply when more than one education authority was involved.
