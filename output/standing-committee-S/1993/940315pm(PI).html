Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: I appreciate the motive for the amendment, but I can assure the Committee that it is not necessary. It is the police authority that is responsible for the income and expenditure of its force. The police force as such is not involved.

The existing requirements for accounts and auditing of police authorities will not be altered by the Bill. First, section 96 of the Local Government (Scotland) Act 1973 requires every local authority to keep accounts relating to all funds of the authority. The same section provides for audit by the commission for local authority accounts. Secondly, the police authority's claims for police specific grant under the Police Grant (Scotland) Order are audited separately by the commission before the amount of police grant payable each year is finally determined.

In the light of that explanation I hope that the amendment will be withdrawn.

I can tell the hon. Member for Fife, Central that the present eight forces will remain, and that there will be no additional bureaucracy. The hon. Gentleman described police civilians as pen pushers: they are nothing of the sort. Gentleman will agree that police officers should be released from such duties for active patrol work and for being on the beat.

I shall give the hon. Member for Dundee, East (Mr. McAllion) a clear and definite explanation of how police funding will operate. We have announced already that, quite separately from the Bill, we intend to cash limit police grants and remove the Secretary of State's control over the manpower levels on 1 April 1996. I assure the Committee that there will be consultation with police authorities and chief constables on the details of the formula for distributing central Government grants. Joint boards will decide the budget for their police forces and all constituent councils will be represented on the joint boards. The Secretary of State will pay the police a specific grant to the joint boards of 51 per cent. of the boards' expenditure up to a specified level. The boards will be free to spend above that level, if they meet 100 per cent. of the cost.

The Police (Scotland) Act 1967 provides that the amalgamation schemes to establish joint boards must make provision for payment by the constituent councils in such proportion as may be specified in the scheme of the expenditure incurred by the boards. The Act does not specify what arrangements are made for payment by the constituent authority.

We expect the authorities concerned to agree to the provision of costs for their boards. If they do not agree, the Secretary of State will decide that, if in future arrangements in an amalgamation scheme prove to be unsatisfactory, they could be changed. That has been done for schemes that have existed since 1979 for Lothian and the Borders. The hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire (Mr. Kirkwood) will be aware that there were complaints about the funding from the board. People thought that there was too much funding. There has been a readjustment accordingly.

The Secretary of State will formulate the amalgamation scheme for the new joint boards. We shall consult on the details of funding. We hope that the constituent authorities will agree among themselves but if they do not, the Secretary of State will decide.
