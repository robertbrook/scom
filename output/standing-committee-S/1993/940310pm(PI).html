Local Government etc. (Scotland) Bill

Clause 32
CO-OPERATION BETWEEN EDUCATION AUTHORITIES

Amendment made: No. 247, in page 27, line 20, after "continuing", insert

"until they are changed by an education authority in accordance with this Act".—[ Lord James Douglas-Hamilton.]

4.30 pm

Mr. Michael Connarty
(Falkirk, East)
: I beg to move amendment No. 329, in page 27, line 20, after "continuing", insert—

"(1D) Where the arrangements for the placing of children or young persons with special educational needs in schools subsisting before the establishment of new local government areas under Part I of the Local Government etc. (Scotland) Act 1994 lead, as a consequence of such establishment, to school education for such pupils belonging to the area of one education authority being provided at schools or educational establishments under the management of another education authority (in this subsection referred to as the provider authority), the education authority shall have a duty to make arrangements with the provider authority to ensure an adequate and efficient education for the child or young person with special educational needs, including an appropriate level of psychological services support and in the case of a child or young person with a record of needs, the services specified therein.".

This is one of the pivotal debates that may divide the Committee. This morning, we discussed various matters that are of interest to Christian denominations and the Minister's one-time sojourn in Catholic education. I am reminded of a quotation that is not exclusive to Catholic education, but which is most appropriate. It was written early in Christian teaching, in St. Matthew, chapter 25:

"inasmuch as ye have done it unto one of the least of these my brethren, ye have done it unto me."

It is appropriate to the clause.

The Minister said in Committee on 8 March that he thought that there was adequate cover for special needs and special educational needs in the Education (Scotland) Act 1980. He stated:

"Under section 60 of the Education (Scotland) Act 1980 education authorities have a duty to establish which children in their area have pronounced, specific, complex special educational needs—which, as such, require continual review—and to keep a record of needs for each such child. Under section 62, it is the duty of the education authority to make certain that its decision for a recorded child includes provision for special educational needs. All such duties will remain in force, and there is no question of their being changed."—[ Official Report, 1st Scottish Standing Committee, 8 March 1994; c. 918.]

That may be so, but they need to be strengthened. Provision for special educational needs should be in the 1044 Bill so that there is no question of any authority—or either, if there are two—being able to walk away from that duty. The amendment would ensure that. Section 60 of the 1980 Act makes it clear that the local authority shall have the duty to ascertain which children in its area have special educational needs, interestingly, section 61 of the Act provides that parents shall be duty bound—they will be liable to a fine of £10—to ensure that their children attend any psychological or medical examination as the authority considers necessary to ascertain whether they have special needs. I spent almost 15 very happy years teaching in a school for children with special educational needs.

That is self-evident; we are trying to provide for it in the course of the debate.

Members of the Committee can envisage circumstances in which a small new council might be persuaded to accept a major development proposal despite the fact that it might breach a structure plan. There is an example of such conflict in my constituency. The Minister will be familiar with the case because he lives in the area.

Archerfield is a fine, but derelict building of great architectural importance. It should be redeveloped, preserved and turned into a public asset. A developer came along and proposed that Archerfield be developed as a hotel with a golf course. As usual, part of the package was consent to a housing development—500 houses to generate the money that was required to pay for the development of the building.

The district council understandably thought that it was worth paying the price. It was prepared to give consent to the 500 houses. No one was keen on the houses being built because the site was not appropriate to the development of what was effectively a large village. Lothian regional council called in the proposal because it was a breach of the structure plan.

It is interesting to note that because Lothian was able to take a grip of the situation, 25 houses, not 500, is the price of the development.
