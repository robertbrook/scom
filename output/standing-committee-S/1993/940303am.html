Mr. George Robertson
(Hamilton)
: On a point of order, Mr. Martin. I do not wish to detain the Committee, but my point of order relates to page ix of the explanatory and financial memorandum.

There is an obligation on the Government to include in each Bill laid before Parliament an assessment of the effect of the Bill on public service manpower. All Governments take seriously that important obligation. As this Bill relates, in many instances, to 300,000 people employed in local authorities in Scotland, that factor is crucial. The element of the financial and explanatory memorandum relating to

"Effect of the Bill on Public Service Manpower"

states:

" Part I will abolish one tier of local government resulting in an estimated reduction in local government staffing levels of between 700 and 2,200."

Much of the Government's argument and calculation is based on that fairly wide estimate. Yesterday, the European Court of Justice made an interim decision, that will effect British law, because directives interpreted by that court take precedence over all British law, including the Bill, should it get through the House of Commons.

In that interim judgment, the Advocate General the European Court of Justice, Walter Van Gerven, said that Britain had failed to fulfil its legal obligations on the protection of workers' rights under article V of the treaty of Rome and under Brussels directives 77/187 and 75/129. Those are the transfer of undertakings provisions. They obviously relate to our consideration of the Bill today and make a mockery of the Government's assessment that there will be savings of between 700 and 2,200 in manpower. If that interim judgment stands—and virtually all such judgments become final judgments—the British Government will not be allowed to make that saving in manpower because all local authority staff currently employed will have to be re-engaged under their own existing terms.

Not only does that interim judgment relate to consultation with workers' organisations—clearly, the British Government have been found to be specifically in breach of European law—it will effect the transfer of undertakings provisions.

760 I suggest that, as that part of the explanatory memorandum which relates to the effect on public service manpower is seriously defective as a consequence of that interim judgment of the European Court, the Government should reconsider whether they wish to proceed with the Bill at all.
