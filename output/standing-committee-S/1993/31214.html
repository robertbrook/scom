10.31 am

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Lang)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of the economy in Scotland.

As we approach the end of the year, I think that it is an appropriate time to debate the Scottish economy. I welcome the opportunity to do so.

The Scottish economy is inextricably linked to that of the United Kingdom as a whole. It does, of course, have some important distinctive features, to which I shall allude later. But essentially, the health of the Scottish economy depends on the health of the United Kingdom economy as a whole, of which it forms a part. That must be the basic starting-point to any discussion on the Scottish economy and its prospects for the future.

Judged by that criterion, there is indeed cause for confidence. The United Kingdom economy has climbed strongly out of recession. Gross domestic product has risen for six consecutive quarters—a year and a half since we turned the corner from the bottom of the recession. The latest Treasury forecast is for higher-than-expected growth in the current year and for accelerated growth in 1994. In parallel with those forecasts, independent forecasters also predict continued GDP growth for Scotland.

The strong performance of the economy has been due, in no small part, to the Government's success in controlling inflation. Retail price inflation has been below 2 per cent. for most of the past year, something that has not happened for over 30 years. Successful control of inflation has also enabled the Government to cut interest rates by no less than 9 percentage points over the past three years, and, at 5.5 per cent., these are now the lowest in the European Union. 4 Recent gains in productivity have been impressive, especially in the manufacturing sector, and have considerably strengthened our competitive challenge in domestic and overseas markets.

It is in the overseas market that the benefits to Scotland, in particular, have been most apparent. Let me refer the Committee to the survey of manufactured exports in 1992–93 published earlier this month by the Scottish Council (Development and Industry). This showed that Scotland's share of UK manufactured exports rose to an all-time high of 10.1 per cent. in 1992 and that Scottish manufacturing exports per employee were no less than 24 per cent. above the corresponding figure for the United Kingdom. In particular sectors, exports of office machinery and data processing equipment were up by over 14 per cent.; of paper and printing and publishing by 13 per cent.; of rubber and plastics by nearly 11 per cent.; of mechanical engineering by 10 per cent.; and of food and drink-predominantly, of course, whisky—by nearly 8 per cent. I could go on, but the message is clear enough.

The hon. Gentleman referred to research and development. I agree that that is important for the electronics industry. However, it is significant that 47 per cent. of all inward investor electronics companies include some element of research and development in their activities in this country. We are helping such companies to tune in to the strengths in our academic institutions. Therefore, great potential exists for the industry.

Mr. Lang
: Certainly, all hon. Members acknowledge the difficulties that Ferranti has experienced in recent years. Those difficulties are caused not only by the competitiveness of the sphere of its activity, but also by the JPEG 5 specific circumstances of the company and its management in recent years. If the Scottish Office Industry Department can be of assistance, we are ready to help. I understand the anxiety of the hon. Member for Midlothian (Mr. Clarke) about the company.

The picture that I present to the Committee includes more than the electronics industry. The picture is of success across a broad and increasingly diversified range of export activity. At a time of very difficult trading conditions in the world economy, particularly in Europe, that is a remarkable achievement by Scottish companies-employers and employees alike—and I offer them my warmest congratulations. Their performance shows graphically what can be achieved when Governments create a stable and fundamentally strong economic base that leaves business free to seek out market opportunities, to take risks, and to create both wealth and employment.

Yesterday, Mr. Hogg, you and I had the opportunity to attend an important trade fair in Glasgow—Europartenariat. There, 1,500 small businesses from European countries, including nearly 350 Scottish businesses, met for two days in Glasgow to help to develop the relationship between the Scottish economy and European economies. Huge potential exists for the future growth of business and for revenue to Scottish companies.

I do not claim that export performance sums up the strength of an economy. I accept, too, that some of Scotland's key export markets in Europe are at present going through a difficult phase and that the recovery in demand in countries such as Italy and Germany might occur only at a modest rate next year. They have not yet steered through the world recession with the same success as we have. But I do believe that our strength in exports is a clear indicator of the underlying strength of the economy. Indeed, we should not forget that Scotland entered the recent recession in a relatively strong position. The Scottish economy grew by more than 12 per cent. during the three years to 1990. That is faster than the average growth in Europe and faster than growth in the United States.

Mr. Foulkes
: As the Secretary of State lives in Monkton, he will know that one of the largest Scottish exporters in the past has been Jetstream. Mr. Lang
: I understand and share the hon. Gentleman's anxiety about the problems that British Aerospace is facing with Jetstream. I met the management of British Aerospace recently to discuss the problems. We must recognise that the market in which it is competing—11 or so manufacturers worldwide are competing for very few orders—creates circumstances that no Government can control. The Government have made to British Aerospace 6 the largest single regional selective assistance offer ever made to any company. That should help to accommodate it in Prestwick and to create the sort of expansion there that the quality of its product justifies. I hope that it will weather the present economic difficulties through which the sector is passing.

Mr. Foulkes
: Will the Secretary of State give way?

Mr. Lang
: No, I will give way to the hon. Gentleman later.

During the 1980s, the Scottish economy grew by an average of 2·4 per cent. a year, contrasting with less than 1 per cent. a year between 1974 and 1979. Manufacturing productivity in Scotland—an important criterion for measuring the success of an economy—rose during the 1980s by 5·4 per cent. a year, as against only 2 per cent. during the 1970s. In the electronics industry alone productivity rose at an average rate of 11·6 per cent. per annum during the 1980s and during the same period the output of the electronics industry increased more than fourfold. Such figures illustrate the progress of the Scottish economy in recent years and go a long way to explain its stronger performance in the recent recession.

Mr. Lang

The electronics industry in Scotland has been a great success story against a background of difficult worldwide trading conditions for the electronics sector. Because it has increased its productivity by 11·6 per cent. per annum, it has remained substantial and competitive in Scotland.

The smaller the base, the more important the ability to sell products abroad. A successful GATT settlement will present opportunities for Scottish industry. Failure to secure such a settlement will damage the Scottish economy. All hon. Members have an interest in ensuring that such a settlement is made.

The domestic context of the debate is the aftermath of a Budget, which ran well as a west end show—the Chancellor of the Exchequer gave a good performance—but does not run well in the clubs and pubs or in the repeats. People are beginning to realise that the Budget sets out the biggest tax increase in British post-war history. Yet the Government were elected on a pledge to cut taxes. The taxes fall particularly unevenly in Scotland. In justifying tax increases, the Chancellor made a virtue of the fact that mortgage rates have fallen. We must bear in mind that fewer people in Scotland have mortgages than the United Kingdom average, and more people in Scotland pay rents. In the past few years, rents have not fallen—they have increased steadily, faster than the rate of inflation. The Budget has meant an even more savage cut in the spending power of people in Scotland than those in the rest of the United Kingdom. In spite of the Secretary of State's attempt to gloss it over in terms of average expenditure, the imposition of VAT on fuel charges falls harder on people in Scotland than elsewhere.

We have discussed the importance of small businesses. I welcome the fact that the formation of small businesses has increased in Scotland. It is disappointing that there has also been an increase in bankruptcies, which means that the net increase is nothing like as dramatic as the overall increase would suggest. I am pleased, however, that in north-east Scotland there has been a net gain in the formation of small businesses. That is not as a result of redundancies but because of a degree of dynamism within the economy.

The Government have been insensitive to the impact of their proposals for statutory sick pay, which will be a burden to small businesses. The Secretary of State was wrong to say that small businesses are exempt. Every employer will have to pay the costs of statutory sick pay for the first four weeks of any illness. Most illnesses do not last for four weeks and consequently there will be a charge on businesses. Considering the vehemence with which the Government have attacked the notion of a social chapter in the European Community and the increasing social costs 34 that employers face, it is extraordinary that they should have imposed such a severe social cost upon employers at an especially difficult time.

Although several measures in the Budget might be genuinely welcomed by successful small businesses, they need healthy markets to survive. Just as the economy is showing signs of climbing out of recession, the Government have imposed the greatest restriction on demand in post-war history. There is a danger that that could lead to the squeezing of markets just as business confidence is rising. The Budget may yet turn out to have been ill timed and ill thought through.
