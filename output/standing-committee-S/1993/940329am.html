Sir Hector Monro: No, I shall not give way now.

As the hon. Member for Hamilton said on Thursday evening, there were 210 water authorities and 234 sewerage authorities at the end of the war. The Water (Scotland) Act 1967 established the 13 water boards and the Central Scotland Water Development Board. The Local Government (Scotland) Act 1973 reduced that number by creating the nine regional water authorities and the three island councils in addition to the Central Scotland Water Development Board. If the Bill is passed, we shall have a new, single-tier authority, the creation of 30 or more councils and the disappearance of others. Change is, therefore, essential—doing nothing is not an alternative.

The Government's policy—set out in the White Paper—is to deliver the best and most cost-effective water and sewerage services possible. To meet the standards that we want in the United Kingdom, the large number of European Union directives on matters such as cleaning up beaches and preventing the discharge of sewage sludge into the sea after 1998 and to bring the standard of water delivery and sewage removal up to that required in the next century, we shall have to make capital expenditure of about £5 billion over the next 15 years—I think that that is generally accepted—and create a more efficient structure.

Costs have risen rapidly under local authority control. In the five-year period from 1989–90 to 1994–95, taking an extrapolation for the community charge period, the average band D household water bill has increased from £32.50 to £67.01—an increase of 106 per cent. Sewerage charges, again abstracted for the different forms of costing, have increased by 54.2 per cent. Those figures were provided this year by the Centre of Regulated Industries.
