Clause 121
THE PRINCIPAL REPORTER

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. George Robertson
: The clause goes to the heart of part III of the Bill and needs to be considered by the Committee.

There is little doubt that part III should not be included: that view is virtually unanimous in Scotland. The people of Scotland were promised that there would be comprehensive legislation in this Session to deal with the welfare of children. There was widespread public expectation that that would occur, which was natural because England and Wales have had such comprehensive legislation on children for several years. Scotland has a major deficiency in that area and, as a consequence, Scotland's children suffer.

It is not simply a desire to consolidate legislation that is behind the demand for such comprehensive legislation. A series of major inquiries have given rise to considerable public concern and there has also been a series of Law Commission reports, which the House should urgently consider.

The Committee and the Scottish community know precisely why the Bill does not contain comprehensive legislation on children. It is because the Government have given priority to their own gerrymandering operations in local government. One issue has been taken completely out of context in a field of enormous importance to people in Scotland, purely because the Government wanted to find time to gerrymander Scottish local government for their own interest. Consequently, they have sidelined, pushed back urgent legislation for children, and that is unforgiveable.

Professionals who know legislation on children intimately and the many thousands of people throughout Scotland whose daily lives lead them to conclude that there are problems with the law, will not easily forgive the Government for putting unnecessary, Unwanted gerrymandered legislation on local government ahead of the clear necessity for legislation on children.

The Government published last year a White Paper on Scotland's children which gave rise to the view that early legislation was being proposed. Much of the White Paper was acceptable and there was a reasonable degree of consensus on it, so there would not have been enormous political controversy. However, even after the consultation and a debate in Committee, we are faced with one crucial aspect of the way in which we deal with children in trouble being taken out of context and precisely the wrong remedy being produced.

My remarks have the corroboration of the children's panel advisory group, an eminent group which brings together the elements involved in children's panels. The consultations about the provision have been all too hasty and the Bill contains the defects that have resulted from that.

1862 The proposals in part III are opposed by the regional children's panels, which include people of all political opinions and of none, and by the children's panel advisory group, which consists of representatives from the 12 regional and islands children's panel advisory committees in Scotland and advises the Secretary of State on matters relating to the general administration of the panels.

I would have thought that the Government would pay close attention to the criticisms by such professional and expert groups. They pointed out, and I stress, that the proposals have the following disadvantages: they will lead to a loss of accountability; to the use of non-local reporters; to more remote centres for hearings; to an increased fragmentation of the service; and to an increased cost of the service.

The proposals will also lead to a loss of the professionalism that the service has grown used to since its inception, a diminished independence for principal reporters and a greatly reduced influence for reporters on resources and services.

That is a formidable list of criticisms of the Government's proposals. By defeating the clause, the Committee could force the Government back to the drawing board to examine the proposals again in the wider context of what is necessary and vital to deal with the problems of children in our community.

I shall be brief, because we intend that the matter will be tabled again on Report. It is right that the Government's haste in legislating out of context should be given a wider audience so that the public understand what has happened. The Committee's debate should be seen as preliminary to the wider debate that will take place on Report and, undoubtedly, in the House of Lords.

There will be a loss of accountability because at the moment reporters are answerable for general management policy and budgetary decisions to the local authorities. Given that a reporter is a locally based public official with significant powers and responsibility over a community's children and young people, such accountability is appropriate and necessary. That was the purpose of the Social Work (Scotland) Act 1968.

The 1968 Act established the children's hearings system and that was based on the profound recommendations of the Kilbrandon report. It stated that the individual problems of young people in trouble were required to be examined in the context of their situation in their community and their families and dealt with by the organisation of suitable resources around the child and the family.

The context of local treatment of children and local accountability of the institutions established to deal with them was at the heart of the 1968 Act. On both sides of the Committee and of the political divide in Scotland there is widespread appreciation of the quality of what the children's hearing system in Scotland does. There is pride in the notion that, perhaps, we have got things right and other parts of the country have got them wrong. It would be an act of great folly to undermine, by rash and precipitate action, what has so painfully been built up. The establishment of a national reporter service will undoubtedly result in a severe loss of local accountability.

First, by definition a national system and a principal reporter answerable to a non-elected board will lose the JPEG 1863 key attribute of local accountability. As an example of that loss, I point out to the Committee that the decision whether to pursue higher appeals to the Court of Session is at present taken by any reporter in a locality, subject only to the decision of the local authority, the members of which are themselves responsible to the community. The Scottish Office has already stated that in the context of the national system that is to be created,

"choices would have to be made"

about the initiation of appeals within the very tight budgetary constraints on a national service. The decision will be taken, not locally, by local people, who are, subject to local accountability, but on a central, bureaucratic basis.

A second criticism is that non-local reporters will be used. The basis of the present system is that people are chosen in their locality, operate there and are accountable to their local authorities. Centralisation would mean the prospect of the principal reporter perhaps moving reporters around Scotland to cover for factors such as illness and leave. That goes against the principle that was firmly established in the 1968 Act, that the reporter should be a person with local knowledge and with close working relationships with other agencies in the area.

Critics of the measure believe that it will lead to the hearing centres becoming more remote. The central reporter might in some circumstances find it practical or reasonable to move the centres for hearings. That would also result in the loss of the local element that is crucial to the system, and in long distance travel for families and panel members.

Increased fragmentation is also to be feared. I quote the information supplied to the Committee by the children's panel advisory group. I hope that all hon. Members have absorbed it and learned from it. Its members are

"greatly concerned that the proposals within the Bill may create a variety of structures which do not fit together well. Thus it is possible the Reporter's Service will be nationally co-ordinated; CPACs organised jointly, and Panels based on local Council areas."

The key part of the group's evidence is its statement that

"This is likely to lead to a fragmented and uncoordinated service, where accountability is lost, and the children's best interests are not necessarily kept in clear focus."

That criticism is made by people who are profoundly involved in the system that is now in operation. The Minister would be well advised to listen to it carefully. If he finds it easy to dismiss such criticism, I question his judgment. The children's panel advisory group continued by stating;

"Children's panels based on the proposed local government areas is not the best way forward. Past experience tells us that fragmentation of existing Panels will lead to a dilution of existing skills, experience and resources, poor consistency of approach, and difficulties over organising training."

That is another devastating criticism.

It has also been pointed out that establishing this centralised service will involve increased cost. The current cost of the reporter service is about £7 million. No one imagines that a centralised service could be operated more cheaply; it is likely that the cost will increase considerably. Dumfries and Galloway regional council—which is close to the constituencies of the Under-Secretary, the hon. Member for Dumfries, and the Secretary of State for Scotland, the right hon. Member for Galloway and Upper Nithsdale (Mr. Lang)—estimates 1864 that the cost of a national reporter service will be well in excess of that amount should current staffing levels be maintained, but it is likely to lead to an increase in those levels as well.

One of the most serious criticisms—which comes back to the point of order that I raised at the beginning of the sitting—is the loss of professionalism that will result from this move taken, as it is, in isolation. We cannot separate the reform of the children's hearing system. The Government are to abolish the statutory requirement for local authorities to have a director of social work and for any such director appointed to have precise professional qualifications. This part of the Bill and the Government's whole thrust will lead to a diminution in professionalism, which is the best guarantee of the high standards that we have come to enjoy and which has made the children's hearing system something of which we can all be proud.

Under the Bill, the new post of principal reporter will not have attached to it any conditions regarding qualifications. That fact, together with the way in which directors of social work have been treated by the Government, should make us genuinely worried about the level of professionalism proposed in the Bill.

When the Minister responds to the debate, perhaps he will tell us what qualifications he or the Secretary of State believe reporters should have. Will he tell us whether the post of principal reporter or reporter will be advertised and what conditions will be placed on those who want to be considered for the job?

What will be done about training after this change takes place? The comprehensive training programmes that the regions have built up will be affected by the change from eight regions to 31 council areas. What will happen about the worthwhile and crucial training that reporters currently receive, and about the training for panel members?

The diminished independence of the principal reporter is also a matter of concern. Although the Government have proclaimed the independence of the principal reporter, clause 128, if passed by the Committee, will allow the Secretary of State for Scotland to give the administration directions on the specific or general nature of its functions, and thereby to the principal reporter. What power will the principle reporter have to dismiss that advice, to give consideration to it or to change it? Will the Government give clear guarantees about the professional independence of the principal reporter? The Minister may care to explain why the principal reporter will be responsible to the Secretary of State and not to the Lord Advocate.

There are a host of reasons why the Committee should throw out the whole of part III of the Bill and why the Government should reconsider this proposal in the wider context of what is required and what is urgently necessary for Scotland's children. As good a reason as any for doing that is to be found in the Finlayson report published in 1992, entitled "Reporters to Children's Panels: Their Role, Function and Accountability".

It is amazing that the Government can casually dismiss the authority of that report and others. The Finlayson report strongly recommended that the office of reporter should remain a local authority function and should not be transferred to a national body. So far, we have heard precious little justification from the Government for departing from that recommendation. The report stated: JPEG 1865

"The relationship between Reporters and Panel membership would be less close and it would be unrealistic to anticipate that a Central Government Reporter official would be in a position to influence the provision of essential local government support and services in the same way as he does and can do as a Local Government Chief Officer. Some of the advantages claimed by advocates for the change would be more illusory than real and the status quo in this regard is to be preferred."

Why have the Government chosen to ignore that pointed advice from an authoritative report? The Finlayson report continued:

"Transfer to Central Government control would affect the balance of the hearings system and a hearings system which incorporated a significant Central Government control would experience difficulty in relating to a local authority structure. The participatory role which the Reporter currently plays in the provision of local appropriate services for children would be seriously inhibited if he were a Central Government, as opposed to a local authority official."

That recommendation was made on the basis of sound, profound, professional authority. The Government have swept it aside as they try to juggle with elements of local government for no better reason than to advantage the Conservative party.

The concept of this part of the Bill is wrong. It is wrong to consider it with measures with which it has little connection. It is wrong for the Government to ignore professional advice, especially that given directly to the Secretary of State. The Government are in grave danger of undermining an experiment in Scotland in which we all take pride. Part III of the Bill should be withdrawn. The Government should go back to the drawing board.

The Government's proposals would remove the responsibilities of the office of reporter from the 1868 independence of its setting in the local authority. Following the inquiry into events in Orkney and Fife, the Government published a White Paper, "Scotland's Children", which highlights the need for further review and changes in the framework of child care law. The strength of the existing arrangements lies in the careful balance of the various elements and the commitment that all who are involved bring to the interests of the children, young people and families involved. The proposal to nationalise the reporter service—probably the first piece of nationalisation undertaken by this Government—will damage that effective balance. It will undermine the present lines of acountability that allow effective work in the most difficult cases, and a proper and appropriate involvement of the reporter and panel members in the overall development of services for vulnerable youngsters and their families. Placed alongside the licence that the Government proposes to give the new local authorities not to have education or social work committees or for those service areas to be required to be managed by a statutory officer with appropriate professional qualification and experience, the Government's proposals represent a step back to the 1930s rather than a step forward.

The children's hearing system in Scotland was introduced as part of the implementation of the Social Work (Scotland) Act 1968, on which some of us worked extremely hard, and represented the most significant change in the juvenile justice arrangements for children and young people in Scotland this century. The crucial role of the reporter was established together with that of panel member. That brought together within a framework of welfare rather than punishment three lay people to determine, after listening to the views of professionals, the family and the child, what course of action was best suited to promote the welfare of a particular child.

The separation of welfare from guilt and punishment has proved to be immensely successful in providing for Scotland a sensitive, compassionate and considered basis for determining how the needs of children and their families can best be met. The reporter is the key person at the heart of Scotland's system of juvenile justice. It is the reporter who carries the responsibility of determining whether or not a child should be referred to a panel to be considered for compulsory measures of care. In exercising that huge responsibility and discretion the reporter relies heavily on his or her close working relationships with the professional agencies that provide services within the community, the most important of which are the social work department, the education department and, frankly, the police.

The reporter's influence is by necessity extensive, becoming involved in influencing and helping to develop corporate child care policies and strategies, and the policies of individual departments in tackling the huge JPEG 1869 problems that are generated by children in need and children in trouble. Because the reporter is a chief officer of the regional authority, he or she is able to exercise that influence through the close working relationships that exist within Scottish regional councils.

No one knows better than the Under-Secretary, the hon. Member for Edinburgh, West (Lord James Douglas Hamilton), whom address in his capacity as I speak as a constituency Member, that the system works extremely well in our area. The development of youth strategies, child protection procedures and policies and services for children under eight and their families are just three examples of the sorts of policies that the reporter will influence because of the unique knowledge that he or she has from exercising his or her role as the reporter to the children's panel. The reporter, whilst retaining professional autonomy and independence, is additionally very influential in advising elected representatives of the statutory social work committee on various aspects of child care and juvenile justice. The close working relationships that exist currently in Scotland between regional reporters, their chief officer colleagues and the elected representatives of regional councils have been a powerful force for the good in securing the necessary political support and financial resources to develop those services that are required to meet the needs of children at crucial points in their lives. It is therefore no wonder that there is widespread concern and alarm throughout Scotland that implementing these proposals will, at a stroke, sever those important links and centralise the management and control of the reporter service from within the Scottish Office. No matter what reassurances are given, there can be little doubt that the changes, if implemented in their present form, will erode the considerable influence for the good that reporters have currently through their social work committees and through the various chief officers and corporate arrangements in the regional councils of Scotland.

Clause 123
APPEAL AGAINST DISMISSAL OF PRINCIPAL REPORTER AND OTHER OFFICERS

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Dalyell
: It seems an innocent enough proposal that there should be an appeal to the Secretary of State. But, at present, at least there is some chance of an appeal being considered against the wishes of the Goverment. If an appeal is made to the Secretary of State, what will that mean? The Secretary of State, as a politician, will obviously have little to do with it. Does it mean that there will be an appeal to Mr. Sinclair or whoever occupies his job at the time?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: I have some knowledge of this because in the previous Parliament I had to deal with appeals from police officers against their dismissal. If a chief constable wished to dismiss a police officer, he JPEG 1895 could appeal to the Secretary of State. I assure the hon. Member for Linlithgow that the Secretary of State gave specific and careful consideration to each case and, in a number of instances, the chief constable's decision was overturned. The same will apply in this case. Advice will be considered fairly. The advice of the Solicitor's Office at the Scottish Office will be brought to bear and both the junior Minister and the Secretary of State will give the matter the utmost consideration because a person's future will be at stake. That, inevitably, is a matter that a Minister must regard extremely seriously.

Clause 126
DUTY OF ADMINISTRATION TO PROVIDE ACCOMMODATION ETC. FOR CHILDREN'S HEARINGS

Mr. Kirkwood
: I beg to move amendment No. 344, in page 87, line 9, leave out

"(but may be sited in another)".

The amendment would make it clear that the children's panel was sited in each local authority area. The Bill does not do that.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: We envisage the present arrangements continuing wherever possible. It is important that children and their families be allowed to continue attending centres nearest to where they live, which may not always be in their own council area. In some areas of the smaller proposed authorities, there may not currently be a reporter's office or centre in a proposed neighbouring council area that is readily accessible and convenient. We do not wish to lay down undue restrictions but would like there to be continuity as a matter of common sense. I therefore recommend that the Committee reject the amendment.

Mr. Dalyell
: I have counted at least four occasions on which the Minister has said that the present arrangements will continue wherever possible. What on earth are the Government doing upsetting a system that is, as my colleagues have said, world famous.
