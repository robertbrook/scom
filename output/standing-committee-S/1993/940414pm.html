Mr. Welsh
: The amendments all relate to the collection of water charges. Amendments Nos. 567 and 568 make a clear distinction between the providing authorities, which are the water boards, and the collecting authorities, which are the local authorities, as opposed to the Government putting the total duty on local authorities for collection and debt enforcement, thus freeing the boards to take the cash but not the obvious responsibility that goes with enforcing their collection policy.

Amendments Nos. 575 and 576 would ban poinding and warrant sales from being used for water service debt collection, as well as ensuring that the costs of collection would not exceed the amount of any sums recovered by such diligencies.

Amendment No. 577 deals with an anomaly and unfairness in the Debtors (Scotland) Act 1987. It is a technical point, but one on which I hope the Government will be prepared to take action.

Amendments Nos. 567 and 568 ensure that the collecting authorities—that is, the new councils—collect only payments due on behalf of the water boards and do not do their dirty work in the pursuit of debts through courts or other debt collection agencies. The responsible body in terms of pricing and debt collection policies should be seen to be the responsible body. That is the import of the amendment. If a water board takes policy decisions, it should also publicly and clearly take the consequences of those decisions. If a water board wishes to pursue debts overdue to it, it should do so in its own right and should not hide behind another organisation.

In other words, I see the role of local authorities as purely an agency one—to facilitate, for an appropriate fee, the due payments and so, sensibly and efficiently, to allow their local offices to be used for customer ease and customer access, as presently happens in the relationship between regional and district councils. The district councils can, on a local basis, collect payments as an agent on behalf of the region. Those local authorities would be able to pass the money on to the water boards.

I do not want the new local authorities to be the fall guys—those who are blamed for the pricing and debt collection policies of the water boards, policies which the local authorities would have no part in creating and which they may utterly oppose. I do not want them to have to enforce those policies against their will, so I propose the separation of provision and the collection of overdue debts. There should be transparency so that the paying public can see who the decision makers are. There are too many examples already of central Government lumbering local government with the blame for policy mistakes. The local authorities, as the collecting authorities, are at the sharp end of the services and get the blame, yet they are only carrying out policies created and placed on them by others. This botched Bill must not be added to the burden on local government, which deserves better.

JPEG 1807 If water boards wish to raise prices or pursue water debts, they should do so openly and in their own right. They must be seen to be responsible for their policies. Amendments Nos. 575 and 576 would stop debt pursuit if the costs of collection are greater than the sum recovered. That is the common-sense approach and reflects my absolute detestation of warrant sales. They are barbaric and unacceptable in any modern, civilised society. Warrant sales are immoral and completely inefficient. They are not cost-effective in any way. The amendments would prohibit the removal of household furniture and effects from the homes of people who have not paid the new bills to be imposed by the water boards for the supply of water.

Any Parliament that makes a pretence of representing the interests of the Scottish community must accept these amendments because the use of that diligence is immoral, and because warrant sales are not effective in the recovery of debt. They do not work. The most recent detailed research into warrant sales was carried out in 1977 by the Scottish Office's central research unit. That research found that in only 3 per cent. of personal warrant sales, as distinct from sales to recover business and commercial debts, was the sum recovered sufficient to pay all the debt and all the expenses. The real beneficiaries of warrant sales are not the creditors, but the sheriff's officers. That is unacceptable.

The Scottish Office research showed that the debtor continued to owe money after 97 per cent. of warrant sales had been held. Even in commercial warrant sales involving building and haulage contractors, for example, the debtor continued to owe money after the sale in 84 per cent. of cases. So in only 16 per cent. of commercial warrant sales were all expenses and the original debt recovered.

The evidence from the Scottish Office shows that warrant sales are not at all effective even in commercial cases, let alone in the traumatic and unacceptable circumstances of personal debt. But my main objection is not their inefficiency. It is that they are barbaric and unacceptable in a civilised society. The Scottish Law Commission agrees. Its research shows that most debtors belong to the lowest income groups. Many are unemployed, or employed only intermittently. They depend for their subsistence on social security or the intermittent earning of small amounts. Those debtors live on the margin of the statutory level of subsistence. They have no savings. Their only property consists of small household items of minimal value. Parliament should protect such people and not render them vulnerable to the barbarity of a warrant sale.

Under the threat of a warrant sale, debtors are induced to make a promise of payment by instalment which they are simply unable to maintain because the level of each instalment is unreasonably high. It is time for warrant sales to cease, especially for a daily, essential water service.

Amendment No. 577 would adjust the Debtors (Scotland) Act 1987 in the interests of fairness and to take account of inflation. Uprating should have happened long 1808 ago. The matter is technical but I hope that the Minister will tackle it. The Debtors (Scotland) Act sets out rules for the use of earnings arrestments. The tables in the Act have not been uprated since it was passed seven years ago. The Act sets out the money that can be deducted each week from earnings of a specified amount. For example, a person who earns £100 a week will have £13 deducted each week until payment is completed. However, inflation has eroded the real value of money since 1987. Therefore, £100 in 1987 is worth only £85 or less nowadays. But the amounts deducted from earnings have not been reduced to take account of inflation. The amendment proposes that earnings should be uprated by inflation and contributions correspondingly reduced to take account of inflation.

The Government should provide new tables so that earnings figures are uprated. The tables could be added, by amendment, to the Debtors (Scotland) Act. Section 49(7) of the Act states:

"The Lord Advocate may by regulation vary tables a, b and c".

 It is not even necessary to deal with the matter by amendment.
