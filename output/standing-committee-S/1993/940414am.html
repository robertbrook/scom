Mr. Tam Dalyell
(Linlithgow)
: On a point of order, Sir James. Thirty years ago, in this very room, during a Committee debate on Scottish legislation, the late Tom Fraser would chide, Peggy Herbison would complain and Willie Ross would fulminate against the then Secretary of State Jack Maclay, for introducing Government amendments at a relatively late stage. Their fulmination was usually against a single sheet of paper with a few, for the most part, clear Government amendments.

To be fair, I admit that when the Labour Government came to power in 1964, Michael Noble and Pricilla Tweedsmuir did, in their various styles, exactly the same—but for a small number of amendments—and gained the sympathy of the then Chairmen, Betty Harvie Anderson and Jimmy McGuinness.

We now have not a sheet or two, but a whole raft of new Government amendments brought before us after 35 sittings of the Committee that have taken more than 100 hours of debate. Yah-boo apart, I feel that this is a disgrace to parliamentary procedure.

I did not give notice of this matter even to my hon. Friends, because until last night I had not checked the nature of the amendments. I find them not nearly as innocent as suggested. I am told by lawyers that they present extreme difficulties. I will give two examples, the first being in amendment no. 828, which states:

"(b) after subsection (1B) insert—

  • (1BA) The rateable value ascribed in the non-domestic rating list maintained under the Local Government Finance Act 1988 to a hereditament in England and Wales such as is mentioned in paragraph 2(1A) of Schedule 6 to that Act shall, for the purposes of subsections (1) and (1A) above, be treated as equal to the rent which, assuming such a letting of the hereditament as is required to be assumed for the purposes of subsection (1B) above, would reasonably be attributable to the non-domestic use of property."

    • Your powers of interpretation, Sir James, may be greater than those of distinguished Edinburgh lawyers. It is absurd to be landed with such amendments without having had time to examine them in detail.
