It is possible that the Government's proposal for boundaries could adversely affect the provision of services. In terms of its boundaries, Central regional council has a population of 272,000 people. Most of the people who live in Central region are concentrated in the south and south-eastern corner within a 10-mile radius of Stirling town centre. Eleven out of the 19 services that local government would have to provide under a unitary authority would have to be provided in the Stirling district area by a joint board or by a contract arrangement with an outside provider. We should consider joint boards in terms of education. I have taught children with special educational needs. The provision of special education was the Cinderella of the system, but, thank goodness, things have improved in the past 10 years. A larger authority, with a large resource base, can provide such a service to anyone and everyone. There is, in a sense, a cross-subsidy between those who do not require such a service and those who do. The smaller the authority, the less chance there is that it will have the flexibility and multiplicity of support services required for education. Educational psychology services, which are not necessarily simply available for special educational needs, are similarly provided. The same is true of many other services. Such provision would be seriously damaged if an authority was not big enough to provide it in a unitary fashion without having to put the services out to contract.

Mr. Gallie

As well as the people and the community councils, the business sector seems to favour the Clydesdale unitary authority. A group called Target Clydesdale, which is chaired by the chairman of Clydesdale Development company, who is a member of the Lanarkshire development agency, has come out in particular support of that suggestion. The group says that Clydesdale covers over half the landward area of the old county of Lanarkshire. It has a population of 58,650. That is seen by many as a weakness in the argument, but other aspects should be taken into account. The size of the population in south Lanarkshire represents JPEG 317 18.5 per cent. of the total population of 317,000. The rural area should be taken into account, with its 800 farms. There is real concern that the voices in the rural areas will be lost. The proposed south Lanarkshire authority would be comprised mainly of urban and industrialised areas and a principle concern expressed by the people of Clydesdale is that their voices and interests could well be lost.

Three specific areas must be considered when the proposal is scrutinised. First, there is a perceived lack of support for a south Lanarkshire council, from the local area. Various cocktails of other options have been offered in the south Lanarkshire area. I am somewhat surprised that none of those appears to have been put in an amendment.

Secondly, we must examine Clydesdale council's ability to deliver all local government services efficiently and effectively. Hon. Members have had recourse to the case presented by Clydesdale demonstrating just that. It acknowledges that there are only four secondary schools in the area, but points to the example of Strathclyde, which has adopted policies of devolving management to local schools. It sees in that the potential for a future education service run by Clydesdale local authority. For that reason I consider that the proposal falls largely into line with Government thinking on the devolved role of schools in their educational management.

In considering the services that would be required for what I acknowledge would be a relatively small local authority, we can perceive further convergence with Government policy. Clydesdale sees itself as both an enabling and a purchasing authority. To that end it will be able to draw upon reasonable pools of expertise from the surrounding areas.

Clydesdale points out that it has a well-managed and organised housing structure. It claims that for many years it has regretted the lack of social work provision. It could benefit greatly if its wish was granted. Clydesdale district council has already earned a reputation throughout Scotland for its approach to the management of its services. It is regularly quoted by the Accounts Commission as an example of best practice and value for money. Although a unitary Clydesdale council would not, of course, be the same authority, there is reasonable expectation that it would adopt the management style and methods of the district council.

Cost-effectiveness is the third element that I referred to in my opening remarks. Clydesdale certainly has a reputation for good value. Hon. Members should look at the policy and financial plan document which it put forward for 1995–96 where they will see good evidence of that.

The council's paper suggests that savings are to be made in the transition from the present dual authority to the single unitary authority. It envisages that £1.5 million a year could be saved. It sees additional savings from Hamilton and East Kilbride councils of some £4.25 million a year. That is very much in line with Government forecasts. On the basis of service provision, identity with the people and cost effectiveness I suggest to my hon. Friend that Clydesdale has put up a good case. I ask him to take those points on board.
