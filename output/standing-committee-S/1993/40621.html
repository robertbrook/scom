10.30 am

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Ian Lang)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered class XIV, votes 17 and 18.

 Before I begin, it is my very pleasant duty to welcome you, Mr. Maxton, to the Chair. It is a promotion to a pinnacle of political activity to which many of us have long looked forward. We congratulate you and wish you well in that position.

I am delighted to have the opportunity to talk about Scottish education, in particular about the Government's proposals set out in our White Paper "Higher Still: Opportunity for all". In Scotland, education rightly enjoys a special status. It is a key part of our national identity and we can be justifiably proud of having a system of education that can stand comparison with the best in the world. Nevertheless, there is no room for complacency and we must strive not only to maintain the quality of education in Scotland, but to improve it. That is the key aim of the proposals set out in "Higher Still" that I announced in March.

I shall focus on those proposals today, but before I do so I shall remind the Committee of our considerable achievements in education in recent years and of the events which have led to the reforms. Real progress has been made in education over the past 15 years and Conservative Members can be justifiably proud of our record. Standards in school education have improved enormously compared with when we took office in 1979. Let me draw the Committee's attention to some of the facts. Pupils are now gaining better qualifications than ever before. In 1991–92, the percentage of leavers obtaining at least one higher rose to 42 per cent—the highest level so far recorded and 10 percentage points higher than in 1981–82. At the other end of the scale, the percentage leaving with no qualifications in 1991–92 fell to under 10 per cent. for the first time compared with 28 per cent. a decade earlier. Staying-on rates in schools have also increased dramatically so that more than 73 per cent. of pupils now stay on into fifth year. The number staying on to S6 has also risen sharply and now stands at more than 37 per cent. More people go on to further and higher education than ever before and gain advanced qualifications.

Those significant advances have come about as a direct result of our education policies through the 1980s and 1990s. We have put in place a broad spectrum of exciting education initiatives over the past decade which demonstrate our determination to ensure that our young 2 people are equipped with the knowledge and skills they need to play their full part in the economy and in society. I shall briefly remind the Committee of a few of those initiatives by setting the scene for our "Higher Still" proposals.

For those in primary school and the early stages of secondary school, we have put in place the Government's five to 14 programme. For those in the 14 to 18 age group, we have developed standard grades and carried out revisions to Highers and to certificate of sixth year studies to make them consistent with standard grade. We have also introduced radical reform of vocational education based on the action plan of 1983 which introduced the national certificate, a qualification based on internally assessed modules. That modular structure has been extended to the higher national certificate and the higher national diploma and is the basis of the occupationally specific Scottish vocational qualification, which we introduced in 1989. we also announced the introduction of general Scottish vocational qualifications—gSVQs—in 1991, which is a broadly based vocational qualification designed to meet the needs of 16 to 19-year-olds at school or in further education. Of course there is also our skillseekers initiative, which gives choice and responsibility to young people to increase their motivation to train.

Finally, during the 1980s and early 1990s, higher education expanded extremely rapidly. The participation rates of young Scots in higher education now stands at a record level of 35 per cent.

Mr. Tam Dalyell
(Linlithgow)
: Would not it be graceful to pay tribute to the part played in all such achievements by the education authorities of Lothian and Strathclyde? Does the Secretary of State recollect that some of us sat through debates for 177 hours, mostly in this Room? Any detailed examination reveals that smaller education authorities, which do not have large resources, might not be able to keep up levels of provision similar to those of Lothian and Strathclyde. Should not the Secretary of State at least pay tribute to what has been done?

Mr. Lang
: I am happy to assure the hon. Gentleman that my courtesy extends far beyond Lothian and Strathclyde. Of course, I pay tribute not only to those education authorities but to all education authorities, the teaching profession and all those involved in seeking to enhance the quality and achievements in Scottish education, particularly the education authorities of Borders and Dumfries and Galloway, both of which have achieved higher educational standards than the substantially larger authorities of Lothian or Strathclyde.

Such major developments in education have meant that more people than ever before want to stay on at school and follow their aspirations to the highest level that they can before going on to further education, higher education or employment. Although the present system has many strengths, it has some significant weaknesses, to which I shall now turn.

Although we have been successful in persuading large numbers to stay on at school, many of those who do so JPEG 3 leave at the end of the fifth year with a mixture of Scottish Examination Board and Scottish Vocational Education Council qualifications that are not easily marketable. Students do not necessarily have an opportunity at school to acquire vocationally relevant skills and knowledge, nor to develop the core skills that are so important in future life, especially in employment.

Although Scotland prides itself on having a broad curriculum, it is not reflected in the achievements of students after the age of 16. Only 20 per cent. of fifth-year students gain four highers or more and over 50 per cent. gain no more than one higher. Such concerns were highlighted in representations from the Scottish Examination Board in 1989. Our response to those representations and to the growing opinion in Scotland that it was time to review the arrangements for courses and awards in the later years of secondary education was to appoint a committee under the chairmanship of Professor John Howie to review curriculum and examinations in the fifth and sixth years of secondary education.

In March 1992, the Committee published its comprehensive and far-reaching report which stimulated a healthy and penetrating debate about Scottish education. There was no serious challenge to the committee's concise critique of the present system. The main weaknesses that the committee identified were: the dash and drift problem in which the pace and progress expected of pupils in S5 was often too great and, by contrast, the programme in S6 was undemanding for some; examination blight in which the business of taking examinations dominated school education each May and June and was preceded by prelims which took up valuable teaching time and broke up the continuity of coherence of learning; and a lack of proper vocational education such that, while arrangements in schools had improved students could still end up with haphazard collections of national certificate modules and, moreover, such qualifications did not enjoy the same status as highers.

In its evaluation of present arrangements, the committee concluded that the status quo was not an option if Scotland were to have an education system worthy of an advanced society in the year 2000. it proposed radical changes, including two distinct types of group awards: the Scottish certificate—SCOTCERT—which would have a vocational slant and a heavy emphasis on internal assessment; and the Scottish baccalaureate, which would be academically baised and the normal route to higher education. The committee also proposed to move standard grade forward to S3.

We consulted on the report and over 300 responses were received, covering a wide spectrum of interests. I appreciate the thoughtful and constructive criticisms and contributions made by respondents. One of most cogent responses to the Howie report came from the Educational Institute of Scotland. Until now, the EIS has made a distinguished contribution to the consideration of policy on upper secondary education in Scotland. For example, the 1990 annual general meeting of the institute approved as policy that there should be an examination structure in upper secondary schools that would 4

"allow pupils the optimum choice, which would encourage young people to enter further and higher education and which would provide a coherent educational experience for all pupils."

 It is little wonder, therefore, that the EIS welcomed the establishment of the Howie committee. In its response to the Howie report, the EIS summed up the position rather well. It said:

"There is an emerging consensus that the status quo is not an option for the longer term."

 However, the institute expressed concerns about the Howie proposals. It opposed the proposal to move standard grade from S4; it opposed the introduction of a group certificate; and it opposed so-called twin-tracking. On each of those three points, the EIS and the Government are at one.

It is difficult to identify any substantial differences between the EIS's proposals for the reform of upper secondary education and the Government's. The institute's deputy general secretary acknowledged that when he said that the package of proposals is:

"quite a good scenario educationally. It meets a number of things we are asking for."

 In the light of all that, to say that my hon. Friend the Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland, the Member for Edinburgh, West (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton), and I are a little perplexed at the institute's behaviour since our proposals were published is to put it mildly.

Having given the background, I shall now turn to the Government's decisions on the way forward. I shall begin by saying that, in reaching the decisions that we have announced, my colleagues and I took careful account of respondents' concerns. The decisions show the Government's responsiveness to the issues raised in consultation. In particular, we were impressed by the need to build on the strengths of the existing arrangements; to provide demanding but attainable standards for all pupils; to encourage pupils to achieve breadth as well as depth in their studies; to ensure that vocational qualifications are properly regarded as being of comparable value to academic qualifications; and for change to be evolutionary. I believe that those aims can best be achieved through the package of reforms that I announced earlier this year, which are detailed in our White Paper.

In January we announced that we had decided not to accept the committee's recommendation that standard grade should be moved from S4 to S3, so standard grade courses remain in the third and fourth year of secondary education as is the case at present. I believe that that is the right decision on educational grounds.

In the fifth and sixth year of secondary education courses of the Scottish Examination Board and the Scottish Vocational Education Council will be brought into a unified curriculum and assessment system. That will be a major step towards parity of esteem between vocational and academic qualifications. We shall also develop new curriculum guidelines covering breadth, coherence, progression and core skills.

JPEG 5 Post-16 courses will have better continuity and progression. All students should be able to continue studying at a level that is neither too easy nor too demanding for them. Courses will be available at five levels: foundation, general and credit, which correspond to the equivalent levels at standard grade; and higher and new advanced higher. Students will be able to take whatever mix of academic and vocational courses is most appropriate to their needs and gain marketable qualifications in the form of national certificates, highers and advanded highers—all leading to acceptable entrance qualifications for higher education.

The highers will remain, but with modifications. Courses will be drawn from current SEB and SCOTVEC provision, so that students are able to study vocational as well as academic courses at demanding levels. The recommended study time for each higher will be extended from 120 hours to 160 hours. The higher courses will be made up of modules with a mixture of internal and external assessment, but external assessment will remain the main method of assessment for full higher and advanced higher courses.

There will be a new level of qualification of advanced higher to set a clear and demanding benchmark for the most able students. It will build on highers to provide coherent two-year courses in S5 and S6, which promote high levels of attainment. It will be broadly equivalent to the level of the certificate of sixth-year studies and will incorporate the content of current CSYS courses, as appropriate.

Candidates who continue to the most advanced level of study will be encouraged to bypass the external assessments at higher level. That will reduce the time spent on examination and enable higher standards to be achieved. All students who are capable of advanced higher will be encouraged to take that route. Students who do not wish to undertake more advanced levels of studies will be able to leave after highers at S5 or do their highers over a two-year period. Other courses, based mainly on existing national certificate modules, will continue to be offered to meet the needs of students for whom highers are too immediately demanding. Some students will take combinations of courses that meet the requirements for the general Scottish vocational qualifications.

Although, as I have said, standard grade will remain in S4, we are taking some steps to encourage the most able pupils to achieve their full potential at that stage. Thus we shall relax the present stage restriction to allow pupils capable of taking standard grade at credit level in a subject at the end of S3 to do so. We shall also allow progression to higher work in S4 by making available and accrediting units of study at that level. In addition, to provide progression for the most able pupils at the early stage of secondary education, a new level F will be introduced into the five to 14 curriculum structure.

Mr. Lang

The national record of achievement, which we launched in 1991, will play an important part under the new arrangements. It is designed to present a simple record of an individual's achievement through education, training and working life. It encourages people to take an active part in their self-development and could help to identify training needs. It also gives employers a convenient and comprehensive record of what someone can do and of their formal qualifications. All courses passed under the new arrangements will be presented on a single certificate and achievement in the new awards will be recorded by students in their national record of achievement folders.

The reforms will provide more demanding targets for able students but will not sacrifice the potential for breadth which has characterised Scottish education. By extending the recommended study time, it is expected that many students who would previously have narrowly failed, will have real prospects of success, while others can expect to earn better grades. The inclusion of vocationally orientated courses at higher and advanced higher level should help to ensure that the curriculum is relevant to a broader range of students. It should also dispel any lingering association of vocational education with lower level education.

The reforms will build on the coherent curriculum and assessment framework that has already been put in place JPEG 7 for five to 14-year-olds. The new framework addresses the weaknesses identified in the Howie committee's review of the curriculum and examinations in the fifth and sixth years and also responds to the concerns of consultees.

We have taken particular account of the current concerns about teachers' workload. We have designed the new arrangements so that they are developed from what teachers are currently doing. Teaching methods will not change and there will be as little change as possible to the content of what is being taught.

We shall continue to react to a changing situation—which is a problem that arises from our success—and to attribute and accord resources to take account of such developments.

Our proposals on "Higher Still" have been warmly welcomed by the public and professionals. There have also been a few negative and ill-founded criticisms from some 8 commentators. Some have criticised the measures as creating a fast track. That is not the case, nor is it a term that we have used. I stress that the new arrangements are designed to provide opportunities for all to gain the best qualifications of which they are capable. The task for schools is to try to make arrangements to enable pupils to progress at the fastest pace at which they are individually capable. The proposals provide a framework to help schools meet the needs of all pupils, including those who can attain beyond credit level in some subjects by the end of S4.

Some commentators have also criticised the time scale for implementation. It is more important to get it right than get it quickly. However, in the interests of pupils, it should be as quick as possible. The implementation date of 1997 to 1998 was selected on two grounds. First, given the universal acceptance that the status quo was not an option, it was important that we should not delay in introducing the desired changes. Secondly, as I have just said, the new framework will be based firmly on existing courses In that sense it will be different from the earlier standard grade development programme or the action plan. Indeed, it will build on them.

The EIS has made a fuss about the proposals. I am slightly puzzled by this since the "Higher Still" proposals appear to match closely what the EIS wanted. I could quote many examples of the Howie committee's findings.

Nevertheless, as hon. Members will know, the EIS has recently conducted a ballot of its members apparently aimed at boycotting involvement in implementation of "Higher Still" and other related developments. What that organisation thinks that that action will do to its reputation as a body that advances the professional standing of education, I do not know. That is an unfortunate approach to take on educational matters that will improve considerably the educational and ultimately employment attainments of our young people. It seems very shortsighted of the EIS to decline involvement in the devlopment process, particularly if it wants to help ensure that the arrangements for delivery of "Higher Still" avoid undue pressures on teachers.

I do not dismiss or underestimate the concerns of many teachers about the stresses and strains of school education. As i have already said, we have taken careful account of the implications of the proposals for teachers' work load and I assure the Committee that there will be no impact on teachers in the classroom for at least a year.
