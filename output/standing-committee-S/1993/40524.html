The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Ian Lang)

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of law and order in Scotland.

I welcome the opportunity to have this debate on the subject of law and order. It is an important issue and one that has been at the forefront of the Government's mind, particularly over the past year. It is a matter that concerns and affects us all.

Incidentally, I have no idea who is manning the camera, Mr. Hogg, but I hope that the Opposition will not feel inhibited from participating in the debate for fear of crossing a telecommunications picket line.

It is almost exactly a year since this Committee debated crime. On that occasion, I was able to report that the figures for reported crime showed a small drop in 1992 compared with the previous year. I am happy to say that I can again report to the Committee a decrease in the levels of crime.

The news over the past two years has been very good. In 1992, the fall mainly reflected a reduction in housebreaking and theft. In 1993, there were substantial falls in almost all categories of recorded crime. Serious assaults fell by IS per cent. and robbery by 8 per cent.—after rises respectively of 11 per cent. and 10 per cent. the year before. In addition—this will especially please my hon. Friend the Member for Ayr (Mr. Gallie) and, indeed the whole Committee—the handling of offensive weapons fell by 21 per cent., after a 6 per cent. rise the year before.

Of course, we cannot be complacent—and we shall not be. Statistics can tell only part of the story and must be treated with caution. But the information that we have entitles us to conclude that the policies of the Government and the work of the police and other agencies in partnership in preventing crime in Scotland are achieving success. For that to be maintained, we must continue to increase our efforts to involve the community as a whole in preventing and dealing with crime.

The people of Scotland want to see more police on the streets—and so do the Government. That is why we included in the justice charter specific reference to foot patrols. We are determined that as many police officers as possible will be available for operational duties, not sitting behind desks. We have increased total police strength by no fewer than 1,400 officers since 1979, and total expenditure has increased in real terms by no less than 55 per cent. I think that more can still be done.

Jobs which do not require the powers or experience of a police constable ought to be done by civilians. During this year and last, recruitment of additional civilians should release more than 350 uniformed officers for operational duty. The welcome, if rather belated, decision by Strathclyde regional council to allow Strathclyde police to recruit up to authorised establishment will provide a further 160 officers. That amounts to more than 500 extra police officers on operational duty in two years. That is a substantial increase which ought to contribute significantly to upholding law and order.

Crime figures are down and police numbers are up, but I am not content to let the matter rest there. I have a number of policies, some already in practice and others in planning, which will further strengthen the battle against crime.

I should like to spend some time on the important subject of young offenders. What surprises and saddens me more than anything is the way that these young people are often presented as a type of homogeneous group. They may all be offenders, they may all commit criminal or anti-social acts, but they are individuals, and an effective response to the difficulties that they create, and to their personal needs, must recognise that fact. There are no simple answers, but we need better ones.

The age of the youngsters and the types of offence that they commit vary tremendously but, in broad terms, children's hearings have to consider four different categories: first-time offenders, periodic offenders, serious offenders and persistent offenders. In the final category, a small number of youngsters account for an inordinate number of incidents of crime. My right hon. and learned Friend the Minister of State recently announced a development project designed to create intensive progammes of intervention with persistent young offenders between the ages of 12 and 15 years.

JPEG 3 That innovative initiative will take the form of a project carried out by a major voluntary organisation in collaboration with local authorities. The project will offer programmes not currently available, to meet a diverse range of need and problematic behaviour in young offenders. Programmes will be tailored to individuals. They will be designed to confront young offenders with the consequences of their actions. Their aim will be to tackle behavioural and underlying problems, with a view to reducing offending. This will not be a soft option. For the young person, attendance will not be optional: the programme will be rigorous and demanding. For young offenders aged 16 and over, our objective is to provide and develop similarly effective penalties and interventions to help tackle criminal behaviour and break the cycle of offending.

It also said that it is a worldwide problem and that it has been described by the secretary general of Interpol as

Such a diverse problem must be met with a comprehensive response that aims not only to curb the supply of drugs, but also to create a climate in which the demand for them is reduced. It is on those twin principles that our strategy has evolved over the years.

I welcome the Select Committee's report of its inquiry into drug abuse and I congratulate the hon. Member for Kilmarnock and Loudoun (Mr. McKelvey) and his colleagues on the Committee on producing such a constructive, thought-provoking and timely report. The Select Committee, like many commentators before it, found that the problem of drug misuse in Scotland was

"larger, more serious and more complex than we at first imagined",

I echo that sentiment. Just as there is no single reason for people taking drugs in the first place, so there is no absolute panacea. I am grateful, therefore, for the Committee's support for the current Scottish Office multi-agency approach to drug misuse. There is already a substantial infrastructure in place. Dedicated police drug squads relentlessly pursue the traffickers; tough sentences are available to the courts to punish the dealers; powers to confiscate ill-gotten gains have been taken; an increasing range of options and facilities are available for the treatment and care of misusers; and prevention activity and education in schools and more widely continue unabated. We shall not relax our efforts on any of those fronts.

I was heartened by the Select Committee's strenuous rebuttal of any policy that encourages the use of a drug such as cannabis. Legislation is simply not the answer. I have no hesitation in reaffirming that decriminalisation is not on our agenda. That would be a counsel of despair.

The Committee rightly stressed the need for effective co-ordination. That is an issue that the drugs task force, under my right hon. and learned friend the Minister of State, is addressing The task force is making good progress and is on schedule to produce its report later in the summer. That report will take account of the Select Committee's recommendations and, in addition to co-ordination, will focus on prevention and the provision of services. I have no doubt that both reports will contribute substantially to the future direction of our response to drug misuse in Scotland.

Mr. Lang
: The hon. Member for Cunninghame, South (Mr. Donohoe) misunderstands what has happened in recent years. There has been a succession of initiatives, and 8 I have listed various things that have been done, including tightening sentencing and strengthening the commitment to tackle drug trafficking. We have done that against a worldwide tide of increased drug offences. We are not an isolated country with an isolated problem, but we have a serious problem, which the Select Committee on Scottish Affairs and criminal statistics have identified. We must continue to consider new initiatives and the co-ordination of resources and effort in a way that will succeed.

We have dramatically turned the tide on non-drug-related crime, as the figures show. I said that figures should be treated with caution and we shall continue to do that. Nevertheless, two years in succession have shown not successive increases, as has been the case for years gone past, but successive and accelerating reductions. Within those overall figures, there is a problem with drug-related crime, which we are now tackling. We are building on the measures that we have already taken and that have effect.

I should like to say a few words about our approach in dealing with the perpetrators of crime. The phrase "making the punishement fit the crime" may be a cliché, but it encapsulates a vital truth. There is room for a variety of approaches to the treatment of offenders. Many of the ways that we have developed for dealing with offenders have been approached through social work services. We have achieved much through allocating substantially increased resources to the provision of such services in the criminal justice system.

This morning, I wish to concentrate my remarks on prisons because for many, prisons are the only effective sanctions. The current prison population is 5,500, which is about 600 more than two years ago. Would that prisons were not necessary, but imprisonment remains an essential part of the fight against crime. It is the appropriate punishment for the serious and persistent offender. Ensuring the secure custody of prisoners is the prime task of the Scottish prison service. The victims of serious crime deserve no less.

For prisons to be effective, levels of security should be appropriate. The criterion of the revised security classification system, introduced a year ago, is the level of dangerousness to the public. The Scottish prison service is committed to keeping good order in prisons. That is no easy task when it works with such a volatile population. Most prisoners will return, at some point, to the community. They are sent to prison as a punishment, which is right, but prisons also have a role in helping prisoners towards reintegregation into society. That requires a balanced approach; it means maintaining security while treating with fairness and humanity. It means demonstrating respect for prisoners' rights, even when they may have shown little regard for the rights of others. It means providing work, as well as opportunities to address their offending behaviour.

Much is now being achieved. Under the sentence planning scheme, which began in 1992, prisoners are encouraged to address their offending behaviour. Individual establishments provide a different range of counselling and training opportunities. For example, there is an addiction counselling and information unit at Barlinnie prison for prisoners with problems with alcohol, drugs, gambling and solvent abuse. It is run with the active involvement of organisations involved in addiction counselling in the community.

JPEG 9 Last year, we took the opportunity in the Prisoners and Criminal Proceedings (Scotland) Act 1993 to reform the parole system, thereby taking forward our response to the report of the committee chaired by Lord Kincraig entitled "Review of Parole and related matters in Scotland." On publication, the report was generally welcomed and it has been valuable in establishing principles that would improve the fairness and effectiveness of the parole system. Most importantly, it will restore meaning to the full sentence of the court.

Although the measures did not significantly affect the structures of penalties available to the court, they were designed to lead to greater fairness, consistency and openness in the administration of custodial sentences. As an extension of that fairness and openness, and as foreshadowed in our response to the Kincraig report, I have stopped applying a presumption against parole in any particular category of case. The restrictive policy announced in December 1984 for those serving sentences of five years or more for offences of violence or drug trafficking, therefore, no longer applies and the parole board considers such cases on the same basis as others.

10 As the Under-Secretary of State for Scotland, my hon. Friend the Member for Edinburgh, West (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton), and I made clear when we last debated the subject of crime in Committee, if we are to prevent and reduce crime instead of just dealing with its aftermath, we must take a broad, multidisciplinary approach that goes to the roots of offending behaviour and involves the whole community. I should like to conclude by expanding briefly on that approach.

I start with the perhaps obvious observation that a criminal act normally begins with the perpetrator's decision to break the law. That decision itself stems from a selfish disregard for the rights of others and the norms of acceptable behaviour.

The key question before us is how to stop people deciding to break the law. That in turn leads to the question of how to enhance people's respect for others and for the norms of acceptable behaviour. Some would say that deterrence has a role to play, and I agree. The prospect of apprehension and punishment can give a would-be criminal pause for thought. That is one of the reasons why we strengthened the deterrent mechanism in October last year by enabling the Lord-Advocate to appeal against unduly lenient sentences in serious cases. No one, I believe, would argue that deterrence is the whole answer. It is not fear of the consequences that keeps most people on the straight and narrow, but an appreciation of right and wrong. We need to find ways in which to strengthen that appreciation.

In a democratic society, there are, quite rightly, limits on what Governments can do to influence the values and attitudes of their people. That is why, to a great extent, we must rely on others—on parents, teachers and community leaders—to promote values that emphasise respect for others and respect for the law. One of the key goals of the Crime Prevention Council, the membership of which comes from a variety of backgrounds, is to identify ways in which to involve others in that task.

The Government also have a role to play. The curriculum guidelines issued last year to schools provide one example of how the Government can encourage the promotion of positive values. I believe that other social and economic policies can also make a contribution. That is why my ministerial colleagues in the Scottish Office and I met periodically to review the contribution that each of our policies can make to the fight against crime. That is why that contribution will be one of the criteria against which major policy initiatives will be evaluated.

We must ensure that the public retain confidence in the criminal justice system. That is why we have continued to ensure that police forces are adequately resourced to meet the demands that they face. That is why we have undertaken a complete review of the delivery of justice in Scotland, to identify how it may be improved in dealing with not only those accused of crime, but others—whether victims, witnesses or members of juries—who may find themselves involved through civic duty. That is why, more than two years ago, we published the justice charter with the aim of raising the standard of service throughout Scotland.

I regret that I have taken so long to make my speech this morning, but I am sure that the Committee will recognise both that I have given way frequently and that there has been a broad range of serious issues to cover.
