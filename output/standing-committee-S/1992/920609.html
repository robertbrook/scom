Draft Education (National Curriculum) (Foundation Subjects at Key Stage 4) Order 1992

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Education (Mr. Eric Forth)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Education (National Curriculum) (Foundation Subjects at Key Stage 4) Order 1992.

 The draft order would amend section 3(2) of the Education Reform Act 1988 by removing art and music from the list of subjects that 14 to 16-year-olds are compelled to study under the national curriculum. It is the last of a series of measures introduced to increase flexibility and choice in the curriculum for pupils aged 14 to 16. Those measures reflect concern about the pressures on the school timetable for that age group if there is to be room for specialisation, for the study of a wider range of academic subjects—for example, the classics or a second modern language—or for vocational study.

It is right to require pupils up to the age of 14 to study all the subjects of the national curriculum. We need to ensure that our children receive a rigorous foundation in each subject of the national curriculum, including art and music. However, as pupils grow older, they and their parents and teachers begin to look at what lies beyond compulsory schooling, whether in work or in further study. They need to be able to make choices that will help them to progress towards their future responsibilities.

Introducing flexibility is not a retreat from the principles of the national curriculum. In the last two years of compulsory education, we can offer room for specialisation and differentiation, and should do so. We therefore propose that the legal requirement for pupils aged 14 to 16 to study art and music should be lifted. I commend the draft order to the Committee.

Mr. Derek Fatchett
(Leeds, Central)
: I welcome the Minister to his new appointment and am glad of the opportunity to debate education with him not only in Committee but on the Floor of the House.

I am not being critical when I say that the Minister is new to the education debate; it is simply a statement of fact. Since he has taken over as Under-Secretary of State for Education, he has doubtless had the opportunity to read the Hansard reports of the Standing Committee's work what was then the Education Reform Bill. I am sure that he would have enjoyed every moment. However, he would also have seen that his argument was put then by the Opposition. It is always a pleasure to see the Minister support Labour party policy. It may be rare, but we welcome it and hope that there will be many similar occasions. I realise that his post is not the subject of election within the Conservative party, so I can sing his praises without damning any possibility of his being elected to a 4 higher position. My inside information suggests that that would in any case be difficult, but if the need arises I shall do my best for him as campaign manager.

I welcome the Minister's conversion to Labour party policy, but it raises several interesting points. It is worth doing more than the Minister has done: We should talk about the success of 14 to 16-year-olds in the arts at key stage 4 and the growth in the number of youngsters taking GCSE arts subjects. The interest shown by youngsters in the performing arts, such as drama and dance, has grown substantially, as has the number of GCSE and post-GCSE examination successes. We should welcome that because the arts make a distinctive contribution to school life. I realise that the Minister is not saying that children should not have that aesthetic experience. However, it is worth remembering that Her Majesty's inspectorate and the Gulbenkian report recognise that the arts can help individuals across a range of experiences. For instance, many youngsters have developed self-confidence and an ability to express themselves as a result of studying drama and the other performing arts. We must not lose that range of experiences.

When the Minister comes to wind up this possibly short debate, he will, I know, want to join me in congratulating those who have been involved in the delivery of the arts in our schools on their success and on the way in which those subjects have played a substantial role in the development of the school curriculum.

The Minister also needs to clarify certain points that are central to the Government's policy. If he had read the comments that we made during consideration of the Education Reform Bill, he would know that the Labour party argued for greater flexibility at key stage 4. The Government have now taken that on board, but the problem with converts is that they come late to a viewpoint and fail to understand it in its entirety. That is the difficulty for the Minister and the Government.

We should not consider the curriculum at key stage 4 piecemeal. The Government need to ask the National Curriculum and the School Examinations and Assessment Council to examine how key stage 4 is working and to consider its integration into other parts of the national curriculum and into post-16 education. The Government cannot have flexibility on some subjects and then try to pretend that that does not relate to the curriculum as a whole. What we need—and what the Government fail to do in this draft order—is to examine the whole of the post-14 curriculum.

Can the Minister give a commitment that at some stage the Government will produce a draft order which deals with the whole of key stage 4? We could debate such an order and consider how to make progress. All the three major political parties now agree—uniquely, perhaps, on educational issues—on this point. We need flexibility, but the way in which the Government are going about it is likely to cause problems for schools. There is a danger, for instance, that the flexibility provided for in this draft order will result in the arts being relegated to a second-class subject area. That may not be best for individual youngsters or for the development of expertise in particular schools.

We need a debate and perhaps a draft order so that we can examine the whole of key stage 4.

Ms. Mildred Gordon
(Bow and Poplar)
: While I believe that 14-year-old should have flexibility of choice, I should be very worried if music and art education were lost in the process.

I should like to indulge in some personal reminiscences, which I believe are valid. When I was a child, my mother was very poor and could not afford the fare or the uniform to send me to any of the exclusive schools that were on offer to junior county scholars, so she sent me to the local grammar school. As it wanted to do its best for east end kids, it pushed us through as many academic subjects as possible for matriculation, to the extent that we learnt scripture by reading the Bible in French to enable us to have extra time studying French. Every pupil at that school dropped music, art and similar subjects at the age of 14. The message was that those subjects were not as important as academic subjects and that we did not have time for frills, which is what they were considered to be.

I had been told that I was tone deaf, so I was glad to be relieved of the humiliation of music lessons. If I had been asked, I would have said that I did not want to study music any more, but, some years later, when I met girls from the more exclusive schools, I began to realise how deprived I was and how much better was their education. They had wider cultural background and a wider understanding of the arts and some knowledge of a musical instrument stays with us all out lives, while the Pythagoras therorem and things of that ilk are forgotten. In the long term, music and art are among the most important things we learn. Therefore, if the curriculum is to be flexible, it must be balanced by strong guidance from the Government to schools so that the curriculum is not entirely academic and subjects such as art and music are not dropped because they are considered to be frills. I know that in primary and junior schools music permeates the day in many ways, but that is not the case in secondary schools. If those subjects do not form part of the curriculum, there is a danger that they will be dropped entirely after the age of 14. I should hate to see that happen. It would be a severe loss to pupils.

Mr. Forth
: Immediately may I thank the hon. Member for Leeds Central for his kind words. I welcome his welcome for my welcome for his welcome for whatever has happened over the past few months. 10 Let me say in passing that I think that the recent election is over and that the result has been decided. I hope that members of the Committee saw the rather attractive picture of me in The Times today, portraying that visit. During that visit, I went into a classroom where music was being taught. I saw how that enthusiasm was communicated to a group of seven-year-olds. They were at the early stage of developing an appreciation which I am sure we all hope will grow. Our view—which, plainly, is shared—is that it is right that such appreciation should develop and grow up to the age of 14 and it is thus defined in the national curriculum. By then, most youngsters can begin to understand—guided by teachers, parents and others—the subjects which they may or may not wish to study in more detail.

As art and music, for example, are included in the national curriculum's subjects up to the age of 14, many young people will want to continue them to GCSE and beyond. That is good; it is their choice. Therefore they determine what they want from education and from which subjects they will derive most benefit. However, to go on from that and from the limited matters covered by the order and say that we should embark on a general review by the National Curriculum Council or the School Examinations and Assessment Council of key stage 4 as a whole—as the hon. Member for Leeds Central and, I believe, the hon. Member for Bath (Mr. Foster) suggested—is not necessarily right at this time.

I have been struck by the extent to which people in education say, "We have had so many upheavals and changes that we need a period of consolidation and stability." Invariably I ask them, "You do not mean, do you, that there should be no changes? You are not suggesting that we freeze everything as it is in every respect, are you?" They answer, "No, we do not mean that. There should be some adjustments, fine tuning or changes", which I believe this order would bring about. However, we must keep in perspective the yearning among educationists for a period of stability and predictability, one in which all those involved can come fully to terms with the curriculum and with testing, to the benefit of the children. That, I believe, should be the priority. Therefore, I tend to resist calls, no matter how attractively expressed, for widespread reviews at this stage. I doubt whether calls for a review would be widely echoed in education. Some people may believe, and legitimately so, that changes of detail can be made. This order is perhaps a good example of that. But please, at this stage, let us not have widespread reviews or more fundamental upheavals. I do not believe that that is what people want.

Mr. Forth
: Certainly. That is an important question. The answer is fairly simple. The National Curriculum Council has its tasks to perform. Soon, we shall have the new inspectorate to which we shall look to provide us with much information about how schools perform and what is happening in them. In that respect, we shall define its remit fairly broadly. We are also putting in place the increasingly broad reporting systems, which may not be universally welcome but which should help all those who want more 12 information about what is happening in our schools, and how they are performing and achieving. Over the next two or three years, that will provide us with a much clearer picture. We shall know what is successful and what is unsuccessful. That process will give us the opportunity to make necessary changes.

I do not deny that changes will have to be made—I am sure that members of the Committee will agree with that—but we shall soon have in place a sophisticated series of mechanisms whereby better and more sharply defined information will be available, on which we can base the decisions that will need to be made to develop education within the broad framework that we have defined. I hope that I shall not need to say much more. I am pleased that on this occasion, my first opportunity to appear in Committee in connecton with my new responsibilities, I have been able to operate against a background of such commonality of approach and harmony.
