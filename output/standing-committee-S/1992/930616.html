4.30 pm

The Chairman: I must advise the Committee that I shall vigorously operate Standing Order No. 101, which states:

"Any Member, not being a Member of the Standing Committee, may take part in the deliberations of the Committee, but shall not vote or make any motion or be counted in the quorum."

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Michael Jack): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Fishing Vessels (Safety Improvements) (Grants) Scheme 1993 (S.I., 1993, No. 1325)

I am delighted to be under your chairmanship, Dr. Blackburn, for my first parliamentary outing on fishery matters since I joined the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food.

I should like to say at the outset that there are, of course, wider fishing issues, which are, I know, still the subject of lively discussion throughout the industry. The new Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food and I have made it clear that we shall listen very carefully to the views of all concerned. To that end, I shall, for example, be going to Brixham tomorrow to meet fishermen where, no doubt, they will be talking about the order, as well as other matters.

The order is about vessel safety. I would at the outset acknowledge that fishing is a hazardous occupation. Indeed, that is not surprising when one considers the uniquely difficult and sometimes unpredictable working conditions in which vessels have to operate. That is why I am glad to introduce the order because, over the next three years, we anticipate spending nearly £4 million on the safety grants, together with just over £1 million on residual grant payments for vessel modernisation.

I am conscious that, since the end of the last scheme, work on vessel safety has continued and I would like to reassure members of the Committee that we have made provision in this order to pay grant to those who carried out eligible works since the last scheme expired at the end of 1991. The Sea Fish Industry Authority, whom the Government pay to administer the scheme, has received some 400 applications for grant, which it has held back pending the laying of this order. I understand that the authority hopes to process all those applications by the end of June. Furthermore, any one who carried out such works since the last scheme expired but who did not submit an application for grant can apply retrospectively, provided the application reaches the Sea Fish Industry Authority by the end of this month.

The scheme we have brought forward provides grants for equipment which is necessary in order to obtain a Department of Transport safety certificate under the Fishing Vessel (Safety Provisions) Rules 1975. The 1975 rules, as amended, have formed the keystone of the Col. 4 Department of Transport fishing vessel safety policy for the past 18 years. The rules set out detailed requirements for the construction, equipment and operation of United Kingdom fishing vessels with a registered length of 12 metres and over and provided for the four-yearly survey and certification of such vessels.

As under the previous scheme—the Fishing Vessel (Acquisition and Improvement) (Grants) (Amendment) Scheme 1990—grant will be linked to the requirements of the Department of Transport safety certificate for boats of 12 metres and over. Without that, such vessels would be unable to go to sea. Grant rates will also remain unchanged. Vessels between 12 metres and 33 metres will qualify for aid at 30 per cent, while vessels over 33 metres will receive 10 per cent. Eligible works will, for example, cover the fitting of electronic position indicator beacons, which broadcast the location of a vessel to the search and rescue authorities if the vessel were to get into serious trouble. Other items that can be included are life-rafts, hand-held VHF radios, flares, rockets and life-jackets, plus any other works which Department of Transport surveyors consider necessary for a safety certificate to be issued.

The safety improvement grant scheme will provide aid for essential safety works until the end of 1996. It shows our commitment to facilitate safe operating conditions for the industry and I commend it to the Committee.

There is still considerable concern about the need to maintain an adequate distance between the massive nuclear submarines, which come in and out of the firth of Clyde, and our small, vulnerable fishing vessels.

The Committee might wonder what that issue has to do with safety provision, but it raises an important question. Would the provisions recommended by the sheriff at the fatal accident inquiry in Paisley be eligible for grant applications under the terms of the order? I am talking about small fishing vessels owned by share fishermen. If safety measures are required of fishermen to ensure that Image Col. 5 adequate warning signals are given to approaching submarines, safety instruments should be eligible for that grant.

The fatal accident inquiry resulted in a court martial which, in my opinion and from my experience as a member of a fishing family, reprimanded the wrong officers. It is the captain of a vessel who is responsible for what happens when he is aboard. In that case, however, the men who were in training were punished. I have always thought that that was wrong.

This matter is important because the United States Navy nuclear submarine, the Sturgeon, almost sank the fishing vessel Lupina in the Irish sea on 23rd September 1992. The United States Navy admitted responsibility for that near-tragic event through a press release but there is still considerable disquiet—as I am sure that the hon. Member for Argyll and Bute and my hon. Friend for Western Isles (Mr. Macdonald) will acknowledge—about keeping such submarines well away from fishing vessels in traditional fishing grounds.

The Minister said that the industry is dangerous—he is absolutely right. The statistics for fatalities and serious injuries exceed those of even the mining industry. European Standing Committee A, which on Wednesday 24th February 1993 and at which the hon. Member for Derbyshire, South (Mrs. Currie) was present, heard the Minister for Roads and Traffic state, among other things:

"Statistics collected by the Department of Transport's marine accident investigation branch show that of a work force of about 22,500, 30 fishermen died and 128 were reported injured in 1990, and 32 died and 165 were reported injured in 1991. The provisional figures for 1992 show that 22 fishermen died and 142 were reported injured."—[Official Report, European Standing Committee A, 24th February 1993; Column 3.]

It goes without saying that there is complete agreement between all members of the Committee that we must reduce those terrible statistics. That is why I offer a qualified welcome to the Order.

I want to ask a couple of questions which have been brought to my attention by Robert Allan who is the Chief Executive of the Scottish Fisherman's Federation and by Richard Banks of the National Federation of Fishermen's Organisations in England. Those gentlemen and I want to know why the order applies only to vessels of 12 metres or more between perpendiculars? That is a reasonable question given that licensing and Department of Transport safety requirements apply to all United Kingdom registered vessels. My hon. Friends from the west coast of Scotland and from the Western Isles will confirm—as is surely the case for the south coast of England—that many fisherman fish on vessels of less than 12 metres. Your own experience will tell you that, Dr. Blackburn.

Fishermen who use static gear—our lobster and crab men—fish on vessels less than 12 metres long. Why should they be excluded? My hon. Friend the Member for Western Isles will acknowledge that the little Minch can be a dangerous sea to fish as can the approaches to Loch Ryan or to the Firth of Clyde. That must surely be the case for fishermen in the English Channel too. Why should those men on small vessels, whose work is just as dangerous as that carried out on a freezer trawler, for example, on the river Humber—and I speak with a little experience as my brother Leslie is a mate on such a trawler and is fishing off Greenland at the moment—be excluded from the measures?

Col. 6 I hope that Conservative Members are smiling in sympathy because there is an inadequacy in the order and I hope that the Minister will re-examine it in terms of all fishermen on small craft, especially those who use static gear. We often hear of such men working the boats alone or with a two-man crew being knocked over the side. The Minister must change the order. I also want to know whether the scheme can be extended to comply with regulations laid down under European Community hygiene regulations. There is a gap there, as there are gaps elsewhere.

The statutory instrument refers directly to the Fishing Vessel (Safety Provisions) Rules 1975, which the Minister mentioned. Those regulations run to 150 pages—and so they should, because they must cover everything from the construction of the hull to life rafts and other emergency safety equipment. The Minister may not be familiar with those provisions in detail, but I am sure that he will familiarise himself soon. I refer to part III, which concerns rules for life-saving appliances. I have already asked whether fishermen could apply for grants to purchase provisions that were recommended by the sheriff principal of the fatal accident inquiry into the sinking of the multi-fishing vessel the Antares. I assume that when a skipper owner or trawler company wants to equip a vessel with the most advanced life-saving equipment—such as inflatable boats, life rafts and life buoys— he will be able to obtain a grant. However, why is the grant restricted or reduced for the owners of vessels that are longer than 33 metres.

While I welcome the order, there are some gaps in it. Is the Minister confident that it is compatible with the requirements that are contained in EC draft directive 9284/92, which is concerned with the safety of fishing vessels and fishermen?

The problem is that the responsibility for the safety of fishing vessels extends beyond the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food to the Department of Transport. Inevitably, confusion may arise. However, I hope that I am in order to ask whether the order is compatible with that draft directive, because I notice that it was given ministerial approval at a Meeting of the EC Social Affairs Ministers at the end of May. It will take time to be implemented. It does not come into force until 1995 and then there is a severn-year implementation period. I should have thought that if an owner of a vessel is trying to ensure maximum safety for his' crew members—I only know of one female skipper in Scotland and so the hon. Member for Argyll and Bute will have to forgive me—and he takes his leave from the draft directive, will an application for safety equipment that is included in that directive entitle him to a grant under the provisions of the order?

I remind the Minister that the draft directive specifies the carriage of immersion suits. The Minister, his officials and members of the Committee do not need to be reminded that most fishermen die from exposure due to immersion in very cold water. The draft directive aims to deal with that problem by requiring that all vessels that are longer than 18 metres should carry immersion suits. They are already part of every day life in our offshore oil and gas industries. No one is allowed aboard a helicopter that goes out to one of the rigs or platforms unless he wears such a suit.

I shall give an example of the importance of immersion suits. During discussion of the Safety at Sea Act 1986, Albert McQuarrie, now Sir Albert, would not accept an amendment that I tabled. It would have led to the carriage Image Col. 7 of immersion suits on fishing vessels that are 12 metres and longer. Seven years ago, a French vessel went down very quickly off Kildare. It was overwhelmed in severe weather. There was a force seven gale and steep seas. Out of a crew of 27, those on watch were able to don their survival suits. Thus, 12 men went overboard wearing immersion suits. The other lads did not have time to put on their suits. French fishing vessels are not allowed out of port unless every man has an immersion suit, but some lads were roused from their berths and went overboard in their underwear. They perished in minutes. The 12 men who wore flotation suits survived for three hours until a helicopter arrived from Lossiemouth. They suffered terrible shock and hypothermia, but they lived. Their comrades died.

The Minister should discuss with his colleagues in the Department of Transport whether the 1975 rules can be amended vis-à-vis the draft directive to ensure the provision of immersion suits for our fishermen. I do not demand that fishermen wear them while they work on deck—the suits are too cumbersome. In some circumstances, as in the case of the Antares, the men would not have time to put on the suits. When I put one on and took it off it took a minute and a half, and that was when I was standing on a level deck. If a vessel is pitching in blackness, it would take longer.

In the light of the draft directive, are Department of Transport ministers considering amending the 1975 rules to ensure that immersion suits are carried? I agree with the Minister about the need to enhance the safety of our vessels. Vessel construction, conversion of vessels for purposes of safety, shelter and decks should be improved.

In 1993, it is time to ensure that vessels carry safety equipment, whatever some fishermen say about cost. I do not say that fishermen are tight, but I once wrote an article that described them as maritime Poujadistes. They do not like to spend money. Some are in straitened circumstances. It is time, however, to give our fishermen the protection of vital equipment. The fishermen of the other European Community maritime nations have such protection. If it is good enough for French fishermen, it should be good enough for our fishermen.

I welcome the order, but it is seriously inadequate, especially given its exclusion of vessels that are under 12 metres I look forward to the Minister's reply.
