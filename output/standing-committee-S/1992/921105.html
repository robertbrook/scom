10.30 am

The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Michael Jack): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Criminal Justice (International Co-operation) Act 1990 (Modification) Order 1992.

The amendment to the Criminal Justice (International Co-operation) Act 1990, which this draft order proposes, represents a small, but nonetheless important, step in the continuing fight against drug trafficking.

The order seeks to bring under the control of the 1990 Act an additional 10 precursor and other chemicals that are essential to those who illicitly manufacture controlled drugs. It does so by adding them to schedule 2 of the Act. The order is needed for us to comply with the United Kingdom's treaty obligations under the 1988 United Nations convention against illicit traffic in narcotic drugs and psychotropic substances. It seeks to implement decisions taken earlier this year by the United Nations commission on narcotic drugs, which in turn stemmed from a recommendation of the international chemical action task force. That was established by the Group of Seven most industrialised countries at its 1990 Houston summit and was in response to much concern expressed by some Latin American countries that have a serious drugs problem.

Before describing the purpose and effect of the order, it might help the Committee if I were to say a few words about the background to the 1990 Act. Provisions in it relate to the control of precursor and essential chemicals. Although the United Kingdom, in common with some other countries, has monitored the trade in precursor chemicals for upwards of 20 years, until recently that was done in a covert manner. It was dependent upon the voluntary co-operation of the chemical industry in bringing to notice suspicious orders and transactions involving the principal chemicals used in the production of amphetamines and other drugs manufactured illicitly in the United Kingdom. The assistance that we have received from the chemical industry has been, to say the least, absolutely magnificent. It has led to the detection of many illicit laboratories. I could go so far as to say that, in this country, we have the best possible co-operation in the world with our chemical industry on such matters. I take this opportunity to put on record my appreciation for the work that it has done.

In the 1980s, it became clear that suspicious reporting was not sufficient in itself to prevent the diversion of chemicals to illicit drug production. That was especially true in relation to the international trade in the fairly Col. 4 common chemicals used in the production of heroin and cocaine, the bulk of which are manufactured in Europe and north America. At the end of 1988, the international community took a major step forward in controlling precursor chemicals when it adopted the 1988 United Nations convention against illicit drug trafficking.

In broad terms, the convention requires parties to take appropriate measures to prevent the diversion of 12 scheduled substances to illicit drug manufacture, and to co-operate with one another to this end. It establishes as criminal offences the manufacture or supply of scheduled substances knowing or suspecting that they are to be used in the unlawful production of controlled drugs. The 1990 Act gives effect to the relevant parts of the convention, which the United Kingdom ratified in June 1991.

I am not a chemist—[Interruption.] Perhaps I am glad that the hon. Member for Cardiff, South and Penarth (Mr. Michael) shares my problem—and I hope that the Committee will forgive me if I do not give it a detailed explanation of the chemistry of each of the substances to be brought under control. However, I shall attempt to do that in broad terms.

Paragraph 2 divides the 10 chemicals into two groups: the five which are to be included in table I of schedule 2 to the 1990 Act, and the five which are to be included in table II. The substances in the first group, like all the substances in table I, are precursor chemicals in the scientific sense of that term. That means that they are effectively incorporated into the drug molecule which is required for a particular drug synthesis. In layman's terms, one could say that those substances are but one step removed from the drugs themselves. The first of the new substances, -N-acetylanthranilic acid, is used in the production of the controlled drug methaqualone. The other four—I hope the Committee will excuse me if I do not read them out—are precursors for the group related to MDMA, or ecstasy as it is more popularly known. Right hon. and hon. Members will be aware of the increasing use of that drug in the United Kingdom and the fact that its illicit manufacture takes place both here and in mainland Europe.

The five substances to be placed within table II are chemical solvents and reagents, and have a much wider commercial use. The chemicals that we are seeking to bring under control in this category are hydrochloric acid, methyl ethyl ketone, potassium permanganate, sulphuric acid and toluene. They are used widely in processing heroin and cocaine as well as synthetic drugs.

What will be the effect of the order on United Kingdom legislation? Its immediate effect will be to bring the 10 substances within the scope of the provision in section 12 of the 1990 Act. That makes it an offence for a person to manufacture a scheduled substance; or to supply such a substance to another person, knowing or suspecting that the substance is to be used in or for the unlawful production of a controlled drug. The maximum penalties which may be imposed for this offence on indictment are 14 years' imprisonment or a fine, or both.

These measures should also be seen in the context of Europe-wide developments. The Community is party to the 1988 United Nations convention and has competence in relation to trade aspects of precursor chemicals.

The European Commission and seven member states participated fully in the work of the chemical action task force. In addition to the proposals for scheduling the 10 additional substances, the CATF made recommendations for regulating the international and domestic trade in Image Col. 5 precursor and essential chemicals and in developing effective enforcement measures for preventing the diversion of scheduled chemicals. The Community has already taken steps to implement the regulatory recommendations. A regulation implementing them in relation to trade between the Community and third countries will come into force on 1 January 1993. This has been widely welcomed by the countries in Latin America and Asia where cocaine and heroin are a major problem. A directive regulating the intra-Community trade is expected to come into force on 1 July 1993, and that should be of considerable help to the law enforcement agencies in preventing and detecting the illicit manufacture of synthetic drugs within the Community.

That is the background against which the proposal in the order should be seen. I have described it in some detail because I hope that the Committee would wish to be aware of the development of international co-operation in such an important area. As I said, some of the chemicals have wide legitimate commercial use and are traded in vast quantities. For that reason, we have sought the advice and co-operation of the chemical industry throughout the negotiations. Our intention is to make life as difficult as possible for the illicit drug manufacturer while at the same time not placing unnecessary burdens on the industry. I think that we have achieved that balance.

I therefore commend to the Committee the changes proposed in the draft order.

I ask the Minister to tell us how that traffic is to be regulated in the new circumstances in Europe. There is worry about the reduction in the number of Customs officials at a time when there may be a need for an increase. That seems curious in view of the changes in border controls. There is a need for clear communication between agencies of central Government, local government, police authorities and the relevant authorities in the countries of our European partners, where often mechanisms and organisational structures will not match one another. Perhaps the Minister could clarify how the system will work. Who will be responsible for ensuring not only that the information is collected and that monitoring takes place but that it is used to good effect and for effective communication between ourselves and our partner countries, primarily in Europe but also outside European boundaries?
