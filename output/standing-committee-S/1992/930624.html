In his letter, he perhaps stated the obvious when he said that I would be a poor substitute for him. However, in some people's eyes the Minister with responsibility for sport and the shadow Minister with responsibility for sport are the right people to debate this subject.

It is the Opposition's view that the Order should not be seen as a censorship measure. The issues go wider that that. I have not seen much Red Hot Dutch television although, thanks to the Broadcasting Standards Council, I saw a video of some of its programmes yesterday. I am not in a position to say whether it falls consistently into the category of soft or hard porn, but from what I saw yesterday, it clearly seems to contravene article 22 of the European broadcasting directive and the Broadcasting Act 1990.

We all have our views of the Broadcasting Act 1990 and I do not suppose that many Conservative Members believe that it should be supported in full. Nevertheless, we must use that Act, as well as the directive, in our support for the order.

As I hinted, we also support the measure on grounds different from the Mary Whitehouses and others. It is our right as a nation to decide what type terrestrial or satellite television should be shown to our constituents. The order relates more to national control than to censorship. Some people argue that most European countries receive these programmes—meny do—so why should not we. However, that is a matter for them. Under article 2 of the directive on television broadcasting activities, transmission of programmes from another member state may be restricted in certain circumstances. I agree with that provision but, more importantly, so did the judges in the divisional court and the Courts of Appeal—Lord Justice Glidewell, Mr. Justice Hirst, Lord Justice Kennedy and, recently, Lord Justice Leggatt and Mr. Justice McCullough. [Interruption.] There is a lot of talking among conservative Members. I hope that they will intervene if I am slightly off course. However, I might be ever more worried if they thought that I was on course.

We believe that the Secretary of State was right to impose a ban on the reception of Red Hot Television in the United Kingdom. As we know, he has the support of the Broadcating Standards Council—which yesterday Col. 4 explained to me why it took that view—and of the Independent Television Commission. The Secretary of State rightly argued in the High Court that the European directive on transfrontier broadcasting applied equally to satellite channels when it dealt with retransmission. We agree that it would be illogical and inconsistent if any other interpretation were put on the directive.

The channel appears to contravene article 22. It can be argued that, as its transmisions take place after midnight in the wee small hours of the morning, it does not infringe article 22 in that way. But we believe that the Secretary of State has a right in law to ban the channel. However, the European Court, as it trundles its way through the relevant machinery may take a different view.

The Government are right in this case. Therefore, it must be equally right to review the law in this area, where there is so much confusion and inconsistency. What is more, the issue should be the subject of a wider and longer debate on another occasion.

10.37am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for National Heritage (Mr Iain Sproat): I thank the hon. Member for Stalybridge and Hyde (Mr Pendry) for his support, and for going through a rather gruesome ordeal yesterday to enable him to take the place of his hon. Friend the Member for Birmingham, Erdington (Mr Corbett).

I am grateful to the Committee for giving me the opportunity to explain the Government's reasons for taking action against the foreign satellite channel Red Hot Television. I agree with the hon. Member for Stalybridge and Hyde that a much wider debate is called into question by the order, which we shall not deal with in great depth or at great length this morning. I should like to explain why the order was made by my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State, and to give the Committee a little of the chronology to show how and why we reached our present position.

The showing of explicity pornographic material on television has aroused serious concerns among right hon. and hon. Members and members of the public. My Department has received about 700 letters on the subject, almost all of which express very strong views against the sort of material the Red Hot Television has been broadcasting.

I should like to make it absolutely clear that the Government will not allow pornography such as that broadcast by Red Hot Television. We will make use of every course of action available to us to prevent the grave offence caused to the public by such material. I hope that the action that we have taken against Red Hot Television will send the strongest possible message to all purveyors of this obnoxious material that the United Kingdom is a no-go area for such pornography.

Before explaining the action that we have taken, it may be helpful to the Committee if I set out the background to the channel and give information about the recent court proceedings, to which the hon. Gentleman referred. Red Hot Television, or Red Hot Dutch as it was then known, began broadcasting to the United Kingdom at the end of July 1992. The service was marketed to customers in the Image Col. 5 United Kingdom who had existing satellite dishes for a subscription of about £50 a quarter. It broadcast in encrypted form on three nights a week, which was then increased to six nights a week between the hours of midnight and 4 a.m.

There has been ill-informed speculation about the content of the channel. To set the record straight, I shall quote from an affirmation made by Mr. Colin Shaw, director of the Broadcasting Standards Council, which formed part of the evidence of the Secretary of State at the recent court proceedings. In describing the content of Red Hot Television, he said:

"From the outset, the channel broadcast films, mainly of American origin, showing explicit sex between males and females, including oral sex, close-up shots of penetration and ejaculation. Explicit lesbian scenes have also been shown. Group heterosexual and lesbian orgies are commonplace. Subsequently, the output, which then generally consisted of two films each of about 90 minutes duration, separated by a music item, included buggery of females by males, and the insertion of vibrators, dildoes and other sexual aids into the anal passages of both males and females. In a number of films, women were shown being penetrated in the vagina and anus, at the time, by two different males. There is little or no story in the films, which almost invariably contain a series of couplings and other sexual practices, usually accompanied by the minimum of dialogue, which is often littered with strong sexual language, used in its literal sense."

The essence of the situation was that a television service was being provided which, for four hours a night, six nights a week, was broadcasting a relentless diet of hard core pornography. I use the term "hard core" advisedly: the material in question would breach any programme code established for United Kingdom broadcasters. There can be no doubt about that. It would be illegal if published in any other media in the United Kingdom because of the terms of the Obscene Publications Act 1959. It contains the portrayal of acts which are themselves offences under the Sexual Offences Act 1956.

When the service began broadcasting in July last year, it was using an uplink—beaming a signal to the Eutelsat satellite—from a base in the Netherlands. In terms of the Broadcasting Act 1990, the fact of a foreign uplink places the service beyond the powers of the Independent Television Commission and defines it as a "foreign satellite service". The Broadcast Act 1990 recognised clearly that the increase in the number of satellite services created the possibility that services would become available in the United Kingdom which we would consider unacceptable.

For that reason, special provisions were included in sections 177 and 178 of the Broadcasting Act 1990 which were designed to deal with such a situation. The sections establish a framework within which an unacceptable satellite service can be proscribed under section 177 for the purposes of section 178 of the Act. Invoking these provisions, in respect of a particular foreign satellite service, enables certain steps to be taken to limit the availability of the service in the United Kingdom.

The steps include prohibition of the sale of decoders for the service, a ban on advertising—that is, either advertising for the service, or advertising on the service—and a prohibition on the supply of programme material for the service. It does not become an offence to watch the service, partly because such provisions would be either unenforeceable or extremely difficult to enforce, but also because we do not wish to criminalise individual viewers. It is the purveyors of pornography whom we wish to stop.

Col. 6 Before an encrypted service can be proscribed, three conditions, which are set out in section 177 of the Broadcasting Act 1990, must be met. First, the Independent Television Commission must inform the Secretary of State that it considers the service to be unacceptable. Secondly, the Secretary of State must be satisfied that it is in the public interest to proscribe the service. Thirdly, any proscription must be compatible with the international obligations of the United Kingdom. In this last regard, as hon. Members are no doubt aware, our main international obligation in the case of our relations with EC countries arises under the European Council directive, "Television Without Frontiers", which is often called the broadcasting directive.

The broadcasting directive is a single-market measure that sets minimum standards for broadcasting in several spheres, such as advertising. Provided those standards are met, member states are required to ensure freedom of reception of broadcasting from other member states. Only if a broadcast breaches the standards set out for the protection of minors may the receiving member state take action.

Under the directive, member states must ensure that broadcasters under their jurisdiction comply with the law. For those purposes the relevant article, to which the hon. Gentleman referred, is article 22. It may be for the convenience of the Committee if I quote that article, which states:

"Member States shall take appropriate measures to ensure that television broadcasts by broadcasters under their jurisdiction, do not include programmes which might seriously impair the physical, mental or moral development of minors, in particular those that involve pornography or gratuitous violence. This provision shall extend to other programmes, which are likely to impair the physical mental or moral development of minors, except"—

and this is a key point with which I shall deal in a moment—

"where it is ensured, by selecting the time of the broadcast or by any technical measure, that minors in the area of transmission will not normally hear or see such broadcasts".

A grave breach of article 22 is the only circumstance in which a member state has the right to suspend retransmissions of a service—and then only when the directive has been breached at least twice and consultations have failed to produce an amicable settlement. That is the complex background to the proscription of Red Hot Television.

I now give the Committee a chronology of events leading to the proscription order before us today.

On 15 October 1992, the Independent Television Commission wrote to my Department to inform the Secretary of State that in its view, Red Hot Television should be the subject of a proscription order. Officials in the Department were, of course, in touch with officials in the Netherlands, who were beginning court proceedings against Red Hot Television. As the Committee will remember, the programmes were originating from the Netherlands. The reason for the Dutch court proceedings was, as we understand it, that Red Hot Television did not possess a valid licence to broadcast. As it was then under Dutch jurisdiction, it was for the Dutch authorities to take action. Indeed, the action that they took resulted in the breaking, on 1 December 1992, of the contract that had allowed Red Hot Television to broadcast from the Netherlands.

Image Col. 7 The Broadcasting Standards Council, which monitors the service in the United Kingdom, then reported that over the weekend of 5 and 6 December 1992, the broadcasts appeared to be coming from Denmark. When approached by British officials, the Danish authorities reported that they hoped to be able to regulate the service in the new year. However, by January 1993, it became clear that Danish action would not be possible for quite some time and that, consequently, action would have to be taken in the United Kingdom if Red Hot Television was to be hindered from reaching a British audience.

My officials discussd with the European Commission on several occasions the scope for taking action against Red Hot Television under the broadcasting directive and under the provisions of the Broadcasting Act 1990 that I mentioned earlier. The case was the first of its kind to be brought under the directive. As is inevitable with new legislation, questions of interpretation were bound to arise.

Mr. Harry Cohen (Leyton): Is the Minister saying that if the channel had been regulated in another European country or had possessed a valid licence, we should not have taken action against it by introducing the order?

Mr. Sproat: No. that is not the case. I apologise if I did not make that clear, but I was running through the chronology to explain how we reached our position. The attitudes of the British, the Dutch and the Danes differ to a greater or lesser degree. The contract in The Netherlands was between the company and the Eutelsat satellite. It was broken on 1 December 1992 because the information that Red Hot Television had given to the Dutch authorities was inaccurate. The inaccuracy of that information invalidated the licence which, in turn, caused the contract with Eutelsat to be broken.

Red Hot Television then went to Denmark where, as we know, the attitude to pornography is different. The Danes were more free and easy. They did not have commercial television and so had to introduce laws to deal with the entire issue. As they have a system of proportional representation, dozens of parties had to be consulted. It was taking them so long that it became clear to us that we had to take action ourselves. Even if none of that had occurred, we should have concluded that the satellite service was unacceptable. I thank the hon. Gentleman for giving me the chance to explain that.

We then went to the Commission. My right hon. Friend the Secretary of State had a telephone conversation with Commissioner Pinheiro, the European Commissioner responsible for audiovisual matters. I am glad to be able to tell the Committee that there was a complete meeting of minds. The Commission showed itself as determined as the British Government to ensure that the broadcasting directive was not abused to create a single market in pornography. After much consideration, the Commission, most helpfully, informed us that the action we proposed to take against Red Hot Television was, in its view, fully compatible with the directive.

As a result, on 19 March, my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State informed Red Hot Television, the Dutch and Danish authorities and the Commission of our belief that directive was being breached and of our intention provisionally to suspend retransmission of Red Hot Television if we could not reach an amicable settlement within 15 days and if the breaches of the directive Col. 8 continued. Those conditions were, indeed, satisfied and my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State duly made an order under section 177 of the Act on 6 April this year.

As we had, by then, been informed that Red Hot Television intended to apply for judicial review and that the date of the hearing was fixed for 23 April, the order was timed to come into force on 1 May, once the divisional court had had the opportunity to form a view. The judicial review was heard on 23 April. Red Hot Television sought to overturn the order on the grounds that my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State did not have the power to make it.

As I have said, two out of the three conditions neccessary for an order were indisputably in place: The ITC had informed us that it believed that a proscription order was appropriate and my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State was satisfied that proscription would be in the public interest. As to the third condition, relating to our international obligations, it would be clear to almost anyone who has seen this service that the material has the potential seriously to damage the development of children. It is interesting to note that Red Hot Television did not contend otherwise. However, it did contend that because the service is broadcast late at night and in encrypted form, children will not have access to it and consequently cannot be damaged. We do not accept that argument. The directive recognises that there is material that has the potential to damage children so severely that it can never be acceptable, regardless of the time and of the technical nature of the broadcast. The material of Red Hot Television is, in our view, firmly in that category.

The second issue on which judicial review was sought was that even if a breach of article 22 had occured, the United Kingdom had no power to suspend retransmission of Red Hot Television, because, in fact, no retransmission occured in the United Kingdom, whether via cable or terrestrial means. The British Government contended that it could not have been the intention of the directive that whereas action could be taken to suspend retransmission of a service from satellite to a cable network and then to the public, no action could be taken to hinder reception of an offending satellite service beamed direct to people's homes. We are talking about a dictionary definition of the word "retransmission". The Commission supported our contention. The divisional court found that the arguments were not clear and decided that the European Court should be asked for its views. In addition, since the matter was to be heard by the European Court of Justice in any event, the court also decided to seek clarification of the meaning of article 22.

The following two questions are now before the European Court. First, does the directive allow the United Kingdom to take action to restrict reception in the United Kingdom of broadcasts transmitted from another member state when those broadcasts are received by the public directly via satellite? Secondly, do the obligations of a member state under article 22—to ensure that television broadcasts do not impair the physical, mental or moral development of minors—apply in relation to broadcasts where it is ensured or attempted to be ensured by selecting the time of the broadcast or by any technical measure that minors will not normally hear or see such broadcasts?

We will, of course, put our case forcefully to the court in Luxembourg in due course along the lines that I have given to the Committee this morning. In the meantime, the order Image Col. 9 remains in force. Red Hot Television failed in its attempt to obtain an injunction from the court to overturn the order. We believe that our action was right. When children are at risk, neither the profits of Red Hot Television nor the tiny minority's wish to watch such pornography count at all. We shall maintain that position forcefully in the European Court and any other court in the future.

My hon. Friend asked what powers we had to do anything about such matters. The powers are set out fairly broadly in section 178 of the Broadcasting Act 1991. Members of the Committee will no doubt wish to refresh their memories, but I shall outline the provision. Acts from which prosecution can arise include the supply of equipment or goods for use in connection with the operation or day-to-day running of a proscribed service or the provision of programme material—which can, by the way, include programme material that is not pornographic. Sending one's happy family video of fun on the beach to Red Hot Television is an offence, even though the film's content is acceptable. Therefore, the measure is pretty tight.

Anyone who incites or suggests to people that they should supply such material is also guilty of an offence. Anyone who advertises the programme or advertises on the programme is guilty of an offence. Those who are found guilty of committing such offences are subject to up to six months' imprisonment in a magistrates court and to two years and a fine of up to £5,000 in a Crown court. My hon. Friend may think that those are not severe penalties and that we should reconsider them. However, two years and £5,000 are the maximum penalties at present.
