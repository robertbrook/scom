Education (Grant-maintained Schools)

(Finance) Regulations 1993

4.30 pm

Mr. Win Griffiths
(Bridgend)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Education (Grant-maintained Schools) (Finance) Regulations 1993. (S.I., 1993, No. 568)

The Chairman
: With the agreement of the Committee, it will be convenient to debate at the same time the Education (Grant-maintained Schools) (Finance) (Amendment) Regulations 1993.

Mr. Griffiths
: The presentation of the regulations and our prayer against them enables us to re-examine how grant-maintained schools are financed.

I was one of those who was fortunate, or unfortunate, enough, depending on one's viewpoint, to sit on the Committee considering the great Education Reform Bill—GERBIL—in 1987. In Committee at that time, the Government made clear their intention that grant-maintained schools would be funded in a similar way to local education authority schools, taking into account the money that grant-maintained schools had received for services that were usually delivered by the LEA to its own schools.

On Report, Baroness Hooper, then an Under-Secretary of State at the Department of Education and Science, said:

"The Government's intention is that the financial effects of opting out should be broadly neutral—for the school, for the national taxpayer and for the local ratepayer … we intend the financial position to be broadly neutral."— [Official Report, House of Lords, 27 June 1988; Vol. 498, c. 1188.]

On Third Reading, Lord Trefgarne, then Minister for Defence Procurement, answered for the Government, I do not know whether that was a sign that they were feeling embattled. However, referring to grant-maintained schools, Lord Trefgarne said that they:

"will not be funded any more generously than they would have been had they remained with their local education authority."—[ Official Report, House of Lords, 17 July 1988; Vol. 499, c487–8.]

In paragraph 53 of its circular 10/88, the then Department of Education and Science said that a grant-maintained school's funding should

"fairly represent what the LEA would have been likely to provide for the school had it continued to maintain it including expenditure on services provided centrally".

Unfortunately, we have moved a long way from that position, even though the Government have not 4 made a formal statement to the House about their decision to ensure that grant-maintained schools will be favoured by a strong element of injustice, unfairness and bias in their funding.

Unfortunately, the Government have convinced themselves with their own propaganda. In announcing the funding changes under discussion today in the Department for Education press release of 9 February, Baroness Blatch said:

"The funding arrangements will continue to be fair."

The truth is that they never were. The only consolation that I can offer schools that are not grant maintained is that the funding bias is being reduced with each wave of schools that become grant maintained. Such schools are receiving less and less in each wave.

Let us consider the practical problems that the regulations will present to local education authorities and their schools. The first is the DFE's insistence, despite strong representations from all local education authority associations, that the data from two financial years shoud be used to calculate the annual maintenance grant. Another problem is that the cash protection element built into the central annual maintenance grant will remain unchanged for the schools that opted out earlier, despite significant changes in the funding arrangements for LEA schools, involving more delegation and greater responsibility for buying services. I should like us to consider that in more detail.

The Secretary of State for Education has often made statements in the House on educational issues that apply to England and to Wales. The regulations that we are discussing today do not apply to Wales. It can be said that the handful of grant-maintained schools in Wales is being dealt with in a way that is fairer to all other local authority schools, compared with what is happening in England. If the system in England is fair, as Baroness Blatch suggested, the few grant-maintained schools in Wales are obviously being treated unfairly. However, if the system is a little fairer in Wales—as I believe—it is evident that the position in England is blatantly unfair.

The way in which cash protection works in the operation of the central annual maintenance grant means that in some local authorities whose schools opted out early there are five different levels of grant support to grant-maintained schools. The DFE is familiar with that. Its consultation paper of November 1992 states:

"So, a lower than average percentage figure for central costs AMG will tend to be in addition to a higher than average"—

the DFE underlined "higher than average"—

"level of LMS-related funds (ie 'direct costs AMG'). The direct costs and central costs elements of AMG therefore need to be understood together and not in isolation."

How they operate means that the earlier a school became grant maintained, the greater advantage it has in its take of the available grant.

JPEG 5 The Local education authorities have already made representations to the Minister. In fact, when the Association of County Councils was a Conservative-controlled body—it is not now—it produced a report that was sent to its education committee for consideration. In that report, and in letters written to the Department for Education, the association has referred to the two problems that I have mentioned and has described the DFE's proposed solution as being "profoundly unsatisfactory". It felt deeply that the way in which the Government decided to calculate grants for grant-maintained schools was unfair. Local authorities wanted the data base to be for one year only. They have even said that they would be prepared to calculate it for the DFE based upon the DFE's own formula. However, that offer was rejected.

The unfairness has been highlighted even for grant-maintained schools. In this year's settlement, the Government took account of the fact that cleaning and ground maintenance would be delegated. Therefore, schools opting out now do not have that taken into account when their central funding is calculated. However, schools that opted out in previous years are given money in their annual maintenance grant funding to take account of that fact. They do not lose any money because of it. In fact, they are paid twice for cleaning and ground maintenance. In some local authorities the position is even worse than that.

I shall illustrate the problem by a hypothetical case and then give some practical examples of what is happening in local education authorities. Take a school that receives a delegated budget of £800,000 and £200,000 in addition, which is the cash value of services provided by the local authority. That totals £1 million, and 25 per cent. of £800,000 constitutes the direct annual maintenance grant. Let us assume that that was the position in 1992–93. In 1993–94, the local education authority may have delegated more money so that a school would receive £900,000. The cash value of centrally-provided services may be £100,000, which is just over 11 per cent. of the delegated budget. However, a grant-maintained school that already existed would receive the £900,000, but not the £100,000. It would receive 25 per cent. of £900,000, which comes to £225,000. Therefore it has an advantage over the local authority school.

What is happening in practice in local education authorities? The one that can be best highlighted is the London borough of Hillingdon because it was at the forefront of the campaign to encourage schools to opt out. However, a feeling of regret is growing in the borough about that policy. Hillingdon has to pay more to its grant-maintained schools than to its own schools on two counts. The first relates to the percentage of money that the local authority keeps back to provide its central services. For Hillingdon, the Department for Education calculated an add-on of 16.5 per cent. in primary schools and 11.5 per cent. in secondary schools for those central services. 6 Hillingdon itself calculated the figures—very precisely—at 14.99 per cent. and 11.09 per cent. As a result of the Departments calculations, opted-out schools in Hillingdon receive 1.51 per cent. more of the central services money and primary schools receive 0.41 per cent. more. That amounts to £110,283.

The real problem occurs in relation to the cash protection given to grant-maintained schools which opted out earlier. Nine opted out before 1992–93. Because of that cash protection, Hillingdon has worked out that the grant-maintained schools receive £505,613 more—a grand total of £615,896 more than they would receive if the money were being distributed fairly. That is the perpetuation of historic funding. Schools that opt out today will not receive the same cash advantages as those that opted out at the beginning of the scheme.

Some authorities have made specific calculations for schools that opted out earlier. Wandsworth, a Conservative-controlled authority, has calculated that it has spent £1 million more than necessary because of the way in which the funding is worked out. Two schools, in particular, that opted out at an early stage are receiving £308,000 more than they should. A grant-maintained school in a Conservative-controlled west midlands authority is receiving £50,000 more than it should. In Calderdale there are five grant-maintained schools which the authority reckons receive £154,000 more than they should for the local education central services provision.

There is another problem. Section 11 money is provided for authorities to give special help to children whose first language is not English. Yet a grant-maintained school in Tower Hamlets, which has no children whose first language is not English, still receives some section 11 money. There is almost an incentive for schools in Tower Hamlets which do not have New Commonwealth children to opt out because they receive a share of the section 11 money which they do not use because they have no children whose first language is not English. There are many examples of such problems.

Does the Minister anticipate such over-funding becoming a permanent part of the system, especially when, from 1994, the Government are committed to introducing the common funding formula, presumably in those areas where a significant number of schools have opted out?

Does the Minister believe that I am wrong about the grant-maintained schools that opted out earlier? A specific cash protection exists for them. It can be eroded only by inflation—a problem that all schools suffer. Before the council elections on 6 April, the Association of County Councils made representations to the Secretary of State in a letter asking straightforward questions. Yet the Secretary of State took a month to answer it. The association did not receive a reply until the day of the elections, or perhaps the day after. The bad news in the letter did JPEG 7 not avert the disaster that the Government suffered in the council elections.

When the letter was written, Dr. Barbara Marshall chaired the education committee of the Association of County Councils. In his reply to her, the Secretary of State makes a misleading statement about cash protection. I cannot use in Committee the word that one expert used to describe the statement, but the expert agreed that "gobbledegook" was an acceptable description for public dissimination. The Secretary of State said:

"The cash protection arrangements are an important safeguard for self-governing state schools. They help them adapt gradually to falling central cost percentages. Over time the protection erodes for each school."

I do not understand how that can happen. There is a fall in the central cost percentage and the amount of money that a grant-maintained school receives. However, if a school became grant maintained last year and was given a central annual maintenance grant of 15 per cent., it will continue to receive that grant. A school that opts out this year, the central costs of which have been calculated to be 10 per cent., will receive only that 10 per cent. cash value of the central services.

Schedule 1(2) of the regulation states:

"For the purposes of regulation 8(1), the relevant percentage in respect of any school for any financial year is whichever is the greater of the percentages determined for the school in accordance with paragraphs 4 and 5."

Therefore, I assume that a school that opted out earlier and received a grant of 15 per cent. will receive a grant at that percentage for at least this year, even if the central costs are now calculated to be 10 per cent. Schools that opted out last year will receive extra money. If that is not the case, I should like some clarification.

Local education authorities have said that because of the cash protection, schools that opted out early with a greater sum continue to receive the percentage that they had at opt-out. That was because the LEA still held many central services. They have since devolved them to other schools and kept back less money. It makes sense, therefore, for a lesser share to go to grant-maintained schools.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Schools (Mr. Eric Forth)
: Quite simply, it is not the percentage that is protected or frozen but the cash amount, hence the term "cash protection". The hon. Gentleman seemed anxious that somehow the percentage was protected. It is not. The deal is that the cash amount is protected. When I come to say my very few words later, I shall touch on that matter if he wishes. But, lest he misleads himself or others, I repeat that protected cash percentage there is not.

Mr. Griffiths
: I accept that. The important point is that grant-maintained schools that opted out a year or two ago receive an annual maintenance grant to cover 8 central costs based upon services that the LEA provided at the time, before they handed them over to all schools. They receive the equivalent sum for services that were provided by the LEA, plus the amount received by schools as part of their direct amount. They are being paid twice. I realise that they do not continue to receive the percentage, but they continue with the percentage that they received at the time of opt out. Therefore a double payment is made to them for the same local authority services.

The illustration of Hillingdon shows that an appreciable sum—almost £700,000—is being paid by that authority as part of the double service cash which only the early opt-out schools receive. Hence the differing levels of payment to schools that opted out at different times. Surely that is indefensible.

What will happen to the regulations when the common funding formula becomes operative next year? According to the Department for Education, it seems that some grant-maintained schools will operate under a common funding formula, as yet unknown, but which, judging by the consultation paper, will probably be based on the standard spending assessment for education in LEAs in which the funding formula will apply. Alternatively, the schools will receive money through the regulations—or through a further amendment to the regulations that may be laid later in the year. It would perhaps be as well for schools that are considering opting out to stop doing so for the time being and to reflect upon the common funding formula. There is great disquiet already about the way in which the SSA works.

Only today in The Guardian, the Audit Commission is reported as having described the proposed system as "confusing and complicated". The commission stated that the system

"no longer performs the task for which it was set up four years ago".

It agrees that the SSA

"works better than any of its predecessors"

but suggests that

"it is neither simple to understand nor stable in its outcome."

It goes on to say that

"the key objective of the system—to assess how much local authority needs to spend to provide a standard level of service"

is not being met at all. The report states:

"No standard level of service is defined or achieved."

It lists 12 criteria on which those matters might be assessed. It calculated that in only two of the 12 instances—17 per cent. in round numbers—did the criteria set in relation to defined services come anywhere near being met.

The hon. Member for Cambridgeshire, South-West (Sir A. Grant) has raised the issue. He said:

"The whole principle of area cost adjustment"

JPEG 9 which is part of the standard spending assessment—

"is completely outdated."

My hon. Friend the Member for Cambridge (Mrs. Campbell) pointed out that

"there is consensus that SSAs are inequitable and … they are being abused."

Figures were provided in The Times Educational Supplement last week. It was pointed out, for example, with regard to Gloucestershire, that Oxfordshire, the adjacent county, received £18 million in area cost adjustment, which Gloucestershire did not. Hampshire, not too far away and, on the face of it a much richer area, received £51 million. There are tremendous problems with the SSAs. Many local authorities spend more than the amount for which the Government has assessed them for the education part of their SSA. What will happen to those grant-maintained schools when the measure comes into being?

I should like to point out one or two other injustices in the system. Regulation 17, for example, permits the Secretary of State to pay special purpose grants to grant-maintained schools, in what he describes as exceptional or emergency situations, to enable them to continue to operate. I am aware of no provision allowing the grant of extra money to a local education authority school to overcome an emergency. Will the Minister have something to say about that?

There are other anomalies in the system. A grant-maintained school receives a percentage in its central annual maintenance grant to cover the cost of local authority central services. However, those local authority central services include doing all the calculations necessitated by the fact that there are opted-out schools in the local authority. The grant-maintained school receives money from the local authority for services that the local authority is having to provide to enable that grant-maintained school to exist. That is a serious anomaly.

There is no statutory provision for nursery education but the Secretary of State can provide money for a grant-maintained school where there are children of nursery age. That money can be recovered from the local education authority. However, because of the dangers of capping, the local education authority may not provide any nursery places at all. In effect, the opted-out school is imposing a precept on the LEA.

I should like to move discussion away from the revenue aspect of the subject and to consider some of the special grants that are given to the opted-out schools. Let us consider the unfairness of the situation. A local education authority that applies for capital funding from the Government advances its bid and then receives the right to borrow money to carry out its capital programme. However, a grant-maintained school simply receives the money for its capital project as a grant which has no revenue implications. The local education authority has only 10 the right to borrow the money to do its work and it has to pay later. In some small LEAs, there are grant-maintained schools that have received as much money for their capital work as the local authority receives for all its schools. In 1993–94—this financial year—one third of the available capital has gone to 2 per cent. of schools in England—grant-maintained schools.

In defence of that policy, the Government have said that they are making up for previous underfunding by LEAs. I should like the Minister to give us chapter and verse on the way in which schools that have opted out have been underfunded for capital expenditure compared with LEA schools. I should like to see that evidence and we are right to demand an answer.

I also wish to consider the special purpose grants that the Government are giving to grant-maintained schools. We should compare what happens to them with the position of LEAs. When an LEA applies for a special purpose grant, it must put forward a programme to the Government. That programme, or part of it—at least one project—must be accepted. If so, the LEA receives 60 per cent. of the funding for the projects that are accepted for special purpose grants.

However, a grant-maintained school automatically receives its share of the money without having to put forward a programme for acceptance to the DFE and without having to add on the extra money that the local authority has to provide. Therefore, it is evident that the way in which the system has worked so far has been unfair to 98 per cent. of schools and pupils in this country. In their effort to bribe schools to opt out, the Government have given them more financial inducements, but overwhelmingly they have not worked.

I wish to make it clear to LEA and grant-maintained schools—the LEAs are well aware of the fact—that a cash crisis is looming for schools and LEAs. The case of Hillingdon demonstrates that. Hillingdon has to find £700,000 out of its budget for overpayments. Under the standing spending assessment, many local authorities pay more. What will happen when the common funding formula comes into operation? As each new wave of grant-maintained schools has come into being, it has received less than the previous wave. However, perhaps "wave" is rather a grandiose word to use considering how few schools have opted out. The common funding formula will put a real squeeze on schools that opted out earlier. The longer the schools have had grant-maintained status, the bigger the squeeze will be. If I may paraphrase the words of the late Anthony Crosland, the party is over for grant-maintained schools—thank goodness.
