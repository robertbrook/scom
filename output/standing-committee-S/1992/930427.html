Mr. Keith Bradley (Manchester, Withington): We shall listen carefully to the Minister's remarks, which will come later, on the totality of the regulations. However, I want to make some short preliminary remarks on the jobplan workshop and restart schemes and on the reduction from two years to 12 months as the period before which penalties may be imposed on claimants if they fail to attend.

While we recognise that any action that tries to overcome the appalling problem of unemployment figures of three million, and that training mechanisms that try to help people get back into work must be welcome, we must question whether the existing schemes are of great value when we consider the drop-out rate and the penalties that are already imposed on people. Between April 1991 and March 1992, 3,804 people had their income support reduced for non-attendance or early leaving. That worked out at about 317 claimants per month. Between April 1992 and December 1992, a further 3,098 people had their income support reduced, which was about 344 claimants per month. If those schemes have such merit and are so valuable in helping people get back into work, why do so many people drop out and have to have a penalty imposed on them? We must question whether such a financial penalty is the best way to encourage people back into the workplace.

The Minister may say that the penalty is a reduction in only one week's benefit because the schemes last for only one week, but any reduction in income support can have a major impact on a person's ability to meet his or her expenditure that week. If the schemes are part-time, for half a day a week for example, they could extend to 14 weeks and the claimant's income support could be reduced each week. For people on the edge of poverty, such a weekly reduction in income support could be extremely damaging. Therefore, although we recognise the value of helping people back into work, we question whether financial penalties are the correct way to proceed.

Related to that, although the amount of the penalty may be reduced, we must question whether a financial penalty is the right way to proceed in cases involving serious illness or pregnancy. I realise that the reduction is from 40 to 20 per cent. in those circumstances, but is it right to reduce the income support of claimants in such cases? We have to consider sensitively the nature of illnesses in the family to ensure that people are not being penalised unfairly.

Col. 4 Related to that is the penalty imposed if a person undertaking voluntary work decides not to go on to a restart scheme. In my experience, voluntary work is a good step for many people trying to find permanent employment. Voluntary work has much value and it may be a better and more valuable experience than the Government's one-week workshop plan. It would be detrimental to those people's interests if they were penalised because they have been forced to give up voluntary work in order to go on to a restart scheme. I hope that the Minister will tell us why those doing valid and valuable voluntary work may be penalised if they refuse to go on to a restart scheme. They may consider themselves to be in a better position to get back into full-time work by undertaking voluntary work than they would be if they went on one of the Government's schemes.

The other related element is the appeals mechanism. If one can lose much of one's income support for failing to attend or for leaving a course, it is crucial to have the right of appeal. The scheme operates in such a way that one cannot make an appeal until the case has been assessed by a senior officer at the DSS. That can lead to tremendous delay. If one is on a week-by-week income, any delay may have detrimental effects on it. There should be a better mechanism to ensure that people do not lose their income support before they have the right of appeal.

One has to question whether the regulations are the best way to try to put people back into work. Any attempt by the Government to mitigate the damage that they have caused to the economy by forcing so many millions on to the dole is to be welcomed. But to have a double whammy—forcing people into unemployment and then financially penalising them if they do not see the Government's restart scheme to be of particular benefit—is not the way to proceed.

It is startling that, in their propaganda, the Government use a balloon flying away to promote the jobplan scheme. The slogan

"Give your jobhunting a lift"

and the image of a balloon flying away are the Government's hot air at its best. They will come down to earth with a bump when they find that the restart scheme and the jobplan workshops do not move people into decent employment. The propaganda claims that the scheme offers people "choices" back into employment, but if people face a financial penalty because they refuse to go on the scheme, one questions whether they have a real choice or whether they are being dragooned on to it. The only choice is to be financially penalised if one does not attend the scheme. Does the propoganda relate to the real world in which we operate? From our advice surgeries, we shall learn whether the unemployed regard the scheme as the liftoff that they were hoping for.

I shall listen with interest to the Minister's response to my points and to his wider comments on the regulations. We shall then be able to decide whether we need to pursue the matter further.

The hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire spoke about regulation 4 and income support for EC nationals. The purpose of the regulation is to put the policy intention beyond doubt in terms of restricting the payment of income support to EC nationals. It deals specifically with the change that we needed to make to legislation following a European Court decision.

The rules effectively make clear that, provided that an EC national is genuinely seeking work, he or she will be allowed to stay here for a period of six months and may claim income support. If work has not been found by the Col. 12 end of that period, the EC national must leave. Other EC countries vary in how long they allow basic income support to be paid to a work-seeker; some are more generous and others less so. We think that six months is about right.

Other persons from abroad—from outside the European Community—have no entitlement to income support, except in urgent cases. The Regulation deals specifically with job-seekers who are EC nationals. That is why the regulation relates to them rather than to other persons from abroad. Definition is, of course, more a matter for the Home Office than for us.

The hon. Gentleman also raised the detailed issue of the maintenance paid to a child with capital in excess of £3,000. This aspect is covered not only in the DWA part of the regulations but elsewhere, in regulation 13.

Perhaps I may give the background to the subject. In all the income-related benefits, maintenance payments made to a child, including a young person, are treated as the claimant's income and taken into account subject, when appropriate, to the £15 a week maintenance disregard. Otherwise, a child's income is taken into account only up to the level of that child's personal allowance and any disabled child premium.

In all the income-related benefits, when a child also has capital in excess of £3,000, no allowance is made for that child in calculating the claimant's applicable amount—in which case, any income of that child, other than maintenance payments, should be ignored.

On the introduction of the reformed schemes, however, this provision was only fully carried forward into income support. In all the other benefits, including council tax benefit, maintenance payments made to a child with capital in excess of £3,000 are treated in the same way as any other income of the child, and are disregarded. That means that when an absent parent pays maintenance for the child to the claimant or partner, this is taken into account in full before the application of the £15 a week maintenance disregard. When it is made payable to a child with capital in excess of £3,000, no account is taken of this. Consequently an inequitable state of affairs has arisen.

This amendment restores the original policy intention and ensures that all maintenance payments in respect of a child are treated as the claimant's income and taken into account, irrespective of to whom they are paid and irrespective of whether that child has any capital. Although it is difficult to gauge the extent of any losers, they should be few, as the likelihood of a child having both capital in excess of £3,000 and maintenance which is payable to that child is remote. Any loss will be further minimised because of the introduction, in April 1992, of the £15 disregard of such maintenance payments.

Essentially, the amendment brings into line across the benefit structure the provisions that deal with maintenance which is paid directly to a child who has capital in excess of £3,000. It is not, as the hon. Gentleman thought, a penal provision. The numbers of people affected will, we consider, be very small.

Mr. Kirkwood: Why have the regulation?

The background, as hon. Members know, is that people unemployed for over two years must attend a mandatory restart course. Those courses offer practical help and advice to the long-term unemployed on job search and encourage them to reappraise the efforts that they are taking to find work. They are not schemes in the sense that, I think, the hon. Member for Falkirk, East meant when he spoke of people being forced on to a scheme. They are courses designed to assist someone who has been unemployed in the long term, and who often has crossed a psychological boundary, and is wondering, "What on earth can I do?" The courses are intended to encourage such people at a difficult time to reappraise their situation and to help them apply. They provide a structure in which unemployed people are offered help in applying for vacancies and are given advice on the training opportunities available which may help them acquire the skills they need to get back to work. The courses have been successful in motivating the unemployed and in encouraging them to accept personal responsibility for their job search.

Jobplan workshops will build on the experience of restart and attendance at these will be compulsory for those who have refused other offers of help or who are unsure about what options are open to them. The workshops involve individual assessments and encourage the unemployed to be positive about their job search. They are intended to give a confidence boost to the unemployed and offer positive advice on how they can enhance their job prospects. As with restart, failure to attend will result in a benefit sanction, which will reduce benefit by 40 per cent. or 20 per cent. as appropriate, for the week of the workshop. The workshops are expected to offer help to 300,000 long-term unemployed people in the first year.

The change that we have made, to target attention on to those who have been unemployed for 12 months rather than for two years, should be seen by hon. Members on both sides of the House as an improvement, not as a penalty. It is important to target help at those who have been unemployed for 12 months, for three reasons. First, that point is often the change-over between unemployment benefit and income support. Secondly, surveys tend to show that employers regard job applicants who have been unemployed for 12 months as belonging to a separate category of the long-term unemployed. Thirdly, people unemployed for 12 months often have their job goals rather less focused because they have been unemployed for so long. It makes good labour market sense to provide encouragement at that particular psychological time. That is the reason for reducing the two years to 12 months.
