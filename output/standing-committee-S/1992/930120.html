legislation National Savings Bank (Amendment) Regulations 1992 (S.I., 1992, No. 2892)

10.30 am

Mr. A. J. Beith (Berwick-upon-Tweed): I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the National Savings Bank (Amendment) Regulations 1992 (S.I., 1992, No. 2892).

We have been here before and you were present, Mr. Hill, when we debated in the House, during the proceedings on the Finance Bill 1989, rises in minimum deposits for national savings. I said then that

"If they get away with this proposal, they will probably raise the minimum national savings deposit again and again and shut off many avenues for the smaller savers."—[Official Report, 11 July 1989; Vol. 156, c.835.]

I shall return briefly to that theme this morning.

Yet again, the Government are raising the minimum deposit to a level which will affect children and pensioners especially. For generations national savings have provided easy banking access for small savers, especially children and pensioners, a means of encouraging the habit of thrift among young people and a valuable service to people in areas where commercial banks are not available. That applies particularly in rural areas where commercial banks are tending to close down branches. The typical small village branch that opened on one or two days a week are closing down and the village sub-post office is becoming the only means of access to financial services for those who do not have transport and cannot easily get to a town. That confirms the value of national savings as a means of saving and handling money for those who have only small sums—for example, pensioners.

The transactions involved in national savings are important to rural post offices and sub-post offices. Presumably the Government's objective is to reduce the number of transactions so as to reduce transaction costs. That will reduce the income of those post offices. It is easy to reach the critical minimum income, at which point there are not enough transactions to keep a post office in business. We have seen the trend towards community sub-post offices opening on a limited basis, but even that may not be enough to prevent a serious threat to the one point of access for many Government services and financial transactions, of which national savings form an important part.

Col. 4 I was brought up to believe that taking money to a post office and saving up for something that one wanted was more sensible than blowing it all on a minor item. The sight of a small child putting his pound or two on the post office counter, which he could hardly reach, is part of our history and folklore and also the means of encouraging habit and thrift in children.

Some commercial banks have gone into that market with promotions for children and children's packs. Those banks sometimes have a conflict of policy. On one hand, they encourage children to open accounts; on the other, they raise minimum deposits and thus make it more difficult for children to use the accounts. The role of providing banking services for children is thrown back to national savings.

The cost of such transactions may be high in relation to the amount of money being deposited, but with children the cost borne at that stage may be an investment in future business if that young person continues to use national savings as a means of banking after acquiring an income as a young adult and working person. If small transactions by children are turned away, that may block the way to larger transactions being made in later life. It is not possible to cost that merely on a transaction basis and decide that the cost of small transactions is prohibitive. In part, small transactions are an investment in new business by National Savings.

We can draw an analogy with premium bonds. The Government announced at the same time in 1989 that they would remove the special facility and increase the minimum deposit for premium bonds. Premium bonds are a fairly bad investment and I would not encourage them, but, if people buy premium bonds, it is more sensible to stick to a small amount. The Government's approach implies that premium bonds are professional gambling, not a mild flutter of a pound or two. The same trend is obvious from the Government's decision that National Savings must cut out small transactions. What are national savings for? When we previously discussed the increase of minimum deposits in 1989, the Government had a public sector surplus. We were suddenly in an era in which we did not worry about the level of national savings. National savings no longer seemed important to the Government's debt management. How quickly the tables have turned. We now have a massive public sector borrowing requirement and there is a return to national savings as an important part of the Government's funding. We should not have been so hasty in dismissing the role of nationals savings in the Government's debt management and in encouraging habits of thrift and sound financial management.

The experience since 1989 should remind us that a generation probably grew up without sufficient guidance and encouragement in sound financial management. Many people became overcommitted. They thought that money would come easily and that Image Col. 5 borrowing could be easily handled. Habits of thrift learnt early might have been much better.

National Savings has a dual role which is undermined. If we increase minimum deposits to an amount that discourages young people in particular from using National Savings and from regularly saving in the small amounts that they are able to set aside, the Government will undermine sound and sensible principles in what to them is simply a cost-cutting exercise. In cutting those costs, they might also be turning away future business for National Savings and thereby imperilling the post office network which is also used for other socially important purposes upon which children and pensioners depend. The Government should reconsider their approach.

10.37 am

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Anthony Nelson): It might help if I make a few brief remarks to guide and inform the debate. I should like to hear what hon. Members have to say. I thank the right hon. Member for Berwick-upon-Tweed (Mr. Beith) for introducing the debate. He has done a service to the House and to the public because he raised important issues about public savings and public funding which need to be addressed, on which the Government have difficult decisions to make and on which it is proper to seek the guidance and views of Parliament represented by the Committee.

The right hon. Gentleman reminded us that he was prophetic. He drew a nostalgic image of the savings ethic, with children reaching out with their coins to swell their savings and ordinary accounts. Of course, that is true and it is something that the Government must take into account. But there are alternatives to thrift which offer to many people, including the young, more opportunities for saving.

The regulations exercise powers under the National Savings Bank Act 1971 to vary the minimum deposits for the national savings ordinary account and investment account. For the ordinary account, the minimum deposit will increase from £5 to £10 and for the investment account from £5 to £20.

Statutory instruments have been laid before the House with a view to introducing the changes on 1 February 1993. I welcome this opportunity to explain why the Government consider the measures to be sensible and necessary.

In his autumn statement, my right hon. Friend the Chancellor of the Exchequer made it clear that public expenditure must be kept under firm control. Like other Government Departments, National Savings has a part to play in that process. Accordingly, I announced proposals for achieving savings in National Savings running costs, including increases in the minimum deposit for the National Savings ordinary account and the investment account.

The main role of National Savings of contributing towards the Government's funding programme has to Col. 6 be performed in a cost-effective way. Each deposit into a savings account costs the same to process irrespective of the amount involved, so small transactions are disproportionately expensive. By eliminating very small transactions on those and other National Savings products, we will achieve useful cost savings of £5 million in a full year.

Mr. Alistair Darling (Edinburgh, Central): Can the Minister tells us the cost of processing a transaction?

Mr. Nelson: Yes, I can. The marginal cost of processing a deposit transaction is 86p. I shall later explain that that £5 million will not be the saving next year but it will be in a full year. I must point out to the Committee, however, that the cost is in excess of that figure which represents only the marginal cost saving and does not take into account the fixed costs elements, which would significantly raise the cost. The whole cost, however, is still well below the current minimum deposit levels that we are considering reviewing.

Mr. Austin Mitchell (Great Grimsby): What research has been done into the amount of business that will be turned away because of the accounts that will be lost through this measure?

Mr. Nelson: I shall come to the amount of business involved for Post Office Counters and the effects that the measure might have.

Deposits below the new minimum levels currently account for nearly a quarter of the total cost of handling deposits into the ordinary account and nearly half the total cost for the investment account, but in each case they represent only about 3 per cent. of the total value of deposits in a year.

In deciding the new minimum levels for the savings account we took into consideration the average value of a deposit, which is already £49 for the ordinary account and £143 for the investment account, well above the proposed new minimum limits of £10 and £20 respectively.

I appreciate that the new minimum deposits could make those accounts less convenient for some customers especially those in rural areas served only nearby by sub-post offices. We hope that more customers will be able to save the minimum deposit, even if they do not have that much available right away.

I have received several letters from hon. Members on behalf of sub-postmasters in their constituencies who want to know what effect the measures will have on their business. For many of the smallest sub-post offices in rural areas there should be no direct effect on their remuneration. They are 1,350 outlets on the minimum scale payment. There are 19,000 sub-post offices in total and, as far as the other sub-post offices are concerned, the effect on their remuneration will depend on their differing levels of savings bank transactions, but given that ordinary and investment Image Col. 7 account deposits represent less than 3 per cent. of sub-post office business in total, I would not expect the earning capacity of many sub-post offices to be seriously affected.

The Government are committed to the maintenance of the sub-post office network, but we must not lose sight of the fact that taxpayers provide the running costs of National Savings, including the fee that they pay to the Post Office, which amounts to £54.9 million in the current year. Some of that fee is passed on to sub-post offices. At present it includes fees for very small deposits which are uneconomic for National Savings to accept. Whatever costs are incurred for the social benefits provided by sub-post offices in outlying areas, I am unable to accept that those costs should be partly borne by what amounts to a covert subsidy.

It is important to remember that the cost of running National Savings is a charge to the Exchequer. We must always strive to maintain a balance between the interests of savers and taxpayers. The amount of money saved by the measures represents a necessary and welcome contribution to the control of public expenditure.

I did not take this decision lightly. I thought long and hard before doing so, but I am convinced that we are making the right decision.

The principle purpose of national savings is to fund the Government's spending requirements. While it is important to maintain and to encourage the savings ethic, we must not lose sight of the cost effectiveness of the principal purpose of national savings.

The ordinary account was started in 1861 and the investment account was started in 1966. Both have a popular following. The former has raised about £2 billion, which remains fairly static. In other words, people are not depositing more.

The investment account has raised about £9 billion. It has been defunding during this year—the overall balance has been falling. Both accounts must be regarded in the context of the total amount raised by the Department for National Savings—about £44 billion. That in turn must be regarded as the amount that people have lent to the Government for their funding requirements. The fund is not available to repay people.

I am conscious of the impact of any changes on the small saver, and I am persuaded that there are more and more alternatives but, most of all, I am sure that my hon. Friends will understand that every Department must play its part in finding necessary and justifiable economies for the public purse. This is a measured and appropriate economy.
