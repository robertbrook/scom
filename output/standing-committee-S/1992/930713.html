Post Office (Abolition of Import Restrictions) Regulations 1993

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Trade and Industry (Mr. Patrick McLoughlin)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Post Office (Abolition of Import Restrictions) Regulations 1993 (S.I. 1993, No. 1324).

The statutory instrument amends section 63(5) of the Post Office Act 1953 by removing the restriction on the import of fictitious stamps and the material means for making them into the United Kingdom from other member states of the European Community. Let me stress that it is a technical amendment designed to bring our legislation into compliance with our obligations under article 30 of the treaty of Rome. It will remain a criminal offence, under section 63(1) of the 1953 Act, for any person to possess or sell a fictitious stamp or to possess the material means for making such stamps anywhere in the United Kingdom. It will also still be illegal for any person to be in possession of these things on entering the country.

Under the treaty of Rome, member states are under an obligation to facilitate the achievement of a single market, and to remove any restrictions on the free movement of goods, persons, services and capital. Restrictions on the importation of goods can be permitted only, if they can be objectively justified as being consistent with article 30 of the treaty, which ensures the free movement of goods, for example under article 36 of the treaty. Given the prohibitions on the possession, selling and making of fictitious stamps in subsections (1) and (2) of section 63 of the Post Office Act 1953, the Government do not feel that they could justify the continuance of a provision that specifically prohibits the importation of fictitious stamps from another member state either by reference to article 36 of the treaty or on any other ground of objective justification. We are, therefore, obliged to remove this restriction—hence the regulations. They form part of a wide range of single-market measures foreshadowed by my right hon. Friend the President of the Board of Trade in a written answer on 17 December last year. They were also the subject of a scrutiny debate in Standing Committee as recently as last Wednesday.

The hon. Member for Ashfield asked for an explanation of the legal base for the regulations. Their purpose is that we should comply with the obligations set out in article 5 of the treaty of Rome, in so far as it relates to the objectives set out in article 8A. Article 5 requires member states to facilitate the achievement of Community aims and to abstain from any measures that could jeopardise the attainment of the objectives of the treaty. The free movement of goods is ensured in accordance with article 30, so member states are under an obligation to facilitate the achievements of the internal market and to remove any restrictions on imports, except in so far as they can be objectively justified consistent with article 30. The Chairman
: I have listened extremely carefully to what the right hon. Gentleman said and I have looked at the Standing Committee procedures. Therefore, without further ado, I must put the motion.
