Social Security Benefits (Amendments Consequential Upon the Introduction of Community Care) Regulations 1992

10.30 am

Mr. Keith Bradley
(Manchester, Withington)
: On a point of order, Sir James, I want to raise a procedural matter. Regulation 3 concerns preserved rights of individuals in residential care. Under Section 43 of the National Health Service and Community Care Act 1990, there is confusion about the way individuals with preserved rights will be dealt with if those rights are undermined, for example, if the home in which they are living goes out of business. That section makes it clear that the local authority that would be responsible for those residents would not have the power directly to take over those preserved rights and the financing arrangements for individuals. New regulations were promised to explain how the local authorities would deal with categories of people who, under regulation 3, have preserved rights. However, it is not clear which groups local authorities will be able to support.

To consider the statutory instrument, which interacts with section 43 of the National Health Service and Community Care Act 1990, when draft regulations under that section have not been laid is an abuse of parliamentary scrutiny because the Committee cannot fully appreciate the consequences of what is being asked to approve. I request that we do not proceed this morning until those regulations have been laid and it is clear how people with preserved rights will be dealt with, if those rights are undermined.

Mr. Archy Kirkwood
(Roxburgh and Berwickshire)
: Further to that point of order, Sir James, I support the claim that we should carefully consider whether to proceed with the regulations. I understand the issue of preserved rights—it has been drawn to my attention and I am concerned about it.

I do not understand how the Committee can sensibly consider the regulations without also examining the top-up provisions and the arrangements for that. Regulations will have to be laid in future to deal with that matter. It would be much better for the process of parliamentary scrutiny if the Minister were to present us with a series of coherent regulations—they all overlap.

The Chairman
: I have listened carefully to the hon. Members for Manchester, Withington (Mr. Bradley) and for Roxburgh and Berwickshire (Mr. Kirkwood). I shall consider their comments but, in the meantime, we should proceed.

Mr. Bradley
: I am grateful to the Minister for his explanation, but I think that it reinforces my point that he recognises that regulations from the Department of Health impinge on the people whom we are discussing. As regulation 3 is entitled "Preserved rights" and the Minister has made it clear that those rights flow through to the new regulations under the 1990 Act and are removed from the responsibilities of the Department of Social Security, we are dealing with only half the issue. The consequence for many elderly people in residential care is that they will be uncertain about how they will be dealt with.

I hoped that, this morning, we would have been able to get precise information about how the regulations will affect those people. That hope has been undermined by the Government's failure to lay the regulations through the Department of Health such regulations should interact with the social security regulations and be consequential on them. To debate properly what preserved rights entail, we have to ensure that those rights genuinely flow through to the new regime after 1 April. I doubt that that will happen. We must ensure that the public are clear on that matter. I hope that we shall have another opportunity to raise our anxieties on the Floor of the House.

Preserved rights have caused concern in the past and are a cause for concern now among many who work in the residential and independent sector. That issue will cause great anxiety when the provisions of the National Health Service and Community Care Act 1990 come into force on 1 April.

JPEG 5 Regulation 3 deals with preserved rights that relate to people who are in residential care and who claim income support. In addition, the preserved rights flow through for people who, because of their income or capital, are not currently paid income support. They retain rights to income support in the future if their situation changes.

That all sounds very grand and as if everything in the world is rosy and clear, but we are discussing not those kinds of preserved rights but preserved rights to inadequate benefits and uncertainty because of the growing gap in, the cost of residential care and the amount of income that people receive through the income support system. Over the years, that amount has increased, but the gap has widened. There is a massive amount of evidence to support that contention.

I am sure that all members of the Committee have received as many letters as I have from independent homes expressing anxieties. Those letters explain that many existing residents and their relatives experience hardship because income support does not meet the full cost of their care. Homes in the private and voluntary sector are running up debts and going out of business because of the shortfall between benefits and fees. Many residents have had to turn to charities to find top-up money so that they are not evicted from homes. A vast amount of evidence from the charities shows that they cannot continue to make that money available to so many residents. The charity commissioners have questioned the role of some of those organisations. They do not believe that topping up the difference between the fees and the income support levels introduced by the Government is an appropriate use of charity money.

Many surveys have shown the extent of the shortfall. A survey for the Association of Charity Officers by the Institute of Gerontology showed that, in 1990–91, 117 charities were providing £4.8 million in top-ups to 6,772 elderly people. The average weekly grant was £20.58. Topping up payments absorbed 38 per cent, of the charities' expenditure on elderly people. The average weekly shortfall in the summer of 1991 for elderly people outside London was £57.61 for those in private residential care, £50 for those in voluntary sector residential care, £55.26 for those in private nursing homes and £56 for those in voluntary sector nursing homes. That is one example of the extent of the problem, which occurs because preserved rights are linked to an income that is too inadequate to ensure that those people have the right to remain in their residential home. Preserved rights preserve the right to help with the shortfall.

It is impossible to judge the effects of regulation 3 because that will depend on how it interacts with regulations yet to be made under section 43 of the National Health Service and Community Care ACT 1990. That section prevents local authorities from paying part or all of the fees for residents who are in homes on 31 March 1993. It is not clear what role the local authorities will play in ensuring that the cost of the gap is met and that the people who have preserved rights will be secure after 1 April 1993.

6 Regulation 3 provides only the right to some income support without guaranteeing that the fees will be covered. What thought has been given to the position of residents? I want to consider some specific groups. There are residents who manage to meet the fees because relatives or friends make up the shortfall. Who will be responsible, under the preserved rights, for filling the gap if those relatives or friends die or run out of money?

If fees rise and income support does not increase sufficiently, a gap will occur which will disadvantage the residents whose fees have increased above the income support ceiling. That is particularly applicable to residents in terminal care because income support limits for those people are being frozen from April. The cost of terminal care will rise and the amount of income, because the terminal care support has been frozen, will be reduced and a gap will open. Those people might be insecure because the fees may not be met and cannot be topped up by the local authority. In addition, those residents who have been living in supported lodgings or small residential homes since April 1987 now appear, under new paragraph (1ZE), to be about to lose their rights to income support payments towards those fees.

Finally, there are people whose fees are topped up by local authorities and who get income support because they were under pension age when they entered care. There is concern about the large number of people who are entering residential care at a relatively young age and a fear that the gap between the fees and income support will grow and grow. There is no certainty that the income support levels will meet that gap or that, over many years, those people or their relatives will have the resources to meet the cost.

The Government changed the rules on capital so that individuals have to sell their homes when they enter residential care to enable them to use the money as a contribution towards bridging the cap. All the research shows that capital receipts from the sale of their homes fills the gap for on average, between only five and seven years. Many people are in residential care for much longer than that. Who will fill that gap once their resources and capital have been exhausted? How can that be termed a preserved right in any shape or form? They are preserved inadequacies of the system and there is insufficient information to explain how the gap will be filled.

Existing residents will be trapped in a no-man's land by the inadequacy of their preserved rights plus the effect of section 43. How will they find help to make up the difference between their benefits and the fees for the home? The effect of section 43 is that they must be evicted from their current placements before a local authority can step in and help. As I understand the section, the local authority cannot intervene in advance of eviction. It cannot step in and top up someone's benefit because it believes that a placement is appropriate. The person must be evicted and thrown on the mercy of the local authority's new assessment procedure before the local authority can redetermine JPEG 7 what care is appropriate for him or her under the National Health Service and Community Care Act.

Many of the people affected have become used to living in their homes. They are happy and their relatives are happy with the care that they are receiving but, because of the financial shortfall, they cannot remain where they are. The Minister may say that everything will be all right when the new regulations have been laid. That makes it difficult to debate the issue, because we do not know how the new regulations that will impinge on section 43 will work.

We have been denied proper parliamentary debate, which is worrying. I was on the Standing Committee that considered the national health service and community care. Section 43,

"prevents authorities from arranging residential care or nursing home accommodation for people already resident … when the new community care arrangements come into effect. Generally these people will have a preserved entitlement to claim income support at the registered homes rate."

That recognises that preserve rights exist, but it is unclear how the local authority will take on its responsibility.

As the Minister may remember, when we debated the issue on Report, an amendment was tabled to give preserved rights. The Under-Secretary of State for Social Security at the time, the former Member for Windsor and Maidenhead, was active in leading the campaign to recognise the problem. That amendment was agreed on Report. Unfortunately, for other reasons, it did not become part of the Bill. The House clearly recognised that there was a problem, which needed to be addressed. That problem has flowed into the regulations and I am not certain that it will properly be addressed.

As we are only one month away from the implementation of the National Health Service and Community Care Act, we must be clear how people's so-called preserved rights will be safeguarded. I have been contacted about the financial situation in many homes. Many have already gone bust; in some ways, it is better that they did, so that the position of the residents could be sorted out before 1 April. If they have no preserved rights, their assessment can be certain before the new arrangements come into effect.

People who live in residential and nursing homes are the most vulnerable in our society. Many are too ill, disabled or elderly to fight for their rights. We must ensure that the regulations do not preserve the gap between fees and income support levels, which is increasing and causing financial anxiety and distress to many elderly people. We must ensure that people who are already in residential care have preserved rights if, through no fault of their own, their current accommodation is no longer available, because either the business has gone bust or they can no longer afford to pay the fees and the home tries to evict them. The matter is serious and there is anxiety about it throughout the 8 country. However imperfect our deliberations, I hope that the Minister will reassure us on the points that I have raised.

Mr. Burt
: I am grateful for the opportunity to discuss the regulations and for the comments of the hon. Member for Withington. Before I reply to his specific points, I should be grateful for the Committee's indulgence while I set out the purpose of the regulations. It is inevitable that the debate has already widened. The hon. Gentleman's procedural point illustrated the breadth of issues covered by community care legislation. We are discussing the social security regulations which have already been laid and are due to come into effect in April. It is not possible to have the sort of debate that the hon. Gentleman wants in this forum and to cover all the regulations that may flow from the National Health Service and Community Care Act 1990. We are discussing the social security regulations and they can be discussed as an entity. The Department of Health is likely to bring forward regulations based on the section to which the hon. Gentleman referred, but it is important for it to see the regulations before us before it does so. There is interplay between the two, but in dealing with the regulations we can see how the benefits system has altered to take account of the changes being brought forward by community care.

The community care reforms were designed to enable people to remain in their own homes wherever possible, to provide proper assessment of care needs and good case management, to promote the development of a flourishing independent sector alongside good quality public services and to secure better value for taxpayers' money. The regulations show how the benefits system is adapting to put those principles for community care into effect.

Currently, higher levels of income support are available to people in independent residential care and nursing homes to help them to meet their fees if they have insufficient resources of their own. This is a benefit that is intended to bring the income that a person already has up to a set level. For people in homes, that level is the amount of the fees or a set national limit, which ever is the lower, and an allowance for personal expenses.

Under the new community care arrangements from April 1993, people entering residential care and nursing homes will be able to claim income support on a broadly similar basis whether they are in such a home or living in their own home. Additionally, they will be able to receive, as part of their income support entitlement, a new residential allowance as a contribution towards their accommodation costs. Anyone who requires help towards their care costs will, before admission, have approached the local authority, which will assess his or her care needs and will meet any extra cost involved for residents for whom they arrange such care. The local authority will take account of the individual's ability to contribute towards those costs. In deciding the ability of an individual to contribute JPEG 9 towards his or her fees in the care home, the local authority will carry out a financial assessment. That will be broadly aligned to the rules that currently apply to income support under regulations and guidance have been prepared by the Department of Health.

In setting out to introduce the new arrangements, we have given paramount importance to the need to reassure existing residents and to avoid imposing too great a task on the local authorities upon whom most of the burden for the new arrangements will fall. To aid the smooth changeover to the new scheme, and to reassure current residents, the present system of income support limits will be preserved in its entirety, including the regular review of the limits, for people already in registered homes at the time of change, even if they are temporarily absent from the home on 31 March 1993 or subsequently leave the home for a short time. Much of the complexity of the package of regulations, which cover housing benefit and disablement benefit—we did not need to discuss them this morning, but they are included in the package of regulations—is due to the provision of that sophisticated system of protection for existing residents across all the benefits involved.

The hon. Member for Withington raised several matters. On shortfall, it is inevitable that some people will contract for fees above the income support limit. Fees are a matter between the home and the residents or their relatives and the Government have no authority to intervene. We have continually made it clear that no Government could meet all fees in full however high they are set. However, we are not aware of any instance of residents who rely on income support being evicted because they could not meet the fees, although residents have sometimes been transferred to other homes. Some homes have closed, but I am not convinced that the closures were a direct result of the amount of help with fees payable through income support.

An increasing number of new places are becoming available in residential care and nursing homes and undoubtedly some will close, not necessarily because of the level of income support, but because of other factors associated with the provisions of care and the larger number of places coming into being.

Perhaps it would help the Committee were I briefly to describe the rest of the regulations. The regulations allow for the introduction of a residential allowance. The decision to introduce a residential allowance as part of a person's income support entitlement, rather than as part of housing benefit as originally proposed, provides a simplification for individuals and local authorities involved. It is also beneficial in that when people enter homes for a period of respite care, they will continue to be able to receive housing benefit for their home address.

It has been universally recognised that the ability to provide support through respite care is one of the great successes of the regulations. Mr. Burt
: That might be a matter on which I shall have to write to the hon. Gentleman.

The rest of the regulations relate to changes that we have had to make to housing benefit. Although the majority of care home residents already rely on income support to help pay their fees, a minority can claim housing benefit instead. As I have explained, the new 14 income support residential allowance is designed to provide a contribution to housing costs for those entering care homes from April 1993 who need public support. The regulations provide that housing benefit should not be available to people with an actual or a potential entitlement to the residential allowance. That measure is consistent with our continuing policy that help with accommodation costs for the majority of people in care homes should be provided through income support, and will avoid creating any anomalies to the provision in the benefits system.

I reassure hon. Members that the regulations do nothing to disturb the rules which allow the continued payment of housing benefit for a person's main home while that person is living temporarily in a residential home for a period of respite care.

We have been mindful of the need to protect the minority in care homes who are entitled to housing benefit at the point of change—to return to a form of preserved rights, in this case transitional protection of housing benefit. Therefore, we have taken measures to ensure that those people will retain their entitlement to housing benefit for as long as they live in the same home. However, if they move permanently to a new home, we believe it right that they become subject to the same arrangements as other residents and that they rely on income support for help with their fees.

The regulations provide that the small group that is granted preserved rights to housing benefit for care home fees under previous legislation should retain those rights indefinitely on the same basis.

The regulations have been dealt with by the Social Security Advisory Committee. In his response to that committee's report, the Secretary of State said that the Government welcomed the SSAC's general acceptance of the draft regulations, which arise as a natural concomitant to the new care in the community arrangements. One of the main concerns that emerged from the SSAC's report was that the introduction of the regulations be carefully monitored to identify quickly any problems. We were then, and we are now, happy to reassure the Committee of our intention carefully to evaluate the effectiveness of the Department's new community care arrangements.
