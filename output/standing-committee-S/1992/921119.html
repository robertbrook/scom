10.32 am

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Nicholas Soames)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Food Protection (Emergency Prohibitions) (Lead in Ducks and Geese) (England) Order 1992.

It may be for the convenience of members of the Committee to know about the order in more detail. The events that gave rise to it are that a private veterinary surgeon sought a diagnosis from our veterinary investigation centre at Shrewsbury of the cause of illness in a goose belonging to a farmer in Billingsley, Bridgnorth. The goose was one of some 78 geese and 300 ducks among which there had been unexplained illness and death. The post-mortem carried out on the goose sent to Shrewsbury revealed very high levels of lead in the liver of the bird—198 parts per million. For comparison, the Committee may find it helpful to know that there is a general statutory limit of one part per million for lead in food. Therefore, the problem was serious.

A farm visit was immediately organised by the Ministry's veterinary investigation staff. It revealed significant numbers of ducks and geese displaying typical symptons of chronic lead poisoning. Lead shot was discovered in the gizzards of some of the birds examined. According to the farmer, his land lies directly in the path of a now defunct 4 clay pigeon shoot. Subsequent analysis of further samples revealed lead levels in kidneys and livers from birds displaying clinical symptoms to be between 100 and 400 times the general statutory maximum.

In discussion with the Department of Health, we concluded that action was necessary to ensure that no birds from the affected flocks were put into the food supply. Against that background, my officials alerted the local environmental health department of Bridgnorth district council and approached the farmer to seek his co-operation in ensuring that the integrity of the food chain remained fully protected. A check of his sales and other records confirmed that there had been no sales of ducks or geese for some months prior to the current incident.

It was put to the farmer that all birds not displaying clinical symptoms should be moved to a clean site. They could then be released into the food chain if, after a further month, they remained healthy. A second option was that all the viscera from such birds should be removed as a prerequisite for sale. Ministry veterinary staff also explained to the farmer and to his own veterinary surgeon that there was little likelihood that birds displaying clinical symptoms of lead poisoning would recover and that they should be humanely destroyed on welfare grounds.

Mr. Campbell-Savours
: Does the Minister believe that that was a major incident, a serious incident, or neither?

Mr. Soames
: The levels of lead found in the geese and ducks was such that, had they entered the food chain and been eaten, the consequences could have been extremely serious. The duty of my Department and of the Department of Health is to protect the integrity of the food chain at all times. We therefore had no option but to proceed as we did.

I shall continue to outline the case; the hon. Gentleman may wish to interevene later. The farmer rejected the options put to him and the advice offered. In the absence of the required undertaking not to sell birds from the affected flocks for food, it was clear that statutory action would be necessary to enable us to be sure that the public were not put at risk. An order was therefore made under Part I of the Food and Environment Protection Act 1985 banning the movement of the affected holding of ducks, geese and any food or feed products made from those birds.

I am naturally conscious of the serious impact that the restrictions are having on the farmer concerned. This morning I had a meeting with my hon. Friend the Member for Ludlow (Mr. Gill), who is his Member of Parliament. He robustly expressed to me his concerns on behalf of the farmer. As I explained to my hon. Friend, we would not take such action lightly, nor would we do so unless there was a paramount requirement to protect the integrity of the food chain.

The evidence of risk to consumers was compelling and, in the absence of the farmer's co-operation, it was right and essential for us to act to protect the food chain. The Department is continuing to monitor the situation and to advise the farmer on the welfare implications of the incident, his management options, prospects for the safe release of birds and food products into the food chain and the future use of the land that has been affected by the clay pigeon shoot. In the meantime, our intention is that the restrictions will remain in place for as long as is necessary to ensure that consumers remain fully protected.

A late colleague of ours, Brynmor John, moved an amendment that I want to draw to the Committee's attention because it gives us our first opportunity to consider whether there should be a change in the law.The amendment proposed that

"The Ministers may jointly by regulations provide for the payment of compensation to any person who has incurred loss or damage as a result of the making of an emergency order in circumstances where … the person responsible for the release of substances giving rise to the making of that order cannot be identified."

That is the problem. Mr. Haycock cannot identify those who were responsible, all those years ago, for polluting the land from which his stock is to be removed—an action that will result in him losing a great deal of money. If that amendment had been accepted, Mr Haycock would receive compensation.

Mr. John said about relevant cases:

"The first is where an order is made and food is not handled, moved or sold and a crop perishes, yet is found to be unpolluted. The other case is where pollution has occurred, but no individual polluter can be identified. I gave the example of the water authorities".

He later said:

"I believe that amendment No. 7 and the linked amendments should be accepted, so that the Government compensate in these two rare cases. This is an emergency happening, and it is inconceivable that small producers should be asked to bear the loss"—

Mr. Haycock is a small producer—

"for an accident over which they have no control and of which they often have no warning."—[ Official Report, Standing Committee H, 21 March 1985; c. 44–49.]

The incidents involving Mr. Haycock precisely meets the terms of that amendment.

"I am assuming again that my comments on insurance"—

A debate raged in Committee for several hours, and was repeated on Report, on whether it was possible to insure against the possibility of orders being taken out in the future. Indeed, we must consider that today, some eight years later.

Mr. John had taken advice from insurers in the market and he said that there was a serious conflict of evidence concerning insurance. So a Member of Parliament was told by people in the market that insurance was not available. That is very important.

When Parliament considered the original legislation that gave Ministers the right to introduce the orders, Ministers expressed the view that farmers could insure against the introduction of an order that would wipe out their incomes. From subsequent information gained from the market, it appeared that such insurance policies were not available. Over recent weeks, lengthy inquiries have been made among brokers and underwriters specialising in agriculture and pollution. In future, a person in a similar position to Mr. Haycock would be highly unlikely to be able to find a policy to cover such a loss.

Mr. Soames
: This is not a trick question, I am trying to understand the hon. Gentleman's point. Will he define what he means by saying that the type of loss involved in that case is uninsurable?

Mr. Cambell-Savours
: Mr. Haycock was contentedly farming his geese and ducks. An order was suddenly imposed on his land by the Government, as a result of which he has lost the use of his land and £5,000 worth of his stock, and he feels aggrieved. Someone should pay the bill. If he could not get insurance from the market to pre-empt that risk, surely the Government should provide a suitable scheme.

We have had only 28 incidents since the Act was introduced in 1985. In reply to a recent parliamentary question, the Minister said that there were five cases in 1986—they related to Chernobyl—one in 1987, none in 1988, 11 in 1989—of which 10 related to the lead in an animal feed incident—five in 1990, three in 1991 and three in 1992. We are not considering vast amounts of public money. The gentleman's livelihood has been placed at risk, JPEG 7 as a result of no action for which he could be held responsible. If it were impossible to secure insurance insurance, he should be compensated by a relevant authority. If a person in a similar position to Mr. Haycock were able to find a policy to cover such a loss, its cost would be prohibitive.

Several insurance experts, such as John Waarr of Fielding Sweeton Jones, were not surprised by that outcome of the legislation. They expected the case to be the start of a landslide of similar cases resulting and say that they predicted such a result at the time. Perhaps they exaggerate by speaking of a landslide, but it is interesting that they predicted it when Labour Members were arguing in Committee that that would be the outcome. They say that the insurance industry in general has moved against pollution liability and, in many cases, places an exclusion clause in general policies against such liability.

Experts such as John Lawson of P. W. Creamer and Company say that at the heart of the legislation there is tension between two legal principles, caveat emptor—let the buyer beware—and the principle that the polluter pays. It is necessary to consider how those principles would be resolved if a major disaster occurred in Britain when the problem was multiplied many times. Thankfully, the legislation has not been tested in Britain by a major disaster—although some people might say that Chernobyl was one. Only in those circumstances will it be possible to test whether insurance claims stand up.

Even if insurance policies exist, it is likely that, because of the amount of money involved, the meaning of the wording in policies will be subject to lengthy litigation. Victims of pollution, especially when the polluter cannot be identified or if he is a man of straw will find that they have no one to sue, no possibility of making a successful insurance claim and that no compensation will be available to them.

Insurance against pollution is available under an environmental impairment scheme, but it covers sudden and unintended incidents that lead to pollution, such as the unexpected rupture of an oil pipe or a chemical tank. Even if Mr. Haycock had such a policy, it is extremely unlikely that the insurance company would consider that pollution caused gradually over the years was sudden or, even given the circumstances, that it was unintended.

The only way in which it would be possible for someone like Mr. Haycock to have a chance of being covered in the future would be if he were to approach an insurance company to arrange for an environmental audit of his land. That is a significant proposition—a person obtaining an environmental audit of his land in the event that such insurance policies were to be made available in future would be unrealistic. Yet in 1985, when we debated the Haycocks of the future, no reference was made to insurance being made available. Such schemes are available primarily for large businesses such as chemical companies and would be prohibitively expensive for a small farmer. One insurance representative estimated that the cost of such a policy in the case to which I am referring could be as high as £10,000 per annum.

Mr. David Knott, of the underwriters Sun Alliance, also confirmed that Mr. Haycock could not have successfully insured his property against loss as a result of pollution. He said that in late 1989, early 1990, insurance companies deliberately inserted a clause into policies to exclude all but 8 sudden and unintended pollution. Sun Alliance said that that would preclude any pollution claim of such a nature and that policies before that date would not cover that sort of loss in any event.

The underwriter also said that Mr. Haycock could not claim on his insurance policy, which would cover only third parties who had sustained loss as a result of pollution from his land. He would be covered against a claim against him by his neighbour, but he would not be covered for losses due to pollution on his own land. As only third parties are covered, Mr. Heycock would have no claim against his neighbour's pollution insurance policy even if he had one. We must bear in mind that the neighbour almost certainly would not have such a policy. Even if he did, it is almost certain that pollution over a long period would not be covered.

According to the National Farmers Union adviser, the overriding legal position is that a buyer of land must beware. Mr. Haycock's solicitor, on purchase of the land at the preliminary inquiry stage, could have asked whether there was any reason why it would not be possible to use the land for breeding geese and ducks. If the solicitor had asked that question, Mr. Haycock could now rely on it. However, the chances, that that question was asked are slim.

The reason I have laboured that specific case is because we are dealing with a principle. It is an example of what we predicted would happen during our discussions in 1985. I spoke at length in Committee and on Report predicting the inevitability of such an incident. We argued with Ministers that insurance would not cover those eventualities because insurance companies would not provide policies at reasonable prices. The Government said that they were not prepared to pay compensation because it was a matter for action in the courts between the parties concerned and they would not intervene.

I do not believe that we are talking about vast sums of public money, but I do believe that responsibility falls on the state when, as in this case, the financial livelihood of a man or woman is prejudiced as a result of the introduction of an order. The state must provide recompense, which is what we argued in 1985. When the Minister replies, I hope that he will say a few words on that matter. If he should find that difficult without taking further legal advice, perhaps he will correspond in some detail on those issues.

An article appeared in the magazine Sporting Gun in February 1991 for a machine called the "Clean Machine". The device was designed by an engineer called Mr. Russell Garrod and the article stated:

"Engineer Russell Garrod is all set to let loose an unusual machine on Britain's most heavily used shooting grounds…to recover lead pellets left lying in the soil. Shooting enthusiast Russell, of Eye Hill Engineering near Ely, Cambridgeshire, has spent months and thousands of pounds perfecting his Clean Machine with civil engineer Peter Dunham. And it's now at work clearing up spent pellets at Lakenheath Rod and Gun Club in Suffolk. With impressive results. Lead, wads and broken clay fragments from years of shooting are being recovered and the soil returned to the ground in 'original' condition… The Clean Machine scoops up top soil and feeds it into a massive, rotating, drum which separates out the lead pellets. Stones and other rubbish are fed on to a conveyor belt and dumped into a trailer. Every grain of soil goes back on the land … The great beauty of the machine is that it's commercially viable and environmentally acceptable; shooting grounds can now be kept clean and any 'lost' lead recycled."

The article refers to a number of sites in the United Kingdom that are polluted by lead shot where the machine could possibly be used. I only draw attention to the article because I believe that departmental officials should be JPEG 9 aware of it. If Mr. Haycock finds it impossible to sell his land, it could be argued that such equipment should be used to clean it. If it is possible, I believe that the state and the taxpayer should pay because the order has been introduced by the Government as a result of a problem that occurred through no fault of Mr. Haycock.

Mr. Bob Cryer
(Bradford, South)
: I have joined the Committee, but not because I was selected to serve on it. That great privilege was awarded to its other Members, but not to me. However, I have taken advantage of the right of hon. Members to speak on a Standing Committee, and a very useful right it is.

I speak also as Chairman of the Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments. One of the beauties of that Committee, laid down by Standing Order of the House, is that its remit includes examination of all affirmative and negative procedure instruments.

The terms of the Standing Order do not allow the Committee to discuss the merits of the Instruments, but it has to draw the attention of the House to any defects that it considers they may contain. It has done so on this occasion.

My hon. Friend the Member for Workington (Mr. Campbell-Savours) raised the question of changes in the date of the Committee sitting. I must express appreciation for one change to the original date agreed by the Government Whips, because if it had not been made the Committee would not have completed its deliberations in time for its report to be available to this Committee.

When we raise a point on an order, we must allow the Department time to provide a memorandum. It is only fair to allow a Department the opportunity to offer an explanation. If this Committee has sat on the original date, the Joint Committee would still be waiting for the memorandum from the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food and would not have been able to print it and provide it for this Committee.

When Committees such as this are being established, it is important for them to inquire whether an instrument has completed its consideration under the jurisdiction of the Joint Committee. The Standing Order of the Commons should be changed to resemble that of the House of Lords, which cannot debate an instrument until the consideration is complete. I hope that that explains one change in the Committee date; it does not explain the second. As a result, the eleventh report of the Joint Committee is available to the Committee.

The point that we queried is relatively small, but it raises consequentially two further points. Schedule 4(2) states:

"No person shall move from the designated area any meat or other product derived from drakes, ducks, ganders or geese which are or have been in the designated area."

The Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food would generally interpret "other products" as food products, but it is a wide expression and it could cover feathers, for example, in which there is a commercial trade. Duvets can be filled with feathers, and if Mr. Haycock, the farmer, is in the serious financial position described by my hon. Friend, he will no doubt want to sell any products that he can that are not part of the food chain. However, the order might be interpreted as preventing him from doing so because it refers to "other product" and paragraph (2) does not specifically say, "related to the food chain".

10 In its memorandum, the Department argues that any interpretation should, of necessity, be based on the preamble to the order, which emphasises food products. I stress two points. First, an order is as important to individuals and affects them as much as primary legislation. The vast bulk of legislation that emanates from this place each year derives not from primary legislation, but from the powers that the House gives Ministers by means of delegated legislation. It is, therefore, important that legislation should be as accurate as possible and should not require interpretation.

As our report made clear, our counsel said that the courts would almost certainly interpret "other product" as products based on the food chain. For the phrase to be defined, it would be necessary to go to the courts. We should not put through Parliament, and Ministers should not produce, legislation that is open to such interpretation. The courts are not available to the generality of our people. They are too expensive, and the Government are considering cuts in legal aid that will make them even less accessible. It might cost £300, £400 or £500 to obtain advice from counsel. From the remarks of my hon. Friend, who has done an extremely good job of investigating the background to the order, it is clear that Mr. Haycock is not in a position to obtain an interpretation from a lawyer or to go to the courts.

It must be a general rule of Parliament that when we have to depend on interpretation by the courts, our legislation is a failure. The Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments has drawn the special attention of the House to the order because we consider the situation unsatisfactory. Orders should stand on their own merits and be clear to the users, because they will be used by ordinary people who will be bound by their terms. In this case the user will be a specific farmer in a specific area of land. An order might affect only a handful of people, such as Mr. Haycock and his family, or 1 million people, but the principle is the same.

I am grateful that the Committee deferred its sitting and allowed me to draw the matter to its attention and have it recorded in its proceedings. As Chairman of the Joint Committee for several years, I know that it is a continuing struggle to try to ensure that legislation is satisfactory. We all have an opportunity of participating in primary legislation, but not in delegated legislation. Orders are produced by Government Departments and presented as a fait accompli. In this case, because the order was produced to deal with an emergency there was little opportunity for consultation. I understand that; it is not a criticism. Orders are not amendable—that is one of the deficiencies. There are very few Acts that allow amendments to statutory instruments. Even though it would be easy to amend an order to clarify it and avoid the necessity for interpretation by the courts, we cannot do that.

We do not have a similar provision in our legislation and there is no point in trying to deal with the position as we would like it to be; we must deal with it as it is in relation to the order. I have noted the points made by the hon. Member for Workington and I give him an undertaking that we shall continue to keep a careful eye on the issues. As he pointed out, the orders are used extremely sparingly and only when they are essential. He also said that we are talking about relatively small sums of Government money. The difficulty is that Ministers must not be constrained in undertaking acts of public protection by the possibility of having to pay large amounts in compensation.

Obviously, that represents a serious blow to Mr. Haycock and I appreciate that the points made by the hon. Member for Workington were directed to helping him in the serious and unfortunate position in which he finds himself. The hon. Gentleman will want to know that Mr. Haycock purchased the land in about 1983, but he did not buy it with the intention of farming ducks and geese. I understand that his original farming operation was for sheep. It was only a short time ago that he decided to diversify into ducks and geese and, in doing so, he built a pond on which they could live. The spoil from the pond was JPEG 13 scattered round the farm and the lead shot was contained in that spoil. We accept that that was a most unfortunate turn of events but, as matters stand under legislation, it is for Mr. Haycock and his advisers to pursue the polluter on the principle that the polluter pays.

I noted what the hon. Member for Workington said about the "Clean Machine" and lead shot. We take seriously suggestions that might be of help to us and I know that my officials will have taken note of that made by the hon. Gentleman. We shall draw the attention of the Department of the Environment to that machine to see whether it could be of use. People in the country have strong feelings about clay pigeon shooting, so we wish to keep the matter under careful review.

The hon. Member for Nottingham, East (Mr. Heppell) asked what the Government were doing to encourage the use of non-toxic shot in shotgun cartridges. The Department of the Environment is pursuing an initiative on the substitution of steel for lead in shot for 12-bore cartridges used in wildfowling over wetlands. It is hoped that such shot will be available in commercial quantitites by September 1995 so that the use of lead shot over wetlands can be fully phased out by September 1997.

The current incident arose from entirely different circumstances, that is, from clay pigeon shooting more than a decade previously. We are considering the best medium for giving advice to farmers on protecting stock on land which may have been shot over.

We have had a thorough and considered debate and the hon. Member for Workington has rightly raised important issues relating to the livelihood of Mr. Haycock, for whom we must all have a great deal of sympathy. It remains the view of the Government that the measures that we took to protect the integrity of the food chain were unavoidable, bearing in mind the high degree of lead poisoning. I hope that the Committee will see fit to endorse the Government's stand.

Mr. Soames
: I do not want to delay the Committee. I appreciate the learned Gentleman's point. I must emphasise that there is no provision under the Food and Environment Act 1985 for the payment of compensation. It is not true that the order caused Mr. Haycock's difficulties; the lead poisoning in his birds caused them. More than half his birds have died. Our order has protected the integrity of the food chain, which we are absolutely, irrevocably obliged to do. I hope that, in the light of that discussion and our shared concern and anxiety about Mr. Haycock, the Committee will see fit to support the order.
