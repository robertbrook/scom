Loch Crinan Scallops Fishery Order 1993

10.30 am

Mrs. Ray Michie
(Argyll and Bute)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Loch Crinan Scallops Fishery Order 1993 (S.I., 1993, No. 177).

 I am grateful to the Leader of the House for arranging for this debate to take place. Although it relates specifically to the Loch Crinan Scallops Fishery Order, which was laid before the House on 5 February, it also gives us the opportunity to discuss similar orders and their effect on the coastal waters and lochs of Scotland. I hope that the Minister will understand and accept the spirit in which the debate was requested. Incidentally, in Scotland "scallops" is pronounced as it is spelt; others may pronounce it "scollops". I shall refer to "scallops".

I shall concentrate first on Scallop Kings plc, the company which in 1979 applied for a several fishery order to allow it to grow scallops on the sea bed at Loch Crinan in my constituency. In 1988, £1.7 million was raised from private shareholders under the business expansion scheme. Together with grant aid from Highlands and Islands Enterprise, the money was invested in the development of scallop farming technology—with the result, I am told by the managing director, that the company is now a leader in the field. It is expected to farm up to five million scallops a year. It will provide employment and help to meet the substantial home and export demand for scallops. If it fails, it will leave France out in front. There are similar operations there mainly run, interestingly, by fishermen's co-operatives.

Following the failure of Scallop Kings to reach agreement with four objectors to the order, the Secretary of State held a public inquiry. The most significant of the objectors was the Clyde Fishermens Association. The presence today of the secretary of that association, Mr Patrick Stewart shows how seriously it views the matter. Under section 7 of the Sea Fisheries (Shellfish) Act 1967, the association's normal fishing acitivities would not be permissible within the area described in the fishery order.

This area is of the utmost importance to the fishermen who have carried on their activities there for generations and should continue to do so. The site is sheltered and free from tide and wave action and is the only suitable and safe place for a considerable distance in which they can carry out activities, such as running off their gear, untangling their nets and removing objects such as boulders or mines, which get caught up in them, gutting fish and tailing prawns.

Scallop Kings has never sought to strike at the freedoms of the fishermen. On the contrary, it wants the order to be made in terms that will accommodate all those activities without hindrance. That has been confirmed to me by Mr Michael Stewart, Scallop Kings; managing director.

4 The Clyde Fishermens Association has no objection in principle to several orders, except where the status quo is threatened and there is a disruption of business. In this case, that is what the several order would do. Granting the order would prohibit the activities that I have described. The association does not object to scallop farming in the area as long as that does not interfere with its activity, and the company wishes to be accommodating. The reporter to the public inquiry took the same view and made recommendations to that effect. The Secretary of State has the authority to vary an order under the Sea Fisheries (Shellfish) Act 1967 and to make the necessary provisions to meet the needs of the fishermen and the company.

A several order is far too drastic. Under section 7 of the 1967 Act, the normal fishing activities associated with the area would be prohibited altogether. I hope that the Minister will consider making a regulatory order, or even go back to the Sea Fisheries (Scotland) Amendment Act 1985, which allows him to make byelaws for the purposes of fish culture or experiments in such culture. The Sea Fisheries Regulation Act 1966, which applies only to England and Wales, allows local fisheries committees also to make byelaws with regard to operations in their areas. However, such committees do not exist in Scotland.

I hope that the Minister will consider the matter again. That is the sensible thing to do. The law should be flexible, and the Secretary of State should be responsive. An order should be made that allows the cultivation of shellfish in Loch Crinan, provided that the operations of the fishermen are not proscribed. If this several order is accepted, the Minsiter would wipe out at a stroke centuries of fishing tradition and would hit at small family businesses. Those businesses were there first and surely have the right to remain. The west coast of Scotland is very dangerous. The safety of the fishermen in my constituency is of prime importance to me. Loch Crinan is a safe haven where fishermen can run for shelter and carry out their work.

I shall say a quick word on the wider issue, which other hon. Members will want to take up. There is a fear that many more several orders for scallop fisheries will be granted—there are abut 17 applications in the pipeline. The Minister will accept that the commercial fishing industry has been going through some bad times. Any further erosion of the customary fishing areas would not be acceptable. Fishermen have been squeezed out already by the proliferation of salmon fish farms, the laying of underwater cable and Ministry of Defence restrictions. Large portions of the sea and sea bed are becoming no-go areas. That is a considerable worry to the commercial fishing industry.

I hope that the Minister will give the Committee a favourable answer and that sense will prevail in this part of my constituency.

10.41 am

Mr. Calum Macdonald
(Western Isles)
: I support the hon. Member for Argyll and Bute and my hon. Friend the Member for Dumbarton (Mr. McFall). The Minister should clarify the scope of the several order. Members of the Western Isles Fishermens Association have approached me and expressed anxiety about the precedent that the order would set. The association is worried that ordinary fishermen will be denied access to and use of the area of Loch Crinan that we are discussing. The reporter to the inquiry said that the Secretary of State had the power to make exemptions to the several order. Such exemptions would allow fishermen to continue with specific activities, for example, creel fishing for species other than scallops. They could thus fish for lobsters or crabs. The reporter said that they should be allowed to do that.

The Secretary of State said in his response to the reporter that although ordinary fishermen would not be allowed to undertake creel fishing, Scallop Kings should be permitted to do that. It is odd to ban ordinary fishermen from using creels for non-scallop shellfish, yet allow Scallop Kings to fish not only for scalops but for other shellfish. That is unfair to local fishermen. It sets a bad precedent.

The order is silent on those issues. The first page states that

"—the Secretary of State appointed an Inspector"

 to conduct an inquiry. It goes on to say that the inspector reported that,

"in his opinion the proposed order should be made subject to certain restrictions".

 Nevertheless, the list of restrictions does not encompass all the restrictions that the inspector or the reporter proposed. I hope that the Minister will clarify that.

Why is a several order necessary anyway for this type of cultivation? Scallop farms exist and fishermen can go out and fish for scallops. They can obtain leases from the Crown Estate Commissioners in the same way as salmon farmers. The leases allow them to have rafts moored from which scallops can be found. Other apparatus may also be used to find scallops. There is the same protection that is given to salmon farmers. No one is allowed to remove scallops from a raft put out by a fisherman on a Crown 6 Estate lease or from hanging lanterns. Why do scallop fishermen need further protection beyond that already given to salmon fanners?

Current practice is for scallop farmers to collect spat in a way that local fishermen accept. Spat collectors put out anywhere little bundles of netting which attract the spat, which are taken back to rafts to hang out and grow. Will that practice be allowed to continue—it is accepted by local fishermen—or will organisations such as Scallop Kings be allowed to dive or even dredge for immature scallops outside its several order area and bring them back to its area to grow them to maturity? If that is allowed, the fishermen will think it is deeply unfair.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Sir Hector Monro)
: No.

Mr. Macdonald
: The Minister says that it will not be allowed, but the order makes no provision to prevent it.

Sir Hector Monro
: I shall tell the hon. Gentleman why.

Mr. Macdonald
: I look forward to hearing the Minister's reply.

England already has the several order system to allow this type of agriculture, but there is a different regulatory system. Under the English system, the whole coastline is covered by local fishery committees which have powers to regulate operations around their coastline for up to six miles. When a several order is made in England, there is consulation with local fishery committees. Scotland has no equivalent; there is merely an opportunity to protest or make a submission to a Government inquiry, after which the Government impose an order.

The laying of this type of order raises the question—which has been raised before, but dismissed by the Government—whether similar local fishery committees should be created for Scotland. If the Government intend to proceed with the system of several orders, they must think seriously about setting up local committees so that fishermen can have more say in the handling and planning of future operations.

10.49 am

Sir Hector Monro
: All of us would much rather be standing outside Crinan hotel in the sunshine than be here this morning. But it is an important order and it is right that the hon. Member for Argyll and Bute should be given a reply.

One gets an impression from the hon. Lady and the hon. Member for Western Isles (Mr. MacDonald) that here we have an insensitive Government, introducing an order without any thought for the consequences. That is absolutely wrong. The several order stems from the Sea Fisheries Act 1868—

Mr. MacDonald
: From the 1967 Act.

Sir Hector Monro
: No, from the 1868 Act—I shall come to the later legislation—which was introduced by the lord high priest of Liberalism, William Gladstone. It was subsequently amended in 1967 by William Ross, the then Member for Ayrshire and Bute, Kilmarnock. It was consolidated by two other great radical peers, Lord Kilbrandon and Lord Scarman. So one cannot say that the several order has been a great Tory plot introduced to allow scallop fishing in Crinan.

JPEG 7 The purpose of a several order is to encourage the establishment or the improvement of a shell fishery. It is designed to enhance and improve fisheries, not to reduce or to restrict them. Because such orders extinguish certain public rights, Parliament has provided a procedure which allows full and open consultation. That procedure permits anyone with an interest in the matter to have his or her views considered.

An order must be advertised in draft form in the locality, which has been done. Objectors are given a month to state their views and put their objections to the Minister. If objections are received, the applicant is given an opportunity to resolve them by negotiation with the parties concerned. If he cannot do so, and if the objections are not frivolous or irrelevant, an inspector will be appointed to hold a public inquiry. If, after that procedure, the order is allowed to proceed, the instrument must be laid before Parliament. That is when hon. Members have a legitimate right to pray against it. It is a carefully set-out procedure which the Government take particularly seriously.

The order has been considered with particular care. I have already explained that the legislation stemmed from the last century, but it was amended in 1967. In Scotland, only one order has been passed, the experimental order by the Sea Fish Insdustry Authority. The order which we are now debating is the second one. As there are more in the pipeline, it is right that this order should be given careful consideration.

Several techniques to cultivate scallops have been tried. The applicant in this case—Scallop Kings—has concluded that bottom culture, or ranching, is the way ahead. That involves sowing juvenile scallops on the sea bed. For that technique to be successful, the company needs to have some legal protection for its stock, to avoid them being taken by third parties. That, in essence, is what the order provides. It gives a shellfish farmer legal title to his stock in a particular area and it prevents others from taking or injuring that stock.

It is misleading to describe several orders as landlordism of the sea. If practised properly, shellfish ranching can complement fishing activity. In some cases, the natural spat-fall from a cultivated bed will spread beyond the immediate area and may benefit the public fishery, because it takes pressure off the remaining area.

As the hon. Member for Argyll and Bute will have seen from the designation of the area, it is only a small area—about 290 hectares—and it is marked off by four buoys, so that everybody knows exactly where it is. She is right to highlight the importance and the balance that we must all take in this matter with reference to employment. The hon. Lady and the hon. Member for Western Isles have tried as hard as anyone else to achieve more employment. Scallop Kings anticipates an increase in employment if the scallop fishery develops. As the hon. Lady said, it is supported by Highlands and Islands Enterprise, which shows that it, too, feels that that matter is important.

The applicants have chosen the site with particular care. Its shallow depth is suitable for harvesting by divers, the only method of harvesting that will be allowed. The company already has interests in a boatyard in the area. The order will have virtually no impact. In general, the operation will be environmentally friendly. It will not affect sailing or fishing boats in the Crinan. They can sail with impunity, through the area that is buoyed off. Sailing and 8 other boats can anchor in the area; it is not sterilised and cut off from all operations except for the taking of scallops off the sea bed, which is where the investment is in the future of the fishery.

There is no evidence of a significant fishery in the area. There are two or three creelers; as far as we can ascertain, the output is about 1 tonne. As the hon. Member for Argyll and Bute said, there will be a substantial increase in output, which will bring considerable rewards in terms of employment and improve the economy of the Crinan area.

We should consider the order in a positive way, because it will have so many advantages. We have looked carefully at the difference between a regulating and a several order. The applicant applied for a several order; he does not want to regulate all fishing in the area, only the right to control the stock that he has laid on the sea bed, from which he will develop his income.

The hon. Member for Western Isles and the hon. Member for Argyll and Bute raised the issue of safety. I cannot understand how that arises; as I said, any boat can sail through the area and anchor there. The order will make no difference whatever.

Mrs. Ray Mitchie
: In fact, safety is paramount. Fishermen come into the area because it is a safe haven where they can untangle their gear, gut fish and so on. They need to do that in an area that is free from storms, wind and tide. I did not say that they wanted to fish in the area.

Sir Hector Monro
: The hon. Lady implied that the fact that this was a restricted area would affect the safety of boats, but boats can anchor there or sail through to the mouth of the canal, as many boats do. It is an exaggeration to say that the order will present a major safety hazard; if it does, we shall look at it very carefully.

We have considerable control over the order; I state categorically that if it does not work it can be withdrawn. However, I am confident that there will be no problems. We should encourage this development and consider each application on its merit.

The hon. Member for Western Isles asked about boats lifting and relaying immature scallops. From 1 May, all boats must be registered and have a licence. If, rather than improving the fishery, lifting immature scallops made it substantially worse, that would be unacceptable and we would carefully watch the situation.

Mr. Macdonald
: How will it be watched? I do not understand how the licensing of vessels will help. Other vessels could be paid to do the lifting, take the scallops to the several order area and plant them there. I do not understand how the Minister can envisage such a practice.

Sir Hector Monro
: The fact that the boats must be registered and licensed from 1 May is important, but the several order's purpose is to improve the fishery as a whole, not only within the restricted area. Lifting immature scallops from outside that area and planting them within it would not improve the overall fishery and would not be acceptable.

Mr. Macdonald
: I freely grant the Minister's intention and agree that what he has described would not be acceptable. However, I do not understand how proper monitoring could work or the relevance of the licensing scheme. Scallop Kings has no licensed vessels, but it could hire other boats to do the job. The landing of immature fish JPEG 9 of any species is monitored when fisheries officers check the size of fish offered for sale. The operation which has been described would not involve selling the scallops, merely their transfer from one part of the sea bed to another. I do not understand how that could be monitored.

Sir Hector Monro
: The hon. Gentleman has his view; mine is that such activity could be monitored. If it went on, people would soon know and would deprecate it as something that would not improve the fishery.

Mr. Macdonald
: Some months ago, the secretary to the Western Isles Fishermens Association described to me a conversation between two scallop divers which he had overheard in a local bar. The divers had talked freely about their intention to obtain a several order for a particular area. Their plan was to dive for immature scallops and then to transfer them to their newly granted, several order area, where the scallops would continue to grow. I do not know whether that pair of divers has applied for the several order area yet, but that is the way that people are thinking.

Sir Hector Monro
: That is interesting evidence. I hope that such evidence is brought to the attention of the public inquiries that will precede any further orders. Such inquiries are part of the consultative process which has been gone into so carefully by the reporter and by the officials who dealt with objectors before the inquiry was called.

We must strike a balance on the question whether we wish to make progress. The hon. Members for Argyll and Bute and for Western Isles have tried to provide evidence that the order will have a dramatic effect on fishing, but we must weigh that against the possibility that it will be a great success and a way of regenerating employment and the economy of the highlands and the Western Isles.

After the consultation, the public inquiry and the careful thought that the matter has received, I believe that the order should be accepted. We shall carefully watch the way in which matters develop, and the experience gained from Loch Crinan can be used to decide whether further orders should be approved. As the hon. Member for Argyll and Bute said, about 17 applications have been made from all over the western highlands and as far north as Orkney.

We should take this opportunity to see how matters develop. As I said, if anything goes wrong, the Secretary of State can withdraw the order and consider the matter further. I believe that the order should be accepted, in the interest of the future of fishing in the highlands and the Western Isles. I hope that it will be a positive development.
