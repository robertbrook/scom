Draft Channel Tunnel (International

Arrangements) Order 1993

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Charles Wardle)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Channel Tunnel (International Arrangements) Order 1993.

In welcoming you to the Chair, Dr. Blackburn, let me say that I shall be brief and shall treat the order with as much dispatch as I can, but as it involves a series of technicalities, it may be convenient for me to go through them.

The order provides the legal backing for policing and frontier controls for the channel tunnel and makes all the necessary consequential provisions for co-operation and assistance between the United Kingdom and France. The order introduces no new controls, but new methods of exercising controls that exist already. It is the technicalities that follow from that concept that will be of interest to the Committee. The order will adapt our current procedures to the unique physical circumstances of the tunnel. The tunnel is being built with private money and will run as a commercial enterprise, but the British and French Governments have an important role in supervising the construction and operation of what will be a new, high-profile transportation system.

Within Government the principal responsibility for the tunnel rests with my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Transport and I do not intend to trespass on his responsibilities today. Later in the year he will introduce other orders, under the Channel Tunnel Act 1987, to deal with security and road traffic matters and the House will have an opportunity to debate those issues then. My concerns, and those with which the order deals, and which will interest the Committee, relate to the practicalities of how we shall police the channel tunnel, deal with offenders and carry out controls on people and goods entering and leaving the United Kingdom through the tunnel. The Home Office is just one of several Departments with an interest in those matters. The order encompasses the requirements of the police, the courts and all the frontier control authorities.

Frontier controls in the tunnel will be juxtaposed at the terminals at Cheriton and Coquelles. The framework for operating juxtaposed controls is set out in a protocol signed at Sangatte in 1991. Some of the provisions of that protocol constitute schedule 2 to the order. Entry controls to the United Kingdom will be completed before shuttle passengers board the shuttle trains. Travellers will find juxtaposed French departure and British arrival controls at Coquelles and a similar arrangement will apply in 4 reverse at Cheriton. Passengers on the shuttle trains will go through ticketing booths and then through the departure and the arrival controls in rapid succession in the control zone. The physical layout of the controls will resemble those with which we are familiar, such as the controls at Dover Eastern docks. The area in which officers will be permitted to work—known as the "control zone"—has been agreed between the United Kingdom and French authorities and will be clearly marked.

The principles of juxtaposition also apply to the through trains that will operate between Paris and London initially and, in due course, from destinations that are further afield, although the practicalities are somewhat different. For those services the trains themselves will be the control zone and immigration checks on arriving passengers will usually take place on board. I say "usually" because sometimes, for operational reasons, that may not be feasible and so controls will be carried out at the point of arrival, such as at Waterloo. For a train with a large crowd of unruly football hooligans or some other group, it may make more sense to carry out controls at Waterloo. Arrangements will be made to put the controls into practice when the train arrives at Waterloo. In the case of the tunnel, like that of all our ports of entry we are maintaining passport checks on all non-EC nationals supported by a light check of EC nationals simply to guard against the possibility of abuse of the EC channel by others. We also attach importance to maintaining the checks necessary for the prevention of terrorism. For those reasons the order extends the provisions of the Immigration Act 1971 and the Prevention of Terrorism Act 1989 to cover passengers arriving and leaving through the channel tunnel.

The controls on crossing the frontier through the tunnel will be identical to those exercised at other places of entry. Routine customs checks—as the Committee will be aware—are now a thing of the past and customs checks are increasingly based on intelligence. Border checks on passengers and freight are carefully targeted to prevent the smuggling of drugs, firearms and other illicit material. Similarly, public health and animal health checks will be conducted selectively where there are grounds for suspicion that our requirements are not being met. In that way we shall ensure that the channel tunnel presents no greater risk to the integrity of our port controls than any other point of entry into the United Kingdom

I now turn to the order. The nature of the tunnel system and its division by the frontier—the frontier is at a mid point in the tunnel and, as it were, under the water—creates unusual circumstances in which to maintain law and order. Articles 38 to 41 of the protocol create a special jurisdiction regime for the tunnel. That is necessary because in the United Kingdom our courts enjoy a territorially based jurisdiction. Without the special jurisdictional regime, difficult problems might arise. For example, a passenger may board a train in Paris and discover on arrival in London that his wallet has been stolen. The offence could have occurred in France, in the tunnel or in England. It would be nonsense if when the culprit was detected he could not be tried because the locus of the offence was uncertain. The order lays down clear groundrules to enable the relevant authorities to decide how to deal with suspected offences and, by means of article 3, gives the courts full legal authority to try and to sentence defendants who are brought before them for offences associated with the channel tunnel.

JPEG 5 I shall now focus more closely on the main provisions of the order as they relate to each frontier control authority and describe how they will operate. I shall deal with the police first. British police officers will have two main functions in the channel tunnel. While in the control zone on the French side they will exercise frontier controls, principally under the Prevention of Terrorism Act 1989. On the United Kingdom side of the frontier they will carry out their normal law and order duties. Furthermore, special provisions have been made in the order to meet the particular policing needs of the tunnels themselves, where it may not always be apparent on which side of the frontier an offence is being committed and where stopping a train to deal with it would not be feasible. One of them is contained in paragraph 2(4) of schedule 3 and it gives effect to article 40 of the protocol. Under that section, United Kingdom police officers may make arrests in accordance with English law in a very limited area of France in the fixed link where a person is caught in the act of committing or attempting to commit an offence or has just done so.

Paragraph 2(5) provides a complementary power implementing article 39 of the protocol so that if an arrest is made on board a train in England en route to France, the legality of the arrest is not affected by the fact that the train continues on to French territory. When the train arrives in France the suspect must be handed over to the French authorities for them to determine formally where jurisdiction lies. Reciprocal arrangements will be made by the French to mirror those procedures.

In cases where the French decide that jurisdiction rests with our courts, the suspect may be brought back to England as though he had remained under arrest the whole time. Similarly where an officer has made an arrest in a control zone under the provisions of article 10 of the protocol—one relating to a frontier control offence or an arrest of a person sought by the United Kingdom authorities—he may continue to hold the detainee in the control zone and return to the United Kingdom with him without referring the matter to the French authorities. That is, from the control zone at Coqueller. Those procedures have been specially designed to respond to the particular needs of working in the channel tunnel. Without those arrangements, extradition proceedings would have to be implemented—that would be excessive in the context of the working practices of the tunnel, where officers will be carrying out their duties in a clearly defined and limited area. However, if the suspect leaves the control zone, other than in lawful custody, there will be no question of simply retrieving him from wherever he may be: there is no general power of extra-territorial arrest.

I have spoken about the police and I turn to the powers available to immigration officers. The order modifies the Immigration Act 1971 to apply it to the tunnel. Sub-paragraph (2) of schedule 4 modifies section 3 of the 1971 Act so that a passenger will be regarded as having left the United Kingdom for immigration purposes if he seeks entry to France at Cheriton and is refused it by the French authorities. In those circumstances, when he arrives back at our control point his request for leave to enter will be looked at afresh—just as though he had crossed the 6 channel on a ferry and returned having been refused entry in France, even though all of that will take place within the control zone at Cheriton.

We have also had to introduce for immigration purposes, a new interpretation of what constitutes entry to the United Kingdom by way of the tunnel services. What we have proposed is a simple solution which can be applied to all the train services. The provisions in paragraph 1(5) of schedule 4 are conceptually similar to those which apply at airports. Thus, a passenger will enter the United Kingdom for immigration purposes when he leaves a control area designated as such under the Immigration Act 1971. For arriving shuttle passengers, that will be when they leave the Cheriton terminal. Through-train passengers will enter the United Kingdom when they leave the designated control area at, for example, Ashford or Waterloo or, in the case of those on board a north-of-London train, when the train itself ceases to be a designated control area aafter leaving Olympia on its way to other parts of the United Kingdom. I am looking to the future as I illustrate those possibilities.

The entry point for the purposes of Customs legislation has to be a little different. I hope that the Committee will allow me to proceed at this pace because I realise that I am spraying references at a fair rate.

Mr. Bob Dunn
(Dartford)
: Will my hon. Friend clear up a matter which has been raised with me—the position of an illegal immigrant? If an illegal immigrant is discovered on an aircraft and his or her documentation is not in order, a financial penalty falls on the carrier. Will he explain how that applies to an illegal immigrant who arrives in the United Kingdom by train with out-of-order documents?

Mr, Wardle
: I will certainly answer my hon. Friend's point. First, such a person would not be discovered on an aircraft. His or her illegal status would be established on arrival at an airport. We have carefully considered applying the provisions of the Immigration (Carriers Liability) Act 1987 to channel tunnel trains, but having taken into account the methods by which immigration controls will be applied, we find that that would not be practicable, workable or realistic.

As I said earlier, passengers on shuttle trains will pass through the ticket booth in the departure control and arrival control within the control zone in rapid succession. So the immigration officers can conduct checks and make a decision whether to refuse entry before the train leaves the United Kingdom. That is very different from the position of a ferry operator or an airline.

To return to my reference to Customs, paragraph 10 of part II of schedule 5 provides that goods are treated as imported into the United Kingdom when they are brought into the control zone at Coquelles or when inward controls become exercisable on board the through trains. This is essential, for example, to prevent people detected with illicit goods in the control zone from arguing that they had no intention of introducing them into the United Kingdom but happened to have them on their persons. Presumably, they intended to leave them on the train. Such a loophole would make nonsense of the juxtaposed controls.

The Customs and Excise Management Act 1979 was modified in 1990 at the time of the breakthrough of the service tunnel in order to extend provisions in it that relate to ships and aircraft to trains arriving in or leaving the JPEG 7 United Kingdom through the tunnel system. The detailed changes proposed in this order must, therefore, be seen in the context of the application of that Act as a whole.

The order has many tentacles, which I am seeking to describe. Many of its provisions apply already to trains because trains are included in the definition of "vehicles" in the Act. The excercise of Customs and Excise powers in general has been constrained by section 4 of the Finance (No. 2) Act 1992, so as not to impede the free movement of persons or goods between member states except in certain specified circumstances. Nothing contained in the order will detract from those overriding principles.

I mentioned earlier that, on present plans, the controls on passengers using through trains would usually be carried out during the journey. That will not be so with Customs and Excise controls on the inter-capital services—Paris to London or Brussels to London. For trains terminating in London, Customs and Excise will operate an airport-style static control at the terminal. Waterloo represents a natural break in the journey and Customs and Excise believes that a static control will be to everyone's benefit. The majority of passengers will suffer no inconvenience as they proceed through. Those who are selected for questioning will have more privacy. The needs of Customs and Excise to relate baggage to passengers will be that much easier and the whole operation will be more cost-effective. On the other hand, for the trains destined for regions beyond London where there is no natural break in the journey, Customs and Excise will carry out its controls on board the train. In those circumstances, there is no practical alternative.

Mr. Peter Fry
(Wellingborough)

British Rail is worried about what will happen in the event of an emergency, such as a bomb scare, resulting in the closure of the terminal point. It is clear from the order that there will have to be a suspension of paragraph 26(1) of schedule 4 to enable that to take place. British Rail simply wants the right in an emergency to obviate that. I accept the problem that would arise for Customs, but will my hon. Friend assure the Committee that such a concern is recognised? Can such a change to the law be made fairly quickly because considerable delay will only cause confusion?

Mr. Wardle
: My hon. Friend makes an important point. I am sure that the Committee will want to know that my officials are holding discussions with British Rail about emergency arrangements. Paragraph 26 is not relevant to the circumstances to which my hon. Friend referred. If a train is forced to stop because it has broken down or there has been an accident, the safety and security of passengers will be the first priority.

I welcome the close co-operation between my officials and the railway operators in ensuring that the necessary arrangements are made to meet the needs of all those involved. The point that my hon. Friend made has been taken—please forgive the pun—on board.

I now want to deal with the provisions in the order relating to rabies defence measures and public health. Part II of schedule 3 deals with the measures that the concessionaires will have to take on the United Kingdom side of the tunnel system to prevent colonisation by rodents and to prevent animals from making their way 8 through the tunnel. The physical barriers will help to prevent animals from making their own way through the system. I assure the Committee that they would have to be super rodents to get through because there will be deep-dug security fences based in concrete well below ground. There will also be closed circuit television, not to mention the trains themselves. It would be a persistent and, I think, lucky rodent that managed to get anywhere.

Mr. Toby Jessel
(Twickenham)
: Will the rodent be unable to get through the space through which the train goes? Presumably the rodent will be smaller than the train.

Mr. Wardle
: My hon. Friend makes a most important point. The rodent, but not the train, will be deterred, if not destroyed by an electrified grid that will take account of precisely what he described. The larger risk of the introduction of rabies into this country, which exists now in our ports of entry, comes from those irresponsible people who smuggle animals upon their person or about their baggage. The controls here would be the same as at other ports of entry. Customs and Excise retains the power to deal with illegally imported animals and other authorities will be watchful for any breaches of our laws.

I have described what our officers will be doing at the terminal in England and in the control zone in France, but there is another aspect to the question. By introducing parts of the protocol into English law by means of article 3(2), we are also giving French officers the legal backing to carry out their functions in the control zone in England. The French officers concerned are from the Police de l'Air et des Frontières—the PAF—and the Douane, the French Customs. They will be responsible for controlling persons and goods entering and leaving France on the same basis under French law as at any other of their frontiers.

Article 7 gives effect to one specific provision in the protocol relating to the carriage of firearms by French officers within the control zone. French officers will be allowed to possess firearms in the exercise of their functions within the Cheriton control zone without holding a firearms certificate. The area in which arms may be openly carried, and the attached conditions, are the subject of administrative arrangements with the French authorities. It may be for the convenience of the Committee, Dr. Blackburn, if I say that I believe that the Select Committee examined the matter recently and accepted the principle that I have described. The French police and Customs officers will be allowed to carry firearms on their person only within the control zone—that is, from the booths and on the road from the booths to the loading platforms. At any one time, there will be two police and two Customs officers within that control zone.

In this country, there is no tradition of officers routinely bearing arms. However, we must recognise that the French authorities have such a tradition. We must balance the expectations of their officers while they are actively exercising the full range of their powers by ensuring that they do so only under those circumstances. Should any French officer act outside the exercise of his function and misuse a firearm he will be answerable to our courts.

I mentioned that reciprocal arrangements had been made in France to implement the protocol. The French can achieve that more simply than we merely by incorporating the protocol into French law. To that end, the protocol recently has been approved by the French Senate. If this order is approved, it will be brought into effect jointly on a date to be agreed between the United JPEG 9 Kingdom and France. Although the opening date of the tunnel may be a little way away yet, the commissioning of the transportation system has begun. It is desirable to have these powers available sooner rather than later, to cater for all eventualities.

Mr. Richard Ottaway
(Croydon, South)
: I wish to make a point about jurisprudence. To what extent will existing powers apply? Is this order an example of a belt-and-braces approach?

Mr. Wardle
: It is more than belt and braces. I hope that I have demonstrated that the approach is thorough. However, it applies the controls that are available in the Immigration Act 1971 to new circumstances in which a train will cross a frontier into this country. The order applies those provisions in a way that is relevant to the new circumstances which are physically different from what is involved when airplanes or ferries arrive from across the water.

I have only outlined the order, but I hope that I have touched on its most important elements. I commend the order to the Committee.
