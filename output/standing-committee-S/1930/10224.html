STANDING COMMITTEE ON SCOTTISH BILLS.
Tuesday, 24th February, 1931.

[Mr. JAMES BROWN in the Chair.]

SMALL LANDHOLDERS AND AGRICULTURAL HOLDINGS (SCOTLAND) BILL.

[OFFICIAL REPORT.]
NEW CLAUSE.
—(Protection against damage by game.)

Without prejudice to Sub-section (3) of Section ten of the Act of 1911,—

  1. the tenant of any holding to which the Landholders Acts apply shall, any law, or custom, or agreement to the contrary notwithstanding, have full right to kill and take on his holding any deer, game, vermin, or other wild animals or birds, so far as reasonably required for the protection of his land, and of any crops or other produce of, or on, his holding against injury or damage;
  2. Where a holding includes a right of common grazing over hill ground the landholders holding the grazing rights shall have full right to kill and take thereon ground game so far as reasonably required for the ,protection of their holdings;

Provided that the right hereby conferred shall be exerciseable only by two of their number appointed by them for that purpose.—[ Mr. Scott.]

Brought up, and read the First time [ 19th February, 1931].

Motion made, and Question proposed [ 19th February, 1931], "That the Clause be read a Second time."—[ Mr. Scott.]

Question again proposed.

The UNDER-SECRETARY of STATE for SCOTLAND (Mr. Johnston)
: This question opens up tremendous possibilities for discussion. It is a question upon which there are very strong feelings, and Members on this side have given very strong pledges on the matter. I am going to make a suggestion which may expedite matters. The Clause is in .two parts. The first deals with deer, 1936 game, vermin, and other wild animals and birds, and the second deals with common grazings. We find it difficult to accept the first part in its present form. For example, a landholder is only to be allowed to protect his land so far as reasonably required for its protection. The interpretation of what is reasonably required for the protection of his land would undoubtedly open up very considerable disputations in the law courts, and it is unfair that the small landholder should have put upon him the onus of either proving or defending his action in such a case. Secondly, it would apparently give power for the destruction of wild birds even in the season when they are protected. I could give other reasons, but for those two alone the Clause, in our judgment, is inadmissible.

But there is a more valid reason why it should not be accepted. One of the first steps the Government took on assuming office was to arrange for a complete examination of the deer forest problem, to see how far we could get agreement among the interests concerned to have admitted grievances remedied. At the instance of the Secretary of State, a conference was called at Edinburgh, which I attended on his behalf, representing deer forest proprietors, farm servants, breeders of black-faced sheep, the National Farmers' Union, and every interest we could think of. We had a long and very amicable discussion, and finally I suggested that this joint conference should itself select a small committee, representing all the interests concerned, which should draft the heads of an agreed policy, if any agreed policy could be arrived 'at, which we might put into legislation at once. We have the heads of an agreement from this committee, and the Law Officers are now examining them and the legal consequences which may follow from some of them.

If the Committee would take our advice, they would negative the first part of the Clause and leave the Government and its legal advisers the right and duty of bringing in legislation to deal with this problem. If the Clause is accepted as it now stands, it means that the small landholders will have power to destroy, under certain circumstances, deer, wing game, and so on, which come to invade their crops, but the larger farmers have JPEG 1937 no such power, and you would have this extraordinary situation of so many smallholders dotted about the county with powers to destroy marauding deer, for example, which are not held by their neighbours in other parts of the county. I hope the Committee will agree that this form of Clause will not do and that the problem of marauding deer, for example, is a much larger question which must 'be dealt with nationally and not piecemeal.

With regard to the second part of the Clause, we think there is an urgent necessity to have the matter dealt with, and we hope to deal with it in the Bill. We think the crofters should have the right to destroy ground game upon their common grazing, which is part of the farm—the same power that is held by any other agricultural tenant—and I hope the Committee will leave my right hon. Friend and his advisers in this position, that on Report stage we will bring in a Clause dealing with the second point substantially in the same form as the present proposal, subject to some technical and legal amendments. I hope the Committee will also agree to negative the first part of the new Clause on the understanding that we are dealing with the subject in a much more comprehensive way.

NEW CLAUSE.
—(Amendment of s. 7 of principal Act.)

Section seven of the principal Act shall be amended by adding the following words:—

"Provided that nothing in this Act shall free or relieve any landlord from liability for any compensation payable to his outgoing tenant, and any agreement to transfer such liability in whole or part to the incoming tenant shall be null and void unless a definite maximum limit is agreed to in writing."—[ Mr. Scott.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. SCOTT
: I beg to move, "That the Clause be read a Second time."

This is one of the Amendments suggested by the National Farmers' Union. It is an Amendment of Section 7 of the Agricultural Holdings Acts, 1923, and it refers to the transference of claims for compensation. May I remind the Committee that Section 7 of the principal Act deals with the right of a tenant who has paid compensation to the outgoing tenant, and provides that where the incoming tenant has paid compensation, then the incoming tenant shall be entitled on quitting the holding to claim compensation in like manner. What happens is that an incoming tenant offers a rent on the general appearance and condition of the farm, without any means of knowing particulars of the outgoing tenant's claim. He may offer a high rent based on the good condition of the farm, and when he enters may find himself faced with a claim to pay over again the value of what he has already included in his rent. He may find himself saddled with a heavy compensation c-Mm which he did not anticipate, and I suggest that this provision is necessary to prevent an incoming tenant being saddled with an unknown liability.

"Unless a definite maximum limit is agreed to in writing."

NEW CLAUSE.
—(Amendment of s. 8 of principal Act.)

Section eight of the principal Act shall be amended by adding after the word "tenancies" the words "either under leases or year-to-year tenancies or tacit relocation," and there shall be added at the end of the said section "For the purposes of this section any tenant who is in the tenancy by reason of succession or bequest shall have the same rights to claim compensation as the previous tenant would have had if he had remained in the tenancy."—[ Mr. Scott.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. SCOTT
: I beg to move, "That the Clause be read a Second time."

This Clause is an Amendment of Section 8 of the principal Act, which deals with the provision as to change of tenancy and provides that a tenant who remains in his holding when there are two or more tenancies shall not, on quitting the tenancy, be deprived of his right to obtain compensation under this Act by reason only of the fact that the improvements were not made during the tenancy on the termination of which he quits the holding. The Amendment proposes that in the second line of the Section there should be included certain words, and the opening words of the Section would then read:

"A tenant who has remained in his holding during two or more tenancies either under leases or year-to-year tenancies or tacit relocation shall not, on quitting his holding be deprived of the right of compensation,"

and to add, at the end, the following words:

"For the purposes of this section any tenant who is in the tenancy by reason of succession or bequest shall have the same rights to claim compensation as the previous tenant would have had if he had remained in the tenancy."

The purpose of the Amendment is to safeguard the tenant's claim for compensation to improvements against being invalid because of his entering a new lease. The simplest case, of course, is where a son succeeds his father in a farm and the son takes on a new lease in his own name. The risk at present is, that the son will thereby lose all the rights of compensation, claims to which he would have succeeded if he had simply carried on his father's lease. It is clearly inequitable that he should lose his rights of compensation, and the Clause is intended to prevent that leakage.

NEW CLAUSE.
—(Addition to Second Schedule of principal Act.)

An arbiter shall not appoint a clerk, or law agent, or any other person to assist him, except with the written consent of the parties, and if the arbiter makes any such appointment without such consent he shall do so at his own expense:

Provided that where the consent to any such appointment is withheld by any party the department shall decide whether such appointment is necessary and the terms of, and liability for, remuneration therefor, and such decision by the department shall be final.—[ Mr. Scott.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. SCOTT
: I beg to move, "That the Clause be read a Second time."

May I assure my hon. Friends above the Gangway that this Clause is not in the precise terms which I understand were put forward by the National Farmers' Union. I have ventured to restate the Clause by making it mandatory that an arbiter shall not appoint a clerk or law agent or any other person to assist him except with the written consent of the parties. It may be that I shall incur the displeasure of my professional brethren in Scotland by putting forward this Clause. I have myself acted as clerk in many references. The intention of the Clause is to obviate the appointment of a clerk in most cases.

The LORD ADVOCATE
: Will the hon. Member be willing that the Clause should be so amended that clerks who have drawn fees shall refund them?

Mr. SCOTT
: It is obvious that where no question of law arises there should be no appointment of a lawyer as clerk to the arbiter. In most of these agricultural references the matters are purely practical ones, and the arbiter is perfectly competent to dispose of them himself, unaided, but the regular practice is that an arbiter almost immediately on his appointment appoints a clerk of reference, who is usually a lawyer. That JPEG 1969 is a matter which has caused a great deal of heart-burning among the farmers of Scotland, because of the expense which has been laid upon them unnecessarily by the appointment of clerks or law agents.

I am told of one case in which an arbiter employed a man as clerk to the reference when no points of law were involved and the arbiter's fees were fixed by the Department on an exorbitant scale. I have another instance where an arbitration did not involve more than two days' travelling, and the fee of the arbiter, who was a farmer and valuer, was 14 guineas. That is more than a lawyer receives per day. In addition, the clerk appointed by the arbiter charged 10 guineas. The total cost of the arbitration, including travelling expenses, was £30 4s. It is not for me to suggest the correct charges but, obviously, these arbiters' expenses can be considerably reduced if it is not understood that it is a matter of general practice for the arbiter to appoint a clerk to the reference. Surely the parties who bear the expense of the reference ought to be con-suited, and, assuming that one of the parties withholds his consent, in the opinion of the other party unwarrantedly, it should be open to the other party to appeal to the Department of Agriculture, and, if the Department in its omniscience thinks a clerk should be appointed, it may be possible to do it.

I should like to mention one other case. I have been asked so often for particulars of cases that I should like to justify this one where I am in a position to do it. The arbiter was a civil engineer and a practical farmer, recognised as one of the foremost land valuators in the North of Scotland. He inspected the farm, fixed the amount, employed no clerk and fixed his own fee at six guineas. Naturally the parties to that reference did not grudge the fee. In an almost exactly similar case the Department fixed a farmer-arbiter's fee at nine guineas, and, in addition, the parties had to pay over £8 for his lawyer clerk's fee. It is clear that the expenses of these arbiters have been bounding far too high.

On these two grounds, I hope the hon. Member will see his way to follow what has become almost a habit with him, and withdraw the Clause.

The LORD ADVOCATE

I think the remedy proposed is worse than the disease, because you will spend far more than the amount of the clerk's fee in arguing the thing out before the Department. There is really no substantial grievance to remedy. It is very much better that the matter should be left to the common sense of the arbiter, who will not employ a clerk or a law agent, unless it is necessary for the work.

Sir F. THOMSON

We sometimes find that the clerk's fees and travelling expenses amount to as much as the fee of the arbiter, and the amounts chargeable against those who are arbitrating are very high.
