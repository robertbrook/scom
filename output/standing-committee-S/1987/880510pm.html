The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Michael Forsyth)
: I was explaining why I thought that the hon. Member for Cumbernauld and Kilsyth (Mr. Hogg) was wrong to seek to enlist our support to put on the face of the Bill provisions about the composition of school boards. I was about to reassure him that we would consult on the detail of regulations before they were made.

The proposals for membership of boards are not new. The proposals contained in the paper that I circulated to members of the Committee are the same as those in our consultation paper. It is untrue to say, as some have claimed, that the proposals have been sprung on an unsuspecting world. Let me remind the Committee what we said in our consultation paper in August 1987. The relevant passage reads:

"Boards should be small enough to be effective management bodies. Parents will have a clear majority of voting members. In addition, there will be one (or in larger schools, two or three) representatives of the staff of the school and two or three co-opted members representing the local community. Thus the boards would have between seven and 13 members, depending on the size of the school."

In the course of our consultation, there were few complaints about the boards being too small. Most of the criticisms that we received took the line that was taken by the Opposition in Committee until we reached this point in the debate, namely, that we would have difficulty in finding sufficient candidates to stand. We had little complaint that there was insufficient staff representation. On the contrary, the complaint was that we were making unreasonable demands of staff in expecting them to take part in the work of the boards. 276 Nevertheless, I shall take careful note of the points that have been made by my right honourable Friend the Member for Kincardine and Deeside (Mr. Buchanan-Smith) and Opposition Members.

In the paper that I circulated to the Committee, I described the detail of our intentions. I say "intentions", for that is what they are. I shall give no undertakings now about those intentions, not because the Government are irrevocably committed to the details of the membership structure that I outlined but because we intend to consult widely about them. I shall take note of what has been said here today, and in particular I can reassure my right honourable Friend the Member for Kincardine and Deeside that I shall take on board what he said. I shall also take note of a wide range of views from authorities and other interests. When we have made our decision in the light of those views, the regulations will be brought before the House and it will be open to hon. Members to seek further debate on them, if they wish.

Although I understand the anxieties of Committee members, I am certain that that is the appropriate way to proceed. It would be extremely unfortunate for us now to put provisions on the face of the Bill and to learn from experience later that that was a mistake. The proposal in the Bill provides for opinion to be consulted and for us to learn in the light of experience. Having given these undertakings, I hope that I can persuade the Opposition to withdraw the amendment.
