"These are provisions to which as a Scottish lawyer I take the strongest possible exception."

Those who know Norman Wylie know that he does not take the strongest possible exception to most matters. He is characteristically a man of balance who sees not in black or white, but in shades of grey. That is a virtue in a court of session judge, if not in a Conservative politician, it was unusual for him to use such strong language. "In my career of fifteen or sixteen years at the Bar I can only think of one case in which I had to advise that there was insufficient evidence to enable an action to be raised, and having regard to the later decisions of Cleisham v the British Transport Commission I would certainly not have given that advice today."—[ Official Report, Scottish Grand Committee, 19 March 1968; c. 25.]

Paragraph 2.8 of the Scottish Law Commission report, which forms the basis of the debate, deals with that argument. It reaches a different conclusion from that of Norman Wylie:

"It might, of course, be said"—

it was, and that is the point—

"that as corroboration can, where necessary, be found so readily, the retention of the requirement does little harm. Cases are not difficult to find, nonetheless, in which the Court has regretfully been compelled to find for a party by reason of the failure of the other party to find corroboration. However, we think that a better conclusion is that while on the one hand the requirement may be an impediment to justice, on the other it scarcely serves any useful purpose."

Unlike Norman Wylie, Lord Maxwell and his colleagues concluded that people have been denied justice in a significant number of cases. I do not want to go on interminably so I shall just say a word or two in conclusion about subsection (2), which removes corroboration by false denial. It states:

"Any rule of law whereby any evidence may be taken to be corroborated by a false denial shall cease to have effect."

There was little discussion about that on Second Reading for reasons that I can understand. Few tears will be shed over the removal of corroboration by false denial. The Scottish Law Commission dispatched it from the living law to the limbo of academic law, devoting only one paragraph, paragraph 2.16, to the subject. Few of us will worry over what was described, in connection with a 1934 case, as:

"'at best a doubtful doctrine'"

The only justification for corroboration by false denial was that, by definition, the act that was subject to inquiry was likely to have been conducted in private—it would be remarkable if it had not been—so the likelihood of having corroborating witnesses was remote. It may be that fact and circumstance would make good the lack, but there could be difficulties with corroborating evidence. Thus, the unusual practice arose by which, if the defender in the witness box denied a material fact which was proved to be true, that was then accepted as corroboration of the pursuer's evidence as to a child's paternity.

It is right that that practice should go and that corroboration is no longer necessary. It would be unusual if a judgment were made on credibility, and it would be a brave man who would make it without corroboration of any kind. It is right to simplify and get rid of an archaic and unpleasant doctrine that has hung around from a previous age.

I have come across a quotation that reminds me of what happened in the old days and which deals with the problem of bastardy—I use the word as a technical term.

"Bastardy hath no effect till declarator, which proceeds upon a general citation against all and sundry, at the market-cross of the shire where the bastard dwelt".

That is a primitive way of dealing with such issues, but there are echoes of it in the concept of corroboration by false denial. Therefore, I welcome the fact that it should go.
