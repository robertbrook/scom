Mr. Kirkwood
: Amendments Nos. 74, 60 and 76 are also in my name. Amendment No. 74 seeks to increase the number of members on the Scottish Homes board. Amendment No. 60 seeks to make the membership of Scottish Homes reflect the geographical and political nature of Scotland, and amendment No. 76 seeks to allow the Secretary of State to satisfy himself that the interests of tenants and the housing 304 association movement are represented in the membership of Scottish Homes.

We do not need as protracted a debate on these amendments as we had on the previous group. The issue is clear, but nevertheless important, and the question is how the Government envisage membership of Scottish Homes will be constituted. Strong representations have been made to me that a system of indirect election, through the local authorities or COSLA, for example, should be considered.

Have the Government ruled out the idea of moving even informally and unofficially to a system that would give places to people from the local authorities? Some of the local authorities in the borders have argued strongly for such places, particularly for the rural authorities. As the Minister knows, the small rural authorities have special problems peculiar to them and they fear that they will be frozen out from membership of Scottish Homes. Of course, a system of indirect election would have a fairly rigid effect and I accept that it might entail some difficulties, but I should like the Minister to say whether indirect election to membership of Scottish Homes has been considered.

Amendment No. 73 seeks to ensure that the members of Scottish Homes are resident in Scotland. I think that it is important that they should be. The Minister founded his defence against accepting earlier amendments on the question of precedent, and often it was all that he could plead in aid. I hope that he will recognise that there is quite a large difference between taking people from the Scottish Development Agency and the Highlands and Islands Development Board and bringing people furth of Scotland to serve on Scottish Homes.

There is an argument for bringing someone from England if he has special expertise. There is a prospective application for the post of vice chairman of the Highlands and Islands Development Board from someone in England. I forget his name, but someone has been lined up to take on the job because of his special expertise in finance for small businesses. In such circumstances, I believe that there might be a case for looking for the best man with the best skills available.

However, if the Minister used precedents of that sort to argue that there should be members of Scottish Homes who come from furth of Scotland, it would be wholly unacceptable, certainly to me and, I suspect, to other Opposition Members. Scotland has a peculiar and distinct housing system that has been built up over many years. Given its traditional set-up, it would not be right to bus in people from outside. I hope that the Minister will give us an assurance on that.

I accept that amendment No. 73 might be slightly restrictive, and I shall wait to hear what assurances the Minister can give us if he is not prepared to accept it. I hope that he can go a long way towards saying that there are very few, if any, foreseeable circumstances that would favour somebody coming from England or Wales to sit on the Scottish Homes board, which would be difficult for people in Scotland to accept.
