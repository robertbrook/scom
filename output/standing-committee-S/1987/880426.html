Clause 1
CONSTITUTION OF SCHOOL BOARDS

10.30 am

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman
: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking the following amendments:

No. 70, in page 1, line 8, at end insert—

"(1A) The timing of the establishment of School Boards will be conditional upon adequate financial provision being made to education authorities to establish and support such Boards. The level of financial provision will be the subject of consultation and agreement with the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities.".

No. 38, in page 1, line 24, at end add—

"(6) Notwithstanding any of the above it shall be the right of the parents of a school not to have a School Board subject to the following:


If at the initial establishment of a School Board or at the time of any subsequent biennial election a number of parents, as specified in section 13(1), request in writing of the Education Authority that the functions of the School Board should be exercised by the Authority, the Authority shall proceed to a ballot (in accordance with provisions of Schedule 1) of all parents of pupils in attendance at the school to determine whether a majority of all parents voting in the ballot are in support of the request. Given that there is a majority of all parents in favour of the request, the functions of the School Board shall be exercised by the education authority until such time as a request for a School Board and a ballot in favour are carried out under the conditions specified above and at the times specified above.".

No. 39, in page 1, line 24, at end add—

"(6) This section shall not apply where not more than 42 days before the date prescribed under section 2(3)(c) of this Act the Education Authority has carried out a ballot of all parents of pupils in attendance at schools in its area and—

  1. the parents have been invited to vote 'yes' or 'no' to the establishment of School Boards; and
  2. a majority of the parents voting have voted 'no'.
and such a ballot shall be conducted in accordance with Schedule 1 of this Act.


(7) Where, under subsection (6) above. School Boards have not been established the Education Authority shall every two years thereafter carry out a ballot in accordance with that subsection and this section shall apply if a majority of the parents voting vote 'yes.'."

Mr. Henry McLeish
(Fife, Central)
: Our previous discussion on the amendment was about choice. Discussions with parents, educationists and administrators in my constituency over the weekend have reinforced our arguments. Those people felt that it would be reasonable to include a provision for parents to use their judgment about the school board proposal and, if necessary, to vote not to have a school board in their area but to reaffirm their commitment to the local authority's education provision. Like Opposition Members, they thought that that would not preclude other forms of parental involvement being considered and implemented for particular schools.

I shall pinpoint our objections to the Government proposals and rehearse our arguments in favour of the amendments. The Government have been talking about and promoting the idea of choice in relation to the Bill. Our first objection is that we do not equate choice with forcing parents, education authorities and professionals into a rigid system of parental involvement throughout Scotland. The Government's definition of democracy is only on their terms. People have the right to say yes to Government proposals but not to say no. Our amendment seeks to extend choice and democracy so that if parents in a catchment area do not want a school board they can vote for the status quo and retain the possibility of discussing different ways of implementing parental choice.

Our second major concern is about the politicisation of school boards. The Government do not appear to have considered that aspect, although something was said at our previous sitting about the problem of school boards being hijacked by one political party or another. It is inconceivable that if such boards are established there will not be a degree of politicisation that will both influence control and direct their functioning. It is naive to assume that because the boards are for educational purposes they will not attract the political extremes. A service that has ostensibly been provided by politicians at regional level could become a service that bypasses the normal democratic processes and the professionalism that is essential for our schools, allowing a politically motivated tiny minority of parents to take major decisions on the welfare of children and the direction of schools.

If parents in an area could decide not to be involved in a school board, that would allow them to take on the issue of the politicisation of the boards and to say that they did not want that level of political debate in the school. School boards for comprehensive schools present a particular problem because such large schools and so many parents would be involved. It would be ridiculous to move from a settled pattern of education and accountability to one in which the nature of school board elections and procedures could lead to a form of educational anarchy which would not be in the interests of parents, children or the development of the school.

The Government must take seriously the politicisation of school boards and ask themselves whether it would be a good thing at that level of the political educational debate where there may be a handful of parents on a committee with a majority of decision-making in their favour. People in my constituency have told me that they do not want schools to become immersed in party politics. The Government JPEG 83 run the danger of that happening if they continue to ignore reasonable amendments which are about giving parents a choice on whether they wish to dissociate themselves from this form of parental involvement.

Linked with the politicisation of the debate is the question of how unrepresentative a group may be when the first elections are held. If, in the first elections, there was apathy and few people participated, there could be two consequences. A wholly unrepresentative minority would be involved in the school and, equally important, it could lead to pressure for the political machines in an area to select parents and ensure that when the votes are taken and the decisions made there will be party political representation. That is worrying Scottish parents, and it is certainly worrying us. The Government may say, "We have a vested interest in politicising everything," but that is not necessarily the way forward for a primary or comprehensive school. Real dangers have been flagged up this morning and I hope that the Minister will respond to them.

We have talked about forcing choice upon parents and the politicisation of schools. There is one further important fact that I should mention. The Labour party is interested in consumerism and its relationship with collective provision, but that can best be dealt with if parents have a genuine opportunity to make their judgments known and be accepted by the system. There is great unease within education authorities about the way in which the Government have seen fit to bulldoze this proposal through the consultation process and bring it forward in a Bill. The consumers—the parents and their children—want the best possible education. The key feature of the Government's attack on the state sector in Scotland is largely to ignore deep concerns and systems of education which have endured over many decades. Why are the Government, in their desire to pursue ideological objectives, not willing to accept the idea not only of collective provision being linked with consumer choice, but of the consumer actually having choice and not, as in the Bill and the board proposals, choice only on the Government's terms?

I hope that the Government will respond positively to what is, in essence, a simple and elementary suggestion for improving a bad Bill. It is also common sense. No one on the Government Benches should deny people an alternative choice in this new system of democracy that the Government embrace so enthusiastically. Parents, educationists, administrators and teachers are asking why on earth, if the Government believe the measure to be so popular—despite a consultation process which massively rejected most of what is on offer—they will not allow parents to decide whether they wish to have school boards imposed on them. If the Government accepted the amendment, it would reinforce their collective commitment to this measure. More importantly, it would take choice at face value and allow parents to say, "Yes, we will embrace that concept" or "No, we want to stick by the education authority and develop other ideas which will give a host of parents wider involvement rather than just the narrow spectrum thrown up by the Bill."

That duty is placed on the elected councillors who are accountable to their electors for ensuring that an adequate and efficient local education service is provided. In practice, councillors discharge that duty through the chief officials of their authority—the director of education and his or her 116 staff. The elected members decide the policies and the criteria or guidelines to be applied locally to ensure that education is provided adequately and efficiently.

It is then the duty of the officers to ensure that the policies passed onto them are implemented as elected members wish. In the Scottish education system there is a clear line, not so much of command as of responsibility. It is central Government's responsibility to ensure, through statutory enforcement, that no matter where pupils live, they are able to attend local school and to avail themselves of the opportunities provided by a national education service administered locally. After the education authorities of regional councils comes the next tier of the administrative system in Scotland—the school councils, as they exist until this legislation is implemented. It points to the need to clarify the limits placed on each tier in terms of education management and administration, so that all three tiers make up a practicable totality and a workable system.

The report goes on to make it absolutely clear that the moment a subordinate body, such as a school board, ignores, in whatever way, a superior body such as a regional council, administrative chaos will ensue. The theme of the report is that, if the basic structure of educational administration in Scotland is to survive, the principle of each subordinate tier's functions being closely defined and in line with the tiers above it must be maintained. Otherwise, the schools and the pupils will suffer.

When we consider the powers of school boards under the Bill, we must establish from the beginning that they are subject to the same overriding statutory provisions as the education authorities. Whether the boards' actions are undertaken as agents of the authority, or whether they confer liability on the authority or directly affect the provision of education by the authority, the boards must be accountable to the education authority within the terms of the Education (Scotland) Act 1980.

Therefore, to clarify the roles of the school boards and the education authorities, the Bill should include a 118 statement setting out the statutory position of the boards, and put their public and legal accountability in context. That is why, in the view not only of members of the Committee but of COSLA and other bodies, the amendment should be accepted by the Committee.
