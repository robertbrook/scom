We now come to a series of related amendments, all dealing with succession. It may be best to deal with them as quickly as possible and wrap up that discussion in the debate on the schedule as a whole, but that is a matter for you, Mr. Lambie.

The amendment seeks to allow for succession by a family member other than a spouse where the family has been living in the accommodation for fewer than five years before the death of the protected or statutory tenant. The present Rent Acts allow a member of the tenant's family to succeed to the tenancy where there is no spouse provided, first, that the family member has lived with the tenant for the six months immediately before the tenant's death. The Bill proposes that the tenant should have lived with the tenant for five years rather than six months.

Moreover, by inserting the phrase "in the dwelling-house" into the five-year provision, the Bill says that unless both the original protected or statutory tenant and the prospective successor have lived in the dwelling-house for five years before the tenant's death there is no right to succeed. If a single parent, for example, dies without having lived in his Col. 960 or her home for at least five years, any children old enough to take on the tenancy will be prohibited from doing so and may lose their home as well as their parent. The additional phrase "in the dwelling-house" will have a severely restricting effect, disallowing the succession to a protected or statutory tenancy for family members other than spouses unless the family has lived for five years in that house. A family member other than a spouse can succeed only to an assured tenancy rather than to the greater rights of a protected tenancy, so there can be no justification for further restricting the right of succession.

Far from making a minor change in the rules of succession, the insertion of the words "in the dwelling-house" would result in many people losing their right to succeed. The rule is already being changed to allow succession only when a family member has lived with the protected or statutory tenant for five years, so it is unreasonable to restrict mobility by disallowing succession where the family member may have lived with the original tenant all his or her life, but where the original tenant took up occupation of the dwelling-house less than five years before dying. The changes in the Bill would have serious consequences.

The Minister has already agreed to consider the issue in the context of housing associations, so I live in hope that he may extend the precedent established then into the wider remit covered by the schedule.
