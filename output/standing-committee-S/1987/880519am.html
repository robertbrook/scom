Mr. Henry McLeish
(Fife, Central)
: I beg to move amendment No. 133, in page 6, line 37, leave out subsection (2) and insert—

"(2) The headteacher shall from time to time consult the School Board on how the funds provided under this section are to be spent and shall consider any advice given to him in this regard by the Board.".

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to take the following amendments:

No. 61, in page 6, leave out lines 38 to 42 and insert—

  • "(a) shall from time to time advise the School Board as to how he has spent and proposes to spend the funds provided by this section;
  • (b) shall take into consideration the views of the Board in deciding upon such spending.".

    • No. 159, in page 7, line 8, at end insert—

  • "(5) In the event of the head teacher and the School Board being unable to reach agreement upon the allocation of funds, the matter shall be referred to the education authority for determination.".

    • Mr. McLeish
    : For the first time we reach the meat of the subject since we are talking about an aspect of the school board's work—the clause deals with books, materials and so on. We need to debate this matter because if we do not get it right it could lead to an unsettling initial period for school boards and could throw into sharp doubt the validity of the relationship between head teachers, the education authority and the school board.

    The issue before us is the power of the school board and the role of the head teacher. The clause suggests that, from time to time, the head teacher will be required to put proposals to the board for expenditure on books, materials and equipment. The crucial point is contained in subsection 2(b) which states:

    "The headteacher shall not spend funds on any proposal unless it is approved by the Board."

    Amendment No. 133 seeks to change that subsection by outlining a more realistic set of circumstances. We believe that the head teacher should consult with the board and should consider any advice given about expenditure. That would reflect more adequately how an education authority works at present. It determines expenditure for a school and that allows the head teacher, in conjunction with his senior staff, to work out how that can be spent appropriately.

    412 The danger in subsection (2) is that the head teacher will be caught in a curious position. He is responsible to the education authority from which he will receive his funds. However he must now submit detailed proposals to the school board, which will have a complete veto on his expenditure, unless the board members concur with his proposals. In a sense, and the Minister will probably confirm this, that veto is the school board's first real power.

    I should like to detail some of the dangers in the clause and to point out some of the omissions that are a grave cause for concern unless tackled by the Minister. Amendment No. 133 changes subsection (2) to a consultative relationship. Amendment No. 159 seeks to tackle a glaring omission in the Bill. There seems to be no mechanism to deal with a failure on the part of the head teacher and the board to agree about expenditure proposals. That problem should be resolved in the interests of the school and the pupils.

    The school board should obviously work on the basis of good will and co-operation between the members of the board and the head teacher, but we are dealing with the harsh reality of a school's financial expenditure. Traditionally, head teachers have exercised an important professional and management function in relation to the quality and quantity of schools' equipment, books and materials.

    If a head teacher submits proposals to the board, the board may legitimately express disquiet. What will happen if, after discussion about expenditure proposals, the school board does not accept the head teacher's method of spending or his priorities? I hope that most difficulties can be resolved by discussion, but what happens if an impasse is reached when the head teacher and the board cannot agree? Where are the means of conciliation? Is the Minister suggesting that the local education authority should arbitrate or that another forum should be devised, through regulation, to accommodate any difficulties? Is he merely hoping that there will be no fundamental differences?

    The issue of the time scale is important. Books, materials and equipment are an important part of the curriculum. If there is a long exchange of views and no agreement is reached, how long will it be before expenditure plans are disrupted, materials and books are unavailable and there is an educational muddle? They are the key issues with which I hope that the Minister will deal.

    I wish to dwell on the link between the expenditure on books, materials and equipment and the curriculum that has been devised by the education authority and which, as stated in clause 15, has not been delegated to the school board. How will the Minister ensure that any resolution of conflict can be adequately accommodated within legislation or regulations? It will clearly be a major obstacle to management functions if the curriculum is not delegated to the school board but the power of veto over expenditure to implement the curricular proposals is. There is nothing in the Bill to allay my fears about that. It may well be that the proposals agreed by a board on an expenditure pattern for materials and equipment are wildly at odds with the JPEG 413 curriculum set down by the education authority, it may involve a departure from the curriculum or it may invalidate it because the priorities defined by the education authority are not fully matched by the decisions taken by the school board.

    Another reason for tabling the amendment is the potential undermining of the head teacher's authority. When we discuss clause 9 we can examine what will happen in the future. Until now the head teacher has ultimately been responsible to the education authority, but he will now face severe difficulties when working for two masters. He will be responsible for curricula to the education authority, but responsible to the board for expenditure on materials, books and equipment to satisfy the requirements of the education authority. His role must be enhanced by a clarification of the two issues that we discussed previously—disagreements and the consultative role.

    Another matter arising from the head teacher's involvement is the system of line management and responsibility. We are not trying to make political points about the head teacher's role, but it is reasonable to suggest that there is a problem. The Minister nods approvingly so I have obviously caught his imagination with the moderate tones with which I have put forward my views. I shall continue in that vein.

    If managment responsibilities in the schools are to be taken seriously it would be reasonable for the Minister to accept that a consultative link between the head teacher and the board may be more productive and meaningful—at least in the first two or three years. Such a link would overcome the problems about the definition of roles and allow the boards to establish some crucial credibility for their decision-making processes. We are anxious about such matters.

    It is interesting that the problem of who ultimately decides on books and materials emerged during discussions on the Education (No. 2) Act 1986, which covers England and Wales. Under that Act, the education authority can delegate responsibility to governing bodies to look after expenditure on books, equipment, stationery and materials. An interesting section of that Act allows a school's governing board to delegate full responsibility to the head teacher and to empower him to take all the decisions on books, equipment and stationery.

    I do not think for a minute that the Minister will accept the amendment, even if he is generous. However, that English legislation assumes that there may be significant problems concerning discussion and agreement on expenditure. It provides a built-in opportunity for the board to acknowledge that and to delegate a specific responsibility to the head teacher, to oversee discussions on books, stationery and materials.

    If the Minister is not convinced I draw his attention to that aspect of the Education (No. 2) Act that at least accommodated some of the fears and anxieties expressed when it proceeded through the House. It captures some of the main arguments that I have tried to outline this morning.

    414 An unstable situation could emerge. We want the professional integrity of the head teacher to be accepted. As a councillor, I have had run-ins with head teachers. Sometimes they assume absolute powers in certain areas and that causes anxiety to parents and others. A head teacher's professional integrity and serious management responsibilities must be acknowledged. Establishing a consultative link between head teachers and boards would satisfy the basic requirements of the expenditure programme. It would allow for the great potential for conflict that will exist under the new system between the education authorities and the school boards. It will enable the establishment of a system of school boards whereby the ultimate powers are not absorbed and accommodated too quickly. The boards can learn to crawl before they walk. The danger is that we shall create a system with built-in weaknesses and conflicts. We could alleviate that further consideration now.

    With those comments I support the amendments, in particular, amendment No. 133.
