As he mentioned that there is a dearth of such books in the House, I may be able to save him some time.

The words "without prejudice" are commonly used in the context of correspondence between parties who are in dispute and specifically in connection with statements on the position of the writer in relation to that dispute. The letter will usually indicate an offer to settle on a "without prejudice" basis and it may contain factual information relating to the Offer.

I said to Opposition members that the inclusion of the words "without prejudice" did not mean that the letter could not be relied on in any subsequent proceedings. That is the case. I am glad to see the hon. Member for Angus, East (Mr. Welsh) in the Committee, and I draw his attention to the case of Watson Towers Ltd v McPhail 1986, Scots Law Times, page 617. In that case it was held that the terms of such a letter containing the words "without prejudice" amounted to a statement of fact rather than a hypothetical admission or concession to secure a settlement. The court held that the words "without prejudice" did not protect the letter from subsequent use as an admission of the facts disclosed in the letter.

I suggested to the Committee that a "without prejudice" letter could not be regarded as binding on the writer. That again is the case. In circumstances where parties are in dispute and an offer of settlement is made, the "without prejudice" reference means that the letter is prevented from being regarded as binding on the party making the offer. That offer can therefore be withdrawn at any time and could not be relied upon in subsequent proceedings, I think that that is what the hon. Member for Garscadden had in mind when he took a somewhat sceptical view of my position on Tuesday.

The clause is essentially a useful extension to all business records of provisions that at present apply only to certain limited categories.
