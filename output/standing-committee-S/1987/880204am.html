Mr. Kirkwood
: I hope that I can dispose of the introduction of the amendment expeditiously. Given the wide remit proposed for Scottish Homes and the diversity of interests that should be represented by its membership, which we have already discussed, it is essential to safeguard the representative character of the membership. It should represent the various interest groups. The Scottish Federation of Housing Associations, has argued that it would make sense to have at least nine people on the board of Scottish Homes. According to the logic of that, the quorum should be no less than half the minimum number of members. Irrespective of how many members there are on the board, it is vital that they should not meet and take decisions unless all the interests of which it seeks to take stock are represented. It makes sense to insert on to the face of the Bill a minimum quorum, which the amendment seeks to do.

The Minister should make clear the Government's position. If he is unable to accept the technical wording of the amendment, I should like an assurance that the regulations or guidance or whatever flows from the primary legislation will make it clear that the Secretary of State will insist that there is a proper and adequate safeguard for the number of people who will constitute the board of Scottish Homes.

Hon. Members will appreciate that the amendments could lead to practical difficulties depending on the numbers of members that Scottish Homes has and how it decides to organise its business. Although I am sure that the major decisions of Scottish Homes will be the subject of majority decisions of the board, it seems unwise to require all board decisions to be the decision of a specific and substantial number of members. Before I speak about the amendments, I should say a little about the sort of people we expect Scottish Homes' members to be. So that it can make the substantial impact on Scottish housing that we envisage, we expect its members to have relevant experience and proven ability. Such people will inevitably come from backgrounds and, in many cases, have interests of direct value to the new body. We cannot expect them to become willing to become members if we require total divestment of those interests, but we can expect prospective members either to dispose of interests which are prejudicial to their work for Scottish Homes or not to take part in decisions.

The Bill makes such provision in paragraphs 4 and 15 of schedule 1. Paragraph 4 places a duty on the Secretary of State to satisfy himself that members do not have prejudicial interests before appointment and requires him to confirm from time to time that the member still has no such interest.

Paragraph 15 deals with individual decisions of Scottish Homes, and not only requires a member to declare an interest but explicitly precludes that person from taking any further part in the deliberation or decision, subject to the ability of the board to decide, in relation to matters not involving 376 contracts, that the interest is not prejudicial to the member's continued participation. I cannot see that there is a deficiency in those arrangements which warrants the change proposed in amendment No. 62. I do not think that a register of member's interests as proposed in amendment No. 63 would add usefully to the comprehensive controls which I have already described. The provisions of paragraphs 4 and 15 of schedule 1 are modelled closely on those in paragraphs 2 and 13 of schedule 1 to the Scottish Development Agency Act 1975. They are adequate for the SDA and they will be adequate for Scottish Homes. I see no merit in creating a patchwork quilt of different statutory controls in relation to similar bodies.
