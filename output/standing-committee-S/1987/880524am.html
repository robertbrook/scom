Mr. Norman Hogg (Cumbernauld and Kilsyth): I beg to move amendment No. 177, in page 9, line 29, at end insert—

  • "(h) the function of consideration of matters relating to remuneration of staff and conditions of sevice;
  • (i) the function of administration and co-ordination of general education policy;
  • (j) the function of the operation of appeal committees set up under section 28(d) of the Education (Scotland) Act 1980;
  • (k) the function of granting of allowances, bursaries, and other financial aid to assist persons to take advantage of educational facilities;
  • (l) the function of opening and keeping Records of Needs in respect of children or young persons who have pronounced specific or complex special educational needs in the terms of section 60 to 65F of the Educational (Scotland) Act 1980;
  • (m) any other function relating to professional areas or to the management functions which extend beyond the interests of an individual school.".
To some extent, amendment No. 177 is a probing amendment. We want to know a little about what is in the Secretary of State's mind. As a trailer to that, in his speech on Second Reading on 2 April, he said:

"The House may be interested in the sort of functions that a board might obtain if it had the approval of parents as a whole. I can give examples, which are not meant to be an exhaustive list. It could seek powers over the responsibility for selecting staff below the level of assistant head teacher. It could seek responsibility for charging for the use of school premises out of hours, or for maintenance of school premises. It could seek responsibility for discipline and school rules, and for associated matters, and for determining the format of reports to parents. Most importantly, a board could seek the responsibility, if it so wished, for all matters covered by the running cost budget for a school."—[Official Report, 12 April 1988; Vol. 131. c.41.]

That list may not have been exhaustive, but it was extensive. I should like to hear more from the Minister about that matter when he replies to the debate.

The amendment seeks to ensure the provision of adequate education. It is important that certain functions are not delegated to school boards but are retained as education responsibilities. The provision of education for children with specific of complex educational needs illustrates that point.

The agencies involved in the assessment and diagnosis of a child's special educational needs operate on a regional basis. We have referred to that point before. For example, the child psychology service, which is well-developed in parts of Col. 484 Strathclyde and other regional authorities, is founded on a regional basis. Likewise, the advice given to head teachers and heads of departments in secondary schools on aspects of their work is given by specialist advisers located at regional level, as is the management. The management is outwith the school: ultimate management and responsibility for the school lies with the education authority.

This proposal could lead to confusion and loss of efficiency if such agencies have to play their part in a locally operating procedure. I cannot believe that that is the Government's intention. However, the Secretary of State more than hinted at that—he was quite clear about it—in his Second Reading speech.

The main trend in the provision of special education has always been towards integrating into mainstream schools children with special educational need. That is absolutely right. Children with special educational needs frequently incur extra expense for schools, such as the provision of ramps or specialised equipment. If school boards were given power to decide who should be integrated into their schools, they might decide against the integration of pupils whose needs require considerable expenditure. That would represent a serious backward step in the development of special education.

As education authorities—not school boards—are the employers of staff, it is appropriate that matters involving teachers' salaries and conditions of service should be dealt with by them. I am sure that the Minister is not proposing that such matters be taken out of the national context. However, to make certain, we should like to help the Minister by inserting this amendment.

Management lies firmly with the regional authority, which should be in charge of administration and co-ordination of general educational policy, the operation of the appeals committees set up under the Education (Scotland) Act 1980, and the granting of allowances, bursaries and other financial aid to assist people to take advantage of education facilities. It should also be in charge of opening and keeping a record of needs of children or young persons who have pronounced specific or complex social and education needs under section 65 of the 1980 Act.

It is important to get these matters right, and that those who serve on the board are clear about what the board is to do. Any confusion over that will detract from the effectiveness of the school boards system that the Government seek to introduce. The list in clause 15 does not go far enough, and our additional list is intended to clarify matters. I hope that the Minister will say that he has accepted our arguments and will accept the additional paragraphs. If so, he will have added to the quality of the legislation and clarified the role of those who will serve on school boards. I am interested to see how the Minister views the development of the delegated functions of school boards. I am not too enthusiastic about the list that he set out on Second Reading.

This is a probing amendment, although it is intended also to improve the Bill. I hope that the Minister will see it in both lights, and that he will be helpful in going through the list.

The amendment represents a confused collection of points. I shall go through them item by item, as the hon. Gentleman requested.

First, on remuneration and conditions of service, the education authority will remain the employer. We do not intend that decisions on the determination of teachers' salaries and conditions should be delegated to boards. They are matters for a contract of employment. Under the existing provisions of the clause, the board is not the employer of school staff. Decisions about national terms and conditions could not be delegated because, as the hon. Gentleman said, they are not authority functions. However, the amendment goes further than the hon. Gentleman suggested. It seeks to forbid boards considering terms and conditions. Why? Authorities may find it helpful for boards to consider the issue and communicate their views to the authority.

Secondly, the hon. Gentleman wishes "general education policy" to be removed from the arena. That is misconceived. Even if an authority contemplated such a delegation, it would be impossible. The Bill allows delegation of functions only in relation to a school and general education policy is not a function in relation to a school.

Thirdly, if the Opposition wish to exclude parents and teachers from forming part of an appeal committee for decisions taken in a school, they can rest assured. That is already impossible by virtue of the Education (Scotland) Act 1980. In any case, boards would not be involved in appeal procedures by "delegation", as section 28(d) of the 1980 Act puts a duty on authorities to form appeal committees, and gives them power to call on parents and others to serve on those committees. It is as a result of the authority carrying out its existing functions that such committees are formed. Delegation has nothing to do with it.

The fourth item about which the hon. Gentleman was worried was the granting of financial aid. He did not say much in his opening remarks, and I do not understand why he wishes to prevent authorities from delegating detailed local management of assistance schemes. If boards were willing to take on those chores, I should have expected authorities to be delighted.

Fifthly, on the record of needs, I appreciate the hon. Gentleman's intention, but I assure the Committee that there is no need for concern. A record of needs is a complex, professional assessment, which is made to assist in determining what schooling is appropriate for the child involved and to which school or schools he should be sent. It is by definition, not a function that relates to a particular school. Therefore, that function could not be Col. 486 delegated—even if a board wished to seek such delegation.

The last item in the amendment gave the game away. Having gone through the motions of trying but failing to find something that could be justified, the hon. Gentleman resorts to the phrase "any other function". I assume that it is woolly rather than mischievous drafting, because the hon. Gentleman would never be mischievious. It would appear to prevent the delegation of anything at all. It would encompass almost anything done in a secondary school, that could be said to extend to the interests of its feeder primaries. This part of the amendment is clearly tailored to prevent co-operative working between boards. Only a few clauses ago, Opposition Members told the Committee how keen they were to promote that.
