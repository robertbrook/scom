School Boards (Scotland) Bill

Clause 5
EDUCATION AUTHORITY OBVSERVERS AND HEADTEACHER

Amendment moved [this day], No. 108, in page 4, line 35, at end insert—

"(6) In exercising his responsibilities in relation to the activities of the School Board the headteacher will remain accountable to the education authority in accordance with the terms of the relevant Contract of Employment.".— [Mr. Norman Hogg]

4.30 pm

Mr. Norman Hogg
(Cumbernauld and Kilsyth)
: I had more or less finished what I wished to say this morning, Mr. Knox. The intention of the new subsection is to confirm the accountability of the head teacher to the education authority, and to avoid creating conflicting responsibilities which could jeopardise that arrangement. That is the essence of our intentions. I hope that the Minister will feel persuaded to take on board what we propose.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Michael Forsyth)
: I regret that on this occasion I shall not be my usual helpful self to the hon. Member for Cumbernauld and Kilsyth (Mr. Hogg), but I may be able to reassure him. As a result of the tutorial that the hon. Gentleman gave the Committee the other day, we know that the basic duty of a head teacher is to his employers, and it is to implement the authority's policies and the procedures necessary for the discharge of the authority's statutory duties. The Bill does not weaken the head teacher's contractual relationship with the authority, and it maintains a proper degree of independence from the board for the head teacher. The amendment is therefore unnecessary.

We intend the head teacher to occupy a key role as the board's principal professional adviser. He will have a duty to explain clearly the authority's policies, and to help the board to evaluate its plans in the context of those policies. It would be contrary to the spirit of the Bill if a head teacher's contractual obligation were ever to reduce him to becoming a mouthpiece for the authority's directions in relation to its dealings with the board. I know that the hon. Gentleman would not want that to happen, so I can reassure him about the head teacher's relationship with the local authority. On that basis, I ask the hon. Gentleman to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Norman Hogg
: It is not my intention to press the matter further, for reasons that must be all too 370 obvious. We may return to the matter at another time, but for the moment I accept the Minister's explanation. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

A clear responsibility is laid at the door of the education authority for whatever the board decides to do. In respect of contractual relations with third parties, the amendments provide that where the education authority incurs liability to a third party arising out of the action of a school board, the authority shall have a right of relief against the school board.

    As the Bill stands, there is an unrestricted guarantee by the education authority for whatever the school board may do, yet the authority has only limited control over what a board may do. For example, a school board could enter into a contract with a third party for grounds maintenance or for the supply of books and materials. If the board is found to be in breach of that contract, the education authority—not the school board—is held liable for that breach of contract. That means that third parties need not worry about entering into agreements with school boards, because there would be a guarantee of payment by the ratepayers—or, eventually, poll tax payers. School boards do not have to worry either, because their members would have no personal liability for any default of which the board may be guilty.
