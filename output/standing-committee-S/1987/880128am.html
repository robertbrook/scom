Mr. Kirkwood
: Thank you, Mr. Martin.

The amendment seeks to ensure that the economic environment is considered as part of any housing-led regeneration. The justification for it is that we believe that area renewal creates an opportunity to improve both the physical environment and the quality of life. It is an attempt slightly to widen the horizons of the new agency and make its powers under the clause more flexible. It is in the Government's interests that they should consider doing that. There is no compulsion, and no additional difficulty will be laid on the plate of those organising the developments. Therefore it is a sensible suggestion at least to consider. It is essential that economic considerations and, in particular job opportunities are taken into account alongside and given equal status to the physical, social and recreational aspects of areas being considered for renewal. The whole area should be treated as a package.

The housing association, certainly in my area, has its roots in the local communities and has quite a good track record for becoming involved in the community which it seeks to serve. Indeed, I can think of one case in Glasgow, at Queens Cross, where the housing association has sponsored community businesses as part of its package of redevelopment, to the great benefit of the local community, the association and those running the community businesses.

I am very keen on community businesses. I have been instrumental in trying to set one up in my own constituency in Hawick and I know how positive they can be in getting people, particularly the long-term unemployed, back to work. But it is so much easier if that housing association involvement means that recreational, social and accommodation facilities go cheek by jowl with small-scale 136 workshops, because they all come together to supplement the development in a way that cannot otherwise be achieved. It encourages people to become involved in community businesses as well as physically providing homes for them.

The amendment would ensure that exciting and successful developments of that kind that have been undertaken by far-sighted housing associations and community businesses in the past should be enabled to continue. I hope that the Minister will think carefully about the amendment. It would not cost him a great deal to concede it. On the other hand, it would make a significant difference in the specific situations that I have described and could spawn a much wider move in this direction, which would be beneficial to communities throughout Scotland. I hope that the Minister will consider the amendment on that basis.

    I am sure that, in some areas, Scottish Homes and the local authority will have to work closely together to develop a strategy based on a thorough assessment of the facts. However, I doubt whether local authorities would welcome the imposition of such a requirement to agree methods of assessment. Indeed, I doubt whether a "requirement to co-operate" could ever be successful, and I advise against the amendment.

    I am sure that Scottish Homes will be a valuable partner for local authorities in carrying out those statutory duties. The Scottish Special Housing Association proved to be a major force in Scottish housing in exactly that role. In another area the Housing Corporation in Scotland has done useful work in assessing the part that housing associations can play in providing for Scotland's future housing needs. Scottish Homes will inherit both those components. It would be wrong to place statutory JPEG 171 duties on two separate bodies, local authorities and Scottish Homes. Honourable Members will appreciate that such dual responsibility would be unworkable in practice.

    Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
    : The cost of the local authority surveys will be supported either by rate support grant or by housing support grant, depending on whether the local authority has decided to charge the expenditure to the general rate fund or to the housing revenue account.

    On the point about COSLA, I have made it clear that local authorities have ample powers to carry out any survey or inspection of their areas to establish housing conditions. The Government are assisting that task by the preparation, with COSLA, of a local house condition survey kit, which will be available this year to give practical help to local authorities in carrying out surveys. We are not, as yet, convinced that a larger survey would yield results at below district level unless the sample size was large, and consequently expensive. However, we are considering further the question of a national survey, and there the matter rests.
