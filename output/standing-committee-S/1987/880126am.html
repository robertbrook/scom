There is also a desparate need for the Government to tackle the growing problems that beset the public rented sector, housing as it does in Scotland about half of all households. It is the needs of those tenants that have been sadly neglected by the provisions—more accurately the lack of provisions—in the Bill.

Many housing bodies' organisations have responded to the consultative document, the White Paper and the Bill. For example, Shelter has drawn attention to the decline in the quality and quantity of houses available for rent in the public sector. That decline has occured at the same time as an increase in the demand for such houses, thereby creating a massive housing problem, compounded by a JPEG 51 Government investment record described housing experts as ludicrous and emasculating.

The Scottish Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux has warned against the Bill's failure to develop and provide a comprehensive housing service in Scotland. It has especially drawn attention to the persistence of long council house waiting lists and the Government's failure to give the required level of funding to solve Scotland's housing problems. Age Concern Scotland has said that it is deeply worried by the Bill's failure to deal with the quality of Scottish housing and what that will entail for those pensioners trapped by their reduced circumstances in inadequate housing.

So it goes on. Almost everyone connected with housing in Scotland except those responsible for the Government's policy has concluded that the Government have failed to tackle the housing crisis by directing investment to the public rented sector. Far from matching investment to the growing housing need and demand, the Government have cut expenditure on that major housing sector. That phenomenon was highlighted by my honourable Friend the Member for Glasgow, Garscadden (Mr. Dewar) on Second Reading. He noted that the Government's contribution per public sector house had been slashed from £238 in 1979–80 to £52 in 1986–87. As my honourable Friend pointed out, that is leading to higher rents and to a squeeze on housing authorities' ability to provide quality management and maintenance services and a deterioration in the fabric of the housing stock.

That point has been echoed by many others, including Professor Maclennan of Glasgow university centre for housing research. To prevent the Committee being tempted not to take him seriously, I stress that his name is Duncan Maclennan, not Bob Maclennan. He identified the under-investment in new building and rehabilitation programmes during the past 10 years as the single greatest factor in the current housing crisis. The Government are responsible for that. No one, except Government Ministers or the lackeys on the Tory Back Benches, would say that the professor was wrong.

Shelter points to the growth in the public sector waiting list which has increased by 35 per cent. between 1981 and 1986. That is one of the prime indicators of the severity of the housing crisis. It also points to unacceptable overcrowding. More than 1.25 million Scots now live in overcrowded conditions. That is a quarter of Scotland's population. Such overcrowding has not been seen in other parts of the United Kingdom for the past 20 or 30 years, but it persists in Scotland to this day. All Governments should be worried about that who have a genuine concern for people's housing needs.

Shelter also points out the problem of pervasive dampness in Scotland's housing stock and to the serious health risks, especially for young children. A study carried out by Edinburgh university showed that bronchial symptoms, diarrhoea and general 52 aches and pains were more often reported among children living in damp homes than among children who were not so disadvantaged.

Depressing as the picture is of the acute housing crisis, there is reason to believe that Shelter has under-estimated the extent of the problem. The appendix to the Shelter report shows that the Dundee district council waiting list doubled from 3,486 to 6,995 between 1981 and 1986. Yet last week I was told by Dundee's director of housing that the current waiting list was 17,000. Perhaps not all of those 17,000 people are in acute housing need or living in grossly overcrowded accommodation. But for one reason or another they are deeply unhappy and dissatisfied with their current housing to the extent that they are actively seeking to remedy the matter. Their only means is through the council waiting list.

There is no evidence in the Bill that Scottish Homes has been equipped or is capable of dealing with the real housing needs of those hundreds of thousands of Scottish tenants.
