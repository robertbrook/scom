Mr. Kirkwood

The Committee would be well advised to spend a moment considering the implications of the duty placed on Scottish Homes by the Bill to consult local authorities, housing associations, other housing providers, tenants' organisations and the relevant voluntary organisations and also its duty to publish 464 an annual investment strategy taking account of representations. So far as I can see, there is no such explicit obligation in the Bill. Given the crucial role of Scottish Homes and the size of its budget, it is essential not only to continue the Housing Corporation's common practice of issuing an annual investment strategy but to strengthen that through the Bill by statutory and stipulated consultation on its content with the range of bodies suggested in the amendment.

In view of Scottish Homes' enhanced role and bigger budget and the Housing Corporation's common practice of issuing an annual investment strategy, it is not unreasonable to spend some time considering the range of organisations that Scottish Homes might be obliged to consult during that enhanced consultation process.

The amendment seeks to do no more nor less than that, and it will be interesting to hear what the Government have to say. One of the Minister's favourite excuses—that of precedent—is involved here because the Housing Corporation's common practice covers at least the principles enshrined in the amendment.

I tabled amendment No. 58 so long ago that I cannot remember why I did so, except perhaps to take a side swipe at the Secretary of State who has sweeping powers under subsection (9). More seriously, the equivalent powers that the Secretary of State already has in relation to the Housing Corporation are much more constrained when one considers the extent of the Housing Corporation's remit. Subsection (9) as it stands is rather sweeping. The Government should justify these extra, unfettered powers to be used by the Secretary of State.

On amendment No. 121, my honourable Friends and I feel strongly that it would be advantageous at the beginning of each year for the budget for Scottish Homes to be broken down to set the tone and the strategy of how the resources will be deployed. The Minister will argue that he wants to give maximum flexibility and as much power as possible to the board, the chief executive and the chairman to do the best that they can in the circumstances. That is a reasonable argument. Some of my hounourable Friends, however, fear that the ability of Scottish Homes, which has no guidance about how the relative priorities should be set in terms of the financial allocations within the budget, is too loose ab initio. No one has any idea how the balance of the allocation of funds will fall. It is right for the Committee to spend a moment or two considering whether it is prepared to allow the membership of Scottish Homes to go completely off at a tangent. In terms of financial allocation, Scottish Homes has power to put all its eggs in one basket and starve other important bodies in the housing sector of the finance that it will have at its beck and all without recourse to anyone whatsoever. The situation should be considered for that reason if for no other.

What concerns me more than anything—it derives from legal experience—is the potential conflict of interest that may arise. As I understand it—I am JPEG 465 always willing to be put right about matters if I have misunderstood them—Scottish Homes will be bidding, for some of the money and using it directly, spending the cash as well as allocating it to other agencies and housing associations. How can it be allowed to dish out money on the one hand while on the other accepting and spending money without any guidance or framework for the balance of the budget allocated to it year by year? If I am wrong, I shall be pleased to be put right. There is a combination of two factors, the wide powers given to Scottish Homes by the Bill and the potential conflit that could lead to claims or suspicions or partiality. I do not wish to impugn the standing or integrity of those who will be elected to the board, but those are important questions.

It would also be sensible and more reasonable for the Committee to ask for broad guidelines to be put into the Bill. We should like broad categories put clearly on to the face of the Bill to which Scottish Homes would have to adhere. They would not be unduly restrictive, because they would be the only categories within which it could operate. If the Secretary of State each year were to say that, plus or minus a margin, which could be 10 per cent.—I am willing to listen to the arguments—within a broad category the allocation of the budget should be fixed, we should know the ground rules and people outside would have an idea where the balance of the finance would go. We should be able to watch that change. No doubt the priorities will change. That will be reflected by the Secretary of State at the beginning of the year in setting out broad categories of finance within which Scottish Homes should work. I hope that the Committee will spend time discussing these clarifying amendments.

Dissolution of SSHA and transfer of its property, rights, liabilities and obligations to Scottish Homes

Mr. Home Robertson
: As is well known, the Bill will have far-reaching implications for the tenants of 83,000 SSHA houses. This point was clearly brought home to me during the latter part of last year when I had a series of opportunities to meet representatives of those tenants in my own constituency. There are 1,150 SSHA tenants in East Lothian. Those tenants left me in no doubt about their anger at the Government's proposal to impose radical changes on the structure of their landlord organisation and on the future arrangements for their tenancies.

It was quite an achievement to get so many tenants to rally round their landlord as those SSHA tenants have done, particularly—as in my constituency—when such tenants find themselves in identical houses and often in the same street as local authority tenants, but paying up to £20 a month more rent. Nevertheless, they were so alarmed at the proposals in the original Scottish Homes consultation document that they were motivated to activate tenants' associations and campaigns to resist the Government's proposals.

It is coming through loud and clear that tenants want to remain in the public sector. Indeed, if they were compelled to leave their current landlord and the SSHA, I believe that most of them would prefer to be transferred to district council tenancies rather than be squeezed into the private sector as the Government evidently intend. They are certainly violently opposed to the prospect of being levered into the private sector according to the Government's original proposals.

We understand from those proposals that existing tenants will remain secure, but there must be some doubt about the future, given the Government's stated long-term aim of reducing the public sector of housing in Scotland and encouraging private landlordism. The Government must reply to that, because they have been all over the place on this issue since the beginning of this debate. The Government said in paragraph 34 of the original consultation document:

"The Secretary of State has in mind that, on the creation of Scottish Homes, the entire stock of SSHA houses should be transferred, as a holding corporation, to a new 'landlord' division of Scottish Homes."

In paragraph 35 they say:

"Arrangements for a 'landlord' division would be essentially transitional and Scottish Homes would be asked to prepare and implement, as a matter of priority, proposals for new management arrangements for tenanted houses in SSHA estates in different areas of the country."

Those statements appear to suggest that the landlord division of Scottish Homes was essentially a short-term arrangement. As we know, it caused much worry and anger among SSHA tenants. The Government have encumbered the proposed 470 Scottish Homes with the temporary ownership of 83,000 let houses, which had very little to do with their fundamental objectives. There are two separate arguments—first, the interests of SSHA tenants and, secondly, whether it is appropriate to insert those 83,000 directly let houses into the new Scottish Homes organisation.

However, with the publication of the White Paper and the Bill, the future of the SSHA landlord division is still uncertain. The Bill simply allows for the incorporation of the SSHA within Scottish Homes, without giving any idea of the future development of the landlord division. But the White Paper suggests at one and the same time that the landlord division will continue and may expand and that it may cease to exist. Paragraph 2.7 states:

"Scottish Homes will continue the existing role of the SSHA as a model landlord in relation to its own stock."

Paragraph 2.18 states:

"the Government no longer consider it appropriate to take a fixed view of other aspects of the management structure, such as the proposal for Scottish Homes' involvement in the management of houses to be concentrated in a single landlord division."

So where exactly are we? What do the Government really want to do with those tenants and their homes?

It is also worth mentioning the £600 million accumulated debts of the SSHA, which caused such interest to the National Audit Office. As I understand it, every penny of rent collected from SSHA tenants goes to pay interest and loan charges on that debt. We shall return later to the finances of this operation and the impact of that accumulated debt on Scottish Homes.

During the consideration of the future of the 83,000 households the Government have treated them at best as pawns and at worst as an embarrassment. Those people are entitled to proper consultation and consideration before major decisions are taken about the future ownership and management of their homes. We have been impressed by the fully justified campaign by SSHA tenants all over Scotland in recent months. We deplore the Government's high-handed attitude and insist that SSHA tenants should be given an opportunity to vote on major decisions about the future of their public sector landlord. The Government are constantly posturing about tenants' rights and preaching about the virtues of ballots. Amendment No. 29 gives the Government an opportunity to practise what they preach for a change.

(Dundee, East)
: I cannot speak with any certainty about the mind of the Minister. There is, however, some evidence that suggests the thinking of the Secretary of State for Scotland. "The abolition of the SSHA along the lines proposed will result in the loss of an important and effective Agency for Housing in Scotland and lead to great dissatisfaction for the majority of its 85,000 tenants".

472 It is not simply a matter of the housing associations and COSLA. The voluntary bodies have also come out strongly against the proposal. Shelter says:

"The inclusion of Scottish Special Housing Association into the New Agency is opposed by the overwhelming majority of tenants of the SSHA. The tenants have made it clear that they, too, are opposed to the idea of abolition and especially to the idea of abolition without being given a right to vote upon it. My honourable Friend the Minister for East Lothian (Mr. Home Robertson) said that he has spoken to people, as I have in Dundee, and there is unanimous hostility to this proposal and a determination to fight it. COSLA gives three quotations but it says that those sentiments have been repeated many times:

"All wish to remain with the SSHA as their landlord."


"We wish to remain as we are under the Scottish Special Housing Association."


"Why change an Association that operated for the good of its tenants and who are happy with the services provided."

That seems to be the universal response of SSHA tenants.

Of course, sometimes one must take the entire population into consideration as well as a group within it. In any case, we are dealing with the lives of between 85,000 and 120,000 Scottish tenants and we cannot legislate vital and dramatically to change people's lives without allowing them a say in the proposed changes. To do that is to be guilty of the worst aspects of despotism and dictatorship, and that accusation can accurately be levelled at the Government's handling of this aspect of the Bill.

The way to deal with despots and dictators who think that they always know best what is good for others is to have safeguards against them, and such safeguards have normally been associated with the ballot box. That is why it is vital that the tenants be given the right to vote on the proposals. The Government claim always to be in favour of ballots and they uphold the ballot as a means by which people can cast off those who try to oppress them. We have heard time and time again how the Government were prepared to give the right to vote to trade unionists so that they could bring the unions JPEG 473 back under their control and away from the trade union barons, as the Government would have had it.

In the poll tax legislation the Government are arguing for a ballot, because they are trying to force tenants, through the poll tax, to use the ballot box against their local authorities and to vote against the provision of quality local services by the local authorities.

But the Government's commitment to the ballot box is paper thin. Every time a ballot decision has gone against them or has been likely to go against them, the Government have not wanted to know about ballots. For example, Londoners voted for a council that the Government did not like and the Government's reaction was to remove from Londoners the right to vote for that council. There was then the recent election in Scotland, when the Scots voted three to one in favour of a Scottish assembly. So the Government simply decided to ignore the ballot. They sit in the dark, afraid to switch on the light, because they know that if they do, the wish of the people of Scotland for a Scottish assembly will be clear for everyone to see. Therefore the Government ignored that ballot result. Then the trade unionists used the ballot procedures given to them by the Government to vote for a strike. The Government did not like it, so they brought out other legislation to allow individuals to opt out of strike ballot results. I wonder when they will bring in legislation to allow Scotland to opt out in relation to England.

The Government simply refuse to give SSHA tenants a ballot, because they know what the result will be: it will overturn the proposals in the White Paper. Mr Derek Mason, the chairman of the SSHA, has admitted as much. He is on record as saying that everyone knows that in a ballot almost every tenant would vote against the Government's proposals. That is the real reason why the Government refuse to give them a ballot on this important matter.

The Government's behaviour in this matter is a disgrace, especially in a House which lays claim to being a seat of democracy. If this sort of action took place in Eastern Europe or in a Socialist African state, Tory Members would fall over themselves to rush into the newspapers and on to television to denounce in high moral tones its refusal to allow the right of a ballot. Yet when it happens unders their noses, by the Government whom they support, they silently look the other way and pretend that they do not hear anything.—Opposition Members will not be silent. Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: I can best explain the position by making it clear that Parliament has the option of extending the limits, should it so desire. I cannot give a precise time scale because I do not know how quick funds will be used on specific projects.

Having approved in clauses 7 and 8 the borrowing and guarantee powers related to Scottish Homes, in clause 9 we turn to the limit on how much borrowing Scottish Homes may incur. Clause 9(1) defines which elements of Scottish Homes' borrowing count towards the overall limit and provides that the total must not exceed £1,000 million. The Secretary of State has power to increase that amount to no more than £1,500 million by making an order which is subject to the approval of this House. Clause 9(3) provides that interest outstanding should count towards the total borrowing.

It is right that the borrowing of a body such as Scottish Homes should be subject to proper control and for increases to be subject to scrutiny by Parliament. The clause provides for both.

Scottish Homes will, of course, inherit the current borrowings of both the Scottish Special Housing Association and the Housing Corporation. We discussed that of the SSHA when we examined clause 3. Its borrowings arising from its long and substantial programme amount to £571 million. The nature of the Housing Corporation's borrowings is different in that it lends on to registered housing associations and borrows to cover that lending. The borrowing is repaid by the housing associations, but at any time there are unrepaid loans, currently amounting to £172.5 million.

The limit on borrowings at £1,000 million gives Scottish Homes adequate room for manoeuvre. If there is a need to go beyond that limit, Parliament has the opportunity to debate it and come to a speedy conclusion.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
