On resuming—

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: Clause 53(4) provides that an application

"shall cease to have effect"

if the tenant "withdraws his consent" by serving notice to that effect on the landlord. That application is by an approved person to exercise the rights to acquire, which part II provides. That is clarified by clause 53(1), which provides that the application is:

"For the purposes of exercising the right conferred by this Part."

Since the application is exercised at right, it will continue to be current, unless it is either withdrawn or ceases to have effect, until the point at which that right is successfully exercised.

Clause 55(1) clarifies that the right is finally exercised by service of a "notice of acceptance". Until the notice of acceptance is served, a current application still exists, which can be withdrawn or cease to have effect. After the right has been exercised, no application is current. There is nothing to be withdrawn or to cease to have effect.

The tenant can put a stop to the proposed transaction by giving notice to the landlord that he withdraws his consent any time before, but not after, the applicant services notice of acceptance under clause 55. In view of the uncertainty, I shall consider whether we can clarify the provisions, but I cannot guarantee that that will be done on Report.

title REFUSAL OF APPLICATIONS

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Home Robertson: I am pleased that a public-sector landlord may refuse an application from an acquiring landlord, but I should like to know on what grounds that can be done, so that we may build on them.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: Clause 56 makes provision for a landlord to serve a notice refusing an application to acquire. I cannot imagine an elected local authority in Scotland discussing the transfer of houses to another landlord—either in the private sector or in the public sector—without consulting the tenants. That would be shocking.

It is interesting that we are holding the discussion in the context of part III, which the Minister sees fit to describe as the "tenants' choice" section of the Bill. What "choice" is there for tenants? They will be trapped into transferring their house from the public sector to an uncertain future in the private sector. At least, until the last minute, tenants can veto the transaction. However, as soon as it has been completed and the house is acquired by an approved landlord, an assured tenancy is established and all rights are gone. Within hours of the completion of that transaction the approved landlord may sell the property to another company or some other approved landlord without the tenant's knowledge. The tenant could find himself in negotiation with an entirely different landlord. What tenant will choose to get involved in that series of transactions?

The Minister has exposed the most deplorable nature of this part of the Bill, yet he has the brass neck to try to dismiss the amendment moved by the hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire (Mr. Kirkwood).

He said that it is unacceptable because it would establish a tenant's veto against onward disposal of the property. I am glad that the Minister intervened, because he established beyond question that the amendment moved by the hon. Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire is absolutely perfect. It achieves exactly what the Opposition want to achieve. It would give the tenant an effective veto over any further transaction involving the roof over his own and his family's heads. What on earth is wrong with that?

Amendment No. 541 was drafted by the Scottish Consumer Council and would require the Secretary of State to prescribe the manner of consultations with tenants about disposals of properties. The Minister said that that would be bureaucratic, a bad thing and a monstrous interference in the freedom of private sector landlords to do what they like with other people's houses. We have come to expect that sort of thing from the Government, but Minister might have had the grace to show a little shame, or even contrition. He says that the Opposition are wrong to suggest that a tenant should be consulted about—and able to scrutinise the details of—the disposal of his home within the private sector, perhaps within hours of the initial disposal.
