Mr. Kirkwood: We now enter the murky depths of part III—an important part of the Bill and one that I find totally unsatisfactory. The Government's stated objective of trying to give the tenants greater choice is laudable so far as it goes as a guiding principle, but under the Bill all that we are doing is giving landlords new rights. The Bill produces a new right to buy which mirrors the 1980 provisions, which gave power to tenants to purchase their own council houses. The right in this part of the Bill gives landlords a right to buy, and the framework of the Bill means that tenants will have precious little choice about what is happening to them. They are being treated as compliant observers and passed around betwixt landlords almost like mediaeval chattels. In my view, the Government have got the formula substantially wrong. Col. 990 I accept the principle of increasing tenants' choice. In that respect, I may differ slightly from Labour Members of the Committee. I understand that they have a greater commitment to the role that public sector landlords have played historically and should play in the future, and I make no complaint about that. There is a case for looking carefully at giving tenants increased choices, even if that means in certain circumstances taking them out of the hands of public sector landlords. But I should be willing to consider moves in that direction only if those moves resulted in tenants moving towards tenant co-operatives or housing trusts, which would be essentially non-profit-making, mutual and co-operative organisations in the full sense of the word.

The Bill does not go nearly far enough in that direction. If it did, I should find it easier to support. As it stands, it is impossible to support. Today's debates on that principle will be important. Far from the Bill providing tenants' choice, it really provides nothing more than Hobson's choice. In the course of discussing these and other amendments we shall see the problems that will confront tenants. When references were made to part III of the Bill at earlier sittings, hon. Members were talking about the "pick-a-landlord" scheme, but the equation is the other way round landlords will be picking tenants under part III, and it is regrettable for that if for no other reason.

My principal objections are many. I am opposed to the proposals as they stand. First, it is regrettable that approved private landlords will be able to approach public sector landlords so that the tenant can be transferred to the private landlord—with the tenant having to agree to the move, of course, but on his own having absolutely no formal right to initiate the transfer process. That is a regrettable omission in the Bill.

Secondly, present public sector landlords—islands and district councils—will have no rights to have tenants transferred to them from another public sector landlord, nor will a tenant have the right to seek a transfer between public sector landlords, for example, from a housing association to a district council. That is also objectionable.

Thirdly, during the transfer process I can find no provision in the Bill for a continuing process of consultation between the tenant involved in the transfer and the other parties to the transaction. After agreeing to start the transfer, which is essential, the tenant will have no further input until asked to agree on the final terms of the transfer, which is obviously another legal necessity. In between times, there is no provision anywhere in the Bill for information to flow between the parties to the legal transaction of the tenancy and the tenant himself.

Fourthly, it is objectionable that tenants involved in a transfer will not have access to properly resourced independent advice as of right. Tenants may therefore not be fully aware of other options available to them or of the implications of the transfer itself.

I wish to illustrate my arguments by reference to the Shetland islands council area. It is instructive, because it will inform the debate and it was one of Image Col. 991 the major reasons behind amendments Nos. 579 and 580. The Scottish Special Housing Association has been trying to effect the transfer of certain SSHA houses and tenants in Shetland to the Hjaltland housing association. The transfer proposed has been under consideration for a full five years; it is nevertheless still incomplete. That is instructive in itself. One of the main causes of the difficulties, which illustrates another intrinsic reason for the amendments, is that the Shetland islands council has been considerably hampered by the fact that the SSHA conducts all its negotiations and decision taking in Edinburgh and therefore people who try to deal with the SSHA, including tenants, find it difficult operating at arm's length. That reinforces the arguments that we heard earlier for a proper regionally based system of decentralised management and operations in the newly formed Scottish Homes. It is important to bear that in mind because it is an important factor in the situation in Shetland.

Although the proposed transfer is under existing legislation, it is relevant to the consideration of part III of the Bill because it highlights the problems and concerns of tenants subjected to the implementation of transfer policies as proposed by the Bill, whereby tenants will have extremely limited rights and powers to participate in the transfer negotiations. Under part III a tenant will have a right to consent to the commencement of the transfer negotiations and an ultimate right to agree or refuse to agree to the transfer when it is to be finalised, but the tenant will have virtually no other right to participate in or be kept informed about the transfer negotiations.

There are several disturbing features about the position in Shetland. First, the negotiations have been unduly prolonged according to the evidence that has been made available to me. A five-year period is far too long. They have been equivocal, and I am surprised about that because the SSHA, by and large in my experience, has been an exemplary body when facing up to difficulties in the past, certainly in my constituency. But in the Shetland islands it has also been unduly secretive.

Secondly, the complaints that have been made to me show that tenants have not been kept fully advised of all the options that are open to them. The options that are open to them will be closed when the Bill is enacted. That is unfair on SSHA tenants in the Shetlands.

The local authority has not been kept fully advised of the transfer proposals, which is deeply regrettable. As the previous factor of the 140 tenants and tenancies involved in the row, it could have played a constructive and fruitful role in bringing about a satisfactory outcome. Neither the tenants nor the local authority have been formally advised that a transfer of tenants is an available option or, alternatively, one that is excluded. Again that is regrettable.

In addition, there are signs that, if a transfer of tenants in stock to the local authority is approved, the stock price will be based on a market value of approximately £3.5 million, which is considerably higher than the likely price based on an outstanding Col. 992 debt of approximately £1.5 million if the stock were transferred to the housing association. That is an important factor that we must consider in the context of what will happen in the future under part III.

The results of a formal consultation, which had a deadline of 31 January 1988 and which sought the views of the 140 affected SSHA tenants on the proposed transfer to the housing association have not yet been published by the SSHA. Nothing has been said for the past month, although tenants and the local council are anxious to know about the eventual disposition of their tenancies.

The tenants have not had any comprehensive and independent advice on the transfer proposals. Such advice as they have received arose as a last resort and out of desperation from the lcoal authority's intervention.

That catalogue of complaints causes me considerable concern. That augurs ill for what will obtain when part III hits the statute book.

Amendments Nos. 579 and 580 seek to address the problem that we experienced recently in the Shetland Islands council, and which we shall experience day in and day out when part III is enacted. I may say in parenthesis, having studied last night the provisions in part III of the English Housing Bill, that the Minister could legitimately say that we should be grateful for small mercies.
