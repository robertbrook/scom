Housing (Scotland) Bill

Clause 17
FIXING OF TERMS OF STATUTORY ASSURED TENNANCY

Amendments proposed [this day]: No. 253, in page 11, line 10, leave out 'one month' and insert 'three months'.—[ Mr. Home Robertson.]

4.30 p.m.

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman
: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking amendment No. 254, in page 11, line 15, leave out 'month' and insert 'three months.'

Mr. Nicholas Fairbairn
(Perth and Kinross)
: I am anxious to speak to the proposed amendments, which have merit for the following reasons. In the Bill a month is allowed for a notice given by landlord to tenant, or by tenant to landlord, after which, if unchallenged, it becomes unchallengeable. A month is a very short time. The person may be away or ill or, even worse, may not receive the letter.

It is a dangerous precept that either the tenant or the landlord should be able to allege that notice of a change of condition was sent a month earlier and if there is no challenge the change automatically becomes a new clause in the contract. It would be a real rogues' charter. Either party could allege that he had genuinely sent it although the other party had not received it, or either party could allege that he had sent it when in fact he had not. It is a dangerous precept that within a month, in the absence of a reply, it can be assumed the contract is altered.

I ask the Minister to look favourably on the amendments, and to say that the matter may be reviewed by those before whom it comes. If one party says, totally dishonestly, that he sent a notice, and the other party says, totally honestly, that he did not receive it, it is a bad principle for that to be sufficient to alter. Alternatively, if one party sent the notice and the other received it but chose to allege that he had not received it, it would be an equally bad principle. I ask the Minister to look carefuly at this because it is an inequitable concept.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton)
: I am glad to respond to my honourable and learned Friend. It would have helped if he had said what period he thought would be most suitable. Three months is 626 proposed in the amendment, but I will consider the matter. I cannot give a hard commitment.

Mr. Fairbairn
: I will not say that I am against periods, but any period could be unsuitable. With respect to the Minister, it would be wise if it was within the discretion of the commissioners to decide whether in all circumstance it was equitable to say that something allegedly or actually proposed was in fact so. I should infinitely prefer those who have to decide the matter in all the circumstances to have some discretion rather than leaving it to a fixed period of a particular duration.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: The length of time should be a balance between the interests of the tenant and those of the landlord. The person giving notice is entitled to know, within a reasonable period, if it is to be challenged. Any length of time giving notice might be too short for landlord and tenant in certain unusual circumstances, but if it were set so long as to cover every conceivable circumstance and eventuality the person serving the notice would have to wait an unreasonably long time before knowing whether the proposals were likely to be accepted. The criticism of the serving of notices at present is that it can take a very long time to achieve a conclusion to the matter, and a shorter period is in the interests of all.

Mr. Fairbairn
: It is unrealistic if applied to a person working in Saudi Arabia, or who is unconscious or who has not received the notice. It is not a question of time. There should be a discretion to say, in the circumstances, that it would not be fair for one party to change the contract one-sidedly on the allegation that the other party had not replied within one month, regardless of whether he was alive, dead, abroad, on holiday or anything else. It does not matter whether it is one month, six months or a thousand months. There ought to be a discretion.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: If a person does not receive the notice, that is a relevant and important point. In practice, recorded delivery post is the most common method used, and a recorded delivery receipt is kept as evidence of service. In the past this matter has been governed by section 114 of the Rent (Scotland) Act 1984, which provides that

"A notice or other document which requires to be served on a person … may be given to him—

  1. by delivering it to him;
  2. by leaving it at his proper address;
  3. by sending it by recorded delivery post to him at that address."

A person's proper address is his last known address. The provision in the Bill is similar to that.

Mr. Fairbairn
: Being in Parliament and not at home in Scotland, recorded delivery post is regularly marked "Could not deliver" and I do not get it. If I happen to be in Saudi Arabia, in hospital, on holiday, in Parliament or wherever, I do not get it. Recorded delivery is not a record of the fact that I have received it. It is a record only of the fact that someone has attempted unsuccessfully to deliver it.

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: This comes back to my original point. The length of time has to be a balance between the interests of the tenant and those of the landlord. Either might serve a notice. I accept that there is no particular magic in the one-month period, but it is right to expect that an appeal in these circumstances should be made reasonably quickly.

Let us take the example of a tenant who proposes to take over responsibility for certain repairs, which were previously the responsibility of the landlord. As a result, quite fairly, he wants the rent reduced. It would be wrong to require him to wait as long as three months to see whether the landlord intended to object to that change. It would then take the rent assessment committee some time to make its determination. On balance, a shorter period is about right. It gives both landlord and tenant a reasonable opportunity to consider the proposal, and to decide whether the proposals are acceptable. It also means that a final decision on the proposed terms will be reached reasonably quickly.

I therefore cannot support the amendment in those terms, but I can say to my honourable and learned Friend that I will consider the matter and will write to him in due course.

Mr. Fairbairn
: It is not a question of whether it should be one month, or six weeks, or three months. The point is that with an absolute period, however long, the alteration proposed, whether by the tenant or the landlord, automatically takes effect if it is not challenged and the tribunal can do nothing about it.

If the landlord writes to the tenant saying, "You may not use coal fires, you are not allowed to have children and your dogs are to be shot," if the tenant does not reply within the time limit that proposal automatically takes effect. If the tenant then says that he never received the notice and the recorded delivery was not achieved because he was abroad, unconcious, on a six-month course at the university of Saskatchewan or whatever, that does not matter and the proposal automatically applies. That is mad. There ought to be a discretion for the tribunal to say that in the circumstances it believes that it would be inequitable and unfair to make the absolute, whether it be against the tenant or against the landlord, just because somebody claims that he wrote. He need only claim that he has written. I beg my honourable Friend to understand the situation. If I claim to have written to a tenant saying that from 1 June his rent will be increased from tuppence to £2,000 per week and that if he does not challenge that within a month it will be absolute, even if the tenant never received the letter he will be powerless to do anything about it. How does one decide whether a person actually sent a notice or merely alleged that he sent it, or received it or whether the other party failed to receive it or simply pretended that he had not received it? Under the clause as it stands, there is no answer. One month after notice is allegedly sent of a variation in the contract, it is absolute if not challenged. That is a very strange concept.
