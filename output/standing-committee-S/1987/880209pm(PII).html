Lord James Douglas-Hamilton: I am pleased that the hon. Member for East Kilbride (Mr. Ingram) has tabled this interesting amendment, which gives me a chance to explain our proposal to exempt low rent tenancies from the assured arrangements.

Low rent tenancies are excluded from the provisions of the Rent (Scotland) Act 1987. In that Act, a low rent tenancy is defined in relation to the rateable value of the property at a certain date. As domestic rates will cease in Scotland on 1 April 1989, that approach will not do for the new legislation. Therefore, we propose to take the power for my right hon. and learned Friend the Secretary of State to set by order a rent level below which a tenancy will not be an assured tenancy. I assure the hon.

Col. 552 Gentleman that we intend to set the rent level sufficiently low that only tenancies that are clearly left at well below market rents will be exempted.

Obviously it is necessary, in deciding whether a rent qualifies as low, to leave out of account any payment made by the tenant for a service, a repair, maintenance, or insurance. Often when a house is let on a peppercorn rent the tenant will be happy to pay the appropriate cost of such services. The amendment seems designed to prevent landlords from cheating by claiming that part of the rent is for services, and thus that the tenancy qualifies as a low-rent tenancy which should be exempted from the assured tenancy provisions.

The amendment is unnecessary because there are two safeguards already in the provision. First, it is our intention to set the limit so that only tenancies with very low rents are exempt. If a landlord tried to obtain an income equivalent to a market rent while setting the amount of rent low enough to qualify, the purported service charge would need to be so high that it would be clear that the landlord was cheating.

Secondly, the provision in paragraph 2 of schedule 4 already states that service charges are to be disregarded unless they could not have been regarded by the parties as service charges. Those two safeguards seem adequately to protect against the type of abuse about which hon. Members have expressed concern. The amendment is unnecessary, and could even have the undesirable effect of bringing into the assured tenancy regime tenancies for which almost no rent is payable but the tenant pays a fair and reasonable charge for some services.

In view of what I have said, I hope that the Hon. Member for East Lothian will withdraw his amendment.

Mr. Kirkwood: This takes me into unchartered territory. Paragraph 9 of the schedule, which deals with resident landlords, sets out exemptions to the assured tenancy provisions, and our aim in amendment No. 138 is to bring resident landlords within the scope of the assured tenancy provisions and thus protect their tenants.

I have received representations from Shelter, telling me how serious the problem is, especially in urban areas where Image Col. 559 houses are in multiple occupation. Both the amendments are important and I crave the Committee's indulgence because I shall need to bury myself in the technical details of Shelter's representations.

At present, tenants of resident landlords do not enjoy full rent Act protection. Such protection as they have is afforded by Part VII of the Rent (Scotland) Act 1984, which gives them the right not to be evicted without a court order, the right not to be harassed and the right to have a reasonable rent assessed by a rent assessment committee. When a resident landlord seeks an order for repossession, that must be granted, subject to the sheriffs power to postpone the date of possession for a period not exceeding three months. But because of their lack of security and their relationship with a resident landlord, few such tenants have ever made use of that right to apply for a reasonable rent or to seek to remain in the property after the landlord has intimated that he wished them to vacate. That is understandable because of the proximity of the resident landlord.

Experience has shown Shelter that the most vulnerable people are often those in properties with a resident landlord. This is especially so in houses in multiple occupation where standards are frequently low. Severe overcrowding may be prevalent and charges can be extremely high in relation to the accommodation provided. The tenants of such properties are often highly vulnerable to exploitation by landlords. They have more frequent contact with them because they are resident.

The lack of full rent Act protection for tenants of resident landlords has much worried housing workers—including Shelter—in recent years. Without security, such tenants and their advisers are virtually powerless to challenge high rents and to improve poor standards. The Bill's proposals exclude the further granting of any new part VII contracts. If all tenants with resident landlords are excluded from having assured tenancies, those tenants will have no security at all. They will even be deprived of their current limited security, as there will be no mechanism for appealing against a proposed rent increase. This in a sector of the rented market which is already noted for its high cost and is often subsidised through the housing benefit system. Therefore, the landlord will not have to institute possession proceedings and there will be no sanction against harassment and illegal eviction.

Shelter and other organisations accept that when a landlord is resident—especially when a tenant shares the family home—he should be ble to exercise choice over whether, and with whom, he continues to share his home. But the purpose of the amendment is to illustrate that the exclusion from assured tenancies of all instances when a landlord is resident goes too far—at least in my submission and that of Shelter—and would leave such tenants without any protection whatever. That is of great concern, especially when the landlord essentially runs the property as a business. If this background and these representations—they were substantially made to me by Shelter—are borne out in fact, they merit the Committee's consideration.

Shelter submits that, without the amendment, tenants in houses in multiple occupation will be deprived of all protection if their landlord is resident. There will be no sanctions against such a landlord who harasses his tenants and no requirement to obtain a repossession order.

The current limited protection granted by part VII contracts will no longer apply to new tenancies granted by resident landlords in houses in multiple occupation. Tenants in houses in multiple occupation are frequently young, vulnerable and pay high costs for low standard accommodation. To challenge the high costs and obtain better standards, they must be given some security of tenure. Paragraph 9 of schedule 4, as drafted, allows a landlord up to six months in which to take up residence in a property foreclosed before ceasing to qualify as a "resident landlord", provided that he intimates his intention to take up residence. We are worried that the proposal in the Bill will be used as a loophole. But our concern would be reduced if the exemption from providing assured tenancies were restricted to resident landlords with six or fewer tenants.
