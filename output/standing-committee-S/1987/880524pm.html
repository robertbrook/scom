School Boards (Scotland) Bill

Clause 15
DELEGATION OF FUNCTIONS TO BOARDS

Question proposed [this day], That the clause stand part of the Bill.

4.45 pm

Question again proposed.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Michael Forsyth)
: I welcome you to the Chair, Mr. McWilliam. When we adjourned I was about to attempt to answer the question of the hon. Member for Dumbarton (Mr. McFall), who was worried about the precise powers available to school boards on junior appointments.

Boards will be unable to extend their roles in senior appointments, the procedure for which is set out in schedule 2. However, they will be able to take powers to make junior appointments, and the hon. Member for Dumbarton was right to point to the extent of those powers. In carrying out those functions, boards will be circumscribed by all the duties placed on them by the Bill.

In granting a delegation order to boards to make junior appointments, it will be open to a local authority to attach any conditions that it considers relevant, including conditions relating to the planning of the deployment of staff, about which the hon. Gentleman was concerned. In any dispute about conditions it would be for the boards and for the Secretary of State to determine what was appropriate or desirable in individual circumstances.

I hope that my explanation clarifies the position, and I invite the Committee to agree that clause 15 should stand part of the Bill.

Mr. McAllion
: The clause deals with the power of education authorities to suspend the delegation of a function in appropriate circumstances. In COSLA's view, the powers granted to the education authorities to ensure due exercise of delegated functions are inadequate. The terms used in subsection (2) relate to serious or persistent failure, to which amendment No. 178 relates. Subsection (4) refers to "gross mismanagement", to which amendment No. 179 relates. They are examples of lax standards and serious consideration should be given to the redrafting of the clause.

It seems to COSLA and to the Opposition that it will be difficult to decide what constitutes serious or persistent failure or, indeed, "gross mismanagement". As the Bill is drafted, the decision appears to rest entirely with the education authority. If it is satisfied that there has been serious or persistent failure or "gross mismanagement", that is sufficient. But that is imprecise and perhaps contentious. Indeed, it would give an education authority a somewhat arbitrary power to use against the school boards. COSLA suggested that more appropriate provisions might be ones similar to those in section 70 of the Education (Scotland) Act 1980. The authority would then have to be satisfied that the school board had

"failed to discharge any duty imposed on them by or for the purposes of this Act".

If it were satisfied that such failure had occurred, an order could be made

"declaring them to be in default in respect of that duty"

and requiring the school board

"before a date stated in the order to discharge that duty."

That seems to be a more satisfactory approach to the problem. It is better than using terms such as "gross mismanagement" or serious and persistent failure, which seem contentious. That seems a more satisfactory way of approaching the problem. It is better than using such terms as "gross mismanagement" and "serious and persistent failure", which seem contentious and which make it JPEG 525 difficult to establish precisely when such irregularities occur.

New Clause 3
SCHOOL COUNCILS

"(1) For the purpose of co-ordinating the functions of the School Boards of the primary schools in a particular area which serve a secondary school and the functions of the secondary school so served, a School Council may be established.

(2) A School Council shall be established where a majority of the School Board, in a particular area so request.

(3) The School Board for each primary school in the area shall delegate two members and the School Board for the secondary school four members to be members of the School Council.".—[ Mr. Buchanan-Smith.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Buchanan-Smith
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

This matter has concerned me for some time, ever since the publication of the original consultation paper. As school boards are to be set up for each 536 school, it is important that there should be co-ordination between the boards. I quote from my submission as part of the consultation process, before the legislation was published. I recommended that

"Where there is a wish of several small primary schools, feeding one secondary, to join together, then there should be an opportunity for joint councils."

I continued in my submission:

"I suggest also an area council for the feeder area of secondary schools which should have say, two representatives of each council in the area and which would meet at regular intervals but much less frequently than individual councils: there would, thereby, be co-ordination between primary and secondary."

My view was reinforced in an earlier debate on the name of school boards. My hon. Friend the Under-Secretary of State at the time said that he preferred the term "board" and that perhaps the description "council" should be reserved for other purposes. That encouraged me further to table the amendment. That is a secondary reason, however.

5.45 pm

My main reason is that I feel that it is critical not only that we consider individual schools—we have spent much time debating individual schools and their school boards—but that we remember that no school is on its own but each is part of a system. The primary schools feed into the secondary schools and there is interest from the teaching viewpoint, for co-ordination with secondary school teachers and for continuity of education, and there is interest from the parents' viewpoint so that they know, as their children graduate from primary school to secondary school, that there is close liaison with the secondary school which eases children through from primary to secondary education. This can be a traumatic stage for children, not only their going out of the small school where they may be quite important, especially if they reach the top class, but the trauma of going to a big school where there are much older people. This applies especially in rural areas where they may have been in a small rural primary school. They have to go to a secondary school which, although it might not have exactly a city environment, has an environment that is akin to that of a town. For all those reasons, and countless others, there are strong grounds for ensuring that there is close liaison between primary schools and secondary schools, in both directions, as already happens. In setting up school boards, we should also provide for similar liaison between school boards if they are to have the effective function and role that this proposed legislation envisages.
