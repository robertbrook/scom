The honourable Member for Roxburgh and Berwickshire (Mr. Kirkwood) asked how frequently orders will be brought forward. An order will 916 probably be brought forward every two years. Clause 38 is evidence of our intention to reduce progressively the incidence of phasing of rent increases for tenancies covered by the Rent (Scotland) Act 1984. After about five years, I expect us to bring forward orders to abolish phasing entirely.

Clause 39 deals with three limited circumstances in which tenancies that begin after the enactment of the Bill can be protected tenancies, as defined in the Rent (Scotland) Act 1984. 4.45 p.m.

In the view of Shelter and the Opposition, the clause is too restrictive. For example, it does not allow a landlord who may wish to grant a protected tenancy to do so. Clearly, landlords want to make the most efficient use of their housing stock to maximise the return on their investment. If a landlord has as tenants a family who have outgrown their existing accommodation and he also has available a larger property suitable for that family, who are good tenants, are not anti-social and who pay their rent punctually, it would be sensible if the family moved-to that new, larger accommodation. However, if the proposed move means that the family will have to surrender their protected tenancy status, the transaction may be jeopardised and the family may decide that it is not worth losing their tenants' right in moving from a protected to an assured tenancy. The family may refuse to move, which will be to their own disadvantage and that of the landlord.

Similarly, an elderly person who has been a life-long tenant and may have built up a close and friendly relationship with the landlord may, for reasons associated with age and health, wish to move to ground floor accommodation. The landlord may have available suitable accommodation that he is 920 keen to offer his or her old friend and tenant, but again the rigidity of the clause and the penalty involved—the surrender of protected tenancy rights—may prejudice the transaction.

There is a possible loophole in subsection (1)(c), which allows the landlord to seek a possession order on the ground that he will be providing suitable alternative accommodation for the tenant. That ensures that the tenant retains his or her protected tenancy on the transfer but it will involve both parties in a wholly unnecessary and unjustified waste of the courts' time in seeking possession through the courts. Moreover, being dragged through the courts is a worrying and unnecessary process.

The amendment suggested by Shelter covers all those circumstances. It introduces some flexibility into housing legislation and makes it possible for the Secretary of State for Scotland to determine circumstances where a protected ternancy may be granted, which otherwise would not be possible under the clause.
