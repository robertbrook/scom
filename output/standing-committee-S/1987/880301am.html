I hope that that was not directed against my good self and other members of the Committee as we have never tried to mislead anyone about the full implications of the Bill for tenants of Scottish Homes or for tenants anywhere.

The right of public sector tenants to choose a private sector landlord is an important issue. It is not as straightforward as the Minister or his Back Benchers would have us believe. It is a right that does not extend to tenants in the private sector, nor does it allow tenants to choose, for example, district councils as their new landlord. It is a restricted right, and it will usually be applied only in circumstances where a tenant of a district council will be allowed to transfer his tenancy to a private sector landlord of his choice.

It is in line with the Government's stated intention, both in the White Paper and in the papers that support the Bill, that district councils should in future have their role reduced as direct providers of housing.

Clause 53 and associated clauses set out to reduce the role of district councils and to tempt tenants away from district councils and into the private sector, but is not a genuine choice. That is what concerns us. For a long time the Government have starved district councils of their ability to fulfil their responsibilities as landlords. As the Minister well knows, only last week in the announcements about housing support grant and rate fund contributions the Government once again undercut the ability of district councils to meet the requirements of their tenants.

Dundee district council used to receive from the Government more than £8 million in housing support grant, which enabled it to maintain and manage district council stock. That sum has been slashed to nothing. For a number of years there has been no housing support grant to Dundee district council. The same is true of the rate fund contribution. Previously the council was allowed to contribute from the rates more than £6 million for JPEG 1075 the upkeep of district council stock. Now it is allowed to contribute less than £500,000. As a consequence, the service that the district council is able to give to its tenants has been greatly reduced. In those circumstances, the tenant is not being given a genuine choice because the district council has been starved of the ability to provide the kind of service that the tenant might expect.

When the Minister went round Dundee on Friday, he saw houses needing new windows to be installed right now, but the council does not have the money to install them. He saw houses that require central heating because of dampness and condensation problems. He saw a clear example in Whitfield where the district council has been forced to erect canopies to protect tenants of multi-storey blocks from falling masonry because there is no money to maintain the buildings. In those circumstances, people cannot be expected to make a genuine choice between a district council with its hands tied behind its back, unable to provide the service, and a new private sector landlord who might make all kinds of enticements to persuade tenants to transfer the property to him.

The amendment seeks to ensure that the landlord must specify his intentions about the works to be carried out. That is important. Craigiebank in Dundee is a very good area. People have to wait up to 25 years to obtain a house there because of the high demand. The tenants tend to be older people and the council does not have the money to carry out the repairs that it would like to do. A private sector landlord might offer all kinds of enticements, promising to put in new windows, central heating, and so on. Unelss we commit landlords to carry out the works that they promise, there is a danger that landlords will bribe tenants to switch over and then leave them in the lurch by not fulfilling the landlords' part of the bargain.
