Mr. Kirkwood
: This is regarded as a key group of amendments by the Scottish Federation of Housing Associations. Amendment No. 135 seeks to give all tenants with assured tenancies the right to a written lease, which shall conform with a model lease drawn up by the Secretary of State. The other amendments pave the way for the principal amendment in the group, amendment No. 162.

The purpose of amendment No. 162 is to allow future housing association tenants, including re-lets, to retain their existing rights with regard to security of tenure. The amendment also allows the Secretary of State to establish housing association assured 506 tenancies and to determine the circumstances in which they will be established and the terms of such tenancies. The amendment allows the increased use of private finance, which the Government seek in the Bill, for housing association projects, while not transferring all future housing association tenants to assured tenancies.

We are now in the depths of the technical issues of private rented sector legislation, so I hope that the Committee will bear with me. I wish to set out as accurately as possible the case that the Scottish Federation of Housing Associations has put to me because it requires careful scrutiny by the Government before they finally firm up their intentions in the Bill. Under the existing proposals in the Bill all future housing association tenants, including re-lets of existing tenancies, are to become assured tenancies. The Government briefing seeks to justify that proposal by the need to introduce private finance into housing association projects. If implemented, the proposal will have major implications for the housing association movement in Scotland and will change the nature of housing associations as we have known them.

The Scottish Federation of Housing Associations makes it clear at the outset that the housing association movement in Scotland welcomes the introduction of private finance where appropriate, feasible and useful. Indeed, housing associations have been using private finance for some time, especially in building-for-sale projects, and they welcome the opportunity to use private finance to a greater extent in future.

The housing association movement nevertheless regrets the impact of private finance on all future housing association tenants, and I have great sympathy with its case. By definition, the Bill reduces housing association tenants' rights. In the movement's submission, with which I agree, it will tend to increase rents and remove any right of arbitration or appeal on rent levels. That is a high price to pay to bring more private finance into the housing association movement.

It is appropriate to consider the loss of rights for housing association tenants because it could have an impact on many hundreds if not thousands of tenants shortly after the implementation of the Bill. Under the Tenants' Rights, Etc. (Scotland) Act 1980 tenants' rights provisions, consolidated in the Housing (Scotland) Act 1987, housing association tenants have unequivocal security of tenure. One of the key features of the right on which the Government properly built was wider security of tenure, which included the test of reasonableness with regard to proceedings for recovery of possession. Thus the sheriff, in making any order for recovery of possession in favour of the landlord has to be sure, first, that the landlord has a ground for recovery of possession—that is fair enough—and secondly that it is reasonable to make the order in the circumstances.

I served happily as a solicitor in Jedburgh sheriff court between 1980 and 1983, so I saw for myself how those provisions were put into effect by sheriff courts throughout the land. By and large, once the teething JPEG 507 troubles were resolved, the provisions worked well. On a number of occasions in Jedburgh sheriff court the sheriff determined the question of reasonableness and acted as final arbiter in eviction decision. That fall-back position was used to good effect by tenants threatened with eviction and the safety net afforded by the test of reasonableness was one of the cornerstones of the tenants' charter provisions of 1980.

Tenants of housing associations and housing co-operatives were given security of tenure in 1980, and tenants of local authorities, new towns and the SSHA were accorded the same protection under that act. Under the Bill, however, in clause 12 and in schedules 4 and 5, tenants of new towns, local authorities, the SSHA and in the future Scottish Homes will retain their security of tenure as set out in the 1980 and 1987 Acts, but tenants of housing associations and co-operatives will lose their basic security of tenure, which will be replaced by the limited security of an assured tenancy. Perhaps the Minister will confirm clearly that that legal analysis is correct.

In the proceedings for recovery of possession, future housing association tenants will lose the test of reasonableness. That is the strongest suit in my case for amendment No. 162. If the Government accept the amendment, tenants of housing associations and co-operatives continue to enjoy security of tenure based on the test of reasonableness.

Under the Bill, if my analysis is correct, future housing association tenants will be subject to the same 17 grounds for possession as assured tenancies. Those grounds are listed in schedule 5, which provides that the first 11 will be

"Grounds on which Court must order possession"

Clause 12, by transferring all future housing association tenants to assured tenancies, brings about a major reduction in the security of tenure which tenants of housing association and co-operatives have enjoyed since 1980.

Future housing association tenants, including re-lets, will also lose rights with regard to succession and the right to arbitration over rent levels as a result of the transfer to assured tenancies. The Government cannot argue that those are other than far-reaching changes. What has the Minister to say about that?

The provisions on tenants' choice in paragraph 6.10 the White Paper envisaged that existing public sector tenants would often wish to transfer to a housing association or a co-operative, but future housing association tenants will have significantly fewer rights than tenants of local authorities, new towns and the SSHA or the 83,000 tenants of Scottish Homes. In those circumstances, it is difficult if not impossible to envisage any public sector tenant in his or her right mind wishing to transfer to a housing association or co-operative as it would involve a significant loss of rights. Housing association tenancies will thus be unattrative not only to housing association tenants but to all public sector tenants.

The Scottish Federation of Housing Association is right to believe that, far from assisting the process of 508 diversification of tenure developed in recent years through the growth of housing associations and co-operatives, the proposal to introduce assured tenancies for future housing association tenants will severely arrest that process. If transfers of public sector tenants are to occur in future, they are far more likely to be to Scottish Homes, where tenants' rights will be maintained. The process has already begun wih the transfer of 1,077 houses in Castlemiik to the SSHA. We had discussions about that this morning. It is probable that Scottish Homes will be a far more attractive proposition for tenants than housing associations and co-operatives, so far from producing diversification and greater choice, these provisions are more likely to cause a significant expansion in the number of tenants of Scottish Homes.

I asked the Minister questions about that this morning, but I am still at a loss to understand what his answers amounted to. I shall study the Official Report with care as I cannot understand what on earth the Government are trying to achieve with these provisions. They are clearly going against the logic of their own case. Having discussed the matter in some detail with the Scottish Federation of Housing Associations, I believe that the Bill will lead inexorably to the position that I have described.

If the Government are not prepared to look carefully at the clause again, this part of the Bill will be fatally flawed and will not work. The Government must tell the Committee what they think they are doing by moving in this direction. If the Minister cannot satisfy us today, other members of the Committee will certainly wish to return the matter on Report.

It is really an English measure, produced for English reasons, with an assured tenancy to deal with the particular problems of, for example, the shortage in the letting market in London. I have had experience of trying to find a flat in London, so I know how difficult it is. The English measures are being transported en bloc to Scotland through this Bill.

The Government Scottish Front Bench has a particular duty, especially with a Secretary of State who is an advocate, to pay some regard to the peculiarities and uniqueness of Scots law. To allow pressure to be applied from other parts of the Government to transport this measure en bloc into Scotland is reprehensible. These significant changes to Scottish housing law are being made virtually without comment and accepted by the Scottish Office without any real consideration. The Bill, particularly part II, does not relate to Scottish housing conditions or the specific problems of Scotland. It is an English measure being imported to Scotland for political reasons, and it has nothing to do with the proper needs of Scotland.
