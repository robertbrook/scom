Mr. Walker

Many of the tenement blocks that were built in the centres of our large cities were built to a high standard. I think that in Glasgow blocks that had tiles on the walls were separated from those that did not. In Dundee, which I know much better, the separation was not always to do with tiles on the walls. Often it had to do with the amenities available. Properties with shared toilets on the stairs were not considered to be suitable facilities for families in the middle or latter part of the 20th century, and many had to be either substantially modified or knocked down.

There were also many fine tenements, however, with excellent flats and very good facilities, including bathrooms and the like. Certainly, Dundee had many tenements of that kind. Young people aspiring to their first home used to obtain lettings in such tenements in the days when private landlords existed. Nearly all, if not all, the tenement blocks of that kind in Dundee were leased blocks, rented out through private landlords. That gave young people the opportunity to have homes near the city centre and close to their place of work, or they could save up to purchase, as some did, although in my recollection few could manage that.

The Labour party set out as a matter of policy to destroy the opportunities for those who could not buy their homes and had to rent. It was largely the private sector that offered that facility up to and immediately after the 1939–45 war. Legislation was then brought in which made it no longer viable for landlords to let those properties because they could not maintain them, and large housing estates were built on the periphery of cities. Again I refer mainly to the city that I know well—Dundee—but the problem was the same in other cities. People were then forced on to waiting lists for houses in the peripheral housing schemes. Today, the problems of those schemes are legend. Many of them were put in the wrong place without any proper amenities and without any sense of community. Dundee has often been described as the largest village in Scotland. When I was a young man, if one walked along the high street on a Saturday or Sunday evening, the chances were that one would know every other person one met. Everyone had a feeling of belonging, but this was rapidly destroyed first, because there were no lettings available in the private sector because it ceased to exist and, secondly, because the peripheral housing schemes took people further and further away from the city.

JPEG 583 One has only to look at some of the streets of Dundee which had fine houses and tenement buildings to realise that they have been removed from the market for young people looking for their first let. Many have become properties in which people have bought flats. Lest is be suggested that Conservative Members do not understand the situation in Dundee—a number of honourable Members know it intimately—the tenement buildings that I have cited and many more were beautiful buildings, built to last for ever if properly looked after. The problem was that they could not be properly looked after if the rents were not economic and were insufficient to maintain the fabric of the buildings. These valuable assets either fell into disrepair or were turned into flats for sale. In recent times, with assistance from the public purse, many of the old tenements have been restored to their former state and some letting, though not nearly enough, is taking place. I am sorry to say that more of the property has gone on the market for sale, although it is good that at the earliest opportunity young people should invest in a home of their own if they can.

The amendment deals, quite properly, with those who, by virtue of the state of the market today, are forced into mulitple occupancy because they cannot afford anything else. The solution to the problem is to make it possible for them to get lets and leases on flats where they can be on their own, as no young couple setting up home for the first time wants to share with other people. The element in the Bill that will make it possible for the private sector once again to return into the leasing market is probably the most important.

Equally, it is important to recognise that the Labour party was responsible for the dramatic shift away from the positoin that existed before and immediately after the 1939–45 war.
