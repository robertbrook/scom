Mr. John McAllion
(Dundee, East)
: When the Committee was interrupted on Tuesday night I was emphasising the irrelevance of the short assured tenancy as a means of tackling the housing problems in Scotland. The homeless and the inadequately housed have nothing to gain from tenancies that will initially give security of tenure for six months but, thereafter, will allow the landlord to evict the tenants. It is important to grasp the seriousness of the lack of security of tenure under short assured tenancies.

On Tuesday, the Minister repeatedly referred to his hope—as there was no evidence for the point that he was trying to stress, it could only be a fond hope—that rents under short assured tenancies would be lower than those under assured tenancies. He took great comfort from that hope, as it gave him reason to believe that landlords would not, as we allege, continually and predominantly let their property on short assured tenancies in preference to assured tenancies. The Minister claimed that the higher rent regime would be sufficient incentive for landlords to opt for assured tenancies. That claim does not bear examination because it fails to recognise the fundamental link between security of tenure and the rents likely to be demanded by landlords.

Fear of dispossession nullifies any legal rights that tenants might have to fair rents or to apply to rent assessment committees for rent determination. Clause 31 gives tenants in short assured tenancies the right to make such an application, but that right is subject to clause 25 (4) which states:

"unless the landlord and the tenant otherwise agree, the rent determined by the committee shall be the rent under the tenancy".

 The critical phrase is:

"unless the landlord and the tenant otherwise agree".

 That provides a loophole that will enable landlords to insist upon an agreement. That is tantamount to landlords imposing on tenants settlements with which they do not agree, but which, because of their in security, they will be forced to accept.

The Minister might argue that few landlords would exploit the situation to impose unjustifiable rents on frightened tenants. However, the Minister has refused to impose an upper limit in the duration of short assured tenancies and there are landlords in Scotland who will use the Bill not as the Government 784 wish, but to obtain the maximum advantage for themselves. Landlords will be able to continue short assured tenancies ad infinitum yet be able to get round the rent assessment committee provisions by frightening tenants with eviction to secure agreement to higher rents than would be reasonable under such tenancies.

The Minister tells us that rents will be lower under short assured tenancies than under assured tenancies, but he has not understood that lack of security of tenure will enable landlords to frighten tenants so that they will not avail themselves of their rights. Even if the Minister believes that such cases will be the exception, he must surely accept that control of exceptional behaviour is the purpose of legislation. The Committee must be certain that the Bill will deal with any possible abuse. I do not believe that the Minister has recognised that.

If the Committee votes to allow landlords to use their strength to force higher rents upon tenants than would be possible under strict interpretation of the Bill, we shall be as guilty as those who perpetrate such exploitation. We must be fully aware of the implication of what we do. I hope that all Ministers who wish to have a clear conscience will vote to make such exploitation impossible. I ask the Minister to take this matter seriously.

The Minister justifies the clause on the basis that 130,000 houses in Scotland are not currently let. He tells us that two thirds of them are in the private sector. Is he seriously suggesting that those houses are not on the market because landlords do not have available to them short assured tenancies which would allow the properties to be let for six-monthly periods only? That appears to be his only argument. He has given us no evidence that 90,000 houses will flood back on to the housing market if short assured tenancies are available. The Minister will have to persuade us by substantiating his claim that the bulk of the houses in the private sector are not on the market today because of the lack of short assured tenancies. Throughout our sittings the Minister has repeatedly made claims and allegations which he has failed to substantiate.

I know that some Conservative Members take the Bill seriously, listen to debates and worry about loopholes that will allow exploitation. The hon. and learned Member for Perth and Kinross (Mr. Fairbairn) is one and I am sure that he is aware that the Minister's argument that the Bill provides reduced security of tenure in exchange for lower rents does not stand up. Because of the loophole, tenants could be frightened by the prospect of eviction into not appealing under the provisions in the Bill. We must not allow short assured tenancies to be part of the Bill until we close the loophole that allows unscrupulous landlords to use pressure against tenants who are in a weaker position.

The amendment would remove the reference in subsection (1), which enables a tenant to refer his rent to the rent assessment committee only if he believes that the rent set by his landlord

"is significantly higher than the rents payable under similar tenancies of similar houses in the locality."

 We believe that all tenants in short assured tenancies should have the right of access to the rent assessment committee. We are worried that landlords, particularly in the early stages of this type of tenure, may try to jack up rents to unreasonable levels. It is important that tenants should have a clear right of access to a committee which will be able to review their rent.

The amendment would also prevent the onus being put on the tenant to prove that he considers his rent to be significantly higher than others payable under similar tenancies before satisfying the rent assessment committee that it has jurisdiction to consider his appeal. As the Bill stands, a tenant under a short assured tenancy has a right to make application to a rent assessment committee for a determination of his rent only if he considers that the rent payable

"is significantly higher than the rents payable under similar tenancies of similar houses in the locality."

 That wording in the Bill is too restrictive and is likely to give rise to difficulties. For instance, what does the word "significantly" mean? Will it be up to the tenant to prove that he has grounds for considering that his rent is significantly higher than rents payable under similar tenancies before the rent assessment committee will consider his referral? What about cases where a tenant's rent is considered to be higher than other rents payable under similar tenancies but is not considered to be "significantly" higher?

The tenant will first have to find out what other rents are in his neighbourhood and will then have to prove that he thinks that his rent is "significantly" higher, whatever that means, than those of his neighbours, before the rent assessment committee can even start thinking about it. Even then that is not JPEG 819 the end of his troubles. How is a tenant supposed to go about finding out what rents are for similar properties in his neighbourhood? How can an individual tenant do that? Is he to go round knocking on doors saying, "Excuse me, what rent are you paying to your landlord? Is your house the same as mine? Can I come in with a measuring tape and measure it up and see what the accommodation is like?" I suspect that a number of neighbours might tell a tenant trying to obtain that kind of information where he could go and to mind his own business, but all he will be trying to do is to mind his own business and protect himself from having to pay an excessive rent. Surely it is the job of the rent assessment committee to work out the comparators and what the fair rents should be, and every tenant should have a right of access to the committee so that it can establish what the rent should be.

The wording of the subsection that the rent must be

"significantly higher than the rents payable under similar tenancies of similar houses in the locality"

 could cause great problems, especially in the short term, where there are unlikely to be enough similar tenancies of similar houses in a locality against which the tenant can compare his own rent. That is of particular importance given the likelihood of the tenant having to prove that he considers his rent to be significantly high in comparison. Far too many hurdles will be placed in the way of tenants who try to have their rent brought down to a fairer level.

In current market conditions, and given that in the short term there will not be a pool of similar tenancies in the locality, the clause as worded would leave the door wide open for litigation by landlords regarding the rent assessment committee's jurisdiction to consider an application for a determination of rent—in other words, whether the rent assessment committee can even start to intervene in the case. My hon. Friend the Member for Aberdeen, South (Mr. Doran) said a minute or two ago that he was grateful to the Government for creating fruitful employment for solicitors and lawyers in Scotland in future. This is yet another example. A landlord could try to prevent the case even being considered by the rent assessment committee on the ground that it did not fulfil the criteria in the Bill.

I suppose that it could be argued that, given that there are no similar tenancies of similar houses in a locality, or very few, the tenant could not consider his rent to be significantly higher and thus had no right to make an application to the rent assessment committee, so he would be cut off on those grounds. If that argument held, the rent assessment committee would have no jurisdiction to consider the application at all.

We believe that all short assured tenants should have the right to make application to the rent assessment committee for a determination of rent which, in the committee's opinion, the landlord might reasonably be expected to obtain. As the clause is worded, the tenant's right to make application is neither direct nor clear. It would be far too easy, particularly in the crucial initial stages of this type of tenancy, for landlords to prevent such cases being considered by the rent assessment committee at all.
