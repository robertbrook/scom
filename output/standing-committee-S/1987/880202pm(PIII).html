Mr. Nigel Griffiths
: I do not wish to waste any more of the Committee's time on amendment No. 73. There are seven other amendments that I have not yet touched on.

It is important to ensure that under schedule 1 we have a board that is representative of housing in Scotland and of all the other co-ordinating bodies and services that affect Scottish housing. That is the aim of the amendments. It is especially important to ensure that the Secretary of State does not restrict the board to nine members appointed by him. It would be better to have an arbitrary selection of nine people than to rely on the method that the Secretary of State has adopted to date. With a limit of 15, there is less chance of the Secretary of State finding 15 Conservative clones. That is the main drive behind amendment No. 74.

If the limit remains nine, the board of Scottish Homes will have no credibility in Scotland. The national housing agency will be subject to widespread ridicule and will have no chance of remedying serious housing problems. That is why we seek an increase to a number that people will feel to be credible.

The Minister said that a board of nine—or, indeed, seven—had been shown to be representative, but it has been clearly demonstrated that those people were not representative. Neither the public nor their elected representatives have had confidence in them. It has taken the personal intervention of elected councillors or Members of Parliament to shift them. The most that even a Member of Parliament seems to 344 have achieved is a minor playground or play scheme in his constituency.

We want more than that. We wish to ensure that the tenants have direct representation and the right to influence their rents, which the Government have attacked in council housing and housing associations. We read yesterday of the increase in the rents by the Scottish Special Housing Association without any consultation with tenants. That must end. We need to see tenants being more involved in the management policies that affect their houses.

Again, this is a direction in which some councils are moving, and some councils have adopted local management committees and are to be commended for that. But Scottish Homes gives no say to those tenants, no encouragement, no responsibility—a favourite word of the Government—and by failing to guarantee even one tenants' representative on Scottish Homes, the Government are denying the tenants the chance to act in a responsible, representative manner about their own housing. We have heard about severe problems of dampness. A tenant member appointed to the board would be the person to draw attention to those severe problems, because he himself might live in a damp house and be ideally placed to present a tenant's point of view to the board.

Equally, unless we guarantee a member of the housing profession on the board—perhaps someone nominated by the Institute of Housing—who can speak for those officers in local authority housing departments who have a great deal of experience, there is no guarantee that the mistakes of the present will not be repeated and the mistakes of the past will not be dug up again, to be inflicted on all the tenants of the housing associations for which Scottish Homes will be responsible.

Those are two groups of people who should, we believe, by statute be represented on the board of Scottish Homes: the tenants themselves, who are the consumers of the service, working in partnership with the managers of the service, who should be almost the employees of the tenants. The managers could thus give from their expert advice and experience concrete data on the problems of management and the scale of the problems in Scottish Homes and say whether there was a need for a house condition survey. I have no doubt that both managers and tenants would unit in calling for such a survey and for proper resources to be made available.

Perhaps in that we reach the nub of the problem. The Government are not willing to give either the tenants or representatives of the housing professions a direct say in Scottish Homes in case they raise such embarrassing issues. They are the sorts of embarrassing issues that are paralleled on the health boards, the Government's policy towards which has been much maligned in the past few hours and days. Health boards are unaware of the problems that their local areas suffer beyond the positive plus and minus balance sheets—that is the accountant mentality that the Minister responsible for health and the Government have inflicted on the health boards. We want to avoid that with Scottish Homes.

JPEG 345 We want to make sure that people are put on to the Scottish Homes board who represent the tenants and who work in the housing service, to make sure that the money available is spent wisely. If cuts have to come—and the Government have plenty of experience in cutting housing budgets—they must not cut almost at random and close at random, as has happened with health boards, but plan for progress in the future and, if necessary, stand independently against the Government to make sure that they are made aware of the shortcomings of their policy.

The Government seek to avoid that, apparently at all costs. I commend the amendments because within them we have the twin benefits of representation of the people who will be most affected by the decisions that Scottish Homes takes, and the secondary benefit of making sure that resources are well used within an independent framework and not subject to Government whims or bias and not subject to the accusations, true or untrue, of people being just "place men" on the body. Let us have elections to the board, a greater representation and more members, thereby avoiding all the pitfalls that I have described.

I shall take your advice, Mr. Lambie, and return to the purpose of the amendments before us, especially to amendment No. 73, which deals with members of the Scottish Homes board having their main residence in Scotland. The justification for the amendment is that a body such as Scottish Homes has a wide remit and powers over Scottish housing, and it is only sensible that its members should understand its distinctive features and problems, and also the issues and problems which the new agency will deal with. That will be difficult if they do not live in Scotland.

It is common sense that anyone who agrees to come on to the board of Scottish Homes will also agree to have his main residence in Scotland. The board is not only to deal with Scottish housing problems, but must experience them at first hand. For example, in earlier sittings it has been remarked that a distinctive feature of Scottish housing is the extent of overcrowding. There are 1.25 million Scots living in overcrowded accommodation. That does not happen in England and Wales. Conditions such as exist in Scotland today have not existed in England JPEG 347 and Wales for the past 20 or 30 years. Any member of the Scottish Homes board who has his or her main residence in England or Wales will not be able to grasp the significance of the overcrowding problem in Scotland. Similarly, the predominance of the public rented sector, which is a feature of the Scottish housing scene, is different from the situation in England and Wales or elsewhere. How can members of the board of Scottish Homes who do not appreciate that difference come to terms with the problems which the new agency will have to address?

My honourable Friend the Member for Aberdeen, South (Mr. Doran) discussed the important issue of the pattern which has begun to be established in Scotland of control over distinctly Scottish affairs being moved out of our country and south of the border. He referred to Britoil and the likely takeover by British Petroleum, which will not be stopped by the Government. The Scottish Transport Group and the electricity industry are under similar threat.

If we go ahead with the provisions of the Bill, which would allow the nine members of the Scottish Homes board to come from outwith Scotland, it will conform to a pattern which every member of the Committee should be aware of and should be alarmed and prepared to do something about. What they could do is agree to the amendment saying that members of the Scottish Homes board should have their main residence in Scotland.

The amendments also argue for a wider membership of the board, in particular, to go beyond the provision for a minimum of nine members on the new board. There is justification for that provision in that, since Scottish Homes will have wide powers over housing affairs in Scotland, it is important that the members should not be restricted to nine, as that might well limit their potential to respond to the challenge of tackling Scottish housing problems, as well as limiting the diversity of interests represented on the board of the new agency. Furthermore, it would be unfortunate if, by failing to provide for a minimum membership, such a large and influential organisation were controlled by what might be unrepresentative and inexperienced members.

Above all, it is important that we do not simply leave the membership of Scottish boards in the hands of the Secretary of State for Scotland, who will appoint all nine members as he sees fit. As we have seen from the experience of health boards in Scotland, that power should not be left in the hands of the Secretary of State. It is tragic that, in the present crisis over the Health Service in Scotland, all but one of the chairmen of the health boards in Scotland are sympathetic to the Conservative party, and therefore antipathetic to the cause which the majority of Scots support, which goes against the Government's policy, funding the Health Service from general taxation.

Mr. Doran
: I beg to move amendment No. 109 in page 41, leave out lines 20 to 26.

The effect of the amendment would be to delete paragraph 7(3) from the Bill. The paragraph proposes that

"Where a person (other than the chief executive)"—

obviously, separate arrangements are made for him—

"ceases to be a member of Scottish Homes otherwise than on the expiry of his term of office, and it appears to the Secretary of State that there are special circumstances which might make it right for that person to receive compensation, the Secretary of State may, with the approval of the Treasury, direct Scottish Homes to make that person a payment of such amount as the Secretary of State may, with the approval of the Treasury, determine."

The Committee has spent much time today discussing the appointment of members to the board of Scottish Homes and its composition, with a view to ensuring that certain interests are represented. The Minister has given us some assurances, but we shall wait to see how watertight they are in practice.

JPEG 369 However, we must raise our concern about paragraph 7(3), because it appears to introduce a new aspect into housing legislation. It seems that it will allow the Secretary of State, who has the power to dismiss a board member or to dispense with his services, to compensate him for the loss of his position on the board. There are a number of ideological points that I want to make about that, because it appears to tie in with what we have said about the develoment of Scottish housing and how Scottish Homes will assist in it.

I have argued before that the Bill will completely change the structure and position of Scottish housing. It will introduce a new element into it which has nothing to do with the basic ideas which honourable Members on both sides of the Committee have recognised, such as the need to provide cheap, reasonable housing of good quality. The problem of homelessness will not be tackled. Housing will simply be regarded as an investment. Here we see the Secretary of State being given unfettered powers to appoint the members of a new body which will concentrate heavily on privatisation of and investment in housing—it will be able to lend in exactly the same way as banks and building lend, for example. He is also being given the power to make separate payments, which may develop into the power to pay transfer fees, for all we know. The power is completely new.

The consitution of the Housing Corporation which appears in the Housing Associations Act 1985 contains no such power. It states that the Secretary of State—not the Secretary of State for Scotland—

"may pay the … Deputy Chairman and members such remuneration as he may, with the consent of the Treasury, determine".

That is perfectly reasonable, as the deputy chairman and members are giving up their valuable time to perform a valuable public function. However, nowhere is there a reference to severance pay or entitlement to it. There is the ability to pay a pension, which again is appropriate and I would not argue against it, and we see a similar provision in paragraph 8 of the schedule, where the Secretary of State has the power to make such an arrangement for certain members of the board.

There is one further disturbing aspect of the provision. Even in respect of the payment of pensions which the Secretary of State may make under the Housing Associations Act 1985 to certain members of the board of the Housing Corporation, paragraph 6(2) of schedule 6 to that Act, which contains the constitution of the Housing Corporation, states:

"As soon as may be after the making of such a determination—

that determination being the determination of the level of pension for those officers—

"the Secretary of State shall lay before each House of Parliament a statement of the amount payable in pursuance of the determination."

That is a perfectly reasonable arrangement: the Secretary of State will determine pensions for those officers who have given their time, but thereafter he is immediately accountable to the House and must 370 lay before it a statement of the amount of pensions. It is not hidden away in the corporation's accounts or something that we shall find only by examining the minute detail of the annual report.

With the introduction of severance pay, which highlights the commercial aspect of Scottish Homes, there is no such provision for accountability. Although we are dealing with a much larger organisation, there is no accountability. Therefore, Opposition Members are entitled to ask the Government serious and searching questions about what they intend by this brand new provision and why they believe it is necessary to make such severance payments to all board members of Scottish Homes when they did not previously see the need to pay them to Housing Corporation officers. If the Government can justify that, why do they not include in the Bill the protection given in the Housing Associations Act 1985 which enables honourable Members to scrutinise the Secretary of State's determination?

Lord James Douglas-Hamilton
: I am happy to reassure honourable Members that the provisions of paragraph 7(3) of the schedule are well precedented. A similar provision occurs in paragraph 9 of schedule 1 to the Scottish Development Agency Act 1975.

Ordinary retirement pensions, allowances or gratuities and payments in cases of death are covered by paragraph 8 of the schedule. The amendment is concerned with payments made in special circumstances, not at the end of a member's term of office.

This provision enables Scottish Homes and the Secretary of State to deal with unusual occurrences which may arise. Inevitably such occurrences will be rare, as I hope they will be. One example may be where a change in the tasks to which a body is directed makes it desirable to change the compositoin of the board membership to bring in new skills or knowledge, and it is necessary to ask an able member who has given good service but who is not as well placed to make a contribution of the new tasks to resign. Other circumstances might occur to honourable Members, but I suggest that there is little to be gained from examining the range of hypothetical cases and even less from my offering a view on whether such a case might merit payment under the paragraph. The paragraph is merely a facility that will be retained in case it is needed. I suggest that the honourable Gentleman seeks leave to withdraw his amendment.
