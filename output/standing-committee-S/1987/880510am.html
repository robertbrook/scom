Mr. Bruce
: The purpose of the amendment is self-evident. It is serious and worth while and should be taken into account. The Government's proposals are that the electorates for school boards are effectively parents who have children at a given school and that they should be the only people to have the right to elect parent members. The clause seeks to extend that right to pupils at the school over the age of 16. The proposal would obviously commend itself not only to the pupils, who would have a direct say in the direction of the school and with the people taking policy decisions, but to a wider community.

JPEG 241 The school-leaving age is 16, so by extending the franchise effectively to those who choose to stay on at 16, we are by definition talking about pupils who wish to be at school, who have a direct interest in further studies at the school and probably thereafter, and who are clearly showing a degree of academic maturity that warrants their views being taken into account. I have often challenged the Government's view that the consumers of education are the parents.

The consumers of education are the children, but, because they are under age, their parents and guardians are statutorily responsible for ensuring that they receive full-time education. However, that is no longer so when pupils reach the age of 16, when there is no statutory requirement for them to stay on at school. I do not deny that parental influence is important, but it is primarily the pupils themselves who elect whether to stay on at school. They should be encouraged to participate in elections relating to their school boards.

The Government and the Minister are in favour of consumerism and consumer choice, so as a side issue, it is fair to record that the Consumers Association would support an amendment such as this one. I must qualify that statement by saying that I do not endorse all of what the Consumers Association has said about the Bill. However, this proposal has some merit, and I put it forward in a genuine spirit of constructive inquiry.

The Minister should tell us what the role of pupils is. If he is so keen to extend democracy, young people between the ages of 16 and 18 who are at school should be encouraged to participate in it. They are the ones who will be affected, and they could represent the interests of younger children. No one is suggesting that children under the age of 16 should have a vote, although they should have a say. It is likely that pupils over the age of 16 will wish to take account of preferences expressed by younger pupils.

Parents, teachers and Government Ministers are not always the best judges of what makes good schools and good teachers. Talking to pupils gives a better idea of who is a good teacher and why and what is a good, constructive atmosphere in a school. That is particularly true if one talks to pupils who have a clear and positive interest in pursuing education, rather than those who are there under sentence of law and are anxious to leave.

Pupils could also provide a useful input to the board. I am not suggesting at this stage that they should be able to elect any of their own number. The amendment suggests that, as part of the constituency, they should be entitled to choose which of the parent representatives they prefer. In those circumstances, they are unlikely to be the majority of the voters, but their views should be taken into account. Many parents would find it useful to hear senior pupils' worries about equipment, discipline and the general running, atmosphere and academic state of the school.

I anticipate the criticism that it is inappropriate effectively to allow young people to act as judges of their teachers. I repeat that the amendment merely gives pupils the right to participate in the election of parent board members. Pupils may often have access to 242 information that is not directly available to parents and may be able to provide a different perspective on the information that is available. For example, pupils might be able to stress that differentials in exam results could be due to internal circumstances that could be changed and are not necessarily an adverse or unduly positive comment on the atmosphere within the school.

The amendment deals with young people who will reach their legal majority in two years and will then be able to vote in parliamentary and local elections. It would help to prepare them for that role by encouraging them to participate in elections that directly affect the running of their school. That would be a reasonable way of introducing them to the democratic process. The amendment is modest, because it simply encourages them to participate in a healthy and constructive way. I hope that the amendment will be treated in a constructive way.

I shall be delighted if the Minister accepts the amendment, but, if he does not, what steps could usefully be taken to ensure that senior pupils will be involved in the running of their schools? School boards will significantly change the way in which schools are run. Pupils should be encouraged to have a say in that. I should like to give them more say than the amendment proposes, but I have deliberately pitched the amendment in modest terms so that it can fit into the Bill, and not fundamentally alter the structure of the boards or the way in which they operate. The amendment will ensure that senior pupils are treated in a responsible manner and encouraged to respond accordingly. I hope that the Committee regards that as a constructive development that has wider implications in the long run, is healthy for democracy, and will create good citizens who accept responsibility for their own community.
