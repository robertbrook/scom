The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton)
: Before the adjournment I was explaining that the amendment seeks to prevent Scottish Homes from collecting community charge, on behalf of a regional council, from its tenants and their families. This function is no different from that carried out at present by the Scottish Special Housing Association and local housing authorities. They collect rates, along with rents, as a service to tenants and as an assistance to regional councils. That is, of course, a good example of the close co-operation between Scottish Homes and local authorities that Opposition Members were so keen to ensure.

Mr. John Home Robertson
(East Lothian)

Paragraph 5(1) of schedule 2 to the Abolition of Domestic Rates Etc. (Scotland) Act 1987 is specific. It says:

"Subject to sub-paragraph (3) below, a levying authority may make arrangements with a housing body for the exercise by the housing body on behalf of the levying authority of any of the authority's functions under this Schedule."

I think that the Minister will concede that Scottish Homes can be considered to be a housing body and therefore to be covered by the Act. It could therefore be asked to issue poll tax demand notices, for example, for the 83,000 SSHA tenants that it takes over together with any other tenants who come under its auspices.

The Minister himself drew attention to the fact that the SSHA already collected the rates through rent payments, but that agreement was mutually and cordially entered into between district councils and the SSHA. It was also convenient for the tenants to pay their rates with the rents, so there was no problem about that. Everyone was satisfied with the arrangements.

That will not be the case with the poll tax. As my hon. Friend the Member for Aberdeen, South (Mr. Doran) fairly pointed out, the poll tax is not like the rates. No one householder will pay the rates for the whole house. Every individual in the house will have to pay his or her poll tax. Nor will one member of the household be able to make five or six different payments with the rent. Each person will have to go to Scottish Homes and pay his or her individual poll tax. One cheque cannot cover five or six different poll taxes. For instance, in my house only my wife and I will be liable to pay the poll tax. The children are too young as yet, although I would not put it past the Government to levy poll tax on children as well. I cannot sign one cheque for the two of us. My wife has to sign a separate cheque. Children have to pay separately from their parents. A cheque from one person will constitute payment only of that person's poll tax.

A large administrative burden will thus be placed on Scottish Homes. It will need special officers to collect the poll tax, not from 83,000 tenants but from 83,000 households. As many as 240,000 people in Scottish Homes houses will be liable to pay the poll tax and will have to go to Scottish Homes to pay the tax individually month by month. Like any other body for which the Government are responsible, Scottish Homes will have a limited amount of cash. The Minister is suggesting that part of that cash should be used to collect the poll tax. That seems to me to be nonsense.

Paragraph 5(1) of schedule 2 to the 1987 Act goes on to say that any of the functions of the levying authority described in the schedule can be carried out by Scottish Homes. That includes receiving appeals against demand notices. If Scottish Homes issues a demand notice telling the people in one of its houses how much poll tax they have to pay, those people have the right to appeal to Scottish Homes against that demand. Scottish Homes will have to set up an appeals procedure to deal with 418 people complaining about the amount of poll tax levied on them. Moreover, if tenants not satisfied with the decision reached by Scottish Homes on their appeal, they can then appeal to the sheriff court over the heads of Scottish Homes. Scottish Homes would then have to go to the sheriff court and reply to the appeal. It will therefore have yet another administrative duty which has nothing to do with housing and will not help to tackle the housing crisis in Scotland but will take up the valuable time of Scottish Homes officers and professional employees.

The absurd situation then arises whereby if the levying authority and Scottish Homes fail to reach agreement ort the terms under which Scottish Homes takes over the levying authority's poll tax functions they can go to the Secretary of State for Scotland to arbitrate between them and to enforce an agreement. There can be no question of real arbitration in those circumstances. While a regional council, which is likely to be the levying authority, is distinct from the Secretary of State for Scotland—indeed, it is likely to be under Labour control and to have a very different view of the poll tax from the Secretary of State for Scotland—Scottish Homes will not be independent of the Secretary of State for Scotland. It will be the lackey of the Secretary of State for Scotland, so in no sense can he arbitrate in a disagreement between the two bodies. He is on one side of the argument all the time and will persistently find in favour of Scottish Homes and against the levying authority, the regional council. It goes without saying that he can also require Scottish Homes to accept the arrangements.

It is not simply a question of the functions laid down in paragraph 5 of schedule 2 to the 1987 Act. Under section 17 of the Act the community charge registration officer has a general duty

"to take all reasonable steps to obtain such information as is reasonably required by him"

in relation to registration. What kind of information could be reasonably required by the community charges registration officer? He will certainly want to know who is in a house at any given time so that he can track people down and put them on the register. He will want to know about the financial circumstances of the people in the house, whether they are in employment and in receipt of wages, whether they would qualify for a rebate scheme which is still to be decided on by the Government. Such information will have to be available, if not to the registration officer at least to the levying authority. He will also want to know who is responsible under the 1987 Act for completing the register, and such information will have to be supplied to him by various bodies.

Section 17 of the Act goes on to say that the registration officer may require any housing body

"to supply him with such information as he may reasonably require in connection with his functions, being information which … the housing body have in connection with any of their functions; and the … housing body shall comply with such a requirement."

The Minister tried to pretend earlier that Scottish Homes would not be subject to that part of the Abolition of Domestic Rates Etc. (Scotland) Act, but it seems clear that the registration officer can require housing bodies to supply him with information that he needs to compile the community charges register. That means that Scottish Homes as a housing body will be included in that part of the Act and will have to supply to the registration officer such information as he may require.
