Mr. Nigel Griffiths
(Edinburgh, South)
: I beg to move amendment No. 51, in page 2, line 12, at end add:

'(h) funding, providing, and assisting in the provision of a programme to eliminate and prevent the problems of dampness and condensation within the housing stock.'.

I welcome you to the Committee, Mr. Lambie, on behalf of all members of the Committee.

We come to one of the most lamentable areas of the Government's custodianship—the presiding over a cut in spending on the eradication of dampness and condensation. It is not as though they are ignorant of the scale of the problem. The 1984 report by the Select Committee on Scottish Affairs, "Dampness in Housing", which was honoured by your chairmanship, Mr. Lambie, examined the plague of dampness in Scotland in detail and concluded that the information available was inadequate, that arrangements for collecting statistics and data on dampness were inadequate and that the collection of the data was unnecessarily complicated.

No less an authority than the honourable Member for Edinburgh, West (Lord James Douglas-Hamilton), now the Minister responsible for housing, grilled the honourable Member for Eastwood (Mr. Stewart), the Minister responsible for housing at that time, about those deficiencies. He asked:

"Is the Minister aware that there is a very strong feeling that this allocation"—

the allocation for spending on dampness—

"has not been made on an objective assessment on the basis of need"?

At the time the Government were saying that only so much money could be spent, but the present Under Secretary of State questioned the basis on which they would decide how to cut the cake. He went on to ask:

"Is the Minister aware that, on the basis of an objective assessment, the feeling is that these figures bear no relation to the needs of the situation, as the Committee saw when we visited Edinburgh?"

The Government's hands are not clean in this respect and the Scottish Development Department returns show that the position is growing worse. This year will see a disgraceful drop in the amount of money that councils have available to combat dampness. According to the Government's figures, the figure is dropping from £78 million last year to JPEG 221 £69,500,000 in the coming financial year and will be about £70 million by 1991–92. Does the Minister accept that that is a disgrace? We are presiding over a decline in spending on dampness when, if anything, there is plenty of evidence that the amount of dampness is increasing as post-war system-built houses continue to deteriorate due to the Government's neglect in underfunding councils to tackle the problem.

The private sector has its problems, too, in relation to dampness. The Secretary of State told the Select Committee on Scottish Affairs that it was primarily a matter for private sector owners to identify and take steps to remedy problems of dampness and condensation in their houses.

That is a sad and disgraceful attitude towards some of the most elderly and vulnerable tenants in Scotland. The amendment would give the Government agency the primary legal responsibility to enforce an effective anti-dampness programme on uncaring landlords registered with Scottish Homes and to plan and fund the eradication of dampness in other Scottish housing.

The figures to which I have referred were produced in 1984. They have been updated and there have been a number of studies to estimate the number of damp houses, lest we be accused of using out-of-date figures. The Government's own labour force survey, carried out two years after the Select Committee on Scottish Affairs reported, estimated that a staggering one in four Scottish homes is affected by dampness. Of those half a million homes, 370,000 are in the public sector, more than 100,000 are owner occupied and 49,000 are privately rented. In many of those properties there is not merely mild condensation, which may be offensive, but severe dampness, which probably affects 160,000 houses in Scotland. Nearly half a million people have been consigned to what would have been a disgrace in the 19th century, let alone the 20th.

When the leader of the Opposition visited Burdiehouse drive in my constituency with me at the weekend, we were in streets where families have suffered from the effects of dampness. In Burdiehouse, and Southhouse, as in other parts of the capital and in towns and villages throughout Scotland, many homes are affected by dampness. Members who visit their constituents regularly in Edinburgh and in other parts of Scotland recognise the symptoms as soon as they reach many of the thresholds. A sickly sweet smell of rotting furnishings permeates the air. I have been taken into many flats where dampness has destroyed carpets, wardrobes, beds and bedding. Drawers have been opened to reveal clothing rotting inside them. In the past, I and my honourable Friends helped people to obtain the small grants available from the DHSS to help with the replacement of such items, and it is a matter of deep sadness that those grants have been all but abolished. It is a disgrace that for many families on the breadline there is now no Government help available for such items.

Items of furniture and clothing can be replaced, but the lungs of the children who live in those houses cannot be replaced, and there is conclusive proof that 222 dampness is making people ill. In 1986 Edinburgh university researchers completed a study on housing and health on a housing estate in Edinburgh which, like so many other housing estates, is badly affected by damp. The study found clear effects of damp on children's health. Respiratory bronchial symptoms, particularly, were far more common among children living in damp houses. Headaches, diarrhoea and other aches and pains were also much more frequently recorded for children in damp houses.

The authors of that study are much respected in their fields—Sonia Hunt, Claudia Martin and Stephen Piatt—are highly respected in their field. They go into the problems in horrific detail. They found that damp houses often breed mould fungi which emit mycotoxins, polluting the environment, settling on bedding and even food. Exposure to this can exacerbate asthma, bronchitis and alveolitis. A different mould, which grows on the walls of damp houses, has been found responsible for serious damage to internal organs after contaminating food. This affects not just a few people but an estimated 120,000 Scottish children whose health is at serious risk from damage because they live in very damp houses.

What do the Government intend to do about this? The spending plans, as I have said, reveal a drop over the next four years from £78 million in 1987 to less than £70 million in 1988 and to only £70 million by 1992. How can the Government contemplate spending less on this tragic problem in 1988 than in 1987 and 1986? Even if they are oblivious to the human suffering, they will be concerned about the property, a matter about which their class has always been concerned, so they will want to take steps to ensure that the cost of eradicating dampness is met now rather than having to meet a far higher cost in future. That is the nub of the problem. The Government have consistently failed both to assess the resources required to tackle dampness and to heed those who have warned about the damaging effects on people living in damp houses, especially children. They have failed to assess the scale of the problem and to programme the resources required to meet it.

The evidence of the Select Committee on Scottish Affairs has been ignored. The 1986 SDD dampness survey was ignored. The independent study of housing and health by Edinburgh university also seems to have been ignored. We now have the 1987 housing checklist returns from the 56 Scottish local authorities, but the Government seem unwilling to step in to ensure that proper planning and funding are provided. Instead of attacking the problem, the Government have withdrawn the resources, and the special allowances for help in heating damp houses have just been abolished. Amendment No. 51 seeks to provide a realistic approach to the problems of damp houses, placing responsibility for co-ordinating the planning to tackle dampness firmly on a Government agency.

When the Minister served on the Select Committee on Scottish Affairs he was not content to bury his JPEG 223 head in the sand as he appears to do now—he wanted the critical and vigorous approach of Government policies at that time. We ask him to continue such an approach to ensure that dampness is firmly tackled within a reasonable time scale. In terms of planning, that should be within five years. As one who is concerned about housing, it sticks in my throat that we have to set a time scale which will condemn many people to live in damp houses for five or more years, but we recognise that we are committing the Government to a major reversal of policy on spending on housing and on tackling dampness. To do that we must persuade them to accept our amendment and give them a reasonable time scale to achieve the results we want.

Shelter has described living in damp conditions and dampness itself as not just "a minor inconvenience" but "a major social evil." That is no overstatement of the case. We want the new agency—if it is set up—to have a remit to get to grips with the problems faced by authorities dealing with damp houses and by private landlords whose tenants suffer the effects of dampness. In particular, it should seek to deal with the problems of people living in houses which are now labelled as a health hazard, something that many of us have suspected for a long time.

Finally, the amendment gives the Government a chance to get to grips once and for all with one of the severest problems affecting our country as well as our housing today. This has been highlighted in a number of independent reports. The inquiry into housing in Glasgow specifically recommended a course of action similar to the one that I am advocating. Professor Grieve proposed that a technical appraisal should be commissioned for each house type to determine the most cost-effective heat and insulation system. He said that the appraisal should weigh the cost of house improvements against replacement costs and should be completed within 18 months to form the basis of a city-wide strategy. That was more than 18 months ago. Glasgow has not been given the resources to carry out that appraisal, and it would have been selfish if the Grieve report had suggested that only Glasgow should benefit from such excellent advice.

As far as I know, the Government have done nothing to carry out that appraisal. I do not wish to go over the arguments for the house condition survey which we went into in some depth last week. But I note the strong support that the Minister appeared to give when he was questioning the honourable Member for Eastwood during proceedings in the Select Committeee on Scottish Affairs.

He seemed to impress on his honourable Friend the need for a house condition survey, or certainly an assessment of properties. That was in 1984. Two years later the Grieve committee met and finally concluded that an appraisal of the housing stock was needed. Only in that way can we be sure that the Government in their capital allocations to local authorities are spending the money well and are not repeating the mistakes of the past. Those mistakes were well documented in the inquiries into housing in Glasgow, Edinburgh and Stirling and in the many excellent reports produced by Scottish local 224 authorities, including the recent Renfrew report, which I understand was sent to the Minister over the weekend.

We back up every word that Grieve says about the need to appraise the state of housing in Glasgow and in Scotland and we fully endorse the need to appraise the housing stock, in line with the needs of housing investment, for dampness and other problems. There is no point in spending money on refurbishing blocks which would be better demolished, but at present councils have no choice but to undertake costly refurbishment programmes because the cost of demolition and rebuilding would be much greater.

We know that the Government would like to avoid not only the cost implications but the implications of having to carry out an appraisal which would embarrass them. That must be set aside in the interests of an effective housing policy and of effectively remedying the problems that we see in our daily lives as Members of Parliament representing those who have to live in such properties. We all know of those problems. It is not open to all of us to have the Boundary Commissions take out of our constituencies houses that we have visited and condemned but no longer represent and, sadly, no longer seem to care about.

We know that the Minister responsible for housing visited the Pilton scheme and saw the problems there. The surprising thing about Pilton for the Government is that, despite a massive investment of public money by a Conservative Administration to subsidise private property developers, the houses that they have refurbished are now in a worse condition than adjacent houses which have been refurbished by the district council. Dampness is now manifesting itself in the homes in west Pilton for which so much money was poured into private developers' pockets. The Government stood by then; I should like to say "on the sidelines", but they encouraged that money to go in.

That is why an appraisal is needed. It is to make sure that whoever is upgrading houses will do so to proper standards and will not indulge in the sort of jerrybuilding which too often went on in Pilton as recently as four years ago and which is now throwing up problems. Either the district council will have to take over those houses to eradicate damp and condensation or the Government will have to give large repair grants to tackle the problems that were not tackled by the Conservatives in what was merely an exercise to throw money at developers. The matter was not of concern to them.

Finally, if the amendment is not accepted, we might as well hold another Scottish Affairs Select Committee on dampness, take the same weighty evidence and ignore it, too. That is virtually what has happened since 1984, and the Opposition do not wish to see that happen again. We want to see effective action taken to appraise the problems in our houses and to ensure that dampness, the scourge of the 20th century, is eradicated.

I conclude my remarks there and hope that the Minister will find it in his brief to accept the amendment.
