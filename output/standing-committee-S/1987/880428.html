: On Tuesday I said that my main concern about the original proposals in the consultation paper, apart from the education aspects, was that any proposals for the structure of the school boards should make sense in relation to the proper management and administration of education in our schools. Many of my worries were allayed when we remove some of the executive powers originally proposed for the school boards, the issue no longer has quite the same importance.

None the less, even though the powers and functions of the new boards are broadly of a consultative nature, there is still an inherent danger of conflict between the decisions of the board and the policy of the education authority at the end of the day. I use that phrase advisedly because the buck eventually stops with the education authority. It is responsible for the administration of the schools within its area.

: We have had an interesting debate on this useful set of probing amendments aimed at clarifying the exact relationship between the school board and the education authority. I am grateful to my right hon. Friend the Member for Kincardine and Deeside (Mr. Buchanan-Smith) and my hon. Friend the Member for Dumfries (Sir H. Monro) for pressing me on that. We have considered carefully the statutory position of school boards in relation to their education authorities.

The amendments seek to constrain school boards and head teachers in the exercise of their functions. They seek to require them to act in accordance with the statutory duties of the education authority and within the policies of that authority. In response to my hon. Friend the Member for Dumfries, there is already a provision to require boards to act in accordance with the education authority's statutory duties in clause 8(5). Indeed, clause 8(5) goes much further and requires the board to act in accordance with the authority's obligations under common law.

I appreciated what my right hon. Friend the Member for Kincardine and Deeside said about policy and about avoiding conflict where it is unnecessary. Clause 9 requires a board to have regard to the local authority's policy on the curriculum when exercising its functions under that clause. The Bill also provides for boards to be directed on the authority's policy on the out-of-hours use of school premises.

Local authorities will be able to attach conditions to the delegation orders, which may reflect particular policy guidelines that operate within the education authority. We hope and expect that local authorities will take the opportunity offered by the orders to devolve power according to conditions that meet the parameters for their particular policy.

The amendments go too far in seeking to constrian the boards to act wholly within education authority policy. That is difficult to accept because of the nature of the Bill and the spirit behind it. What is the point of giving boards delegated powers and functions if they are required to act in accordance with detailed central instructions? One of the Oppositions's unfair 124 criticisms of the Government is that we are centralising, yet the amendments seek to advance education authorities' central power in matters that are properly left for decision at local level.

I accept what my right hon. and hon. Friends said about the need to reflect the agency relationship that will exist between boards and the authorities and the obligation of the boards to their authorities. I hope that I have reassured my right hon. and hon. Friends how the Bill does that. There are some teeth in the Bill, which will reassure those members of the Committee who are worried about the boards' obligations. Schedule 4 amends section 70 of the Education (Scotland) Act 1980 and gives the Secretary of State power to take default action against school boards in the same way that he can take action against an education authority that fails in its statutory duties.

The boards' obligations to their authorities are clear. We have drafted the Bill in such a way as to ensure an appropriate degree of freedom of action at local level within the context of a relationship of mutual confidence backed by the authority or the Secretary of State. Therefore, I urge my right hon. and hon. Friends and the hon. Member for Cumberanuld and Kilsyth (Mr. Hogg) to withdraw the amendments. The Bill already provides for the spirit of what they seek.
