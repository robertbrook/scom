The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Alexander Fletcher)
: I think that it will be for the convenience of the Committee if I now reply to Thursday afternoon's debate on the amendments.

In moving amendment No. 48, the hon Member for Fife, Central (Mr. Hamilton) returned to the question of including examination results in the information to be made available to parents to help them choose a school. I have little to add to what has already been said in the Committee on this question. The Government—and, I think, the Committee—recognise that the results should be presented in a way that does not disadvantage one area or school in comparison with another. That is why the Government have laid before the Committee the consultative document on information for parents.

We shall be assisted in the presentation of examination results by two things. First, there is the nature of the Munn and Dunning proposals and the assessment 290 on three levels—foundation, general and credit—which will provide a more broadly based view of a child's performance. I think that the number of Munn and Dunning places taken in a school will itself be a guide to parents and to others as to the population and general academic performance of that school.

Secondly, the Scottish certificate of education examination board is currently considering a factual report on post-16 examinations. Clearly, that will include consideration of the credit level in the Munn and Dunning proposals and its effect on the Scottish certificate of education. We can feel fairly certain that SCEEB will wish to consider the effect of the credit level on the SCE and to make suggestions for the future. I believe that I can assure the Committee that everyone concerned with providing information for parents intends that this important information about examinations should be presented in a way that is acceptable to the large majority of people both inside and outside the field of education.

My hon Friend the Member for Perth and East Perthshire (Mr. Walker) said that assessments should be made in context. That is precisely what we are trying to achieve by issuing a consultative paper on the subject and by trying to make sure that parents who wish to make a choice of school for their children are provided not just with information but with relevant information. Under amendment No. 66, the parent would have the right to be supplied with:

"information about the choice of course available to the child within any school",

and,

"if the parent and child opt for a course within the school attended by the child, then the child shall have the right to be admitted to that course."

I shall consider first the proposal that an education authority should give a parent information about courses available at any of its schools. We are anxious that the fullest possible information should be made available to parents. I believe that that information should include information on the courses on offer at any school, but I do not believe that, on the question of courses within schools, main legislation is appropriate. As the Committee knows, we have issued for comment a consultative paper setting out our proposals for regulations under new section 28B. Paragraph 12 of that paper suggests that the information to be supplied about any school on request should include

"the range and level of curriculum provided for pupils of different ages: subject options and choices available and arrangements for parents to be consulted about such choices".

The hon Gentleman can therefore be assured that this will be a matter covered by the regulations, but I would prefer to wait for comment on the consultative paper before deciding precisely the terms in which the information should be defined.

On the second part of the amendment, I do not accept that because a parent opts for his child to be admitted to a particular course as a result of the information he receives, it follows that the education authority automatically should be required to accede to that request. This takes no account of the possibility that there might be more applicants for a course than there was room for. Where such a case arises it will be for authorities to decide the priorities JPEG 291 for admission to the course. This is essentially a matter for school management, and we cannot legislate for every aspect.

While, therefore, I cannot recommend the Committee to accept the amendment, as the hon Member has already mentioned, we are proposing to repeal section 28(2) of the 1980 Act, and this strengthens the position of parents by removing the barrier to the selection of a course from which, in the authority's opinion, the pupil is unlikely to profit. This is a considerable step forward in helping parents with school and with any involvement they may wish to have in courses within a school. The repeal of this provision must strengthen the position of parents with regard to choice of course. I cannot accept the amendment.
