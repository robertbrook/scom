Mr. Martin J. O'Neill
(Clackmannan and East Stirlingshire)
: This morning we have chosen an education topic for debate, which may seem surprising after the consideration given to the Education (Scotland) (No. 2) Bill and to the variety of subjects which were dealt with on Third Reading. We felt that it was important, indeed vital, to look at education as it affects the 16 to 18 or 19 years age group. I say "18 or 19 " because there are some youngsters at school who are over 18, so although the Minister has published the document 16 to 18 it stretches into the 19 years age group.

This area probably presents the most important challenge facing our education and training service —how we cope with the rising tide of youth unemployment, how we can best prepare our young people for a society whose economy at present is apparently incapable of utilising their talents and abilities, and how we make best use of the existing educational resources to ensure that young people do not lose out on any of the opportunities that are available to them.

On 29 October 1979 the Minister issued a document " 16-18s in Scotland " and he sought responses by 31 December 1979. In an Adjournment debate in January 1980, which was instituted by my hon. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Maryhill (Mr. Craigton) the Minister said that he hoped to comment once all the replies had been received and that he had extended the date for the replies until March 1980. Some 15 months later we have yet to get a reply or a reasoned response from the Minister on that area. We have had one or two gimmicky initiatives but we have not really had any serious coherent policy statements from the Government.

The Opposition appreciate that a Government committed to laissez-faire economics will find concepts of manpower planning otiose and distasteful. For them the free market should be able to set the wage levels in such a way as to ensure that everyone should get a job, that the able will be motivated to study and that the less able will leave school to opportunities commensurate with their talents. We know that in the past two years this 88 has come to be a blatant nonsense and the unemployment figures which were announced this week bear testimony to that.

At present some 30,000 school leavers are looking for work, competing with another 270,000 Scots in a contracting job market. Figures of this enormity will not melt away overnight. The problem of training and retraining our work people will be the major preoccupation of the 1980s and well into the decade thereafter. It will require far more than the simplistic crisis management of the last two years. It will need far more than the vague statements of intent that we heard from the lips of the Secretary of State for Employment yesterday, when he said:

" If an extra 100,000 places are necessary in order to fulfil our undertaking "—

to provide training opportunities for young people—

" we shall have to find the necessary resources."— [Official Report, 24 June 1981; Vol. 7, c. 256.]

This is not the way in which a Government should be looking at the problems of the young unemployed. They are simply saying that they will extend the resources to meet the crisis without any clear indication of how and where the places should be provided or, how they should be funded; they are simply saying that if there is a problem they will somehow find the means to resolve it, but they, are not describing in any detail the means which should be opened.

We hope that today the Minister will be able to reveal some of his thoughts on the matter because, as we say, he has had about 15 to 18 months to consider the responses to his discussion document— a document which, I think it is no secret to say, appeared on the desk of my right hon. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Craigton (Mr. Millan) during his period as Secretary of State for Scotland which he regarded as inadequate and, therefore, did not publish. Subsequently, it was dusted down and brought out under the imprimatur of the right hon. Member for Ayr (Mr. Younger).

It was perhaps a measure of the lack of concern in 1979, or the complacency of the Scottish Office at that time, that it felt that an inadequate discussion document of that nature was sufficient to instigate a debate on a matter of this importance. Those engaged in education and training are despairing of ever getting the leadership and the breadth of vision which Government must bring to problems of this magnitude.

In some respects the hon. Member for Edinburgh, North (Mr. Fletcher) is in a unique position to meet such a challenge since he has responsibility for both education and industry. The Secretary of State for Scotland has responsibility for the activities of the Manpower Services Commission.

We shall be delighted this morning if the Minister can tell us his intentions. The Opposition feel that a new, flexible and coherent system of education and training opportunities should be provided and should extend throughout adult life. About 50 per cent, of people in the 16 to 18 age group lose contact with the education service as soon as they leave school. Falling school rolls will result in a JPEG 89 very short time in even those who are at school having a limited opportunity to pursue the courses of their choice. Indeed, Strathclyde region has forecast that of the 25 comprehensives in the region which now have rolls over 200 in years five and six, by the year 1990 only five of these comprehensives will have rolls of that size and that the average roll for the senior part of a secondary school will be between 50 and 60 pupils.

This is a very serious problem because it means that those young people who stay on for the final years of secondary education will have a very restricted choice of subjects, given the cuts which the Government are carrying through. The expensive courses which need capital expenditure— courses such as those in technical subjects and physics, for which there are shortages of teachers, are the subjects in which we shall see the most drastic cuts in opportunities for young people.

I recognise that for those who are in full-time education in this age group, it is not simply a matter of focusing attention on the problems of the comprehensive schools. We have to consider the possibilities which are open to us in respect of further education colleges, and we have to look at a far more flexible use of these institutions. Ironically at the moment in many areas the further education colleges are bursting at the seams as the schools become less and less well used because of falling school rolls. I do not think any member of the Opposition would deny that schools will have to close, but when schools closed we would hope that the buildings and the equipment within them could be used in a more flexible way, with arrangements made for young people to go to colleges of further educations while pursuing courses in those schools which remained.

We must also take account of the fact that in urban areas these problems can be resolved relatively easily. There are transport facilities, there are opportunities for the re-utilisation of buildings, the re-designation of institutions. But in the country areas and in the smaller towns, far greater imagination will have to be applied to the problems and far more resources made available.

As regards any consideration of post-compulsory education and training, I would suggest to the Minister that, if the Minister has not had the opportunity to read Strathclyde region's discussion paper on education and training after compulsory education he would be well advised to do so. Had he sought to call it his and give us that as his statement of intention, many of us would have welcomed it as the policy of the Government in Scotland. But given the changes which are to take place in a very short time in our society, indeed which are taking place at the moment, we have to recognise that the eduction service is by and large failing half of the children over 16. At present no more than half of them are touched by the education system once they leave school, and of those over 18, about 70 per cent, are not involved in any form of formal education.

90 I do not lay the failure of the formal education services at the door of this Government. It is a responsibility which should be borne by the whole of the education service. But we have to change the position where for a large part of our population education finishes at the age of 16. Indeed, one can almost say that for a large part of the population, education probably finishes considerably earlier than that.

This can be made easier by the fact that there will be a drop in population and we should be able to spread resources more thickly over those who will be of the age about which we are talking. But we know that the likely unemployment trends will persist with sustained unemployment among young people. We know that the trend is for more and more young people to be out of work for longer than 26 weeks. The Manpower Services Commission is striving to provide courses, at times against a background of lack of sympathy from employers and suspicion from young people because many of them feel that the courses which are being provided are nothing more than the use of cheap labour.

Misgivings have now been expressed by trade unions as to the quality of the youth opportunity schemes and there is a crisis of confidence in many of these projects. The Government will have to provide sufficient funds to ensure that the job experience schemes as well as the youth opportunity schemes become attractive to employers, that young people can be taken on and their energies harnessed, and that they can be given the opportunity to appreciate the importance of being in regular—albeit artificial—employment.

The Minister will doubtless tell us that about 70 per cent, of those who go through the schemes eventually find full-time employment. I shall not quibble about the figure of 70 per cent. It is the one which he invariably uses. But there are other figures which suggest that in some parts of the country it could be as low as 50 per cent. The problems of long-term youth unemployment lie at the heart of the difficulties of the 16 to 18 years olds.

We feel that the problems which are created by falling school rolls can provide us with opportunities for better use of existing facilities. There would be greater flexibility. Young people who are staying on at school should be given courses which are of greater relevance to the future job prospects.

While we may have some misgivings about the Munn and Dunning proposals, we know that they will take time to work through the system anyway. But there are SCOTEC and SCOTBEC courses which could be made avilable in schools and which would be far more useful to those youngsters who will not be pursuing academic courses after the age of 18. If young people could take a wider range of SCOTEC and SCOTBEC courses, that would go some way to help.

We also hope that the barriers between the school system and the further education colleges can be progressively broken down. To do that requires a degree of goodwill and trust in the teaching profession. Frankly, the way in which the Minister has JPEG 91 dealt with the teaching unions does not suggest that they have a great deal of confidence in his ability to ask them to be, on the one hand, more flexible, and on the other, so flexible that they will give up thousands of jobs.

We must also look for flexibility in the use of the community education service which, in Strathclyde for example, has played a leading role in working with the long-term unemployed. We must have far greater integration of these schemes.

I am not suggesting that there should be some sort of central control. One of the features of this Parliament has been the extent to which the power and independence of local authorities have been eroded by central Government. But the Minister should seek to encourage local authorities to integrate the plethora of organisations operating in this field. It is to the Minister that we look for leadership. We need more flexible courses and local authorities should be given more responsibility backed by adequate funding. We do not accept the argument that because school rolls fall as a result of a dramatic fall in population, the level of provision should fall at the rate which the Minister envisages.

As I said earlier, our biggest long-term problem in education is to provide opportunities for the young unemployed, to compensate in some cases for the failure of the education system and in other cases to provide them with the opportunity to prepare themselves for employment when it comes. Yesterday the Secretary of State for Employment said that if there were more unemployed he would provide more money for courses, without giving serious thought to the content and purpose of the courses. We are also concerned that the Minister seems to think that throwing into schools a few microprocessors in the form of mini computers is the answer to the problem. We accept that these are desirable, but they do not provide the exclusive answer.

Thirdly, what changes are needed in the range of courses offered in the first two years of post-compulsory education? The paper suggested that two new categories of full-time post-16 courses might be introduced. The first was a range of courses offered as an alternative to SCE higher grade examinations. Such courses would include technological or business subjects and would prepare students either for entry to technician level employment or for higher education.

The second category was courses at a lower level for pupils whose performance at O-grade was not very good. These courses would continue general education but with a vocational emphasis, and would prepare the students for entry to appropriate vocational further education courses conferring recognised national qualifications.

Although these suggestions did not command universal support, they clearly appealed to a large number of those who commented on the consultative document and who welcomed the possibility of developing courses which provided a better pre 98 paration for working life. Considerable study has already been given to the principles upon which such courses might be based and how they might best be fitted in with traditional academic courses, so that premature specialisation and unnecessary demands on resources are avoided. The aim will be to produce a flexible range of studies which can be offered to young people and which match their interests and aspirations.

Work is also proceeding in the Scottish technical and business education councils on the development of courses of a broad vocational nature but for students whose level of attainment does not warrant intensive academic study or a premature choice of specialised vocational studies. These new studies would reflect the importance accorded to experience and work outside the educational institution, an emphasis on learning methods which take account of the young person's independence, and a programme designed in a flexible way to meet differences in the duration and pattern of study.

I think that there are genuine prospects for useful development here, but a good deal of curriculum development needs to be undertaken in consultation with the educational interests concerned. It will also be necessary to study further the implications for the future structure and organisation of secondary and further education, about which I shall say more in a minute. We must also ensure that any development of new courses on the lines I have indicated is properly co-ordinated with the Munn and Dunning development programme. Action along these lines would help to bring about a closer integration of compulsory and post-compulsory education and a more coherent provision for 16 to 18-year-olds.
