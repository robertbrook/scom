: When the Committee ground to a halt on Tuesday my hon Friend the Member for South Ayrshire (Mr. Foulkes) had just finished his contribution. We have not attempted in detail to amend schedule 2, but that does not preclude us from raising a number of matters of relevance and interest. Indeed, we have a duty so to do.

Schedule 2, in the Government's estimation, is a matter of some importance. We have always been led to believe that there is an important libertarian theme behind the Bill. Some of us have greeted this news with a little scepticism, but we understand that it is a measure which is intended to remove the chains and to unfetter local authority discretion. The rather slim claim to justification for this is found, as I understand it, in this schedule.

The notes on clauses, say of clause 22, the paving clause for the schedule:

"These relaxations contribute to the implementation of the Government's policy to allow local authorities to carry out more of their functions without unnecessary interference."

I think that I speak for all my hon Friends when I say that we are astonished to know that it is the Government's policy to allow local authorities to carry out their functions without unnecessary interference. Possibly the Under-Secretary would have to shelter behind a technical argument as to the meaning of "unnecessary". My impression is that the Government are determined not only to interfere but to meddle and if necessary to do damage, for all sorts of political and ideological reasons. Perhaps that is something else which we shall have a chance to discuss under clause 23.

In any event it is important to establish that we regard the (relaxations of control that are enumerated and set out ad longem in schedule 2 as being very petty and minor concessions. We are obliged to my hon Friend the Member for Berwick and East Lothian (Mr. Home Robertson) for a fine sustained essay. I was going to say "flight of fancy", but that would be unfair, because he drew attention to a matter of interest to those involved in debates on the countryside. The hon Member for South Angus (Mr. Fraser), who is up to his hocks in countryside matters, with a Private Member's Bill, will appreciate 534 that. The important point about a large number of these "concessions", as my hon Friend illustrated, is that they are not concessions of substance at all.

The Highways (Provision of Cattle-Grids) Act 1950 and the removal of the power to make regulations on cattle grids got us into the private and charming world of hedgehogs at our last sitting. The important point is that the Government coyly reveal, in the notes on clauses, that these powers have not been exercised.

If we are saying that these are important concessions, that local councillors will feci that Big Brother is no longer breathing over their shoulders, and that the world is a freer and happier place, in which they can exercise discretion, it is unsatisfactory to discover in so many cases that the powers being abolished have in fact fallen into desuetude and are redundant lumber upon the statute book. The appearance of liberalisation and the colouring that the schedule purports to give to what is in fact a dictatorial and illiberal Bill are fraudulent.

In the notes on clauses we are told of clause 22, the trigger clause, that:

"The Convention"—

I assume that that is the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities, COSLA—

"has been consulted and most of the relaxations have been agreed."

I do not know whether there is any significance in the word "most". I should say in fairness to the Minister that I have had no particular representations. COSLA and most individual local authorities have merely groaned when they have looked at schedule 2 and recognised that it has nothing of reality in it for .them. But it might be worth ascertaining from the Minister which of the relaxations referred to in the schedule have not been agreed with COSLA and what is the significance of "most".

It would not be fair to delay the Committee for long on this schedule. I said at an earlier stage that I was greatly looking forward to debating the Burial Grounds (Scotland) Act 1855, the Cremation Act 1902 and certainly the Methylated Spirits (Sale by Retail) (Scotland) Act 1937, because of my ignorance, which I fully conceded, of the details of those measures.
