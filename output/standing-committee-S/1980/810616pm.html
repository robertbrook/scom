Clause 14
ATTACHMENT OF POWERS OF ARREST TO MATRIMONIAL HOMES

Amendment moved [this day], No. 128, in page 15, line 43, at end insert " unless it appears to the court that in all the circumstances of the case such a power is unnecessary".—[ Mr. Dewar.]

4.30 pm

Mr. Donald Dewar
(Glasgow, Garscadden)
: I was inviting the Committee to consider whether the saving phrase in the amendment should be added to clause 14(l)(a). I suggested that it would be a sensible and useful change to the Bill.

The important point is that, the matrimonial interdict having been obtained,

"The court shall, on application of the applicant spouse, attach a power of arrest".

 That is a mandatory duty that falls upon the court, triggered by the application of the spouse who has obtained the interdict. The interesting distinction that is clearly and intentionally made in the clause is that there is no level of discretion open to the court in a matrimonial interdict attached to an exclusion order. Whereas in another form of matrimonial interdict—one that might deal with violent conduct, harassment and so on, but had been obtained, for example, during the course of other matrimonial proceedings—the court can at least listen to argument that in all the circumstances of the case such power is unnecessary.

It would be sensible for that flexibility to be given to the court in both cases. It is not a matter of disagreeing with the logic of the Bill. I do not understand the basis of the distinction that has been made. Surely, both interdicts could have dealt with similar conduct. It would be distressing no doubt, and many cases would justify the attachment of a power of arrest. A husband—if it is a husband— might find it difficult to convince the court that, in all the circumstances, the power was unnecessary. But I believe that the loophole should be allowed so that the subject of an interdict can make representations.

The power of arrest is obviously a serious matter. It can be a considerable sword of Damocles, and should be used only in extreme circumstances. But, before it is imposed, there should be open to argu- 136 ment whether, in the circumstances, it is necessary. Therefore, to put it simply, I should like to see that spread to both paragraphs (a) and (b). I am encouraged in my view because it appears from clause 14 as a whole that there is no real distinction to be drawn in the way that it has been drawn on this occasion.

I should be more reassured if the matrimonial interdict were inevitably part of the process of the granting of an exclusion order. My right hon. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Craighton (Mr. Millan), during consideration of principle, said that as far as the Opposition can see—we are open to correction—it might be a staged process where the applicant spouse perhaps obtained an exclusion order and a matrimonial interdict at the same time, but at a later stage decided to make an application for a power of arrest. The judge or sheriff hearing the clause 14 application might not be the judge who presided at the earlier motion for the exclusion order and interdict. If one assumes that there is that staged process—the gap between obtaining the order and interdict and the attachment of the power of arrest—it is important not simply to have a mandatory duty to attach that power of arrest, but to have the safeguard in paragraph (a) applying to paragraph (a).

My hon. Friend the Member for West Stirlingshire (Mr. Canavan)—who, I am sure, stands by us loyally—has no doubt read the remarks of Women's Aid. That organisation has strongly opposing views, and calls for the omission of the proviso allowing a non-applicant spouse to make submissions about whether the power of arrest is necessary. The Women's Aid organisation would like to take that proviso from paragraph (b). My amendment seeks to add it to paragraph (a)

In the past I have found the submissions of Women's Aid very useful and, on many occasions, very persuasive. However, on that point I have had to disagree with the organisation. I prefer the logic of the argument which I have advanced. I hope that that argument finds some favour with the Minister.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Malcolm Rifkind)
: Before I comment on the amendment, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will allow me to discuss a point that he raised earlier about whether the Tenants' Rights, Etc. (Scotland) Act constituted an interim provision. I do not intend to spend too long on the matter, but, if the hon. Gentleman consults Hansard, he will see that in a debate last year I emphasised

"that they are interim proposals, pending the final conclusion of the Scottish Law Commission".—[ Official Report, 11 June 1980; Vol. 986, c. 745.]

 I believe that the point was made clear at that time.

I shall now turn to the amendments to Clause 14, which are before the Committee. The views of the hon. Member for Glasgow, Garscadden (Mr. Dewar), are—as he has acknowledged—at variance with those of Women's Aid. He may not realise that they are also at variance with the recommendations of the Scottish Law Commission. Examination of the JPEG 137 reasons advanced by the Commission show the strength of the Bill in its present form.

First, there is a considerable distinction between matrimonial interdicts, which are ancillary to an exclusion order, and other matrimonial interdicts. A matrimonial interdict may be granted for a number of reasons, which include the possibility of violence causing physical or mental injury to the applicant or to the children of the marriage. Such an interdict may, however, be granted for far less serious reasons. The husband may be creating a nuisance. He may be interfering with his spouses's occupancy rights. There may be a number of factors, which do not pose any serious danger of physical or mental injury to the applicant spouse.

When granting an interdict as an ancillary to an exclusion order, the court must be satisfied—in terms stated in the appropriate clause of the Bill—that there is a risk of physical or mental injury to the health of the applicant or of any of the children of the marriage. That distinction must be made, because we are discussing circumstances in which there is a definite risk of physical or mental injury. It is therefore of supreme importance that, once an exclusion order has been granted, all necessary action should be taken to ensure that that order is observed.

As has been recognised by the Law Commission, Women's Aid and other bodies, one of the main problems in the matrimonial sphere is that, even when an interdict has been granted, the husband— through his natural disposition, the influence of alcohol or other causes—may seek to breach the terms of the interdict and of the exclusion order. Therefore, it is essential that all that is possible should be done to prevent his doing that.

If a wife is under threat because of her husband's inclination to take such action, the police are—in most circumstances—the only body to which she can apply for help. In paragraph 4.33 of the Law Commission's report, reference is made to the views of the domestic violence working party, and it quotes the working party's remarks:

"They also emphasised strongly that the involvement of the police in the process of enforcing matrimonial interdicts was necessary in itself. The police were the only agency upon which a wife could call at any time of the day or night, and the prospect of their intervention was calculated to have a deterrent effect."

 It was for such reasons that the Law Commission went on to express the view that a discretion in the courts would be sufficient only where normal matrimonial interdicts were concerned. If the court were satisfied that an exclusion order was justified, because there was a danger of physical or mental injury to the health of the applicant spouse, the right of arrest should automatically follow. If there is an alleged breach of an interdict and exclusion order, it is for the police officer in question to decide whether to use that right, but it seems appropriate that the power should be provided in these circumstances. Otherwise the important rights given to the spouse by the Bill might have much less significance in practice than they are intended to have. I have no doubt that it was for such reasons that the Law 138 Commission put forward recommendations in line with the current form of the Bill.
