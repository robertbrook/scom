Opposition Members correctly pointed out that simply to assume that one region is comparable to another is a facile and superficial approach. To compare the Highland region with the Strathclyde region or the Edinburgh district with the Sutherland district would be a meaningless exercise if it were based merely on the fact that both were district or regional councils.

Equally, some districts are comparable on any commonsense interpretation, either in terms of size or population or in terms of their needs, as the hon Member for South Ayrshire (Mr. Foulkes) commented, or in terms of any other area of expenditure. Although we need not reach any definitive view at present, a particular district taken as a whole might be considered very similar to another district taken as a whole, or a region might be considered as similar to another region. I do not state that as a matter of categorical faith, but, in a relatively small country such as Scotland, there is clearly some basis for making comparisons. We all do so in the judgments we make, because we believe that an authority is either overspending or underspending. That is essentially a comparison between the way in which local authorities have responded to a recognised need.

The provision is not simply a facility for the Secretary of State but also a safeguard for the local authority. When the Secretary of State wants to act under these powers, the provisions ensure that he must make clear to the local authority and to Parliament the basis for his decision. If he feels that a comparison between an authority against whom he wishes to act and another is reasonable, he will have to state his reasons for so doing and show which areas of comparability he believes to be appropriate.

That could either relate to the total expenditure of the authority against which he contemplates action compared with another comparable authority or it JPEG 321 might relate not to the aggregate of expenditure but to the expenditure on a particular area of policy such as education, social work, roads or transport. It would be possible to make a comparison between one authority's expenditure in that respect and that of another.

However, whichever criteria were applied would have to be disclosed to the local authority in question, which would then be able to challenge the assumptions on which the Secretary of State had come to the conclusion both in terms of the statutory procedure that is already provided for—by making direct representations to the Secretary of State—and because the Secretary of State in putting forward an order to Parliament for approval has to state his objectives and reasons, as well as the representations that he has received from the local authority if its conclusion differs from his. Therefore, although the Secretary of State certainly has an element of discretion, there is a safeguard for the local authority in that it will be aware of the assumptions that are taken on board when the Secretary of State proposes to act against it.
