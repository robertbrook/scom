10.30 am

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. George Younger): I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the matter of the colleges of education in Scotland. I am extremely glad that we have an opportunity to discuss this very important matter, and I am grateful to the Opposition for having suggested it for debate today and given me an opportunity to explain in more detail the decisions I announced on 6 August.

There are two main issues upon which I hope all members of the Committee who speak will be able to express an opinion. Firstly, is there or is there not any reason to reduce the size of the teacher-training system in Scotland? Secondly, if it is necessary to reduce the size of that system, what is the best, fairest and most sensible method of doing so?

Perhaps I should start by outlining the facts, and let them lead us on to the main issues. The main purpose of colleges of education is, of course, to provide initial training for teachers, and the number of teachers needed must obviously depend upon the number of pupils in our schools. In 1973 there were 636,000 primary pupils in Scotland; by 1979 there were only 545,000; and the number is expected to continue to fall to some 445,000 by 1985 before beginning to rise again.

Even on the assumption that there will be some increase in the birth rate, the 1979 figure is unlikely to be reached again before the mid-1990s. The number of secondary pupils is now at its peak of about 410,000 and is expected to fall to about 288,000 by 1990 before starting to rise again.

I should emphasise that these changes have not been accompanied by any worsening of pupil-teacher ratios. On the contrary they have steadily improved, and both in primary and in secondary teaching they are now the most favourable in the United Kingdom.

But inevitably the drop in the number of pupils is reflected in the number of teachers whom we need to train. In the early 1970s the colleges of education provided pre-service training for about 12,700 student teachers each year; in 1979-80 the number was only 5,455, and by 1983-84 it is expected to fall to about 4,500 before beginning slowly to rise again.

The total number of all types of student in colleges of education, which stood at nearly 15,000 in 1972-73, fell to about 8,000 in 1979-80 and is of the order of 7,300 in the current Session. It is likely to go below 7,000 in 1982-83 and is not expected to rise again to any significant extent before the end of the decade—and then only by a few hundred places. These students are at present trained in 10 separate colleges of education, with a total capacity in terms of student places which was originally related to the peak numbers of the early 1970s.

Col. 2 In 1977, when the last general review of college capacity took place, there were 14,450 places in the college system. The previous Secretary of State, the right hon Member for Glasgow, Craigton (Mr. Millan), decided—and I agreed with him—that the right course was to retain the 10-college system but to run down the number of places and students in all the colleges.

According to a consultative paper produced by the right hon Gentleman in January 1977, it was thought that a system with an overall capacity of something just under 12,000 would be about right—the actual figure in the paper was 11,900. Since then, the overall capacity has in fact been reduced to about 11,150 places by the diversion of surplus accommodation to other uses. But the fall in the number of pupils in the schools, and so in the number of students needed, has gone even further than was then expected; and in addition, wastage from the profession is less than was envisaged by the right hon Gentleman in 1977.

The reality of the position in the 1980s is that even in the reduced college system of 11,150 student places, there are at present nearly 4,000 places unused and surplus to requirements. It is abundantly clear that, if nothing is done about it, there will be even larger surpluses over the next few years. It seems to me to be clear beyond any doubt that it would be a gross waste of resources to continue to train decreasing numbers of student teachers in a system which was designed to accommodate many more, and even worse to train a large number of young teachers who would be most unlikely ever to get jobs in teaching.

Mr. Tam Dalyell(West Lothian): Does the figure of 11,000 include the social workers in places like Jordanhill?

Mr. Younger: Yes, that figure is the present capacity of the system—not its usage—as it was reduced by the right hon Member for Craigton in his last reform. The theoretical capacity is now 11,150. The total need at present is about 8,000.

I am of course fully aware of various educational developments which will have implications for the colleges of education in coming years—for example, the proposals for the curriculum and for assessment which are contained in the Munn and Dunning reports, the proposals in the Warnock report for special education, new developments on in-service training of teachers, and the intention, which I announced in August, to proceed towards all-graduate entry to primary teaching. However, even on the basis of a most generous allowance for these developments and for the other types of student now trained in colleges of education, I have no doubt that an overall capacity of 8,000 student places in the college system will make ample provision for the period into the 1990s.

These are the facts which everyone must face up to. They lead us inescapably to the conclusion that some reduction has to be made to the 10-college system. I turn now to the second main issue. If we have to reduce the system—how should it be done?

It has been suggested that this situation does not require the closure or amalgamation of any colleges of education, and of course this is a possibility which I have examined very carefully. After the most anxious consideration of all the relevant factors, however, Image Col. 3 I have not felt able to accept that view. The gradual run down which has been taking place in all the colleges over the last few years—by diverting accommodation to other purposes, by diversification of courses where possible, and by reducing staff numbers through a non-selective process of natural wastage and voluntary redundancy—has gone about as far as it reasonably can. Inevitably it has resulted in imbalances of staffing and it has given rise to a situation of increasing frustration and operational difficulties in most if not all colleges.

I have been forced to the conclusion that further run down of the college system by these methods would seriously damage their overall ability to provide the educational services which the country is entitled to expect. We have had many complaints from the colleges themselves about the increasing problems involved in spreading a limited—and reducing—intake of students over the existing 10-college system; and, as I have indicated, the necessary skills and experience of academic staff are now inevitably spread very thinly in some areas.

Some colleges are already close to the point where, if the present general rundown were to continue, it must be seriously questioned whether they could remain viable. Of course 1 do not want to close any colleges. But the conclusion seems inevitable that, if the colleges of education are to play an effective role in the educational life of Scotland, what is needed now is a more compact system within which student populations, staffing and other resources can be so distributed that the total educational effort can be not only maintained but improved.

It has been suggested also that all 10 colleges should be retained on the basis that each of them would be fully utilised either by training greater numbers of teachers or by diversifying into new areas of education, or both. I see no prospect of diversification on the scale that would be required. There have in the past been many proposals for colleges to undertake new functions, but most of these would merely have duplicated provision which is already made perfectly adequately in other institutions such as universities, central institutions or further education colleges. It would be no proper answer to the college of education problem simply to spread it more widely over other tertiary education establishments.

Mr. Younger: have not actually referred to that side of matters. It has been discussed on many occasions. Mr. John Maxton(Glasgow, Cathcart): Has the right hon Gentleman taken into account the fact that there will be a massive bulge in the numbers requiring higher education in Scotland—even if we accept that Col. 4 there will be no increase in the percentage—when he says that he does not want to give to other tertiary education establishments work which is at present covered by universities and other colleges? Mr. Younger: I appreciate the hon Gentleman's point. Of course the school population and, indeed, the further education population is subject to troughs and bulges. The trough is moving through the school system at the moment, and the higher numbers at present in further education will decline as that trough moves through into the further education system.

I appreciate the hon Gentleman's remarks, but I do not see this as a solution which could be used to keep open colleges of education which would not otherwise be kept open for their main purpose, vecause it would merely spread it further over the rest of the tertiary education system.

From the demographic figures which I have already given it is quite obvious that there is no way in which I could justify using the empty space in the colleges of education system simply to increase the output of teachers. As I have already said, pupil— teacher ratios in the schools compare very favourably with those elsewhere. In the primary sector the ratio in 1979-80 was 20.3, compared with 22.7 in England and 21.9 in Wales. In the secondary sector it was 14.4 compared with 16.6 both in England and in Wales. It surely cannot be held that we should use all the existing capacity of the colleges for teacher training whether the teachers are needed or not.

Let me now say something about costs. Many people have asked about the details of the savings expected to be made. Although saving money is by no means the only object of these changes it is certainly something we expect to achieve, after the initial costs of closure and redundancy have been met. In the current financial year it is costing £3,750,000 to run the three colleges directly affected. Of course I do not expect to save to all of that sum immediately, but savings there certainly will be. The students in the colleges concerned will be accommodated in spare capacity elsewhere in the system. This will reduce average unit costs because of the savings which will accrue from operating a smaller number of establishments. There will be staff savings too.

The extra staff needed at the remaining colleges will be fewer than the existing staff at the colleges to be closed. I cannot be precise at this stage, but I expect that perhaps a quarter of the academic staff will not need to be retained in the system, and with the closing of complete establishments most of the non-academic staff will no longer be required. Capital savings will also arise from the disposal of the buildings for other uses. One does not have to be a very brilliant economist to see that there is scope for considerable savings in those ways.

Mr. Younger: Much of that has to await the determination of the precise method of closure which will be determined by consultations with those directly involved. There is no use trying to pre-empt that decision because the whole point is to try to get the best solution and timing.

Mr. Harry Ewing(Stirling, Falkirk and Grangemouth): If, as the Secretary of State says, one does not have to be a very brilliant economist to know what the savings will be, will he persuade some of his not-very-brilliant economists to publish the figures so that we may all discuss them? Mr. Younger: If I were looking for not very brilliant economists, I should not have to look very far.

There is no point in publishing imaginary figures. We shall be happy to publish the figures when we have established the nature of the closures and their timing. Everyone is entitled to know, but we cannot—as the right hon Member for Craigton found a few years ago when he tried to do it—make up costings and proposals until we know the precise details.

Unless some colleges close, there is a danger of producing more teachers than we really need simply to keep a 10-college system viable. There is no justification for incurring the additional cost, which would undoubtedly amount to several million pounds, of training teachers who are not needed. I will not contemplate the training of students with no real prospect of employment. If we were to retain a 10-college system whose capacity were not needed, we would inevitably be driven to training more teachers than were needed. That would be not only wasted expenditure but very considerable expenditure.

Mr. George Foulkes(South Ayrshire): The Secretary of State keeps referring to teachers who are not needed. Would not local authorities like to employ more teachers to improve the education service but are unable to do so because the Secretary of State is cutting rate support grant?

Mr. Younger: That is a false point, as the hon Gentleman knows. Expenditure on school education is marginally higher per pupil than it has been in the past. That is because of the falling number of pupils.

The conclusion that some colleges must be closed is, in these circumstances, quite inescapable, on educational as much as on financial grounds. Indeed, if I had been considering economic arguments alone, it would no doubt have been tempting to go for a more centralised system, with the 8,000 student places, which is the maximum we need, located in perhaps as few as four or five colleges. That solution would be quite unacceptable to me and, I would have thought, to most other hon Members. There is surely a strong case for trying to Col. 6 ensure that our college system covers as wide a geographical spread throughout Scotland as possible, and this has been uppermost in our minds in working out the best solution.

Mr. David Steel(Roxburgh, Selkirk and Peebles): If the Secretary of State is so certain about his conclusions, why has he been so reluctant to consult the colleges on what to do?

Mr. Younger: I am coming to that point.

In reaching my decisions I have therefore put considerable stress on geographical and regional factors. I have considered the circumstances of each college, taking account of the area that it serves and of the feasibility, in the event of its being closed, of maintaining services in some other way. It was clear that the real options for closure were in the central belt, where considerations of distance and the existence of a concentration of colleges make it practicable to plan the continuation of services on a new basis.

It is not pleasant to have to decide that any particular college should close. I have, however, concluded that, on the criteria which I have indicated, Hamilton and Callendar Park colleges should close and that it would be appropriate for Craiglockhart to cease to operate as a separate entity and to be merged, preserving a distinct Roman Catholic unit, within another institution.

In reaching these decisions I took account of the numbers of students in these colleges, their geographical location, their proximity to other colleges and the feasibility of continuing services which they at present provide. I should like to make it clear that my decisions imply no criticism of the academic performance of any of the colleges in question.

We originally thought that it would be right to issue a consultative document and invite another period for public discussion. Later developments, however, led me to conclude that this would not after all be the best way to proceed. My hon Friend the Minister responsible for industry and education, had meetings with a large number of interested bodies and we received a considerable volume of representations about the future of the colleges generally and the circumstances of particular colleges. It was clear that a strong body of opinion throughout the college system was anxious to end the uncertainty.

Against this background it seemed to me that it would be disastrous to plunge everyone into dismay and uncertainty by consultation about which colleges should be closed. Everyone would have jumped into action to prove that his own college should be sacrosanct and none would have volunteered to close. All the colleges would have endured a wretched period of insecurity.

I felt it as my duty to spare everyone that agony by making the hard decisions to the best of my ability. I stand by these decisions and although I greatly regret their necessity, I am glad to defend them as basically right.

Mr. Younger: We are looking at the precise amount of teacher training that will be needed for a known number of pupils. The right hon Member for Western Isles (Mr. Stewart) must accept that the figures I have given this morning make a clear case on this matter. I must rest on that.
