The old dictum says that schools were built for children, not for teachers. Examination results by themselves are misleading, because of the manner of their presentation. I make no bones about this, because pretty well anything can be proved' from examination statistics. The position on examination results is complicated. I shall go through the types of examinations which are open to children in Scottish schools. The examinations organised by the Scottish examination board are the O-grades and the higher grade Scottish certificate of education.

Following the raising of the school leaving age, there has been an attempt to bring more children into the ambit of the O-grade examination, which now has five levels, from A to E. The letters D and E mean a fail. A certificate is issued which states that a child has achieved a certain level in the O-grade exam. Therefore, it is possible for a child to receive a certificate when, quite clearly, he has not met the standard required by the examination board.

The number of marks in D level is between 40 and 49 and the marks in E level is between 30 and 39. It is in some respects a recognition of the youngster's having completed the course, but it is not really an adequate yardstick of his educational performance. It creates an illusion that the exam has been passed, whereas he has not met the required standard.

How does the Minister propose to take account of D and E levels? In my experience as a former teacher, I found that it was often a source of encouragement to youngsters, and certainly an encouragement to the teachers, when effort could be recognised'. One of the problems which was created by the raising of the school leaving age was that the O-grade exam was based on a different school population from that at present. There are now more youngsters of middle ability staying on and the O-grade exam does not meet their requirements. As a compromise the D and E levels were introduced.

It is a source of consolation to youngsters who have started off their courses, perhaps without much hope of passing, to be told that they achieved between 40 per cent, and 50 per cent, in the exam and if they tried hard next year, they might pass. The encouragement given to youngsters to take such an examination is justified

. Nevertheless, it makes it difficult to produce neat and tidy league tables showing three categories of O-grade examination passes—those who achieved over 50 per cent., those over 40 per cent, and those over 30 per cent.

The problem is that parents will be looking at examination results to ascertain the numbers who passed. Even there, we have to take account of the fact that these bald statistics are, to say the least, misleading. The number of pupils who pass an exam does not always reflect the number who could have been presented. One of the difficulties which we 230 foresee is that some schools will start to prevent young people from being presented. None of us would argue that youngsters should be presented for examinations when they have no chance of success.
