The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Malcolm Rifkind)
: Both amendments refer to clause 4 and deal with the procedure that will follow once an exclusion order has been granted by the court.

As far as clause 4(4)(b) is concerned—the granting of an interdict—it is certainly the view of the Government that it is almost inconceivable, where an exclusion order has been granted, that it would not be appropriate to grant an order for interdict at the same time. Otherwise, the whole force of the exclusion order would be severely jeopardised. I find it impossible to conceive of a situation where one would be simultaneously considering that an exclusion order was appropriate but for some reason interdict was not. Therefore, it seems sensible to draft the clause in those terms.

On amendment No. 156, I fully appreciate the point raised by Women's Aid. It is concerned that a non-applicant spouse might give a convincing assurance which turned out not to be backed by the facts. I think that in practice that is covered by the discretion that is available to the court. The Bill is really saying that normally a warrant for summary ejection would follow. The onus would be upon the husband to satisfy the court that there were particular circumstances in the case in question where that would not be appropriate.

If an exclusion order were granted after the husband had already moved out of the house into some other property, it would be nonsense to get a formal warrant for summary ejection. Therefore, to make it a compulsory requirement of every exclusion order would bring that aspect of the Bill into disrepute as it would clearly be nonsense in certain circumstances.

It seems more appropriate, taking into account the sort of situation that the hon. Gentleman has quite properly raised, to put the onus on any applicant who does not wish a warrant for somebody's ejection to be part of an exclusion order to satisfy the court as to the reasons for it. I have no doubt that the court would need some pretty strong evidence before it would be convinced that this was appropriate in the circumstances.

For that reason, the hon. Gentleman's amendment would not be appropriate, as it would allow the sort of nonsense that I have mentioned to arise, at least in a certain proportion of cases, and I am sure that would not be his intention.

Amendment negatived.

The background to this JPEG 101 amendment stems from the Government's decision not to adopt the matrimonial notice procedure which had been recommended by the Scottish Law Commission and which is perhaps the biggest single difference between the Bill and the Law Commission's recommendations.

The reasons why we chose not to exercise the matrimonial notice option are basically twofold. First, the Law Commission's view was that if a spouse had lodged a matrimonial notice which indicated her occupancy rights, then that should be an entirely overriding right which gave her total protection as regards occupancy of her home. There are many attractions in this approach, but I think the biggest single unattractive aspect is that it is unlikely that other than a relatively small minority of spouses would have taken advantage of such an opportunity, because of an unawareness of the opportunities existing under the law, but, more importantly, because spouses are unlikely to conceive of the need for such precautions as long as the marriage is working well. By the time the marriage has broken down, it might be too late to start thinking in terms of the lodging of a matrimonial notice. Therefore, it seems sensible and appropriate to have a wider-ranging provision, which will give a high level of protection to the vast majority of spouses, whether or not they have at an earlier date, when the marriage was working quite successfully, anticipated the problems that might arise in the event of marital breakdown.

That, therefore, was the main reason why the Government did not feel that the matrimonial notice approach was suitable. There are also the additional bureaucratic requirements that such a procedure would involve. It would involve very significant increases in staffing in the Department of Registers in terms of dealing with the lodging and processing of matrimonial notices. These and other implications which I mentioned earlier led the Government to conclude that a different approach, as outlined in the Bill, would be more suitable.
