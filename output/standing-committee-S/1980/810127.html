Clause 11
DIMINUTION OF POWERS OF OFFICERS OF COURT AS REGARDS ACTINGS FOR RECOVERY OF RATES

Mr. Dewar
: I beg to move amendment No. 15, in page 6, line 6, at end add

"and nothing in the said section 247 of the Local Government (Scotland) Act 1947 shall entitle officers of the court to poind, seize, remove or secure property belonging to the spouse of the debtor".

The amendment takes us into an area of some controversy and interest to members of the Committee. Its purpose is largely to clarify the existing law. Indeed, the whole clause could be said to be very much on that basis. The clause tries to remove ambiguity and intends to deal with the rather strange phrase that remains in section 247 (2) (a) of the Local Government (Scotland) Act 1947, under which it is possible to

"poind, seize, remove or secure property belonging to or in the lawful possession of the debtor".

The effect of the clause is to take out the words

"or in the lawful possession of".

It does no more than that. In the clause stand part debate we may want to examine whether the clause ought to be trying to do more and whether it is too timid in its approach. I shall confine my remarks to the amendment, which also seeks to clarify the situation. It deals with the position of a spouse when goods are poinded following the passing of a decree in an action for recovery of debt. The amendment, which seeks to clarify the phraseology of the 1947 Act, may seem unnecessary because, if the intention of the clause is to ensure that a television set owned by Radio Rentals, say, or a car owned by a finance company cannot be poinded, presumably something owned by a spouse would fall into a similar category. But I am not sure that that is the present position, and I look for some information from the Minister on that point. I do not pretend to be an expert in the law of diligence. I have found that men who are experts in the law of diligence are usually depressed and alienated creatures living in a private world, and I hope that I am not yet in that category, although a few more months on this Bill may drive me some way into it. With some timidity, I now venture into what I understand the law to be, and I hope that the Minister will help me. As I understand it, if goods are being poinded in a house, the tenant or owner of the property is deemed to be the owner of the goods. That presumption can be altered only by application to the sheriff. That situation is, in general, justified by expediency. The sheriff's officer arrives and has a duty to make inquiries into the ownership of the goods. Of course, it is difficult to do that satisfactorily. The debtor may plausibly say "That is not mine, but my Auntie Jeannie's" or "That belongs to the local rental firm down the road, and I am getting it in return for a weekly payment." Even if a receipt is produced which shows that the goods were bought by the wife or someone else, it is possible for the officer to be misled, because the goods might have been bought by the person named on the receipt acting as an agent for the debtor. Clearly the law 168 presumes that goods in the house belong to the debtor if that house is owned or tenanted solely by him. That presumption can be lifted only by recourse to the courts at a later stage. The position becomes more complicated, as the Minister will agree, because Scottish law sometimes falls behind changing social customs. The number of joint tenancies has substantially increased and will continue to do so because local authorities are becoming more conscious of the wife's position, particularly as regards her removal from a house on the break-up of a marriage. I think that there may be increasing pressure to think in terms of joint tenancies to protect the wife's position. We may deal with women's rights in the matrimonial home elsewhere, perhaps in the Bill on family violence. In any event, joint tenancies may become a more common feature on the local authority scene. More and more married couples now insist or decide—insist suggests an element of strife—that the title of the matrimonial home should be in joint names. That complicates the presumption which I spoke about. As I understand the present law, if there is a joint tenancy or title, there is a presumption that the household goods are in joint ownership. Since goods in joint ownership cannot be poinded, the matter will become increasingly complicated as the trend becomes more pronounced. The old presumption, which is based upon sole tenancy or on single ownership by the debtor, becomes less of a prop on which to rely if— it is a big "if", and no doubt we shall discuss the matter on clause stand part—it is assumed that the present system should survive largely unchanged.

11 am

I have tried to show the difficulty which surrounds a spouse, and the goods owned by a spouse, when poinding takes place. In Scotland there has never been a well-developed law on matrimonial property. Indeed, any practising solicitor, advising on the unfortunate circumstances of a marriage break-up, has a little ritual piece which explains that almost everything is owned by someone. It has either been bought by one of the parties or there was an intention on the donor's part when the object was gifted; it was given to the husband or the wife and on that ownership ultimately will rest. It becomes a somewhat artificial concept when we speak about Auntie Jeannie who presented the gift to the happy couple on the occasion of their wedding; presumably it is then jointly owned and the apportionment under the law becomes something of a lottery.

However, such presumptions are unsatisfactory as a basis for future operation. Even leaving aside the wider considerations of the general reform of warrant sales procedure, their social significance and social justification, the Government's attempt—which in general I support—to remove from the 1947 Act the words

"or in the lawful possession of"

still leaves the question of the spouse's position, or goods owned by the spouse, very much in the air.

I have glanced at Memorandum No. 48 produced by the Scottish Law Commission—the Second Memorandum on Diligence, Poinding and Warrant Sales— which deals at some length with this matter. The Minister will recall that among the options considered was whether we should go down the line with Northern JPEG 169 Ireland on this point. In Northern Ireland in 1965 we had the Anderson report. As warrant sales are of current interest and some controversy, no doubt the Minister, being in charge of law reform, will be familiar with this run of argument.

As I understand the Anderson report, it was suggested that when the equivalent of a sheriff officer—I do not know the technicalities of the Irish system—seized goods to meet a debt requirement, he might seize a spouse's goods, even if they were owned by that spouse, if the debt being met from the proceeds was incurred for the family's benefit or, in the context of our deliberations, if it was in connection with the rent of the matrimonial home.

I do not believe that we should go down that road, because it involves a value judgment by the officer of the court, or whoever will carry out the process. He must decide—I would not like to be in his position—whether the debt was incurred for the benefit or enjoyment of the family. It is perhaps clearer in the second category; if the local district council is trying to obtain rent arrears, it would clearly fall under the Anderson concept. But I do not wish to make a value judgment in this matter. I do not, therefore, favour going along the Northern Ireland line.

The Law Commission discussion document—I stress that Memorandum No. 48 is a matter for discussion; the Law Commission has not come to firm conclusions —puts up suggestions that are probably not a bad guideline for what the Law Commission intends ultimately to do about the matter. It proposes that, in homes that are in joint ownership or joint tenancy, there should be a presumption that one can poind all the household goods and that it would be up to the wife later to have recourse to the courts to rebut that presumption and to rescue her goods from the maw of the creditor or the creditor's agents.

I can see that that proposal has a certain attraction, on the assumption that it is workable. It would allow the poinding to go ahead smoothly and would allow the sheriff officer more or less to poind any household goods owned by either husband or wife and leave the courts to sort the matter out. My objection is that, very often, he would end up poinding goods that belonged to the spouse—perhaps even to a separated spouse, who knows?—and, in the course of human nature, the idea that the other spouse would get a solicitor, go to the courts, rebut the presumption and unpoind the goods belonging to her that had been poinded for her husband's debt is a theoretical concept which people like we in the legal profession imagine happens but which does not often happen. At the end of the day, goods that should not be poinded would be poinded and, what is more, if it went as far as a warrant sale—which I agree is comparatively rare—might ultimately lead to injustice.

There may even be a moral case—I put it no higher— for that presumption of joint ownership based upon the indivisibility of a marriage, but many of us were forced some time ago to depart from that idea in terms of social reality and social policy building. I am, therefore, not happy about the recommendation in the discussion document. It was to ventilate the issue and perhaps to draw the Minister that the amendment was tabled.

I accept that the clause is not concerned with the whole range of warrant sales. It is concerned with 170 warrant sales made for one specific and limited purpose —the recovery of rates. But the principles are the same. The decisions we reach on the clause in that context will influence and perhaps even pre-empt what may happen on another occasion.

The basis of the amendment is simply to put the wife on an equal footing with her husband. It seeks to prevent an assumption that her goods are her husband's goods and that a debt against the husband can be enforced against her. It is an attempt to recognise that the wife is a separate entity, legally, morally, physically and in every sense and that, if a debt arises against one spouse, it should not be automatically enforceable and there should be no presumption that it can be recovered at the expense of the other.

To be fair, that is not quite what the Law Commission document presupposes. Its presumption is that the goods are the husband's when in fact they may not be. One could work on that presumption and leave it to subsequent legal action to sort out, but it may be better to state the matter simply in the terms that, when we are exempting from poinding, seizing, removing or securing

"property … in the lawful possession of the debtor"

on the grounds that that should give no excuse for detaining a third party's property, we should take the same general attitude to the property of a spouse.

I accept that that gets us into practical difficulty about how the sheriff officer establishes or knows that he must accept what is said to him. But the same argument arises with rented goods, hire purchase goods or anything of that kind. If we accept that the sheriff officer can see his way around that imbroglio—as there are very few houses that do not have even a television set on hire purchase—and if we can surmount the practical difficulties in such a case, I would have thought it possible to meet the practical difficulties of saying that there should be no poinding of a spouse's effects to meet the liabilities, obligations and debts of his or her married partner.

These are complicated matters. The Minister may take the view that this is the wrong vehicle in which to consider them. That is always a convenient way out of an argument, but I hope that he will not take it. The massive tomes in front of me, the Law Commission memorandums, which landed on our desks last October, are open for discussion until, I believe, the end of March. It would be a convenient argument for the Minister merely to say " We are waiting for the flood of representations to come in, and do not wish at this point to take any decisions in the matter." That might be an argument on the general attitude to warrant sales, which may crop up under stand part, or warrant sales in connection with the recovery of rates, although I still think that it would be enormously valuable to draw the Minister on the matter and perhaps to allow the Opposition to express views which could be part of the consultation process.

I accept that on the general point we have here a small tidying-up clause which was introduced by the Minister of his own volition. If he is making the minor amendments here by removing this ambiguity, by removing the words


"or in the lawful possession of",

and if we are to start making that kind of minor reform—there is never a bad time for law reform and clarifi- JPEG 171 cation of statute, as it is obscure enough, and many of us could suggest how it could be improved—in regard to section 247 of the Local Government (Scotland) Act 1947, it is an opportunity to go that little bit further and consider whether we should not clarify the position of a spouse. I am convinced that that needs clarification on the basis of what the Law Commission has said, and on my general impressions of how the system has been working.

I hope that I have at least set out the problem and what we are driving at. I await with interest the Minister's comments.
