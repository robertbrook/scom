We do not think that the exercise of parental choice by a significant minority should be allowed to endanger the rights of the majority of children in the schools concerned. Amendment No. 45 is one of the most important amendments, and one with which all the professional teaching organisations are involved because they are concerned not only with the basic trade union aspects of teaching but—more important in some ways—with the professional standards of education and the quality of the service provided.

The amendment seeks to draw attention to the serious dangers that could arise and affect the spread of courses, which everyone has accepted has been one of the most desirable features of comprehensive education. There would be a wider spread of education within the comprehensive system at least in years 3, 4, 5 and 6. Numbers are normally sufficient to afford a degree of flexibility in years 1 and 2. Options are open to parents to choose languages for their children and foundation or "taster" courses where subjects such as domestic science and technical drawing are available to children of both sexes.

It is ironic that since the start of the comprehensive system in Scotland many young boys have been given the opportunity to take what used to be called cookery classes. From my experience I know that many youngsters, especially in cities, go on from those to further education courses—such as the City and Guilds—to prepare for employment in restaurants and hotels. It is important to sustain numbers in schools in order to retain that degree of flexibility. One problem likely to arise as a result of the uncontrolled and unfettered exercise of parental choice in certain schools is that the course choices open to the majority of parents for their children will be limited.

Amendment No. 45 seeks to protect not only course choices, but the viability of courses. We recognise that other considerations are often involved. There is the capacity of education authorities to pay teachers' salaries, and there are the problems that arise when those salaries are not adequately funded because of cutbacks in the rate support grant.

In many respects the problem will be exacerbated by the application of an unfettered and unrestricted choice among parents. It is important that educational aspects are taken into account when considering the question of parental choice if the viability of courses and the equality of educational opportunity are threatened.

Our amendment will not affect a great number of schools, but a school can have an unfortunate name and be considered less attractive by parents. Because of its buildings or unfortunate publicity, or because schools close by enjoy better physical surroundings, the assumption is that school A is better than school B. I have taught in schools that are modern and well-equipped, and also in older traditional inner urban Image Col. 185 schools where parents are anxious about the quality of education offered. Their anxieties are often ill-founded, but that does not necessarily prevent them from wanting to send their children to other schools.

We recognise that parents have a right to choose, and we have stressed that all along. We want to see the right to choose made as freely available as possible, but, nevertheless, we do not see the right as inalienable if it interferes with the rights of other parents who are happy for their children to go to local schools.

There are a variety of reasons for parents wishing to send their children to local schools. There may be several children in one family attending secondary school and it is often helpful if the older children can take the younger ones along. The travelling arrangement may be convenient. There may be a grandmother near who can give children lunch or look after them when the parents are out at work. These are non-educational, but very important, reasons, especially for working-class families in which a second wage is of considerable significance in boosting the family income.

A vast number of children, especially in urban areas, are sent by their parents to local schools. Those parents have confidence in the capability of the local authority to sustain the education service which they think that their children require. But that confidence will be undermined if the unrestricted right of parents to choose to send their children elsewhere affects the viability and number of courses, and consequently the equality of educational opportunity.

We consider amendment No. 45 to be very important. It is, in effect, an addendum, because it seeks to add a paragraph. We hope that it will be accepted by the Minister, because he must recognise the rights of parents whose children's education is already catered for in what the parents consider to be a satisfactory manner. It will clearly not be satisfactory if the quality of education is undermined by the unfettered exercising of parental choice by a significant minority in these schools.

We would go further. We feel that education authorities are entitled to assistance in their longer-term education planning. There is a whimsical quality about the ways in which parents exercise parental choice. It is not always based upon fact or reason. We recognise that in a free society people must be entitled to indulge themselves—if that is the appropriate expression—but we do not think that that exercise of choice should harm children, interfere with their rights to a decent education, or undermine the capability of local authorities to provide education for the majority of children in the area in which the school is based.
