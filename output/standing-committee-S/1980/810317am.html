Mr. O'Neill
: As I said earlier, we are grateful to the Minister for providing copies of the notes on clauses. We expect that we shall have plenty of time to read them. Unfortunately, it appears that, if the motion is carried, the time that we shall have to read them will be mainly in Committee. We are concerned about the amount of time that we shall be spending in Committee during very busy parliamentary schedule. Many Committee members have a variety of responsibilities, both in the House and towards our constituents. Therefore, the amount of time that we spend here will detract from the service which we can provide for our constitutents. At the same time it will probably mean that we are not able to give the Bill the attention that it requires.

This is the third sittings motion on which I have spoken this year, and I find that the same arguments have arisen. The strength of the case against the motion is evident for all to see, apart from Government Ministers who are helping to push through the legislation. In this instance, we shall have to consider the nature of this complex legislation, which seeks to amend the Education (Scotland) Act 1980. The Bill is a compendious document, covering all aspects of Scottish education, and it is not the easiest legislation to come to grips with. The Bill is, in effect, an insert to the 1980 Act. We are not expecting a Bill from the Government to be attractive reading, but it does not make easy reading, and so we feel that it should be taken very slowly and carefully and not in the manner in which the JPEG 153 Minister seeks to railroad the Committee.

The Bill requires a great deal of consideration, not just by the House, but by a whole plethora of pressure groups within Scotland. This morning Labour Members, had the opportunity of an all-too-brief discussion with the chairman of COSLA. Our consultation was of necessity very brief, because he came down from Scotland. We met him at 10 o'clock and we had only about 25 minutes with him. People have to come to London to speak to us, because we do not have time to meet them in Scotland. We must be down here for most of the week. We do not have easy access, as English Members would have, to the Association of Metropolitan Authorities, and similar bodies, which are based in London. Therefore, those of us who feel obliged to undertake extensive consultations about the legislation are in considerable difficulty when we are required to meet as frequently as the Minister suggests in the motion.

Consultation lies at the heart of the case. We have already entered into discussions with groups such as the Scottish Consumer Council and the Scottish Parent-Teacher Council about the parents' charter. The latter group has made fairly copious and, in some instances, well-argued points about the legislation. My speech is falling on deaf ears. I knew that the Government Whip was a mute individual, but he appears also to be deaf.

As a responsible Opposition, we have an obligation to listen to Scottish opinion. I use the term "responsible Opposition" because it is clear that the Government listen to very few people in Scotland. We feel that we should meet as many groups as possible to consider with them their views on this important legislation.

We have already met the Scottish Parent-Teacher Council, the Scottish Consumer Council, the Scottish Secondary Teachers' Association and the Educational Institute of Scotland. We have now received representations from one of the parliamentary advisers to the National and Local Government Officers Association, and we have heard from societies that assist children who have hearing problems and from groups concerned with mental health and the mentally handicapped. Furthermore, some of us are trying to visit institutions that could be affected by the legislation. We consider that our meetings play an important part in equipping us to deal with the legislation.

We accept that the Minister has the full weight of the State machine behind him and that the Scottish Education Department is always ready to give him any document that may assist him in his arguments; on occasions those documents assist him considerably. However, we are not given that sort of back-up, so we must go into the highways and byways of Scotland to obtain information. I repeat that if we are in Scotland doing that, we are not down here carrying out the other tasks which are required of us as responsible Members of Parliament.

It is possible for us to travel to and from Scotland fairly quickly. For example, shuttles leave almost hourly from Glasgow, and every two hours from Edinburgh. We can also have early morning meetings 154 and ask groups whether they will see us anywhere that is available so that discussions can take place. But it is always embarrassing to have to brush off those who may have devoted their lives to matters covered by the legislation with a 35-minute meeting—for instance, one is always looking at one's watch so as not to miss the plane or train to London. Therefore, consultation—which we consider lies at the very heart of the Bill—is difficult.

Not only does the sittings motion make it more difficult for us to equip ourselves to consider the legislation, but it affects the treatment of the Bill. If we have to sit here for 10, 15 or perhaps 20 hours a week, we must undertake a lot of work in order to frame our amendments. With the best will in the world, no legislation is perfect. Even if we were to agree with it, we are conscious that the Amendment Paper is littered with Government drafting amendments, so there are many errors. Once or twice we have found spelling mistakes as well, but we do not wish to be too pedantic about that. My hon Friend the Member for West Stirlingshire (Mr. Canavan) was only a mathematics teacher, and was not therefore very concerned with spelling mistakes. But some of us who had other preoccupations in the classroom were conscious of the need to improve it. The legislation, even judged by the Government's own actions, is not perfect, and we must consider each amendment seriously.

We are grateful to the Government for providing us with the copious notes through which we shall now have to struggle. They will have to be digested and set against the amendments which the pressure groups have asked us to put forward on the matters about which they are unhappy. We shall doubtless have to see these groups yet again. The difficulty that will arise through these extended consultations is that it will undermine our service to our constituents in other respects.

I am not speaking from a partisan Labour point of view. There are four Government Members here who are members of the Scottish Select Committee—the hon Members for Aberdeenshire, East, for Renfrewshire, East (Mr. Stewart), for South Angus (Mr. Fraser) and for Galloway (Mr. Lang)—as is my hon Friend the Member for Glasgow, Cathcart (Mr. Maxton). The work involved in that Committee is considerable, and much attention is paid in Scotland to its activities. I have here a copy of the Glasgow Herald—which I have not yet had an opportunity to read because of my consultations this morning with a friend from COSLA—which I am sure gives extensive coverage of the Select Committee's proceedings yesterday.

The Select Committee has considerable political import in Scotland. Its activities are followed with great care by Scottish people. There are innumerable television programmes showing members trooping in and out of the meetings. The proceedings cannot be shown, because a Select Committee is a Committee of the House, and parliamentary affairs cannot yet be televised apart from the State Opening of Parliament.

The Select Committee has attracted much interest.

JPEG 155 We know that the Secretary of State is concerned about it. I know that the Under-Secretary, the hon Member for Edinburgh, North (Mr. Fletcher) has taken great care to follow up some of its recommendations. Ironically, the recommendations on inward investment were those of the Labour minority in the Select Committee and not those of the Conservative majority. This matter dominated the Scottish political scene in August. Even in Edinburgh during the festival people spoke of nothing but the report of the Select Committee on inward investment.

The five members of this Committee who are also members of the Select Committee have onerous and time-consuming responsibilities in respect of the Select Committee. It is a worthwhile job, and they should not be overburdened by the work of a Standing Committee.

It would be easy for us to say "The Conservatives are the majority. They can stuff the Committee with whomever they choose". But the political reality in Scotland is that there are not enough Conservatives to go round. The Conservatives may be good at winning elections in other parts of the United Kingdom, but they are not much good in Scotland—they could not even beat me. So we recognise that they have difficulty in manning committees.

We are not endeavouring to assist the Government, but we recognise that they are imposing burdens on Conservative Members. We also have a concern for the whole of Scotland, and feel that the Scottish people are entitled to representation of as good quality as anywhere else in the United Kingdom. If we are forced to sit in this Committee, the representation which the Scottish people will get will not be satisfactory.

There are many problems in Scotland and in the United Kingdom as a whole which we would like to consider, and if we are here, meeting in the afternoon as well as in the morning, we shall not be able to devote the amount of time that we consider necessary to the affairs of the country as a whole. For many of us that is a source of considerable regret. We accept that we have to specialise and do a job of work wherever the Whips tell us to do it. We do not complain or cavil about that. But the burden should not become unduly onerous simply because of the Government's desire to railroad legislation through the House—legislation which is wanted by very few people.

On the part of the Bill that we have been considering, relating to the parents' charter, we have had representations from the Confederation of Scottish Local Authorities. It has made it clear that it will be virtually impossible to implement the legislation in the coming academic year. So there is no pressing need for that part of the Bill.

The assisted places scheme deals with only a relatively small number of people. We think that the extension of privilege can wait another year. If the people have lived this long without it, they can wait that bit longer.

In regard to the Warnock part of the Bill on special educational needs, if for a moment—I think that my hon Friend the Member for West Stirling-shire will echo my feelings—we thought that the Government were prepared to spend a significant 156 amount of money to provide for the special educational needs of children, we should allow the Bill to go through in a flash. But we know that they are not prepared to do that. They have said that they are not prepared to provide any money to make this cherished dream a reality. They are prepared to pussyfoot around the Warnock report and make hollow noises, but they are not prepared to put their money where their mouth is.

If the Government were to undertake to provide the necessary £10 million or £20 million to make the dreams of Warnock a reality, we should seriously consider assisting the passage of this legislation. But as it is, we are not prepared to assist. We shall continue to give this legislation our detailed consideration. We shall not filibuster. I know that you have taken great care, Mr. Hunt, to equip yourself with a full understanding of our earlier proceedings, and I am sure that your reading of the Committee Hansards is illuminating in one respect, if not many others. That is in regard to the amount of time taken by the Minister to answer the case that the Opposition have put forward.

The inordinate length of the Minister's replies tends to suggest that he is trying to set us up for further sittings—either that or he is filibustering. We have seen the look of despair in Government Members' eyes when the Minister has gone on and on trying to reply to debates. We were wondering why that was. Then on Thursday all was revealed. It was a very simple plot—it had to be simple, because we know that the Minister is not a complicated man—to try to convey the impression that the Opposition were spending an undue amount of time on our reasonable and serious amendments. The manner in which he sought to convey that impression was by taking, in many instances, as long to reply to a debate as the debate had taken in the first instance.

We do not wish to attribute to other members of the Committee any such motives, but we feel that the Minister is, because of last night's vote, running scared. He wants to see this legislation pushed through. He feels that he must get something on the statute book which he can call his own. I think that this is the first major piece of legislation that the Minister has sought to put on the statute book.

The other monument which may well be constructed to the Minister's memory in Scotland will he written in the unemployment statistics of the Department of Employment Gazette, which, I think comes out today. He shares with the Secretary of State the major responsibility for those appalling figures, because he is Under-Secretary with responsibility for industry as well. In his responsibility for industry he is doing even worse than—
