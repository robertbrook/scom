(Clackmannan and East Stirlingshire)
: I should like, Mr. Hunt, to welcome you to the Committee this morning. As you may appreciate, Scottish Standing Committees are somewhat more intimate than others—even in this large Committee Room—partly because Scottish Members are in fairly constant contact with our colleagues from north of the border. If the meaning of a joke or a remark may on occasion escape you, I am sure that a note can be passed to you to explain the hidden meaning.

My hon. Friends and I believe that our sitting this morning may well be a historic one. We realise that there is a spirit of rebellion on the Tory Back Benches today. We believe that our amendment may catch the mood of the Tory Back Benchers, because it is our opinion that the Minister is seeking in some respects, through amendment No. 7, further to restrict freedom of parental choice, and that this may well arouse opposition among his Back Benchers.

There appears to be the prospect of a meeting this morning between the Chancellor of the Exchequer and senior Scottish Back Benchers about the Budget. I appreciate that we are not discussing the Budget. Nevertheless, what we are discussing is a contentious issue, and it may well be that rebellion on the Conservative Benches will break out in the form of a rash—as it sometimes does in the Labour Party—rather than take the normal discreet Conservative form.

This amendment in some respects brings us to the heart of our opposition to the Bill as regards the placing of pupils, because one of the consequences of the placing of pupils is the problem that some schools have in sustaining courses, as a result of the movement of pupils from what some parents 116 would consider to be less desirable schools. The consequence can be felt in two ways. First, staffing ratios in the schools that are thought to be less desirable will cease to be supportable by the numbers in the schools. Consequently, local authorities will be required, if they are to provide for the children in the other establishments, to transfer staff from the so-called less desirable schools to the more desirable schools.

Amendment No. 8 seeks to clarify the position in terms of the types of teachers. We wanted to make the point that it would be not only class teachers who were affected by the Bill but those specialist teachers who are in some instances peripatetic, and who are in very short supply. If the result of a more liberal system of parental choice was to undermine the staffing position in existing schools, that would undermine the quality of education provided for those children whose parents hitherto had been satisfied with their education. Consequently we are trying, through our amendment, to have the position clarified.

If the Bill affords more parental choice—and at the moment there is some doubt about whether it will do so—the Government could well endanger the educational provision for the majority of children, those whose parents have hitherto been content to send their youngsters to local schools. If parents in great numbers exercise parental choice in such a way as to denude some institutions of large numbers of pupils, we could reach a situation in urban areas in which only certain desirable secondary schools will continue to provide a wide range of courses— especially past the S4. Therefore, children wishing to pursue higher grade Scottish certificate of education courses might have a limited range of course options. The Opposition consider this to be a bad prospect which could bring us back to the bad old days of junior secondary school and senior secondary schools.

Whether it is the express intention of the Government to undermine the comprehensive system remains to be seen. From the expenditure White Paper that accompanied the Chancellor's Budget Statement it is clear that the Government have no strong commitment to maintaining both educational standards and pupil-teacher ratios at the levels reached in the present year. It is clear that it will be many years before the pupil-teacher ratios return to anything like the levels enjoyed in Scotland since the mid-1970s.

We are at a loss to know the Government's intentions concerning their commitment to the comprehensive ideal and the quality of education. This is not a purely partisan view. The General Teaching Council for Scotland expressed great concern about the effects of the cutbacks both in provision for education and on the quality of education.

The clause gives rise to grave concern about the future of Scottish education as we have known it during the past 10 years, since the firming up of the comprehensive system. Unlike that in other parts of the United Kingdom, the traditional system of education in Scotland has been comprehensive in the JPEG 117 sense that the majority of children have gone to the local school, not on the basis of class or ability, but because it was the school nearest to their home, so in the main the schools have reflected the community.

At one time we had systems of junior and senior secondary education in the urban areas, which meant that some children received inferior education because they were less able, and other children had a better chance because they were able to pass what used to be called the qualifying examination.

It is not for me to say that the Bill will bring us back to the bad old days when authorities like Edinburgh town council, the precursor of the progressive liberal Lothian region, provided education which has now frightened the Government into introducing legislation to curb that authority's expenditure on such worthwhile projects as education.

We are concerned that if parents are allowed unrestricted choice, or even choice restricted by the Bill, the exercise of that choice will have implications on staffing. We are concerned, first, that the staffing implications shall be quantified in financial terms, in the sense of its not resulting in local authorities having to take on more class or specialist teachers.

Secondly, we are concerned that local authorities will not choose, or see fit, to start moving staff around within an authority because if they do so there will be a condoning of mass movements of pupils from one part of a city to another. I say " city " because in most instances the effect will be felt most severely in urban areas. In other parts of Scotland the local school will be the only realistic option that the parents will have.

In urban areas where the parents could take advantage of the charter there are implications for those schools which are deemed by the parents to be less desirable. The reasons why parents will deem particular institutions to be less desirable may be many and varied. On Second Reading I quoted at some length from the Scottish Secondary Teachers Association's document which explained clearly the range of reasons why parents decide to change their child's school. It can be because of an alleged scandal in a particular school. On occasions the scandal may have no substance but it will be headlined in the local papers.

A school may have a sporting success, which may be a fluke because one generation of pupils happens to be good at a particular sport. If in one set of open examinations the children do extremely well, an impression is given that all the children receive a particular form of academic education which will be available to everybody who attends that school.

Another reason could be that in certain papers or journals a head teacher is praised for what could be called the progressive education ideals that the school seeks to implement. In some instances that may result in parents thinking that they do not want progressive education. Invariably journalists are responsible for an impression given about a school, 118 but the quality of that journalism is, to say the least, suspect. While some education correspondents, especially in Scotland, have a high standard, there are others who endeavour to present the education system in the least favourable light. Certainly those people who are unfortunate enough to have to depend on The Daily Telegraph for their information have over the years been subjected to a sustained and largely unsupported attack on the comprehensive ideal. I know that at a later stage we shall discuss what the Government may see fit to consider as a means of providing information. I do not wish to stray on to such matters as league tables and examination results, because those matters are not our consideration at present. I am merely trying to suggest that there are a variety of reasons why parents would wish to have their children moved from one school to another. However, in many instances, these reasons are not based on fact, or on any serious consideration: sometimes they are based on snobbish caprice. But in a free society I recognise that that is a consideration which parents are allowed to take into account.

The Committee should bear in mind, more than anything else, that the vast majority of children in Scottish schools are there because their parents are content with the education that their children receive. We have a responsibility to the children and to the parents who at present do not object to the education provided by the local authority.

Many pupils from that housing scheme would not move to King's Park secondary school in order to do those classes, even if they were given that option, because they believe that the pupils of King's Park secondary school are different from them. It would mean that practically all the brighter pupils, who provide the educational incentive for the rest of the school, would disappear from the school at 16, if not earlier.
