Mr. Donald Dewar
(Glasgow, Garscadden)
: We last debated this particular group of Estimates on 26 July 1977. On that occasion the right hon. Member for Ayr (Mr. Younger), the present Secretary of State for Scotland, said:

"the cost of supporting public transport is bound to fall upon Government and public finance generally, in this country as in most others.— [Official Report, Scottish Grand Committee, 26 July 1977; c. 49.]

The Secretary of State is noted for saying rather pedestrian and commonplace things with an air of great conviction, but on this occasion it is not a bad starting point for our debate. I certainly agree with him that the cost of supporting public transport is bound to fall upon the Government and upon public finance.

It is a burden that is almost inescapable.

When we look at these Estimates, we are in a very real sense talking about resources. The tragedy is that in many areas there is a great deal to be done and precious little with which to do it as a result of the Government's general approach to local government finance and to public sector finance. The system undoubtedly is in the tails, and the tragedy is that in the background all we hear from the Government are jarring and petulant lectures about economy, the virtues of self help and the philosophy of make do and mend.

It is a vast area and I cannot cover it in any comprehensive way without hopelessly overtaxing the patience of my hon. Friends, but I want to raise a number of specific issues with the Minister and, more in a spirit of optimism than confidence, invite some specific replies from him.

First, can I ask him to look at railway investment. I realise he is not directly responsible for that in the broad sense, but, of course, we do have the Strathclyde passenger transport executive and I would like him, if he would, to address his mind to the problem of reinvestment there. As I understand it, rolling stock 2 and other assets are depreciating very rapidly. We have an enormous amount of equipment which now, in the words of one top railway official talking to me the other day, can only be described as clapped out. We have DMUs and EMUs, which are 25 or 30 years old.

Basically, if we look at the capital budget for the Strathclyde region in terms of transportation and roads, we are looking at a figure in the present financial year of £50 million. If we assume that £36 million of that is going on roads, we are left with a mere £14 million to subsidise the running of public transport—the railways and the buses. There is genuine anxiety among officials as to how they are expected to finance the kind of reinvestment in the railways with the money that the Government are making available.

I accept that this is a general problem. We know that at the moment, unless we get an increase in investment in railways generally in Scotland of about 30 per cent., we are almost certainly going to be faced with deteriorating services in the years that lie ahead.

The particular question about Strathclyde is: who pays? It is time that the Under-Secretary put a little flesh on the bones and told us how the substantial replacement costs that will occur over the next few years will be met. I wish that the Government would spend a good deal more time wrestling with the problems of railway policy generally than with some of the irrelevancies that seem to take up so much of their attention. For example, we had the recent knock-down sale of assets in the form of three very prominent Scottish hotels. If those transactions had been carried out on the same basis by a public company, I suspect that the directors might well have been in default of their responsibility to their shareholders. I wish that we could get away from that kind of ideological nonsense and look at the great problems of deteriorating services because of lack of proper investment in public transport.

Could I ask the Minister to say a few words about the Kilmacolm line and the Scottish Office's attitude to it? I know that it comes under the jurisdiction of the Secretary of State for Transport, but presumably Scottish Office Ministers have considerable influence and interest in what is to happen there.

Going back to the speech of the right hon. Member for Ayr (Mr. Younger) in 1977, I was interested to note that in col. 52 the right hon. Gentleman specifically said:

"On lines where the losses are so great that the burden on British Rail is too great, and where the local authority can make only a limited contribution, which may not be quite enough to keep the line open, there is a major role for regional development funds."— [Official Report, Scottish Grand Committee, 26 July 1977; cols. 52-53.]

The right hon. Gentleman went on to say that the Government—in those days it was the Labour Government—should make regional development funds available in order to save a line in that situation.

The Scottish Transport Users' Consultative Committee has definitely recommended that this line should be retained. I hope that the Minister will use every power at his disposal to ensure that it is retained, and that the Government will take the admirable advice that the now Secretary of State gave in that debate in 1977 and will find the cash. Clearly it would JPEG 3 be unreasonable to put that burden on a PTE which is already heavily overstrained.

The final matter I should like to mention is electrification. I see in the press this morning that it is alleged that the Cabinet has decided in principle to go ahead with electrification. I would not quarrel with that. In fact, I welcome it. It is perhaps unreasonable to expect the Minister who is to reply to our debate—who, after all, labours very much in the kitchens—to know exactly what is happening in the salons upstairs in Downing Street, but at least he ought to be able to say something about the priorities.

I am encouraged to think that one of the top priorities will be the electrification of the Ayr line. If we get the kind of impartial approach to the problems of that area that was instanced when we were looking at the colleges of education, for example, I think that we may be able to hold out a great deal of hope for early action on the Ayrshire line. It is nonsense not to be pushing ahead with that kind of infractructure investment while the dole queues grow longer.
