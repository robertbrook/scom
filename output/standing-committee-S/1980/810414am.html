Here we have every option, from an independent chairman to annual alternates or an independent chairman plus two vice-chairmen.

The clause has given rise to expressions of anxiety across the board about the chairmanship of the committee. We recognise that the chairman has only a limited role, but unfortunately that limited role assumes great significance at times of crisis when discussions break down. We wish to ensure that at such times the chairman would be independent. We must take account, too, of the fact that there are responsibilities to be fulfilled in between crises.

Our amendment specifies an independent chairman and two vice-chairmen who would be able to deal with the committee's tasks in relation to salaries and conditions. The teachers' union backs that view, feeling that that would be the most even-handed way of dealing with the matter. Indeed, the EIS made representations to us and we framed the amendment round its words. We should be interested to hear the Minister's reply.

We may press our amendment to a vote, but in view of the arithmetic of the Committee that may be rather an empty gesture. Perhaps, too, the Minister may wish to reflect on his intentions towards the Committee and consider what has been said by hon Members both on his side and from the Opposition. Opposition hon Members all feel that the chairman must have the independence that would allow him to enjoy the confidence of the committee.

Relations between the local authorities and the teachers' side are normally good. However, because of expenditure cuts, resulting in teacher redundancies becoming commonplace, many of us expect that those relations will be soured. We hope that the establishment of the principle of an independent chairman would be a means of coping with the distrust and anxiety that may well develop on both sides as the education crisis in Scotland deepens. That is bound to happen when the education service is starved of resources and subjected to considerable cutbacks in staffing, resulting in teacher unemployment and so on.

If things get worse it will be even more desirable to have an independent chairman, with two vice-chairmen, one from each side, who will be able to deal with the other business of the committee over the course of the year. The role of the independent chairman, like the chairman's role in the past, will be restricted to activity in crises, when talks break down and there is a clear failure to agree.
