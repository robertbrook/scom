The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Malcolm Rifkind)
: I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman for pointing out that the amendment is purely probing in order to enable discussion on clause 3. The purpose of the clause is to provide for the

"Valuation of non-industrial buildings other than dwelling-houses"

direct to net annual value, rather than the present requirement of valuing them first at gross annual value and—after certain statutory deductions— establishing the net annual value.

This matter has been considered for some time. Indeed, the Layfield report on local government finance recommended that this system for non-industrial property other than dwelling houses had become outdated and burdensome, because with such commercial property it had become the practice to include leasing arrangements in the leasing out of such property. Therefore, there was an adequate basis, when considering the rental value of such properties, for including the landlord's responsibilities and the rental value actually determined.

It became not only inefficient but absurd to fix an arbitrary gross annual value and subtract from it certain statutory deductions—which may bear no relation to the costs involved—instead of going straight to a net annual value for rating purposes. This clause provides a simplification. It does not extend to dwelling houses because for those such an arrangement is not normally made—although there are exceptions. It is therefore more straightforward for the assessor to go to gross annual value and then to make the statutory deductions.

The hon Gentleman asked whether the recent decision by Edinburgh district council requiring tenants to pay for certain repairs had any significance. Although this policy is new to Edinburgh, it is not new to Scotland. East Lothian district council, for example, has pursued such a policy for some years. Neither is it relevant to the point under consideration, because, as I understand it from correspondence with Edinburgh district council, as a constituency Member, it would continue to accept all the responsibilities which a landlord would be obliged to accept. But it is not prepared to continue with discretionary services which it has provided over the years for various minor repairs to properties of its tenants which would not be the landlord's responsibility in the private sector and which it has no legal responsibility to maintain.

There has been some dispute about where the borderline should be drawn, and individual tenants JPEG 121 may be pursuing these matters in the courts or in other ways. The counts may have to clarify the situation, hut the point still has no effect here, because any legal responsibility of a local authority is landlord would be excluded.

I hope that that general explanation of the purpose of the clause will enable the hon Gentleman to seek to withdraw his amendment.

Mr. Rifkind
: From the assessor's point of view, the situation becomes far simpler. In the past he has had to look at the rental and then gross it up to the gross annual value and make certain statutory deductions to bring it to the proper net annual value. In future, he will be able to assume, for commercial property, that the evidence of the rents being charged includes the cost of the tenant's responsibility for repairs. He can forged about the concept of gross annual value in the case of these properties. He simply looks at the rental being charged and uses that as the basis for determining the net annual value. With a full repairing lease, the landlord will have included the cost of repairs when he determined the rent.

Of course, it is necessary to revise them only in the light of the next revaluation. It is not necessary to propose appropriate figures in the Bill itself, but it would be the Secretary of State's intention to use such a facility to introduce new figures in time for the next revaluation.

The amendment is unnecessary. As drafted, the clause enables the Secretary of State to amend the table of deductions. The amendment seeks to insert a more detailed description of how the table may be amended. It is not necessary for the clause to be so specific, because the options that the amendment implies are included in the clause in its present form. For that reason, I assure the hon Gentleman that he would be quite safe in seeking to withdraw the amendment.
