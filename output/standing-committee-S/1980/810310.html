The system of devolving placement requests to schools councils in Strathclydye seems to work fairly well. My hon. Friends and I were given some statistics by the representatives of the regional council which referred to the out-of-zone transfers between 1976 and 1980. In 1976–77, 2,093 applications were received, 1.768 were granted and 325 were refused. In 1977–78, 3,225 applications were received, 2,877 were granted and 348 were refused. In 1978–79, 3,381 applications were received, 3,049 were granted and 332 were refused. In 1979–80, 3,707 applications were received, 3,373 were granted and 334 were refused.

The figures for the applications that were granted include appeals. Over those four academic sessions, the total applications received were 12,406, those granted, including appeals, were 11,067, and those refused were only 1,339. In other words, 89 per cent, of the applications received during that time were granted.

The Strathclyde schools councils appear to be doing a reasonably good job and are keeping most of the parents there fairly happy. That brings into the question the need for the Government's proposed new system. With the possible exception of parts of Edinburgh, the majority of parents in Strathclyde and the rest of Scotland are happy with the way in which the local education authority—or in Image Col. 77 this instance, the schools councils—deal with placement requests and out-of-zone transfer applications.

But we are intrigued to note that the Government have realised that to afford wide opportunities for parental choice is inconsistent with the best interests of those whose children are at present in local authority schools. It is inconsistent with the expenditure patterns that they are seeking to impose on local authorities and with the best interests of education. To that extent I suppose that we could say that the Government are beginning to learn something about the implications of unlimited and unfettered parental choice as envisaged in the original parents' charter, which was paraded as a breakthrough in education.

To that extent we are not unhappy that amendment No. 8 is grouped with Government amendments Nos. 7 and 31, but we should like to think that at a later stage in our discussions the Minister will amplify his earlier remarks and explain why he has decided on this volte-face. We do not think that he has been nearly full enough in his explanation. We consider that amendment No. 7 is significant and has shown a major change in Government policy. I am sure that it does not necessarily enjoy the support of all Back Benchers.

We are told on Second Reading that somewhere enshrined in our constitution was the inalienable right of parents to choose. We argued that that inalienable right was very dangerous, that it could interfere with the education of others, and that it had to have limitations. We are grateful in many respects for Government amendment No. 7, although we should probably have been happier with amendment No. 8, which is more explicit.
