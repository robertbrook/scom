10.30 am

Mr. Donald Dewar (Glasgow, Garscadden): When we ended our previous sitting I had started to discuss amendment No. 7, which draws attention to the phrase

"as regards Scotland as a whole".

That relates to the preservation of the ratio between the classes of property which might be valued or revalued under a specification made under clause 2 by the Secretary of State.

I have given some thought to .the matter over the past day or two, and I am now even more doubtful about the phraseology and its implications. I say that with proper modesty because, although in my wilder moments I occasionally think that I am literate, I am conscious that by no standards am I numerate. The understanding of ratios and the .interrelationships between mathematical formulas is not one of my stronger attributes. We may be arguing about methodology and about a point that does not exist. I shall try to explain what I mean by that, and when I have done so perhaps the Under-Secretary will be able to put my mind finally at rest, so that we shall not need to spend a great deal of time on the issue.

To help the process of my understanding I thought it might be best to use a very simple model—the model would have to be simple if constructed by me. We might presuppose that there were only three classes of property which could be the subject of valuation under clause 2. Let us assume that they are A, B and C. In Scotland the ratio in the existing valuation roll—that is, before use of the powers in clause 2—is 30:50.20. If there were an order to revalue by 100 per cent, class A, the class with the rateable value which will produce 30 per cent, of the total, and if in the original roll that stood at £500 million, that figure would become £1,000 million. The distortion would have to be dealt with by the machinery set out an subsections (6A) and (6B). Leaving aside the complications of multipliers and divisors 'being used, it would be simple to apply a divisor of two, thus reducing £1,000 million to £500 million in order to maintain the ratio in that Scottish aggregate total. The advantage of doing that—I do not know whether the advantage is substantial enough to justify all the work, anxiety and puzzlement—is that even in the £500 million which would thus be maintained the proportions Col. 84 borne by individual properties would vary.

I 'think that that is clear and simple enough in terms of the Scottish figures. Let us stick with A, B and C. Let us assume that the Scottish ratio of 30:50.20 is totally different in a region where there are perhaps specific circumstances, such as a high oil-related industrial content. Instead of their being 30:50.20, let us assume that the proportions are 40:30.30 in that region, because presumably within Scotland there will be variations. Let us assume again that class A is revalued and, instead of going up by 100 per cent., it goes up by a considerably larger factor—a factor of three.

If the rateable value of class A in that region had been £100 million and it had gone up not, as the national figure did, by a factor of two but by a factor of three to £300 million, what would be the right divisor to apply? That is the crux of the amendment. We are trying to discover from, the Under-Secretary the answer to that question and the results that flow from his answer.

There are two ways to approach the problem. It depends on which way the Government intend administratively to do it. We could say that that £300 million total has to be divided in such a way as to preserve the ratios. We can do it on an all-Scotland basis and then divide by two, because that is what has happened to class A on the national, aggregate figures. Or we can say that we shall do it on a regional basis and apply the divider of three, because that is what this class of property has increased by. That is the multiplier, as a result of revaluation, on that regional basis.

At the end of the day, as a means of preserving the ratio on an all-Scottish basis, as I understand it, it would not matter which method we used, but it would have a considerable effect upon those who were paying the rateable value within that region. If we take the £300 million which is the new total rateable value for class A property within that region and divide it by two, because we are doing it on a Scottish basis, we see that clearly they will have to pay £150 million. If we divide it by three, because we are doing it piecemeal, region by region, they will pay only £100 million. That would make a distinct difference to the individual ratepayer.

If someone has a rateable value of £600,000 within the £100 million class A total in that region, if we apply the Scottish figure the rateable value will be £300,000. But if we apply the regional divider of three, the rateable value will be £200.000. Therefore, the end from which we approach the subject becomes important, and it is not clear from the wording of the Bill.

If we were to apply the national divider of two in the very artificial example that I have picked, it would be a considerable disadvantage to ratepayers in that category of property in that region. But there would no doubt be people in other regions—because of the offsetting so that the average would be reached—who would see it as a considerable advantage. If, instead of going up by a factor of three, revaluation had put someone up below the Scottish average of two—let us say, only by 50 per cent, rather than by 100 per cent.—to use the national divider would be a considerable advantage.

Image Col. 85 I hope that the Under-Secretary follows the point that I am making, perhaps a little laboriously. I think that it is fairly self-evident. In a sense, it does not matter, because if one was the regional basis, and takes Grampian, Strathclyde or whatever the assessor's district is and applies the multiplier or divisor on the basis of that region, one is keeping the ratios the same in each region. At the end of the day, one will reach the same aggregate position for Scotland as a whole. It seems self-evident— perhaps the Under-Secretary will hasten to assure me of this—that that is the only fair and equitable way in which to approach the subject.

I tabled the amendment because when I first read new subsection (6B) and read the phrase

"as regards Scotland as a whole"

, I assumed that we would be having an aggregate Scottish figure and ensuring that on an aggregate basis we would be maintaining the ratios. This would result in the distortion which I have suggested, and the victimisation of certain categories of ratepayers who fall into revaluation classes in particular regional areas.

Am I right in my assumption that the revaluation will be done on a regional basis? If the appropriate divisor is applied to each region on a regional basis, the ratio for each region will be preserved, and if each part is the same, the sum of the parts must be the same. If it is to be done like that, it ought to be made clear in the Bill. To remove the words

"as regards Scotland as a whole"

would make the point clearer and not encourage others innocently to fall into the misapprehension which I originally entertained—maybe with some justification. I understood that the ratio was a Scottish ratio and that a divisor, multiplier or combination of both would be applied to the whole of Scotland, absolutely and completely across the board. If that is done, the resultant distortions and difficulties will be considerable.

I hope that I have made that point clear. I see that the Under-Secretary is nodding in agreement. I hope that the Committee will find these highly artificial models useful in illustrating the point. I look forward with interest to the Minister's reply, on the basis of which we shall consider whether the point is of substance or, as I suspect, a matter of style. The removal at the words

"as regards Scotland as a whole"

would clear the way to a simpler understanding of what is behind this complicated machinery.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Malcolm Rifkind): The hon Member for Glasgow, Garscadden (Mr. Dewar) has done us a service by tabling the amendment and giving us the opportunity to deal with his very relevant points. He has pointed out, correctly, that the clause in its present form, referring to a requirement to maintain the ratio for Scotland as a whole, could be interpreted as meaning that as long as the Scottish aggregate between, for example, domestic and non-domestic ratepayers, was maintained it would not matter if there were individual variations as a result of partial revaluation.

Col. 86 The hon Gentleman was correct in pointing out that if the clause were interpreted in such a way it could lead to a possible unfairness between ratepayers in particular areas. If a simple Scottish aggregate were applied it could lead to considerable disadvantage for particular classes of ratepayer unless care were taken to ensure a local variation.

The hon Gentleman was also correct in saying that the problem might be a matter of style. As I read the clause, there would be nothing to exclude the Secretary of State from choosing a method which differentiated between individual valuation areas, as long as the Scottish aggregate was maintained. But I accept that it would be preferable that instead of that being a possibility it should be declared as a clear intention of the clause.

We are not satisfied that the hon Gentleman's amendment would achieve that purpose, because it simply removes certain words but does not indicate the objective of the application of this part of the clause. It simply declines to refer to Scotland as a whole. But I am prepared to accept the hon Gentleman's amendment in principle, and if he will allow us to consider more appropriate drafting, I undertake to introduce such an amendment at a later date.

Mr. Dewar: I am grateful to the Under-Secretary. I laboured away at the point, and was not sure whether it was of much substance. But the more I thought about it, the more I felt that it was important. Clarification was needed, and I am grateful to the Under-Secretary for offering to take the matter to avizandum to arrive at a better form of words.
