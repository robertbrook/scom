I was expressing some anxiety about the difficulties outlined by the Minister. He had argued that one was that it presupposed the investigation on a discretionary basis of an alleged maladministration which took place under the aegis of the local authority prior to the local government reorganisation of 1975. The Minister went on to argue that that would be difficult, because although there was a moral obligation—and no more—on a local authority to take action as the local government Commissioner suggested, that moral obligation would be much weakened if the original sin was the responsibility of, say, a county council that predated the regional council or the district council which was now the successor authority.

I can see the force of that argument, but I am not entirely convinced by it. I should have thought that most local government units would accept that they were responsible to a large extent for the actings of their predecessors and that there was an element of continuity. Obviously, the further back the incident being investigated, the more difficult it is to get to the root of the trouble. People will have retired, people will have died, the files may even have been pulped. If the material to reach a conclusion does not exist, there will not be this difficulty, because there will be no recommendation. There will be a decision not to investigate or the investigation will be abortive.
