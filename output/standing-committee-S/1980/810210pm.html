Local Government (Miscellaneous Provisions)(Scotland) Bill

Clause 13
REDUCTION OF RATE SUPPORT GRANT WHERE LOCAL AUTHORITY'S ESTIMATED EXPENDITURE EXCESSIVE AND UNREASONABLE

Question proposed [ this day], That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

4.30 pm

Question again proposed.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Malcolm Rifkind)
: When the Committee adjourned, I was replying to points made by the Opposition and, in particular, describing the timetable that might apply if these provisions were passed and the Secretary of State wished to apply them. I said that we hoped, if an order proved necessary under these provisions, to make it available for consideration by Parliament in about June or July of the year in question.

However, although that might be the time when Parliament considered those matters, the local authority would know some time before that an order was contemplated in respect of it, for the Secretary of state would have had to submit the order to the authority for its comments. The Secretary of State would, of course, respond to any order of the courts. The Bill provides, as did the previous legislation, that if the Secretary of State cannot persuade the local authority of the rightness of his cause, Parliament might wish to take that into account. That is for Parliament to decide when considering whether to approve an order.

Gentleman can be reassured, if he is concerned that the local authority would not be able to accommodate a reduction in its rate support grant, that the question is at what stage the local authority had reason to believe that it might be likely to lose rate support grant. Under the existing statute, if the Secretary of State wishes to withdraw a grant, not only does the local authority not find that out until the following year, but it finds it out at a time when it is unable to reduce its spending because the spending in respect of which the RSG has been withdrawn has already taken place.

In many respects, therefore, the proposals under the Bill are far more attractive from a responsible local authority's point of view than would be similar powers exercised under existing legislation, because under existing legislation it would be too late to try to economise and to make savings in that year. The clause concerns the fair distribution of the total sum available for the resources element. The total sum is determined by the Secretary of State. Once it has been decided, the question arises of whether an individual authority or a minority of authorities are getting an unfair share of that resources element. It they are doing so, they get it not at the expense of the ratepayer or taxpayer, but at the expense of other local authorities. That cannot be avoided unless provided for.

I shall explain why an authority which was proposing either unnecessarily high spending or excessively high spending gets an unfair share of resources element. It operates as follows—and this explanation answers the hon. Gentleman's question. A local authority's entitlement to resources element is calculated by multiplying the authority's rate poundage by the amount by which its actual penny rate product is less than its standard penny rate product.

The important point is that we are multiplying the rate poundage of the authority. Therefore, the higher an authority's rate poundage, the greater the amount of resources element it will receive. The local authority is in a beneficial position. It is in control of deciding its own rate poundage, but it can do so in the knowledge that the higher it fixes its rate poundage, the more resources element it will receive. It will receive a larger slice of the available cake and, therefore, less will be available for all the other local authorities.

We fix the sum due to an individual authority by multiplying that authority's rate poundage by the amount by which its actual penny rate product is less than its standard penny rate product. The standard penny rate product is found by multiplying the authority's total population by the national standard amount per head of population.

Clearly, the next question is, how do we determine the JPEG 433 national standard amount per head of population? The aggregate amount that is available is determined by the Secretary of State when he fixes the needs to resources ratio. But the national standard amount per head is determined by the Secretary of State and it represents the amount that every authority receiving resources element will thereby be enabled to raise for every member of population from a local rate of 1p in the pound.

I shall describe the formula in algebraic terms, which may give the hon Gentleman a chance at his leisure to see how this matter is determined. The formula for the national standard amount applied by successive Governments is the total rateable value divided by the population and multiplied by the rates plus the resources element divided by the total rates. I do not expect the hon. Gentleman to have absorbed that as 1 said it, but he asked how these matters were determined, and they are inevitably complicated. This is not a new formula but the one which has been applied over the years. However, it determines the national standard amount used in the calculation.
