We feel that it is necessary at this time to impose obligations on local authorities. We think that with the best will in the world, local authorities, although their financial grants are reduced, should have these obligations imposed upon them, and consequently they must try to find the money or they must pressurise the Government to make money available.

We think that the provision of adequate educational facilities for those youngsters with special educational needs must be given as quickly as possible. I am sure that we shall return to the fact that this is the International Year of Disabled People. We feel that the Government cannot simply hide behind this legislation and say that this is all they will do. Amendment No. 72 seeks to place an obligation on the local authority to provide for special educational needs. Simply to say that there should be a power is not sufficient. Over the years local authorities have done what they have seen fit to do to deal with these matters, but their performance has not been good enough. Following on the recommendations of the Warnock report, we hope that in the first instance there will be an obligation on local authorities to provide for special educational needs.

We make this point in respect of those children who have not yet attained school age, because the provision of nursery education in Scotland is uneven, especially in the regions resulting from the aggregation of old local authorities, which in the past had differing priorities. Consequently the level of provision across a region such as Strathclyde is not consistent. Therefore, amendment JPEG 327 No. 76 seeks to ensure that the obligation on local authorities to provide for the special education of young people below school age should be irrespective of nursery provision.

This may seen contradictory in the light of the calls that have been made for integration at every level of education, wherever possible, but an exception has to be made for children with special educational needs, a group which is poorly enough served by normal nursery or pre-school playgroup provision. The legislation should reflect the commitment of giving high priority to the special educational needs of the under-fives.

Children under 5 are in some respects probably the most sensitive group. Their parents are having to come to terms with the children's disabilities and they need as much relief as possible and assistance so that they can adjust to the children's special needs—not only educational needs. Nursery provision is a tremendous help in this respect. But when these children get older and their natural school life has ended at the age of 16, a variety of problems can confront them. It is desirable that during this crucial time between the ages of 16 and 19 they are retained, where necessary, within the school system.

We appreciate that in some parts of the country there are intermediate centres. Unfortunately, there are far too few of them, and in certain circumstances they are having to be closed because of expenditure cuts. Nevertheless, there ought to be an option for young people between 16 and 19 to be included within the school system without any prejudice to their subsequently being provided for elsewhere.

We are looking to this legislation for a comprehensive system of care for those with special educational needs, and we do not envisage those needs stopping at the age of 16. Some groups could benefit from continued education within the school system. Others might do better at an intermediate centre, if that were possible. If it were not possible, and if there were places within the school system, we would prefer these children to remain within the school system until places could be found for them elsewhere. We think that the extesion of age from 16 to 19 would be preferable.

Employment opportunities for this age group are probably more limited than for any comparable group of young people aged 16 to 19, and with present high unemployment it would be desirable to keep them within an environment where they would feel settled and at home, and where their emotional development could be sustained in a way which would not cause undue anxiety.

We recognise that there would be problems for the 16- to 19-year-olds in terms of the sheer age, weight and, size of many of the youngsters, but we feel that there should be an adequate back-up system to meet the challenge of this extension of educational provision. At this stage, we should not talk only about youngsters with severe special educational needs. We would also want to make sure that implicit in the Government's approach in an attempt to ensure that these young people can live as full a life within the educational system as is possible.

It was built in the late 1960s as a purpose-built comprehensive with 328 community facilities and a special education unit. It serves the area of the county of Clackmannan, extending into parts of Sterlingshire around Dunblane and Bridge of Allan. The children with educational difficulties are mostly educationally subnormal. I do not think that there are any with physical difficulties. They are included within the normal timetable of the school so far as that is practicable. They participate in un-streamed tutor groups. Although they spend a fair amount of time in the educational unit receiving remedial education and the basic educational skills, they are a part of the life of the school.

We ought, to be working towards the maximum degree feasible of integration.
