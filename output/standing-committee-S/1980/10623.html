Mr. Bruce Millan (Glasgow, Craighton)
: We are discussing local government finance this morning. The first thing that I want to say is that it is in a state of crisis—a worse crisis than I ever remember. The reason for that inevitable crisis has been the way in which the Government have behaved towards local authorities in Scotland in the past two years or so.

The Government line has been—and no doubt we shall hear some of it this morning—that they have been very reasonable, even generous, to local authorities, that they have had the interests of the ratepayers at heart and that, if it were not for a few extravagant authorities which have defied the Government, everything would be all right. To add to that kind of line, the Government have tried over the past couple of years to sow the maximum amount of confusion in the public mind, knowing that the general public do not always understand the intricacies of local government finance, with the aim of putting all the blame for the present situation on the local authorities themselves.

Yet, the reality of the situation is completely different from what the Government have been trying to present to a rather bemused public in Scotland. If we take, for example, the authorities which are budgeting in the current year to spend more than the Government's guidelines, we see that of 65 local authorities in Scotland, far from there being only a few extravagant authorities spending above the guidelines, 59 are above the guidelines. What is more, the expenditure of 41 out of the 65 is 10 per cent. or more above the Government's guidelines, and there 44 are 28 whose expenditure is 20 per cent, or more above the Government's guidelines.

The guidelines were never intended to be firm and absolute; only indicative to local authorities. They were never intended to be mandatory, but they were intended to be accurate. The reality is that, far from the problem that we are facing being the result of the extravagance of a few local authorities, the Government's figures are meaningless. What they have done over the past couple of years, as I have said on earlier occasions, is to pile artificial figure on top of artificial figure, so we now have a situation where the kind of figures that the Government are talking about are completely out of touch with the reality of local government finance at the present time.

The basic dishonesty of the Government in all their dealings with local authorities over the past couple of years has led to a breakdown of co-operation between local authorities and the Government because Ministers have not come clean with them. They have not been honest with them. They have not been honest with the public. They have treated the local authorities in a extremely dishonest way. That is the basic reason why local authorities—not just a few, but local authorities as a whole—are no longer seriously co-operating with the Government. The Government have lost their trust by the way that they have behaved over the past couple of years.

This dishonesty goes all the way through the rate support grant. It goes through the housing support grant negotiations as well. The relevant expenditure figures, on which the grant is paid, are utterly phoney. The Secretary of State knows that they are phoney and that they cannot and will not be achieved. That means that, as the Government pay grant on phoney figures, there is a real cut of grant, which the Government have not the honesty to admit.

We have false inflation figures—false cash limits— written into the RSG settlement. That happened again in 1981-82, which the Secretary of State will not deny. I shall be interested if he denies it, because the costed figures demonstrate that in the current year most of the inflation cash limit figures have already been eaten up and there is very little left for inflation in the rest of the year. That also means a cut in grant. Added to the real inflation figures—which, whatever they are, are not the responsibility of local authorities but the responsibility of the Government—they present considerable problems for local authorities as for many other people.

In the current year we have had an explicit cut of 1 per cent, in the rate support grant. That, added to the cuts in grant over two years, which have been massive indeed, and in the current year a reduction in housing support grant of no less than a third, is the real reason why district and other rates have gone and are going through the roof. It arises directly from cuts in housing support grant, which again the Government have tried to hide, obscure and confuse with all sorts of figures.

The way in which this has been done raises the whole question of central and local government relationships. The central Government's line at the JPEG 45 minute, put brutally, is " We decide policy and the local authorities simply follow us. We lay down policy from the centre. Whitehall and St. Andrew's House know best. As soon as we have decided what ought to happen, the local authorities are meant to follow tamely behind."

An even greater distortion is that that has always been the relationship between central and local government. That is historically completely untrue. What the Secretary of State ought to keep in mind is that local authorities in Scotland are elected, and they are elected on a sounder mandate than the election of the Government in Scotland. They have elected members. They have certain rights and responsibilities, and they are entitled to take a view about their own levels of expenditure. Local government always has been entitled to take a view about its own level of expenditure. Local authorities do not share the Government's view that at the moment it would be good for the economy and employment if we cut public expenditure even further. They take the view—which I too share—that further cuts in public expenditure at the moment are economically illiterate and damaging and will succeed only in adding to an ever-lengthening dole queue in Scotland which, when the figures are published today, is likely to go over 300,000. So they are against Government economic policy. They do not think that it makes sense.

They also notice that the Government do not seem to be particularly successful in cutting their own expenditure or keeping their own public expenditure under control. They notice that the Secretary of State for Defence cannot control the Defence budget, although it is on a rising curve. Unlike local authorities, which are asked to cut their expenditure, the Secretary of State for Defence is to be given more money by the Government, and even he is not able to keep his Defence budget under control.

In the light of all this there has been a breakdown in central and local government relationships. That has arisen because the Government have basically not tried to persuade local authorities. They have tried to bully and coerce local authorities into obeying the dictates of the Secretary of State and St. Andrew's House. They have done it with a complete lack of honesty all the way through the negotiations over the past couple of years. The great triumphant result of this for the Secretary of State, who keeps telling us sanctimoniously how worried he is about the burden on the rates, is a 32 per cent. increase in rates in Scotland in 1980-81, and in the current year a 36 per cent, increase, although we were told at the time of the last RSG settlement that it would be very much less in the current year and would be within modest figures. The result is that over two years the domestic rate burden in Scotland—quite apart from the burden on industry and commerce—has gone up from £149 on average to £271. If one is unfortunate enough to live in a council house, one will pay additional rent as well. A person who is buying his house on mortgage will be paying additional mortgage payments as well.

Is the penalty, the £100 million, reduced, or is it then distributed among the other authorities in Scotland? We are entitled to an answer.
