Matrimonial Homes (Family Protection) (Scotland) Bill [Lords]

10.30 am

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Malcolm Rifkind)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that .the Committee have considered the Matrimonial Homes (Family Protection) (Scotland) Bill [Lords] in relation to the principle of the Bill.

Before speaking to the Bill Mr. Henderson, I extend what I am sure are congratulations from the whole Committee on your appointment to the Chairman's Panel, and I hope that you find this morning to be a suitable example of the height of activity over which you will have to preside in the coming months.

The Bill will, I believe, command the support of both sides of the Committee and, indeed, of the vast majority of people in Scotland. It has two main objectives. First, it provides certain occupancy rights for the spouses of a marriage. In the event of the breakdown of that marriage a spouse will be able to seek the approval of the court to occupy the matrimonial home. Secondly, it deals with the particular problem of domestic violence and, indeed, with what is often known as the case of the battered wives. It gives a certain extra protection, that has not been available until now, for those who have experienced violence, or fear violence from a spouse and so seek the court's protection with regard to the matrimonial home.

The Bill is drafted in terms of complete neutrality between the sexes, as would be appropriate at present, but in effect it means a considerable shift in the balance towards the interests of the wife, as it is normally she who has been the person who loses out as a consequence of a marriage breakdown, either because the matrimonial home has been in the name of the husband only or because any questions of potential violence to her or to the children are more common than those of violence towards the husband.

The actual incidence of domestic violence is, by definition, a statistic which is impossible to show with a degree of certainty. Clearly, in many cases where domestic violence has taken place wives have, over the years, been reluctant to give information either to friends and neighbours, or, more importantly, to the police, and so it is a phenomenon which, to some extent, can only be assumed rather than stated definitely.

However, there is no doubt that the bulk of domestic violence which takes place has been directed towards the wife rather than towards other members of the household. A survey taken in Edingburgh and Glasgow in 1974 showed that in 2 incidents of domestic violence 70 per cent, had been directed towards the wife and only 10 per cent, had been directed towards the children of the marriage. Even though 10 per cent, is an appalling figure in respect of violence towards children, the figures show that it is the spouse, and in particular the wife, who has been the person most subject to domestic violence as a consequence of various social attitudes and habits.

Clearly, those habits have, in part, been due to the traditional domination of the husband as head of the household. A belief that has persisted until recent years is that in some circumstances the husband was entitled to exercise a degree of physical chastisement in relation to his wife quite apart from the normal chastisement that parents might exercise towards their children. Values and standards have changed considerably over the years, but it is a matter of great regret that, even in modern times, there is a degree of physical injury exhibited towards wives in particular. Therefore, it is appropriate that protection should be provided, and the Bill is, I believe, an important contribution towards dealing with that problem.

Throughout the years, criminal law has been able to provide protection to a spouse who has experienced or fears the exercise of physical threats. But, for obvious reasons, wives have been reluctant to bring in the police or the prosecuting authorities. For that reason, it is important that the improvement of the civil remedies are available to a spouse, and should be improved.

The Bill is based on a draft Bill, produced by the Scottish Law Commission, which was published last July. Hon. Members who took part in the Standing Committee on the Criminal Justice Bill will recall pressing the Government to take urgent action when the Commission's report was published. I gave an assurance then that the Government considered that those proposals should be dealt with as a matter of urgency. I think that both sides of the Committee will agree that the Government have fully met the terms of that commitment. It is a matter of unusual precedent that a Bill based on a report which was first published in July last year should already be more than half way through its passage towards receiving Royal Assent. For that the Government can claim some proper credit. It is also a matter for congratulation to the Law Commission because, to a large extent, the Bill is based on its draft Bill, which was annexed to the report. That has made the task of preparing legislation much easier.

Hon. Members will be aware that the Bill has already completed its passage through another House. They may have noted that during that passage the Government suffered one major defeat when their lordships took exception to the use of the phrase " a titled spouse". They considered that a titled spouse could be only a spouse with a title and that outside another place it was difficult to believe that that was a relevant description. I do not wish to comment at this stage on the merits of that view, but we are now considering whether the phraseology is the most appropriate. Clearly, it is a matter which will be dealt with in Committee.

JPEG 3 I turn now to the clauses and shall specify the significance of each. Clause 1 is fundamental to the Bill since it confers the basic right of occupancy in the matrimonial home on a spouse who is neither the owner nor the tenant. It provides, however, that these rights may be renounced in relation to a specific property on condition that the renunciation is sworn before a notary public or justice of the peace as being made freely and without coercion.

Clause 2 makes the occupancy right effective and meaningful by providing for the non-titled spouse to make any necessary payments and carry out repairs to the matrimonial home so that the occupancy may be enjoyed to the full. It also empowers the court to apportion certain household expenditure between the spouses, for example, to prevent a husband who is the sole breadwinner from refusing to pay the household costs of the home for his wife and any children.

Clause 3 gives the court powers to enforce or regulate the occupancy rights of either spouse and to safeguard their use of the furniture and plenishings in the matrimonial home. Where there is a loss or impairment of occupancy rights, the court may order compensation to be paid by the titled spouse. It also sets out the main considerations to which the court shall have regard in regulating the occupancy rights. These are the conduct of the respective spouses, their needs and financial resources, the needs of any children and the extent to which the matrimonial homes have a business use.

Clause 4 provides the court with an entirely new power to enforce the occupancy right. It can make an exclusion order to suspend the occupancy rights of either spouse where this is thought necessary for the protection of the other spouse or any children from conduct injurious to their physical or mental health. Provision is made for certain interdicts to be attached to the exclusion order. For example, the court is required to attach an interdict prohibiting the offending spouse from entering the matrimonial home. It may also grant other interdicts requested by the applicant spouse, including one prohibiting the other spouse from entering or remaining in a specified area in the vicinity of the matrimonial home. That will prevent an excluded husband from loitering menacingly at the corner of the street in which the matrimonial home is situated.

Clause 5 empowers the court to vary or recall any order relating to occupancy rights, including exclusion orders. It also sets out the circumstances in which such orders will cease to have effect, including the termination of the marriage.

Clauses 6 to 8 are of a more technical nature. Clause 6 provides that the occupancy rights of a non-titled spouse, usually the wife, may not be prejudiced by any dealings of the other spouse except in certain circumstances. That is to prevent a husband from selling the matrimonial home to defeat his wife's occupancy rights.

It will normally be necessary in relation to future house purchases in Scotland for a wife's consent to be obtained before the matrimonial home is sold. In order to simplify conveyancing practice on that 4 point, the consent must be in a form to be prescribed by the Secretary of State. For the purposes of the Land Registration (Scotland) Act 1979, the occupancy right shall be regarded as an over-riding interest which is not registrable, just like a short lease.

Building societies have made representations expresing concern at the implications on a transaction where the titled spouse, who would normally be the husband, may fraudulently have concealed the fact that he was married and therefore the wife's consent was neither sought nor obtained. They have been concerned both at the position of the building society and of any bona fide third party who may have purchased the house in ignorance of the existing of a spouse whose occupancy rights^ may be continuing without his knowledge. We are looking into this matter urgently and it may be debated at greater length. However, I wished to make the position clear now.

Clause 7 sets out the circumstances in which the court may dispense with the wife's consent. These are where it is unreasonably withheld, where it cannot be given by reason of physical or mental disability, where the wife cannot be traced or where she is aged under 18. It also prevents a wife who is the owner of the matrimonial home from taking advantage of the Married Women's Property (Scotland) Act 1881 to apply to the court for an order dispensing with her husband's consent to a dealing.

Clause 8 extends certain provisions of the Bill, suitably modified, to the increasingly common circumstances where the spouses are co-proprietors of a matrimonial home. It is likely that a growing proportion of young couples buying their first home will be joint owners, but for a significant proportion of elder people, the matrimonial home will be in the husband's name.

Clause 9 to 11 are further technical provisions to safeguard the non-titled spouse—usually the wife—against contrived bankruptcies and other contrived debts intended to defeat their occupancy rights. Although the Bill is silent on the point, the existing law will apply where a titled spouse unfortuately gets into debt. The existence of the other spouse's occupancy rights cannot prevent a trustee in bankruptcy from ingathering the bankrupt estate for the benefit of the creditors, even if that requires the sale of the matrimonial home. This is consistent with the Scottish Law Commission's general principle that the occupancy right should not confer greater benefits on the non-titled spouse than those otherwise available to the titled spouse.

Clause 9 empowers the Court of Session to recall the sequestration or to make other orders where it is satisfied that a sequestration has been contrived to defeat occupancy rights. It also imposes a duty on a trustee of a bankrupt estate to notify the sequestration to the bankrupt's spouse.

Clause 10 empowers the court to set aside a poinding of furniture and plenishings, or otherwise to protect a spouse's right to their use, where the diligence has been contrived to defeat the right.

JPEG 5 Clause 11 empowers the Court of Session to reduce a decree of adjudication of the matrimonial home or to make other orders, where the diligence has similarly been contrived to defeat the occupancy rights of the non-titled spouse.

Clause 12 is concerned with rented accommodation only and introduces certain important new provisions which will protect a spouse in the situation that I have described. It will have an important effect upon the many couples living in local authority housing in Scotland. It gives the court a power to transfer the tenancy from one spouse to another and, where appropriate, for payment of compensation to be paid by the spouse to whom the tenancy is transferred. It also allows the power to be exercised by the Court of Session on granting a decree of divorce. This will avoid a spouse having to raise two separate actions—one for the transfer of tenancy and the other for decree of divorce. The clause sets out the factors which the court should have regard to in considering whether to grant the transfer or not, and makes provision for the landlord to be given an opportunity of being heard by the court as well as being informed of any application for a transfer of tenancy and of its granting, if that is the court's decision. It repeals the interim provision in the Tenants' Rights, Etc. (Scotland) Act 1980 which enabled the housing authority to seek a possession order in the interests of a wife, where the marriage has broken down, and enables such a wife to raise an action for a transfer of tenancy herself.

Clauses 13 to 15 make detailed provision as to court orders regulating occupancy rights or providing protection against domestic violence, so that they may bite in an effective manner. Clause 13 sets the scene by defining a matrimonial interdict as one which restrains or prohibits any conduct by one spouse towards the other or a child of the family, or which excludes a spouse from the matrimonial home or from a specified area in the vicinity of that home.

Clause 14 provides that where a power of arrest is sought in an application for a matrimonial interdict ancillary to an exclusion order, the court is required to grant it without exception. Where any other matrimonial interdict is involved, not arising from an exclusion order, the court is obliged to grant the power of arrest unless it is persuaded by the spouse against whom the interdict is sought that a power of arrest is unnecessary in the particular circumstances of the case. It also provides that a power of arrest shall be exercisable without warrant by a police constable where he suspects that there has been a breach of the interdict. The power of arrest will remain as long as the interdict is valid. The clause provides for the appropriate chief constable to be informed of any power of arrest attached to an interdict and of any variation or recall. I recognise that these represent stiff measures in the context of the civil rather than the criminal law. Nevertheless, I believe that they are necessary if the aims of the Bill are to be effectively secured.

Clause 15 is complicated. Nevertheless, it is of particular importance if these rights are to be effect- 6 tively enforced. It sets out a procedure to be followed after arrest for breach of interdict in what is a grey area between the civil and criminal law. Where the breach of interdict also involves a criminal offence and the procurator fiscal decides that criminal proceedings should be taken, the normal criminal procedure will apply rather than the provisions of clause 15. However, there may be circumstances where a breach of interdict has taken place which, in the event, was not sufficiently grave to justify criminal prosecution, possibly because the presence of the police forestalled more serious conduct. The clause therefore provides that the police may release the arrested spouse if they are satisfied that there is no likelihood of further violence. They will report the facts and circumstances which gave rise to the arrest to the procurator fiscal, who, if he decides that criminal proceedings are not to be taken, will advise the applicant spouse and her solicitor that they may, if they wish, take the necessary steps to raise an action for breach of interdict.

Where, however, the police are not convinced that there is no risk of violence, they may detain the arrested spouse, even though no criminal proceedings are to be taken against him. The clause goes on to provide that the arrested spouse should be brought before the sheriff on the first lawful day after his arrest. Before his appearance in court, the procurator fiscal is required to take all reasonable steps to alert the wife and her solicitor to the situation and to establish whether or not they wish to raise an action for breach of interdict. When the court appearance does take place, the sheriff will hear from the procurator fiscal the circumstances of the case and whether the wife wishes to raise an action for breach of interdict. If he considers that a substantial risk of violence by the arrested spouse would arise from his or her release, the sheriff may order the further detention of the husband for up to two days. This will enable the wife to raise her action for breach of interdict, secure in the knowledge that the husband will not be released in the interim to molest her or to coerce her into withdrawing her action.

I have described these provisions at some length, because I recognise that they involve a degree of infringement of individual liberty. The Scottish Law Commission's draft Bill did not cover these vital procedural points, and the Government have therefore been at some pains to establish a procedure which will be both workable and fair. It is, moreover, necessary to reconcile providing a reasonable protection to a wife who has taken the significant step of legal action against her husband and yet remains aprehensive for her safety and, on the other hand, the risk that her husband, who may only be technically in breach of an interdict and whose motives may be far from vicious, being treated too harshly. I believe that the clause represents about as good a balance as is possible in the circumstances.

The only other significant clause is clause 16, which covers couples who are cohabiting but are not married to each other. It accepts that limited accupancy rights should be provided to such couples, but provides that these shall be limited initially to JPEG 7 three months and thereafter may be subject to further extensions of not more than six months at a time at the discretion of the court. Thus, the clause follows the thinking of the Scottish Law Commission in not seeking to provide the same rights for cohabiting couples as are conferred on spouses, but takes a rather more liberal line on the duration of occupancy rights for cohabiting couples. The Scottish Law Commission proposes an absolute maximum of six months. The Bill, however, enables the court to extend that period at its discretion. The main factor that we wish to take into account in allowing this discretion is, of course, the position of any children of the association.

The remaining clauses are of a narrow and technical nature, but I emphasise that the principles underlying the Bill are relatively straightforward and have been widely welcomed. Clearly, there are a number of important principles which the Committee that considers the Bill will have to consider at some length. In particular, the possibility of a husband being in prison for any time before a final question of interdict is determined is a matter of significant importance, as is the position in clauses 6 to 8 of third parties who have acted bona fide in purchasing a house.

Mr. Rifkind
: There is no provision in the Bill enabling a spouse to be imprisoned for a long time. If a spouse is suspected of having broken the terms of an interdict, including an exclusion order, in certain circumstances he may be arrested. He will be kept in custody only if the procurator fiscal has decided to bring charges, whereupon the case would proceed as a normal criminal case with the question of bail to be considered. If the procurator fiscal does not decide that criminal charges are appropriate, that person will have to be brought to court at the first opportunity and he will be released, unless the court is satisfied that the other spouse intends to bring an action for interdict. In that case, a maximum of only two days would be permitted for the further detention of the offending spouse. That would be in circumstances only where the court was satisfied that there was a risk to the physical or mental health of the other spouse or the children of the marriage. Only in those circumstances would the detention be justified. I hope that answers the specific point raised by the hon. Gentleman.

The Bill will make an important contribution towards creating genuine protection for spouses in Scotland—in particular, those that have been subjected to real or threatened physical or mental injury.

I am confident that the Bill will receive the support of both sides of the Committee. In that spirit, I commend it to the Committee.

The Minister said that the Bill will be welcomed. I very much welcome the Bill and pay tribute to the Government for introducing it so quickly after the Law Commission report. Although the Bill is expressed in mutual terms, as the Minister said, it is also true that, in the majority of cases, the additional rights which will be granted under the provisions of the Bill will be of benefit to wives rather than to husbands. That is absolutely right because the present law of Scotland in respect of rights to the matrimonial home is completely unsatisfactory, out of date, illiberal and offensive in that it does not give the wife of the husband who is the owner or tenant of the matrimonial home any rights at all. That is unacceptable, and it is necessary that we put that right.

The present position has led to considerable injustice to wives. Wives who have been subjected to violence by husbands often have had no effective remedy because of their fear of eviction. In some circumstances—such as pending divorce actions— the lack of right to stay in the matrimonial home has enabled husbands—or sometimes wives—to exert pressure and blackmail on a spouse who may be reluctant to agree to divorce proceedings. Therefore, I welcome the Bill.

However, I say straight away that it would be foolish to believe that, having enacted the Bill, we would effectively deal with all the problems with which it is intended to deal. I do not believe that any law, however carefully drafted and implemented, can deal with all the circumstances that may arise in the breakdown of a marriage.

The law provides a certain framework within which certain rights can be exercised, but it does not provide anything like a complete solution. In that respect I must say that, when one is dealing particularly with the question of battered wives, although the Bill is a tremendous step forward, I think we would be fooling ourselves if we believed that it provided an effective solution. Even when the Bill is on the statute book, there will still be many cases of wives who have to leave the matrimonial home, or feel that they have to leave it, in distressing circumstances. The resources of the social work department or of voluntary organisations such as Scottish Women's Aid will still be necessary.

I should like to draw the attention of the Minister to the fact that the present provision of refuges for battered wives in Scotland is wholly unsatisfactory.

JPEG 9 Not nearly enough facilities or places are available. The financing of the refuges is also wholly unsatisfactory. I hope that when the Solicitor-General for Scotland replies we shall hear something about the Government's attitude towards, for example, the recent COSLA report on the provision and financing of refuges. Such matters will still be very important even if we pass the Bill. There will still be a great deal to be done by way of social work, by both statutory and voluntary services.

As the Minister has said, the Law Commission produced a draft Bill. Anyone who has read the Law Commission's report will recognise that although the matters we are dealing with may seem at first sight to be relatively simple, they are, in practical terms, very complicated indeed. The area of law concerned is complicated, and so is the Bill. It will repay a good deal of attention in Committee, and I hope that the Government will adopt a flexible attitude; what the Minister has said this morning suggests that they will.

Despite the efforts of the Law Commission and of the House of Lords, which—I pay tribute to it— looked at the Bill very fairly, a number of points in the Bill remain unsatisfactory and I hope that they will be amended in Committee or on Report. But I do not want discuss all those matters in detail this morning or to deal with simple Committee points; I intend to confine myself to a number of points on which important issues of principle arise. The more one studies the Bill, the more one sees in it, and it would be possible to make a long speech about it while dealing only with important points of principle.

For convenience, Mr. Henderson, I shall go through some clauses of the Bill and make comments as I do so. Clause 1, which lays down the basic right of occupancy, is basically acceptable. It is essential to the whole working of the Bill. I am still not happy however, about subsection (6), which deals with renunciation of rights. It is not normal for me to argue in favour of the involvement of lawyers unless I believe that that is absolutely necessary. Here, however, there is to be a renunciation of a basic right. I believe that the only protection for the person making the renunciation is independent legal advice, which is not provided under the Bill. We all know from our constituency and other experience that many people give up basic legal rights without understanding what they have done and without taking advice. Although subsection (6) was amended and improved in another place, it still requires a certain amount of additional amendment.

Clause 2 is also basically acceptable. There is no point in giving a basic right or occupancy to a spouse or wife—I shall use the term " wife " for convenience unless she is able effectively to exercise that right. Clause 2 makes the right effective, and that is welcome, although some of the drafting is somewhat obscure and I am not entirely clear as to what circumstances subsection (4) is directed. If it is intended to apply to joint owners or joint tenants of a house where both already have occupancy rights, 10 I am not sure why it has been included in clause 2. I would have thought that it should appear as a separate provision in some other part of the Bill. That is a point that can be dealt with in Committee.

With clauses 3 and 4 we come to the heart of the Bill. Clause 3 gives power, by application to the court, to have the rights of occupancy determined, enforced or adjusted between one party and the other. I shall make two points on clause 3.

Clause 3(3) directs the court as to the circumstances to which it must have regard in making decisions under the clause. Clause 3(3) covers a mixture of circumstances which will, in many cases, conflict with one another. It appears that one factor may work to the benefit of the wife and another to the benefit of the husband. There does not seem to be a satisfactory way of amending the factors laid down in clause 3(3) and at the end of the day I think we must have regard to the decisions and discretions of the court on these matters. When there are children in the family I would attach over-riding importance to the needs of the child or children. That is covered by subsection (3) (c). I hope that the courts will interpret the clause in that way.

The second point is more difficult, because as I understand it—and the Solicitor-General for Scotland will no doubt correct me if I am wrong— clause 3 is not intended to provide an exclusion, either legally or de facto, from the matrimonial home. It is intended to deal with circumstances in which there is difficulty between husband and wife, but the assumption is that both will remain living in the matrimonial home, or if one has left or been forced out, he or she will return and both will five there. If there is difficulty between them the court is allowed to assert that both have a right to stay there and can adjust the rights of occupancy between one and the other.

This matter is important but we would be deluding ourselves if we believed that in a large number of cases the clause, by itself, as drafted, would be effective. It is all right if one is talking about a very large house, where it is possible and practicable—although it may not be agreeable—for a husband and wife who are in serious disagreement to live separate lives on the premises because they can to a large extent avoid one another and adjust their living arrangements in a practicable manner. But in a three-apartment council house it is unrealistic to believe that, under clause 3, with the best will in the world there can be an arrangement which will adjust the respective occupancy rights of the husband and wife and allow them to live close together, sharing a bathroom and kitchen, in reasonable amity. I do not believe that that is possible. I cannot imagine how such a result can be achieved, even with the best will in the world, the best drafting and the most sensitive handling by the court. I believe that there are practical difficulties.

: It would be improper for me to comment about a matter which does not fall within the ambit of the Bill. I suppose that if one has such a pathological dislike of solicitors as to prevent one buying a house through them, one could live under the bridges instead.

If an occupancy right is arranged under clause 3, it may de facto amount to an exclusion. For example, if a couple have two flats and the occupancy right provided that one party lived in one flat and the other party lived in the other, that would be a mutual exclusion or a de facto exclusion of one spouse from the other flat and the other spouse from the first flat. Arrangements under clause 3 might amount to exclusion de facto. Such arrangements under clause 4(2) could be the subject of an exclusion order.

If the right hon. Member for Craigton believes that there is a gap between the stringent requirements to be established in order to obtain an exclusion order and what he might call a sort of surreptitious exclusion order under clause 3, I am sure that we shall consider sympathetically anything which fills that gap. I do not think that it is necessary, but I understand that he would not want a surreptitious exclusion order under clause 3, and under clause 4 a situation in which the party had to be branded as an undesirable and violent person in order to obtain ah exclusion order. It is a valid point and we shall look into it.

I come to clause 6, which deals with a matter that troubles us all. To find a solution is extremely difficult. I agree with my hon. Friends the Members for Galloway (Mr. Lang) and South Angus (Mr. Fraser), together with the hon. Member for Garscadden, that a bona fide third party who buys a house should not be put at a disadvantage because the unknown wife turns up from Australia three years later. The difficulty is that one cannot prove a negative. The hon. Member for Garscadden cannot prove that he is not married. He can prove that he is married, but he is not able to prove to us that he did not marry Brigitte Bardot in a register office in Paris last night. The evidence of his vigour this JPEG 37 morning is an indication that that was unlikely— that is, if young French women understand the necessity of consumation of marriage.

The difficulty is important and intractable. We might consider some concept such as the limitation of time under which the spouse could exercise the right. But there may be difficulties in respect of that too. We are giving urgent consideration to the matter, a solution to which, if we are not to breach the protection of the non-titled spouse, is difficult.

On the question of compensation under clause 12, in the private sector there might be a lease which could be transferred for value. Clearly it would be wrong if a person could obtain a transferable asset without compensation to the person from whom that asset had been obtained compulsorily.

My hon. Friend the Member for Galloway asked what the English experience was. The English experience is varied. We must understand that in the Bill we are attempting to deal with marriages and property situations which will be of infinite variety. I do not think that it is useful to look at the English experience as a particularly good guide, to what our experience may be in Scotland.

My hon. Friend the Member for Galloway asked about casual relationships and the difficulties which arise in clause 16 in respect of cohabiting couples. I agree that this is an area into which we should wander with caution, because the protection of subsection (5) to the married spouse, whereby the occupancy rights end on divorce or the ending of marriage, could in no circumstances apply to cohabiting couples of whatever strength, duration or casuality. There is no legal process by which the relationship can ever be ended.

I agree that there are anomalies and difficulties. No doubt the Committee will want to consider carefully the question of the extent to which cohabiting spouses should be protected. On the other hand, with the abolition of the regular forms of marriage many people may live together for long periods, and they should have protection in circumstances in which they may be made homeless.

The hon. Member for Dundee, East (Mr. Wilson) was worried about household furniture. If the hon. Gentleman looks at subsection (5), he will see that household furniture is covered. It is also covered in clause 3 (2). Furniture owned or hired, therefore, is covered by the Bill. However, I understand his anxiety.

The hon. Gentleman raised the question whether there should be corroboration of violence. I shall deal with the criminal matter in a moment. Of course, the spouse must be believed. The court will have to take fairly strong and far-reaching actions, and clearly corroboration would be beneficial. But the spouse must establish that the application is bona fide and not merely spiteful or vicious. The 38 hon. Gentleman also raised the matter of tied houses which is dealt with in clause 12 (7).

My hon. Friend the Member for South Angus asked particularly that the third party should not be affected in terms of clause 6. Perhaps that is a matter to which he can give his attention and obtain the golden apple for finding a solution. He asked if the matrimonial interdict and power of arrest were mandatory. They are mandatory. As the hon. Member for Garscadden said, if one were to apply for the interdict and then provide for the power of arrest, it is possible they could be obtained mandatorily in that form. We shall certainly have to see if the procedure can be improved.

It is very difficult to adjust the concept of protecting the spouse by interdict in what is half a civil dispute and half a criminal act in circumstances of domestic tension. That is the method that we have come up with and we hope that it will work in practice.

As the hon. Member for Fife, Central said, this law is an attempt to adjust human behaviour. The motivation of human behaviour is something which not even he, I think, would claim to solve.

I fear that the Bill will not prevent spouses from assaulting one another. I regret that it will not prevent domestic unhappiness or domestic dispute. The most that we can claim for the Bill is that, in the unhappy circumstance of domestic dispute, it will make more equitable the results that flow from that dispute, and in the event of violence, continued violence will not be necessitated because one of the spouses either has no alternative but to go back and live with the violence or have nowhere else to live.

As the hon. Member for Garscadden said, frequently, a client may say " I shall not give evidence against my husband ", because after mature thought she has said to herself either " I shall give it another try for the umpteenth time" or " What shall I do and where shall I go if I give evidence against him? ".

The Bill is an attempt to solve the difficulties of a spouse who cannot escape from the cage of violence. We hope that the provisions in the Bill will go a long Way if not to preventing violence, at least to preventing its unnecessary repetition.

I commend the Bill to the Committee. I am sure that our future discussions will be constructive and fruitful and will make the Bill as adequate as possible in very difficult circumstances.
