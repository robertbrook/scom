The amendment will correct an omission in section 1 of the Licensing (Scotland) Act 1976, under which licensing boards of districts, divisions and islands areas are constituted. Recently, the Western Isles council wished to merge the two divisional licensing boards within its area, and found that section 1 of the Act precluded it from doing so. The Act provides for areas to be divided for licensing purposes upon the determination of the district or islands council concerned, but not for existing divisions to be merged.

There seems to be no good reason why a council 658 cannot choose to reverse an earlier determination when circumstances demand, and the amendment will enable mergers to take place.     'In section 42 of the Lands Valuation (Scotland) Act 1854 as amended by the Lands Valuation (Scotland) Amendment Act 1912 and by Schedule 6 of the Local Government (Scotland) Act 1975 the proviso added by virtue of those amendments shall cease to have effect and the following proviso shall be inserted:—


    "All machinery fixed or attached to any lands or heritages; provided:

    1. That in any building occupied for any trade, business or manufacturing process the expression machinery fixed or attached shall be construed as including all machinery, machines or plant in or on the lands or heritages for producing or transmitting first motive power or for heating or lighting such building, but shall not include machines, tools or appliances which are only so fixed that they can be removed from their place without necessitating the removal of any substantial part of the building and
    2. That the expression machinery fixed or attached excludes:
      1. After the years 1977-78 any electrical motor in any industrial or trade process whether in a building or not.
      2. After the years 1981-82 any other item on the 680 heritage whether in a building or not the total cubic capacity of which (measured externally and excluding foundations, settings, supports and anything which is not an integral part of the item) does not exceed two hundred cubic metres, and which is readily capable of being moved from one site and re-erected in its original state on another without the substantial demolition of the item or of any surrounding structure.
      3. After the years 1981-82 any item or part of such item whether in a building or not is moved or rotated by motive power as part of the process of manufacture.
      4. After the years 1981-82 a pipe or system of pipes whether in a building or not forming part of the equipment of, and wholly situate within, a factory or petroleum storage depot or premises comprised in a mine, quarry or mineral field.".'—

    This is an important new clause, but I shall be brief, because this matter has been the subject of public debate and hon Members are probably well aware of the issues involved. The amendment looks very esoteric, but perhaps I may summarise it for the benefit of the Committee.

    The new clause contains a significant difference of approach to valuation for rating purposes of large industrial plants as between Scotland and the rest of the United Kingdom. The introduction in England, Wales and Northern Ireland of changes in valuation regulations in 1960 and 1974 had the effect of excluding from valuation items of moveable plant and machinery. In Scotland, however, there has been no updating of the relevant legislation since 1902.

    As a consequence, because action wsa taken in England, Wales and Northern Ireland and not in Scotland, plan and machinery inside buildings is largely exempt throughout the United Kingdom from rates. Plant in the open air, however, is largely exempt in England and Wales but is highly rated in Scotland. It is an important matter. In certain cases the burden in Scotland can be as much as five times that in England and Wales, and the sums of money involved can be as much as £4- million between comparable plants north and south of the border.

    The consequences of the anomaly are threefold. First, it is in principle highly undesirable that this state of affairs should exist. Successive Governments have tried to equalise the rating burden on manufacturing industry north and south of the border. That is, for example, why we have industrial derating in Scotland. Secondly, it means a higher cost burden on industries such as petrochemicals in Scotland, making them less competitive. Thirdly, such a burden clearly could be an element in an investment decision about the location of such a plant north or south of the border. I believe that we should do nothing artificially to discourage petrochemical and similar industries in Scotland.

    I confirm that the courts cannot provide a remedy for this anomaly. There has been a test case, and it is clear that legislation is necessary. I know that the Scottish Office has taken action. A review body was set up in 1979 under the working party on local government finance in Scotland. A test case was considered at JPEG 681 Ardeer in Ayrshire which concluded that an anomaly existed.
