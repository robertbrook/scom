Mr. Fletcher
:I now come to the substance of the amendments, which I mentioned in my opening remarks. I remind the Committee that on Second Reading my right hon Friend the Secretary Of State said that we would table an amendment which would give a right of appeal to the Secretary of State in any case where a proposal relating to a denominational school would materially worsen the position of such schools in comparison with other schools. The amendment honours that undertaking.

I should like to make the Government's position on denominational schools quite plain. We have said on different occasions that we have no intention of altering the balance achieved by the 1918 Act and certainly have no plans to end the existence of separate denominational schools. What the Bill does is to repeal many of the controls exercised by the Secretary of State over non-denominational and denominational schools alike. Instead, there will be new provisions requiring education authorities to undertake consultations with parents and other prescribed persons before they can implement any change of a prescribed kind in their educational arrangements.

Certain proposals will also continue to need the Secretary of State's approval, and these will include, under new section 30C, any proposal to close a denominational school, which will mean that the pupils who attend that school will have to go instead to a non-denominational school. Those were the words that I read to the Committee in respect of amendment No. 198, when I said that they strengthened subsection (3) on page 32 of this Bill.

There has been some public misinterpretation of the amendment. It has been suggested—not, I am glad to say, by members of this Committee—that in some way the amendment would give Roman Catholic parents a right of appeal to the Secretary of State, whereas non-Catholic parents would not have such a right, that, where two schools side by side were to be closed, the Catholic parents would be able to appeal to my right hon Friend where the other parents would not. I hope that hon Members will agree that the terms of the amendment make it absolutely clear that that impression is untrue.

Roman Catholic parents will not have a right of appeal to the Secretary of State. The position of Roman Catholics or any other group of denominational parents will be no different from that of non-denominational parents and, in the type of case that I have just quoted, where denominational provision and non-denominational provision were being reviewed together, there would clearly be no reason to claim that there had been discrimination or that the balance was being disturbed.

The purpose of the amendment is to give any person authorised by a church or denominational body the right to refer to the Secretary of State any proposal by an education authority relating to a school of his denomination where he considers that the proposal would result in a significant deterioration in the position of those schools compared with that of other schools managed by the education authority.

Any such reference is unlikely to be made lightly. The denominational body making the reference and the authority must, under the terms of the amendment, have held discussions and failed to reach agreement.

JPEG 653 To invoke the appeal to the Secretary of State at this stage is a significant step, indicating in effect a breakdown in relations between the education authority and the denominational body. After the reference is made, the Secretary of State has to satisfy himself that negotiations have broken down. If he is not so satisfied, the authority and the denomination must continue their discussions. He must also satisfy himself that the claim was justified, in that the proposal would lead to a significant worsening in the relative position of the denominational schools concerned. If he is so satisfied he will examine the proposal, and if he decides to approve it may impose conditions as regards the religious instruction of any children who will no longer receive their education in a denominational school.

I wish to underline that only persons authorised by a denominational body can appeal to the Secretary of State. Parents cannot do so. That must be understood by all who are interested in this part of the Bill, especially hon Members who may wish to explain it to their constituents over the weekend.

Such a reference could not be made lightly. The amendment retains, in the new situation produced by the relaxation of controls, the essence of the balance of denominational and non-denominational education that exists in Scotland today. I invite the Committee to accept the amendment.
