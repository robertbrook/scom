Mr. Maxton
: The Minister is much more charming than his colleague, but, even so, his answer was unsatisfactory. It is not good enough, when we are talking about industrial relations matters, to say "That is what is in existing legislation, that is what is done now, so please let us carry on doing what we are doing". Since the previous legislation was drawn up, there have been changes both in the law—apart from this law—in industrial relations, and, more importantly, in the spirit in which we handle industrial relations these days.

The Minister said that there would not be agreement if it were left to the two sides of the committee to come to a decision on what the arbitration should be, the matters in respect of which the committee should have arbitration, and how the settlement should be formulated. It is not impossible. I sat on a salary negotiating committee at its formation which did exactly that. We sat down—admittedly over a reasonably lengthy period—and settled the ways in which arbitration should be carried out. We decided that there had to be clear agreement that agreement could not be reached. Once that point was reached, it was up to the chairman to rule on the matter. If the chairman ruled that agreement could not be reached—he clearly did not do so until after lengthy negotiation—it went to arbitration. Which matters were put to arbitration was again settled by agreement—with difficulty.

I do not say that it is easy, but it is a better way of solving the problems of arbitration and thrashing them out than the method that the Minister suggests. Not only does such a method come to a conclusion that is at least agreeable to every person on each side in the machinery —the best agreement to reach is clearly that to which everyone on the committee agrees—but, if we examine what the Bill says, we see that the method that I suggest would take less time.

I assume that the Secretary of State cannot act until the machinery is set up, for he does not know exactly which bodies will be represented on the committee until he has set it up. That is what new section 97B(2) states. He cannot, therefore, start to consult about the arbitration procedure before then. When he does so, it is not with the two sides of the committee that he has to consult but with the various bodies that will make up the committees. That is a different matter from discussing the issue within the committee.

By the time the Secretary of State had had meetings with each of the six different unions, the various local 816 authorities, the governing boards of colleges of education and the governing boards of central institutions, and has tried to reach a consensus of all of them as to the right approach, I am convinced that it would take him longer than the procedure outlined in my amendment. The amendment shows a better way of solving the problem, both because it is done by agreement, consultation and discussion and, because, in my view, it would be more efficient than the method that the Minister suggests.

I find it insulting to people who are experienced negotiators—much more so than the Secretary of State, his Ministers or, with respect, his advisers—from local authorities and trade unions and who have spent a long time on the subject and know what they are talking about, for the Government to say that they are not capable of reaching agreement. Although it is not an easy task, it can be done, and I have seen it done. The Minister should reconsider the matter to see whether the method that I suggest might not be better, and not simply cry "This is the existing law and the better way of proceeding". Agreement should be reached in the committee.

I wish to clarify the position regarding orders made by my right hon Friend about the maximum number of representatives on committees. There was some confusion about this. The position is that the provisions enable the Secretary of State to prescribe by order the maximum number of representatives on each committee. The Bill does not provide that such orders will be subject to any form of parliamentary procedure, as the order will be an administrative order signed by the Secretary of State.
