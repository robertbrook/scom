Colleges of Education in Scotland
LOCAL GOVERNMENT IN SCOTLAND (STODART REPORT)

10.30 am

Resolved, That the Chairman do report to the House that the Committee have considered the matter of Colleges of Education in Scotland.

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. George Younger)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do report to the House that the Committee have considered the matter of the Recommendations of the Committee of Inquiry into Local Government in Scotland (the Stodart Committee).

I am grateful for the opportunity to enable members of the Committee to give their views on the conclusions that I announced on the Stodart report. My statement on 17 June spelt out the Government's reaction to the report. The committee made 72 recommendations and, as I announced on that day, we have managed to accept no fewer than 60 of them. The committee gave special attention to those areas of local government where there is concurrency of function between regions and districts. Twelve such areas were identified, but when our reactions to the report take effect only three of the 12 will remain and only one concurrent function— that relating to community centres—will be completely untouched.

In order to allow the maximum time for Back Benchers to contribute to the debate, I do not propose to go through all the recommendations. I propose to concentrate on two main topics which are the subject of important parts of the report—industrial development and tourism. My hon. Friend the Under-Secretary of State will be glad to pick up any other matters that may be raised if he catches your eye towards the end of the debate, Mr. Henderson.

I should like at the outset to express again the Government's gratitude to Lord Stodart of Leaston and his committee for the comprehensive and constructive report that they produced within such a tight timetable, and, indeed, for the many helpful comments submitted by. local authorities and other bodies since the publication of the report, which have been immensely helpful to us.

It is our intention that our proposals should be implemented as soon as practicable. That is essential if, as has been frequently stated, the period of uncertainty for local government is to be kept to a minimum. Some of our proposals can be given effect administratively; others will require primary legislation. But it is our aim that the necessary Bill will 2 reach the statute book in time to allow the new arrangements to operate from 1 April 1983.

I turn first to industrial development. It is important to get clear from the start what we do not propose to change the specific powers and responsibilities of local authorities to provide sites, factories, mortgages and other financial support for industrial development within their areas. Those represent very worthwhile functions of which district councils as well as regional councils can and have made good and proper use. Districts have their contribution to make, and we have rejected the Stodart majority recommendation that powers to assist industry should be confined to regional councils. On past experience, we need not fear that any problems will stem from the fact that regions and districts discharge these responsibilities concurrently, although it is clearly right that local authorities in the same area should seek to co-ordinate their activities as far as possible to avoid the possibility of duplicated effort or overprovision of factories, and to ensure provision of supporting infrastructure.

I think it right to pay tribute to the way in which many of the district authorities have used those powers to help launch initiatives designed to generate employment opportunities in their areas. Some local authorities have, with presence of mind and imagination, devised constructive responses to the problems of unemployment, not only individually but in collaboration with each other and, indeed, with other organisations and private sector industry. Good examples of the invaluable support that local authorities can bring are found in North Ayrshire, Clydebank and Leith, to mention only three examples. As a result of the Stodart committee's report, we are now trying to introduce greater order into industrial promotion efforts, to make them more effective, and to get the maximum benefit from the central role of the new Locate in Scotland unit.

Industrial promotion is a very serious business today as the competition for industrial projects— particularly mobile projects from overseas—becomes more intense. We seek to ensure that Scotland as a whole enjoys the maximum advantages of an effective and properly co-ordinated selling effort. The first step has been to respond positively to the report of the Select Committee on Scottish Affairs on inward investment. What we propose now, in the wake of the Stodart report, could be best described as the next logical step to that. We wish to ensure that, in undertaking promotional activity overseas, regional councils will operate in closest consultation with the Locate in Scotland unit and the new town corporations.

I have no wish to become involved in the details of every individual visit abroad, and intend to seek a power to enable me to issue general consents for particular classes of promotional activity. That will reduce the need for authorities having to seek my approval for individual visits. I have it in mind, in accordance with the Stodart committee's suggestion, to issue guidance in a statutory code of practice on the role and interrelation of bodies working in this important area. Regional councils undertaking overseas promotional activities in accordance with such JPEG 3 a code of practice—participating in LIS-sponsored activities—would not, therefore, require my prior specific consent. However, I should want to examine carefully the need for visits abroad which were not covered by the code of practice.

The Government wish to concentrate promotion on the regions, but that does not mean that the districts would have no part whatever in industrial promotion. We foresee that the districts might be invited by LIS and regional councils on appropriate occasions to be an integral part of an overseas selling mission. Under such arrangements, districts would be able to incur expenditure on such missions at the invitation of the organisers. However, we hope that the districts will concentrate mainly on the important and substantial functions of providing the accommodation and the related services which are such a valuable part of local responses to local problems. Their powers form an important part of the armoury for job generation. We see no role for independent district promotional effort outside their areas, but we recognise that there will be a need for districts to advertise the availability of specific factory premises throughout the United Kingdom, in the course of their normal property management activities. The legislation would allow for that.

The legislative changes which the Government have in mind relate first and foremost to industrial promotion. Ultimately, we are aiming at good sense and a balanced approach to industrial promotion, coordinated on the lines that I have explained. Our proposals recognise the valuable work which local authorities have done in industrial development, and the creation of employment opportunities in conjunction with the Scottish Development Agency and central Government. I do not wish to stop regional authorities doing promotional work abroad, or to stifle their initiative, but I shall seek co-ordination. It does no one any good if Scottish regions take no account of what others are doing in undertaking overseas visits. Therefore, my control will not normally stop regions doing overseas promotion but help them by ensuring that they get the greatest benefit from that for themselves and others.

Mr. Robert Hughes
(Aberdeen, North)
: The Secretary of State has spoken about co-operation and co-ordination between regions and districts. However, he will be aware, I am sure, that in the city of Aberdeen—where unemployment has increased by 50 per cent, in the past 12 months—the district council is so dissatisfied with its arrangements with the North East of Scotland Development Authority that recently it set up its own development authority. Is the Secretary of State closing the door entirely to district authorities being involved in promotion work abroad?

Mr. Younger
: I have tried to explain how I feel that district authorities should fit in. I do not believe that such authorities should be precluded from involvement in overseas activities as part of a regional effort or a joint mission.

Mr. Hughes
: Only as part of a joint mission?

4

Mr. Younger
: Only as part of a joint mission. I believe that the appearance of so many individual district representatives overseas merely creates confusion. Certainly, the greatest possible benefit cannot be obtained from such an arrangement.

I shall not require individual regional authorities to submit proposals for approval to me, provided that they adhere to the proposed code of practice, and I reject suggestions that we are unreasonably restricting local discretion. I hope that hon. Members will accept that the proposals are in the best interest of securing maximum investment and employment throughout Scotland. That, I am sure, must be our shared objective.

Tourism is another area where I generally agree with the report's recommendations; in fact only in one respect am I unwilling to implement them. Tourism is of vital importance to the Scottish economy, and we have been steadfast in our policy of maintaining Government support for the industry which provides so much employment. It is important, however, that the industry should be in the proper shape to tackle ever-increasing international competition, and I am confident that the steps we shall take to provide a more effective link between the public sector bodies and the trade will allow the industry to surmount those challenges and move on to a new prosperity.

The Government's views on the committee's recommendation that the Scottish Tourist Board should have independent powers for the promotion overseas of Scotland as a tourist destination have already been made known to the House. We consider that there would be wasteful duplication of effort if the Scottish Tourist Board were to have such powers. Inevitably the English and Wales tourist boards would also seek to promote abroad. We would then have each of the national tourist boards in competition overseas with one another and with the British Tourist Authority, which is of course the body responsible at present for overseas promotion of all of Great Britain. Such a system could never become as cost-effective as the present arrangements. Here again, duplication is the enemy of success.

Mr. Gordon Wilson
(Dundee, East)
: The Scottish tourist industry seems at present to be in a state of collapse. The right hon. Gentleman's comments about moving on to greater prosperity do not exactly ring true in that context.

If the British Tourist Authority is incapable of servicing the Scottish tourist industry and ensuring its growth, is it not desirable that powers should be given to the Scottish Tourist Board to do the work which the British authority is obviously unable to do?

Mr. Younger
: I do not accept either of the hon. Gentleman's premises. I shall proceed to explain how I believe that the system should work.

To refuse to implement the committee's recommendation is not the same as accepting that the status quo is satisfactory. I have discussed the matter at length with my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Trade, and we have taken JPEG 5 the matter to the Scottish Tourist Board and the British Tourist Authority respectively. As a consequence, there is total agreement that a new director of overseas tourism post should be created at the Scottish Tourist Board. The director will have responsibility for liaising with the various tourism interests in Scotland—within an organisational structure which I shall come to shortly—and with the British Tourist Authority, to agree the priorities for inclusion in the Scottish component of the British Tourist Authority's overseas programme. I am confident that that will prove a positive and beneficial development, and I am heartened by the attitude of co-operation with the new approach which is evident on all sides.

I propose to introduce legislation to bring the committee's other recommendations on tourism into effect. Although that will mean that district councils alone will have responsibility for tourism promotion, I believe that it is important for regions to retain powers to contribute to tourism projects. The scope for regional authority interest in infrastructure and other aspects must also be maintained. I am convinced that it is in the industry's best interests for tourism functions to be concentrated at district level and for district councils—with their special grasp of local conditions—to participate directly in an area tourist organisation network covering all areas of Scotland and under the co-ordination and leadership of the Scottish Tourist Board. Such a network operates most successfully in the Highlands and Islands at present, with the encouragement and participation of the Highlands and Islands Development Board, and I see no need for change in those arrangements.

Furthermore, although we are talking about a revision of local authority functions, it will be clear to all that the Government are taking this opportunity to respond to the requests, which interested parties have been making for some time now, to introduce a radical adjustment to the organisation of tourism sponsorship in Scotland. Not only will this new organisation provide greater prospects for successful co-operation amongst all those involved, and a more clearly defined role for the Scottish Tourist Board—whose preparation for this challenge I am pleased to acknowledge—but, of greater importance, it will pass the lion's share of the decision-making powers to the trade itself. This is entirely appropriate, as it is the trade which, in the final analysis, faces the consequences of these decisions.

I believe that under the leadership of the Scottish Tourist Board all the parties concerned will see for themselves the advantages of the area tourist organisation system, and will co-operate together in deciding what organisational systems and collaborative arrangements they think are best suited for their own areas. The network of area tourist organisations will also be brought together nationally under the Scottish Tourist Board. I expect that in some areas the area tourist organisation will involve collective groupings of districts and in others there may be a division into more than one ATO within the same district. This will depend on the tourism characteristics and potential of each area. Among the first questions to be answered are what is the 6 appropriate combination for each area, and how is a consensus on that obtained amongst the various potential partners?

Such a system depends on co-operation and the effective participation of all the parties,and I am sure that this will be forthcoming. We must, however, be conscious of the possibility that a district council may be unwilling to enter a particular organisation or may have its own strongly-held views on the appropriate solution for its area. One way to overcome this problem would be to ask Parliament to provide me with reserve powers to require district councils to collaborate in the formation of particular area tourist organisations and to co-operate in the drawing up of schemes of tourist organisation within that area. I am reluctant to assume that such reserve powers are necessary, but it may be wise to seek them, since a system based entirely on co-operation might run into difficulty if even a very small number of authorities were unwilling to collaborate. I would certainly hope that such reserve powers would never have to be used, as district councils would recognise the value of the new arrangements.

I think it is important that tourism promotion carried out abroad by a district council should conform to the broader strategy and priorities being formulated by the Scottish Tourist Board and the network of area tourist organisations. I intend that the district councils should continue to be able to exercise their tourist promotional powers overseas, but, to avoid conflict or dissipation of effort, they will be able to do so only with my consent. Again in practical terms I foresee no difficulty about this and expect that good sense and a spirit of co-operation will prevail in the interests of all concerned.

The Government's proposals for tourism organisation, therefore, have been developed after careful consideration of Stodart's comments and recommendations, in the light of proposals put forward last year by COSLA, HIDB and STB, and taking into account public comment and criticism of current administrative arrangements. They represent a sensible and practical approach to ensuring that we are able in Scotland to make the most of our tourism potential.

I expect that the implementation of the Stodart report will be a useful tidying-up exercise after the first five years of our new system of local government. After having done this, I hope for the sake of everyone in local government that we can look forward to a period of stability, so that the system can settle down. We undertook in our manifesto to conduct this review. We have duly done so and I now look forward to hearing the views of anyone who wishes to give them, before I carry through as many of these changes as possible with the approval of the House.

Mr. Bruce Millan (Glasgow, Graigton)
: I do not think that anyone here today has found anything that the Secretary of State said very exciting, and I should like to put the debate in a rather wider context. But I shall come to the specific points JPEG 7 arising from the Stodart committee report which the Secretary of State dealt with. Although we are grateful for some of the further details that he gave, we are not sure that he has exactly clarified some of the issues.

It is extraordinary that there has been so little in the way of representations about the Stodart report since its publication. There have been virtually no representations from local authorities, or even individual letters. That is partly because some of the matters with which Stodart dealt are not particularly exciting. It is also because local authorities in Scotland are at present in a state of crisis and are preoccupied with financial problems to an extent that it is almost impossible for them to turn their minds to the problems of the Stodart committee report. I shall say something about that.

I do not believe that questions of finance need necessarily bring about a crisis of relationships between central and local government in the way that has happened between the Government and Scottish local authorities, although local authority finance is an important and difficult subject, and no doubt some of my hon. Friends will have comments to make about it and its reform. The crisis has arisen basically because of the Government's attitude towards local government in their financial dealings with them over the past two years. Also, the way that the Government have dealt with disputes with local authorities has been calculated to bring about maximum prejudice against them when they do the work for which they are responsible.

Before we deal with some of the services mentioned in Stodart, it is worth putting on record that the health of any community depends considerably on the competence of its local authority and the quality of services it provides. It does no good to community relations or to communal peace to refer to the recent riots in England—fortunately, none took place in Scotland—because it creates great prejudice against local authorities which have considerable responsibilities for providing services which help prevent outbreaks of communal disturbance. Whatever view we take of the root causes of these outbreaks, we cannot solve the problems without strong local authorities with adequate finance to deal with social problems. That is especially true of urban areas, but it also applies to rural communities.

Anyone who considers the conditions in our cities especially will see a gradual deterioration in the physical environment. It is not only a problem of the past two years or so; there has been a gradual deterioration of the physical environment. Housing cuts especially will make that considerably worse. In many urban areas, an extremely unhealthy situation is building up, and the Government have contributed to that in many ways by economic policies and by their general attitude towards local government.

The debate has therefore to be set in the general context of difficulties with local government finance and of whether there should be a reform of the 8 local authority financial system. I shall say something about that which will not perhaps make me popular with some of my hon. Friends.

I do not believe that some criticisms of the rating system are justified. There is no perfect system of local government finance, and we would be fooling ourselves if we said that we believed that we could get a perfect system. In my opinion, much more depends on the general relationship between central and local government than on the way in which local authority revenues are raised.

Layfield came out in favour of a local income tax, and I see considerable advantages in that. But, even with that, the rating system will continue to play a major part in local government finance for many years to come. I say that despite the fact that this Government have promised a consultative paper about local government finance in the autumn. I may say now, before that paper is published, that I am extremely sceptical whether it will produce an acceptable solution to the problems of local government finance.

We remember the previous promises of this Government—for example, on the abolition of domestic rates. It is both ironic and tragic to observe what has actually happened on domestic rates. By courtesy of the hon. Member for Renfrewshire, East (Mr. Stewart), some figures were published the other day. They showed that domestic ratepayers in 1978-79 paid £192 million, by 1980-81— only two years later—they were paying £309 million, and in the current year they will pay more than £400 million. That means that the rates will have more than doubled under the present Administration in three years.

We are talking about financial and other crises in local government. Local government will not be able to survive if increases such as I have mentioned continue. Yet there is every sign that such increases will continue, if present Government policies go on putting local authorities in an impossible financial position.

I believe that local government finance must, in any case, be considered in the context of devolution. That is a factor that weighed heavily with the last Labour Government. If that is true of local government finance, it is also true of major reform of the local authority system in Scotland.

I do not believe, for a variety of reasons, that this is a sensible time to talk about major reform of local government structure in Scoland—partly because the system is still setding down after the reform of 1975 and also because, in the kind of financial crisis that local government faces at present, uncertainty about an early major reform of local government would only make matters worse.

The Stodart report dealt at least incidentally with some of the problems of major reform. I shall come shortly to what it said. However, I wish to put on record my own views on what happened about the Wheatley committee report. It seemed to me when it was published that there was too much emphasis on regional planning in the Wheatley report and an attempt to put local government JPEG 9 into a structure that satisfied certain optimistic expectations about the possibility of regional planning. The Committee will remember the talk about the estuarial principle, and so on. That was why Wheatley recommended the system that he did, which is basically the system we have now.

While I do not join those who criticise the inadequacy of local government in any significant way, or say that the two-tier system has been a failure— I do not believe that that is so—I have, nevertheless, much sympathy with some of the criticisms of the present system of local government. If we were to reform it—again, this must be seen in the context of devolution—we should certainly have to see whether we should have a one-tier system instead of a two-tier system of local government.

Quite apart from the problems of concurrent functions that were dealt with by Stodart, there are wider issues arising from the links between the services—housing and social work, for example— which are difficult to solve while we have a two-tier system of local government. While, unfortunately, in some areas, there is disagreement between the regional authority and the district authority, and for that matter a good deal of jealousy, there is also considerable confusion in the public's mind about the respective functions of districts and regions. I have, therefore, a good deal of sympathy with the critics who say that it might have been better if we had ended with a one-tier system rather than a two-tier system of local government.

However, these are wider issues, and it would not be sensible for any Government to tackle them at present. But on the issue of the single tier, I have particular sympathy for the four cities, although not exclusively for them, when one considers the population figures which are set out on page 92 of the Stodart report. One can appreciate why a district council such as Glasgow finds it difficult to understand that, with a population of nearly 800,000, it should have a restricted set of functions, whereas a region such as the Borders, with a population of fewer than 100,000, should deal with the major functions such as education and social work.

It is a perfectly understandable criticism that areas such as Glasgow, Aberdeen, Dundee and so on make about the present system. I had sympathy for that view at the time of the Wheatley report and the 1973 Act, and I still have it, but now to make a change of the nature that, say, Glasgow would want would be a major reform of local government with complications and implications for the system. Whatever the merits of the matter, I do not believe that the problem can be tackled at present.

There are, of course, other problems such as boundaries, and whether Argyll should be part of the Strathclyde region. As a ratepayer in Strathclyde, I should not worry whether Argyll was outside the Strathclyde region because I believe that in proportion to its size it is very well treated by the Strathclyde region at present. However, again these are matters which take us beyond the tidying up operation which Stodart was mostly connected with 10 and for the reasons I expressed I believe that they have to be left for the present.

Therefore, the Stodart report basically becomes a tidying up operation. It is not to be criticised for that reason, and I do not do so. In fact, I believe that on most of the matters it dealt with it came to perfectly sensible and reasonable conclusions. There are one or two exceptions to that view which I shall mention shortly, but where it dealt with matters such as concurrent functions it seemed to me that it came to reasonable conclusions. Interestingly, when it came to decide whether a particular concurrent function should be exercised by the district or the region, in virtually each case it came down in favour of the district. On pages 94 and 95 of the Stodart report there is a list of items in respect of which the decision was that the matter should go to the districts. I believe that on most of those matters there is no difficulty of principle, although there may be some practical difficulties in making the transfers from the regions to the districts.

I turn to three of the matters mentioned by the Secretary of State and, first, to industrial promotion. The Secretary of State made a statement today which we shall obviously want to consider, because I was not satisfied with his original statement in the House.

I went for the minority report of Stodart in regard to industry. With present levels of unemployment, it is impracticable and unreasonable to say that large district authorities such as Glasgow, Dundee or Aberdeen must be excluded from industrial promotion. Frankly, that is silly in the present circumstances and, therefore, there has to be a role for the districts. However, if we agree that there has to be a role for them, it is not possible to make the sort of distinction that I believe the Secretary of State is still making—it was made in the note of reservation and in the main report of Stodart—between, on the one hand, the provision of facilities and, on the other hand, promotion, since the two aspects go together. It is unreasonable to expect a district to be enthusiastic about providing facilities which represent a burden on the rates if it does not at the same time have a considerable say in promoting its own area.

I do not believe that the Secretary of State's remarks this morning about districts perhaps being invited to take part in particular overseas missions and so on can be a solution to this problem. I agree with him about the need for co-ordination. It is not sensible for people to fall over one another in trying to encourage the same kind of investor to locate in different parts of Scotland. But I do not believe that the problem necessarily has to be solved by taking the sorts of legislative powers which the Secretary of State has in mind.

I shall not go into this matter in detail, because I think that the Minister accepts my general point. However, I appeal to him to see whether agreement cannot be reached without taking legislative power and whether, through discussion with COSLA and the others and with the Locate in Scotland unit acting as an information centre about trips overseas JPEG 11 and so on, co-ordination can take place and authorities can be prevented, in their own interest, from unreasonable or unprofitable actions. It is in the interest of a district or region to know whether the dates on which it may wish to make a particular trip are sensible in relation to the activities of other districts and regions and of the agencies of central Government. I believe that this can best be done by informal co-ordination rather than by legislation.

I take the same view about overseas trips. The Secretary of State went a long way this morning to make it clear that he does not want to interfere in ordinary overseas promotional trips. However, whether we like it or not, there is considerable prejudice in the press and elsewhere about overseas trips by councillors, who are constantly accused of junketing. Political capital is made out of that. The Secretary of State is deluding himself if he takes these powers and believes that he can be excluded from that sort of political controversy.

Some of the prejudice that is created against local councillors on these matters is absolutely appalling. I am not in favour of junketing but, equally, I am in favour of local councillors and officials having the opportunity to do sensible and reasonable things for their districts, even if that involves overseas journeying. Members of Parliament go on overseas trips from time to time for perfectly reputable and justifiable reasons. We should try to get away from this prejudice against local councillors which causes deep offence to many conscientious councillors and prevents many councils from effectively carrying out perfectly sensible duties. Therefore, I do not believe that the way in which the Secretary of State intends to deal with this matter is wholly satisfactory.

Mr. Michael Ancram
(Edinburgh, South)
: I am most interested by the hon. Gentleman's remarks. Will he tell us his views on bringing back councillors who are on holiday in places such as Greece and Spain at a cost of £2,000 to the ratepayers of Lothian region in order to vote against the interests of those ratepayers when they get back?

Mr. Millan
: That has nothing to do with overseas trips for industrial promotion. But if the hon. Member for Edinburgh, South (Mr. Ancram) would like an example of a completely unjustified trip at the ratepayers' expense, will he consider the Lord Provost of Edinburgh going over to the United States to present some sort of trophy to a golfer who was to be in Scotland only a few days later? That is the sort of thing that brings local government into disrepute, and it was done by a Tory council and a Tory Lord Provost of Edinburgh.

I turn to leisure and recreation. I do not understand what the Secretary of State's statement in the House really meant and, again, I do not believe that one can have absolute tidiness in this area. For example, to say that leisure and recreation should be a district function does not deal with the problem of the community centres, which are basically owned by the regions at present. It does not do any good to say that it is basically a district function but that 12 we will allow the regions to carry on using their community centres. Either it is a concurrent function or it is not, and it seems to me that the Secretary of State is here producing a continuation, in a slightly different form of concurrent functions. If that is what he is doing, it is probably the only reasonable way in which to deal with this matter.

There is, for example, the use of schools for leisure and recreation functions, and I see that the Stodart committee mentioned the 1967 circular which I know well—I was responsible for it. It encourages education authorities to allow schools to be used for recreation and leisure purposes in the evenings. I dare say that there have been umpteen circulars on the same lines since 1967, and I cannot say that we are as far forward as I should have liked as there is much resistance, but it demonstrates that on leisure and recreation it is no use saying that it should be either a distinct or a regional function. At the end of the day, it will be a function with which both the districts and the regions will be involved.

One of the difficulties of putting the emphasis on the district provision rather than on the regional provision lies in the naive belief that under that system the regions will be willing to contribute towards district expenditure. That belief could soon be exploded, I believe, by the reality of events, because, if one took away the function from either the districts or the regions, I do not think that it would be easy to get them to contribute towards its provision in the future.

As for tourism, I do not believe that what the Secretary of State said is satisfactory even from the point of view of district co-ordination. He seemed to suggest that a new set of quangos would be established and called area tourist organisations. As he explained how they would work, their effectiveness became less and less plausible—at least to me. Again, we shall have to look at the provisions that the Secretary of State disclosed this morning.

As regards overseas promotion, I remain of the view—that taken by the previous Labour Government and provided for in the Scotland Bill—that the Scottish Tourist Board should have a direct role in overseas promotion. That is the view of the board itself and the present chairman, as it was the view of the previous chairman. The British Tourist Authority is too centralist in its attitudes, and the present chairman of the British Tourist Authority is particularly centralist and London-oriented. I do not believe that we can break the present unsatisfactory position unless we give independent powers to the Scottish Tourist Board. That was my view when in Government, and it remains my view. There is no way in which Edinburgh or Glasgow could become all-purpose authority without a fundamental change or, some would argue, a disintegration of the original areas in which they are situated.

Some have suggested that if they could not become all-purpose authorities perhaps they should become most-purpose authorities and that Edinburgh and Glasgow should have greater functions than other district councils.
