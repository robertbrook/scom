Mr. John Maxton
(Glasgow, Cathcart)
: Housing is obviously an extremely important debate, although it may not always appear so to some Conservative hon. Members. About 80 per cent, of my constituency case load concerns public housing problems, such as transfer or repair, as well as problems in the private rented sector. Hon. Members who attend tenants' association meetings—I know that my hon. Friends do—and other meetings of community groups in our constituencies, particularly in housing schemes, know that housing is almost the sole topic of conversation. It arouses enormous passion among constituents, and people feel strongly about what the Government are doing about it.

Secondly, housing is important because, among all the public expenditure programmes in Scotland, housing has been asked to bear the heaviest brunt. Expenditure on housing has been reduced by more than any other public expenditure programme. That means that it hits some of the poorest people hardest.

Thirdly, it is difficult to see what the Government are trying to do on housing. What have they done so far? First, there was the sale of council houses. That was to be the major plank of the Government's housing policy. I do not think that they can claim—although I am sure that the Minister will make some attempt to do so—that it has been a great roaring success. In Glasgow, there are now 2,500 applications to purchase. That may appear to be a large number, but it represents only 1-3 per cent, of the total public housing stock in Glasgow. Therefore, there appears to be no policy, except to cut expenditure for the 98-7 per cent, of tenants left.

There is a second aspect of the Government's policy. To be fair, the Government introduced in the Tenants' Rights, Etc. (Scotland) Act 1980 which gave new rights to tenants in council houses. That was Labour Party policy, although the Minister tries to lay claim to the success of that policy. But what have the Government done? They have given rights to tenants to secure tenancies in houses which will deteriorate because local authorities cannot afford to keep them up. They have also given them the right to improve their properties. Most do not have the money to do so and cannot pay the costs out of their own pockets. Tenants can now apply to the local authority for an improvement grant, but they cannot get one because the 172 local authority does not have any money to give give them, anyway. Therefore, the hopes that might have been raised by that aspect of the Government's policy have been foiled.

The third and most damaging aspect, and the one that appears to be the only clear dominating feature of this Government's policy, is the choice of public sector housing as its major public expenditure target. The figures must be put on the record, so that we know exactly what the Government have done already and what they plan to do in the next three years. First, in 1979-80, the Government spent £765 million; in 1980-81, the figure was reduced to £687 million; in this year, 1981-82, it will be down to £614 million; by 1984, according to the White Paper on public expenditure, it will be down to £460 million. Over the total lifetime of this Government until 1984—it will not continue after 1984—that is a fairly massive reduction of some 40 per cent, in total housing expenditure. Before Conservative Member begin to complain " The last Labour Government did the same: they cut housing ", it is worth noting that over the same number of years—some people would have disagreed then with that Government's policy of cutting housing expenditure—the percentage cut was 11 per cent. Therefore, it is not fair to compare this Government's housing record with that of the last.

That is the Government's housing plan at present, but presumably it is based on the understanding that they will get their economic policy right and that we shall eventually bottom out of the recession and start to climb back up again.

In 1980, fewer council houses were built in Scotland than at any other time since 1922, which was even before the famous Wheatley Housing Act of 1924, except, of course, during the war. Secondly, the Government's policy has meant considerably higher rents for council house tenants. Even higher rents are to be imposed on them by local authorities—certainly through no choice of Labour authorities which would prefer to keep the rents down. They are to be imposed on local authorities by the Government and, in turn, forced on tenants. This is happening at a time when many council house tenants are facing problems of unemployment and deprivation, to say nothing of the new social security legislation which is an incredible burden on them. The Government may say that rents are up to average earnings, but that does not take account of the low wages and unemployment problems in large council house schemes, particularly in areas such as Glasgow. If local authorities do not put up their rents to the level that the Minister considers necessary, he will cut their capital allocation and therefore they will be able to spend even less on the building of council houses.

Thirdly, there is no doubt that the present council house stock is deteriorating. The repairs and improvements that should be taking place in council house stock are not being carried out. As a result, houses built in the 1930s and even some of those built in the 1950s are in need of urgent repair. If local authorities are unable to carry out those repairs, some of those council houses will eventaully have to be demolished, and we shall lose housing stock as a result.

There are problems of dampness, which the Minister has recognised. Given that about 150,000 houses in Scotland require some form of action for dampness, that problem will not be tackled. I believe that £500 million is required to tackle the problem. It is unlikely to be carried out because local authorities will not have the money.

As a result of the massive cut in spending, there is a large increase in unemployment in the building industry. One in four of recognised building workers in Scotland—25 per cent.—are now unemployed. That is not in the public sector since, in many cases, private builders carry out the work. That is from a Government who came into power to help the private sector. All that they are doing is damaging the private sector, particularly in building.

There are still 110,000 subtolerable houses in Scotland. Any hon. Member can see from the standards laid down in an appendix to the Green Paper what is meant by " subtolerable ". Unless there is a fairly massive programme to improve and bring up to standard that property, many of those houses will have to be demolished and will be lost to the housing stock in Scotland.

At present 157,000 people are on the waiting list in Scotland with little hope of getting a house. The 174 Minister will say glibly, as he has been saying for some time now, that that is not a realistic figure as many of these people are on transfer lists and therefore are not genuine applicants on the waiting list. However, many of them live in the 150,000 damp houses and have a very good reason for requesting to move out. Probably two people every two or three weeks come into my surgery with clothing which is green with mildew as a result of being hung in cupboards which are damp, complaining that they have young children with chronic chest complaints because they are living in damp houses. The whole family often has to live and sleep in one room since two rooms out of the three are not fit for habitation because of dampness. To say that the people in those houses who are seeking transfers to better housing are not genuine applicants on the housing list is, of course, nonsense.

I have had case after case of young people genuinely seeking accommodation who, having got married, are having to share houses with their parents and in-laws, with all the stresses that all of us understand can be created in that way. A large number of them are seeking houses.

That figure is perhaps not entirely genuine, but a large percentage of it consists of people who genuinely wish for better housing.
