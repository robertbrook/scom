Mr. Maxton
: The amendments in this long list all relate to the same point—that, in my view and the view of certain organisations, there has not been sufficient consultation about new negotiating machinery to warrant the Government's legislating for the machinery at this time.

Before I speak about the amendments, it is necessary for me—with your leave, Mr. Gourlay—to say something about the background. The new sections stem 746 from the report of the committee of inquiry into the pay of non-university teachers, presented in December 1974 and commonly known as the Houghton report on the pay of teachers.

The report led to many changes. Its main results were a large pay settlement for teachers and changes in the structures of the promotion scales and so on. Its recommendations also included the recommendation that negotiating machineries in Scotland should be reorganised in two respects: first, that the salaries committees and the conditions of service committees should be brought together into one joint negotiating machine, and, secondly, the post-school system should be separated from the school system.

At present there are about five negotiating committees in Scotland: the salaries committee for school teachers and lecturers in further education, the salaries committee for lecturers in colleges of education, the salaries committee for lecturers in central institutions, a conditions of service committee for teachers and FE lecturers, and a conditions of service committee for lecturers in colleges of education. As far as I am aware, there is no conditions of service committee for lecturers in central institutions. Houghton suggested that those should all be brought within two committees —one for teachers and one for lecturers in the three different sectors of further education, colleges of education and centre institutions—and formed into one negotiating machine. The Houghton Committee brought the salary scales for all lecturers into a common grouping.

The report was published in December 1974. During the seven years since then all those committees have continued their negotiations. With the exception of the occasional normal interruption of good industrial relations in the sectors concerned, the committees have not been especially bad at negotiating salaries and conditions of service. There is a question-mark over whether, seven years after the negotiating committees now proposed were recommended, it is still necessary to introduce them.

For almost five years, until I entered the House, I was a staff side member of the salaries and conditions of service committees that negotiated the post-Houghton settlements in the colleges of education sector—that is, two out of a total of five committees. Throughout 1975 we were requested by the Secretary of State's advisers to make written submissions about the proposals for new negotiating machinery. However, throughout my five years on the committees I never attended a meeting with a Minister or his advisers to discuss the new negotiating committee or to consult with anyone about it. Our written submissions were soon forgotten. Every so often we would write a letter asking what had happened to the new negotiating machinery and when it would be introduced. The reply was always that the Minister was still considering the matter.

So far as I am aware, throughout that period— certainly in the colleges of education sector, and, I believe, in the centre institution sector too—the trade union that represented lecturers and participated in the negotiating machinery had no meetings with Ministers or civil servants for consultation about the nature of the new machinery.

Therefore, before we make law this part of the Bill, we should ensure that there is consultation to establish whether there is agreement between employers and registered trade unions. Nearly all the amendments in this group are concerned with consultation. They seek to set up a way in which the two sides can come together with the Secretary of State and agree the negotiating machinery required. That would be proper negotiating machinery. In most other industries—certainly in the private sector—negotiating machineries are set up by trade unions and employers working together, to find what form the negotiating machinery should take, how many people should be involved, which trade unions and employers should be represented, and how they should be represented, and what arbitration procedure there will be. All those things are decided in the early stages by employers and employees in negotiation, and are not imposed from above by a Secretary of State.

The other point running through the amendments concerns the employees' side. It is proposed in section 92(1) that:

"the Secretary of State may, for the purpose of setting up the committee, invite any body which appears to him to be representative of teaching staff".

I have suggested that within the law—this is one of the things that the law has changed since the introduction of the Houghton report—there are recognised bodies which represent employees and that they are registered trade unions. Within the school and post 748 school sectors, recognised registered trade unions— all, I think, affiliated to the STUC—represent the large majority of people in their sectors. They probably have higher percentages of membership than most other trade unions have in their own sectors. Yet the Secretary of State proposes that he will

"invite any body which appears to him to be representative of teaching staff".

In the negotiating machinery that I took part in, 96 per cent, of the people for whom we were negotiating were members of the union which I represented: a registered trade union. But there was one other organisation—the Association of Higher Academic Staff in Colleges of Education—representing assistant principals, deputy principals and principals, a small percentage of the total work force, which was written into the negotiating machinery with negotiating rights.

Normally there are only two votes in a negotiating machinery—a staff-side vote and a management-side vote. That gave almost a power of veto to one representative from this non-representative organisation on the staff side. If he spoke out against the agreement, the chairman was entitled to say that there was no agreement on the staff side, and so he could not accept that staff-side submission. My hon Friend the Member for South Ayrshire (Mr. Foulkes) is frowning, but that was how it operated.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Alexander Fletcher)
: The hon Member for Glasgow, Cathcart (Mr. Maxton) was critical of the consultation. That seemed to be the main theme of his remarks. His main complaint was that there had not been sufficient consultation.

The suggestion that changes should be made in the negotiating committees started with Houghton in 1974. In 1976 extensive discussions took place on that report, and there was a large measure of agreement among the main teacher bodies, which felt that the Houghton proposals would be helpful and should be introduced. I do not wish to make a party point now, but it is clear that the previous Labour Government had the opportunity of introducing such legislation and of taking advantage of those recommendations and the agreement that then existed.

The Bill was published on 10 December 1980, so clause 14 cannot be said to take anyone by surprise. Indeed, our intentions were known many months before that. But it is our intention, subject to parliamentary approval of the Bill, to consult employers, trade unions representing teachers and others who can make a contribution, or who have asked to be consulted before the new negotiating committees are set up initially. I do not, therefore, see any necessity to amend the Bill to secure what will happen anyway.

The term "employers" would not include the Convention of Scottish Local Authorities—I am referring to amendments Nos. 231 and 238—which we wish to consult. The term "registered trade unions"—I believe that the correct term is in fact "listed trade unions"—would exclude at least one association which is at present represented on a negotiating committee, namely the Association of Higher Academic Staff in Colleges of Education in Scotland.

What I am saying is that, without prejudice to the eventual outcome of consultations, I am not at present prepared to say that particular organisations will or will not be represented on the negotiating committees. It is not necessary for me to do so, because the way in which the Bill is drafted gives us the flexibility to undertake wide-ranging consultations. I believe that that deals with the hon Gentleman's main point.

The effect of amendments Nos. 234 and 241 would be that, instead of the Secretary of State prescribing the maximum number of places on negotiating committees for his own representatives, management and staff, the number of persons on the Committee would be agreed in consultation between the Secretary of State, employers and trade unions. We are not anxious that the Secretary of State should be involved in the constitution of the negotiating committees to any greater extent than is strictly necessary, but I see no feasible alternative to the arrangement proposed. Someone must decide how many members there will be, and what the general balance of representation on the committee will be. This will be done after consultation with the affected interests, but at some point there must be a clear decision.

I cannot imagine a better recipe for confusion and delay in setting up the new negotiating machinery than that such a decision must depend on agreement being 750 reached between various interests that are almost bound to have differing views. Some of them will wish to go back to the beginning with a clean sheet and fresh ideas and to throw aside the years of consideration that have been given to these proposals. But we must make progress. I have already said that the previous Labour Government should have made progress—

Mr. Maxton
: Why?

Mr. Fletcher
: It is the opinion of this Government, which is borne out by experience, that the issue of salaries and conditions of service should be considered together. We wish to make progress along those lines. Realistic consideration and discussion of salaries or conditions of service, or both, must take place in the same committee, because one cannot be discussed without the other.

Amendments Nos. 235 and 244 propose that, instead of the Secretary of State determining which bodies should be invited to nominate persons to the new negotiating committees on their first being constituted, different methods should be laid down. The proposed method of constituting the negotiating committees would not be an improvement on what is proposed in the Bill—which is that the Secretary of State should be responsible for the initial constitution of the committees.

The effect of amendments 236 and 245 would be that in either of the proposed new negotiating bodies, after they were initially constituted, the teachers' side could invite only registered trade unions to nominate a person to membership of the committee. The amendments appear to be unduly restrictive on the choice that could be exercised by the teachers' side of the committee. As I have said already, at least one teaching staff association which is at present represented on a Scottish negotiating committee is not a listed trade union. It is not right to exclude the possibility that such a body might in the future be invited to nominate a member.

For these reasons I recommend that the Committee should not accept the amendments.
