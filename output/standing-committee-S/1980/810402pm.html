Mr. Fletcher
: When we adjourned this morning I was discussing amendment No. 132, which would limit the payment of entrance fees for public examinations entered by an assisted pupil to those for examinations "for which, in the opinion of the pupil's parents, he should be presented."

Throughout the discussion we have been comparing the State sector—the public sector, as we more sensibly call it in Scotland—with the private sector—once again, the Scottish term is more sensible. The Government have tried to even out the regulations relating to either sector or both sectors. That is also our view on presentation for examinations.

We believe that in both the public and the private sector that is a matter not for legislation but for school management—for the schools and the parents. In most schools it is normal practice for consultations to take place between the school and the parents about the subjects and grades of examinations for which children are presented. The Bill leaves such matters in those hands, and we believe that they should remain there. T cannot therefore recommend the Committee to accept amendment No. 132.

I have already intervened on the subject of amendment No. 133, during the speech of the hon Member for Kilmarnock (Mr. McKelvey). The amendment would remove the Secretary of State's power to pay assisted places grant on the basis of a notional fee that he considers appropriate if the participating school proposes to charge an excessive fee in respect of an assisted pupil. As I told the hon Member for Kilmarnock this morning, we expect fees charged by schools in the scheme to be acceptable in terms of the assisted places scheme. However, we must make provision for cases in which we think that the fees charged by a school are, for whatever reason, excessive. Subsection (8) enables us, when the Secretary of State believes the fee to be excessive, to establish a notional fee in respect of the parental contribution.
