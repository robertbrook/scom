616 I hope that the fact that the amendment is very long will not greatly depress members of the Committee. Despite its length, it is quite simple in intent. Indeed, those who were not aware of that intent and started to read it and compare the new section 89 contained in it with the existing provision in section 89 and in schedule 5 to the Housing (Scotland) Act 1966 probably wondered when the differences would appear.

The amendment would write into the Bill a new definition of overcrowding. Overcrowding has always been on the statute book, but, in the view of a number of interested organisations, it has long been in need of updating. As I understand it, the original definition which appears in schedule 5 dates back to 1935. Even someone as old and grey as I cannot remember 1935.

It would appear to be something of an academic argument. In many ways the standard of overcrowding laid down in the Act is ignored by many local authorities —not through any fault of their own; I am sure that every local authority would like to eliminate overcrowding. But there is a realisation that the present stringency on local government finance means that there is a long road in front of most urban local authorities before they meet the standards laid down in schedule 5 of the 1966 Act.

I might be asked why I should be artificially raising this matter and putting the elimination of overcrowding beyond reach. The answer is that we ought to have standards which are realistic, but realistic not only in terms of their immediate achievement but in people's expectations of decent, minimum standards for which, as a society, we ought to aim.

I do not think that it would help the Committee if I became involved in arguments about local government finance. But we all ought to assent to the proposition that I have just stated and at least be in favour of making progress towards a reasonable definition of overcrowding. There would be considerable moral purpose in doing so, and it would be an incentive both to central and local government to try to make progress in this thoroughly desirable direction.

There are certain penal provisions on statute which deal with overcrowding. The fine, which is currently £1, will obviously not frighten anyone. I am not thinking in terms of penal sanctions for situations in which the standards are not met. But I think it is important that we set our sights on something that we ought to be striving to achieve. Therefore, the changes embodied in this amendment are thoroughly desirable.

In Schedule 5 and annex, the accommodation is described in terms of rooms in a house. We are now working on bedrooms and this would bring the statutory definition of overcrowding into line with that of most local authorities. I take Glasgow as an example, with which I am most familiar, and I am sure it is no different from most of its major colleagues in the local authority housing field. When defining overcrowding, they leave the living room of the house aside and lay down rules which give bonus points because of overcrowding. For example, they assume that two children under 10 can share one bedroom but if they are of different sexes or over 10 they cannot share a bedroom. An adult over 18 is expected to have his or her own bedroom; and so on. On that basis, in terms of the bedroom accommodation of the house, they calculate what is a reasonable standard to meet. If one is a room short, one gets 30 JPEG 617 points; if one is two rooms short, one gets 30 plus 35 points; and if one is three rooms short, one gets up to 100 extra points when it comes to allocation.

That is a straightforward and sensible recognition of the fact that if one is looking at overcrowding, it is sleeping accommodation which is the easiest and most efficient indicator of what the standard should be. We are suggesting that that system might be followed by statute in laying down the standards to which the 1966 Act refers. That is a straightforward point and I do not want to labour it.

I am indebted to Shelter, which points out that a change is necessary because the existing standard in no way represents current expectations. I give two examples. A family of five—husband, wife, two children of 8 and 6, and a baby of 6 months—living in a two-apartment dwelling, would not be deemed to be overcrowded. A family of six—husband, wife, adult daughter, teenage son, and two children of 9 and 5—living in a three-apartment dwelling would not be deemed to be overcrowded. That situation is clearly unsatisfactory and comes from the arbitrary decision which takes the number of apartments in the house and assumes that they are all sleeping apartments.

I accept that many Scottish families are in the situation where the settee has to become a double bed and the members of the family who are going to occupy it cannot go to bed until the television set has finally gone dead and all members have retired to what space there is in the rest of the house. That is not a living arrangement which any of us would consider ideal and I do not think it is an assumption that should be built into a statutory definition of overcrowding. I believe we are justified in excluding the living-room from the calculations and dealing with the floor area of the bedrooms and with the number of bedrooms in the house.

This change will not revolutionise the Scottish housing scene—its impact will probably be modest for a considerable time—but this is a convenient opportunity to raise our standards. I think the proposal is worth discussion and I hope that the Minister's response will be sympathetic.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Malcolm Rifkind)
: Amendment No. 129 deals with overcrowding and seeks to replace the standard of the number of rooms by a standard of the number of bedrooms. Particular kinds of bedrooms are defined and a specific number of points is allocated, depending on the size of the room.

The complexity of the amendment indicates the difficulty of that approach. I think that before one considered such an amendment, one would have to be convinced that it would lead to substantial practical benefit for the local authorities concerned.

The 1935 Act and the two subsequent Acts, which were consolidation measures concerned with these issues, provide a statutory basis, but there is at the moment nothing that prevents a local authority applying any extra criteria or subjective approaches to such matters that it thinks appropriate.

There is a twofold difficulty in the approach proposed by the hon. Gentleman. First, it makes no allowance for, for example, the considerable number of bed-sitting rooms, where the person concerned may have 618 a one-roomed house which is deliberately designed to be used as both a living room and a bedroom. That is not just a minor point. It involves a whole class of housing to which the approach the hon. Gentleman has suggested would be inappropriate.

The major factor however is that while the number of bedrooms may be a useful indication of the level of overcrowding in particular premises, it is not necessarily the most accurate guide. It seems to me far more sensible to look at the overall accommodation and relate that to the number of people living there. The way in which the tenant of the property chooses to use the rooms will vary from family to family. If we are talking about statutory criteria, it seems to me to be unnecessarily rigid to assume from the outset that particular rooms must be identified as either bedrooms or other rooms when there may be no physical, visible difference between a room which in the past has been used as a bedroom and one used for other purposes.

Shelter is right to draw attention to the fact that the standards were laid down in 1935. There may be an argument for looking at the whole question of overcrowding, and how the provisions have operated, but amendment No. 129 merely takes one aspect of the 1935 situation and seeks to change the statutory basis on which it is provided. That seems a rather artificial attempt at improvement—although I do not say that it was intended to be so. It is an artificial change in designation that would make not the slightest difference to a local authority's capacity to deal with the problems of overcrowding. The hon. Gentleman has rightly pointed out that even with the very narrow definitions of the 1935 Act, local authorities have not sought to enforce these standards, because they have often been incapable of doing so for reasons that are known to the hon. Gentleman.

I cannot see that amendment No. 129 would be any more likely to influence a local authority's behaviour. We should be wary of making cosmetic changes which have no significant effect on the problem of overcrowding. But my main proposition is that it is on balance more sensible to look at the overall accommodation in the property rather than to seek to define in advance whether it has X or Y number of bedrooms, which assumes a rigid approach to the use of the property.

On that basis, while I think Shelter is right to indicate that we could usefully look at the whole provisions, I do not believe that a change such as this would be of any significant benefit to those living in overcrowded accommodation.
