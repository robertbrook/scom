Mr. O'Neill: I am grateful to you, Mr. Gourlay, for allowing us a couple of extra minutes, during which the usual channels doubtless worked their wonders to perform.

When we adjourned I was saying that the amendment was concerned with providing the Minister with information upon which the scheme could operate. It is not my intention that this should necessarily be a delaying amendment, because I am sure that the Minister can carry out the survey fairly quickly. The information that I have provided—and I am sure that other information will come to hand—will enable him to see more clearly the distribution within Scotland and between the sexes, and to consider the question of the means by which the schools propose to accept children for the scheme.

My reading of the situation so far suggests that probably only the Rudolf Steiner school in Edinburgh places no great emphasis on rigorous and rigid methods of assessment. I might return to the subject later, because I did some reading today on the philosophy of Rudolf Steiner and found a word that I had not previously discovered—anthroposophy. I am not sure of its pronunciation, but perhaps a learned Conservative Member will have had an opportunity to check on it.

Anthroposophy requires a school to be fairly liberal, and one of the criteria, probably the only one that is applied to entrants, is the ability to pay. There are no vigorous tests. It sets itself against academic testing. I know that other schools are involved in the scheme whose academic standards are not all that high, but in Edinburgh there are establishments that eater for what can only be called the idiot children of the spendthrift middle class.

I recognise that once again the Minister has not tried to discriminate in any way when dealing with the academic standards of these institutions. Some of those applying for the scheme may wish to send their children to these schools not for academic reasons, but because they want them to attend what they consider to be a posh school. Whether that school will be desirable will remain to be seen. I accept that pupils of limited ability could be accepted by some of the less desirable schools whose academic records are none too high. Under the old criteria the Scottish Education Department was prepared to regard such schools as acceptable for general standards, but they probably do not measure up to some of the local authority schools whose catchment Col. 532 areas do not lend themselves to high academic achievement.

If the Minister is to spend taxpayers' money on a system, it should be prepared and organised far more rigorously than the system that he has presented to us. I accept that a great deal of thought has gone into the regulations—for example, the arrangements by which children will be given food in the middle of the day and the fact that they will not lack school uniforms—but the most important aspects of the whole business have been ignored. I have in mind academic standards, the manner in which a school will assess the ability and acceptability of a child for the school, the geographical distribution, the need to ensure that children all over Scotland have access to the scheme, and the vexed question of sexual discrimination and whether young girls should be entitled to similar opportunities to those for boys.

The Minister has glided over those matters very easily so far, and we feel that paragraph (c), which we seek to add, would be a helpful way in which to make sure that this system, if it has to get off the ground— we do not want it to—does so on a far better basis than it is likely to do at present.

The amendment is almost self-explanatory. To keep within order I had to explain it in a little more detail than I felt necessary at first, but I think that the points have been made. We want a survey to be carried out to ensure that if this wretched scheme has to go ahead it will do so on the basis of an equitable system of assisted places throughout Scotland between all boys and girls and throughout the ability range.
