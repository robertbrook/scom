That would have enabled many parents who live outwith the 25-mile radius to consider, with some equanimity, sending their children to a certain school.

As I said earlier, parents are entitled under the Bill to send their children to these schools, but some of us have grave misgivings about the time it would take for children to travel from their homes to the educational institutions concerned. It is commonly regarded, Mr. Gourlay—and I think that your experience in local Government would bear this out— that there is a maximum time for which children should be expected to travel, partly because of the amount of time that they have to spend at school, partly because of travel circumstances such as the bus services in Scotland, and partly because of the desirability of young people being able to participate in the full range of activities provided by the school.

That is one of the interesting points which jumps out of the pages of books such as "The Schools of England" which is "a book of reference for parents". In lighter print beneath the title it says

"Wales, Scotland and Ireland, with tutors, careers and continental sections".

The book makes clear the range of extra-curricular activities which the schools provide.

It would be unfortunate if any well rounded education should deny children the opportunity to participate in the full range of activities offered. Therefore it is vital, if the Minister is to present the scheme to the country at large, that he does so having made all strenuous efforts to ensure that all the schools in Scotland capable of participating in the scheme have not only been given the opportunity to participate but have been made aware of the amount available in the form of fee remission if they take advantage of the scheme. We should like a survey to cover the question of distance for the equitable distribution of assisted places at all grant-aided and independent schools in Scotland.

Second, we wish to ensure that equal opportunities are available to both sexes. One of the most distasteful aspects of this thoroughly distasteful system is that among many families in Edinburgh, which is the capital not only of Scotland but of fee-paying Scotland, it is considered appropriate to spend the available money on fees for boys but not for girls. I can remember from my own days at school and from my days as a teacher that there were often girls at the schools that I attended or taught in whose brothers went elsewhere because the parents felt it more important that the boys should have an education and that the girls should take second best.

It is essential that strenuous efforts be made to redress that imbalance so that the opportunities may be shared by both sexes. We recognise that there may be fewer places in fee-paying schools in Scotland for girls than there are for boys. Some of us would say that that simply bears out the argument that I have advanced from my experience. But it imposes JPEG 529 upon the Government a responsibility to try to ensure that even more of the schools for girls are included in the scheme and that even more girls are encouraged to participate, if only to redress the imbalance between the sexes.

Having said that, I come to the question of ability range. The Bill is just another example of the Government's Pontius Pilate approach to enforcement of any kind. They have said that they will leave things to local authorities under the Local Government, Planning and Land Act and under the Tenants' Rights, etc. Act, and in this instance they will leave the selection of children to the schools. The Government will give the schools the money to do with as they wish; they can spend it on whatever type of child they wish, regardless of the educational needs of the children. The Government are prepared only to provide the money. It is up to the schools to use it as they see fit.

As we know, a variety of tests are available. The Minister has suggested that the old examinations and tests with stereotyped papers—an English essay plus a paper in arithmetic and a couple of papers in general knowledge—may represent an out-of-date approach. It may have penetrated those bastions of educational conservatism that there are other methods.

There are some schools which deal with children with special educational needs. George Watson's college in Edinburgh has a scheme for the assistance of dyslexic children which has achieved a degree of fame throughout the Scottish education system. It is possible that in some schools a wide range of pupils will be allowed in and that responsibilities in respect of their special educational needs will be met. There are children whose needs are outwith the ambit of the normal education service. Perhaps the hon Member for Aberdeenshire, West (Mr. Fairgrieve), the Minister responsible for health and social work, will be able to advise us on these matters.

There are some schools which cross the ability range but there are not enough of them. We do not know which they are. The Minister freely admitted 530 that he did not know which schools would provide testing and which would not. Earlier this evening, I quoted briefly from "Scholarships at Independent Schools"—priced £2-25, and published by the Truman and Knightley Educational Trust Ltd. That body has gathered together the whole range of schools in the United Kingdom which offer scholarships for fee remission. There are quite a few Scottish schools in the scheme, but not so many as would suggest that all the schools in Scotland will necessarily adopt the system. I have already given examples of some of them, and later in the debate I shall give further examples. But the mere fact that scholarships are available and that they are awarded by an examination system does not suggest that across the ability range places will be available for children to participate in the scheme.

The Minister admits that he does not know the position on the ability range. He says that he thinks that the English legislation is different and that, from the total figures, this availability is more likely in England because of the experience there of the examination system and the children will be assessed prior to participating in the scheme. We are not satisfied with that answer. We think that the Minister was making a manful attempt to answer a point on which he did not have a great deal of information.

Even with the best will in the world, it is most unlikely that any hon Member on the Government Back Benches will be prepared to vote with us to defeat the legislation. I should like to think that some will, but if they do not we are faced with the problem of not knowing for certain to what extent the children will be from the schools in question. The Minister has said that the range would be 25 miles. I have grave doubts about that.
