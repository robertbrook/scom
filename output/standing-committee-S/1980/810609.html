Mr. Rifkind
: I cannot give a categorical assurance that the hon Member for Garscadden (Mr. Dewar) will be improved either by what I say or by anything else that takes place in the Committee, but I can certainly answer his specific questions.

He is correct to say that the Bill in its present form encompasses two categories—the entitled spouse and the spouse who is permitted to occupy a property. The purpose of these provisions is to ensure that the rights of the non-entitled spouse—normally the wife—will be comparable to the legal position of the entitled spouse— normally the husband. The hon Gentleman's amendment would replace the terms used by two specific forms of entitlement, namely, that of the owner or of the lessee. In the vast majority of cases the nature of the spouse's legal rights will be properly described. But these are not exclusive and, therefore, it would be inappropriate to use these words in preference to those in the Bill.

There are five examples of categories which would not come under the definition of owner or lessee. The first is where a life rent existed, when the spouse would not technically be a lessee, although one might think so given the terminology of a life rent. The second is where there had been permission from the trustees of a discretionary family trust or from another person, such as a friend or relation. The third, is where there was a permission from an acquiring authority where the matrimonial home had been compulsorily acquired. The fourth, is where there was a permission from the landlord who had terminated the husband's entitlement to occupy the home under the lease, but who nevertheless allowed him to remain in occupation until the property was disposed of. The last is where there had been a permission from the trustee in sequestration, who by operation of law became vested in the home on the husband's bankruptcy, but who allowed the husband to remain in occupation until the home was put up for sale

Those examples are of fairly unusual circumstances. They will not happen often, but there is no particular reason why a spouse's right will not apply in these cases.

Mr. Rifkind
: I share the relief of the Committee that you, Mr. Henderson, have chosen to group together approximately 100 Government amendments in this category. The grouping is not unreasonable, because the amendments all say exactly the same thing and arise from the massive defeat sustained by the Government in the House of Lords on a matter of considerable drafting importance.

The Bill in its original form referred to the two categories of spouse as, on the one hand a "titled" spouse and, on the other, a "non-titled" spouse. However, the noble Lord Selkirk, in the other place, took great exception to the use of the phraseology on the grounds that, as his Lordship eloquently argued, a "titled" spouse was, by definition, a spouse with a title, and spouses with titles were only those sitting in the upper House.

10.45 am

Therefore, if this phraseology were allowed to remain in the Bill, it would clearly imply that only those who sit in the other place need be the subject of exclusion orders or who batter their wives and do various other kind or unkind acts within the matrimonial relationship. We were not convinced by that approach. The phrase a "titled spouse" and the concept of a title are used in other parts of the law, and they have little to do with membership of the House of Lords.

Nevertheless, the other House, by a large majority, was persuaded to accept the amendment that had been tabled. In view of the strong feelings expressed, we agreed to consider whether an amendment would be appropriate. The most sensible phraseology that we might use seems to be "entitled" and "non-entitled", which will have the same effect and implications and meet the concern expressed by their lordships.

That is the basis on which the amendments have been tabled. They all deal with the question of phraseology. The only amendment in the group to which I should draw the Committee's attention is amendment No. 55, which not only incorporates the terms "entitled" and "non-entitled" but clarifies the intention that it is only the occupancy right related to the specific matrimonial home in respect of which the entitled spouse ceases to be entitled and which will cease to have effect. The amendment deals with the duration of orders under the Bill. I thought it might be useful to explain it.

We have tabled this massive list of amendments because we do not wish to face the danger of a constitutional conflict with the upper House. Although we do not maintain that this wording is superior to the original wording, nevertheless, it has the same effect and will meet the point that their lordship felt so strongly about.
