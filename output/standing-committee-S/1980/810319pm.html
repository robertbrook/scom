Mr. William Hamilton (Fife, Central): I beg to move amendment No. 47, in page 3, line 26, at end insert

"or in any other schools or educational establishments within their area;".

The alleged principle on which the Bill is based is the extension of parental choice with the maximum degree of information being made available to parents by education authorities. The Government have laid great stress on the need for open government, the maximising of information available to parents and the mandatory provision of that by local authorities.

My amendment takes the Government at their word. It says, in effect, "All right, if that is the real principle underlying the Bill, let us extend it to the private sector. There is no reason why that sound principle should not be comprehensive in its application." We are talking about a minority of parents who will be deprived with regard to education and none of us want that.

When one reads the draft regulations—I do not know what stage they have reached; I suppose that they simply are an example of what might happen—one finds that local authorities will have a statutory requirement to provide information about all sorts of matters, whether they like it or not. Minimum standards will be laid down and the authority will be free only to go beyond those minima, rather than the reverse. I do not see why parents who choose to send their children to fee-paying schools should not have access, by law, to the same information as will toe provided by local education authorities for the public sector.

Let us consider some examples from the general guidelines with regard to priorities for admission. Why should not parents who are thinking of sending Col. 264 their children to fee-paying private schools toe given those general guidelines about how other children have access to them—apart, that is, from their parents' ability to pay the fees? There must be some other qualifications for admission, and the parents should have a right to know what they are.

There is also reference to the policy on school transport, and boarding accommodation. Some of the schools are 'boarding schools. The parents should have a -right to know exactly how and to whom the boarding facilities are allocated.

At the bottom of page 5 of the draft regulations, it is stated that the basic information should include

"a contact point for parents who think their child may have special educational needs".

It may well be that two of those wealthier parents have a backward child. They may want to protect the child from the buffetings they think he would have to face in the public sector. In order to protect the child from that buffeting, they may want to send him to a private school. They should have the right to know, before they commit their cash, what special provisions are made in that school.

It is no good the Minister's saying—as I suspect he may—that if the parents are paying fees, they will make sure that all the necessary provisions are there before they commit themselves. In other words, there will be a monetary discipline on parents and they will therefore make sure that they are given such information. But there is no mandatory provision. Moreover, a school can mislead parents and the parents have no redress, apart from taking their child away when they find that they have been diddled by the proprietors of that school.

I turn to page 7 of the draft regulations, where they state that the parents should have access to:

"the authority's general policy with regard to the school curriculum and examinations".

Why should not the parent who is paying fees have a legal right to access to information of that nature? The next provision on page 7 is about

"Gaelic-speaking areas within the area of the authority"#x2014;

meaning the area of the local education authority. Parents sending their children to private fee-paying schools should have similar access. They ought to know, before they commit their cash, whether the school teaches Gaelic and to what standard. They have a right to know about

"the availability of tuition in Gaelic"

in that school.

I turn to sub-paragraph (if) of the regulations. What arrangements are made by the proprietors of fee-paying schools for

"the education of children with special aptitudes, eg in music",

or in art, perhaps, or sculpture? Parents who are committing their own money should have a mandatory right to access to such information.

Sub-paragraph (g) refers to the "policy with regard to the integration of handicapped pupils". That brings me back to what I said a few moments ago. The parents of a handicapped child are particularly concerned to protect that child as far as they can. If they are to have special treatment in the (public sector—that is something we will discuss later—they should have similar treatment in the Image Col. 265 private sector too. The private schools should be obliged to provide ail the information that, in the public sector, the Government are obliging the local education authorities to provide.

The authority would be obliged to provide information about its

"general policy with regard to the provision of school meals, including free school meals".

Parents with children at private schools might be making such great sacrifices to pay the fees that their children might qualify for free school meals. I do not know whether there is such a case in a fee-paying school of a child having access to free school meals. But at least some knowledge ought to be available about the provision made for sustenance in private schools, as well as in local authority schools.

The same applies to the general policy on school uniform and the provision of financial assistance towards such uniforms, sports and other clothing. 1 cite the case off Gordonstoun. Certain children of a very high social status go to Gordonstoun and they might need help with the provision of their uniforms. The parents, however low or high, should have the right to know whether their children can have free school uniform, £10 towards it, or whatever.

Some of the children who go to Gordonstoun are deprived in many ways. Their parents ought to have access to this information. There may be people in very high places following our proceedings carefully in that regard. They have additional heavy expenditure coming soon, although I expect that we shall pay most of the bills. It is important that they should have access to as many sources of public funds as possible. They are expert in knowing what access they have to all kinds of public funds. They should also have the right to know the general policy on bursaries or maintenance allowances. Parents with children at private schools often make heavy sacrifices to pay the fees. They need all the help they can get.

There is also all the information about individual schools provided for on page 8 of the draft regulations—names, addresses and telephone numbers; everything except fingerprints. There is also mention of a statement of a school's educational aims. I have heard from many lads who have gone to private single-sex schools in England of the great sexual and moral dangers. There may be a need to provide statistical information about the homosexual tendencies of a particular single-sex school. That would be very important information for parents. We know that it goes on at Eton. We see some of the products sometimes in the House. I suppose that the matter is covered by extra-curricular or extramural activities, but it cannot come into the category of correspondence courses.

Parents who send their children to private fee-paying schools will have no statutory right to the information that will be available to parents who send their children into the public sector. That is class distinction in reverse. We are to punish the parents who send their children to fee-paying schools. That is appalling. It is persecution of a minority in Scotland which we ought not to perpetrate. If we Col. 266 want to protect minorities, we should protect, as far as we can, the educationally privileged minority. We should treat those parents in exactly the same way as we treat parents of children in the public sector.

That is the full purpose of the amendment. I do not see how the Minister can oppose it. If the principle of parental choice, based on maximum information to be provided by the authority responsible for the schools, is valid in one sector of education, it is equally valid in the other.

I look forward to his contribution to the debate as he is about the only one on the Opposition side who can speak from personal experience in these matters. I cannot. I say that quite freely and frankly, because I was brought up, educated in and taught in the public sector all the time, and so I know about the private sector just from hearsay. I see some of the results from it on the other side of the Committee, and I am not very impressed by what I see on occasions.
