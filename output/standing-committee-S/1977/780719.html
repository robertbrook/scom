DRAFT ROADS AND ROAD TRAFFIC (NORTHERN IRELAND ORDER 1978

10.30 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for Northern Ireland (Mr. Ray Carter)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft Roads and Road Traffic (Northern Ireland) Order 1978.

In a speech to the delegates attending the 1977 Northern Ireland Road Safety Congress I referred to the level of road casualties in Northern Ireland as bordering on the obscene. I believe that to be a totally apt description of the scale of the problem. Statistics show that road deaths in Northern Ireland per 10,000 vehicles registered are double that of Great Britain and that in every 1,000 road accidents, there are 60 fatalities as compared with 25 in Great Britain.

It was my concern about these figures and the growing social problem they presented that prompted me in November 1976 to invite a small group of local people with a proven interest in road safety to examine the problem as a road accident study group and to make appropriate recommendations. I believe that the wide publicity which the group's report received focused public attention on the problem. One of its principal recommendations, a general increase in penalties for traffic law offences, is embodied in this draft order. Others are being acted on through administrative action within the Department of the Environment for Northern Ireland or are being more closely studied. This is notably the case on the issue of drink and driving where the report expressed concern at the adequacy of existing legislation particularly in the light of the recommendation of the Blennerhassett Committee on the law and practice in Great Britain on this subject. I am all too aware of the high level of road casualties brought about by the drinking driver especially young drivers in Northern Ireland. Based on 1976 statistics, 4 30 per cent. of all fatal accidents had drink as a principal factor. But the complexities of the law on the subject, bound up as they must be with police and court procedures, have persuaded me to postpone legislative action until I have studied the conclusions of a major review of the Province's existing law and practice on the subject. This review is currently under way.

The main purpose of the draft order is to bring the law on road traffic and safety in Northern Ireland more closely into line with that in Great Britain. A substantial part of the draft order is concerned with road safety. That I believe is timely because, as the security situation in Northern Ireland improves, I sense a growing public concern at the scale of road casualties in the Province. It is, therefore, appropriate that this Committe should take the lead in tackling the problem by strengthening the relevant legislation. I believe that this draft order contains a variety of quite significant improvements to road traffic and safety law which will assist the vital on-going programme to combat road casualties.

I turn now to the detailed provisions of the draft order. Article 3 seeks to remove the hazard which can be caused to other road users by placing builders' skips on public roads. I should perhaps explain that the term "builder's skip", by definition in article 5, covers all skips, whether they are used by builders or by householders for materials or for rubbish. Hon. Members will no doubt have noticed that the draft order published last December for public consultation would have required every skip deposited on a public road to have the permission of the Department of the Environment, but that in the order now before the Committee there is no requirement for such permission. The change has been made as the result of representations made during the consultation period by the Northern Ireland Federation of Building and Civil Engineering Contractors to the effect that the objective of road safety could be achieved without the delay, inconvenience and expense of having to seek the Department's consent to the placing of each skip. The Federation contended, and I have accepted, that the same measure of safety for other road users could be achieved by JPEG 5 requirements that each skip should be carefully sited, properly marked and guarded, and should be lighted at night, together with power for the Department and the police to have skips removed. These general requirements parallel those in section 31 of the Highways Act 1971. The full detail of the requirements will be set out in regulations.

Briefly, then, the onus will remain on the owner of a skip to ensure that he does not deposit it in an obstructive or dangerous place, and that he complies with the requirements to ensure that it is immediately visible. Otherwise he would be committing an offence. Article 4 supplements these provisions by giving both the Department and the police the powers to remove or require the removal of any skip and if need be to recover any costs incurred.

Article 6 provides a revised framework for the issue of driving licences. It is proposed that the licence should be valid for 10 years instead of the present three years and that persons over 70 years of age should be required to renew their licences at three yearly intervals. I should make it clear that there is no question of requiring persons reaching 70 years to take a further driving test, or necessarily to undergo medical examination. The proposals affecting that age group are in line with the requirements now operating in Great Britain. The article grants the Department discretion in relation to the issue of licences to persons suffering from disabilities or diseases and contains additional requirements for information concerning the physical fitness of drivers. No decision has yet been taken on the cost of the driving licence but some increase beyond the present £1 is inevitable.

Hon. Members will readily appreciate that the 10-year period of validity proposed for Northern Ireland driving licences falls short of the period of validity in Great Britain. The principal reason for this is that the particular circumstances in Northern Ireland dictate that driving licences there should carry a photograph of the holder and the 10-year period is seen as the most practical compromise between facial recognition, durability of licence material and the Great Britain " until 70 " period.

Article 7 extends the restrictions on 6 the sale or offer for sale of defective vehicles to the component parts of the vehicle. This is designed to close a loophole in existing law and is clearly of very real practicality where such components as tyres or brake linings are concerned.

Article 8 is concerned with the minimum ages and training of young drivers of heavy goods vehicles. At present the minimum age is 21 years but the article proposes that persons between 18 and 21 years will be able to drive such vehicles subject to meeting specified conditions as to training and employment. This age reduction has two purposes. The first is that young persons should not be deterred from joining the road transport industry by having to wait unduly long. The second is that new recruits to the industry should receive the best possible training. It is my intention to ensure that regulations made under this article offer a genuine apprenticeship scheme, not a cheap form of labour for employers in the industry.

Article 9 mirrors the main provisions of the Road Traffic (Foreign Vehicles) Act 1972 and deals with the enforcement of road traffic and transport law against foreign goods and passenger vehicles and their drivers. In speaking of "foreign vehicles" I am referring to vehicles registered outside the United Kingdom. Many thousands of these vehicles enter Northern Ireland each year, mainly from the Republic of Ireland, but as EEC commitments increase, from other countries as well. These vehicles are required to observe our traffic laws but not all do so. Some are grossly overloaded. some are mechanically unsound and others may not carry the requisite documents. Yet, as matters stand, the law cannot be effectively enforced against them since the offending foreign vehicle or driver will normally have left the Province before a summons can be served on him.

Equally the foreign owner or driver is usually a person resident abroad and hence beyond the jurisdiction of the JPEG 7 Northern Ireland courts. Effective sanctions are, therefore, minimal. Accordingly article 9 seeks to apply domestic enforcement procedures to foreign vehicles and their drivers but with the additional powers to meet the peculiar circumstances of the foreign driver or operator. As with domestic vehicles, power is sought to impose a prohibition on driving defective foreign vehicles until any defects are made good. Additional powers are sought to detain the vehicle and if need be to arrest the driver without warrant. The European Economic Commission has been consulted and has offered no objections.

Article 10 updates the requirements of the law on the issue of deposits and securities as alternatives to motor insurance policies. The present monetary levels have remained unchanged since 1920 and clearly have no relevance to today's money values.

Article 11 removes the concept of dangerous driving from road traffic law and replaces the existing offences of dangerous driving and causing death or injury by dangerous driving with the new offences of reckless driving and causing death or injury by reckless driving. The offence of careless driving remains unaltered. The proposals in this article adopt the provisions of section 50 of the Criminal Law Act 1977. The concept of dangerous driving has been criticised because of its all-embracing nature ranging from deliberately hazarding other road users to relatively minor faults which can scarcely be distinguished from careless driving. This difficulty does not arise with the concept of reckless driving since the court's interpretation of the word "reckless" precludes this.

Article 12 arises as a result of a decision in the courts. The police have power to give directions to traffic in a wide range of circumstances but not, in the opinion of the court, to directing traffic for surveys and censuses. The article enables them to do this. Answering questions at such surveys, however, remains absolutely voluntary.

Article 13 read with schedule 1 varies the penalties for road traffic offences and largely brings these into line with those in Great Britain. The effect is to increase the maximum fines on summary conviction for most offences under the Road Traffic Act (Northern Ireland) 1970 and 8 to remove the option of imprisonment for all but the most serious offences. It is worthy of comment that the power to imprison for summary offences under the Road Traffic Acts in Great Britain is now restricted to the single offence of driving whilst disqualified. In Northern Ireland both the police and resident magistrates feel that the power to imprison should be retained for a wider range of offences and the article recognises their argument. Accordingly, it is proposed to retain imprisonment as an option for the summary offences of drinking and driving, driving whilst disqualified, failing to stop after an accident, reckless driving, forgery of documents and taking a vehicle without the owner's consent.

Article 14 together with schedule 2 deals with a very wide range of miscellaneous amendments. The proposal to increase the fixed penalty, applicable mainly for parking offences, from £2 to £6 is both necessary and overdue.

Article 15 clarifies the Department's powers to issue driving licences while article 16 and schedule 3 deal with repeals.

I appreciate that I have presented a very wide and varied set of proposals but I am sure that hon. Members will appreciate that this is in the very nature of the subject we are dealing with. There is no single solution to our road safety ills. The problem can only be tackled on a series of fronts each of which we hope, will make some positive contribution. That is the purpose of this draft order which I commend to the Committee.
