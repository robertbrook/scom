Miss Margaret Herbison: There are today many problems facing all who are interested in education, but I think everyone would agree that two of the greatest problems—indeed, problems from which some of the other problems arise—are the shortage of teachers and the inadequacy of school building. I propose this morning to devote the greater part of my short speech to the first problem, the shortage of teachers, and if tomorrow I am fortunate enough to catch Mr. Speaker's eye, I hope to deal with the problem of the inadequacy of school buildings.

Last year I dealt with the second problem in this Committee and I am afraid the Joint Under-Secretary of State at that time did not appreciate the real seriousness of the problem of the inadequacy of school building. I should like to congratulate the Department on the very full part they have devoted to technical education in Scotland, pointing out the good aspects and also the weaknesses that must be rectified.

Command Paper 8721 deals with the supply of teachers and the main conclusions are repeated in Command Paper 8813, published by the Scottish Education Department. We find quite a number of Col. 2652 points on the supply of teachers which must be causing the Ministers, the Department and all interested in education, serious concern. We learn that 34,000 certificated teachers are in Scotland at present and that by 1957 that number is expected to have risen to 36,000. We also learn that in October, 1951—there is not any later date given even in the Command Paper—there was a shortage of 2,300 teachers and that this shortage, by 1957, will have increased by another 1,000 to 3,300. In 1957 there will be not only a grave shortage in science and mathematic teachers, but also a shortage of primary teachers.

We are told that the normal recruitment and the output from the emergency scheme, plus the output from the special recruitment scheme, between now and 1957 will more than overcome the normal wastage in the teaching profession. But, after 1957—when we can expect very few, if any, from the emergency scheme and very likely a decrease in the number from the special recruitment scheme—unless normal recruitment increases, we shall not be attracting to this profession even sufficient people to overcome normal wastage. The conclusions show very clearly that the shortage of teachers at present is serious and is likely to become most serious. Having been for a short time at the Scottish Office, I am only too well aware, as I am sure the Ministers and the Department are aware, that this problem is not easy of solution. But, so far as I am aware, the present Government have made only one suggestion for increasing the number of teachers and that seems a most retrograde suggestion. They have published a draft Statutory Instrument and with it they have sent to all those concerned—local education authorities and so on—circular No. 269.

In that circular and in the draft Statutory Instrument it is suggested that for those girls and young women entering training colleges the qualifications will be lower. The qualifications at present, I insist, are not high for that type of student. At present they must sit five subjects in the examination—two on the higher standard and three on the lower standard. Of the two of higher standard one must be English.

Another point we have to remember is that we do not ask those girls to take those five subjects in one sitting. If they collect those five subjects in two sittings at Col. 2654 present they have the requisite qualification to enter a training college. The new proposal is that, instead of having a pass in those five subjects, they may be allowed to enter with a pass in four subjects. Those passes in four subjects may be three lowers and one higher, and the one higher need not be English. That seems a very serious form of dilution, particularly when we remember that the girls are allowed two sittings to collect those passes in five subjects.

It is suggested in the circular that only those who have just failed to pass in the fifth subject will be considered. Their mark will possibly be given the committee, who will give them a personal interview and the decision will be made on the personal interview. But at present the Department very carefully examine every marginal case. If a pupil has failed to receive the requisite pass now in any subject, when the oral examination takes place in the school Her Majesty's inspector pays particular attention to that girl in that subject.

Not only that, but the inspector takes account of the estimate mark that the school has given the girl in that subject and, in addition, the inspector may ask to see the previous written examination papers and the written work of that girl. After going through all that careful procedure, the Department decides that the girl is not fit to pass in that subject, but that is the girl who is to be given a chance to enter a training college. It seems completely wrong that this suggestion should be made by the Department.

It is true that a girl would not be teaching primary children anything like the standard of the subjects in which she is expected to pass in the higher leaving certification examination, but the fact that after two sittings and after careful scrutiny by the Department she could not obtain a pass in English seems to make her not a fit person, academically, to have the important charge of educating our children in primary schools.
