10.32 a.m.

Motion made, and Question proposed,

"That if the proceedings on the University of St. Andrews Bill [Lords] are not completed at this day's Sitting, the Committee do meet on Wednesday, 22nd April, at Half-past Ten o'Clock."—[Mr. J. Stuart.]

Mr. A. Woodburn: We are anxious to co-operate in this matter. Occasionally unforeseen speeches occur during Scottish Grand Committee proceedings, and it may not always be possible to plan methodically and exactly so as to finish at the desired time. But my hon. Friends will exercise a self-denying ordinance. We realise that the controversial points of this Bill may be discussed extensively on Committee stage.

Question put, and agreed to.

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. James Stuart): I beg to move,

"That the Chairman do now report to the House that the University of St. Andrews Bill [Lords] has been considered in relation to the principle of the Bill."

I do not wish to take up too much time, because I think it is in the general interest that we should complete this part of our proceedings today. As the right hon. Gentleman the Member for Clackmannan and East Stirlingshire (Mr. Woodburn) has said, some of the points which may give rise to difference of opinion will be suitable matters for discussion in Committee.

I am sure that hon. Members representing Scottish constituencies will regret the introduction of legislation dealing with the independent universities, but there is no doubt about the necessity for it. I am glad to think, however, that we all have the best interests of university education in Scotland at heart, and I hope that on the general principles there will be no party division.

I do not wish to trouble the Committee with a detailed account of the difficulties which have spread over a number of Col. 2320 years. Anyone who wishes to study them will find them very carefully set out, both in the Report of Lord Cooper and in the report of the Royal Commission. I must, however, deal briefly with certain points.

It is as long ago as 1889 that Parliament had to legislate on matters concerning the organisation of the Scottish universities. No Government would willingly embark on such a matter without very weighty reasons. But the controversy has reached such a pitch that it is now endangering the future of St. Andrews University. All previous attempts to resolve these difficulties by negotiation have broken down. The Government feel that if harmony is to be restored certain defects in the university's constitution which are at the root of the trouble must be removed by legislative action.

The trouble originated some 60 years ago when the old colleges in St. Andrews were united with the new University College in Dundee. This union was never a happy one. From the passing of the Universities (Scotland) Act of 1889 up to the promulgation of the new constitution in 1897 there was wrangling and acrimonious discussion about the arrangements to be made for the government of the university.

These years of dispute ended with a compromise arrangement which did not go to the root of the trouble. The mistake made at that time, in my opinion, was the failure to establish a single unassailable authority in control of the whole university. The college in Dundee was allowed to retain partial autonomy. Another initial error was the failure to draw a clear distinction between the organs of the university and those of the United College. For example, the principal of the United College was made ex officio principal of the university.

This rather undecided and muddled distribution of power opened the way for the disharmony which followed. I do not wish to apportion blame to either side. What we desire is an amicable and satisfactory settlement. The Dundee element wish to secure added prestige for the City by the establishment of its own university. They also wish to secure the cultural and technical benefits which they think can be attained only by an independent university in Dundee. The administrators Image Col. 2321 in St. Andrews on the other hand were actuated by a natural adherence to tradition and a desire to preserve the distinctive character of the university. Whatever the motives, the resulting disputes threaten grave harm to the university. Therefore, they should be resolved.

When Secretary of State for Scotland in the previous Government, the right hon. Gentleman the Member for Clackmannan invited Lord Cooper, assisted by two assessors, to inquire into the organisation of university education in Dundee and its relationship with St. Andrews University. Hon. Members will have a copy of that Report. As anyone who knows Lord Cooper would expect, it presents a very clear and able statement of the position. The advice is well reasoned, but, unfortunately, it was not acceptable to the disputing parties and therefore no solution was reached in that way. We owe Lord Cooper a debt of gratitude for the able Report which was produced.

The only alternative was to have an authoritative examination to decide on the best course to adopt. Therefore, in 1951 a Royal Commission was appointed under the chairmanship of Lord Tedder. The terms of reference of that Commission were,

"To inquire into the organisation of University education in Dundee and its relationship with St. Andrews University. and to recommend what changes, if any, should be made in the constitution, functions and powers of the University of St. Andrews, of University College, Dundee, or of any other body or institution concerned."

I wish to pay a tribute to Lord Tedder and the distinguished members of his Commission for the energy with which they applied themselves to their task. They lost no time in dealing with the problem, and they have propounded a solution which will be found in the Commission's Report.

I think it fair to say that the Report had a good reception. It was supported by the university court, the university senate, the council of University College, Dundee, and by the United College of St. Andrews. I admit that it was not supported by the governors of University College, Dundee, or by the Dundee Corporation.

Mr. John Rankin: Or the university court?

Col. 2322

Mr. Stuart: I think I am correct in saying that they supported it.

After full consideration the Government came to the conclusion that the recommendations of the Commission offered the best hope for the future of university education in both St. Andrews and Dundee. This Bill has been drafted to implement that conclusion. One change which the Bill does not make is that of setting up a separate university in Dundee. The Commission are quite clear on that point. I will quote from their Report:

"We cannot recommend that separation is needed, still less that it is desirable… Its advocates have failed to prove that the gathering ground is great enough to provide two universities with an adequate intake of students to ensure their growth and vitality… We have decided without hesitation that Lord Cooper was right in rejecting the extreme solution, not because we have failed to appreciate the motives of its advocates or doubted the sincerity of their determination to make it practicable, but because we have not been convinced by their arguments."

The difficulties are that there is not room or population to justify the maintenance of separate universities; there are not sufficient students in the area from which to draw, and there is no justification for financing two separate universities. In addition, there are other difficulties, with which I will not deal in any detail, such as facilities for the teaching of medicine and science in two universities.

10.45 a.m.

If the university is reorganised, as the Bill proposes, I suggest that Dundee should benefit because university education will be so developed that without any new financial burdens the city will enjoy a vigorous and developed academic life within its boundaries. The accepted solution embodied in the Bill provides not for the division of the existing university into two independent parts north and south of the Tay, but for its unification and integration. With this will be coupled a measure of devolution in administration, so that the university can draw upon both the civic spirit in Dundee and the old traditions of St. Andrews. That seems to us to be the right line in the interests of both.

The university court will be reconstituted to deal impartially with the several parts of the university, and in future the court should no longer be criticised on Image Col. 2323 the ground that it is mainly concerned with the colleges in St. Andrews. The present governing bodies of University College, Dundee, will be dissolvd. In thir place the Bill will establish a single unincorporated college in Dundee which will incldude the advanced medical and dental schools.

The court will thus be the final authority in the university and there will be two new College Councils, one for the new Dundee college and one for the two St. Andrews colleges. The powers and duties of these councils are laid down in general terms in the Bill.

One feature of the reconstitution of the court which has attracted much attention, and to which, no doubt, there will be a good deal of reference before this Bill completes all its stages, is the proposal to replace the rector by the principal in the Chair. I must remind the Committee that under the Bill the principal ceases to be the principal of the United College and is to be appointed by the Crown.

The Royal Commission recommended this change and gave very strong reasons for advocating it. To quote just one short extract from the Commission's Report, they recommended that the principal should occupy the chair of the university court:

"because the special conditions of St. Andrews make it essential. It is impossible to overestimate the importance of the Chairmanship of the Court both during the period of reconstruction of the University and later, and, if the position is given to a non-resident officer who is elected triennially, stable government and continuity of policy may be gravely endangered."

I hope that the Committee, as I am sure they will, will give very careful thought to these words.

The Bill is drafted to follow out the recommendations of the Royal Commission, and, as the Committee will be aware from proceedings in another place, it is the Government's intention to adhere to the recommendations of the Commission for the reasons given. I will not go further into that at this stage except to say that they will very carefully consider this point before coming to any other decision. It must be borne in mind that the new principal, whoever he may be, will have a very onerous and important task to shoulder when he takes on the re- Col. 2324 organisation of the university, if and when this Bill becomes law.

During the initial period of reorganisation there will be up to six Commissioners appointed by the Crown to undertake the detailed organisation of the university. In the main, these acts of reorganisation will be carried out by ordinance necessitating, in the first place, consultation with the university court, the senatus and the general council. Objections will be considered and ordinances must be laid before Parliament. Therefore, full Parliamentary control is assured.

As I said, I do not want to be responsible for delaying the operations of this Committee today. The Committee are aware that owing to reasons outside our control there was some alteration in our business which made it impossible for us to sit yesterday. It would be of great assistance to the Government if we could get through the consideration of the principle of the Bill before Easter and thus be able, after a proper interval, to proceed to the Committee stage after Easter. At the same time, I do not, of course, wish to press that point unduly. We must see how we get along.

I do not pretend that the passage of this Bill will at once put an end to all disharmony. That, I am afraid, would be asking too much, but I do believe that the Bill will remove the causes of the disharmony which has existed for so many years. I trust that in the end it will prove to be in the best interests of our oldest university whose well-being, I know, all members of this Committee have so strongly at heart.

Miss Margaret Herbison: I am certain that all Scottish Members of Parliament and, indeed, the majority of people who have the best interests of Scottish education at heart will welcome this Bill, even though some of us may have certain reservations on one or other of the provisions contained in it. I am certain that during this Second Reading and on the Committee stage these reservations will be brought out very clearly indeed.

It is true, as the Secretary of State has said, that although there was legislation in 1897 it united the University of St. Andrews and the College of Dundee in name only, and that since that date, over 50 years ago, there has been nothing else Image Col. 2325 but dissension and bickering between those two bodies. I hope that when this Bill reaches the Statute Book it will go a long way towards bringing an end to that dissension because where there is dissension in such matters as this it is most detrimental to the best interests of education.

I, like the Secretary of State, also wish to pay my tribute to Lord Cooper for the immense and good work which he did. I am very sorry indeed that his findings and recommendations were not accepted. Had they been, it might have meant that by now we should have been on the way to the harmony which we all desire. I also wish to pay a high tribute to Lord Tedder and to the members of his Commission. In eleven months they produced a Report on a problem which to many of us appeared almost insoluble. I do not know of any Royal Commission which has ever worked so hard or so successfully and which has produced a Report so quickly.

Having examined the whole Report, it appears to me that the recommendations contained in it are capable of bringing about harmony and doing away with the disunity which has existed for so long. We find that in the recommendations the possibility of a separate university for Dundee is turned down. I agree with that recommendation for the reasons given in the Report and particularly because of the student population. I cannot see how the student population in that area could possibly uphold two universities, one in St. Andrews and one in Dundee.

I do not agree with one of the reasons given by the Secretary of State, that St. Andrews was anxious to preserve its traditions. It is, perhaps, a good thing to preserve traditions but not to preserve at the cost of the prestige of another city. Therefore, I do not accept that reason, although I do accept the reason that the student population makes it impossible to uphold two universities. I understand and sympathise with those who feel disappointed at this decision. It is a good and admirable thing that a city should have civic pride and should desire to uphold its prestige, but I think a part of the trouble has been caused by the attitude among the people in Dundee.

Although I sympathise with their desire and realise that a city would be doing less than its duty if it did not try Col. 2326 to uphold its prestige and maintain its civic pride and dignity, I am still convinced that a separate university for Dundee is not a feasible proposition.

Under this Bill there will be two councils, one representing the United Colleges of St. Andrews and the other the College of Dundee, and the principal will be in a very different position from the principals in the three other Scottish universities. He will be in an independent position and will be attached to neither college. That is a very wise provision because it seems to me that if he is attached to neither of the colleges he will be in a position to serve the best interests of both. He will not have the interests of one college pulling against the interests of the other as would be the case if he were the head of one of them.

If the right man is chosen—that is a very important thing indeed—although he will be faced with immense difficulties, he will also have great opportunities. The Secretary of State is right when he says that the mere passage of this Bill will not in itself do away with the disharmony and the disunity, but, as I have said, the Principal will have great opportunities.

Many of the recommendations contained in the Tedder Report do not require legislation, but just as the Government have brought forward this Bill to give effect to the recommendations which require legislation, the desired unity will not be achieved if the recommendations which depend on administration are not carried out. I wish to stress that point this morning.

The Commission's recommendations, especially those numbered 26 to 41, deal with the studies which should be carried on by the various colleges. A wise principal will ensure that those recommendations are upheld, and that seems to me one of the biggest jobs that the principal will have to do.

If these recommendations are upheld and brought into effect the College of Dundee will be in a much better and happier position than it has been for the last 50 years. With the extension of the faculties, and the strengthening of existing faculties in Dundee College, I feel that a higher prestige will be brought to this College of Dundee. I hope that whoever Image Col. 2327 is chosen as the principal will do everything he possibly can to implement these recommendations and will have the support of the other persons concerned in this matter.

The Secretary of State touched on one or two controversial matters. One point which I should like to raise concerns the position of the rector. Under this Bill the rector is to be put into a different position from that of the rectors in the other three Scottish universities. Instead of his being the chairman of the court the principal is to have that position. I shall leave some of my hon. Friends who feel strongly on this matter to deal with the pros and cons of it, but when we examine Clause 3, in page 3, line 10, we find these words:

"That person presiding at any meeting of the University Court shall have a deliberative vote and also a casting vote in the case of equality."

Under the new constitution of the university court as outlined in this Clause the senatus will have eight representatives, plus the principal, who is to be the chairman. The chairman is to have a deliberative vote and, if necessary, a casting vote.

This could mean that the Senatus representatives could, on occasion, obtain a decision against the wishes of every other lay member of the court. I have doubts whether that is a wise provision. It may be said that one would never get a group of professors and lecturers so agreed on any matter that they could combine and get this majority vote, but there may be occasions when that situation will arise, and the principal will then be placed in an almost impossible position. It is for that reason that I ask the Secretary of State to look at this question again. I put it no higher than that.

A university does not exist for the interests of professors and lecturers alone. The students and the community have interests that must be safeguarded, and among the lay members on the proposed new court we shall find the Lord Provost of Dundee and the Provost of St. Andrews. In this case Dundee seems to have a higher civic dignity than St. Andrews. Those officers, among others, are representative of the community, and I should never wish to see the Col. 2328 academicians in such a position that they could override the desires of the student and community representation. I do not want to labour this point any further, but for the reasons I have given I hope the Secretary of State will consider this question.

I trust that this Bill will have a speedy passage. Lord Tedder at no time claimed that the recommendations of his Commission provided an ideal solution. I do not think Solomon himself could have found an ideal solution to this very difficult problem, but with good will, plenty of common sense and a very wise principal, I think that the future of the great University of St. Andrews will be better and happier than it has ever been in the past.

Lieut.-Colonel Walter Elliot: This is an occasion which is very dear to the hearts of all Scottish Members. We are discussing a subject in which we probably take a more passionate interest than any other, namely, education, and especially university education. It is desirable that we should also remember that we are following in the steps of great and distinguished men and that we are part of a chain of thought and work which goes back into the very dim ages of Scotland and Scottish education. We are directly following up the Bull of Benedict XIII in 1413, and the foundation of the first university in Scotland at the request of King James I.

It is an inspiring thought—and one which adds dignity to the proceedings of this Committee—that we are handling a problem of such age and importance to our country, for this is one of the great gateways through which education entered Scotland. I will not concede that it is the only gateway, for in the west, also, we have long traditions of learning which we may claim go back to even earlier times, but they were not incorporated in this way. We certainly concede to St. Andrews, without any question, the position of being the premier university in Scotland in age, in beauty and in certain forms of academic distinction.

We have the reinforcement of the work of the Popes and the Kings in the Middle Ages and the great influx of strength, as well as weakness, which was brought to our country by the Industrial Revolution. Image Col. 2329 The citizens of the great City of Dundee founded Dundee College in 1881. There was a great gap between the early work —the Papal Bull of 1413, the foundation of St. Salvator's College in 1450, St. Leonard's College in 1512 and St. Mary's in 1537—and the founding of Dundee College in 1881. It is from that fact that some of the difficulties with which we are now dealing arise.

I do not favour this reorganisation merely for the purpose of resolving a long-continued dispute. There would be a danger in having the University of St. Andrews alone. It might become a backwater, or wither away into a merely interesting medieval survival. It needs the thrust and drive of a great industrial city to complete it, just as the great industrial city needs the dignity and tradition of the older foundation to bring it into the great sequence of our Scottish history of education.

I am not specially interested in the argument that this area is not large enough to draw sufficient students to maintain two universities and two colleges, because the University of St. Andrews is already drawing students not merely from all over Scotland but from England and other places outside the boundaries of Scotland. The interest which exists in this University goes far beyond the boundaries even of Europe, because many of its greatest recent endowments have been given from the New World.

The mass, the thrust and the drive of the City of Dundee are required by St. Andrews as much as St. Andrews is required by the City of Dundee, and these two things must be kept closely together. That is why I should be against the erection of a new university in Dundee, even though it has all the finance, facilities and arrangements necessary to produce a university.

With St. Andrews at its door, on the other side of the Firth, it would be foolish to set up two universities, one at each end of what we hope will be the Tay Road Bridge at no distant date. It would divide two things which were intended to be brought together. I therefore hope very strongly that we shall see a community of spirit as well as of administration resulting from the Bill which is now before us.

The mass of the University of Glasgow is too great, and it would be an advantage if we could find some method of compartmenting that enormous structure.

The position of St. Mary's has been of advantage to the university, and too great an inroad on its powers and prestige might be a bad thing for the new University of St. Andrews which we are trying to bring into existence this morning.

Member for Lanarkshire, North (Miss Herbison) has referred to the council, the position of the court, and the danger that we might find the general council in a position where they were Image Col. 2331 overridden in the senatus. That brings me to the third point, the position of the Rector. The Secretary of State said that this was a Bill which would not lead to a division upon party lines. I am very glad he intimated that that was so. I realise the responsibility of the Government for backing up the Tedder Commission, because Lord Tedder, as has already been said, did a great work. He worked well and swiftly. So did his Commission. They came to a unanimous Report to which we should give the greatest weight, but I cannot feel on this point that we should necessarily write into an Act of Parliament the recommendation of the Report.

It is said that the rector might lead to a lack of stability in the government and in continuity of policy. I myself have had the honour of being the rector of two of our Scottish universities, of Aberdeen and Glasgow. I am not aware that I interfered with the stability of their government. I am not aware that I made inroads into the continuity of their policy. The person chosen as rector has undoubtedly great responsibility. He holds an historic office, which, in times past—let us face it—was a much more active office than it is now; but still it is an important and a great office, and it is an office conferred upon him by the votes of the students. That also goes back a very long way; that was also laid down by Benedict XIII.

We should think twice before we make inroads on an institution so old and so respectable, because we differ greatly in this from the University of Aberdeen, which was founded by Alexander Borgia, who, perhaps, will not be regarded as so admirable a founder as Benedict XIII, against whose character, so far as I know, very little is said.

It is difficult, of course. The new principal will have a difficult task. I consider that he would have a reinforcement in the rector, occupying his traditional position in the chair of the court. The present Lord Rector of St. Andrews is a man whom any principal would be proud to have as his right hand, a man whom any principal would be proud to sit under if on occasions he took the chair. It is not necessary that the Rector should take the chair. The present Rector of Glasgow University, Col. 2332 who attends frequently, does not take the chair, although he is entitled to do it; he moves himself out of it and the principal thereupon takes the chair.

However, it is an ancient office. It is an office which in the past has been of great use. It is an office which brings the student body into direct relationship with the people of great authority and of, I think, considerable renown—present company always excepted—in the counsels of the land. The rectorial election is an interesting occasion for the students, the first time many of them have of exercising a vote. It may be that they do foolish things, but unless people are allowed to do foolish things they will never learn to do wise things. Unless they feel the weight of responsibility upon them they will be rash and irresponsible in exercising it. The position of rector would be weakened if it were held that he was simply an extra figure elected to the court as part of a spree or jamboree on a given day in the winter session by the students.

Indeed, it may be very difficult, for he may come to be regarded by the students and by himself as a type of special representative of the students' interests, whose business it is to watch the special interests of the students and the students alone, and put a veto down when some proposal is being produced on which he may think there is some disadvantage to their special interests. But let us not Image Col. 2365 dwell longer upon the past; let us rather look forward.

We are now entering upon what we believe will be a new period of justice, harmony and progress in which Dundee will share to the full, and to which Dundee will bring its own peculiar contribution of vigour, drive and generosity which will be of immense value to the whole university.

I listened to and was moved by the references of my right hon. and gallant Friend the Member for Glasgow, Kelvin-grove (Lieut.-Colonel Elliot) to the traditions of St. Andrews. They are indeed very wonderful traditions of learning, wisdom, pageantry and democracy in learning. As the hon. Member for Spelthorne (Mr. Beresford Craddock) said, one cannot walk through the old streets of St. Andrews and past its old stone houses, its ruined cathedral and its university buildings without being moved by the centuries of history that speak from every stone. But St. Andrews too must move with the times. It is no more able today to sustain a university on its own than is Dundee. As my right hon. and gallant Friend the Member for Kelvingrove said so well, as Dundee needs St. Andrews so St. Andrews needs Dundee—to preserve and expand the heritage that is jointly theirs. Though St. Andrews may very well feel regret at the passing of old customs and long-established forms, I do not doubt that in the interests of the reputation and the advancement of the university it will play a full and honourable part in the new arrangement.

If that be so, I hope that hon. Members, in contemplating their Amendments to the text of the Bill, will aim not at upsetting but at strengthening the Col. 2366 carefully and delicately poised structure which the Royal Commission have offered for the guidance of Parliament. If I may use the friendly metaphor of the hon. Member for Dundee, East, this is, in a sense, a peace treaty. As he knows from his military background, however, once a peace treaty has been drawn up it is seldom wise to interfere with its individual terms, especially when, as in this case, the terms include detailed provisions not only for the government of the university, but—much more important—for an enlightened distribution of studies in the two sites of the university.

Chapter V of the Tedder Report sets out what is intended, and I commend it to the attention of the hon. Member for Orkney and Shetland. It will be the purpose of the Commissioners to introduce ordinances to carry into effect those academic recommendations. That is their function, as it has been in other Scottish universities of days gone by.

In the end, everything depends on the spirit in which this proposal is accepted. The hon. Member for Dundee, East talked about the spirit of acceptance and the spirit of trust that was needed. Given that on both sides—and I believe we shall have it—there is, as he said, an exciting opportunity offered to both Dundee and St. Andrews to restore, revive and expand the oldest and, in my belief, the greatest of the Scottish universities.
