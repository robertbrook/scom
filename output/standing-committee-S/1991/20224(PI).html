10.30 am

The Chairman: Before I call the Secretary of State to speak, I wish to make it known to the Committee that more than the usual number of hon. Members have applied to speak in the debate. Given the time that the Front-Bench spokesmen will take to make their contributions, it would be better if speeches were curtailed to less than 10 minutes, so that hon. Members will not be disappointed.

Dr. Norman A. Godman (Greenock and Port Glasgow): On a point of order, Mr. Martin. As you know, Mr. Speaker Weatherill has authority under Standing Order No. 45A to limit the length of speeches to 10 minutes. I wonder whether you can call on that Standing Order to issue a similar edict. I ask that because I have a vested interest.

The Chairman: I do not have those powers. I am in the hands of the Committee.

10.31 am

The Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Ian Lang): I beg to move,

That the Chairman do report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of the proposals of the Scottish Constitutional Convention.

I am glad that the Opposition accepted my invitation to devote all four matter days in the Scottish Grand Committee to debating the most fundamental issue affecting Scotland's future, namely, its place within the constitution of the United Kingdom. I am glad, too, that we are beginning the debates with a double day here in Edinburgh, and I hope that the Opposition will take the opportunity that that affords to seek to prove their case.

The onus of proof is on the proponents of change. They want to set aside 300 years of successful partnership and evolutionary constitutional change in which Scotland's constitutional circumstances have been fully recognised. Indeed, the fact that we can debate the issue in Edinburgh is evidence of that evolutionary change under the hands of this Government. The responsibility for proving their case lies with Opposition Members.

The proponents of devolution must specify the full financial, social and political costs of such a change. As the Leader of the Opposition said in an article in The Daily Telegraph in January 1976, the Opposition must prove the need for accepting those costs. The Opposition have so far failed dismally to do that.

Events have now moved on with the travelling roadshow appearing in the Usher hall and the Assembly rooms. During those debates it became clear that the Labour party's flagship policy—HMS Devolution—is holed below the water line, before it has even left the harbour.

Col. 4 I shall focus today mainly on the economic and tax-raising powers proposed in Labour's constitutional convention. I hope that we shall discuss in later debates constitutional aspects and the European dimension. I welcome in particular to the Committee the hon. Members for Livingston (Mr. Cook) and for Dunfermline, East (Mr. Brown), and the right hon. and learned Member for Monklands, East (Mr. Smith). I hope that they will participate in our debate, make their positions clear and, possibly, say whether they consider it more appropriate that they should be members of a Scottish Parliament or a Westminster Parliament.

After all, it was the hon. Member for Livingston who said:

"After devolution, the position of Scottish hon. Members will be untenable."—[Official Report, 14 November 1977; Vol. 939, c. 157.]

He said that in 1977 but, in fairness to him, his view has not waivered. He has further refined it by saying that, once a Scottish Parliament is set up with powers over health issues in Scotland, he cannot remain as a Minister for Health in England. Perhaps that is the Livingston answer to the west Lothian question, but I think that it is a blinding glimpse of the truth. How can a Scottish Member of a Westminster Parliament protect the health interests of his constituents when health matters are controlled by a separate Parliament? How can a Scottish Member, as a Government Minister, credibly control health matters for England and Wales while his constituents live in a separate jurisdiction? Those questions must be answered if the scheme proposed by the constitutional convention is to have credibility or stability. By the same principle, the issue of voting rights must be considered.

Mr. Robert Hughes (Aberdeen, North): Has the Secretary of State read that one of his ministerial colleagues has conceded that the Tories in Scotland will be wiped out, and that it would be necessary and tenable for a Member of Parliament from England to serve as Secretary of State for Scotland? Does not that go against the right hon. Gentleman's argument?

Mr. Lang: No, it does not. The point suggested by the hon. Member for Aberdeen, North (Mr. Hughes) was not conceded by one of us. In a unitary Parliament, there is no problem with hon. Members who represent a certain part of the country serving in Departments which are responsible for other areas. For example my right hon. and learned Friend the Member for Edinburgh, Pentlands (Mr. Rifkind) is Secretary of State for Transport and the hon. Member for Clackmannan (Mr. O'Neill) is shadow Secretary of State for Defence. Problems would arise only when there were separate entrenched powers in separate rival Parliaments with separate jurisdictions. The issue is not a storm in a teacup, as the hon. Member for Glasgow, Garscadden (Mr. Dewar) has suggested.

Under such a proposal, we would have two-tier Ministers, two-tier Members of Parliament and a two-tier Cabinet. We Scots would become second-class citizens at the very heart of our democracy. That would create a deep fissure, which would strike at the core of our constitutional roots.

Mr. John Home Robertson (East Lothian): We already have two-tier Members of Parliament. The majority of hon. Members who represent Scotland are Opposition Members and we have been prevented from taking part in the legislative process in Committee on issues such as the Image Col. 5 poll tax, whereas English Members of Parliament have been drafted in repeatedly by the right hon. Gentleman's colleagues to pack Committees that deal exclusively with Scottish legislation, thereby inflicting unwanted legislation on Scotland.

Mr. Lang: The hon. Gentleman does not understand the fundamental principles of a unitary Parliament in a unitary state. It is interesting that he has already made his choice. In fairness to him, unlike some of his hon. Friends, he has opted for the second division of the Scottish Parliament to sit in rivalry to his hon. Friends in the Westminster Parliament.

I am especially glad that the right hon. and learned Member for Monklands, East is in Committee today. I wrote to him last week and asked him to be here—[Laughter.] Opposition Members may find that funny, but he has never before attended these sittings. If I am wrong about that, I shall withdraw that statement. The right hon. and learned Gentleman has rarely been here on previous occasions. He has never spoken in our deliberations. I urge him to speak today and to make his position clear on the Cook doctrine. Is the Livingston dilemma the answer to the west Lothian question?

I hope that the right hon. and learned Gentleman will take the opportunity to clarify the overall effect of his United Kingdom tax proposals. Will he explain the eight new tax pledges that will add new tax burdens on the whole of the United Kingdom for the purpose of going some way to fund the £35 billion and more of increased spending that his hon. and right hon. Friends have promised? Will he make it clear how much freedom he, as Chancellor of the Exchequer, would grant to a Scottish Parliament or how much he would feel constrained by the separate entrenched powers that Opposition Members wish to set up?

We know answers to some of the questions. We know that the Opposition might allow tax to rise by as much as 3p in the pound. That would create a new position because, alone in the United Kingdom, Scots who pay income tax would pay more tax than their counterparts elsewhere in the United Kingdom. [Interruption.] Opposition Members seem to find that amusing, too. That policy would mean an extra £7 a week for a single man on average earnings which would be a separate burden that was not shared by others. Such a policy would raise only about £430 million, which is a fraction of the present Scottish Office budget.

I wonder whether the right hon. and learned Gentleman can guarantee that, given that position, those resources would be additional to the existing advantage that Scotland derives from the United Kingdom, whereby the United Kingdom Treasury continues to spend, for every £4 per head that it spends in England, £5 per head in Scotland. The benefit on identifiable public expenditure is £600 higher in Scotland than it is in England, 24 per cent. higher and £500 higher than the average in the United Kingdom.

As the Daily Record said, we in Scotland, do not do too badly out of the United Kingdom Exchequer. The Scotsman said that there would need to be an adjustment in spending, and there is no dispute about that. The right hon. and learned Gentleman may think that there is, so perhaps he will intervene in the debate and clarify the position. If he cannot give that extra guarantee to which I Col. 6 referred, presumably extra Scottish tax would be needed just to stand still and make good the erosion of the resources coming from the Westminster Parliament. At present, the extra benefits that we derive are based on need and are related to population, but that is a vulnerable calculation. It is not enshrined in law; it is open to negotiation and renegotiation. Such a process must be defended.

Mr. Donald Dewar (Glasgow, Garscadden): Is the Secretary of State saying that, when the Scots express a clear wish for a greater say over their own affairs, within the framework of the United Kingdom, and when that is agreed by Westminster, that justifies a punitive raid on Scotland's public finances and a departure from the principle that resources are allocated on the basis of need?

Mr. Lang: No one is talking about a punitive raid. We are talking about the vulnerability of Scotland's position—we would no longer be able to defend or justify, morally or practically, the awarding of the existing substantial additional benefit to Scotland by the United Kingdom Parliament, once there was a separate tax jurisdiction in Scotland. If the resources raised in Scotland were entirely additional to resources from England, how would the shadow Chancellor, if he became Chancellor, maintain control on public expenditure? Would he make offsetting cuts in public expenditure in England, thereby making the English-Scottish ratio even higher? What borrowing powers would he give to a Scottish Parliament? The last Labour Government left debts of £24 billion, which were a massive burden on the people of the United Kingdom. In today's prices, that would be over £50 billion. How would their planned borrowing powers enable a Labour Chancellor to maintain control over macro-economic policy?

Mr. John Smith (Monklands, East): I object to the condescending way in which the Secretary of State invited people to come to Committees on which they have a place as of right. That condescension is in no way justified by the support that his depleted band will win from the people of Scotland.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Mr. Allan Stewart): Get on with it.

Mr. Smith: I shall ignore the hon. Gentleman's rude interruptions and deal with the Secretary of State's foolish points. First, if a Scottish Assembly decided to raise a certain amount of money in extra taxation, that money would be genuinely additional. Secondly, any borrowing would be agreed with the British Treasury. We have made that clear from the beginning. I do not know why the right hon. Gentleman wants to pursue an argument based on fantasy, not fact.

Mr. Lang: In other words, there would be higher taxes, which the right hon. Gentleman would not be able to control, in Scotland. How would he be able to control his macro-economic policy when, as he accepts, part of the United Kingdom's tax-raising powers would be outwith his jurisdiction?

Mr. John Smith: Will the right hon. Gentleman give way?

Mr. Lang: The right hon. and learned Gentleman is entitled to speak in this debate. I challenged him to do so last week and I look forward to hearing from him. As Image Col. 7 Chancellor of the Exchequer, he would be compromised, inhibited and constantly looking over his shoulder. Under a Labour Government, public expenditure in Scotland would be totally out of control. In the rest of the United Kingdom, it would be largely out of control—the usual situation of Labour Governments. Things would be allowed to slide. There would be higher taxation and higher spending. It would be business as usual under Labour.

I shall deal with the powers of the Scottish Parliament. A cursory glance at the Scottish Constitutional Convention's document makes it clear that, far from devolving power, the proposals would involve the most substantial translation of power to the centre of Government since Labour's disastrous policies of the 1960s. At a time when eastern European countries are abandoning socialism and embracing capitalism, the Labour party proposes to take Scotland back to a dark age of control that would suffocate enterprise and stamp out growth and prosperity. The document's language is hair-raising. It talks about "strategic economic planning powers"; about

"powers to co-ordinate the planning of the Scottish components of UK industries";

about

"power to initiate some form of public ownership or control";

and about responsibility for the protection of Scottish industry. In other words, Labour would build a smothering cocoon around Scottish industry. It would be interventionism and protectionism from the real world. Scotland would be turned into an industrial museum. It would stagnate and decline.

Mr. Malcolm Bruce (Gordon): I hope that the Secretary of State is aware that the Parliament will be democratically elected by a reformed electoral system? Would not the Conservative party put up candidates for that election? If the people of Scotland did not like those proposals, might not they vote for the Conservatives—or have the Conservatives written off Scotland altogether?

Mr. Lang: If the hon. Gentleman thinks that he will get the reformed electoral system that he wants—proportional representation, which Lloyd George described as a device for defeating democracy—he has another think coming. The Labour party will never introduce proportional representation—the hon. Gentleman and his party are being taken for a ride.

The proposed powers—which the document states is not an exhaustive list—would damage the strength and depth of Scottish industry. The Scottish Trades Union Congress wants to nationalise gas, electricity, telecommunications, steel and coal. Perhaps the hon. Member for Garscadden will tell us whether he agrees with that policy or whether he thinks, as he said the other day, that nationalisation is no more than a slogan, available only to a party that has no expectation of gaining power. I think we should be told.

It is no wonder that the business community in Scotland consistently gives the thumbs down to those proposals. The document lists 30 areas ranging from coastal erosion to inland waterways, in which a Scottish Parliament would exercise powers. Many of those powers duplicate or triplicate existing arrangements. They complicate the key issue that the convention has not even begun to address—the relationship between a Scottish Parliament and the Westminster Parliament.

Col. 8 Let us examine regional aid as an example. The document is worded in the flimsiest and vaguest of language. There is almost no reference to regional aid, other than an oblique comment about powers

"to negotiate with multi-nationals … within the context of UK regional policy."

Later, in apparent contradiction to that, the document talks of

"powers to establish Scotland as a competitive and attractive location for companies to establish and to encourage competitive advantage for Scottish-based business".

I shall leave aside the grammatical uncertainty of that sentence. Powers to make Scotland an attractive location for inward investment already exist. Who is it proposed that Scotland should be competitive with? With other European countries, as at the moment, or with England? Regional aid is not a Scottish bloc issue. It is funded by the United Kingdom Treasury, its resources are separately negotiated each year and the United Kingdom Parliament draws up the regional aid map. As the document says, the proposals of the Parliament would retain it within the context of United Kingdom regional policy.

At present, two thirds of the population of Scotland are within the regional assistance map. That is far higher than the proportion of the United Kingdom population as a whole, as dominated by the regulations of the European Commission. The figure for England is around 20 per cent. In the last five years, over £400 million of regional aid has helped create or protect 70,000 jobs and attract £3·5 billion of investment to Scotland. That map and those funds would be jeopardised if the policy were divided between a Scottish Parliament and a United Kingdom Parliament. They would become non-existent if Scotland tried to compete with the rest of the United Kingdom and to pursue a separate policy.

A similar confusion would exist over Europe. At the moment, regional and state aids, and all regulations on regional policy, are negotiated between the United Kingdom Government and the European Community. I do not think anyone is saying—perhaps the hon. Member for Garscadden will make that clear—that a Scottish Parliament would be able to circumvent the treaty of Paris and that a Labour Government would be able to pour help into the steel industry. I have not heard such an idea from the Labour Benches. The Scottish National party claims that it could do that—and it is for that party to defend that. The European regional development fund has directed resources of £1·4 billion into Scotland over the past 12 years. The importance of that cannot be overrated. However, the convention's proposal to establish an office in Brussels to—as the document puts it—"facilitate relations" with Europe and to establish a statutory entitlement to accompany United Kingdom delegations to the European Community would add nothing to the existing powers, but would considerably diminish them and the benefit that Scotland derives from Europe. I already have the power to attend Councils and to speak at them. I have done so. As a Member of the United Kingdom Parliament, I already have the power to influence the vote in the European Community. I have already "facilitated relations", with President Delors, with Commissioner Brittan, with Commissioner Andriessen and with Commissioner MacSharry—I have had meetings with all of them. The European Commission negotiates with the United Kingdom Parliament over enterprise zones, state aid, RECHAR, RESIDER, LEADER, Image Col. 9 objective 5B schemes and so on. The United Kingdom is the signatory of the treaties and would continue to be: a Scottish Parliament would have no status in those negotiations.

Mr. Jim Sillars (Glasgow, Govan): Will the Secretary of State tell us how many times he has headed the United Kingdom delegation to the Council of Ministers? What happens when the Scottish Office Agriculture and Fisheries Department disagrees with the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food south of the border? How are such disagreements resolved? Does Scottish opinion go with the United Kingdom's 10 votes, or is it extinguished?

Mr. Lang: The hon. Gentleman misunderstands the point about disagreements. The United Kingdom speaks with one voice in the Councils of Europe because its policies are agreed, with a full input from Wales, Northern Ireland, Scotland and England, before going to a Council of Ministers. However, the hon. Gentleman is absolutely right to fix on that point of disagreement, because if the Scottish Parliament delegate to a Council of Ministers disagreed with the United Kingdom delegate, he would have absolutely no power to change the decision; it would be a decision of the United Kingdom, not of the Scottish Parliament. I hope that the hon. Member for Garscadden can make it clear how, in those circumstances, the powers of a Scottish Parliament could enhance Scotland's place in Europe. The same is true over fishing. Scotland has half the United Kingdom total catch, yet we would disqualify ourselves from full rights as part of a United Kingdom Council of Ministers. If a Scottish Parliament breached the Commission's treaty—

Mr. George Robertson (Hamilton): If it is so detrimental to Scotland's interests to have a separate Scottish voice in those delegations, how has the Federal Republic of Germany managed to do so well over the years by having exactly that relationship—the German states having their own officers in Brussels and a special status within the Federal Republic's delegation? They seem to have done it effectively and efficiently. Why can that not apply in the United Kingdom?

Mr. Lang: The hon. Gentleman is arguing that we should have a federal system in the United Kingdom. The Federal Republic of Germany is just that—it is a federation. The Länder do not have the powers that the Scottish National party suggests that a Scottish Parliament should have. They do not have the right to vote in the Council of Ministers. A separate Scottish Parliament would have a similar lack of power. If a Scottish Parliament breached an EC treaty, the United Kingdom Government would be held responsible. How would the United Kingdom enforce its authority over a Scottish Parliament with entrenched powers? Commissioner Bruce Millan has given us some idea of what might happen, but we should be told. I hope that we shall be told today.

The biggest of all the unanswered questions is: who would pay? Who would pick up the bill for that patchwork of added burdens and muddled thinking?

Mr. Tom Clarke (Monklands, West): Will the Secretary of State give way?

Mr. Lang: Not at this moment.

Col. 10 There is no doubt that it would cost extra money. The Labour party has ambitious and costly plans for an unsuspecting Scotland. The hon. Member for Livingston again helps with his clear, honest view that:

"We have to be honest about this. The Assembly will inevitably expand public expenditure. There can be no doubt about that."—[Official Report, 14 November 1977; Vol. 939, c. 159.]

He is right. A Scottish Parliament itself would cost the people of Scotland over £1 million a week to run, before it started doing anything. That is why, alone among the countries of the United Kingdom, Scotland has not had a promise from the Labour party that its income tax would not go up. If a Scottish Parliament comes into being, income tax will go up. The only question will be by how much.

Mr. Clarke: The Secretary of State spoke of unanswered questions. Will he answer this question? If the Scottish Parliament would be such a disadvantage to Scotland, why did Lord Home of the Hirsel intervene in the 1979 referendum, promising an Assembly with even greater powers? Why has that never been delivered?

Mr. Lang: Like many people before and since, Lord Home has continued the search for a solution. Like many before and since, Lord Home recognised the great difficulties, which the Opposition do not recognise and will not address. They have not found a way of funding the programmes that they want a Scottish Parliament to set up. The convention document states that the revenue from Scottish income tax and Scottish VAT should be assigned to a Scottish Parliament. Last year, however, those two sources raised only £7 million. That is less than half the total of identifiable public expenditure in Scotland and less than a third of total Government expenditure in Scotland.

Where is the rest to come from? We should be told. Would that huge funding gap be filled by English taxpayers, who already spend 25 per cent. more on us than they do on themselves? Would it be filled by the European Community, to which a Scottish Parliament would make us less accessible and to which the UK is already a net contributor? Would it be filled by borrowing, which would have to be paid for, or would it come from the dear old International Monetary Fund—a friend of the previous Labour Government?

The fact is that a Scottish Parliament would run out of benefactors. It would run out of soft options. It would quickly run out of money. The taxpayers of Scotland would pay. No wonder Professor Arthur Midwinter, one of the gurus of the Labour party, spoke recently of instability and disillusionment.

The shadow Chancellor admits to an extra 3p in the pound on income tax. Uniquely in the United Kingdom, we Scots would pay a higher rate. We would pay an extra £442 for a Scottish police constable—£442 more than your English counterparts; an extra £585 for a primary school head teacher—£585 more than our counterparts in Wales; and so on right across the board. But 3p extra in the pound would raise only £432 million, or 4 per cent. of my Scottish Office budget for next year. That would not go far. It is 3p in the pound for starters. Labour's new pay roll tax to take £80 million out of Scottish businesses—a tax on jobs—would be 0·5 per cent. of the pay roll for starters. The uniform business rate, towards which we have so far secured £330 million to bring it down to the United Kingdom average, would disappear, as every council lets rip once the protection of capping is abolished.

Image Col. 11 The Scottish National party complains about business rates being higher in Scotland than elsewhere in the United Kingdom, but that is the only tax that is not under the control of the United Kingdom Government. That tax is high because local authorities in Scotland spend 30 per cent. more per head than is spent south of the border, despite higher subsidies being paid from the United Kingdom Parliament than are paid to local authorities in England. That is a glimpse of the sort of tax burdens that a separate Scotland or, indeed, a Scotland with a separate Scottish Parliament would face. The Leader of the Opposition said that

"The proponents of devolution must specify the full financial, social and political costs … they must prove the need for accepting these costs".

For once, he was right. In a range of ways, a Scottish Parliament would run amok among the business and individual taxpayers of Scotland to fund policies that would bring Scotland to her knees.

It need not be like that. There is no room to destroy the Union or to damage Scotland's place in it. There is no need to offer a cure for which there is no known disease. The hon. Member for Garscadden said in the Usher hall:

"I think Scotland is alive, and I think it's flourishing."

He is right. Within the Union we have had 300 years of progress and growing prosperity. The Union has done us proud. During this Parliament, under this Government, and within the United Kingdom Scotland has achieved all-time records on manufacturing output, investment, employment, exports and living standards. The Opposition's proposals would undo all that. They want to erect economic barriers and to create a two-tier, two-geared economic policy for the United Kingdom that would make Scotland the loser.
