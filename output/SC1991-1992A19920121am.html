Clause 48
STOPPING UP AND DIVERSION OF CROSSINGS

Question proposed [16 January], That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Question again proposed.

Mr. Andrew F. Bennett
(Denton and Reddish)
: I was about to try to persuade the Committee that clause 48 was unnecessary last Thursday when the Government Whip intervened to suggest that we defer considering until this morning. All members of the Committee, and certainly all hon. Members who spoke on Second Reading, are extremely concerned about the safety of rail crossings, particularly for pedestrians. All who read the report of the incident at Doncaster have been horrified by it. Accidents involving pedestrians on rail crossings are, fortunately, rare although accidents on level crossings in general are far too frequent; on most occasions they involve people in vehicles.

My hon. Friend the Member for West Bromwich, East (Mr. Snape) said that the horror and trauma of the accident were experienced not only by those injured and killed and their relatives, but by the engine drivers involved.

The whole Committee wants improved safety on all level crossings, especially those used by people on foot or on horseback. British Rail's approach seems to be to abolish crossings rather than to make them safe. It is unfortunate that it took a long time for the Health and Safety Executive to make its recommendations on how level crossings could be made safer. I hope that, following consultation on that document, British Rail will act speedily.

It is also regrettable that despite having had the powers to install warning lights for pedestrian crossings on railways, I could not find—nor could the Minister in a parliamentary answer—any sites where British Rail had done so. If British Rail were concerned about safety, it would surely have looked at one or two places where a substantial number of people use a crossing and where, because of the increased speeds of trains, just looking up the line to see whether it was clear was not satisfactory and installed the system of signals that are used on crossings used by cars. It has not done that.

British Rail's approach has been simply to say that crossings should be closed. I argue that it is unnecessary to 278 do that because under the Highways Act 1980, as amended by the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981, there are sufficient powers for British Rail to divert such crossings. It would be far more reasonable for it to use those powers. If the crossings are not used there is no safety problem and British Rail can close the path under the Highways Act 1980. There is no need for a new procedure, because sufficient powers already exist.

The Ramblers Association is not convinced that the case for new procedures for rail crossing diversion and extinguishment orders has been made. Furthermore, the rights of way review committee, which is an informal, non-statutory committee chaired by the hon. Member for Saffron Walden (Mr. Haselhurst), is not convinced that the new power is needed. Normally, Governments need much persuasion before they introduce a new statutory procedure, yet the enabling clause 48 and schedule 2 will give the Government a new procedure for closing footpaths.

When British Rail submitted evidence to the Joint Committee on Private Bill Procedure, it referred to the Highways Act 1980 as originally passed and not as amended in the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981. At that stage, it did not seem to be aware that the proposals had changed. That amendment was suggested by the rights of way review committee, which at that time was chaired by the hon. Member for Worcestershire, South (Mr. Spicer). It allows orders to be made to divert public footpaths and bridleways in the interests of the owner of land or in the interests of the public. As British Rail owns the land, it can ask for a diversion in its interests: in many cases it can also argue that a diversion will be in the public's interest because it may make the crossing safer. That shows that grounds for a diversion order already exist.

It is interesting to note that, in the west country, British Rail realises that it has sufficient powers because earlier last year an order to divert a path was made under the existing power by Taunton Deane council in Somerset. At Tursdale in Durham, British Rail is trying to gain parliamentary approval to divert a dangerous crossing. The crossing is subject to the provisions in the East Coast Main Line (Safety) Bill, which came before Parliament in the previous Session and has been reintroduced in this Session. Unfortunately, British Rail is being obstinate about paying the county council's costs of making the order. If BR is worried about safety, surely it should be only too willing to pay the costs.
