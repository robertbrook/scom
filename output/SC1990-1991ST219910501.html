That the Committee has considered the National Health Service Trusts (Consultation before Establishment) (Scotland) Regulations 1991.

First, I must tell my hon. Friend the Member for Falkirk, West (Mr. Canavan) that we shall endeavour to bring the issue back to the Scottish Grand Committee at a future date.

The debate today, which is about the consultation that must take place before a hospital opts out, comes at an especially significant time. Guy's hospital, the flagship of opting out in England, is already in severe financial difficulties and must shed jobs. The cuts in services at Guy's hospital are being made not because the services are not needed but because, in the words of the chief executive, they are "not profitable". I repeat, it is not because the services are not needed, but because they are not profitable. It seems that, like the rest of the flagships, Guy's is sinking fast.

JPEG 5 I appreciate that Guy's hospital and, indeed, all the other hospitals in England are not the responsibility of the Minister of State, Scottish Office, but they are of particular relevance to the consultation process that we are discussing today.

In the case of Guy's hospital, information was held back from those consulted and the consultation leading to opt-out was a sham, which we consider will happen in Scotland. The decisions had already been made and Guy's was allowed to opt-out, irrespective of the consultative process and of the hospital's serious financial position.

It was boasted at Guy's hospital that it was very well managed. It was considered to be so financially sound that it was given an external finance borrowing limit of £8.5 million. We should remember that two thirds of the opted-out hospitals were not given any borrowing limit. But Guy's was considered to be so financially sound that the Treasury gave it an £8.5 million borrowing limit. Yet, only one month after Guy's opted out, it is in severe financial difficulties.

The parallels with Scotland are uncanny. Aberdeen Royal Infirmary and South Ayrshire district general hospital are the flagships of opt-out in Scotland. It is claimed that they, too, are well managed and financially sound—exactly the same claims were made for Guy's hospital.

Therefore, can the Minister assure us that in those hospitals, and in the Royal Scottish National hospital at Larbert, there will be no job cuts if they opt-out? Can the Minister assure us that no doctor, nurse, paramedic or ancillary worker will lose his or her job as a result of any of those hospitals opting out? During the consultation process that has now started, I believe that everyone is entitled to know the answers to those questions, and we await the Minister's reply with considerable interest.

Certainly, Mr. Barbour, the manager at Foresterhill, feels unable to give such assurances. Therefore, we must conclude that, as at Guy's, hospital opt-out in Scotland will mean not only job losses but, more importantly, a significant reduction in patient care. As at Guy's hospital, services will go simply because they are unprofitable, not because they are not needed.

The business plans of these hospitals do not give us confidence. Unlike my hon. Friends in England, I have seen the business plans of the three hospitals in Scotland. Ayrshire and Arran expect to retain a surplus in the first year of £161,000—a 1 per cent. return on capital. That is hardly enough to be viable. Foresterhill in Aberdeen requires a capital injection, according to the business plan, of £13 million. Are those hospitals to opt out on that basis? The fact that the hospitals have even applied to opt out is astonishing when one considers their business plans. The Ayrshire and Arran business plan states:

"These close links with the community mean that the Trusts will respond sensitively to local needs."

Is not it odd that the hospital has ignored local opinion by pushing ahead with the proposal against the wishes of local people? No wonder the business plans are considered to be nothing but cynical hype.

There is more rubbish. The Ayrshire and Arran business plan continues:

"A series of tasks was identified and responsibility for completion allocated under the timetable set."

That is terrific. It really helps to make up our minds whether it is a sound proposition.

6 There is more:

"The premise for this plan is that the South Ayrshire Hospitals will become an NHS trust."

That is a piece of information of which we were not aware! The business plans are a mixture of shaky premise, nasty hype and dodgy financial assumption, nothing more than vague waffle, eulogising warm weather, mom and apple pie. The business plan for Aberdeen Royal infirmary is so vague that it is impossible to know what is going on and to make judgments.

The Minister should accept that now is the time to stop the silly, unwanted, unloved and damaging process of opting out. The hospitals should be kept firmly with the health board and within the health service.

If, despite the shambles, consultation is to go ahead, the wishes of the staff and the local people should be decided only by a ballot. Why has the Minister told the health boards not to hold ballots? Can he confirm that the regulations do not prevent a health board from taking a consultative ballot of the staff and the local community?

The Minister is opposed to ballots, and I understand why. Every ballot that has been conducted so far has shown that a majority of staff and local people is opposed to opting out.

I have no brief to speak for Guy's hospital. However, one point seems to have escaped the hon. Gentleman—that Guy's hospital had a serious problem because its expenditure substantially exceeded its income. Hospital management wanted to develop new services, and that involves the transference of resources from one area to another. Decisions about possible redundancies would also be required.

None of the central difficulties of Guy's hospital arose from its becoming an NHS trust. The Opposition put forward the extraordinary proposition that it is better to take diffucult decisions about finance and about meeting commitments to improve services above the heads of hospital management, at health authority or departmental level. I find that difficult to understand. We should take a step forward in patient care if the managers and doctors who are directly involved on a day-to-day basis were given responsibility for taking their decisions—which is implied by NHS trust status—if they were released from the bureaucracy and red tape about which they have complained for years, or if they were given the advantage of carrying money forward from one financial year to the next.

The hon. Gentleman asked, "What guarantees can you give me about jobs?" We should be seeking guarantees on behalf of patients. The health service in Scotland enjoys expenditure that is between 25 per cent. and 30 per cent. higher than that south of the border. None of the hospitals that are applying for NHS trust status has any financial problems. Intrinsic to the idea of NHS trust status is the freedom for hospitals to develop or introduce new services. Gentleman that no NHS trust will be approved by my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State unless he believes that it will improve patient care. The hon. Gentleman should welcome that.
