The problem is how we know when detention starts. There is no declaration and there are no trigger words or phrases used. Detention starts when it starts, but how do we know when it starts? "indicates to a person that he is required to go to a police station or other premises."

That is not academic dancing on the head of a pin. There is a great problem here. There will not always be a situation—in fact it will probably be rare—in which the police constable goes up to someone and says, "I detain you under section 2 of the Criminal Justice (Scotland) Act 1980." What will happen is that someone will be just asked to go to the police station— "invited", as he often is under the present system—and it will not be clear to him whether he is there on a voluntary basis, the "voluntary" being pretty peremptory, or whether the powers under clause 2 are being used. That will give rise to legitimate confusion in the mind of a suspected person.

Even more commonly, if the clause is to have any use, it will be brought into play when someone has been in the police station, clearly on a voluntary basis, for some time and decides that he wants to leave. He may not actually say "I want to leave", but at some point that voluntary stay in the police station may be transubstantiated—if I may use an unlikely religious term—into the powers under clause 2.

The Minister may be assuming that there will be a statement by the police constable to the effect that that is happening. The Minister may say that that is implied and that if we say that detention starts when detention starts, clearly we must tell the man that detention is starting. But that is not necessarily true.
