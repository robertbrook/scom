TRANSPORT BILL
Clause 9
GRANT OF ROAD SERVICE LICENCES FOR CERTAIN EXCURSIONS OR TOURS

Amendment No. 110 proposed [29 January], in page 10, line 23, leave out "directly" and insert "in any way".—[ Mr. Booth.]

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

Mr. Albert Booth
: In the headlong rush during the early hours of Wednesday morning to make as much progress as was consistent with a respectable examination of the Bill, I moved amendment No. 110 with what the Minister very fairly described as commendable brevity. I hope that when we deal with these matters with commendable brevity we provoke a constructive response from the Minister.

The issue relates to whether, in considering applications for road service licences by people who are operating tours or excursions, the commissioners should consider them under the first or the second leg of subsection (1). If they decide that they are competing with established stage carriage services in the area, they will be considered under the first leg. Presumably the commissioners can then turn down the applications on the ground that the proposed service for a tour or excursion is already adequately covered by stage carriage arrangements in the area. That is particularly true if one accepts the present wording of the clause, which refers to competing "directly". Presumably, if there were an application to run a service which had exactly the same effect on passenger transport within a much narrower time range the commissioners could turn it down because the exact service was already available and there was no evidence that it could not cope with the passenger demand on the day.

Another consideration gives rise to the amendment. It could be held that the slightest variation between what was 862 proposed as an excursion or tour and what was already in existence was not competing "directly". It could be argued that it was an indirect form of competition and, therefore, would not fall to be considered under subsection (1)(a). Presumably, the commissioners would then have to be convinced that it was laid on to deal with a special event.

That is the area with which the amendment deals. It proposes that if the commissioners receive an application for a tour or excursion they have to be satisfied that the proposed service would not compete "in any way" with other road services for which a licence had already been granted.

If the Minister would like to reply now on that point, I shall gladly sit down. We may then consider whether we need to deal with the more difficult aspect that follows from the way in which the definition of the services, which flows from our decision on the express coach, has a bearing on the matter.

Mr. Clarke
: As of now—I shall take the opportunity to correct it later if I am wrong—I do not anticipate a separate form of application. The Bill does not indicate any different type of application. There would be an application for a road service licence and the commissioners, of their own volition, on receiving an application would have to decide, first, whether it was a section 8 case—that is, there are absolutely no other competing transport systems—which need not bother us too much, or a section 9 case, an excursion or tour that does not compete or is a special event. If, on examining it, the commissioners are satisfied that it is a section 9 case, they must issue a road service licence.

There is no need for a regulation to cover that. They will simply sift those applications which they are satisfied come within section 9, for which the Bill obliges them to issue a licence. There is no need to go through the whole process of an ordinary application for a road service licence.

The amendment deals with the precise definition of the competition with which we are concerned. As the Bill stands, the commissioners must be satisfied that the service does not compete "directly". The right hon. Gentleman prefers the phrase 864 "in any way". I think he will agree that we are splitting hairs to an extent. However, it seems to me that the effect of the phrase "in any way" is a more severe test than the word "directly".

An excursion or tour around villages or resorts would at some point be almost bound to cover a section of journey that coincides with another stage carriage service, yet the most minute coincidence of the two could be argued as being competition "in any way". The definition preferred in the Bill—"directly"—imports a meaning, and has a real bearing on the commercial position of a service with which there may be competition.

I suggest that it is not necessary to stiffen the language in the way that the Opposition propose as the commissioners will have to satisfy themselves that there is no damaging competition before they adopt the procedure. They are experienced in traffic matters, have a background knowledge of stage carriage services, which is a primary duty, and understand transport networks. The requirement that they must be satisfied is quite a heavy one. There must be a clear case before they will use the section 9 procedure. If there is any doubt, and they are not satisfied that the short procedure will apply, there must be a full hearing.

I believe that whether the phrase "are satisfied" is combined with the word "directly", that confines section 9 to only the most clear-cut cases. If one stiffens the requirement to the further extent that the commissioners must be satisfied that the proposed service would not compete "in any way" with any other road service, in a fanciful chain of events the abstraction of just one passenger would be enough to go through the full performance for an excursion or tour licence. That is not necessary. One can trust the commissioners to make sure that clause 9 will not open the way for excursions and tours being permitted if there is the possibility of any real damage to the road service network for which they have a responsibility.

I hope that in view of that explanation the right hon. Member for Barrow-in-Furness will accept that the Bill intends to confine section 9 to clear-cut cases, that its present wording will enable the commissioners to do that, and that it would be ultra-cautious to stiffen the language as the amendment requires. Indeed, there might be a danger that there would be hardly any section 9 excursions, and JPEG 865 perfectly routine holiday tours, for example, would be subjected to the whole traffic commissioner procedure, with unnecessary expenditure and time-wasting, and no advantage to the road service network.

It cannot be held that there is competition merely by virtue of the fact that over part of a route there is a coincidence of a stage carriage and a tour or excursion. Therefore, I believe that in many cases the coincidence will not be the matter that the commissioner will have to take into account. What he will have to take into account—presumably that would occur with either form of wording but I agree that that would probably occur in more cases with the amendment—is whether what is proposed as a tour or excursion has on its route stops which coincide with other stops on a service that is covered by a road service licence, and, if it has, what the conditions are for carriage.

That is not what is considered by most passengers to be a tour or excursion, however. They apply for a seat on a tour or excursion and normally expect to be carried along a particular route from one place to another and back, stopping not for the purpose of picking up passengers, in the main, but to call at a pub or cafe or see some beauty spot. That is what the majority of people consider a tour or excursion.

It adds considerable point to our amendment if we say that that kind of tour JPEG 867 or excursion will not cause the commissioners difficulty. It would be the kind of service that travels to specific places on specific days within the radius prescribed in the Bill for express services. In this case, there is a similar limitation on a requirement for licence.

Someone may propose to run an excursion on days when a certain team is playing at home from one village or town to the place where the team was playing. That person may regard that trip as an excursion. In such case, the commissioner must consider whether that is competing "directly" or "in any way", according to whether the amendment is accepted. If he considers it on the basis of competing "directly" then he can look at similar services and turn it down on that basis.

But the shrewd operator who has followed our proceedings with great care—for which he has our sympathies—will realise that what we have done here is make an overlapping provision between the definitions of express services and tours or excursions. He will realise that there is one way to avoid the need to go to the traffic commissioner, and another to ensure that if he does, the commissioner will use the short-cut rather than use the full section 5 procedure.

The former will occur in many cases where the distance of the route from end to end begins to approach 30 miles. Let us take the example of a tour or excursion to a football match, jazz concert or special event. I am not trying to distinguish between the services provided. I accept that people attending any of these events are equally entitled to be provided with bus services. The operator in those cases will know that all he has to do to get outside the licensing provision altogether is to have a point somewhere on the route that is 30 miles from the point at which it starts or at which it finishes. That is one option open to him.

The other option is so to arrange his stopping places that they are slightly different from the stage carriage services operating in the area. He can then tell the commissioners. that it does not compete directly because it is slightly different.

868 Commissioners are practical men, but they have a great respect for the decisions of the House of Commons. If we say "directly", they will have to assume that we mean directly. Therefore, any different service may not be held to be competing directly. If we say "in any way", the commissioners will use their judgment, knowing that we mean a way which has a detrimental bearing on the provision of public transport in the area.

Therefore, in terms of the practical tests that the commissioners will apply on receiving applications for licences under this procedure, we believe that it is better to leave them this scope rather than putting in an unreasonable restriction by saying that the route must compete "directly".

I appreciate the strength of the Parliamentary Secretary's argument. Indeed, to shorten our proceedings, perhaps I may make part of his argument as well as my own by saying that that argument is strengthened because there is no appeal against refusal of an application under this clause. We understand that the commissioners' decision is final. However, if the commissioners say that it competes in some direct way and therefore do not issue a road service licence under clause 9, that does not mean that the applicant has failed in his application. It merely means that, without any further action on his part the application will go forward to be considered under section 9 of the Act.

Therefore, I believe that the balance of advantage lies with the wider definition. That would enable the commissioners to exercise a judgment on what we are agreed is the narrow area of whether the application was suitable for the short-cut procedure or whether it should be considered under the full procedure. I believe that commissioners can do that very effectively.

I cannot agree with the spirit of part of the Parliamentary Secretary's approach. He tells us that commissioners have a primary duty to protect stage carriages. Of course they have. But it comes ill from him or his right hon. Friend to tell us that, when they themselves are proposing legislation which cuts down the ability of the commissioners to carry out the primary duty to protect passenger services. It is the Government who are cutting back that line of defence. We may have quite sincere differences as to whether the public passenger transport service interest is best served by having commissioners, but we JPEG 869 should not be arguing that aspect of their role in this clause.

Like the Parliamentary Secretary and the Government, we believe that if there is to be a short-cut procedure it must be subject to rather special tests. What we are arguing about is the width of the test on the first leg. We believe that a more practical approach, which will be better understood by the commissioners, the public and the operators, is that under the alternative second test of the clause there should be the wider definition that we propose.
