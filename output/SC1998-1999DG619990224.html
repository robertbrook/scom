Draft National Assembly for Wales (Transfer of Functions) Order 1999

4.30 pm

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Alun Michael)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft National Assembly for Wales (Transfer of Functions) Order 1999.

The order is the remaining piece in a rather large jigsaw. Since the last election, the Government have made it clear that the Assembly will take over the functions of the Secretary of State for Wales and the staff and property that go with his Department. Our purpose has always been to do away with the government of Wales by an unaccountable and unelected hierarchy at the Welsh Office and to replace it with a new and democratic Assembly.

The Committee will be aware that the formal arrangements for establishing the Assembly are largely in place. The order completes the picture by giving the Assembly the great bulk of its functions. As it reflects our long-standing policy, there are few surprises in it. Every statutory function that I exercise will, with a small handful of exceptions, pass to the Assembly.

In essence, we are considering a list of functions. Life is, however, never quite that straightforward. It is certainly possible quickly to identify the key functions that I exercise in major policy areas and which the Assembly will need. Beyond that, an exhaustive and painstaking exercise was needed to identify not only the functions that I and the Welsh Office exercise but those that we have not used and on which the Assembly might take a separate view. The result is a broad and comprehensive document, which sets out the powers that will enable the Assembly to meet the expectations of its electorate.

Inevitably, the order contains important functions and I will mention six. First, it transfers to the Assembly a full range of powers and duties in relation to the national health service in Wales under the National Health Service Act 1977, the NHS and Community Care Act 1990 and other health statutes. Secondly, the Education Reform Act 1988 and later education Acts give the Assembly similarly broad powers over all aspects of education in Wales. Thirdly, the Welsh Development Agency Act 1975, the Industrial Development Act 1982 and several other Acts give the Assembly powers to support the Welsh economy and to improve job creation. Fourthly, the Environment Act 1995 and a host of other environmental statutes contain powers that make the Assembly the guardian of the environment in Wales. Fifthly, the Highways Act 1980 gives the Assembly full control of the trunk road network in Wales. Sixthly, 4 the Agriculture Act 1967 and many other agricultural Acts will allow the Assembly to take charge of agricultural matters in Wales and tackle the problems that rural areas face. Alongside those powers are many others in the same and other important areas, such as housing, planning and local government. For brevity, I will not dwell on them all now, but I will be happy to answer questions, particularly when I close the debate.

Mr. Michael
: I shall take notice of that remark, Mr. Welsh, and make my reply short.

I have pointed out why the circumstances about which the hon. Member for Ribble Valley spoke could not arise. There must be a good relationship between the Assembly, its decision makers, the Secretaries in the Assembly and Ministers in Whitehall, both at official and elected level. That should ensure what the legislation is all about—devolution. It is not about separatism or moving Wales off coast. It is about Wales being able to take appropriate decisions in an intelligent manner, in cognisance of its own interests and its relationship with other parts of the United Kingdom. I am confident that the fears expressed by the hon. Gentleman are misplaced because of the arrangements that the Assembly will follow.

It may help the Committee if I outline how the order works. For the most part, the transfer of functions is straightforward. Where I am able to exercise a function now, the Assembly will be able to do so. An unqualified provision in the order means that the Assembly will have sole responsibility in Wales for the relevant functions after 1 July.

In many cases, legislation requires the Secretary of State to act jointly with other Ministers in exercising specific functions. For other functions, I have to seek the Treasury's consent before acting. As an elected body, the Assembly will be accountable to the people of Wales so, where possible, we have removed requirements for joint action and for Treasury consent. That point was decided by Parliament in paragraph 9 of schedule 3 of the Government of Wales Act 1998.

6 In a few instances, however, it would not be prudent to make such changes. For example, the Assembly could not sensibly make separate decisions about bodies that serve the whole of Great Britain, such as the Meat and Livestock Commission. In those cases, the order transfers to the Assembly only my share of a joint function, leaving it to act jointly with the other Minister concerned. For example, appointments to the Meat and Livestock Commission will be made jointly by the Assembly, my right hon. Friend the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food and the appropriate Minister in the Scottish Parliament.

Mr. Evans

Mr. Michael
: If I may just finish this point: In terms of the arrangements that remain jointly with the Treasury, the Assembly will only need the Treasury's consent for the form of public accounts and matters relating to public sector pensions, borrowing in foreign currency and certain statutory fees. Those are matters which are wholly or partly outside the Welsh block.

Devolution does not mean separation. United Kingdom Departments will still need to act in Wales on many matters that are not to be transferred to the Assembly—for example, social security, broadcasting and prisons. Furthermore, UK Ministers will still need to exercise some functions alongside the Assembly, and in those cases the order makes the functions concurrent.

Some areas are not referred to in the order because they will be dealt with by concordats, which are being developed between the Welsh Office and other Government Departments in preparation for the Assembly. Those relationships will, I believe, be productive and positive. They are necessary so as to ensure that Wales is not deprived of UK or Great Britain-wide programmes. For instance, my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Education and Employment is reponsible for the Employment Service in Wales and will remain so. However, my Department also operates a range of training and guidance services, which rely on the same statutory authority—in that case, section 2 of the Employment and Training Act 1973. The order thus provides for that function to be JPEG 7 concurrent. Both my right hon. Friend and the Assembly will be able to exercise it in Wales, but neither will need the consent of the other to do so. Those types of co-operative arrangements will not cause a problem.

Mr. Michael

We are following the best practice of the arrangements that currently exist by formalising them and making them more open to scrutiny, as many other things will be as a direct result of the Assembly coming into being. I assure my hon. Friend that the specific understanding of the operation of concordats that he seeks will be facilitated by their scrutiny by interested Members of Parliament and Assembly Members.

Mr. Evans

Mr. Michael
: My understanding is that they will not be open to judicial review, because they are arrangements within government. I can give the hon. Gentleman more detailed response later, if he wants. The arrangements are intended to facilitate the machinery of government, and I hope that they will do so in a constructive and positive manner. They will not change the actual responsibilities of the respective Departments or of the Assembly, which I think is the sort of matter subject to judicial review; they are arrangements about how powers will be exercised by the respective bodies.

As I said, duplication is theoretically possible; in practice, it is highly unlikely in elements, such as the Employment Service, for which the DfEE takes responsibility. I understand the point made by the hon. Member for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy (Mr. Llwyd), but that is more of a possibility than a real problem.

If there is a problem in future, is there an arrangement whereby the Secretary of State would have precedence over the First Secretary, or would that have to be resolved in some other way?

Mr. Michael
: Where there is a power for both to exercise—as in the example raised—there is no legal system of resolution; however, there is a system through the concordats and arrangements. It is not the law but good relations and administration on which we depend for that. It should be understood that an awful lot of government depends on good relationships and understandings. The concordats are meant to formalise the necessary arrangements to ensure that we have practical guidance for the operation of the machinery of government.

I am conscious that I must press on, as I know that many hon. Members will wish to speak. I should at this point say something about the most complex provisions in the order: those relating to water. As we recognised in the White Paper, "A Voice for Wales", water policy requires special treatment under devolution. Rivers do not respect the border between England and Wales; nor do water companies. The order makes special provision for many functions under the Water Industry Act 1991 and the Water Resources Act 1991.

The Water Industry Act 1991 contains the great majority of powers regulating the water industry. The order gives the Assembly powers as regards Dŵr Cymru, and allows my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions to retain them as regards Severn Trent. That JPEG 9 reflects the respective roles of the Welsh Office and of the Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions at present.

In other words, we are building on what has worked in practice. That means that the Assembly will have jurisdiction over Dŵr Cymru's operations in Herefordshire and Cheshire, while my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions oversees the Severn Trent's activities in Montgomeryshire. That is the only sensible way to proceed. It would be nonsense for one undertaker to be subject to oversight from two different authorities. Again, I think that any problems will be dealt with by good will.

Mr. Livsey
: Will that also apply to the water that is being transferred from the Elan valley to the Severn Trent authority? That agreement was struck some four or five years ago by Dŵr Cymru. A lot of water is being transferred within Wales from the Dŵr Cymru area to the Severn Trent area.

Mr. Michael
: I shall take advice on that point, but I believe that the whole of Dŵr Cymru's operations come under the regulation of the Welsh Office. Where there is an interface between the two, it will be a question of which is the primary organisation. However, I am sure that the hon. Gentleman will acknowledge that there is no problem at present, nor has one arisen. We are reflecting in the responsibilities of the Assembly and the relevant Ministry what happens at present.

The Water Resources Act 1991 contains comprehensive powers to safeguard and manage water resources. The Assembly will need these for Wales; they are a key part of its wider environmental responsibilities. But it will need to recognise that some of its decisions with regard to the rivers Dee, Severn and Wye and their tributaries can easily have an effect on England. For instance, if the Assembly were to authorise discharges into one of those rivers, it would affect water quality on the English side of the border.

I am sure that the Assembly will act responsibly in this respect, taking into account English views and needs before acting. I hope to establish informal means of resolving any disputes by concordat. However, in extreme cases, we need to ensure that the Assembly does not cause serious damage to English interests; that has no place in a sensible scheme of devolution.

The Secretary of State will retain a reserve power to intervene where there is a risk of such damage and to exercise the function himself. In doing so, he will also have to act reasonably. The order lists the functions to which the reserve power is to apply.

Mr. Llwyd
: How many instances of the reserve power are there in the transfer of functions to the National Assembly?

Mr. Michael
: Very few. I am drawing attention to a specific power because in general, as I said at the beginning, the power transfers either wholly in part. For instance, a joint power between the Secretary of State for Wales and the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food will continue to be exercised 10 jointly but by the Assembly and the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food. That is not reserving the power, but exercising it jointly. Reserve powers are being kept to an absolute minimum. That is why I am drawing attention to specific powers.

Mr. Llwyd
: But how many are there in the whole transfer process?

Mr. Michael
: Few. I have read every line of the transfer process, but I cannot say that I kept a calculator on one side and added them up. I shall write to the hon. Gentleman with the precise number, which I know is not large. [ Interruption.] In fact, I am told, with the speed of light, that only some functions under the Water Resources Act—the area that I have been talking about—are affected. That is some 35 powers out of 5,000.

Mr. Llwyd
: So somebody has read the order line by line. [ Laughter.]

Mr. Michael
: Oh yes. I assure the hon. Gentleman that this is not a Maastricht treaty job. I have been through the order and the technical guide line by line, but I do not pretend to have retained every line of those documents. However, I have satisfied myself on the detail of the order before us.

Mr. Evans
: May I ask the right hon. Gentleman for another concrete example of when reserve powers may be used? If the Assembly decided that beef on the bone should be allowed, but the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food decided that it should not, where would the power finally rest?

Mr. Michael
: The power in Wales would lie with the Assembly.

My hon. Friend the Member for Bridgend (Mr. Griffiths), who I am pleased to see in Committee, and very close to the end of the document, assured the House on 22 July last year that the Assembly would have sole control over the construction and enlargement of reservoirs in Wales. The order fully discharges that assurance. In no circumstances will a United Kingdom Minister be able to authorise such works. That will be a matter for the Assembly alone. I suspect that that may provide the reassurance, by a different route, that the hon. Member for Brecon and Radnorshire (Mr. Livsey) was seeking a few moments ago.

I propose that the order should come into force on 1 July this year. That is the date on which the Assembly will acquire the great bulk of its functions and it is the same day on which the Scottish Parliament will acquire its powers. The choice of that date reflects the urgency with which we want to establish the Assembly.

I accept that a lead-in time of seven weeks is very short when compared with the 11 months that was allowed at the time of the local government reorganisation. However, hon. Members will appreciate that the date must be at the beginning of a month because the Welsh Office's management accounting system, like most others, operates on a monthly basis. Attempting to transfer responsibility for public expenditure to the Assembly mid-month JPEG 11 would risk making it impossible to account properly for expenditure. Both sets of accounts could be called into question and inadequate information would be available to auditors.

We must ensure that the Assembly's own structures are fully in place when its functions are conferred upon it. Following the elections, all Members of the Assembly will need to be sworn in and the Assembly will have to hold its own elections. The First Secretary will have to choose his or her Cabinet, following which the Assembly must establish its system of subject Committees. The Assembly itself will have to put in place the new arrangements, so a transfer of functions on 1st June would not be sensible. However, I do not want the Assembly to have to wait until August or beyond to begin its work in earnest, although it will undoubtedly begin to consider and debate matters before the transfer of functions on 1 July.

The order is at the heart of the new government of Wales. It gives the Assembly comprehensive powers to improve public services, the economy and the environment and to do so solely on the basis of its democratic mandate. I commend the order to the Committee.

Thus, the issue is allowed for in the primary legislation. The necessary arrangements for other international obligations are made in other primary legislation. For example, the European convention on human rights was incorporated into UK law by an Act that was passed recently, so its requirements would of course have to be observed by the Assembly, as by other bodies within Wales.
