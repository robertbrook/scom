Trends in Employment

1.

Mr. Dafydd Wigley
(Caernarfon)
: If he will make a statement on trends in employment in manufacturing in Wales during the past 12 months. [48379]

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Peter Hain)
: The number of employee jobs in manufacturing in Wales increased by 6,100–2.9 per cent.—between March 1997 and March 1998 to a total of 214,000–21.8 per cent. of all employee jobs.

Mr. Wigley
: I thank the Minister for that answer. I also thank the officers of Merthyr Tydfil county borough council for the welcome that we received. New Economic Agenda for Wales

11.34 am

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Ron Davies)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee has considered the matter of the new economic agenda for Wales.

I am delighted that the Welsh Grand Committee is meeting in Merthyr Tydfil. You will recall, Mr. Jones, that our programme of meetings so far has given us a glimpse of the geography of Wales. We had our first meeting at Mold in the north, we then went to Carmarthen in the west, and today we are in Merthyr Tydfil, in the heart of the south Wales valleys. I hope that the way in which we have moved around Wales has benefited hon. Members and the communities that we have visited, and that there will be opportunities to visit other areas of Wales in the coming months.

At the start of our proceedings, Mr. Jones, you paid tribute to the officers and staff of Merthyr Tydfil county borough council and to the people of Merthyr Tydfil for their hospitality. I should like to add my thanks for the ready co-operation that we at the Welsh Office have experienced from the people of Merthyr Tydfil and the members and staff of the county borough council. My hon. Friend the Member for Islwyn (Mr. Touhig) referred earlier to the Wales new deal and we should in fact give the people of Merthyr Tydfil a double "Thank you", because the town is also hosting the Wales new deal advisory task force.

The subject of today's debate is the Welsh economy, specifically the agenda that I launched last Tuesday with the Under-Secretary of State for Wales, my hon. Friend the Member for Neath (Mr. Hain). That agenda aims to take the economy forward and to spread prosperity throughout Wales. It is fitting that such a debate should take place here in Merthyr Tydfil, a town that exemplifies both the economic successes of the past and, unfortunately, the economic anxieties of the present.

At the turn of the century, Merthyr Tydfil had an international reputation as the bustling and innovative epicentre of the coal and steel industries. It now finds itself too often portrayed in the media as a capital of unemployment and sickness. That sort of decline will be familiar to many hon. Members who represent other parts of Wales. However, it is far from being the whole picture, and it must be set alongside more positive experiences such as the attraction of inward investment, the continued strength and variety of our manufacturing plants, the persistence and the success of our modern steel industry and the growth of new sectors such as information technology, film and animation.

Despite those successes, it is evident that we are not yet free of the virus of decline. It clings to us tenaciously and can recur suddenly and unexpectedly, often after long periods of economic good health. We have come a long way from the crises of the 1960s, 1970s and early 1980s, when so much of Wales' traditional employment disappeared. However, a great deal remains to be done.
