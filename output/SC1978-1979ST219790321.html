DRAFT MEAT AND LIVESTOCK COMMISSION LEVY SCHEME (CONFIRMATION) ORDER 1979

10.30 a.m.

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. E. S. Bishop)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft Meat and Livestock Commission Levy Scheme (Confirmation) Order 1979.

The effect of this scheme is, firstly, to revoke and replace the Meat and Livestock Commission levy scheme 1968, consolidating various amendments. It thus provides for the continuation of the system under which the Commission is enabled to impose charges to meet its expenses and to recover such charges. Secondly, the scheme makes two changes to the existing provisions. These are an increase in the maximum charge leviable in respect of each head of sheep from 8p to 16p, and an extension of the leviable charges to exports of livestock, excluding pure-bred breeding cattle.

The statutory maximum rates of levy were last increased four year ago, in 1975, and it is to the credit of the Commission that it has been able for so long to work within the present limits while maintaining and developing a wide range of services to the industry. I should like to say something about these services before discussing the changes in the levy scheme.

When the Meat and Livestock Commission was set up in 1968, the pattern of the meat industry's development was uneven. The Government believed that the Commission had an important role to play in furthering the development of the livestock and livestock products industries, to the benefit of producers and consumers. Subsequent events proved this to be true. The Commission now provides a range of services to the industry which has brought, I am sure many will agree, substantial improvements which were unlikely to have occurred had matters been left to the operation of normal market arrangements.

Since 1975 the Commission has continued its livestock improvement work, its 4 marketing and economic services, fatstock certification, weighing and classification services and its support for scientific projects. All have played essential roles in the industry's progress and development. Fresh ground has been broken in several important areas. For example, there has been the start of the retail meat price reporting service, supplying information now used by news media all over the country. The meat industry development and advisory service, operated in conjunction with the Meat Research Institute, has been launched. Also, perhaps of most significance, the Meat Promotion Executive, launched nearly four years ago, has steadily developed its role as a powerful stimulus to the demand for British meat.

Carcase classification schemes for cattle, sheep and pigs have continued as a major element in the Commission's programme. But while the pig scheme has maintained its success, with nearly 80 per cent, of clean pigs slaughtered being covered in the year 1977–78, the numbers classified under the other two schemes have been at a much lower level. The significance of these schemes is underlined by the prospect of an EEC classification scheme for beef and, possibly, one for sheep. The Government share the Commission's view that the development of our British schemes should make a positive contribution to the introduction of any EEC schemes.

The Commission is required to present annual reports and accounts to Ministers, and copies of these are laid before Parliament. The report for the year ending 31 March 1978 shows in considerable detail the wide range of the Commission's activities, and for this reason I do not propose to dwell on them further. I should like to take this opportunity of commending the Chairman and the other Commissioners on the way in which they have discharged their statutory duties.

There has been one further development since 1975 which should perhaps be mentioned. The production and distribution advisory committees provided for in the Agriculture Act 1967 failed to meet the wishes either of committee members themselves or of the organisations represented. The Commission concluded that there was a need for a review of the structure, and this was duly carried out. As hon. Members may recall, the outcome was that under the Agriculture (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1976, these committees were abolished and replaced by a JPEG 5 system of direct consultation of organisations representing the different interests in the production and distribution sectors of the industry. I think that hon. Members who remember the Act, particularly the hon. Member for Westmorland (Mr. Jopling), who took part in the debate, will recall that the Consumers' Committee was, however, retained.

As a result of these changes, a widely-based industry forum was set up, and this is now the body which the Commission customarily consults on matters of interest to the industry.

I now turn to the crucial issue of the Commission's financial position. Like everyone else, it is beset by inflationary pressures which are placing a severe strain on its resources. During recent months it has accordingly made a rigorous examination of its level of expenditure on levy-funded activities and has made a number of cuts in its budget for the financial year 1978–79. Despite this, it is estimated that there will be a deficit for the year of some £450,000, which will come out of its reserves. It has further estimated that in default of increased levy income it will incur deficits of £530,000 in 1979–80 and of £850,000 in 1980–81, making a total of £1,830,000. These deficits must be met by increased income from levies. Clearly, the present state of affairs cannot be allowed to continue.

The Commission therefore proposes to raise the levies as follows. The levy on cattle will be increased under existing statutory powers from 78p to the statutory maximum of 90p—a rise of 12p. Under the draft order now before the House, the levy on sheep would be increased from 8p to 16p and half of the revenue from this increase would go for meat promotion. In addition, the Commission intends to raise the pig levy by 8p for meat promotion purposes. The increase on pigs can, like that on cattle, be contained within the existing statutory maximum.

These proposed increases, and indeed the level of the Commission's levy-funded activities, have been the subject of very thorough consultation with the industry during recent months. There is a wide measure of agreement on the proposed increases to cover expenditure on meat promotion. There has been criticism from some sectors of the industry, both on the general level of the Commission's activities and on the size of the proposed 6 increase on the maximum rate for sheep. The Government appreciate the force of these criticisms, coming as they do from sectors of the industry which are themselves under economic pressure, but we believe that it is essential that the Commission should be given the means to get its financial resources back into equilibrium.

The search for economies will, however, continue, and I welcome the Commission's resolve in this matter. Its decision to set up an independent study group to review its commercial beef and sheep recording services—which take up a significant proportion of its resources—is very much indicative of that resolve.

I turn now to the other new provision in the levy scheme: the proposal to extend the levy to exports of live animals. This is being introduced because the Commission and the industry believe amongst other things, that as exporters benefit from the Commission's various services in much the same way as do those engaged exclusively in the home market, they should bear the levy on an equal basis. So this is a measure to restore equity in the raising of revenue from the industry.

There is one exception to this provision. Exporters of dairy cattle intended for breeding do not benefit from the work of the Commission, and it would be inequitable for them to bear the levy. For practicality of administration, it is proposed that the exemption should apply to all pure-bred breeding animals of the bovine species.

I am, of course, aware that exporters of livestock may regard this levy as something of a disincentive. But I think that it should be placed in its proper perspective. The levy will represent a tiny fraction of the value of the animal. I do not consider that a charge of the order proposed will be at all unreasonable, bearing in mind the desirability of treating all trade on an equitable basis.

It is entirely understandable that, in a period of economic difficulty for the meat and livestock industry—which, after all, is the Commission's paymaster—there should be considerable pressure on the Commission to reduce its level of expenditure substantially. But I believe all sectors of the industry recognise that such a body is needed, and that it must have sufficient resources to do its job. I believe also that the Commission is responsive to the need for economy, consistent with an adequate JPEG 7 level of services. This order will enable it to recover its financial balance, so to speak, and thus enable it to continue its task of promoting the greater efficiency of the meat and livestock industry in this country.

We are still hopeful of securing further improvements in this year's CAP price fixing negotiations. The 5 per cent devaluation will cut MCAs by about 20 per cent.

With regard to the export of animals—my hon. Friend the Member for Hemel Hempstead (Mr. Corbett) referred to exports—there is the wider aspect of animal welfare. We have taken the view that we should ensure that the welfare regulations are complied with and strictly enforced. However, this matter will be under consideration by the House on another day.

With regard to the levy on the export of live animals, a number of organisations have been consulted by the MLC and by the agriculture Departments. The National Cattle Breeders' Association, the National 18 Pig Breeders' Association and others with an interest in the export of live animals have made their views known.

We and the MLC claim that the levy will correct an anomaly by requiring exporters to pay for the services they receive from the MLC in the same way as other meat traders. This seems to us to be a matter of common justice within the industry and between the exporters and those who believe in slaughter at home.

The hon. Member for Westmorland asked about the exclusion for breeding cattle and why the order was limited to pure bred animals. The exclusion was introduced to cover dairy animals which, as I have said, do not benefit from the activities of the MLC. The wording to which the hon. Gentleman objects reflects the wording of the customs tariff. The limitation to pure bred animals also reflects the difficulties of administering a more broadly defined category for exclusion.

Mr. Jopling
: I thought that the Minister said that the classification by the Department of Trade allowed a wider grouping. That was exactly my point. It does not. This exclusion for breeding animals ought to be wider to include pure-bred and crossbred animals for breeding, because of current and growing practice within the industry. The Minister cannot justify that by saying that the definition gives a broader classification. It does nothing of the sort.

Mr. Bishop
: Perhaps I can clarify the position. The MLC in response to pressure from sections of the industry, has taken the view that, since exporters of animals may be assumed to benefit from the general services in much the same way as traders engaged solely in the home market, it would be equitable for them to bear the levy at the normal rates. For ease of administration and other reasons, the MLC considers that, with one exception, the levy should apply to all live animals whether exported for immediate slaughter, fattening or breeding.

The exception relates to pure-bred breeding animals of the bovine species. Representations have been made by the National Cattle Breeders' Association and the British Friesian Cattle Society that, as dairy animals exported for breeding do not benefit from the MLC services, they should be exempt. That was considered to be a reasonable objection because, in JPEG 19 practice, it might be difficult to differentiate between beef animals and dairy animals. The exemption is worded to cover both types. Cross-bred animals are clearly more difficult to identify. Therefore, they have not been brought within the scope of the exemption.

The National Pig Breeders' Association also objected to the levy, with particular reference to breeding pigs. My right hon. Friend, who received similar correspondence from Mr. Manchester, appreciates the pig breeders' concern about the introduction of the levy on exports. But the purpose of the measure, proposed by the Meat and Livestock Commission, was to meet a widespread concern in the industry that exporters who benefit from the Commission's services do not contribute to the cost of those services in the same way as other sections of the industry.

The Commission provides a range of services—livestock improvement activities, recording, testing and market information services—which are of direct benefit to exporters, including exporters of breeding pigs. The purpose of excluding pure-bred breeding cattle from the application of the levy was to meet the objection to which I have referred. The same consideration does not apply to exporters of breeding pigs or sheep. We do not, therefore, feel justified in accepting the view put forward by the National Pig Breeders' Association.

Mr. Jopling
: I must press the Minister on this matter. Surely he acknowledges that it is unfair that the man exporting pedigree Aberdeen Angus heifers—a breed used exclusively for beef—should be allowed to do so free of levy, whereas the person exporting pedigree breeding pigs should have to pay? There cannot be any justification for that anomaly. It must be unfair. If that is acknowledged, surely it would be better to exclude all breeding animals from the levy, and that could be done by a reference to the price. If the price at which the breeding animals had been exchanged was significantly higher than their slaughter value, that would clearly establish that they were breeding animals. Would not that be a better way of going about the matter? The Minister must acknowledge that there is a serious anomaly here between beef cattle and pure-bred sheep and pigs.

Mr. Bishop
: This really is a matter for the MLC.

Mr. Jopling
: It is for the right hon. Gentleman. It is his order.

Mr. Bishop
: We are putting forward the recommendations of the MLC. In practice, it is difficult to differentiate between beef and dairy animals. That is why this wording has been used to cover both types. Cross-bred animals, which are more difficult to identify, are not brought within the scope of the exemption. The term

"pure bred breeding animals of the bovine species"

is not defined in the scheme. It is a matter for the Commission and for Customs and Excise to decide how this shall be operated.

I pay tribute to the Commission for having dealt with aspects which were of great concern to the various parts of the industry—the abattoirs, the slaughtering industry, the breeders and others. I believe that the MLC, whose existence no one challenges, must be allowed to continue its work, albeit in close consultation with the industry, and have sufficient income to enable it to do so. The levy rates which it is proposed should be introduced next month are, in our estimation, the minimum necessary to combat the financial pressures which the Commission is now meeting. Neither the Commission nor anyone else can foretell how long these rates will last, but the expressions of support for the scheme from hon. Members will be an encouragement to the Commission and its staff as they seek to maintain the high standard of service to our meat and livestock industry.

Whatever the future holds for the industry, this kind of investment will be for the benefit of our industry and will help to be competitive in the future.

I trust that the Committee will support the order.
