Mr. Eadie
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the Coal Industry (Borrowing Powers) Order 1974.

Section 2(1) of the Coal Industry Act 1973 substituted a new Section 1(3) of the Coal industry Act 1965 to provide that the limit on the borrowings of the National Coal Board, and its wholly-owned subsidiaries, can be raised from an initial £550 million to a total not exceeding £700 million by Statutory instrument. The time has now come to raise the borrowing limit to the full £700 million, and the order is designed to do this.

In order to avoid any confusion, I should, perhaps, emphasise that the borrowing limit has nothing to do with any losses made by the board or with Government assistance to meet those losses. Like any other large private or public industry, the NCB has to engage in major investment projects which do not produce revenue for a number of years and has to borrow to cover the period between capital expenditure and the revenue resulting from the projects. Indeed, the time between beginning to invest in a new mine and full production is very long.

The background to this order is that one of the first steps taken by the Government was to establish the joint Government-union-Coal Board examination of the future role of the industry. The interim report which was published in June brought to an end more than 10 years of run-down by endorsing the NCB's proposal for a massive increase in its investment. The sum of £600 million 14 is to be spent over the next 10 years on 42 million tons of new capacity derived from new mines and the development of existing ones. This £600 million is on top of previous plans to spend about £80 million a year; in other words, investment will be almost doubled and will amount to about £1,400 million over the next 10 years.

During the years of run-down and competition from oil, the National Coal Board was unable to build up any reserves. It would be unrealistic now to expect it to finance this expansion programme out of earnings in the next 10 years. No private company would try to bear such a burden. Even if market conditions permitted, financing this crash programme of long-life projects would be an unfair burden on present consumers. The NCB will therefore properly have to borrow much of the money and pay it back over a longer period.

In addition, all of us concerned with the industry welcome the fact that the NCB will in the next year or so be paying out compensation to pneumoconiosis sufferers. Negotiations between the Board and the unions are going well and the Government have made it clear that they will contribute towards relieving the board's finances of the burden of the past in respect of existing sufferers. At least the initial payments will be financed by borrowing, and I can assure the Committee there will be no unnecessary delays.

The particular timing of the order is governed largely by the board's heavy borrowing needs in the near future. In addition to its rising investment programme and the pneumoconiosis scheme to which I have already referred, the board will need to borrow in order to raise its share of development expenditure on North Sea oil discoveries. Although the board's North Sea oil interests are to be taken over in due course by the proposed British National Oil Corporation, the board meanwhile needs to borrow to raise its share of the finance needed.

There is one thing which I should take up with the hon. Gentleman—and here I am making a wholly non-party point. He referred to the 10-year rundown of the coal industry, although I suspect that it is rather longer. But I honestly do not think that Governments of either party need to don the white sheet and talk about running down the coal industry, castigating themselves for lack of foresight. On the contrary, Governments of both parties did an enormous amount and spent large sums of the taxpayers' money in slowing the rundown of the coal industry during the period of security and abundance of oil.

We took massive action to prevent the industry from running down much faster than it did. One has only to indicate the measures that were taken: the fuel oil tax introduced in 1953; the ban on imports of foreign coal when we could have had much cheaper Russian or American coal; substantial revenue subsidies of various kinds, to which I shall come in a moment. All these were intended to slow down what was seen at the time by Governments of all parties to be a long-term trend in coal as against other forms of energy.

The Conservative Government did a great deal in the 1973 Act. Perhaps that Act was the high water mark of Government support for the coal industry, in order to make sure that its rundown was slower and accompanied by far less social deprivation than would otherwise have been the case. The 1973 Act followed extensive consultations between the miners' union and the Coal Board, and subsequently with the Government. It ushered in what was then seen as a new era of joint participation in the management of the industry. Wholly new procedures were set up. There was a joint review of collieries to examine the question of economic closures. There was the agreement that real increases in wages should come from increased productivity. A joint sales programme was set up. Every member of the industry was encouraged to become JPEG 17 a salesman for coal. There was the Joint Policy Advisory Committee whereby the union at all levels was given the opportunity to comment on policies that were being pursued and proposed by the industry.

The last Government weighed in with massive financial support. Capital was written off to the tune of £475 million. Over the five years from 1973 to 1978, Government support for social costs will amount to nearly a quarter of a billion pounds; £100 million was provided for additional coal-burn by the generating authorities; £110 million was to be provided for extra coal stocks; £45 million was to be provided for additional coking coal subsidy; and over £200 million for regional grants specifically to support the Coal Board's activities in the peripheral areas.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Leicester, South (Mr. Boardman) the then Minister for Industry, said when speaking to a Standing Committee on Statutory Instruments, on 18th July 1973:

"With the Coal Industry Act and now with the formulation of the new scheme the Government have fulfilled their undertaking to provide a new framework in which the industry can work towards the resolution of its problems. We may fairly claim to have given the industry the tools to enable it to establish itself on a secure, long-term basis and to make an appropriate contribution to the country's energy needs."—[OFFICIAL REPORT, Standing Committee on Statutory Instruments, 18th July 1973; c. 10.]

That was in July 1973, almost exactly a year ago. I have been re-reading the debates on the 1973 Act, and one is very struck by the unanimity which united both sides of the House and the Committee when the Act was introduced. The measure was supported by the present Secretary of State, who was then leading for the Opposition.

How short-lived proved to be that euphoria of 1973. By the end of the year the industry, first as the result of the overtime ban and then of the complete stoppage, had brought the country perilously near its knees, had provoked a General Election, and had contributed to the fall of a democratically elected Government. I do not want to go over past history, as we must look to the future. Here we are again with another great plan for the coal industry, this time the Coal Board's ambitious "Plan for Coal", which has been endorsed, as the hon. Gentleman has 18 already mentioned, by the interim report of the Coal Industry Examination. Once again we are faced with a massive new injection of money, with capital investment rising by an additional £600 million over the 10 years 1974–84. There are in addition further important social advances with the proposals for pneumoconiosis, which all of us must welcome.

It is very tempting to sing the song, "Tell us the old, old story", but I believe—and here I hope I shall carry the whole Committee with me—that it would be totally wrong, as we are now in an entirely new situation so far as the coal industry is concerned, to say that conditions today are the same as they were a year ago and that this is merely the industry coming back for yet further financial support. Following the massive rise in oil prices, the future of the coal industry has been entirely transformed, from having to be an industry subsidised by the taxpayer on a very substantial scale in order to stay in business at all and always taking a smaller and smaller share of the country's rising energy consumption, to one which can now look forward to an era in which it can compete fairly and freely without having to rely on subsidies to any substantial extent from the Exchequer.

Coal is now, and can remain if sensible policies are pursued, fully competitive with oil.
