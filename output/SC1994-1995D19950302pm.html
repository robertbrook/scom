Ms Hilary Armstrong
(Durham, North-West)
: Welcome back to the Chair, Mr. Winterton. I am happy that you are here so that we can make good progress with the rest of the Bill. My voice is improving slightly.

The clause refers to profit-related pay and the way in which the Government are changing the guidelines on how it can be claimed. My attention has been drawn to a survey that was carried out by the Pay and Benefits Bulletin along with Bucks Consultants. They questioned organisations on a range of issues dealing with performance-related pay. They asked 198 organisations to specify the single most important objective for their organisation to introduce profit-related pay, and received 169 usable responses. The bulletin states that:

"the most commonly noted aims are those that focus on the benefit of PRP to the workforce in terms of reward or simply tax relief".

I understand that that was not the original aim. It seems to confirm one of our concerns that the measure would be used by companies as a means of avoiding paying tax that they would otherwise have had to pay. The bulletin continues:

"Profit-related pay has been adapted by employers … and … changes in the legislation in the past two years have done little to damage its perceived flexibility … Half of all respondents preparing or running schemes have used the legislation to gain tax relief for payments from an existing profit-sharing bonus or to launch a new bonus arrangement. Just over a quarter have replaced a predetermined portion of each employee's basic pay with an element of PRP, so that even if the profit-related pay generated does no more than replace the salary foregone, employees experience a net increase in pay because the payment is made free of tax. Another one in five organisations has substituted PRP payout for an annual pay increase."

In other words, it is being used by some employers as a means of avoiding paying basic increases every year. The Minister must respond as it seems that the taxpayer is again subsidising the employer. The employer is not meeting his obligations to his work force to ensure that it is being rewarded from the profits that the company is making. If the pay is profit-related, the company must be making profits and it should be taking into account the recompense that should be made to the employer from that profit.

578 Some employers are using the scheme to avoid their responsibilities by ensuring that they receive tax exemption. Therefore, the taxpayer is ending up paying for the increased performance of the company and is receiving a due reward. That was not the intention of the scheme, nor is it what was meant by the initial flexibility of the scheme. What will the Minister do to ensure that employers do not use profit-related pay on that basis? In answer to what effect the changes in last year's Budget would have on the operation of their schemes, the respondents all said that it would have little effect on the popularity with employers of profit-related pay. Individuals further down the chain of command are powerless to affect their remuneration, and therefore their tax relief, under the taxation provisions. I contend that such schemes do the opposite. PRP is no excuse for the proper involvement in the destiny of companies or employees at every level within them. That is the way to ensure the increased profitability of companies, rather than under this varied system of tax relief for some employees, especially as such employees have a limited ability to affect the performance of companies.

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Sir George Young)
: This has been a helpful debate about profit-related pay. From the Government's point of view, the policy has been successful. When the Government introduced PRP, their primary goal was to promote the establishment of pay mechanisms in a number of companies that linked part of the pay of employees to the performance of the company.

One can measure the success of the policy by the take-up. About 2 million employees are now in PRP schemes. About 8,000 companies have introduced PRP schemes. There were two objectives behind the policy. One was to promote an identity of interest between the employees of a company and the fortunes of a company. No one would object to that. Involving employees in the fortunes of a company is a goal of its own. There are other components of the policy, as the hon. Member for Monklands, East (Mrs. Liddell) suggested. The involvement of employees in decision making is not the only component, but it is important.

The other objective, which hon. Lady mentioned, was to cope with the number of people employed in companies over the trade cycle. If a company's labour costs reduce automatically during a recession, the employer is not confronted with the option of reducing the number of people who work for him to reduce his labour costs. Because firms covered by PRP find that labour costs reduce automatically as their profits reduce, they are not confronted with the harsh option of reducing labour costs by cutting their work forces.

Those are the two broad objectives of the policy. The cost is about £500 million, which is linked to the take-up. The fact that the cost is high does not mean that there have been no associated benefits or that the policy has not been cost effective.

What do we know about the impact of the scheme? As to the first objective—promoting an identity of interest between the employees and the fortunes of the company—most available studies of profit-sharing schemes support the view that the schemes increase employee involvement and that they are associated with modest increases in productivity.

The information about the other objective—the impact of PRP on pay flexibility and employment—is less conclusive. That is partly because in the complex and 582 varied world of commerce it is sometimes difficult to establish any clear relationships between cause and effect. It was always expected that the benefits associated with that part of the policy would be more apparent in the longer term.

The Government have commissioned an independent survey of employer attitudes to PRP. We hope to publish the findings soon. There may then be scope for further econometric work on that aspect of the policy.

The hon. Member for Monklands, East mentioned that the scheme did not extend to the public sector. As the public sector does not make a profit, one cannot have a profit-related scheme in the public sector, but one can have performance-related pay, which increasingly plays a part in that sector. That is not affected by the clause, which specifically relates to profit-related pay.

On the more general point about the scope for abuse, the operation of the relief is kept under regular review. Inevitably, attempts will be made to guarantee amounts of PRP paid under tax exempt-schemes, but that would negate the purpose of the relief. When deliberate exploitation comes to light, the Government do not hesitate to act. Thus, two pieces of anti-avoidance legislation were to be found in last year's Budget and Finance Act.

The hon. Member for Durham, North-West asked for more detailed information. I think it best if she tables a written question, which I will be happy to answer, about the distribution of PRP schemes among industry groups. I could give her the information now, but it would be tedious. We do not have any information on the regional impact of PRP. We do not see PRP as an element in our regional policy. We do not have the information because tax is collected by tax offices and the figures cannot be broken down according to the distribution of a company's branches. For example, all the Marks and Spencer tax records are allocated to the company's registered office in Baker Street, although Marks and Spencer employ staff throughout the country. We do not have information on the distribution of PRP by region. In any case, it is not used as part of a regional or industrial strategy. It is part of an industrial relations strategy—an employee involvement strategy.

I was asked more specifically about the clause. The clause is intended, as far as possible, to preserve what happens now. At the moment, companies are able to leave out of account certain unusual items when calculating PRP. For example, a disposal of an asset might throw up a big profit. The legislation gives an employer the discretion to leave that profit out of account. The 584 employer is also allowed to leave out of account one-off losses that would similarly benefit the employee. The accountancy profession has cut back on the scope of extraordinary items for general accounting purposes, so we can no longer use that definition when calculating PRP. The clause simply deletes "exceptional" and inserts "extraordinary". That may sound an odd way of going about it, but it preserves the employers' discretion to leave out of account certain one-off items when they identify those items that are more directly related to the efforts of its work force. It therefore causes minimum turbulence and has a minimum distributional impact within the firm. It preserves the status quo.

With the exception of the information about the breakdown by industrial category, I have responded to most of the issues. It has been a useful debate. We will keep the possibility of abuse under review. I hope that the Committee will agree to the clause standing part of the Bill.

The Paymaster General (Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory)
: I come briefly out of retirement to invite the Committee to reject the amendments. I appreciate that they have been tabled in a probing spirit and I am happy to answer the general points that have been made.

The amendments are illustrative of the general issue because they invite us to exempt from tax all charities' trading profits. We all know the good work that charities do and, in recognition of that, trading profits are indeed exempt where the trade is exercised in the course of carrying out a charity's primary purpose or where the work is carried out by the charity's beneficiaries. However, the amendment goes further and wants us to exempt all trading profits. That would be unfair to the rest of the trading and business community. The Federation of Small Businesses published a report last year which complained about what it saw as the tax privileges given to the charitable sector. The report pointed out that, for example, all charity shops enjoy an 80 per cent. business rate relief which is sometimes increased to 100 per cent. by local authorities. Charities are also exempted from capital gains tax. The hon. Member for Durham, Northwest mentioned the sale of goods in charity shops. I can confirm that relief is extended to goods given to the shop for resale but not—and I think quite rightly—to the sale of new goods, which are sold in direct competition with the normal retail shops next door.

I have the most recent figures, which are for 1993–94. In overall terms, tax relief to charities is worth £1,450 million. That comprises £830 million direct tax relief, £420 million non-domestic rates relief and £200 million VAT relief. I think that we have got it about right. To extend the exemption to all trading profits would upset the playing field—to use that very overworked cliché—and would also probably get us into trouble with the European Union obligations on trade and competition. However, if a charity wishes to trade, the best way that it can do so—and one which has the full encouragement of the Charity Commission—is to carry out trading through a trading subsidiary, formed specifically for that purpose. That subsidiary company can covenant its profits to the parent charity and that has considerable advantages; it brings tax relief and it also ensures that the risky business of trading cannot put the charitable status or assets of the parent charity at risk.

I do not know whether I am trespassing on the clause stand part debate, Mr. Winterton, but with your permission, as it flows so naturally from the amendments, I shall deal with the object of the clause, which is to 588 extend tax relief to certain lotteries that are run by charities in their trading companies. It was previously thought that by using the mechanism of a trading company that covenanted profits, the profit of the lotteries would thereby be relieved of tax. However, the advice that we have received is that that is not the case and that, unless we pass the clause, tax could be leviable on the parent charity. That is the underlying purpose of the clause.

The group of amendments tabled by the hon. Member for Durham, North-West contains amendments Nos. 261 and 262, although she did not mention them. I must resist those amendments simply on the grounds that they are unnecessary because we have already exempted from tax by concession all the profits in question from 11 July 1994. That announcement was made by my predecessor, my right hon. Friend the Member for Northavon (Sir J. Cope) and a press release was issued at the time. The purpose of the amendment is to make the start date Budget day last year, which would be unfavourable to the charities involved.
