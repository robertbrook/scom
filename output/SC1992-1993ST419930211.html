10.30 am

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Nicholas Soames)
: As the Committee will be aware, this order lifts the restrictions previously imposed on a farm in Derbyshire because the concentrations of dioxin present in some of its food products have now fallen to acceptable levels. The restrictions originally imposed last March prohibited the breeding of food animals, the movement on to and off the farm of animals, crops and any food derived from those animals and the movement off the farm of food and feed.

I am sure that hon. Members share my satisfaction that it has now proved possible to lift those restrictions. Resolution of this difficult problem has involved a careful and thorough scientific investigation by the Ministry and its satisfactory conclusion owes much to the dedication and skill of those involved. But it has depended, too, on the co-operation and active assistance of the farmers involved and of their two neighbours who were affected by non-statutory restrictions on the sale of their milk. I shall say more about those farmers later.

It may help the Committee if I rehearse past events. Dioxins are ubiquitous environmental contaminants produced as unwanted by-products of incinerators, other combustion processes and certain industrial processes. The problem near Bolsover was first identified by the extensive programme of surveillance which my Department has been carrying out as a follow-up to the publication of a Government paper—"Dioxins in the Environment"—which was published in 1989. The results of the testing programme showed that exposure to dioxins in foods for the population as a whole were well below international safety limits. The full results were given in the paper on food surveillance which was published in January last year.

Testing for dioxins has paid particular regard to milk as the major potential route of dietary exposure 4 and a useful monitor of environmental contamination. In June 1991, our programme revealed elevated levels of dioxins in the supplies of milk from two adjacent farms near Bolsover. There was no risk to health from these supplies because they were routinely bulked with other supplies prior to sale to the public. Nevertheless the contamination was clearly unacceptable and the Milk Marketing Board took immediate action to prevent milk from these farms from entering the public supply. Further testing in the area identified a third farm where levels were also high. However, in that case the farmer was operating a suckler cow herd and no milk was sold for public consumption. There was therefore no need to place a prohibition order on any of the three farms in relation to milk production as no risk to public health existed.

The problem was discussed with the farmers, who readily agreed to assist in the comprehensive research programme on milk, meat, herbage and soil from the area, which was immediately put in train. It was of course essential that no livestock should be moved from the holdings while that research was being carried out. The farmers were fully involved in the research and monthly payments were made to them both for their participation in the programme and also in recognition of the considerable disruption to their farming activities.

The results of the research programme were published in March 1992.

Mr. Soames
: First, as my hon. Friends know, everything that the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food does is consistent. The hon. Member for Workington will not be able to find any inconsistency, but I shall discuss the matter with my advisers to see whether it will be possible to provide the information. I shall write to him about that.

There is no comparison with that famous discussion about lead in geese and the poor gentleman in the constituency of my hon. Friend the Member for Ludlow (Mr. Gill) who had such an unfortunate experience. The pollution in that case was clear and was discovered very quickly. We are discussing the serious matter of dioxins, analysis of which is difficult and requires meticulous and long-term scientific work. I shall write in detail to the hon. Member for Workington about the basis on which we dealt with the matter and how we came to our conclusion, and I shall send a copy of that letter to the hon. Member for Bolsover.

The problem was discussed with the farmers concerned, who readily agreed to the research, which was published in 1992 and which revealed unusually high levels of dioxins in the samples of meat from one farm. An order was introduced to ensure that consumers were not exposed to unacceptable levels of dioxins in food from the farm. When the first order lapsed in June the House debated a replacement order. The order revoking that second dioxins order is the subject of today's debate.

Scientific evidence showed that the levels of dioxins in the meat from the other two farms represented no health risk and that, therefore, it was not necessary for sales of livestock to be prohibited. Results from the analysis of soil samples taken by the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food supported the conclusion that the potential problem with livestock was restricted to one farm.

6 We always intended to lift the emergency order and other restrictions as soon as it was safe so that the farmers could resume their normal business. Analysis of samples of the produce from the three affected farms was therefore continued throughout the summer and autumn. The results showed that dioxin concentration in the milk samples from the two diary farms had, by August 1992, fallen below the maximum tolerable concentration previously set by the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food and the Department of Health. That result was confirmed by samples taken in October and, in addition, the dioxin concentration of the meat from animals on the farm on which the order had been placed had also fallen significantly to levels found earlier in animals from the other farms. In view of those findings, the Milk Marketing Board agreed to accept supplies from the two dairy farms. The Department of Health concluded that meat and offal from the third farm was safe for consumption.

My right hon. Friend the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food was, therefore, delighted to be able to inform the House on 16 December 1992 that the emergency order would be lifted and that food produced by all three farms was once more safe to consume. He also announced that a full report of the investigations would be placed in the Library.

Having briefly reviewed the history of the incident, I should like to deal with more general points.

First, I should like to emphasise the prompt action taken by the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food to ensure that there was no risk to the public food supply. All the completed studies confirm our original assessment that the problem was highly localised. The research undertaken by the Ministry has been complemented by work of Her Majesty's inspectorate of pollution and the National Rivers Authority. None of those studies suggested that there was any risk to public health. We have identified and satisfactorily resolved a potential problem with food safety. However, we are never complacent about the safety of food and shall continue to keep the situation under the closest surveillance.

Secondly, I should like to emphasise again the crucial part in the successful programme that was played by the farmers. Their businesses, indeed their very lives, were turned upside down by the discovery of the contamination, but they reacted positively to the challenge of investigating the extent of the threat and ensured that detailed information was produced to evaluate and to combat the problem. They readily and willingly changed their feeding practices, retained animals that would otherwise have been destroyed, collected milk samples on our behalf and, in a variety of ways, took effective action to co-operate fully with the scientists to ensure that the most complete assessment was made. They played a full part in the team effort, which enabled us to announce that food supplies from that area were perfectly safe and that there was no threat to the health of consumers.

JPEG 7 The Government's overriding responsibility is to ensure the safety of the food supply and to take whatever steps are necessary to that end. However, acting in the wider interest, we have been conscious of the plight of the farmers affected by the restrictions. My right hon. Friend the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food visited the two farmers first involved in July 1991 to express his personal concern over what had arisen and to assure the farmers that our scientists and advisers would be working with them during the difficulties that would inevitably follow. Since then, our officials have had regular discussions with all three farmers and the Agricultural Development Advisory Service has provided detailed advice at no charge to the farmers on their farming options. The farmers have had to endure a difficult period and I am sure that we are all delighted that restrictions have been lifted. I have already placed on record the Government's appreciation of the farmers' help and co-operation during the incident but I should like to re-emphasise that.

During the course of the incident there was considerable debate about compensation for the farmers. The Government made their attitude to compensation quite clear from the outset and I explained that carefully in the debate on the original order. Nevertheless, the question is of fundamental importance and one about which the hon. Member for Bolsover is anxious. I believe that it is worth repeating the Government's opinion today.

There is, quite deliberately, no provision in the Food and Environment Protection Act 1985 for compensation. That was considered in 1984 and 1985 when the Bill was debated. During the passage of the Food Safety Act 1990 the House again took a clear decision not to support any such provision. On Report, an amendment was agreed in another place providing for Government compensation where precautionary restrictions were introduced but where food was subsequently found not to have been unsuitable for human consumption. However, the House rejected a compensation provision even in those limited circumstances. The amendment was unacceptable not simply to the Government, but also to the Opposition. The House recognised that in acting to ensure the safety of the consumer it would be quite wrong for Ministers to be distracted by consideration of the financial implications that their decisions might have.

The Government's long-standing position on pollution incidents such as this is that it is the polluter who should pay. It would be wrong to expect the taxpayer to have to pick up the bill for compensation for pollution that the Government had no hand in causing. I am of course aware, as I am sure are other hon. Members, that the company operating the incinerator in the vicinity of the three farms has been negotiating with the farmers involved. The farmers or their advisers will, doubtless, have been able to make use of the analysis that ADAS has made of the financial consequences for their businesses. They 8 have also been able to use the information on the nature and extent of contamination established by the Department and other agencies which has all been placed in the public domain. However, this issue is now subject to legal action for compensation between the National Farmers Union, representing the farmers, and the company. I hope that the Committee will realise that it would be quite improper for me to comment further.

Finally, I should like to mention the presentation of the results of our investigations. The Minister announced at the outset of the incident that he intended to publish the results of all the Department's scientific work. That is standard practice in my Department. We stuck by that commitment and produced three separate reports last year. We have also done our best to answer the concerns of local people who were rightly and obviously worried about the possible implications of the incident. Indeed, I met the hon. Member for Bolsover and a large delegation of his constituents last July for that purpose. The hon. Gentleman has been a doughty and robust champion of the farmer's interest.

Included in the delegation were members and officers of the district and county councils, members of the Shuttleworth and District environment action group and, of course, the farmers. To place before them the most expert assessment of the situation that we could provide, I had with me our chief scientist of food, the senior scientific specialist who led our investigations in the field and a senior medical adviser from the Department of Health. It has not always been easy to answer all the questions put to us. I am grateful to the hon. Member for Bolsover for mediating during that meeting. Inevitably, in the course of an investigation, not all the facts are known. But I believe that our policy of openness has gone a considerable way towards allaying local concerns.

Throughout this incident the Government have acted quickly and effectively to prevent any food safety risk. It is now clear that the contamination was localised. Only one farm was affected to the extent that it proved necessary to place statutory restrictions on the production of livestock or livestock products. I am delighted to say that the levels of contamination have declined more quickly than we once feared was likely and that the farm has now been brought back into production. The restrictions which precluded the Milk Marketing Board from accepting any milk produced on the two neighbouring farms have also now been lifted. The Department will continue to follow developments in the area and I shall not hesitate to take further action should it prove necessary to safeguard the public food supply. In conclusion, let me repeat again my satisfaction, which I am sure that the Committee shares, that this, unhappy incident has been resolved.
