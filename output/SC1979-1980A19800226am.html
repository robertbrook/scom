I checked back when this matter was last referred to in the House. We debated industrial tribunals on 21 November 1977. Member, who—I say this with no disrespect to him—no matter what distinction he may have achieved in other matters, has never acquired a particular reputation in these. There was hardly any reference then. I should like to know what justification there is for the change. The onus is on the Government to make out their case for change rather than for us to make out the case for our amendment. None the less, I shall make the case for the amendment.

First—this has been said repeatedly by Ministers and others—when an applicant goes before the tribunal to complain that he or she has been unfairly dismissed, the only person who knows why the dismissal has taken place is the employer. That is why the legislation has long required the employer to go to the tribunal and establish that he has, in his opinion and JPEG 647 within the law, fairly dismissed the applicant.

Only on Sunday evening a constituent who had been dismissed came to seek my advice. His experience illustrates as well as any that springs to mind why the employer should have to make out a case before the tribunal. His name is Arthur Boughey, and he is employed by a firm called Solar Tubes in Doncaster. It has not been organised by trade unions for a long time. It has existed in my constituency without, to the best of my knowledge, any trade union organisation. In January this year the workers, expecting what they have come to expect—an annual pay increase—were disappointed when it did not take place. They approached the boss and asked "What about a pay increase?" After some difficulty and persuasion, he gave them an increase of several pounds and then deducted their bonus, which was about twice or three times the amount of the increase that he had given them on their basic pay.

Not unnaturally, the workers protested. The most vociferous of the protesters was Mr. Arthur Boughey. The employer dismissed him instantly and ushered him off the premises. He told him that the reason for his dismissal was redundancy. Manifestly, in the circumstances, redundancy was the least likely reason. My constituent can assume and we can assume the real reason for his dismissal, but he does not know officially what it is. He has not been given, as is required by the Employment Protection Act, written notice of the reason for his dismissal.

The Under-Secretary may say that such a man can go to a tribunal and demand that the employer give him written notice of the reason for his dismissal. But there would be a time-consuming interval, and if he were to go to the tribunal with his claim of unfair dismissal and say that he had not been given the written notice, the tribunal might well say—as it ought—"That is a matter for concern or reproach and we could order the employer, but that is not the issue before us. The application is about unfair dismissal." The Under-Secretary would have it that that individual should have to make out a case showing why he had been unfairly dismissed. But he does not know. He can assume or reasonably guess, but he does not know.

Let me round off that little saga. It is 648 relevant to our present and future discussions. When Mr. Boughey was dismissed, 13 of his workmates immediately went on strike to protest against that unfair dismissal. They were instantly dismissed also. Then they went to join a trade union. The trade union accepted them into membership and sought to represent them with that employer. The employer refused to receive the trade union and has made clear that he will not receive or recognise any trade union. So we have here all the makings of another unhappy Grunwick situation—a mini-Grunwick.

Mr. Arthur Boughey does not know officially why he has been dismissed. He does not know the facts. In this case, because the Government changed the law relating to the period of employment that a worker must serve before he can submit a claim to the tribunal and this individual had worked for the firm for 51 weeks, apparently he is to be denied his chance to go to the tribunal—except that in this case the employer did not give him the requisite notice required by law. I was therefore able to advise my constituent to make his application to the tribunal and point out that, had he been served the notice, he would have got his 12 months' qualifying period. But we are almost at the end of February, and these unhappy events occurred at the beginning of January, so he is running perilously near to exhausting his time, and he still does not know the reason for his dismissal.

That is the case again and again. Equally, as in that example, very often the reason given by the employer is not the real or full reason for the dismissal. Let us assume that the employee has been dismissed for bad time keeping. It is not sufficient to say that he knows all the reasons for his dismissal when the employer, in compliance with his statutory obligations, gives the applicant a piece of paper which says "The reason for your dismissal is bad time keeping." What that employee is entitled to know is whether he has been fairly or unfairly selected for dismissal for bad time keeping. He may be a bad time keeper, but 90 per cent, of the employees may be even worse time keepers, in which case he would fairly have the right to expect that, if people were to be dismissed in pursuit of the proper disciplines required to maintain punctuality and good time keeping, he JPEG 649 should not be unfairly chosen and discriminated against. One could give many other examples.

We are familiar with the protestations in the past that the employer, contrary to the principles underlying British justice, is having to prove his innocence whereas natural justice requires that it is his guilt that should be proven by the other party. But what is too often overlooked is that, when the proceedings come to the tribunal stage, the employee has already been the victim of an arbitrary action by an employer, without any chance to defend himself at that stage. He is the person who has automatically, often without words in his defence, been declared guilty by the employer, and when it gets to the tribunal it is then a case of an appeal by an individual against an arbitrary act which may have the most far-reaching consequences for his whole career and life.

Last week I quoted from the Royal Commission report showing how Lord Donovan and his colleagues saw the serious consequences of dismissal for a worker. First, it deprives him of a livelihood—the livelihood not only of himself but of his family. Moreover, it often deprives a man of his self-respect. It can be destructive in terms of his career. It can be demoralising. It can leave a scar that lasts throughout his life. It is a very serious matter to which it is reasonable to expect an employer to be required to give serious consideration, and part of that consideration must be the likelihood that he will have to explain his actions in an industrial tribunal.

Oddly enough, no one ever seems to challenge the fact that the Redundancy Payments Act lays the obligation fairly and squarely on the employer in redundancy cases to prove that he has dismissed his employee because of redundancy. No one seems to question that the onus of proof in that case should lie on the employer.

I make one final point. I am not and never have been in the ranks of those who say that the law has no role in industrial relations. I recognise, pay tribute to and support the voluntarist principle in industrial relations. Equally, I believe that the more we can keep the law out of involvement in industrial relations the better. But I recognise also that there must be statutory protections, statutory safeguards and rights for individual 650 workers, not only to protect them as individuals and to give them the rights that they properly deserve but also because I believe that in this area the law can make a positive contribution to improvement in industrial relations and help to keep industrial peace.

: I hope that I shall be forgiven for forestalling the hon. Member for Newham, North-East (Mr. Leighton), because I think that it will be for the convenience of the Committee if I explain the effect of the clause 5 and the reason why the Government think it right to resist the amendment. I can agree with a great deal of what the right hon. Member for Doncaster (Mr. Walker) said. Of course, we understand the desirability of moving behind the stark terms of a contract of employment relating to notice and the importance of providing a measure of job security by legislation over and above what the contract provides. That is why in the 1971 Industrial Relations Act, brought in by a Conservative Government, the concept of unfair dismissal was introduced into our law for the first time.

I think that I am entitled to take up the somewhat repetitive reference to the "infamous" Act of 1971 in this context and to point out in a gentle way that it was the infamous Act of 1971 that, for the first time in our law, conferred upon workers a measure of job security over and above that which either the common law or their contracts provided in the form of notice.

I absolutely agree with what the right hon. Gentleman said as to justification for that major change in our employment law. Broadly speaking, he said that it was the employer who knows best the facts that led to the dismissal and it would be inequitable to apply the strict burden of the common law standard and burden of proof that he who alleges must prove—since here the employer has the advantage and is in the best position. That was the basis for the 1971 Act, and it remains the basis for the legislation which is now incorporated into the Employment Pro- 654 tection Act. Although TULRA 1974 began with the resounding declaration that:

"The Industrial Relations Act 1971 is hereby repealed"

the Labour Government were careful to retain by subsequent legislation—perhaps not so resoundingly proclaimed—the unfair dismissal provisions of that Act.

I think that the right hon. Gentleman has unwittingly misled himself into supposing that clause 5 reverses the burden of proof. That is not so. All that an employee has to do in order to establish a claim for compensation for unfair dismissal is to show that he has been dismissed, and that he has worked for the minimum qualifying period. Thereafter, the burden shifts to the employer, and the employer has first to show the reason for dismissal, and then that that reason is one of the four "OK reasons"—justifiable reasons—set out in section 57(2) of the 1978 Act, or that it was for some other substantial reason for which he can give evidence. Under the existing legislation which clause 5 bears upon, and only bears upon, the employer must go on to show that he acted reasonably in treating that OK reason as grounds for dismissing the employee.

It is only that last limb that clause 5 bears upon. If the clause is enacted, the burden will still remain on the employer to show the reason for the dismissal, It will still remain upon him to show that it was an OK reason. The burden will not shift to the employee to show that the employer acted reasonably in relying upon that OK reason as ground for dismissing him. It will be, as it were, in the middle. The tribunal will have to satisfy itself whether the employer acted reasonably. That is all that clause 5 will achieve.

Therefore, if I may say so, the right hon. Gentleman read too much into the clause. His argument would have been appropriate to a clause which shifted the whole burden of proof on all those limbs to the employee, but it is not appropriate to the circumstances that I have described.

It is worth bearing in mind that the concept of unfair dismissal is, so far as I am aware, unique to our system of enforceable rights and obligations. Apart from the Redundancy Payments Act, with which it goes pretty closely in harness, there is no other legislation which confers enforceable rights upon a claimant which, so soon as the claim is made, are awarded JPEG 655 unless the respondent to that claim discharges the burden of proving relevant matters. I do not complain of that, because, as I said at the outset, the circumstances of a contract of employment are such as to warrant that reversal of the ordinary rule of our law. That is why the Conservative Government thought it right that that should be enacted in the 1971 Act.

In 1974 the new Labour Government enacted that it should be for the employer to prove that he acted reasonably—in other words, that the employer should shoulder the burden of the third limb now set out in section 57. The tribunal had previously to say "We have looked at the evidence and the employer has established"—or has not—"the reason for the dismissal. He has established that it was one of the four OK reasons." Now, the tribunal has to see whether the employer acted reasonably. Under the Trade Union and Labour Relations Act 1974, the new Government said that the burden of proof should be on the employer in that respect also.

I do not know whether in those days that was regarded as a legal aridity, to use the right hon. Gentleman's expression. Presumably, it was not. But I agree with what he said. We are all concerned with establishing and underpinning good industrial relations. But the Government are concerned also with increasing the opportunities for employment. We believe that in terms of law the clause will have little effect but in terms of psychology it will have a significant effect. We believe that in 1974, in putting the burden of proof expressly on the employer to show that he acted reasonably in regarding the OK ground that he established as a reasonable ground for dismissing the worker, the Act went too far.

It is hardly surprising that the legislative package as a whole can be seen as providing that to sack someone, for whatever reason, is to be guilty of unfairness until one has proved oneself innocent. None of us can be surprised that this legislative package, now incorporated in section 57 of the 1978 Act, can be regarded in that way, and so great a departure from our ordinary principles does not make people want to hurry to become employers; nor does it win friends for the important concept of unfair dismissal.

I hope that I have said enough to assure 656 the Committee that the Government regard the concept of unfair dismissal as important and are as much behind it in 1980 as their Conservative predecessors were in 1971 when they introduced it. We are not putting the burden of proof as to whether a dismissal was reasonable on to the employee, but we are leaving it in the middle so that the tribunal looks at the whole of the evidence.

But, as it happens, I have been a member of a trade union, so I understand the argument about free riders.

    There are arguments that can be advanced against free riders. For example they do not ask the union to represent them in their negotiations. The argument is that free riders get the benefit but they do not pay for those who represent them in obtaining the benefit. But if they have never asked the people obtaining the benefit to represent them, anyway, it is arguable whether in those circumstances it is acceptable that they should be paid to represent them, even though they have not asked them to do so. That is one counter to the free rider argument.

    The second is that, if we went through life saying that one must pay for everything from which one derives benefit and equally accepted the alternative of that argument that one does not pay for things from which one does not derive benefit, we should again be in an interesting and intriguing state as a society. For example, I, as a bachelor, would never be called upon to pay any rates for education. That would be a glorious situation for me. I have no children; why should I pay for the education of everyone else's kids? But that is what society is all about, is it not? We participate in a social structure which means that sometimes we pay for benefits which we do not get and on occasions we get benefits for which we do not pay.

    I do not know where there is a civic theatre; I think perhaps Coventry has one. If I went to Coventry and attended the civic theatre, it would be subsidised by the local authority. I would get the benefit of the subsidy as a consequence of going to the theatre, but it would not prevent my 694 going to the theatre, and the citizens of Coventry would be subsidising my seat—or seats, as the case may be—at that theatre.

    That is the whole point of this free rider argument: if we really grind it down to its basic level, it becomes absurd. Of course, there will always be a situation in which some people will receive benefits for which others are paying, but I believe that is a fact of life, and I do not believe that it is sufficient to detract from my argument or to say that we as a society should continue to go along the road of the closed shop.

    However, if that argument is not acceptable, there is always the final argument. The amendment might be able to be accepted subject to a further amendment to it, namely, that there should be some compulsory deduction from wages, equivalent to the union fee, which would be paid to a charity or similar body, and I would still find that more acceptable than the closed shop principle, though I do not really find it acceptable. But it is something that I would accept if the consequence of accepting it ensured that the closed shop was abolished.

    My final point is the position of this country in relation to the EEC. I receive all sorts of letters indicating to me that the European Court of Human Rights is likely to say that the closed shop is incompatible with the Treaty of Rome and is certainly incompatible with human rights. I do not know whether the information I have is correct. I have letters from three people who have appealed and who seem to think that that will be the court's decision. I have other letters from very responsible people indicating that they believe that this will be the attitude of the Court of Human Rights.

    It would be interesting, at least, to have an assurance from the Minister that, if the Court of Human Rights were to rule that the closed shop was incompatible with human liberty and human rights, the Government in those circumstances would feel compelled to take some action in outlawing the closed shop. Indeed, if the Government can give us any indication of the likely findings of the Court of Human Rights in this matter, it will be extremely interesting to the Committee.
