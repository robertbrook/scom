JPEG 451 Amendment No. 329 would tighten up the wording of clause 118(1) which, at present, is a little vague. It should be clear to the respondent what type of behaviour will make him liable to the arrested. When the respondent is brought before the court, the court and his legal advisers will have the task of ensuring that he understand the order that the court has made against him. If a power of arrest were made ex parte and served on the respondent, he will not be brought before the court and will probably not have the benefit of immediate legal advice. It is important that it can be made as clear as possible to the respondent when he will make himself liable for arrest under the power of arrest. It is also of the utmost importance that there should be no uncertainty surrounding the criteria that the constable applies when deciding whether to make the arrest.

The first part of amendment No. 330 is identical to section 2(5) of the Domestic Violence and Matrimonial Proceedings Act 1976, which concerns the arrest of a person for breach of an injunction. The amendment would increase the prospect of the respondent being dealt with for the breach at the first hearing of the matter—in other words, when he is brought the court for the time after his arrest.

The police are already under a duty to bring the respondent before the court within 24 hours; that is made clear in clause 118(2). The amendment would place an additional duty on the arresting constable to contact the court as soon as possible. That would enable court time to be allocated at the earliest opportunity, increasing the likelihood of the court being able to hear the case fully.

The duty to notify the local authority is, in our view, essential. The experience of practitioners in cases of domestic violence for which injunctions are granted is that many court hearings are ineffective because witnesses are not present to give evidence. That results in a further waste of court time, and a waste of taxpayer's money should the respondent be legally aided. The duty to notify the local authority is geared to increasing the likelihood of witnesses being in court if the respondent appears in court after being arrested. It is in the interests of the victim, the respondent, the court, the prison service and the local authority that remands to future dates are avoided whenever possible. The repondent's breach should be dealt with as soon as possible. If the case is not disposed of at the first hearing, the local authority may wish to make representations as to whether the respondent should be remanded in custody or on bail.

Amendment No. 331 would add further conditions to subsection (4). The Bill gives the court the power to issue a warrant for the respondent's immediate arrest—a bench warrant, issued to those who apply to the bench in person. The respondent may then be held in custody for up to 24 hours. After that, he may be remanded in custody until his case is heard. The only precondition to the court's power to issue a bench warrant is that the injunction in respect of which the warrant is sought was granted in the circumstances set out in subsections (2) to (5) of clause 115 or subsections (2) to (4) of clause 116. That encompasses any behaviour that is a nuisance or annoyance to neighbours or persons residing in the district of the local authority, whereas the power to grant a power of arrest under clauses 115 and 116 is strictly limited to 452 cases of violence or threats of violence with a risk of harm to others. The power of the court to issue a bench warrant is not limited in that way.

We suggest that, except for cases where there is fear of witness being intimidated—a factor mentioned in an earlier debate—the same criteria should apply when a judge is considering whether to issue a bench warrant as when considering granting a power of arrest. It is inappropriate that the court should entertain applications for arrest and remanding in custody if no harm is threatened to others, such as in cases of noise nuisance. It is important to note that the power to issue a bench warrant arises only in cases where the judge, on the application for an injunction, either did not attach a power of arrest to the part of the injunction that the respondent is alleged to have breached, or did not attach a power of arrest to any part of the injunction. It is difficult to see why an alleged breach of an injunction granted in such circumstances should give rise to the power to grant a bench warrant, if the judge was not persuaded, or did not have the power, to grant a power of arrest when the case first came to court.

The amendment would add the power to issue a bench warrant when necessary to avoid the court being frustrated by the respondent and if there are reasonable grounds for believing that the normal committal procedure—for example, the respondent being given advance notice of the allegation of the breach—will result in the respondent intimidating witnesses into refusing to give evidence. The local authority may then seek to have the respondent held in custody until the case is hard.

New Clause 12
OFFENCE OF HARASSMENT OF RESIDENTIAL OCCUPIER

'(1) In this section "residential occupier" in relation to any premises means a person occupying the premises as a residence:

(b) under a lease;


(c) under a contract;


(d) by virtue of any enactment or rule of law giving him the right to remain in occupation or restricting the right of any other person to recover possession of the premises.

(2) If any person does any unlawful act or acts which interfere with the peace or comfort of a residential occupier or members of his household knowing or having reasonable cause to believe that that conduct is likely to cause a residential occupier to give up the occupation of his home or to refrain from exercising any right or pursuing any remedy in respect of his home, then that person shall be guilty of an offence if the residential occupier gives up the occupation of his home wholly or partly by reason of that act or those acts.

(3) A person guilty of an offence under this section shall be liable—

  1. on summary conviction to a fine not exceeding level 5 on the standard scale or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 6 months or to both;
  2. on conviction on indictment to a fine or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 years or to both.
