It is a probing amendment that is designed to discover why the Government have suggested that the mayor should be responsible for the strategies listed in clause 33—transport, the London development agency, biodiversity, municipal waste, air quality, ambient noise and culture—but no other strategy or combination of strategies. It seems an almost random selection. The only strategy that is obligatory on the mayor or the authority is the London development strategy, which is required under the Regional Development Agencies Act 1998.

If the Government wish to impose strategies on the mayor, my hon. Friends and I suggest at least four more. For example, we could include a strategy for health promotion. We shall come, in the next amendment, to a strategy for sustainable development. Doubtless there will be a strategy for wealth creation—the Government so clearly now believe in that; it is the new socialist mantra. There could also be a strategy for environmental improvement.

The alternative, which we would prefer, is the procedure suggested in the amendment. We do not want a list of strategies, because the mayor, the assembly and the authority should be able to decide for themselves what they want to do. This will be a recurring theme during our debates. The Bill is either telling the GLA, the mayor or the assembly what to do and how to do it; or it is saying, "We shall not tell you now; the Secretary of State will tell you later." Neither is consistent with regional government having proper authority, status or self-management.

Even if the Government are now resiled from it being regional government and are calling it city-wide government, the government of our capital city should, as far as possible, be free to decide what it wants to do. We may argue that it should be required to do certain things, but the list that the mayor has to follow is, to cite a phrase used yesterday in the House of Commons by the leader of the Conservative party, a list of things into which the Government are meddling unnecessarily.

238 All these matters could properly be decided upon after the election of the mayor and the assembly next May. It is inconceivable that the mayor and the assembly will not come up with a transport strategy because they will have to run transport for London. It is inconceivable that they will not work out a strategy for municipal waste, because they must run the service, or that they will not have an air-quality strategy. Stating that the authority needs a culture strategy seems extremely municipalist and centralist. Stating that it needs strategies for specific matters, such as ambient noise, seems superfluous.

Our preference is to remove those strategies from the Bill, leaving the authority to decide what it wants to do. However, there should be an overarching strategy for the sustainable development of the city, and we shall argue for that in the next debate. If, at first blush, the Government resist the amendment—it is just conceivable that they will retain the strategies—amendment No. 314 would at least put them in the context of sustainable development.

We tabled amendment No. 314 as a fall back in the event that we are unable to persuade the Committee of the merit of amendment No. 69. We hope that Labour and Conservative Members will agree that it is not for the House of Commons to tell the mayor what strategies to have. The mayor should decide on his or her priorities within the overall powers given to the authority.

During the previous sitting, my hon. Friends questioned the Government on whether the authority should have a responsibility to engage with the business community, the voluntary sector and so on. Ministers were helpful and said that they thought that a good idea and that the Government would return with some proposals.

I am sure that the Government understand the difference between establishing the mechanisms by which the authority will operate and defining its powers and what should be done by individual players within it. That is why we think it proper that the Bill should not state in detail how the authority should consult the business community or the voluntary sector but simply establish that they should be partners in consultation. We think it inappropriate to list six particular strategies and not others; it will be perfectly possible for the authority to sort out such matters itself.
