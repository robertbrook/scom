Mr. Milburn
: I hope that we do not stretch your vocal cords too much in the course of today's proceedings, Mr. Winterton.

In discussing the amendments, I hope that the Committee has the opportunity to explore some of the issues surrounding the enterprise investment scheme. Amendments Nos. 179 and 181 best highlight the differences between the approaches of the Opposition and the Government to how best to deliver an agreed principle: the need to improve investment in small and medium-sized enterprises and unquoted companies.

I have a special interest in the subject as, prior to coming to this House, I worked in the economic development sector for a local authority on Tyneside, seeking to provide assistance to small and growing firms. One of the issues that continually came to my attention—and to the attention of other hon. Members who have worked in that field, most notably, my hon. Friend the Member for Dudley, West (Mr. Pearson)—was the shortage of investment capital for companies that were starting up or growing.

It is obvious that small and medium-sized companies are the growth sector of the economy, in terms of both output and future job creation, but such companies encounter difficulties in raising finance. The enterprise investment scheme that was introduced last year was intended to bridge that investment gap. The then Chief Secretary to the Treasury who, as we speak, may be facing a dressing-down from the Prime Minister and the Cabinet—our sympathies are with him—and whose ghost continues to stalk the Committee, said last year during the debate on the enterprise investment scheme that:

"The purpose of the provision is to recognise that unquoted trading companies can often face considerable difficulties in raising 296 relatively small amounts of share capital. The new scheme is intended to provide a well-targeted means for some of those problems to be overcome."

He went on to say:

"The EIS will be targeted specifically at trading companies and relief will not be available for investment in companies providing private rented housing."— [Official Report, Standing Committee A, 22 March 1994; c. 773.]

We have some concerns about clause 60 which are reflected in the amendments. Amendments Nos. 180 and 182 will allow us to probe the Government's intentions in relaxing the operation of the enterprise investment scheme. We fear that the Government are, once again, emphasising the needs of property-backed companies, rather than manufacturing companies.

As stated last year, the intention behind the enterprise investment scheme was to provide a targeted incentive for new equity investment by individuals in unquoted companies and, in particular, to encourage business angels—outside investors—to become involved, not only in terms of investment, but by lending their expertise and management skills to the company involved. That objective was to be achieved by enabling outside investors to take an active role in management as paid directors without losing any entitlement to relief. To that end, many forms of tax relief were made available under the EIS.

One of the major concerns expressed in Committee last year—which we still have—related to the scattergun approach to investment adopted by the EIS and whether such tax incentives provided the best means of levering in investment to those small and medium-sized enterprises in most need of it. I am thinking in particular of fledgling companies with potential for growth in the high-tech manufacturing sector. Although their need for additional financial assistance is proved time and time again both anecdotally and statistically, they have some difficulties in acquiring it. Those concerns from last year have been heightened by the proposals in this and subsequent clauses to make the EIS even less targeted.

Clause 60 provides for the abolition of two safeguards which were deemed necessary—indeed, they were reinforced—in last year's Finance Bill: the parallel trades rule and the interests in land rule.

The existing parallel trades rule denies relief for investment in a company to an individual who is part of a group which controls that company or is involved in another business carrying out a broadly similar trade. That provision was one of the five main safeguards when the enterprise investment scheme was introduced last year and it is a useful anti-avoidance measure intended to prevent people from investing in a business with which they have very close links and from obtaining new relief.

The Government now propose to abolish the rule after it has been in operation for only one year. I hope that the Financial Secretary will be able to tell us why the Government have deemed it necessary to get rid of that rule having made it central to the EIS last year. Was last year's Bill badly drafted or was it another case of the Government rushing hastily into a piece of ill-thought-out legislation without having consulted fully those who were deemed to benefit from it? If pressure is being brought to bear on the Government to abolish the rule, will the JPEG 297 Financial Secretary tell us the sources of that pressure? Will he also tell us the costs to the Exchequer of the abolition and explain the Government's thinking on loosening EIS provisions more generally in future?

Our amendments reflect our concerns about the further relaxation of the rules governing the enterprise investment scheme. In amendment No. 180 we suggest a different approach to targeting investment at particular groups of unquoted companies. Rather than giving a blanket relaxation of the rules, as the clause proposes, we seek to use the EIS to develop particular sectors of the economy where assistance is most needed by using existing taxation provisions. We have highlighted two areas in particular: certain parts of the film trade and those companies engaged in research and development, which is regarded by hon. Members on both sides of the Committee as vital for the future development of the British economy. It is particularly important for small and medium-sized enterprises, which tend to be less risk averse than some of the larger companies, which are more likely to research and develop new product lines. It would benefit the economy if assistance could be targeted on those companies.

Amendment No. 182 deals with land interests. The amendments are probing amendments, and are designed to flush out the Government's intentions about the future of the enterprise investment scheme. The Government should have adopted the approach that is outlined in amendment No. 180. The Government could have used existing tax provisions to target help more accurately on where it is most needed. Instead, they have decided to relax the operation of the EIS. That is the clearest in the Government's proposed changes to the interests in land rule. Amendments Nos. 179 and 181 would prevent the abolition of the parallel trades rule and of the interests in land rule.

The Opposition are worried about the abolition of the parallel trades rule, and about what that reveals of the Government's intentions. However, we are more worried about the interests in land rule. The current rule prevents relief being given if more than half of the value of a company's assets is represented by land and buildings. If the rule were broken, the investor would lose relief. Clause 60 will abolish that rule. To some of the more cynical members of the Committee, that sounds like we are harking back to the bad old days of the business expansion scheme.

The initial purpose of the business expansion scheme became perverted, and its investment benefits became skewed towards property-backed companies. I shall give some figures: none of the BES companies was in rented housing during 1987–88, but 59 per cent. of companies and 95 per cent. of the amount invested, were in private rented housing for the year 1992–93. The clause does not stipulate that the EIS can be used for the benefit of companies working in housing, but it will bring the return of property-based investment companies.

Opposition Members and many Conservative Members are worried that the little help that is available will be targeted to the British manufacturing sector. No wonder manufacturing under the BES lost out. The Inland Revenue's statistics show that in 1987–88, 35 per cent. of companies and 14 per cent. of total value was invested in 298 manufacturing companies, but by 1992–93, 13 per cent. of companies and just 2 per cent. of the value was invested in manufacturing. The value of investment in manufacturing under the BES fell from £28 million to £22 million over that period. The BES has not been a success story in the manufacturing sector of the economy.

With the background to the BES and the EIS, I should like to ask the Financial Secretary some questions about the relaxation of the interests in land rule. First, will he confirm that the rule was initially introduced to prevent the abuse of the scheme by those who set up assets of property-backed schemes to provide lower-risk, tax-enhanced investments? Secondly, if the Financial Secretary says that there is a problem with the 50 per cent. rule—I understand many assets of small companies are tied up in land or buildings—instead of removing the entire restriction, why not merely vary the percentage of assets held in land or buildings? He has the power to do that by order and does not even have to introduce such a proposal in the Bill. What representations has the Financial Secretary received on the issue? From where is pressure coming?

Thirdly, what sort of schemes will the abolition of the interests on land rule facilitate that would not previously have benefited under the enterprise investment sheme?

The concerns felt about that matter are not only felt by Labour Members. I quote from the Small Company Investor, which comments on the provisions in clause 60 and subsequent clauses on the enterprise investment scheme:

"Our bet is that there will be an avalanche of lower-risk property-backed schemes taking money away from entrepreneurial small companies."

"Tax Shelter Report" observes that:

"Investors should expect to see a number of asset backed companies seeking funds, particularly property development companies."

Why have not the Government taken the opportunity to focus any available relief on companies which need the funds and are vital to the future development of the British economy? I am particularly thinking of small and medium-sized, high-tech companies in the manufacturing or research and development sectors. Such companies face a problem from the provisions in the clause and subsequent proposals.

If the Government do not focus the relief, such companies face a double whammy, First, they already have difficulties in raising finance in competition with property-backed businesses—investors tend to go for easy, safe investments. Secondly, their problems will be made worse if the property-backed business can not only raise finance, but can do so with a subsidy from the taxpayer. That makes clause 60 a particular cause for concern. Last year, the enterprise investment scheme was nodded through the Committee without a Division. We asked for assurances and some were given, although they seem to have altered in that 12-month period. When the Financial Secretary responds, will he offer all members of the Committee who are concerned about future development of the manufacturing sector, real assurances about the Government's intentions?
