    Mrs. Beckett
    : Clause 16 deals with rates of tax and tax allowances. We want to raise the issue of how the married couples allowance will operate under the comparatively new system of independent taxation. Although that new system represents far greater independence and privacy for marital partners than the previous system, discrimination on the grounds of sex still persists at the edges. Only the husband can receive the married couples allowance and only if he cannot use in full the allowance can he transfer part of it to his wife. A man and woman may receive the same pay, but the man will have the greater take-home pay solely because of his sex.

    Amendments Nos. 46 and 47 raise some of the issues arising from that difficulty. In our response to the Budget, we said that the obvious and ideal solution would be to leave to the discretion of the couple how to split the allowance, but we realise that the Inland Revenue might be less than delighted by the administrative problems that such a proposal might cause. As it is ever our wish to keep life as simple as possible for the Inland Revenue so that it can raise the largest amount of tax revenue that is due, amendments Nos. 46 and 47 put forward more simple proposals.

    I want to discuss amendment No. 47 first because, in a sense, it is more straight forward. It would permit a man to elect how to treat the married couples allowance. He may choose to give all of it to his wife, if the couple decides that that is right, or to divide it equally between them.

    Amendment No. 46 is perhaps a little more radical, but none the worse for that. The amendments are similar in some respects, as they permit the transfer of the whole or part of the allowance. Amendment No. 46 allows for half of the allowance to pass to the wife if she so wishes and notifies the Revenue that that is her choice—irrespective of whether her husband consents.

    I move the amendment not only because it is morally right to treat couples equally, but because a couple may be in dispute. Separation or divorce may be pending. They may not be able to agree about how the married couples allowance should be divided. The amendment would help to overcome the problem of a wife being denied access to half of that allowance.

    Another important issue is irrevocability. Amendment No. 46 states that if an election were made under our proposals, it would be "irrevocable", as the Revenue prefers. However, it remains right to deal with the issue in the way proposed by amendment No. 47: to allow the revoking of such an election at least once. We understand the administrative problems that might be created by such a proposal, which is why this option should be permitted only once. The issue should be raised, although it is separate from the problem of the transferability of part or all of the married couples allowance.
