10.30 a.m.

The Minister of Transport

(Mr. Norman Fowler)
: I beg to move,

That during the proceedings on the Transport Bill the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock.

This is an important Bill and I am sure that the Committee will want to scrutinise it carefully, but I am also sure that the Committee will want to make good progress with our discussions. I look forward to a constructive debate on the Bill. It is our custom to meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays, as is proposed in the sittings motion, and I believe that that will be the most convenient way to proceed.

I hope the sittings motion can be agreed and that we shall be able to proceed.

Mr. Albert Booth
: It is pleasant indeed to be able to begin on a note of agreement with the Minister on what is, as he rightly says, a very important Bill but, a very controversial Bill. It is also, of course, pleasant to note that the Minister agrees that we want to have a very detailed discussion of the Bill and we will come to some of the difficulties in doing that arising from the Minister's actions a little later.

I trust that the Minister will appreciate that the rare, if not unique, construction of the Bill, particularly in its most amazing first clause, poses difficulties in doing what he is suggesting and he will, I hope, be prepared to facilitate us in meeting the aims which he has defined by having a detailed discussion.

I think it is most important, therefore, that the Minister should be present with us for those sittings when we are examining this unique clause. I understand, of course, that since he has to be in Cabinet on Thursday mornings that would place a restriction on our ability to comply with the terms of the sittings motion. Of course, I realise that we would not want to hold up the Bill unduly on that account, but at least for the first of our two sittings when we discuss clause 1 it would be eminently reasonable that the Minister 4 should be with us. Therefore, I should like to move, at the appropriate time, an amendment to the sittings motion to achieve that aim.

In doing so, I must point out that before we reach the subsequent clauses it will be necessary for the Opposition to receive the views of a number of important bodies of people affected by the Bill. We have not been able to do that because of the way in which the Minister has operated on this clause. For example, as soon as we received the Bill my hon. Friends and I set about contacting the local authorities, the unions, the employers, and in particular, the bus company employees, affected by the early provisions of the Bill. We found them as unaware as we were of some of the provisions and they are still examining these with a view to advising us on the matter. It seems to me that I, and my hon. Friends, rather than having a happy yuletide will be spending most of our Christmas Recess in discussions and communications with some of these bodies in order to equip ourselves to do the job which we should have been well-prepared to do if the Minister had indicated clearly, as is normally the case on a controversial measure, the scope that he was envisaging.

I find to my deep concern that a number of issues raised in the Bill have not been covered by the consultative document. An outstanding case is the provision of an appeal against the refusal by the London Transport Executive to reach an agreement. I suggest that somebody involved in the drafting of the Bill has overlooked that because it is not even mentioned as one of the purposes of the Bill in clause 1, which purports to outline the purposes of part I. The discussion document, incidentally, has misled a number of interested parties including myself in that it said that the fare and distance would be the basis for defining the limits of the road service licence. No such restriction of fare limits is indicated in the Bill. The discussions which we conducted before publication of the Bill on that basis have been largely a waste of time and will have to be repeated.

We have had other difficulties in that there was no indication in the consultative document of the major change in contract carriage provision. That, again, is a matter which seems not to have been understood by the Minister, judging by his interventions on Second Reading. It is certainly a matter which now, having been drawn to JPEG 5 the attention of some of those affected, is causing very considerable alarm and must be the basis of consultation which we should naturally want to complete before discussing those parts of the Bill.

Equally we have been misled in the consultative document in that it clearly outlined the intention of the Minister to lay a Bill which would prepare for the introduction of EEC rules as from 1 January 1983. We can find no provision within the Bill which relates to that. Therefore it seems that we must on this occasion very regretfully—because nobody is keener than we are to debate some of the basic issues and to ascertain what some of them are in the Bill—I conduct a discussion with the Minister as to his purposes.

We have not been the only ones facing this difficulty in the House. Our Library assistants, who serve us so well in stating in a fair and impartial way the provisions of Bills for those Ministers who are interested, prepared a document on the Transport Bill, Ref 79/11 dated 20 November. Its authors say

"It will not be possible to work out the exact implications of the changes in the law for some time."

—I can only agree heartily with them and regret that we should have to consider the Bill when its legislative implications cannot be worked out without very detailed discussion.

It will therefore be my intention before we reach a decision on the sittings motion to move an amendment to provide that we sit on days when the Minister can be present until we have completed our discussions on clause 1. I hope that we can then proceed, taking proper account of the Minister's need to attend Cabinet meetings.

Mr. John Prescott
: I would like to support my right hon. Friend in his submission about the sittings motion. My right hon. Friend has made a valid point—a point on which we have not yet heard from the Minister who has not had an opportunity to reply—that the right hon. Gentleman has to be in Cabinet. This is a matter for discussion, presumably, between my right hon. Friend and the Minister, about the difficulties he faces with his job. If that is the case, clearly there will be difficulties, certainly in the early stages of the Bill in understanding and clarifying all aspects, particularly in the part dealing with express carriages, 6 and the redefinition of public vehicles. On Second Reading we said that the complexity of the changes is, as the Minister agreed fundamental and radical and therefore have considerable implications. It is here that we are placed in considerable difficulty. If the Bill were dealing with something that was familiar to us, we could willingly put down amendments and debate them. But the implications of the proposals make it very difficult for us to get the proper advice from the authorities concerned—the various bodies who are affected by the proposals. That makes it difficult for us to put down amendments which we are satisfied are substantive, and not those which could lead to the Opposition being accused of delaying tactics. There is very important substance here and even today we have received correspondence from the county authorities suggesting that certain parts of the Bill of which they had not been fully aware would have the same implications and therefore ask the House to consider this aspect of consultations. That will be dealt with when we come to this part of the Bill.

Our main concern is that we are asked to launch almost immediately into the clauses which are the subject of considerable controversy.

One of the difficulties of getting proper information is that whilst the Minister produced his report in August, the Opposition side did not receive the consultative document until we visited one of the organisations concerned which was kind enough to photostat it and give it to us. I did not realise until some months later that there were other appendices to that document that further spelt out what the consultative document meant.

The Minister said that he would see that these procedures were corrected. We accept that and they are now working to that effect. That has meant considerable difficulties for us, however, in being able to carry out our functions as an Opposition, particularly in this first couple of weeks, in discovering the substance of the point and how we may establish what we think may be the mistakes in these amendments.

We address our amendments to particular clauses and have not concentrated on the other more definitive areas as laid down particularly in clauses 2 and 3.

So I hope that the Committee will accept that we have some difficulty, perhaps all Oppositions do, in securing advice JPEG 7 about these matters in order to make a proper and substantial contribution to the debate. This is a controversial area. The authorities concerned are the best sources of advice alternative to that given by the Minister, and they are authorities, which by the nature of their organisation, are democratic bodies which are bound to consult committees and other organisations before they can give us a proper assessment of their opinion. Therefore we find ourselves in considerable difficulty in giving proper consideration to these clauses, and the sittings motion would make that more difficult. I do not suggest that we anticipate any difficulties, or that we shall ask for delays immediately after the recess, but in the period before the recess we find ouselves in considerable difficulties and we hope that the Committee will give consideration to that matter.
