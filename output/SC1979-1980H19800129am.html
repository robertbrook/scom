As the amendments stand, it is possible—though in practice it almost certainly would not be worked that way—that where a long-distance service of, say, 80 or 90 miles, stopping frequently enough to be classed as stage carriage, passed, say, through five or 10 miles of a traffic commissioner's area, that commissioner could give a road service licence for the whole length of the route, the other traffic commissioners having no jurisdiction, other than the right to be consulted.

The right hon. Gentleman looks concerned about that. To judge from what he said, that was plainly not his intention. But that would be the effect of the amendments.

I turn to the main problem, which is whether the present system of backings should be retained, and whether the proposals in the Bill represent a complication of the system. I hope to be able to satisfy the hon. Gentleman and the Com- JPEG 613 mittee that what is proposed will, in practice, be no more complicated than the present legislation, and that it is a perfectly reasonable system.

Under the existing legislation, there is a strange system of backings. A road service licence issued in one area, which is known as the "primary area", is backed in any other area in which passengers are picked up or set down. What happens is that the applicant has first to obtain his road service licence in the primary area, and then to obtain backings from the other—one or more traffic commissioners. The procedure for obtaining, granting, refusing, suspending and revoking a backing are, in all respects, the same as those for a primary licence. In practice, it works out that a whole lot of applications for road service licences must be made for the different areas, just as we propose.

The only difficulty that occasionally arises is that it is necessary, under the present law, for the primary licence to be obtained before the backings can be sought. Therefore, procedurally, it is possible, in some cases, for delay to be built in because it is necessary to have two bites at the cherry. One must apply first for the primary licence, and then for the backing.

In practice, that difficulty has been resolved by the traffic commissioners in a fairly sensible way. In most cases, they hold joint sittings, and will consider applications for the primary road service licence and for the backings at the same time. Nevertheless, the backings procedure has built into it the possibility of itself causing delay, because one application has to come before the other.

To replace that, therefore, it is proposed in the Bill that a road service licence is needed from each traffic commissioner in whose area the passengers are picked up or set down. That matches those parts of the Bill which give the traffic commissioners sole authority over their territorial areas.

When I first contemplated this procedure, I was concerned that it might lead to more delay and repetitive hearings. However, the understanding is that, in practice, the traffic commissioners would continue the present desirable practice of holding joint sittings in almost all cases where a route goes through the area of several traffic commissioners. All the evidence, all the representations from objectors, and so on, could thus be heard 614 at the same time. Each traffic commissioner would then have full jurisdiction to grant the licence for his area. Obviously, in a joint sitting, the whole thing would be rejected or granted.

Equally importantly, each traffic commissioner would have sole jurisdiction to apply such conditions as he thought appropriate for that section of the route passing through his area. That seems to be a reasonable proceeding, which would not involve any further complications than the present practice and should, therefore, prove generally acceptable.

I was asked about the reaction of people in the industry. I gather that some consideration of the proposed new legislation is still going on, because it is new and the industry is anxious to know whether this will mean a dramatic change from the present position. As I understand it, some of the operators are quite happy with what is proposed, though I am not sure that anyone has formally put on open record to us that he is happy. But I do not believe that there will be any real resistance once it is appreciated that in practice the change is fairly technical and that things will carry on much as they do at present.

I come back to the amendments. As they stand, it is arguable that they are vastly beyond what the right hon. Gentleman wanted, and that they cast doubts on the ability of the other traffic commissioners to impose conditions and so on. They could vest sole jurisdiction for the whole route on one traffic commissioner. Certainly, they do that. So the traffic commissioner would be able to grant a road service licence for a service which might cut across another traffic commissioner's territory and, indeed, go across much more of the territory of one of his neighbours than his own. That does not seem right in principle, and though it is a great simplification, it goes far too far if we are to retain traffic commissioners at all. Accordingly, I ask the right hon. Gentleman to withdraw the amendment.
