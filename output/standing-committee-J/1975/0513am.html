Mr. Davies
: Perhaps I can now go into a little more detail on these amendments, which we were discussing when the Committee adjourned yesterday, particularly in relation to Clause 23 (7). The committee will recollect that I put the amendments in yesterday and there was no time for adequate discussion and debate then.

The amendments are to limit the existing charge under subsection (7). The first amendment is introductory and the second—which is the main substantive amendment—is to limit the amount of the realised development value chargeable under subsection (7) to the market value of the property held by the body in question at the time it ceases to be a charity. Furthermore, any of the property which continues to be held for a charitable purpose, but by some other body, is to be disregarded.

Clause 27 (3) is aimed at the avoidance of tax by the use of what are known as "time charities ", that is, bodies which are 1164 charitable for a period of time and then cease to be so. Subsection (7) provides that, where the disposal of an interest by a charity is exempt under the clause, and the charity subsequently loses its charitable status, an amount of realised development value equal to the amount which was exempt on the disposal is treated as accruing to the former charity immediately after its loss of charitable status, and so is taxable. When the charity loses its charitable status, there is again a trigger, and the amount that would have been payable if it had not been a charity becomes payable. The rule in the subsection, however, bites no matter how long before the loss of charitable status the exempt disposal occurred.

The amendments meet the criticism made by the churches Main Committee and the National Council for Social Services, which have pointed out two areas in which subsection (7) could affect charities which are not strictly time charities. The first is where the charity has disposed of all its funds for charitable purposes and is then wound up. That charity may not be a time charity; it may be a normal charity which is then wound-up. If, during its life, the charity has made one or more exempt disposals, the trustees will find themselves faced with a DLT liability and no funds to meet it, they all having been spent on perfectly respectable and honourable purposes. That is the first case which the amendments are designed to meet—the genuine case where the charity is wound-up.

The second case which the amendments are designed to meet is where a charity ceases to exist on merging with another. Here, too, subsection (7) by itself, as drafted, would impose liability if there had been previous exempt disposals. The Charity Commissioners, while acknowledging the need to prevent avoidance by the use of time charities, make the same points in relation to that kind of situation. So we have attempted, in the amendments, to meet these two particular difficulties, in the case of genuine charities being wound-up and in the case of one charity merging with another, where in neither case is there a time charity.

I undertook yesterday to ensure that the cy-près doctrine was taken into account, and I shall let the Committee know whether that is the case. I expect it would be, and, indeed, it probably should be.
