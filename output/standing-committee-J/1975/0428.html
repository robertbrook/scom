Clause 9
DEVOLUTION ON DEATH

Question proposed—[ 27th April]—That the clause stand part of the Bill.

4.30 p.m.

Question again proposed.

Mr. Graham Page
: When our last sitting was adjourned I was floundering somewhat in trying to find the right way to express what I had in mind in my complaints on this clause, so it is probably best if I give an example to show what I had in mind. Let us take a Dickens example on this occasion.

Suppose that Mr. Pickwick dies, owning Blackacre. Blackacre is sold by Mr. Pickwick's personal representatives—no doubt, Messrs. Dodson and Fogg—to a stranger, say, Sam Weller. That is treated as a sale by the late Mr. Pickwick, if I understand this clause correctly. But if Blackacre passes to Messrs. Dodson and Fogg, not as personal representatives but as Settled Land Act trustees, or as representatives of some other kind, again the sale to a stranger is treated as a sale by the late Mr. Pickwick. I think I am correct so far.

If the personal representatives of the late Mr. Pickwick transfer Blackacre to themselves, as trustees for a specific legatee or devisee who is an infant—say, the Fat Boy—that seems to expend their exemption and if the trustees, who were personal representatives but who have transferred the property to themselves, as trustees, then sell, say, to Snodgrass, that is a disposal which will result in a tax liability in respect of the realised development value. At least, it does not seem to be protected by this clause, as drafted.

This is a case where personal representatives have transferred to themselves, as trustees for, say, an infant beneficiary. In fairness, the realised development value which may result from that should not be taxable. It may be that that disposal 644 comes under Clause 10—disposal by a donee—but that is not clear. It is a disposal by trustees for a donee and, as such, as we have said in the course of amendments, is less beneficial if it comes under Clause 10, than if it comes under this clause.

Let us take the next example. If Messrs. Dodson and Fogg, as the personal representatives, decide to transfer Blackacre to Mrs. Bardell, as a beneficiary under the will of the late Mr. Pickwick, instead of handing over to her the proceeds of sale—and there seems to be little difference between the proceeds of sale, if the person represented himself, and handing them over in specie, which we touched on in an earlier amendment—if she eventually sells, that sale, as I understand it, will be a taxable disposal. That seems to be grossly unfair and something of a nonsense. Again, it may come within Clause 10, because she will have been a donee under the will, but it will not be so beneficial to her as if it came under this clause.

Suppose, in another case, Pickwick left Blackacre to Snodgrass in his will, and Whiteacre to Mr. Jingle. After he dies, Snodgrass wants Whiteacre and not Blackacre, and Jingle wants Blackacre and not Whiteacre. So they ask Messrs. Dodson and Fogg, as the personal representatives, to draw up a deed of arrangement, swopping the properties. But Blackacre is worth £10 more than Whiteacre. So Jingle pays the £10 balance, and he receives Blackacre.

As I understand that, neither Snodgrass nor Jingle get the benefit of Clause 9 if they then dispose of either of their properties. However, Snodgrass gets the benefit of Clause 10 because he received his property without paying any consideration for it. Jingle does not, however. get the benefit of Clause 10 because he paid some consideration, however de minimis. Clause 10 applies only if there is no consideration passing.

I suppose one can shrug one's shoulders and say that is fair justice or, with Sam Weller, "There's a Providence in it all". I recollect that his father replied, "Of course, there is. What would become of the undertakers without it, Sammy? ". It seems unfair in those cases, and I imagine that this clause will give rise to a great deal of litigation if that sort of situation arises. I hope that the matter can be looked at again to see that the beneficiary in such cases does stand in the JPEG 645 shoes of the deceased if he has merely received the property straight from the personal representatives. There does not seem to be any difference between that and receiving the proceeds of sale. I have a suspicion that if the clause stands unamended it will be an invitation to litigation.

I conclude by again quoting Mr. Weller, not Sam, who said: "Battledore and shuttlecock's a wery good game when you ain't the shuttlecock and two lawyers the battledores, in which case it gets too excitin' to be pleasant."

The Minister of State, Treasury (Mr. Denzil Davies)
: The right hon. Member for Crosby (Mr. Page) has raised a number of interesting examples, and I would be foolish to try to give clear answers to every example. In the main I go along with most of what he said.

The case he first outlined was that of Mr. Pickwick dying and of personal representatives then transferring the property to trustees for beneficiaries or an infant. He is right on Clause 9, as I read it, that the exemption would not help in regard to the transfer from the personal representatives to themselves as trustees or, indeed, to anybody else as trustees, although whether to themselves or somebody else should not make any difference. That transfer would not be protected by Clause 9. Whether it is protected by Clause 10 is something that we can discuss later when we come to that clause, because the transfer will be, in most cases, for no value and would seem to be within the principles of Clause 10.

The right hon. Gentleman then dealt with the exchange of property, where Blackacre and Whiteacre are exchanged and a payment of £10 is made. He is again correct. If £10 is paid, that is a sale or a transfer at an undervalue, and does not get the benefit of Clause 10 in relation to costs of acquisition. However, since the transfer is merely for £10 at an undervalue, there will not be a tax charge at that time. I accept that later on, as I believe the right hon. Gentleman was implying, if the property was sold the cost of acquisition would be £10, and not the historical cost of acquisition, taking the matter back through previous ownerships. To that extent there may be an injustice, 646 but no doubt we can also deal with that when we debate Clause 10.

Once the property moves from the personal representatives to either trustees or beneficiaries, the benefit of Clause 9 is lost. Whether some other protection will be obtained, in relation to Clause 10 or otherwise, I shall consider in due course.

I accept that there may be problems here, and we shall look at his examples to see whether or not we are creating anomolies and try to make it as easy as possible for personal representatives in these cases. Of course, there will always be circumstances in which it is impossible to legislate, and there may be cases where people will have to think about the legislation and act upon it in relation to how property is transferred from personal representatives. The personal representatives will be under a duty, as they are now, to consider the taxation provisions for estate duty, capital gains tax and so on before they come to their final decisions on the dissolution of the estate.

JPEG 647 We have added one special factor where we have tried to suggest that the market valuation on 6th April 1965 is introduced instead of the cost of acquisition. Of course it is always possible to point to individual transactions such as a famous one which has taken place recently which make the hon. Gentleman's point for him. I do not think any of us would disagree with that in principle.

But hard cases, or even special cases, make bad law and I think that we need to look a little more at what the longer term effects will be if pension funds have to suffer development land tax. After all, the prime objective, as the Minister of State has readily agreed, is to provide a flow of income which will support the pension benefits of the members of those pension funds for the indefinite future.

In dealing with pension funds one should not devote too much importance to the fact that many of them are of relatively recent foundation. Since the Second World War pension funds as a form of investment institution have grown up and flourished in the years of prosperity up to a few years ago. Indeed, the greater consciousness of companies in providing for their employees both during their working lives and afterwards has led to a desirable spread of the institution of private pension funds by a number of companies. This, although it originally applied to the larger companies, has now spread to smaller ones.

724 In the longer term, these pension funds will have to redevelop their properties. They will not necessarily feel that they want to replace an office by an office, or a shop by a shop, or that they will have to redevelop a property purely within a 10 per cent. margin or a larger area for a fire escape. In the longer run, therefore, it would be damaging to the renewal of the sort of property investments which are held by pension funds—factories in which people work, shops in which business is done, and offices in which commerce takes place—if they were to be either discouraged from replacing their assets unless by exactly the sort of building which existed before, or if the incidence of development land tax, coupled with the redevelopment costs of pulling down an old building and building a new one at a high cost to replace it, make it difficult to re-establish a flow of income for a further generation of two for their beneficiaries.

It would, therefore, be a great mistake if the clause were to go through without an exemption for pension funds. Although the results may not be perceptible for five or 10 years or so, in the longer run pension funds are likely to be among the few important continuing bodies which hold land and redevelop it as circumstances dictate to produce a flow of income for their beneficiaries in a way which provides new buildings for industry and commerce. I think that point ought to be on record before we leave the discussion of this topic.
