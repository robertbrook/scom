    I now turn to Amendment No. 362, which defines the circumstances in which a disposal is regarded as forming part of a sale and lease back transaction, and makes consequential amendments to fit a sale and lease back into the general provisions about triggering deferred liabilities.

    Subsection 6(A) of the amendment defines a disposal forming part of a sale and lease back transaction as a disposal of the owner's interest in the whole or part of the land in which it subsists in return for a grant of a lease in the whole or part of that land. It is, therefore, apt JPEG 1053 to cover, not only the case where the owner disposes of his freehold interest in the whole or part of his land in return for a lease, but also where he has a lease and disposes of it in return for a sub-lease.     Subsection 6(B) provides that if the lease is subsequently disposed of the appropriate part of the deferred liability will be triggered of.

    Subsection 6(C) provides that if the disposal forming part of the sale and lease back transaction extends to only part of the interest in land a subsequent disposal of that retained interest is treated as if it were a corresponding part disposal of the original interest in land—that is, the interest in land held when the industrial development was commenced. As I said, this amendment, taken with the previous amendment, fulfills a promise we made on Second Reading, after consultations, that we would deal with the sale and lease back situation.

    I turn to New Clause 11, which is concerned with amalgamations and reconstructions, and, again, is to introduce a relieving provision in the sense of a deferral of tax in regard to amalgamations and reconstructions. The clause ensures that a disposal of an interest in land during the course of certain company reconstructions and amalgamations does not give rise to a DLT liability. The situation with which the new clause is intended to deal is where, as part of a scheme of reconstruction or amalgamation, one company takes over the whole or part of the business of another company, that other company receiving no consideration for the transfer of the business except by at the same time transferring the business liabilities.

    The consideration for the transfer takes the form of shares in the first company issued direct to the shareholders in the second. In other cases the consideration may go to the liquidator of the second company for distribution to its members. The new clause deals similarly with disposals during the course of the amalgamation of two or more registered industrial and provident societies.

    Amendment No. 359 provides that neither sort of disposal will trigger off any 1054 industrial deferment and Amendment No. 361 provides that company reconstructions or amalgamations will not trigger off any statutory undertaking deferment. The provisions about disposals during the course of company reconstructions or amalgamations parallel those for capital gains purposes. They are not very different from the capital gains tax provisions on amalgamations and reconstructions.

    Section 267 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1970, which is a corresponding capital gains tax provision provides for such a disposal to be treated as being made on a "no gain, no loss" basis.

    The provisions about disposals during the course of the amalgamation of two or more registered industrial and provident societies also parallel those for capital gains tax purposes, which also treat these as "no gain, no loss" disposals.
