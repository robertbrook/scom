With it we were discussing Amendment No. 58, in page 6, leave out lines 24 and 25.

To recapitulate briefly, subsection (5), as we understand it, is designed as a tax avoidance measure in its present form. I made the point that, if the Government Amendment, No. 58, were carried, the wording would be altered, and I described how subsection (5) would then apply in relation to the rest of the clause.

We accept that there could be, without subsection (5), a shuttlecock of transactions in the same interest in land between two connected pe ssons, and that, without some such provision as subsection (5) or the amendment, it might be possible in theory to achieve a mark-up in value in the land by transactions between connected persons. We think it reasonable that such an attempt to avoid tax should be prevented by the terms of the measure.

380 The Government's proposed method of doing this is to disallow the 10 per cent. mark-up on the cost of the chargeable person's acquisition of the interest as described in Base C or, in the case of Base A, to deprive him of the special addition which is a material part of the increase in value which the chargeable person can claim when establishing the base value for computation of the tax.

I do not want to anticipate what the Minister of State may say when he formally moves Amendment No. 58, but since the Bill was drafted it seems that the Treasury has realised that the current use value could not be affected by transactions between connected persons, and therefore there is no need to disallow the 10 per cent. margin of tolerance about which we were talking in another context at our last sitting, when Base B is involved. No doubt, the Minister of State, when he moves that amendment, will give the Governments authoritative explanation, and I hope that I am correct in interpreting its intention.

We recognise the problem which subsection (5) is designed to meet, but we would prefer it to be met by a different approach. Our suggestion is that the connected persons should in these cases be treated as one person. This, we feel, might not have exactly the same effect as the provision as it stands, but at least it would meet the point of avoiding artificial moves to increase the cost by reference to which the computation for development land tax would be made.

We feel that it would be more sensible to retain the basic principles of the three bases, A, B, and C, and to allow the special addition to be retained for the whole period during which the interest in land remained in the ownership of either of the connected persons, whichever of them at any point during that period was the actual owner.

This would be more equitable.
