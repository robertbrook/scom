Mr. Graham Page
: We are in the course of debating Amendments Nos. 3, 4 and 32, which deal with the rate of development land tax as set out in the Bill, and I do not wish to detain the Committee any longer from hearing the Minister's reply to the points already made. I merely say that of course we recognise that any tax on the disposal of any commodity is bound to be some sort of deterrent, if only a partial one. Our case on this is that the rate—whether 66⅔ per cent. for a certain part of the development value disposed of, or the full 80 per cent.—is almost a full deterrent.

We are supported in that view not only by the Treasury and the Secretary of State himself, by the reduction from 80 per cent. to 66⅔ per cent. over a certain part of the development value—and, indeed, by his giving the reason for the reduction as being that the higher rate would have caused a dry-up in the suply of land, or so he was advised. We also have the support of the professional bodies.

The case put by those who will actually be dealing in the land is a good one, but it is obviously suspect because they are pleading that they be relieved of a certain 46 amount of tax. But the professional bodies—those who have to operate in this field, not in the actual buying and selling of land to or from themselves, but in their professional capacity acting for others, also support a much lower figure than that in the Bill.

Whether one takes the lower figure at 50 per cent. or 60 per cent. is not, I believe, very material to the debate at this stage. We are putting the case that we think that the figure chosen by the Government is far too high, and that, according to the advice which we and the Government have received from the professional bodies, the figures should be between 50 per cent. and 60 per cent.; that is the point we are arguing with this amendment, and in order to be entirely co-operative and willing to compromise on this, we have put the figure at 60 per cent.

The main point is that, if in fact this tax is imposed at the rate desired by the Government, and if it does cause a dry-up in the supply of land, the only alternative put forward by the Government is that local authorities with enough money to bring forward sufficient land to meet may then be distributed to those wishing to develop it. That is an entirely impractical suggestion for the period during which this transitional tax is intended to operate.

The Public Expenditure White Paper—the statement of the Government's intention about public expenditure—makes it perfectly clear that they will not be prepared over the next few years to provide local authorities with enough money to bring forward sufficient land to meet demands. Even if the Government were prepared to do that, the delays, and indeed the increase in staff of local authorities and the expenditure of applying compulsory purchase over a wide area, would cause chaos to the whole industry of land development.

I want to say at once that we are discussing only the rate of tax for this period before the second appointed day. We are wholly opposed, and put forward no compromise whatever, to the second appointed day when the whole of the development value will be confiscated by the Government. So we are dealing only with the transitional stage now.

Two procedural points should be mentioned. We are debating three amendments and I hope that you will allow us, Mr. Fitch, to choose Amendment No. 4, which indicates the point of the debate JPEG 47 best and most succinctly, on which to have a Division.

Amendment No. 32 would allow a variation in the rate of tax with an affirmative resolution of the House. It is put forward as a tentative suggestion because there may be a time when a Government would wish to make a quick alteration in the rate of tax in order to deter or encourage, as the case may be, a particular kind of development. As it stands, the amendment does not say that the rate can be varied geographically or for particular purposes, but that is the intended implication of the amendment. It is not unusual that a rate of tax can be altered by a draft order put before the House and subject to affirmative resolution.

It may well be that when the Government see the effects of this tax, they will wish to encourage development in urban areas as against development in what we call the green field sites. It might be that, in order to provide that encouragement, one would allow a lower rate of tax on urban development. It is a tentative suggestion and is put forward to discover what the Government think about being given this flexibility for altering the rate, not behind the back of Parliament in any way, but by bringing it forward on a draft order for the approval of the House.

I shall not delay the Committee further. I intended only to sum up on those two points and to refer to the procedural points. I hope we shall hear from the Minister on the questions raised by my hon. Friends during the debate at our last meeting.

One of the questions raised during the debate which is fundamental to all our discussions is whether it is possible to distinguish between a development profit and an entrepreneurial profit. A number of hon. Gentlemen have suggested that it is not possible so to distinguish. I say that it is fundamental because, unless one can distinguish between the two, any rate of tax higher than the ordinary rate of corporation tax cannot be justified. That applies as much to a 60 per cent. rate of tax as to an 80 per cent. rate of tax. In accepting the principle of a special tax on development values the Opposition are, in principle, accepting the possibility of making this distinction. We think that in the Bill we have succeeded in making this distinction. It is a matter which we will have to discuss later to see how successful we have been in mak- JPEG 49 ing a distinction which is fundamental to the whole operation, and it would be better if the question were discussed in more detail on later amendments.

I come to the question of how we decide what is an appropriate rate of tax on development values. I start with a matter of principle. It seems right to the Government that, as development value is created by the community, in principle there should be no objection to any rate of tax the State may decide in acting for the community. Here is a value not created by entrepreneurial activity. It is created by the community, which is entitled to decide what proportion of that value should be recovered for the community.

It is not a question of punitive rates. Hon. Gentlemen introduced high moral attitudes into the discussion. It is not a question of the State disliking particular activities. It is a question of the right of the community to recover for itself a value which it creates. It is, therefore, appropriate that Parliament should decide the rate of tax; and whatever tax it decides could, in principle, be justified.

It is not a uniquely Socialist attitude. It is an attitude which has permeated a wide spectrum of political thinking for at least 100 years. Hon. Gentlemen from time to time, and whenever they dislike something, say "This is an example of dogmatism, Socialism, and so on." There are contradictions among the Opposition as to what is Socialism and what is not. On Second Reading the hon. Member for Hitchin (Mr. Stewart) said that a flat rate was a peculiarly Socialist conception. The hon. Member for Hove (Mr. Sainsbury), on the other hand, accepted a flat rate as a desirable conception. I think that in Committee, hon. Gentlemen should concentrate on the issues rather than on the abuse.

I think that this assessment is correct. I make these comments on it. First, we do not know what the effect will be. We cannot calculate it accurately on the evidence available, mainly because recent economic circumstances have confused the effect of the development gains charge. We do not know what the effect of the development gains charge on the supply of land has been because it has been operating at a time when there has been a marked fall in land prices and general low economic activity. Therefore, no one can be sure of the effect on the supply of land of the development land tax or of rates of tax such as the Government propose or, indeed, as the Opposition propose.

I noticed that the right hon. Gentleman in making his remarks this morning used an interesting phrase. I noted it, as it seemed to me to be striking. He said "If this rate of tax does cause a dry-up in the supply of land …". He said "If", so even the right hon. Gentleman in his position as advocate for this rate of tax, is not absolutely sure what the effect will be.

Hon. Members opposite have said that the Treasury is not primarily the Department concerned with land supply and that JPEG 51 this is a matter for the Department of the Environment. That is true. But I can assure hon. Members opposite that the Treasury is interested in the effects of its actions, although they may sometimes doubt it. We have considered the problem seriously and in the course of considering the Bill have had a number of confidential consultations with eminent figures in this industry. I was interested in their view, which was not a certain view. Like the right hon. Gentleman, they did not say "This will certainly have this effect". They said that it would probably have this effect. It is a matter of judgment. No one really knows what the degree of the effect will be.

The lessons I derive from this analysis are these. They are not the lessons which hon. Members opposite derive from the analysis. First, whatever the rate of tax, if there is to be a tax on development value, there must also be a positive element in the situation. I think that positive element is bound to be action by local authorities. That would be true of any rate of tax. Whether it is the Community Land Scheme or some other formula for local authority action, there must be some local authority action to make up for whatever effect a rate of tax may have on the supply of land.

After all, the Opposition are not in a position to deny the need for local authority action, and reference is frequently made to the £80 million which they provided for local authority acquisition of land for private development. I understand the point which hon. Gentlemen make when they say that there is a particular entrepreneurial action in finding certain types of land for development, and I understand that we need that sort of action to continue within the time scale of this legislation. Nevertheless, one can overdo local authority arguments about their incapacity to find land for private development.

At the moment, after all, taking total land for development the local authorities provide about 50 per cent. Admittedly, their contribution to land for private development has been very much smaller, though no doubt it was given some stimulus by the right hon. Gentlemen's £80 million.

We believe that the local authorities can operate in this field, and we have provided in the Public Expenditure White Paper estimates of the sort of contribution they may be able to make.

Mr. Dell

The trouble with Lord Barber's proposals is, not merely that they are today a constant source of embarrassment to the Opposition, but that they did not contain any such positive element as we have provided within the Community Land Scheme. Lord Barber proposed what was, in effect, a very high rate of tax—75 per cent. on many acquisitions—and yet he did not add to it this essential further element of positive compensating action by local authorities.

That is the first lesson I derive from this analysis. There must be action in some quarters by local authorities.

The second lesson I derive is that to reduce the risk of a land shortage—I say the risk, not a certainty—it is necessary for a period, particularly while the local authorities are building up their capacity to act on their own, to have a lower tranche. That is why we now propose that there should be this lower tranche.

I may comment, incidentally, that I believe that from the point of view of land supply development land tax is a very much better tax than development gains charge. Therefore, that is an argument for getting this bill legislated as quickly as possible.

I come specifically to the arguments which have been presented during the debate on land supply. I am not sure—this is another of the uncertainties involved—how far one can relate the number of transactions to the supply of land. One effect of the high prices in 1972–73 may have been that there were a number of transactions in respect of the same quantity of land by people engaging in speculative activity. One effect of the development gains charge may have been to eliminate certain types of speculative activity, thus reducing the number of transactions. Therefore, I do not think it right to argue that the fall in the number of transactions means a comparable fall in the coming of new land into the pipeline. In any case, the number of transactions is now rising. The probable explanation for this is that the supply of land has more to do with the economic situation, with the fall in land prices as compared with 1973–74, than with the tax situation.

For the future, we have existing stocks, local authority activity on a growing scale, and the fact that the market will be helped by the 66⅔ per cent. tranche. "this may be expected to give a modest boost to housebuilding among other things."
