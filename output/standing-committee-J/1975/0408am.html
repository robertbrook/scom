Mr. Latham: Here we are again. We start on a new clause—Clause 6—where we are concerned with the valuable and significant concession which is designed to be of assistance to vendors. The Committee will realise that there are basically two separate concessions in the clause as drafted. If the land was owned on White Paper day—12th September 1974—base A can be increased by 15 per cent. for each year before development takes place, up to a maximum of four years, or 60 per cent. If the land was bought between White Paper day and 1st May 1977, the proposed addition is to be 10 per cent. per year, up to a maximum of 40 per cent.

As a series of amendments have been grouped together and as most of them deal with the problem in a slightly different Col. 440 way, I shall try to deal with each in turn.

Amendment No. 55 would take out the requirement that the land has to be acquired before 1st May 1977, and it is therefore of relevance to the 10 per cent. addition. The purpose of the special addition is presumably to offset financing costs, and it is far from obvious why there should be a time limit on such a concession. The date appears to be entirely arbitrary, and I see no reason why it is necessary to have such a date at all.

At the conclusion of describing each amendment I shall say a brief word about financing costs in general.

Amendments Nos. 78 and 79, as the Committee will realise, are fall-back positions. If the idea of eliminating the date 1st May 1977 altogether does not appeal to the Government, the Opposition suggest two alternatives. One is All Fools Day 1984—a year of Orwellian fame, by which time the complete workers' State will have been created if this Government remain in office—[Interruption.]—I say that without delight or expectation. The other proposed alternative is St. George's Day 1978, which seems a fine patriotic date to symbolise the return of a Conservative Government. It is also Shakespeare's reputed birthday, and the prospect of the continuation of this Government brings to mind Richard II's remark on his abdication.

"Let's talk of graves, of worms, and epitaphs … Write sorrow on the bosom of the earth."

Most of the earth will be socialised, anyway, if the Community Land Act comes into operation.

Those are the alternatives we propose in Amendments Nos. 78 and 79.

Amendment No. 80 would extend the number of years for which the concession is available from four to six. It is a particularly important amendment. Six years is far more appropriate regarding the larger land assemblies which often take longer than four years to bring to fruition, especially as considerable planning delays often occur.

It is unreasonable when one considers, that in documents such as "The Strategic Plan for the South-East" it is envisaged that large tracts of land will be assembled for development in the growth areas. For example, where land has been purchased with planning consent for 1,000 houses, but infrastructure or other considerations Image Col. 441 require a planning condition that the houses should be built at, say, 100 per year—which is a perfectly normal phasing condition for local authorities to impose—it will be seen how ridiculous is the four-year limitation in relation to financing costs. I believe that our proposal for six years would be much fairer.

Amendment No. 52 would scrap the requirement that land has to be bought before 12th September 1974, the White Paper day, if it is to get the 15 per cent. per year addition or, alternatively, the 10 per cent. addition if bought before 1st May 1977. The amendment attempts to meet the method of financing carrying charges by giving a 60 per cent. addition to base value in every case.

Amendments No. 98 and Amendment No. 99 are selected separately and deal with a different point and no doubt, Mr. Fitch, we shall reach these later.

That leaves Amendments No. 56, 57, which are amendments to Schedule 2, and which are consequential on the earlier amendments which the Opposition have put down.

Before saying a few brief words on the question of financing costs, I refer to an important point which is not the subject of our amendment, although it would be met by the amendment which seeks to delete dates altogether. It is a subject which has been raised in submissions to the Committee by the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors. This says:

"In our memorandum on the Inland Revenue statement of February 1975, we said that it would be more equitable to use 4th February 1975 as the qualifying date for the purpose of the special addition in Clause 6(2) to base (a). The addition was not announced until 4th February 1975 in the Inland Revenue Press Statement, and so purchasers of interests in land after 12th September 1974 but before 4th February 1975 could have had no knowledge of the differential rates until the latter date."

I think that is a fair point, too. It would be met if the Committee were to accept Amendment No. 52.

I turn briefly to the question of carrying charges generally. I am not looking a gift horse in the mouth. I appreciate that Clause 6 is an important concession. It cannot be said too often that the private house-building industry in particular—I have no specialist knowledge of commercial development, although other members of the Committee have—is necessarily a highly- Col. 442 geared industry. The buying of land in order to allow development to take place must clearly be done by borrowing, and the repayment of the mortgage which has been raised by the land must come entirely from cash flow from sales of the houses. So there are many points during the process of development when the developer is in a dangerously highly-geared situation should the market change quickly, as it often does in house building and when the developer is exposed to additional carrying charges. The viability of his business can be messed up if the market situation changes at a time when he is most highly geared.

Interest rates have dropped recently, although they may increase again. However, a multiplier of 10, such as is suggested for the four years under the provisions for land bought since White Paper day, is certainly unlikely to do anything to compensate fully for interest paid, bearing in mind that many developers will still be financing the construction of the land which they bought at higher rates.

I hope that I have explained the amendments fully. They are important amendments, and I hope that the Government will not think us churlish in asking them to go further than Clause 6, but the Opposition believe that the Government are not wholly seized of the importance to the developer of financing carrying charges.

I have never criticised capitalists for operating as capitalists. I expect them to operate as capitalists, but the problems of 1971 and 1972 occurred as a result of what the Tory Government did.

As a result of what the Tory Government did, they saddled developers with enormous burdens, and the blame does not lie entirely with people who relied on the previous Government and who hoped and thought that the previous Government were one of sound fiscal management, which they were not. As a result, such people suffered, and they still suffer.

In order to compensate developers to some extent we have introduced a special addition because of the high cost of borrowing and the commitments those people introduced during that period. In good faith many of them relied on policies of the previous Government which after a while destroyed many property companies and caused great difficulties. That is why we introduced this special addition to help developers finance acquisitions that were made during a very difficult period.

The hon. Member talked about the costs of builders and the cost of borrowing. We accept entirely that builders, like other business men and those in other industries, have costs of borrowing. But the learned Gentleman should not forget costs of borrowing are generally allowable for Corporation Tax purposes. When a builder incurs costs in relation to borrowing, in most cases he gets relief for that cost against his profit. If he makes a profit on the sale of his houses, he will normally be allowed a deduction in relation to the cost of borrowing.

We are not talking about denying or tapering off this cost in relation to builders' profits. We are merely talking about the Col. 444 cost of financing the cost of the land itself, which was bought in many cases at a time when land prices were extremely high—artificially high—and when interest rates were high and costs of financing were considerable. This is an addition to the present general situation where interest payments, in general, are deductible by a developer or builder when he develops.

This concession is a reasonable one. The dates we have chosen are to some extent arbituary. I am not going to defend these dates and say that they are not arbritary. Any date one chooses must, in the order of things, be arbitrary. We have thought that a period of four years is reasonable. The dates chosen in Clause 6–1st May1977 and 30th September, 1974—are reasonable dates. These periods, in view of the background and the reason this concession is being introduced, are recognised as reasonable by most people who made representations to us.

Obviously the taxpayer would like the concession to be extended. There is nothing wrong in representations being made for more concessions, but at the end of the day the Inland Revenue and the Government will then have to decide what is fair, not only to the particular taxpayer, but to taxpayers in general and what is fair to the country. We think that we have arrived at what is a reasonably fair compromise, giving developers considerable and generous reliefs to compensate them for a difficult time when they acquired land at high rates of interest and high cost.

The hon. Gentleman mentioned the representation of the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors in relation to the debate 4th February 1975. He said that that should be the date and not 13th September 1974. I do not promise to accept what he said. I have not listened to the argument before. I will look into it. I cannot say any more than that and I am not holding out any hope. As I had not come across this point before, it is only fair to the Committee that I should look into it and consult with a view to seeing whether it is possible to do something about it. At the moment, my inclination is to leave the dates as they are.

I am sorry that I have not been more forthcoming, but we have gone a long way to give relief. Therefore, it would not be fair if we went further and accepted one or any of these amendments, all of which Image Col. 445 seek to extend the time.
