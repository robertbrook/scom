The Minister of State, Treasury (Mr. Denzil Davies)
: Perhaps I can deal with a number of points raised by the right hon. Member for Crosby (Mr. Page) at the end of our last sitting.

The first point that the right hon. Gentleman raised concerned notices of part disposals. He suggested that the exclusion of part disposals, under paragraph ( c), from the scope of sub-paragraph (2) of paragraph 37 of the schedule would lead to the Board receiving large numbers of notifications of trivial part disposals. Perhaps I may point out to the right hon. Gentleman that in fact paragraph 37 applies only where the person making the disposal is chargeable to DLT as a result.

Paragraph 37 in general deals with situations which are not covered by paragraphs 35 and 36. Paragraph 37 is intended to ensure that the Inland Revenue is notified of all other disposals of interests in land which may give rise to liability to DLT. This is done by requiring a person who is chargeable to DLT as a result of a disposal of an interest in land to submit a notice if the circumstances are not covered by pargraphs 35 and 36.

Under sub-paragraph (2), a notice is not required if the disposal relates to a dwelling house which with any connected land comprises one acre or less—the disposal is not a part disposal and the consideration does not exceed £25,000. The purpose of this provision is to give a blanket assurance to vendors of most private houses, and their advisers, that they need not consider the possibility of a DLT notification being required, and thus to guard against 1538 notices being given out of excessive caution.

We therefore do not think that in fact excluding part disposals will mean that the Board will receive a large number of notifications of trivial part disposals, as paragraph 37 applies only where the person making the disposal is chargeable to DLT as a result.

This provision is similar to those in Sections 78 and 85 of the Taxes Management Act which apply for income tax and corporation tax respectively. New Clause 4 provides that where a person transfers a business to a company, that transfer should not rank as disposal of an interest in land for DLT purposes, and the acts of the person shall be treated as the acts of the company. This means that the transferor will never have any DLT to bear in consequence of the transfer, even though part of the consideration may be cash; nor will there be any liability if and when he disposes of the shares. On a subsequent disposal of an interest in land by the company the base values will be derived from the transferor's acquisition cost.

New Clause 5 is different in that it is based on the full capital gains tax rules as JPEG 1575 set out in Paragraph 15 of Schedule 19 of the Finance Act 1969. Unlike new Clause 4, the transfer is regarded as a DLT disposal, but there are certain reliefs. It provides that on such a transfer the realised development value is reduced by a "qualifying amount"—again, that is the capital gains tax language.

New Clause 4 in effect provides that the transfer should be ignored, so that no tax arises at that point, and the company is enabled to look through to the acquisition cost of the transferor for the purposes of any subsequent disposal. Under new Clause 5 the transfer remains a DLT disposal, but the development value realised by the transferor is reduced according to the extent to which the transfer proceeds are in the form of shares.

The new clauses are not acceptable for a number of reasons. However, we are looking at the problem, and we shall bring forward an amendment on Report to to try to deal with it. I do not want the Committee to be under any illusions about the difficulty of drafting a new clause to cover every eventuality, and I do not think our amendment will go as far as the new clauses would require. However, I shall outline the difficulties.

Under New Clause 4 the transferor completely escapes DLT, despite the fact that he may have realised development value. The company will not be able to use the consideration it gave for base value purposes. In fact, there will be no arrangements for picking up the tax at a later stage. The arrangements under New Clause 5 are inadequate in that nothing is triggered when the company disposes of its interest in land itself and the transferor may have no intention of selling the shares.

That is why it is extremely impractical to follow the capital gains tax example in relation to DLT.
