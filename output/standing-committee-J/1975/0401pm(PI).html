Mr. Graham Page: When the Committee rose at One o'clock I was in course of moving Amendment No. 24, in page 74, line 37, leave out 'no'.

We are also discussing, as had been explained, the following amendments: No. 25, in page 75, line 16, leave out:

These amendments combine to make a comprehensive amendment.

Without getting out of order, I hope to refer back to earlier discussions we have had, and to refer on in order to find some definitions. To refer back first, we had a very comprehensive debate on Clause 2 and discussed the sort of interests in land which were covered by the provisions of Clause 2. Some of them reappear in paragraph 8(2) of Schedule 1. It is to those that paragraph 8(1)(c) applies. That paragraph states that when they are assessing

"the market value of an interest in land immediately before a project of material development is begun"

we should take no account

"of any interest which is an incumbrance falling within sub-paragraph (2) …".

Thus we disregard those incumbrances entirely in ascertaining the market value.

The ascertainment of market value governs the amount of the development value, which itself, governs the amount of tax to which the person will be chargeable. Let us assume that a freeholder carries out a deemed disposal—that is, Col. 248 he starts a project of material development, Then if paragraph 8 of Schedule 1 applies, we assess the market value of the interest that he is deemed to dispose of at that moment and to re-acquire at the same moment without taking regard of these other interests of the person who is entitled to the benefit of an easement or of the benefit of a restrictive covenant.

Those interests are bound in practice to reduce the value of the freehold of that land, or even if it is a leasehold that the developer is holding. If there are those restrictions, they in fact reduce the market value of the land. It is thus a fiction if we say that they are not to be taken into account for otherwise we are valuing the land at a greater value than in fact it is worth. Therefore, the chargeable person—in my example, the owner of the freehold who is carrying out the development—will be charged on the basis that these interests do not exist.

But Clause 2 tells us that we must take some of those interests into account and that the person who is entitled to the benefit of them will be assessed to development land tax. It thus seems that there would be a form of double taxation.

I am leaving for a moment a matter which comes later, namely, contingent liability, but taking it up to that stage, in Clause 2 we value the benefit of having an easement over land, or having a restrictive covenant, and then we are told that the subservient owner—the owner of the land which is subject to the easement or restrictive covenant or other right expressed here—will pay on the basis that it does not exist.

The excuse for that is that if eventually he has to pay to remove the easement or the restrictive covenant, he is entitled to treat that as a contingent liability. Up to that point one can say "It is true that you have been charged tax on the basis that you will not be liable to pay out for this other interest, but when you pay out for it you will be entitled to bring that into account and treat it as a contingent liability." That means, of course, the possibility of his paying tax and then having to reclaim it eventually when a payment out is made.

The story does not end there. Contingent liability does not really mean contingent liability at all. I shall spare the Committee returning to Humpty Image Col. 249 Dumpty and "Through the Looking Glass" and saying that the words mean what I intend them to mean and do not take any notice of what they are; I make the words that I use do a lot of work—I am not quoting that quite correctly, but it was the gist of Humpty's statement to Alice.

We are referred to Schedule 2 to find out what one does about contingent liabilities. Paragraph 22(1) says:

"In determining for the purposes of this Act the consideration for the disposal of an interest in land, no account shall be taken, in the first instance, of any contingent liability assumed by the chargeable person, by the person acquiring the interest or by any other person."

In assessing the market value—that is, in assessing the consideration—no account is to be taken of the contingent liability.

So no account is to be taken of the other interests in the land, such as easements and restrictive covenants, but they are to be treated as contingent liabilities, and then no account is to be taken of contingent liabilities. I am completely puzzled as to what happens.

I go on to Schedule 2(22)(2) which says:

"If it is subsequently shown to the satisfaction of the Board that a contingent liability which was not taken into account in determining the consideration for a disposal has become enforceable and is being or has been enforced, such adjustment, whether by way of a further assessment or the discharge or repayment of tax or otherwise, shall be made as is required in consequence."

That is a rather vague statement, but it seems to say that account shall be taken of the contingent liabilities when they bite and when one has to pay. They are contingent liabilities that have not been taken into account in assessing the market value but one has taken them into account under Schedule 1(9).

Where are we? At one stage we are told that the other interests in land will be valued and assessed and development value of them will be charged to tax against the person who owns them. Next, we are told that they will not be included in the market value of the freeholder or lease-holder and, therefore, he must pay tax on them. Then we are told that if he does that he is entitled to bring them in as contingent liabilities. Further on we are told that contingent liabilities are not to be taken into account at all in considering the consideration.

I am confused. I hope I have not confused the Committee more than I have confused myself.
