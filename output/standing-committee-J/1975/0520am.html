However, the criterion used for the basis for interest on unpaid tax is not the due date but the reckonable date.

We would not attach so much importance to the question of interest on unpaid tax were it not for the fact that the interest proposed will be extremely high at 9 per cent. Furthermore, 9 per cent. interest in many quite plausible cases might have to be paid over two or three years. The Minister did not accept our arguments last night that in many cases the appeals procedures might take at least a year, and possibly two or three years, during a period in which the matter of the tax is under dispute.

If one assumes that in two and a half years' time the rate of tax will be 80 per cent., and the rate of interest on unpaid tax is to be computed three months from the reckonable date, if our amendments are not accepted in some form there will be many who will be paying over 100 per cent. in taxation under the terms of the Bill. That is unfair, even on the Government's terms.

It is unfair to allow a situation where many people, because of the lengthy, complex, protracted nature of the appeals procedure—the Bill does not allow for any speedy appeal procedure—will be paying more than 100 per cent. in taxation, and that will happen if the existing position concerning the reckonable date, and the period from which interest to be charged, is maintained.

Amendments Nos. 419 and 432 attempt as a small and, to my mind, unsatisfactory step, merely to follow the logic of the Bill. They suggest that, if the due date is to be as defined on page 153, as either the reckonable date or a date based upon the notice of assessment, the period from which interest on unpaid tax is to run should at least reflect that. That is the very least to be expected from the Bill.

However, my own feeling is that we should—I hope to carry my hon. Friends with me—press much more firmly for Amendments Nos. 229 and 421 to be accepted. These amendments propose Image Col. 1433 that the period during which interest should be payable on unpaid tax should run from the point at which the assessment has been agreed. That was the situation before the Finance (No. 2) Act 1975, and it is an eminently fair arrangement.

If we accept the absolute minimum position where the interest runs from the notice of assessment we will have the inspectorate issuing absurdly high assessments at a very early stage, as is already proving to be the practice under the terms of the Finance (No. 2) Act 1975. The taxpayer is then faced with paying tax which both sides consider to be absurdly high, and trying to recoup something at a later stage, with all the problems of endeavouring to get interest on that over-payment. Alternatively, he is faced with the situation of having his interest on unpaid tax computed from that point.

There is also an additional problem. We are lengthening the procedure which could face a taxpayer, but it is not only the question of appeals. There is also the slowness with which district valuers appear to proceed these days, possibly through no fault of their own and perhaps due to the burdens placed upon them by central Government.
