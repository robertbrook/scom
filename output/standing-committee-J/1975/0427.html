Mr. Davies
: The answer to that is "Yes", and this is how the legislation is drafted. I shall come to that point in a moment.

592 Once there is a grant of an option, that is treated as the creation of a new interest in land. As the legislation is drafted, where that option, or, indeed, where the total purchase price for the land in respect of which the option is granted, includes development value, the money received for that option is taxable. The actual money received for the option is chargeable to DLT.

As an example of the development land tax case and development value case, let us consider a situation where someone acquires an option to buy an acre of land and the price of the option is, say, £100 and the price of the acre of land, if he exercises the option, is £19,900. Let us suppose that the right hon. Gentleman gets an option to buy a plot of land; he pays £100 for the option and, if he wants to buy the land, he must pay another £19,900 for one acre. I am assuming here that that £19,900 plus the £100—that is, £20,000—would reflect development value. In other words, he is buying land which has substantial development value. In such a situation where the ultimate purchase, if it takes place, would be reflecting development value, the £100 option is chargeable to development land tax.

The hon. Gentleman said that it would not be taxed at that time and, although the Bill provides that it could be taxed at that point, in practice it will not be, because, as happens for capital gains tax purposes, the Revenue will wait to see whether the purchase goes through, as it would in most cases, when the whole lot would be taxed as one amalgam.

But in theory, and in practice sometimes, the £100 is chargeable to DLT and there is no base value to set against it, which is what one of the amendments seeks to do. There is no base value to set against it, because the Bill and the capital gains tax legislation are drafted in such a way as to assume that, when an option is granted, something new is created and the cost of creating that something new is nothing. There is no cost for creating that option; it merely means a man saying "For £100 I will grant you an option to buy this land." Therefore, we contend—this is how the clause is drawn up—that in reality there is no base value for that option. It is something plucked out of the air almost, and sold for £100 in my example.

JPEG 593 In that instance, in theory tax would be chargeable immediately on the £100, subject to the assessment machinery, and the balance of the tax would be payable on the £19,900. But, at the end of the day, of course, it would just be tax on £20,000 and the base values would be taken into account—bases A, B and C—in calculating the tax on the £19,900. Therefore, no more tax would be paid. In effect, tax is paid on a kind of instalment basis if it is charged on the grant of the option. The amount of tax payable on £100, if tax were paid, is taken into account in the total final reckoning when we consider the tax payable on the extra £19,900. So tax is payable ultimately on £20,000, as it would be, anyway, if there was no option and if the land was sold for £20,000.

Let us take another situation where there is no development value in the land, as in the simple case of an option fur £100 to acquire an acre of land for £400, so that the total cost of buying that acre would be £500. Let us assume that there is no development value: the figures have been pitched in that way to reflect the lack of development value. There would then be no charge on the grant of the options because the option does not relate to the purchase of land which has any development value.

We are concerned here only with options to buy land when the purchase price at the end of the day reflects development value. That is the only case where the option is taken into consideration in coming to an assessment of tax under this legislation.

I hope that I have clarified the matter. We are concerned, not with options to buy land at current use value, but only with options to buy land at development value. In theory, the option is chargeable and at the end of the day the balance of the price is chargeable, but the total tax will remain the same in either case.
