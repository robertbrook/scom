: We now come to a deceptively bland clause which introduces the interaction of development land tax with other taxes. It occupies a page and a half of the Bill, and on it hangs the whole of Schedule 6. Therefore, in discussing the amendments to the clause we should bear in mind that there may be points which need to be followed through in Schedule 6, and, as in this case, there may be a consequential amendment in Schedule 6.

The amendment relates to the introductory arrangements for the interaction of taxes, as set out in subsection (1). It deals with situations in which development land tax falls to be charged on realisation of development value, which is also brought into account for the purposes of certain other taxes. Those taxes include chargeable gains, tax on profits or gains of a trade, estate duty, capital transfer tax, and then in paragraph ( e):

"section 80 or section 82 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1970",

where certain sums which may appear to be received as capital are taxed as rent and, therefore, as income.

In the case of Section 80, if a lease is granted as a premium, part of the premium may be taxed as income, and the purpose of including Section 80 is to provide the proper relief. Section 81 relates to a case where there is not a premium, but there 1216 may be an assignment. Under the Bill as drafted, there appears to be no provision for relief to the income tax which may be charged.

I hope that the Government will accept this amendment or produce something similar. As they included Sections 80 and 82, I am worried, because the Government must have looked at Section 81 and decided not to put it in. I hope that that is an accidental omission, and I shall be glad to hear the Minister's comments.

The Minister of State, Treasury (Mr. Denzil Davies)
: This amendment seeks to include Section 81 as one of the provisions for which interaction relief can be allowed for development land tax.

Section 81 is an anti-avoidance section. So are Sections 80 and 82, although Section 81 is aimed at a transaction in many ways even more artificial than would be the case under Section 80 or Section 82.

Section 81 is aimed at avoidance of tax by granting a lease for a high premium and a sub-standard rent. The premium would be subject to capital gains tax while the rent would be liable to income tax or corporation tax.

Paragraph 4 of Schedule 6 gives interaction relief where part of a premium on the grant of a lease is charged as income, under Section 80, or where an amount is chargeable as income on a sale with a right to reconveyance—Section 82. It does not allow relief, as the hon. Gentleman has said, where there is an income charge under Section 81. These amendments seek to fill that gap.

In logic, possibly one could argue, as the hon. Gentleman has argued, that once relief is given for Section 80 and Section 82, there is no reason why relief should not be given for Section 81. But Section 81 is aimed at even more artificial transactions than either Section 80 or Section 82 and, although representations have been made to us that even a Section 81 situation could be genuine, the bodies which made these representations have been unable to come up with any situation which was genuine and would be taxed under Section 81 and denied relief because of the exclusion of Section 81 from the interaction relief provisions of this legislation.

However, it appears illogical on the face of it, and although we are dealing with anti-avoidance provisions, it could fairly be said that if a person enters into JPEG 1217 an anti-avoidance transaction and gets his fingers burnt, why should we give him relief for development land tax. That is one view. On the other hand, it could be said that we give him some relief for Section 80 and Section 82 anyway, why not go all the way. Without making a commitment on whether relief shall be extended to Section 81, and without accepting the amendment, I am prepared to consider the matter. I want to think about whether some genuine transactions would be caught.

Therefore, the addition of an extra item relating to Section 488 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act would bring into consideration another tax which is on the statute book but which, as the Bill stands, is not dealt with for the purposes of interaction.

Section 488 is a very wide ranging anti-avoidance section which applies wherever
  • "( a) land, or any property deriving its value from land, is acquired with the sole or main object of realising the gain through disposing of the land, or
  • ( b) land is held as trading stock, or
  • ( c) land is developed with the sole or main object of realising a gain from disposing of the land when developed, JPEG 1219 and any gain of a capital nature is obtained from the disposal of the land—
    1. by the person acquiring, holding or developing the land, or by any connected person, or
    2. where any arrangement or scheme is effected as respects the land which enables a gain to be realised by any indirect method, or by any series of transactions, by any person who is a party to, or concerned in, the arrangement or scheme;
    and this subsection applies whether any such person obtains the gain for himself or for any other person."
    • I felt it right to quote that in extenso because it is necessary to see how wide ranging Section 488 is. It is clearly designed to catch cases where transactions have been set up in such a way among different parties, between a person and a company, and so on, with the idea of realising profit from developing land or other transactions in land which would not be caught directly by any specific taxes on the statute book.

    Section 488 has had considerable sway in recent years. It may not be fully appreciated by the Treasury how wide a sway that is, because many transactions which could potentially have been caught by Section 488 have never seen the light of day. I know from my many years' experience as a banker that many straightforward transactions which involved land have had to be reconsidered, sometimes shelved temporarily or even abandoned, because of the danger of a Section 488 assessment at income tax rates. Taken in conjunction with other taxation which might be levied on parts of the transaction in a different form, perhaps within a company—whereas the Section 488 assessment would apply to the individual—this assessment could produce such a high rate of tax in the ultimate that it would make the transaction uneconomic. Indeed, it could produce a rate of tax in excess of 100 per cent., because a high rate of personal tax coupled with other forms of taxation may produce no residual profit, or go beyond that.

    It is true that there are in theory procedures whereby clearance can be obtained for bona fide transactions which might otherwise be caught by Section 488. But since inspectors are naturally reluctant to throw away all prospect of calling Section 488 in aid if at a later stage in the proceedings they might wish to make use of it, there is naturally a great reluctance on their part to give clearance in advance under Section 488. This is and has been a considerable disincentive to transactions, 1220 many of which were designed for the avoidance or lessening of tax. In conjunction with that, many straightforward transactions have also been frustrated.

    It seems to me that the whole reason behind Section 488 is that it is the intention of the Revenue that a very high rate of tax should be suffered by those who make substantial gains out of trading in or the development of land. That is what the development land tax is designed to do. Without that tax one can see the reason for Section 488, but with development land tax that case is very greatly reduced. Within a company, development land tax could be suffered at 66⅔ per cent., 80 per cent. and, eventually, 100 per cent. At the same time the shareholder in that company might pay tax on the same transaction at anything up to 83 per cent.
