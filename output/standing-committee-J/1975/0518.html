Question proposed, That this schedule, as amended, be the Sixth Schedule to the Bill.

Mr. Ian Stewart: We had a long discussion on Thursday on the technical aspects of the interaction of taxes. I have been reading the OFFICIAL REPORT to pick out the various points where the Minister showed his generosity and his warm heart and also to consider some of the comments from his ice-cool brain. I should like to refer to one or two points. In two cases they straddle Clause 33 and Schedule 6 and we had amendments down in both places, so I hope that it is in order to deal with these even though the main debate took place under Clause 33.

The first relates to Amendment No. 215 to Clause 33 and Amendment No. 274 to Schedule 6, and the discussion we had about Section 81. I do not want to refer to this at length or to go into the arguments again, but after some consultations I am advised that the representations which were made about this had underlying them genuine non-avoidance cases even though they may not have been put in this way to the Revenue. I hope, therefore, that this can be reconsidered.

The second point is the more important one. It concerns the discussions that we had on Amendment No. 380 to Clause 33 and Amendment No. 390 to Schedule 6. These were the amendments dealing with Section 488 and the possibility of interaction with development land tax. We saw dangers that the accumulation of taxes might lead to a charge at a very high rate. The Minister gave us some assurance that it would not normally be the case that the Revenue would seek to press an assessment under Col. 1280 Section 488 if it could be shown that development land tax had applied to the underlying transaction, or at least not to aggregate the two. However, I think that there are still some points of doubt about this.

The Minister said this:

"On the first point, therefore—the transfer of land—the position is that the tax would be deductible in computing the Case VI charge under Schedule D."—[OFFICIAL REPORT, Standing Committee J, 13th May 1976; c. 1221.]

He went on to make further comments about this in columns 1223 and 1224, concluding that "There is no problem"—an attractive way of summing up the situation, but I wonder whether it is precisely true.

The difficulty seems to arise in that the Bill provides generally that development land tax is not allowable as a deduction against other taxes. The reference, I think, is Clause 33(2). This may have been a slip of the tongue and perhaps the Minister was actually referring to that part of the value to which development land tax applied. I am not sure. He may have been referring to chargeable realised development value on which development land tax was charged. It may be that he meant exactly what he said, but if I understand it correctly this would be the only case in the Bill where development land tax itself was to be treated as a specific deduction for the case of other taxes.

Clause 33(2) relates to profits or gains, and there might be something in the definition which deals with that point. In column 1223 the Minister quoted a part of Section 488 dealing with profits or gains under Case I of Schedule D, and he later cross-referenced that to the computation of tax under Case VI of Schedule D. That illustrates the fact that we need precise guidance. Even if we cannot have a specific relief against Section 488, we need to know exactly what we are talking about.

My next point relates to Amendment No. 265 to Schedule 6. In moving that amendment I referred to cases where there was interaction between development land tax and development gains charge. The Minister answered the point by referring to provisions in Clause 35 regarding current use value as a means, in practice, of ensuring that in one of the cases I mentioned there would not be a tax both to development gains charge Image Col. 1281 and development land tax on the same increment in value. That was a satisfactory answer. However, the Minister made this important comment:

"Perhaps I should add that the Schedule 6 offset against the chargeable gain will, first of all, go against the development gain element—there will be a top slice relief first."—[OFFICIAL REPORT, Standing Committee J, 13th May 1976; c. 1240.]

The Minister then elaborated on that.

I was not able from reading the Bill to find exactly where this was provided. It is not clear to me, and it would be of assistance to know whether this is a provision of the Bill as it stands or whether it is a gloss on the method of interpretation which the Minister has given to us. If the latter, it is important that it should be formalised so that it is well understod that in cases relating to Clause 36, as well as to Clause 35, there is a proper provision for offset. The Committee would be grateful for further elucidation on that point from the Minister.

I do not want to go further than that on the detailed points. We raised a number of topics on which we were disappointed, but, equally, we found that the Government's thinking had moved towards our own on a number of others.

However, I want to make one very important point which has run as something of a theme through our discussions. The rates of the development land tax make it so much more important that we carefully define the boundaries of the tax for the purposes of interaction with others. Where one is dealing with taxes at lower rates, even capital gains tax and corporation tax, the difference is not great. Here we are dealing with taxes where two-thirds of the total value is the lowest rate at which the tax will be applied, where 80 per cent. is the standard rate, and it is expected to rise to 100 per cent.

Therefore, all the areas of doubt and all the precedents which have been called in aid from the operation of other taxes need to be looked at again because of the higher rates at which this tax will be assessed. Our discussions last Thursday on advance corporation tax illustrated that there comes a point where one cannot go on repeating the provisions which have been applicable in other cases. If one reduces the amount of corporation Col. 1282 tax payable by a whole sector of companies, the ACT provisions will bear more hard on those companies as a result of the introduction of this tax than was presumably intended at the outset. A similar case could be made in relation to Section 488.

I take the hon. Gentleman's point that some provisions which at one time were vital may not now be vital because of interaction with DLT. We shall look at that general point.

The third point that the hon. Gentleman made related to Amendment No. 265—the top slice relief against development gains charge. I am advised that it is not necessary to have any specific provision, as the relief operates in that way, anyway. The calculation of a development gain by way of deducting something from the chargeable gain is the way in which the relief works. If we reduce the chargeable gain by the DLT slice, that automatically reduces the development gain. In other words, it goes automatically against the development gain and is a deduction from the development gain.

I put a gloss on that by saying that it is a top slice relief. It is not a top slice relief in the sense that it is written into the Bill as a top slice relief, but if the development gain is charged at 83 per cent. the effect is that it is a top slice relief because it is a relief against that higher liability tax. I am advised that it is not necessary for the legislation to provide for that, because it works like that automatically.

In some such cases there might be a conditional contract, and the rules regarding conditional contracts are treated differently. The definition is different for development land tax purposes than for capital gains tax purposes.

For example, for development land tax a conditional contract which is conditional only on completion and payment of the purchase price, is treated as an interest in land acquired at the moment of the conditional contract. For the purposes of DLT, any such acquisition which occurs before the appointed day is outside the DLT rules. On the other hand, for capital gains tax rules—and, indeed, for development gains charge rules, which follow the capital gains tax rules—conditional contracts are not treated in the same way.

If we were to abolish development gains charge in relation to everything that happened after the appointed day, there could have been a pre-appointed day situation which was exempt from development land tax in a conditional contract situation, but under the capital gains tax rules that would throw up a gain later after the appointed day. Without a development gains charge, that gain would be charged to capital gains tax, although it would have been a development gain, because the development gains charge would not apply.

They are not to impose two lots of tax in relation to what happens after the appointed day. However, there are some cases where there will not be a DLT charge, but without the development gains charge there would be no charge at all, except, possibly, a capital gains charge in relation to what happened after the appointed day. These clauses are intended to deal with that kind of situation.
