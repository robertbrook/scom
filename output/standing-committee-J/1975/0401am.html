Mr. Graham Page: I beg to move Amendment No. 37, in page 71, line 15, leave out from 'relates' to end of line 16.

This amendment seeks to leave out the words:

"and the time of the grant of that permission shall be treated as the time at which that project is begun."

In paragraph 3 of Schedule 1 we are dealing with the cases where there has been a grant of planning permission for a limited period—that is, the use of property for a certain purpose has been allowed for a limited period and, when that period runs out, this paragraph takes effect to decide whether the renewal of permission for the use is a project of material development.

In order to explain the reason for seeking to omit the words, the paragraph provides that if any material development is carried out in accordance with planning permission which was granted after 12th September 1974 for a limited period, and subsequently the planning permission was granted for the retention of that use on the land, then

"the retention or continued use … shall be treated as constituting a project of material development consisting of a material change in the use of the land to which the planning permission referred … and the time of the grant of that permission shall be treated as the time at which that project is begun."

As I read the paragraph, the grant of that permission may be either the original grant or the extension of it. If it is the original grant and if the matter is to date back to that date, it may be disadvantageous to the developer and he ought to have a chance to query that.

Most of our amendments to Schedule 1 seek to give as much discretion to the developer as possible in deciding the date on which a specified operation starts. The Col. 196 date on which a developer's project starts make a very great difference to him because it may make a difference to the amount of development value on which he will be charged tax in any financial year and, secondly, it is important for the developer to be able to choose the area of the project. I do not mean that he should be able to go against the law completely in his choice, but he should be able to choose which project is starting when he has a number of phased projects to carry out.

The amendment covers the point that a developer should not necessarily be tied to a mandatory provision such as this, because one must take the time of the grant of that permission as the time at which the project is begun. It may be that the Minister will be able to explain that my fears are unfounded, but I think it would be a much easier paragraph to administer both from the Revenue's point of view and that of the developer if the words were omitted.

The Minister of State, Treasury [Mr. Denzil Davies]: The right hon. Member for Crosby (Mr. Page), in moving the amendment, set out correctly the purpose of the sub-paragraph and of the amendment. The words of the amendment relate to a situation in which there is a grant of planning permission for a limited period, say, for five years. Then, at some point between year one and year five, renewal of that planning permission for possibly a longer limited period, or even a permanent period, is granted by the local authority.

The Bill provides that, at the time of the granting of renewal of planning permission, be that in year two or year four, the developer is treated as having started another project of development so that a development land tax charge falls at that point in relation to the next limited period. We believe that that is the right way of looking at the issue. I assure the right hon. Gentleman that there is no intention of going back, as it were, to the beginning of the original planning permission. There will not be a charge going right back to the beginning of year one. The charge will arise in relation to the point where the local authority gives the developer a further period of planning permission, be it in year one, year two, year three or year four, during the period of the first planning permission.

The intention of the legislation is that at that point, when planning permission for renewal is given, we consider that the developer is acquiring another asset, perhaps not an asset in the technical sense, but he has acquired a valuable right. The legislation is based on the assumption that the developer is getting something valuable, and at that point a charge against it arises, and the spreading provisions apply as they Col. 198 do generally.

I take some of the right hon. Gentleman's points. If the amendment were accepted, I understand that the charge would not arise until the termination of the first limited period. In other words, if the permission was for five years and there was a renewal of permission in year four, under the Bill the charge would arise in relation to year four. If the amendment were accepted, the charge would arise at the end of year five, at the termination of the first period.

This is not a matter where we want to stick rigidly to the legislation, and I am quite happy to recommend to the Committee that we should accept at least the principle of the amendment; that the charge is not triggered off until the termination of the limited period. At present it would be triggered off by the actual granting of planning permission by the local authority. No revenue loss is involved here. The right hon. Gentleman's suggestions may be thought fairer. There are arguments on both sides, but I am happy to accept the principle of the amendment. If it is necessary to make some amendments to his draft amendment we will do so, but I am happy to accept the situation where the charge will not arise until the end of the limited period.
