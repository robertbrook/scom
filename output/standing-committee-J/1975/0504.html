Clause 13
REDUCED RATE DURING INTERIM FINANCIAL YEARS

Amendment proposed— (29th April)—No. 167, in page 19, line 4, to leave out 'the year 1979' and insert 'the year 1984'—[ Mr. Budgen].

10.30 a.m.

Question again proposed.

The Minister of State, Treasury (Mr. Denzil Davies)
: Perhaps I should not reply at too great a length to Amendment No. 167. However, at our previous sitting there was not time to reply to this amendment, so perhaps I should do so now.

The amendment seeks to substitute the date 1984 in place of 1979 in relation to the transitional provision, whereby for three years development land tax shall be 66⅔ per cent. of the first £150,000 of development value, after the £10,000 slice

I appreciate that hon. Members opposite think that the rate of 80 per cent. is too high. I would not want to wade through the arguments. But perhaps I may mention again the reason the lower rate was chosen. It was not chosen necessarily because we felt that the 80 per cent. rate was too high, although I can appreciate arguments in that regard.

Representations were made to us that there might be some difficulty in land coming on to the market during the next two or three years because of what has happened in the past. Because of the general economic situation there might be problems in relation to land supply, and if the rate were reduced over a period of three years on a tranche of £150,000 smaller parcels of land might be brought on to the market. In response to those representations, we decided to establish this lower rate for three years.

If we were to substitute 1984 that would defeat the purpose of the lower tranche 840 of £150,000. I accept that hon. Members might say that they want a lower rate. But if one accepts that one of the purposes of having a lower tranche over a three-year period was to bring land on to the market during the next three years by putting in 1984, we would be defeating the object of that exercise. We would be creating a reduction in tax but we would be defeating the object of the exercise, which is to give an additional incentive, if that is required, to bring land, especially the smaller plots of land that the smaller builders will want, on to the market during the period of three years.

Indeed, it seems that builders are to some extent responding to this concession and incentive. I have here an advertisement. I will not give the name of the builder concerned but perhaps I could say to right hon. and hon. Gentlemen that there have been advertisements appearing in several newspapers in the South East of England by a large firm of housebuilders to the effect that they are

"urgently seeking potential house building land".

 They go on to point out

"the concessionary tax rate on the first £150,000 (which is only for a limited period)"

 will enable one to benefit by selling one's land to them.

Therefore, this is a case of a large firm of housebuilders in the South East of England which is advertising at the moment on the basis of this concession. It is saying "We want this land to come on to the market. This is a three-year concession. Please sell your land to us because you will pay less tax. We need this land to build homes for people." I think that indicates to some extent that the objective of the concession is, perhaps, fulfilling the good reasons we had for introducing it.

If we were to put in "1984" that purpose would be defeated, and that kind of advertisement could not appear in any of the local newspapers.

I do not think that I need to labour the arguments. There is a difference between us on the rate of the tax. But we think that this is fair and that it would bring housing land on to the market. We believe that both sides of the Committee would agree that this would be all to the good. Therefore, I ask the Committee not to accept the amendment which seeks to insert the date 1984 instead of 1979.
