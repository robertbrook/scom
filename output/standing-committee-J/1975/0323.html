Mr. Page
: Amendment No. 2 follows the theme of Amendment No. 1.

This is perhaps an appetiser rather than anything else, on the problem of when the tax is to be paid. The Opposition would not have produced a Bill of this kind, this size and this complexity, nor a tax which is payable so early in the proceedings of development. But taking the Bill as it stands, and the kind of wording which the Government have decided to use for the imposition of this tax, we are faced with the kind of amendments which indicate the policy which we would adopt towards a tax of this kind.

When we come across the very first subsection of Clause 1, and find that the tax is to be charged in respect of the realisation of the development value of land, it seems to imply that the person who will eventually pay the tax is to be charged immediately upon realisation of the development. That 6 might seem reasonable if one read it logically, but we only have to look ahead in the Bill, even to the next clause, to see what realisation of the development value means.

Of course, it does not mean realisation at all when one has a deemed disposal; it is merely deemed realisation. So we have been looking at the point as to when the payment shall occur, and also when the tax shall be assessed, and if it were possible that the tax could be assessed at the very early stage of contemplation of development, but not be payable until the development value was realised—as everyone would understand the word "realised"—it would not be such an unsatisfactory tax. By Amendment No. 1, we are asking the right hon. Gentleman whether he will agree to the tax being assessed at the outset of development. The person concerned in the development should know his liability at an early stage, but should not have to meet it before he has received any proceeds of the realisation of the development.

10.45 a.m.

The tax is a capital levy to be paid before any capital has been received. It is a levy on an anticipated profit when there is a deemed disposal. I have no objection to the tax being paid when money is passed on the sale, but when there is a deemed disposal and no money has passed, it is grossly unfair on the developer that he should pay the tax out of his own pocket or out of borrowings. We have also to consider how much a developer would pay in interest on any borrowings to pay the tax or how much he is losing by keeping his own money idle by way of a payment to the Exchequer, for the tax.

Amendment No. 2 provides that payment should be made when a developer actually receives the proceeds from a realisation. It may be said that this would not cover the Harry Hyams position, when a project of material development is completed and, in order to obtain something out of inflation if it continues in respect of buildings as it has in the past, the owner retains the development. Perhaps we should not have an amendment like No. 2 which provides for the tax to be paid out of proceeds. Perhaps the tax should be paid on the completion of the project of material development so that one avoids a Harry Hyams situation.

The point of Amendment No. 2 is that a developer should be allowed to carry out his development without interruption to his JPEG 7 cash flow by having to pay out this tax. I appreciate that later in the Bill developers are given a year of grace, and that the tax can then be paid in instalments, but it often takes much longer than one year to carry out a development and, during that time, there will be a continual drain on the builder's cash resources.

A developer may also have to give bonds for road developments and the provision of water and other services to an estate. He has to meet heavy expenses before seeing the proceeds of a development, and if the tax is added to the normal outlay, it will deter many developers. In many cases it will tip consideration over the margin when developers are working out whether a development is worthwhile. If that happens, the so-called development land tax will not result in the development of land. I am sure we wish to make progress on this matter. This is a reasonable tax on windfall profits, but it should not delay development.

Buildings of a particular class can become more valuable because there is increasing demand for them, but they increase in value whether they are let, owner-occupied or empty. Usually, the increase in value is greater if the building is occupied because there is an income and the building is properly maintained.

It is significant that there has not been a great rush by the London Borough of Camden to exercise its powers of com- 8 pulsory purchase on Centre Point. The Government seem to have fallen between two stools with this tax. In practice, it is a levy on the granting of planning permission for a change of use or for initial development. In those circumstances, one could logically ask for the levy to be paid on one of two occasions—either when the permission was granted or when an income flow results from the development.

I do not know if the Treasury should be regarded as having special expertise or knowledge in the property world, but the Government have fallen uneasily into saying the tax will be paid a year after the event which gives rise to the levy. This is a totally inadequate time if one is asking for the levy to be paid when income is resulting from the development. If it is the Government's intention to relate the timing of the payment to benefit from the development, they must reconsider this period of one year.

On the other hand, if the Government think the levy should be paid on the granting of planning permission, why wait a year? We all wish to encourage investment, and investment in land is not just carried out by developers. A great deal is done by owner-occupiers, including the entire distribution system in this country, without which there would be no point in having a manufacturing industry. An inefficient distribution industry imposes a heavy burden on manufacturing. As proposed, the tax has to be paid a year after the development, and this will impose great extra costs on wholesalers and retailers who develop premises for their own use. That may be the intention of the Government. If so, they should come clean and admit it.

If the Government wish to see a more efficient distribution system, they should ensure that the levy is payable only when there is some benefit from the development. Improvements in the physical distribution system are most likely to result from the JPEG 9 work of owner-occupiers. We are unlikely to see the large-scale developments which characterised the 1960s and early 1970s. Interest rates and confidence are among the factors against such developments. However, owner-occupiers will be discouraged by the timing of this tax.

I am a director of a company called Kings Reach Investment. If hon. Members looked out of the window on the other side of this room, they could see the Kings Reach site, where a development which includes 198 flats has just been completed. It has taken about five years and I hope it has benefited the community. I think it is quite good-looking. However, if the owners, when assessing the possibility of carrying out this development, had thought they would have to pay the tax after only a year—four years before any benefits would flow—it is likely that there would have been no development.

I know the development includes offices, shops, and an hotel. We may have differing views about the benefits of this type of development, but if extra unnecessary burdens are imposed at an early stage of development, before there is any income, the result is likely to be no development. Perhaps the Paymaster-General could tell us why he has chosen the period of one year? Why not two years, five years or six months? What is so magical about one year? It is neither the time of the event giving rise to the levy nor the time of benefit arising from it. I hope that the Government will consider this matter in the light of our representations.

The Minister of State, Treasury (Mr. Denzil Davies)
: The right hon. Member for Crosby (Mr. Page) said that the two amendments were fairly general and that they related to the general way in which tax is charged. He said they were not specifically related to the deemed disposal which occurs at the start of material development, although that situation was dealt with in the amendment. The amendments go further than the question which has been raised and whether some tax should not be payable at the time of a deemed disposal time. The amendments provide that tax should not be paid until some benefit has been received.

The hon. Members for Hove (Mr. Sainsbury) and Melton, (Mr. Latham) concentrated on the deemed disposal at the start of material development. The right hon. Member for Crosby, by implication, showed the difficulty that one would be in, and the difficulty for the taxing authority, if the amendments were accepted. He said that the amendments implied that liability should not occur until payment has been received. He mentioned Mr. Harry Hyams but I will not pursue that matter. The right hon. Gentleman said that liability might arise on completion of the development. That is a different situation. One then has to ask when completion will take place, because it may be easy to delay it and there might, therefore, be scope for avoidance and delaying payment. In general, our present system does not provide that tax shall not be payable until taxable money has been received. That would be simple and appears to be a fair doctrine, but it is not the case in our tax system. Tax is often levied before the money is received. For example, a trader's tax bill is not computed on actual cash receipts. There are some people, such as 12 members of the Bar and actors, who are in the favourable position of not having to pay money in tax until they are paid. But that is not the general situation.

It is more general that, when a chargeable event occurs, after time for appeals and assessment, tax is paid. Otherwise, cheques would lie in drawers, money would be withheld, perhaps for years, and tax would not be paid until cheques were received and in one's bank account. We cannot have that situation, and that is why tax, including capital gains tax and this tax, is generally, payable when a chargeable event occurs, having given time for assessment. We are not, therefore, imposing any different rule with this tax. Developers know how to compute their corporation tax, for instance. They do not pay the tax every time they receive some money.
