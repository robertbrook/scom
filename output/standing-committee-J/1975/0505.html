    : I think it would be for the convenience of the Committee if I slightly amplified the remarks I was making at out last sitting, but only briefly, for reasons that I do not need to go into.

    Under the Bill as drafted, one of the geteways for exemption for the building of a single house, is that, at the time of the deemed disposal, the owner, or an adult member of this family, as defined in subsections (3) or (4), intends to occupy it for himself or for themselves.     Amendment No. 135—Amendment No. 191 is consequential on Amendment No. 135—this Amendment is intended to introduce a similar exemption to that in respect of capital gains tax, where the property is owned by trustees in settlement but occupied by persons within the relevant classes which are those in subsection (3).

    The property may be owned by trustees of a settlement and they permit, say, the building of an additional dwelling house on the land. The point I want to stress is that the value attaching to the option will not just be whatever was given for it when it was granted. That would be the case only if the option was to buy the land for its full development value. In that case, the developer will have no liability to pay anyway.

    More usually, and this is the type of case I had in mind, the option will be to buy at a price rather less than the land would be expected to command when the option has been granted. It follows that when permission is granted the option becomes a thing of value, commanding a price on the market corresponding to the difference between the price fixed by the option and the full market value of the land. In other words, the increase in value conferred by the permission is attached to the option. Clause 16 relief worked out for the option as I have described will, in turn, reflect that increase, but it may help if I give a simple example.

    Suppose a builder pays £5,000 in 1972 for an option to buy five acres of building land for £50,000. Planning permission is given by 12th September 1974 and in, say, October 1974 he exercises the option. In 1976, after the development land tax appointed day, he starts a project of development. The market value of the land with the right to carry out the development now becomes £100,000. Ignoring the stock relief provisions, he would be realising £45,000 development value, that is £100,000—the value of the land at the time of commencement of JPEG 929 material development—less his total acquisition cost of £55,000—£5,000 for the option and £50,000 for the land.

    Looking at the option, if it had continued to exist it would have been worth £50,000 in the market, because it gave the right to buy land worth £100,000 for only £50,000. He paid £5,000 for the option, but when he started to develop the land and DLT applied, the land was worth £100,000. That option to buy the land for £50,000 was to buy land which is now worth £100,000. The option is now worth £50,000—possibly a little less—but the option is worth a lot more than what he paid for it because he now has the option on an asset which is worth more than originally. The option cost £5,000, so the development value would be £45,000.

    The measure of the stock relief, therefore, would be the £45,000, and it would extinguish the whole charge on the development. In other words, stock relief attaches to the increased value of the option and is not related to the original value of the option when the value of the option might have been a lot less than it is when the land is being developed, because originally the value of the option related to a lower value of the land but the land, when developed, may be worth a lot more. The option in those circumstances might be worth more, despite the fact that the option is exercised after White Paper day. The stock relief still relates and is given against the later value of the option at the date of exercise, and not the value of the option at the date of grant.

    I am sorry if that is complicated. I tried to read it out in order to give right hon. and hon. Gentlemen and anyone concerned about these matters the chance to study it. We can come back to it if it does not seem clear. We have tried to maintain the stock relief exemption, and maintain it against the greater value of the option when it is exercised, instead of restricting it to the lesser value of the option when the option was acquired. The lesser value will reflect the lesser value of the land, and the greater value will reflect the greater value of the land when development takes place.

    I hope that what I have said is reasonably clear, but I shall understand if hon. Gentlemen say that it is not, and we can come back to it.
