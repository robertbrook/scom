The Minister of State, Treasury (Mr. Denzil Davies)
: Perhaps I can enlighten the hon. Member for Hove (Mr. Sainsbury) in relation to Clause 10 before coming to the amendment, because the provisions of Schedule 2 are related to and influenced by the regime set up in Clause 10 in relation to gifts.

I should make it clear at the outset that Clause 10 does not adversely affect the taxpayer in any way. In fact, Clause 10 provides that for the purposes of development land tax there will be no charge at the point of a gift. Where land is given by a person making a gift of land and that land has development value, for the purpose of development land tax there is no charge at that point and the charge arises only when the donee disposes of the land for a consideration. The doner's cost of acquisition is then taken to be the donor's cost of acquisition, and all the tax falls on the actual disposal for a consideration by the donee.

The whole object of Clause 10 is to avoid a situation where there would be a charge to development land tax on a gift. Most hon. Members will agree that it would be undesirable to impose a charge to development land tax on a gift. The hon. Gentleman is right when he says that capital transfer tax applies in relation to a gift and we deem the disposal to be at market value, but capital transfer tax is a different tax. It is meant to impose liability on gifts when a person transfers JPEG 539 the value of his estate for no consideration. That is why there is a charge for capital transfer tax.

But as regards development land tax, the Bill deliberately and intentionally excludes any charge on a gift, so the charge applies only when there is a disposal, which I think is the logical and right way to deal with the problem.

We come later to Schedule 6. This is a complicated schedule dealing with the interaction of other taxes and there will, no doubt, be questions relating to credit in relation to capital transfer tax.

Clause 10 is designed to help to prevent a situation where there will be a charge on a gift, unlike the present position—I am not making a party political point—under the development gains charge, which imports all the rules of the capital gains tax legislation. One effect of importing all the rules of capital gains tax legislation, under which there is a charge on gifts, was to charge the present development gains charge on gifts of land for no consideration. Therefore, as the law stands at present, when land with development value is given under the development gains charge régime there is a charge, in fact, although there is no disposal.

We have deliberately tried to avoid that situation in relation to development land tax and to provide that there will be no charge when land is transferred for no consideration. We shall see later what happens where there is part consideration, but where land is transferred for no consideration it is intended that there should be no charge.

I turn to paragraph 3 of Schedule 2. This deals with assembled land—that is, parts of land which may be acquired at different times; and, when it is disposed of, it is important that the cost of acquisition in relation to different parts should be amalgamated into the total base value. Again, it is meant to work in the main in favour of the taxpayer, so that he does not lose the base values on any particular cost of acquisition. The whole cost of acquisition is added up in relation to different parts of the land into one assembled part.

It is necessary to refer to Clause 10 to ensure that, w hen the person who disposes of the assembled part is disposing of some land which was given to him for no consideration, in arriving at his tax 540 on the disposal of the assembled part he gets the original cost of acquisition to the donor as a cost of acquisition for the purpose of disposing of the land. In other words, without paragraph 3 he would lose the cost of acquisition to the donor in respect of any part of the assembled land which had been given to him for no consideration at all. The object is to include the cost of acquisition.

This provision is complicated, but I hope I have made it clear. The object is to enable the person disposing of the land, who had acquired it for no consideration, to get the benefit of the donor's cost of acquisition.

I accept the reason for tabling these amendments and make no criticism, but, if we were to accept them, that cost of acquisition would not be the donor's cost of acquisition. It would be the market value, as I understand it, of the land at the time of the acquisition. In other words, the amendments would import the notion of a disposal of market value in relation to a gift, whereas, under the legislation, as I have tried to explain, we prefer to do it on the basis of no disposal on a gift and to take the original cost of acquisition as the cost of acquisition.

We think that is better. As the amendment stands, of course, there would probably be no charge on the gift, despite the fact that the gift was deemed to be made at market value. I make no comment on that, because I do not think that it is important. We are concerned with ensuring through Clause 10 that there will not be a charge on a gift. The charge arises on disposal. The cost of acquisition to the donor is taken into account in assessing the final disposal both in respect of an ordinary disposal and in the case of a disposal of assembled land. Paragraph 3 ensures that the taxpayer gets the benefit of that cost of acquisition.

I hope that makes it reasonably clear to the Committee. If there are any other points, I will certainly deal with them.

Gentleman was making—and using the relevant base of the assembly—that is, base A, base B or base C, as the case may be—as the relevant base of that interest.

It may be suggested—and perhaps this was the right hon. Gentleman's suggestion—that a different base should be allowed if it produces a higher base value. I am told that this is mistaken because the higher the base value the smaller the amount of the exemption. I shall amplify that point in a moment. When the disposal of the assembly is a deemed disposal it could happen that the amount of the exemption exceeds the actual realised development value. This could happen, for example, where the development does not extend to the whole of the assembled land, so the amount of the exemption could exceed the actual development value if the development was to only part of the land.

Paragraph 7 preserves the balance of the exemption for use on a subsequent disposal. If it did not do so, this balance would be lost because the separate parts of an assembly completely disappear following a deemed disposal and re-acquisition. So in effect the stock-in-trade exemption is preserved if all of it is not used up on a part disposal. It is like carrying forward a tax credit.

Once we get to the deemed disposal situation, the assembly comes to an end because all the bits of the assembly are deemed to have been sold together, and there is then one re-acquisition of the whole area. To preserve the stock-in-trade exemption which attached to the one piece of land—the pre-12th September 1974 land—paragraph 7 is necessary, otherwise there would be danger that the developer would lose part of the benefit of this exemption since he was unable to use it all at the point of the actual deemed disposal.
