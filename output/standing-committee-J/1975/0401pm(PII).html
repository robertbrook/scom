There is the basic exemption of £10,000, and if a person was within that exemption, as he would be in a small case, there is no duty upon him to inform the Revenue. Compensation would be paid by the local authority to the individual, but the individual would pay the tax direct to the Revenue.

If there is no development value, there will not be any difference between current use value and market value, and there will therefore be no tax to pay. It is only when market value is higher than current use value that we have a situation of betterment tax or development land tax, as we call it in this legislation. If there is no betterment value, there is no tax.

We are concerned with possibly a large number of situations involving compensation where large sums might be paid by way of compensation for withdrawing planning permission. They are two identical situations. At the end of the day the owner of the land has money in his pocket which reflects development value. We tax only that money which reflects development value. We do not tax anything which does not reflect development value.
