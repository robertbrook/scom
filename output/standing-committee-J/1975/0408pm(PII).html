Mr. Tim Sainsbury: I beg to move Amendment No. 116, in page 98, line 28, leave out 'ten' and insert 'forty'.

The Chairman: With this we may take Amendment No. 117: page 98, line 39, leave out 'three' and insert 'ten'.

Mr. Sainsbury: The first amendment seeks to allow a far longer period when the building which was on a site which has become a cleared site shall be taken into account. The Bill, as drafted, refers to

"the carrying out of works for the rebuilding … of any building which was in existence at the relevant time,"

which is not very long ago—

"or of any building which was in existence in the period of ten years immediately preceding the day on which that time falls but was destroyed or demolished before the relevant time".

I note particularly the use of the word "destroyed", because that brings to mind enemy action. Indeed, it will be within the knowledge of members of the Committee that one need not go very far from this place to come across sites which are still cleared sites—bomb sites, as they are called—which were destroyed during the Second World War.

If we are left with only 10 years, the building which was on the site prior to the wartime demolition cannot be taken into account. That seems unreasonable, particularly if we have cases where the rebuilding of the war-damaged or war-destroyed building has been held up, as it has been on some occasions, by long outstanding discussions with the local authority or planning authority as to what the use should be, or the desire on some occasions of the authority that the particular site—a small site—should be Col. 488 included within a larger and more comprehensive development. To my certain knowledge on the South Bank there are such sites, I think in Comprehensive Development Area No. 1, which is noted for the relative absence of comprehensive development within the area. It is also noted for the fact that there exist bomb sites—I imagine most of them date from 1940, but there are some from subsequent years. It seems reasonable to me that the building previously on the site should be taken into account in those cases.

Amendment No. 117 relates to a different circumstance—whether the temporary use of land for car parking shall constitute material development. It is suggested in subsection (e) that the temporary use of the land for car parking for a period not exceeding three years should not constitute material development. This seems to be a rather arbitrary restriction. The complexities of property development are such—and I do not think they are made any less complex by the Bill—that it is frequently the situation that a site which is to be part of a larger development may go out of use as a building and become available for car parking.

On some occasions—I have in mind particularly occasions when that site forms part of or adjoins a shopping centre—there will be pressure to use the cleared site for car parking purposes. Inevitably, because of the time these things take—and it is not only the proceedings of the Committee which sometimes seem to be interminable, it is also the deliberations of planning committees about large-scale planning schemes—the temporary use will continue for a number of years. It therefore seems entirely reasonable that a much longer period than three years should be allowed for the temporary use of the site for car parking without that use constituting material development and triggering all the rigmarole that necessarily follows according to the provisons of the Bill.

I am sure that if the Government wish to come back and say that they do not like 10 years but they like seven years, we could reach a reasonable compromise on it. But I hope that they will accept that three years seems an unreasonably short time bearing in mind the period that often elapse before a whole site is ready for development and the development can proceed.

Nevertheless, this is a judgment that we have to take into account.

The rules in Part III of the Schedule, concerning leases and reversions on leases, would not work satisfactorily where a lease is granted out of land in which the grantor has different interests.

Paragraph 2 provides for the grant to be treated as separate part disposals of the separate interests instead of a single part disposal out of an assembly. It also provides for consideration for the grant of the lease to be apportioned on a just and reasonable basis between the separate part disposals and for any other necessary apportionments—for example, the incidental costs of disposals.

The paragraph arises out of a decision taken recently by the courts. Whether it was a correct decision is not for us to say, but the draftsman has to take it into Col. 536 account and has had to draft this paragraph as a result. If it had not been taken, it might not have been necessary to include the paragraph. I hope that that explanation is sufficient.
