Mr. John Butterfill
(Bournemouth, West)

Mr. Alan Howarth
(Stratford-on-Avon)

The suggestion is that agents acting on behalf of firms and organisations who are signatories of the ROLAC agreement should only be obliged to state that they are in receipt of commission on a fixed scale. They need only say that it is possible for an investor to write off to an office somewhere to get a note of the scale of commissions.

That is not good enough. The investor should have available to him, immediately, as of right, a note of what the commissions are. That would be fair to the investor and to agents acting on behalf of themselves or of firms who are not signatories of ROLAC. as they are otherwise liable to be squeezed on to the margins of this business and possibly into extinction. That is a possible injustice and an unsatisfactory aspect of the proposed arrangements, which I hope that the Minister will be prepared to reconsider and to ask MIBOC to reconsider.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Trade and Industry (Mr. Michael Howard)
: In answer to the point made by the hon. Member for Workington (Mr. Campbell-Savours), the designated agency's rules must continue to comply with the principles in schedule 6. The Secretary of State will have the right to be informed of any change in rules by the designated agency. He may also revoke any powers that he may have previously transferred to the designated agency if he considers that it no longer complies with the relevant 464 criteria in the Bill. His absolute safeguard is that if he does not like the way in which the agency exercises the powers he has transferred to it, he can revoke them. In company with the governor of the Bank of England he has the power to appoint and dismiss the members of the designated agency.

With that combination of powers, the Secretary of State is in a more than sufficiently strong position to ensure that the designated agency continues to comply with the criteria with which it has to comply in order to have powers transferred to it.

Mr. Campbell-Savours
: I wish to clarify exactly what that means. Let us say that MIBOC or SIB produces a rule that covers a wide range of matters and subsequently decides to remove that rule because of pressure. Is the Minister saying that the only way that he can deal with that problem is by using the nuclear deterrent of wiping it out and starting again? Has he no power to intervene under the rules made at the time of designation?

If no power is being retained surely that is an irresponsible way to proceed. It means that the fine tuning necessary to secure the right regulatory arrangements will not fall within the ambit of the Minister's responsibility.

Mr. Howard
: The hon. Gentleman is right. The Secretary of State will not be able to fine-tune the mechanism, nor is it our intention that he should do so. That is not in the framework provided by the Bill. The Secretary of State is not intended to fine-tune the conduct of business rules. He transfers his powers to a designated agency if he is satisfied that the designated agency can do its job properly and that its rules comply with the principles set out in schedule 6. If the designated agency ceases to comply with those principles, the Secretary of State may revoke the transfer of powers. It is important to note that he may revoke the transfer in part: he is not obliged to revoke it altogether. He can specify areas in relation to which the transfer of powers would not extend and he has the power, together with the Governor of the Bank of England, to dismiss the members of the designated agency.

Clause 49
INDEMNITY RULES

Mr. Gould
: I beg to move amendment No. 40. in page 32, line 32, leave out subsection (2) and insert—

"(2) Rules under this section may require recognised self-regulatory organisations and recognised professional bodies and their members who are authorised persons for the purposes of investment business to participate in any scheme for indemnity established under those rules, irrespective of any additional indemnity scheme which the organisations and bodies may themselves operate."'.

This amendment takes us to one of the most important aspects of any regulatory system—the provision of JPEG 467 proper arrangements for compensation for those who may lose if matters go wrong. One of the weaknesses of Lloyd's, for example, has been the absence of proper compensatory arrangements. We have seen that clearly demonstrated in the notorious case of the PCW syndicate, where Lloyd's has striven hard to avoid what, in any ordinary lest, would be an obvious obligation. I suspect that that arises directly from the fact that Lloyd's has never seen fit to introduce adequate compensatory arrangements.

Clause 49 provides for such arrangements, but my amendment would go further than that. I would allow the Secretary of State, or the designated agency, to set up a compensation scheme, and then require, in appropriate cases. SROs and other bodies to belong to the scheme, irrespective of any additional arrangements that they wish to make.

That is important, because there will be grave doubts whether many of the bodies operating under the Bill, when it is enacted, will have the resources of ability to run an adequate compensation scheme. Recognised professional bodies may be all right, presumably because the scale of their business will be relatively small, and they are usually well-established bodies with compensation arrangements, which may or may not be adequate for their general purposes.

It may be worth reporting to the Committee my conversation with one of the professional bodies, which one would expect to seek recognition under the Bill. However, I was firmly assured that it would not be seeking recognition, and that few professional bodies may do so. In that case, the Securities and Investments Board may have to handle many applications for direct authorisation. The Minister may yet regret his rejection of our amendment, which would have empowered SIB to direct people to join the scheme.

There must also be grave doubts about self-regulating organisations. Admittedly, the stock exchange is in a special position, as on many other issues. It has the experience and resources—at least until this point—to run a reasonably effective compensation scheme. But it is difficult to see how those self-regulating organisations that have yet to be brought into existence, or have only a recent history, will raise the money to provide an adequate fund for such a scheme, and how they will obtain adequate insurance cover, if that is the route that they choose to take.

Mr. McCrindle

SROs. would no longer be necessary? In other words, is he suggesting that we should have a single or double banking of indemnity? In view of the limited capacity that, as he may be aware, exists in that field, he should be careful that we do not introduce something that cannot be achieved.

Mr. Gould
: I believe that the hon. Gentleman's intervention supports the point that I am trying to make. I fear that many individual SROs—particularly those that have yet to become fully operational—are simply not in a position suddenly to emerge overnight with a 468 fully-fledged compensation scheme. They will not have the resources and they will not be able to get cover on acceptable grounds.

If there are SROs that have difficulty in establishing an adequate indemnity arrangement for their own members, the amendment would give the SIB the power to require them to join the scheme that it intends to set up.

Mr. McCrindle
: Or carry their own?

Mr. Gould
: That would be an option for them. There is nothing mandatory here; all that is being suggested is that the SIB should have the chance to take that power if it decided that it was appropriate—no doubt after consultation with the SRO—that the cover could be provided by such means. If there were an existing scheme that went beyond the cover provided by this scheme, or if SROs subsequently decided that they wanted to top it up in some way, there would be no objection to that.

It seems to me—and to SIB itself—that this is the only way we can be confident that from the moment when the SROs take up their responsibilities, there will be in each case a fully operational and effective indemnity arrangement.

Mr. McCrindle
: I apologise for again interrupting the hon. Gentleman's speech, but there are some details that are inescapable when arguing an amendment such as this one.

The hon. Member for Dagenham (Mr. Gould) will be aware that the indemnity required of. for example, a large firm of pensions consultants may run to many millions of pounds, whereas the indemnity required of a small firm of life assurance brokers in. for example. Dagenham would be comparatively small. Is the hon. Gentleman suggesting that in addition to all the SIB's other responsibilities, it should be required to set the level of indemnity for each individual member who has registered with the SIB or with an SRO?

Mr. Gould
: The SIB proposes to put in place a compensation scheme which, as I recall, provides a maximum of £30.000 on each claim and there will be many instances where the indemnity will have to cover a much greater potential loss than that. That would be unaffected. But equally, there will be cases where an SRO that has only just got off the ground may find it difficult to put in place a scheme that provides even that minimal cover. The SIB would welcome an amendment to provide an administratively simple way of guaranteeing that basic cover.

It is worth pointing out that although the SIB recognises the special case of the stock exchange, it is inclined to say that in rapidly changing market conditions, with the breakdown between broking and jobbing and the greater difficulty of securing insurance cover in many cases, even the stock exchange may not find it as easy as it has been to maintain the compensation arrangements that it has operated successfully up to now.

The hon. Member for Brentwood and Ongar (Mr. McCrindle) is right to draw attention to the points that he has raised, because of the consequences that may flow from the establishing of a central fund. SROs may wish, or may be required, to come into such a central fund initially, and as they become stronger and more JPEG 469 established they may find it easier to set up indemnity arrangements on their own initiative and under their own control. One would have to take care that the withdrawal of key SROs did not leave behind it a central fund that was gravely weakened.

We should not be too deterred by that prospect, or by the need to face that problem, since there are many other spheres—banking, for example—where there is a straightforward requirement to join a central fund as a condition of being able to operate at all. I do not believe that we should cavil or demur too strongly if it were suggested that that should apply to this sphere, too.

Mr. McCrindle
: Equally, the hon. Gentleman will remember that insurance brokers are under one of the few SROs at present in existence—the Insurance Brokers Registration Council. The 1975 Act requires a person to have professional indemnity before he can be registered as a broker, but it is left to the individual applicant to arrange it. Although there may be attractions in the centralist argument that the hon. Gentleman deploys, there may equally be arguments in favour of leaving it to the individual participant.

Mr. Gould
: I am not sure that the Insurance Brokers Registration Council is to become an SRO. That is one of the untidy elements in the structure that yet have to be resolved. Nevertheless, the hon. Gentleman could equally accept that ISRO. or perhaps even NASDIM. which has been in existence for some time now and which is perhaps further developed than most, may recognise the merits of accepting the central role that SIB could play. Perhaps the hon. Member for Beacons-field (Mr. Smith) could enlighten us on that point by intervening later in the debate.

This is yet another issue in which we must examine how seriously the Government are approaching the problem of regulation. In banking there is no problem. There is simply a requirement that banks and licensed deposit takers, and so on. should be part of the deposit protection fund. There is no question of their being able to opt in or to opt out: that is the condition on which they operate.

If the Government are saying that they have fundamental and deeply rooted objections to a much less mandatory system, or even the possibility of a system being introduced, we are entitled to ask whether that calls into question the seriousness of the Government's purpose in approaching the regulation of the City. Will the new regime be so much lighter and less effective than that required for banks?

We shall listen carefully to the Minister's reply as we regard this as another touchstone showing the Government's seriousness of purpose.

Finally, in case I have not already made it clear, although I tabled the amendment without the benefit of SIB's drafting advice. I understand that SIB welcomes and supports the amendment.

Mr. Howard
: The hon. Member for Dagenham (Mr. Gould) was a trifle disingenuous in the early part of his speech when, in reply to an intervention, he said that there was nothing mandatory about the amendment. I would not normally level such a charge against him. but it is justified on this occasion.

470 The amendment would give SIB the power to make it mandatory for SROs to join the SIB compensation scheme. The Bill requires every SRO to make protection arrangements equivalent to those provided by SIB itself, but SROs would not be obliged to join the SIB scheme. They could make their own arrangements for protection as long as they complied with the equivalence requirement. So the stock exchange, for example, could confidently be expected to rely on its considerable record of providing adequate protection and continue to do that in its own way and according to its own rules.

If some of the other SROs encounter problems to which the hon. Gentleman referred, there would be nothing to stop them joining the SIB scheme. If they find that that is the best way of providing the equivalence that the Bill requires, that course will be open to them, but it would be unnecessary to give SIB a power to make it mandatory for all SROs. including the stock exchange, to provide protection for investors by joining a compensation scheme set up by SIB. I cannot begin to see how that can be regarded as a touchstone of the seriousness with which the new regime is to provide an effective, regulatory framework and for that reason I invite the Committee to reject the amendment.

Mr. Tim Smith
: I beg to move amendment No. 170, in page 34, line 17, after "money", insert "and interest thereon"

The clause deals with clients' money and subsection (2) gives details of the regulations covering such money. Subsection (2)(d) states that regulations may

"require the keeping of accounts and records in respect of clients' money".

That is fundamental. However, I am advised that interest on clients' money is not clients' money and an accountant should report on compliance with regulations on interest. Therefore, the amendment is designed to make it clear that records should be kept of clients' money plus any interest accrued on that money.

Mr. Gould
: I beg to move amendment No. 90, in page 44, line 26, leave out sub-section (4).

The amendment is designed to enable the intervention powers defined in chapter VI to be exercised by the Secretary of State or the designated agency in respect of members of self-regulating organisations and recognised professional bodies as well as in respect of those who are directly authorised.

It is a familiar point; it concerns the relationship between the SIB and the SROs. I am encouraged to think that it would be sensible to accept the amendment when I look back to clause 54 which firmly prohibits disqualified individuals from carrying out investment business. However, in that clause the indviduals are described as employees or exempted representatives. There is no question of such people sheltering behind their employers or their firms. The Secretary of State or the designated agency says, quite rightly, "We must be able to get at the person who has carried out this deal or has given this investment advice."

For the Secretary of State or the designated agency to be precluded by clause 59(4) from exercising that power in respect of individual members of self-regulating organisations is to get the balance between the SIB and the SROs just a little wrong. Taking the analogy of clause 54, the Secretary of State or the designated agency should be able to intervene in respect of a given person for the purposes set out in chapter VI.
