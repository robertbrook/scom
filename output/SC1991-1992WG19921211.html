6 I am very pleased that we have this opportunity to debate the vitally important subject of the urban and rural regeneration of Wales. That subject is very close to my heart, and one to which the Government is prepared to devote a high level of resources.

On Monday, I published the results of the rural initiative consultations that I launched in this Committee last February. As well as setting out the way forward to press ahead vigorously with the development of the economic, social and environmental fabric of rural Wales, I announced an additional £29 million of spending in the rural areas.

Unlike last February, today we are discussing urban as well as rural Wales. And today I am announcing my decisions on the urban programme, the projects that I am supporting as projects of special or regional importance, and local authorities' capital allocations for next year. The urban programme includes more than 200 projects; there are 10 new special and regional projects; and total local authority capital funding has increased by 24 per cent to £536 million which I expect to lead to total spending of £631 million. The important issue is how best to target resources and to use them to maximum effect. For that reason, I have given a consistent emphasis to four objectives, right across the range of urban and rural regeneration policies.

First, we must create a sound economic base. Without that, our policies could not be sustained and would not carry conviction. Our success so far gives us confidence that we are on the right track. Secondly, we must involve the community. Regeneration will not succeed unless it responds to what local people want and need. The most effective way to achieve that is for communities to have the opportunity to be fully involved in the action.

Thirdly, we must work together. The lesson of successful urban regeneration from around the world is that all the agencies must act in concert. Fourthly, we must concentrate our effort where it is most needed—and back it with resources and Government action.

We have for a number of years given strong emphasis in the urban programme to economic projects. We set a target of putting a minimum of half the resources into projects with a direct economic benefit. In the coming year's programme, the proportion is almost 60 per cent. But we must involve the community which is why I launched the community revival strategy. Another £1 million of urban programme funding will go to those projects in the coming year, bringing the total to £2.5 million. We are empowering the people to realise their own projects for the benefit of their communities.
