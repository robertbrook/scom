Draft Education Reform Act 1988 (Application of Section 122 to Institutions in Wales) Order 1991

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Welsh Office (Sir Wyn Roberts)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Education Reform Act 1988 (Application of Section 122 to Institutions in Wales) Order 1991.

Public sector higher education in Wales was last debated three years ago, in the debates on the Education Reform Bill. At that time, it fell to me to explain to hon. Members why it was decided to follow a different path for the Welsh local authority colleges from that taken in England.

The Welsh public sector of higher education—or the non-university higher education sector as we are increasingly corning to call it—is small. Three years ago there were 14,000 students in that sector in Wales, although that number has now risen to 16,000. That has shown that our higher education policies are working. Only six institutions, including our one voluntary college—Trinity college, Carmarthen—were large enough to meet the criteria then established for incorporation and funding by the Polytechnics and Colleges Funding Council, the PCFC. The small size of our local athority sector and the good relations that existed then—and still, I hope, exist today—between the Welsh Office and the local education authorities, made it considerably easier to plan than the much larger English sector.

I wish to emphasise that the good relations between the Department and the LEAs, and the smooth working of the Wales advisory body which resulted from them, were a major factor leading to the decision to leave our higher education institutions in the local authority sector when the English polytechnics and colleges were being made independent bodies. At that time, the factors pointing towards independence for the Welsh colleges—and there were some—were outweighed by the success of the Wales advisory body and the good relationships with the local authorities that existed in Wales. Nevertheless, while deciding to leave our higher education institutions in the local government sector, we took care to ensure that the options for the future were left open.

All that was three years ago, and during those years relationships between some of the colleges and their maintaining authorities have been uneasy. Colleges have resented their lack of independence, compared with their English and Scottish counterparts.

Hon Members will know that reconsideration of the position of the non-university colleges as a whole was prompted by the report of Price Waterhouse and Co. on the polytechnic of Wales, which is our largest non-university institution of higher education. With almost 5,300 full-time equivalent students, made up of 5,000 full-time and 1,500 4 part-time students enrolled, the polytechnic is larger than all the colleges of the university of Wales, except the University college of Cardiff, The Price Waterhouse report recommended that the best future for our polytechnic lay in granting it independent status and direct Welsh Office funding, It also recognised that, because the polytechnic forms such a large part of our non-university sector, that recommendation carried implications for the other higher education institutions, although the consultants' terms of reference precluded them from making recommendations about the future of those other institutions.

The consultants' report was received in May last year, when my right hon Friend told the House that there would be wide consultations on the recommendations. In those consultations I met the parties most affected face to face. It transpired that among the people most directly affected—those in the LEAs and in the institutions—there was no consensus on the best way forward for non-university higher education in Wales. The LEAs, without exception, wished all the maintained institutions of higher education to remain in the local government sector, despite the fact that, at an earlier stage, Mid Glamorgan county council had asked my right hon Friend to take over responsibility for the polytechnic, On the other hand, majority opinion in the institutions and their governing bodies was clearly in favour of independent status for the institutions, with Welsh Office funding.

There were, however, two points on which opinion was unanimous, No one wanted to create a situation in which the polytechnic had a different status from the other colleges, and no one thought that the Welsh institutions should become the responsibility of the PCFC. Those two points emerged from the consultation. There was a consensus that the character and identity of the Welsh non-university higher education sector should be preserved.

As well as considering the results of that consultation, account has been taken of the experience of the English institutions, which were given their independence in the PCFC in April 1989, and the experience of the Scottish sector. The equivalent institutions in Scotland have had independent status and have been funded by the Scottish Education Department for many years. There is no doubt that English institutions have flourished since 1989. We make no apology for being much influenced by the evidence of the benefits that independence has brought them in the past two years, and the calmer waters in which the central institutions of Scotland sail.

In weighing the evidence, we have had the interests of one group of people in the forefront of our minds—that is, of course, the students. Our objective is to establish a framework that will give the widest possible opportunities and the highest possible quality of higher education to the future generations of students, both those from Wales who wish to study in their home country and those from other parts of the United Kingdom and from overseas who choose to study in the Principality. It is their interests that should carry the day, and not, if they conflict, the interests of institutions or authorities.

In the light of all the evidence, and the best information available on the likely future trends in higher education—such as modular courses, transbinary co-operation, changes in the demands for courses, the need for ever-increasing links with industry—we have concluded that the best interests of the students, and of the institutions that exist to provide their higher education, lie with independent JPEG 5 status for the institutions with funding from the Welsh Office. To assist the Department in its funding responsibility we shall establish a small advisory group, to which we shall look for expert advice. We believe that that is the right way forward, rather than independence with funding by the PCFC, an option that is open to us but which has been rejected.

I pay tribute to the local authorities that, over the years, have brought our higher education institutions into existence and nurtured them to the position they have now reached, where they can stand on their own feet and face the world without the protection afforded by the parent LEA.

Dr. Kim Howells
(Pontypridd)
: On a point of clarification, can the Minister tell us whether all colleges will go the same way as the polytechnic, and will they all go that way at the same time?

Sir Wyn Roberts
: Yes. I thought that I had made it clear that the Price Waterhouse report stated that there were implications for the remainder of the sector. When deciding to fund the polytechnic direct, we also decided that it would be in the best interests of the other institutions to be funded direct.

The colleges are the children of the LEAs and, like all children, they have grown under the benevolent wing of their parents to a size and maturity at which they can lead their own independent lives. It is to the credit of the local authorities that the polytechnic of Wales and the major colleges have reached that position. I hope that, on reflection, the LEAs will accept that the decision to incorporate those institutions and set them on a path of independence is the right decision for the institutions, and especially for the students who will come to them in future years. The colleges will continue to be an important resource in their own localities after independence, and the LEAs will be represented on their governing bodies. I hope very much that colleges and LEAs will continue to work together as equal partners to the benefit of those who live and work in their area.

The draft order under section 227(4) of the Education Reform Act 1988 will not effect any changes to the institutions. Its purpose is to give my right hon. Friend the power to establish higher education corporations to conduct institutions of higher education that meet the criteria for incorporation laid down in the Act. The institutions that fully meet those criteria are the polytechnic of Wales, the Cardiff and West Glamorgan institutes of higher education, the Gwent college of higher education and Bangor normal college. The Welsh college of music and drama in Cardiff does not fully meet the criteria, but may be incorporated with the agreement of the maintaining authority. I am glad to say that South Glamorgan county council has given its agreement and that the college of music and drama is therefore eligible for incorporation with the other colleges. If the draft order is approved by the House and in the other place, the power will be used to make a second order, under section 122(1) of the Education Reform Act 1988, that will establish higher education corporations to conduct those institutions. The corporations would be established in the autumn to prepare to assume responsibility for their institutions on 1 April 1991. My right hon. Friend already has power to fund higher education institutions under section 100 of the Education Act 1944—that is the power used to fund Trinity 6 college, Carmarthen—and would use that power to fund the new corporations from April 1992.

I close with a word of thanks to those wno have served on the Wales advisory body and its standing working group. I have chaired that advisory body for the last six years, and we can be proud of what has been accomplished in that time. I am especially encouraged by the decision of the advisory body at the meeting in March that it should remain in being for one final year to advise on the funding allocations for 1992–93. I am sure that that is a clear signal that the local authorities, the colleges and the Welsh Office can continue to work together in the new situation to build on what has already been achieved in non -university higher education in Wales.

Sir Wyn Roberts
: I am grateful to hon. Members who participated in the debate, which has covered more ground than the scope of the order. I do not complain about that.

I appreciate that there are what are known as transbinary problems—those of co-operation between higher education institutions in Wales and the university colleges. They have the common characteristic that they provide degree courses and award degrees. This issue has preoccupied the Wales advisory body for as long as I can remember. I recall that, partly as a result of our preoccupation with those issues, a sub-committee of the Universities Funding Council was established for Wales with cross-membership from the Wales advisory body.

The hon. Member for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy (Dr. Thomas) would have me disclose the contents of the forthcoming White Paper, where these issues will be further discussed, together with possible solutions. I assure him, the hon. Member for Torfaen (Mr. Murphy) and others that we shall have the interests of Wales very much in mind when we formulate the content and composition of the White Paper. I assure the hon. Gentleman that Wales has not been forgotten; it is far from an afterthought. The interests of Wales and Welsh higher education are at the forefront of our minds as we approach the White Paper.

The Committee has focused on the question of resources, and I must make my position clear. I shall continue my argument with the hon. Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney (Mr. Rowlands). It is nonsense to suggest that local authorities, to which I pay tribute for their provision of higher education and their support for educational institutions, should be compensated for what they have done. They were acting under statute and were providing for the interests of their localities.

Proposed changes through incorporation do not involve a change of function for institutions of higher education, which will continue to provide the services that they have provided in the past. The whole thrust of incorporation is to enable them to perform services better than before. I remind Opposition Members of their own document on the future of local government in Wales. At the end of paragraph 21 of the chapter entitled

"The future of local government in Wales"

they recommend that

"regional government should take on the responsibility for public sector higher education."

16 Of course, I accept that we are incorporating and funding these institutions directly from the Welsh Office rather than from the regions, as suggested by Opposition Members, but I see no basic difference of principle.

The hon. Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney also mentioned interest charges. The assets of colleges will be transferred from local authorities to new bodies from 1 April 1992. Meanwhile, they remain in the ownership of local education authorities. The transfer will be dealt with as provided for by the Education Reform Act 1988 through the Education Assets Board. A clear procedure has been laid down for the transfer of assets.

I have already explained that we require no special powers other than those contained within the Education Act 1944 to fund any higher education colleges. We already fund Trinity college, Carmarthen from the Welsh Office. Resources will be transferred from local to central government to form the basis of Welsh Office funding. The Secretary of State will decide, after consultation in the summer with the Welsh consultation committee for local government finance, on the amount to be transferred. A preliminary consultation with local education authorities at official level is being undertaken now to establish the expenditure of LEAs on higher education.

At present, funding is provided through the local government route by the Welsh Office, by means of revenue support grant to higher education colleges. In future, after the negotiations that I have described have taken place, money will be given directly to those colleges rather than through local authorities. On incorporation, the colleges will become legal entities. At present, they have no legal right to hold property and so on other than through their local education authorities. Incorporation will give them a legal status of their own.

Some hon. Members have asked questions about different scales of funding for colleges in England, Scotland and Wales. We are not comparing like with like, as different funding arrangements in the three countries make it difficult to compare true figures. For example, Welsh figures exclude the significant topping-up payments paid by local education authorities, and the PCFC figures exclude some of the cheaper local authority higher education provision in England. After transfer on 1 April 1992, funding of the institutions will be determined by the Secretary of State during the annual public expenditure round, and it will be easier thereafter to compare Welsh arrangements with English and Scottish arrangements. I repeat that they are not directly comparable.

I can tell the hon. Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney that we appreciate the contributions that have been made by local authorities, but we must not forget the contributions of taxpayers generally. The colleges serve local communities, and will do so after incorporation. Their change of status from maintained colleges to independent self-governing institutions, and the change of funding responsibility to the Welsh Office, will not change their ability to contribute to the well-being and advancement of young people.
