DRAFT WELFARE OF LIVESTOCK (INTENSIVE UNITS) REGULATIONS 1978

10.30 a.m.

The Parliamentary Secretary to The Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Gavin Strang)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft Welfare of Livestock (Intensive Units) Regulations 1978.

As the welfare of farm animals is a matter which has always aroused deep concern in Parliament, and rightly so, it is important that I make it clear from the outset that the purpose of the draft regulations, now before this Committee, is essentially technical in nature.

The United Kingdom is already a signatory of the Council of Europe convention on the protection of animals kept for farming purposes, which was opened for signature by member States of the Council in March 1976. The convention, which was drawn up by the Council's committee of experts on the protection of animals, sets out, in general terms, principles for the welfare of animals kept for farming purposes with particular regard to animals in modern intensive stock-farming systems.

The protection of animals kept for other purposes falls outside its scope.

In addition to the United Kingdom, nine other member States of the Council have signed the convention and four have taken the further step of ratification. The convention came into operation on 9th September last, when the pre-condition of ratification by four member States of the Council had been fulfilled. To be able to ratify the convention, member States of the Council have to establish that their national law on farm animal welfare is in conformity with its provisions.

The Committee will, I am sure, be pleased to learn that legislation in Great Britain, as contained in part I of the Agriculture (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1968, conforms with the convention in all but one respect. This exception is that our law does not specifically require, as does article 7 of the convention, the daily 4 inspection of livestock and equipment in intensive units. The regulations now before the Committee will, together with similar regulations to be made under the parallel Northern Ireland legislation, make good this deficiency for the United Kingdom as a whole.

Only those countries which have ratified the convention will be able to become full voting members of a standing committee which, under article 8 of the convention, the Council is required to establish once four members have ratified the convention and brought it into force.

The standing committee will have the important task of producing detailed provisions for the implementation of the principles set out in the convention and will be expected to make recommendations to the contracting parties to the convention. It will also be required to facilitate settlements of dispute between contracting parties over implementation of the convention and to give advice when contracting parties seek it. The standing committee will have no executive power since the contracting parties may choose not to accept its recommendations. Nevertheless, the standing committee's recommendations and advice are bound to carry great weight in a wide international forum.

Mr. Strang

I am sure that the Committee will agree that this country should not be excluded from participating actively in these important developments. Approval of these regulations will clear the way for us to ratify the convention and so enable us to play a full and vigorous part in the work of the standing committee.

JPEG 5 The draft regulations before the Committee rely on powers in part I of the Agriculture (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1968 under which it is an offence to cause or knowingly allow farm livestock on agricultural land to suffer unnecessary pain or unnecessary distress. As hon. Members will know, the Act requires that the interests affected by these proposals are consulted. This has been done and, as a result, some changes have been made to the proposals as originally circulated. Our Farm Animal Welfare Advisory Committee has also been consulted. These consultations have confirmed that daily inspections of livestock and equipment in intensive units is already a normal practice in this country.

I would now like to direct the attention of the Committee to some of the details of the draft regulations. Modern intensive methods of stock-keeping have come to rely more and more on equipment much of which is operated automatically and on which the animals kept in this way may depend for their lives. Therefore the welfare, and indeed the survival, of livestock in these intensive units depends on the effective running of this equipment. It also depends, to a considerable extent, on high standards of stockmanship being observed. The Council of Europe took note of these developments in article 7 of the convention which stipulates that stock and equipment in intensive livestock units should be inspected at least once daily and that any defects in the equipment should be remedied with a minimum of delay. If there is a defect which cannot be put right at once, all measures necessary to safeguard the welfare of the animals are required to be taken immediately. In this context, hon. Members will note that " intensive unit " in the draft regulations is defined in terms of the extent to which the welfare of livestock kept in this way depends on automatic equipment.

Regulation 2 sets out the requirement that stock and equipment in intensive units, as defined, should be inspected at least once daily and that, if the stock show signs of being other than in a state of well-being, or if defects are found in the equipment, all necessary measures to remedy the situation should be immediately taken.

Hon. Members will, I am sure, agree that the British farmer and his stockman, in the great majority of cases, show a real concern for the welfare of their livestock. Our consultations with interests on these 6 regulations, as I have already said, have confirmed that it is standard practice to inspect stock and equipment in intensive units daily—and sometimes more frequently. Such inspections are an integral part of good husbandry practice and the need for them is reinforced by the natural concern of the farmer for the source of his livelihood.

I hope that I have said enough to explain why the Government propose to make regulations which require those keeping livestock in intensive units to act in a way which is already normal practice. Approval of these regulations will help clear the way for us to ratify the convention, to become full and active participants in the standing committee and to play a full and constructive role in making European policy in this important area.

Mr. Nicholas Winterton
: On the Tory side of the Committee we welcome the draft regulations. I also welcome the Minister's remarks in introducing them.

As the Committee is aware, there are already in existence animal welfare codes which provide general guidance on welfare matters affecting intensive livestock production. Since the Minister did not mention these codes, I remind the Committee that they do not have the force of law. They are general requirements regarding equipment and inspection which are fully met by all responsible farmers and those involved with intensive livestock units.

The proposed regulations rightly give effect to requirements that intensive livestock producers must—I emphasise the word "must"—inspect their stock and equipment on a daily basis. I am sure that all responsible producers in the United Kingdom already undertake that duty and have no objection to this responsibility being made mandatory.

The Ministry press notice to which the hon. Member for Hemel Hempstead (Mr. Corbett) referred, and which deals with these regulations, indicates that the United Kingdom Government would like to ratify the Council of Europe convention on the protection of animals kept for farming purposes, which sets out principles for the welfare of farm livestock, including those kept intensively.

Our legislation—as the Minister clearly said—in Part I of the Agriculture (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1968 and the JPEG 7 parallel Northern Ireland legislation meets the criteria of the convention, except that it does not require the daily inspection of livestock and equipment in intensive livestock units. The Minister has clearly taken the decision, which the Opposition fully support, that these general requirements should not be given the force of law by introducing these regulations.

It is important that the animal welfare lobby—whose presence here this morning is obvious and who no doubt will make useful contributions to this short debate—and all those who are concerned with the treatment of farm animals are fully persuaded that farmers and producers are happy and willing to accept any sensible and reasonable regulation or instruction to improve further the welfare of farm animals in this country. I am pleased that we are seeking at an early date to ratify the European convention so that all countries in Europe may work to improve further the treatment and welfare of animals.

The Minister will admit, however, that these regulations are unlikely to have any significant effect. Intensive livestock producers in this country already carry out the type of inspection specified in these draft regulations, and they rightly carry out these inspections on a regular basis.

In general, therefore, the Opposition have no objection to the principle of these regulations. We warmly welcome them. It is surely in the interests of all intensive livestock producers to ensure that their stock and equipment are well looked after and that they are checked at least once a day. Thus, the regulations give effect to the good practice that already exists in this country, both from the welfare and from the commercial standpoints.

I should be grateful if the Minister could give us some clarification on certain technical points. In regulation 1(3) the term "automatic equipment" is used. It is used throughout the regulations. But no definition of what" automatic equipment" includes is provided. It could well be interpreted by the industry as meaning solely the electronic controls which are used to switch on equipment which can otherwise be operated manually—for example, temperature sensors or time switches. It is not clear from the regulations whether that is the intention or whether the term "automatic equipment" covers the whole system. The absence of a definition is particularly confusing because some of the 8 equipment provided with automatic controls can often be used manually by means of an overriding control system. I hope that the Minister will deal with that question.

I have another question which again relates to regulation 1(3) where it says in the definition of "defect" that it includes the

"total or partial failure of that equipment due to a cause other than … the equipment itself".

One of the biggest difficulties facing intensive livestock producers is the fact that they may be given no warning whatsoever of the interruption of services by the electricity, gas or water undertakings. As I am sure the Minister and all members of the Committee are aware, that can cause considerable problems. Clearly, such circumstances may occur at times other than when the daily inspection of equipment and stock is taking place. It is surely, therefore, unncessary to include causes other than the faults in the equipment itself. Regulation 2(4) provides that:

"The automatic equipment.… shall be thoroughly inspected".

If it is accepted, for example, that in a ventilation system controlled by automatic switching the fans are part of the automatic equipment, it may well be impossible for the stockkeeper—in the words of the article—thoroughly to inspect the fans.

The definition of "inspect" as given in the Concise Oxford Dictionary is "to look closely into". Many intensive livestock systems rely on fan units situated in trunking in the roof. While it is perfectly practical to check whether each fan is being operated by the automatic controls in accordance with the required ventilation rate, it would not be possible to thoroughly to inspect each fan without the risk of causing additional distress to the livestock. For example, a stockman would have to bring a ladder into the unit every day and clamber up into the apex of the roof to inspect the fans. Normally such close inspections take place when the building was empty. The term "thoroughly inspected "is therefore not practical, and, clearly, in the instance of fans, it should be sufficient to ensure that they are operating properly.

I realise that these are technical matters, but I seek answers and assurances from the Minister on them because they obviously concern the industry, the National Farmers Union, farmers and those producers involved in intensive farming. I conclude by JPEG 9 warmly welcoming these regulations on behalf of the Opposition.
