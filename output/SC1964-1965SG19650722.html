TOURISM IN SCOTLAND
OFFICIAL REPORT

10.30 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Dr. J. Dickson Mabon)
: I beg to move,

That the Chairman do now report to the House that the Committee have considered the matter of Tourism in Scotland.

Hon. Members will need no reminding of the part which tourism can play in the social and economic life of Scotland. Although the rôle of tourism in the economy is under study by the Scottish Economic Planning Board, it is already clear that the industry offers one of the most hopeful prospects for the development of large areas of Scotland. It is, of course, a notable foreign exchange earner and foreign exchange saver and so ranks high in the export drive. At a time when we are facing a balance-of-trade problem of such severity, this must obviously be a point of great importance.

Tourism is one of the growth industries of the world. We must improve our position in this fiercely competitive international market. We must pay attention to the need for planning and investment, the prerequisites of expansion in modern industry. Hon. Members will remember that when we came to office in October the Government were presented with a number of problems, of which tourism was certainly high on the agenda. It has been a long and searching analysis which we have gone about in trying to secure what was the right thing to do. One of the important things, particularly in present economic difficulties, was to decide where and how much we could spend on particular enterprises. That is why we recognised the need for research

For all its importance, we still do not know enough about the exact ways in which tourism makes its impact upon the economy. We do not really know what our capacity is and how our potential could be most effectively developed. The facts are still guesswork. We are determined to put an end to this hit-or- 6 miss approach. We cannot, for example, make a really informed drive to attract capital investment on the scale which is needed.

One of the main obstacles facing the Scottish Economic Planning Board in assessing the actual and potential rôle of tourism in the economy is the need for reliable data. Recently the Board commissioned an economist from Glasgow University to carry out a preliminary reconnaissance. This has confirmed the urgent need for research. The day of the amateur approach to tourism is past. This is the age of the research team, of management techniques, of data processing and of computers It is an age when every effort is made to take the unnecessary guesswork out of decisions. Our international competitors are very well aware of the importance of know-how in service industries as well as in technology and have applied research with great effect in tourism.

It requires only a cursory examination of the statistics published on tourism in Scotland to show that they cannot be relied upon even to indicate trends. Indeed, they do not square with the Tourist Board's own assessment of the progress of the industry or with a number of independent indications which we have. The Board's Report for 1962, for example, described tourism as Scotland's fastest growing industry, although the figures which it published of numbers of persons accommodated in hotels, boarding houses and apartments for that year showed a substantial fall compared with 1961. In its Report for 1963, the Board again reported a continuation of the expansion of the industry—and again, in 1964, another year of progress. In spite of this, the pessimists, relying on the statistics published by the Board, say that the industry is stagnant or in decline.

The Scottish Economic Planning Board has already given some attention to this problem, and provisional calculations, based on statistics collected from a wide variety of sources by a consultant economist carrying out researches for the Board, suggest that expenditure expanded by 9 per cent. in 1962, by 11 per cent. in 1963, and probably by a considerably greater percentage in 1964. The lessons of all this are that pessimism about the prospects of the industry is not well founded, and that it is of first importance JPEG 7 to get as reliable data as we can about one of our main industries.

It was against this background that Ministers had to take a decision on the Scottish Tourist Board's request for a renewal of the £15,000 grant, which was first given in 1959 for a period of three years, later extended to six years, and was to be spent on the development of tourism in the Highlands. Bearing in mind that in future the Highland Development Board would be able to give special help to the Highlands in various ways, Ministers came to the conclusion that something more positive than a simple extension of the temporary grant was required, something more closely directed to the present needs of Scotland as a whole. The answer clearly lay in money which would be spent in generating more money; in a word, on research.

While Ministers were developing their thoughts on this, the Scottish Tourist Board was itself making a fundamental re-appraisal of its position. It decided that although the original conception of a lay board had served it well, the time had come for a constitution more directed to present-day needs. Accordingly, the Chairman, Lord Rosebery, and the Vice-Chairman, Sir Alexander King, put proposals to the Secretary of State for a fundamental re-organisation of the Board, designed, by providing for direct representation of the main sections of the tourist industry, to make it more professional and generally better equipped. The Board is confident that this reorganisation will result in a much larger measure of financial support from all sections of the industry.

Lord Rosebery has now advised the Secretary of State that reorganisation on the lines which they discussed has been approved in principle by the Board and is going ahead. The Secretary of State has told the Chairman that he welcomes this forward-looking decision and that he will be happy to offer the new Board a grant-in-aid of up to £75,000 for an agreed programme of research to be spread over three years.

I want to express the Secretary of State's warm appreciation of the contribution which the Board has made over the years to the cause of tourism in Scotland and his gratitude to individual members 8 for their several contributions; not least for their acceptance of the need for change.

I take the opportunity of apologising to the Committee for the fact that hon. Members have read some of this information in one of the newspapers. I assure the Committee, on behalf of my right hon. Friend, that neither the Government nor any of the officials who serve the Government are in any way responsible for this. It will be for the Chairman of the Board to announce the details of the reorganisation.

No doubt the Committee will meantime be interested in the items which the Secretary of State and the Board think might be included in the new research programme, which it is hoped will provide a basis for assessing the extent to which the industry in Scotland needs to be developed if it is to play its full part in the social and economic life of the country. It will not all be backroom study. The Board will be able to try out some of its ideas in pilot schemes and to sponsor market research.

Better statistics on volume and value are required, on occupancy rates, on investment return. Where practicable these should be collected on a basis which will make for ready international comparison and contact will be made as necessary with the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development in Paris.

In all this it will be important to keep in close touch with others engaged in research in the British Isles, notably the British Travel Association. We want to discover the patterns of changes in leisure and trends in personal expenditure and what significance they have for us. The dilemma for tourism in Scotland is that the season is too short to attract major investment and that only major investment is likely substantially to extend the season. Winter sports have proved a most effective way of extending the season, but we must not think that this is the only way of doing so and we intend to see what help research can give us in tackling this whole problem.

The Committee will no doubt know that the Government, indeed previous Governments, have been attacked for not providing substantial sums of money for the extension of the tourist industry. I appeal to the Committee to reflect that it must decide before joining in such attacks whether it believes that tourism, like agriculture, should be heavily subsidised by the Treasury. If they believe this, it is a point of principle that the Government should give certain assistance, which I shall describe, and should give it in certain specific and effective 10 ways. This is the option which the Government have taken in this matter.

I turn to another aspect in which the Government are being helpful in this regard—planning for tourism by local authorities. There was little systematic planning for tourism until, after two large-scale conferences on tourism convened at Braemar and Edinburgh by the Town Planning Institute and the Scottish Tourist Board, the Scottish Development Department issued its now celebrated Circular No. 2 of 1962. This recognised the increasing demands that would be made on the countryside by the growth of tourism and the needs of our own people for open-air recreation. It emphasised the importance of ensuring that in catering for these we did not encourage or indeed allow offensive development. Accordingly, the circular asked local planning authorities both to identify areas of great landscape value and to make plans for tourist provision.

Basic planning of this kind is of the first importance. Without it, effort becomes fragmented; resources can be easily dissipated or actual harm can be done. A tourist plan is as important as an industrial plan in its own field: it is, in effect, notice to potential developers of the kinds of investment which will be encouraged and of how development can be expected to fit into its environment. In this very competitive field authorities cannot hope to attract investment unless they have their prospectus ready.

Only two of these plans out of a possible 57 have so far been formally presented and a few more are coming up to the finishing line. From what we have seen of these they are quite first-class, and they have demonstrated beyond any doubt the value of the exercise when it is well carried out. We appreciate the difficulties facing local authorities, in particular the shortages of technical staff. Nevertheless, progress has been disappointing. It is vitally important that more progress should be made and made quickly. I appeal to each hon. Member to consider how best he might encourage his local planning authorities in his constituency to push ahead with this proposal more speedily. Suggestions have been made to local authorities for speeding up these plans, such as by the engagement of consultants. The Department JPEG 11 would be glad to give advice and help on any particular case put to it.

I turn to the ten-point plan, the other submission which the Government had on coming into office. This was prepared last year, I understand, and laid on the desk of the new Secretary of State, whoever he was to be, in September last year. The Government have looked closely at this, and I should like somewhat sketchily to comment upon the ten points. Hon. Members who are familiar with them will be able to check this as we go along.

The first point is the early completion of the national tourist plan which the Board see emerging from the individual plans of local authorities under the 1962 circular, to which I referred earlier. The initiative in that lies with the local authorities. The Government are anxious, and have done everything they can in this regard, to encourage them. We had a ceremony of acceptance of the Roxburgh plan and the East Lothian plan in June.

Points two and three call for the establishment by the Government of a capital fund for loans at reasonable interest rates for new holiday accommodation and for a Government-built chain of hotels in rural areas. There are at present eleven extensions or new projects which involve 900 beds. Nevertheless, we accept the need for new and extended accommodation. It is, however, for the industry to provide it. The Government will encourage and help it to do so, but it is fundamentally for the industry to provide this. Grants and loans are available under the Local Employment Acts in most, although not all, tourist areas in Scotland. Between April, 1960, and May, 1965, the assistance offered was almost £650,000.

We should like a greater use to be made of these facilities. They are there for the asking if the industry wants them. In the Highlands, they will be supplemented by the help which the new Highlands and Islands Development Board will be able to give if for any reason in a particular case the existing help is not enough. When we reflect that the Third reading of the Bill which establishes the Board will take place elsewhere today—and I hope that it will not be long before 12 it receives Royal Assent—we see how relevant it is to put this matter in context.

The fourth recommendation is for an advisory service to hotel keepers. This is an excellent idea. There is no reason why this advisory service should not be self-financing. We shall co-operate with the industry in any way we can to get it going.

We accept and heartily endorse the need for an accommodation inspection scheme—the fifth point. At our best, our hospitality is excellent, and it is quite wrong that our reputation should be put at risk by the backsliders. We shall encourage the new board to make this a priority in its programme. A trial scheme on a voluntary basis may be advisable in the first place. At least, when experience is gained it will be clear whether a statutory scheme is desirable. If, however, the Board wants a statutory scheme, the implication is that there must be a statutory board. This is a difficulty which we face in this regard. That is a nettle which we shall have to grasp at a future date, which may not be too far away.

The sixth point—the more effective training of workers—is of central importance. A two-year full-time course, leading, we hope, to a new ordinary National Diploma in Catering will be introduced in 1965–66 at Napier College, Edinburgh, and perhaps also at Langside College, Glasgow. More advanced hotel and catering courses are being provided in the Scottish Hotel School which forms part of Stathclyde University. Other courses leading to City and Guilds of London certificates are provided by local education authorities and, at a humbler but no less important level, there are cookery classes for crofters' wives and others who give bed and breakfast. More important still, the Minister of Labour is giving a lead in the setting up of an industrial training board for the industry. I hope that this proposal will be welcomed by the industry, especially in Scotland.

On the provision of information bureaux—the seventh point—I was delighted to open a few weeks ago an information bureau in Berwick. It was built with enlightened self-interest by one of the great oil companies—Shell. It will be organised and run by the Scottish Tourist Board and its expenses will be met by the surrounding local authorities.

Friend the Minister of State, representing the Government, will attend the championships of the European Federation of Sea Anglers at Ullapool in September. With regard to additional facilities for motoring visitors—the ninth point of the plan—the Government propose to spend £123 million on building and improving roads in Scotland during the next five years up to 1970. Almost all of these improvements will benefit the tourist industry. That is one of the boasts which we made in the roads debate. Even with our present inadequate roads, Scotland is still a better country in which to go touring than is England. I must not, however, be tempted, as the hon. Friend the Secretary of State and I have met. We propose to meet more of them in the summer to get more clearly what the picture should be in this regard.

The tenth point of the Board is an appeal for money for research. This is exactly what we have done.

Tourism, I agree, deserves a new deal in Scotland and we are determined that we shall have it. If our economic position were substantially different, we might look at other possible ways of helping the industry, but within the present context we are doing as much as the Government think is wise and possible. Despite our limited resources, we are making a contribution which amounts for three years to more than one-third of the Board's existing disposable income and which is substantially more than the industry itself contributes. We are directing it in the way in which it will be most productive. It is the Government's duty to stimulate and support what is basically a private enterprise industry. It is now up to the industry itself to respond—to play a fuller part not only in the Board itself but in the need for better education, better investment and better information. Only then can the assets of Scotland as a tourist nation—the great natural beauty of the country and our traditional hospitality and friendliness as a people—be fully realised.

I am glad that the Tourist Board also recognises that "more sound work has been done in Scotland than in any other part of Britain to face future demands on holiday and leisure-time facilities". I am also glad that it pays a well-deserved tribute to the work of the Scottish Development Department.

Hon. Members will all have read the Board's latest report, which seems to me to make very good sense. I want to comment briefly on some of its proposals, as the Minister has done.

As he said, there can be no doubt that the main problem faced by the industry is the shortness of the season. There is also the weather, but that is part of the same problem. Any research directed to finding out how the season can be extended by means of staggered holidays or fresh amenities in different parts of the country will be well worth while. In most parts of Scotland—with the possible exception of the winter sports areas—hotel keepers and those engaged in the tourist industry generally can count on being busy, and very often too busy, for only four or five months in the year—if that—while for the other seven or eight months they are almost certainly losing money.

This is bound to mean that they have very little, if any, spare cash for improvements or extensions of their premises, or even for maintenance.
