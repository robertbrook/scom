Major life activities: guidance—

".—(1) The Secretary of State may issue guidance about the matters to be taken into account in determining whether a consequence of an impairment substantially limits one or more major life activities.

  1. The guidance may, among other things, give examples of—

Mr. Hague
: It is a great pleasure to start our consideration of the Bill. Clause 1 is fundamental to the Bill. It is its cornerstone, as was clause 1 of the Disability Discrimination Bill. It defines disability, so it is sensible that it should be at the beginning. It delineates those who are to enjoy the rights conferred under the Bill. It is crucial to the success of any legislation of this sort that its definitions be right. The Committee will easily agree about that.

I spent much time in the autumn, when drafting the Disability Discrimination Bill, and the Committee spent much time when debating that Bill, considering its definition of disability. It is a difficult question. There is no absolute answer. We have to draw a line and people will inevitably fall on either side of that line. The Committee debating the Disability Discrimination Bill spent much time considering whether people with a history of disability should be included in the Bill's 44 definitions. My hon. The approach adopted in the Government Bill, which does not use the words "major life activities", is better suited for use in the United Kingdom courts. We must frame the law in a way that will work in the UK courts, which have a different tradition from that of American courts. Courts in this country have a tradition of literal interpretation. Amendment No. 2 would thus omit the words "of that person" from the definition. That difficulty has been recognised in the United States. Regulations published under the Americans with Disabilities Act list activities such as sleeping, breathing and walking as examples of major life activities. The amendment relates directly to clause 1, but it relates indirectly also to part II of the Bill, which provides for the Secretary of State to appoint an organisation that will be named the Disability Rights Commission. One of its duties will be to issue codes of practice to provide practical guidance on the elimination of discrimination against disabled people.

The hon. Member for Derbyshire, North-East said that the Government do not support the establishment of such a commission. We believe that there is more than one way in which to tackle discrimination. However, the Civil Rights (Disabled Persons) Bill seems to require that, of the organisations and individuals that the commission consults to formulate codes of practice, voluntary 78 organisations that represent disabled people should comprise only disabled people.

I wonder whether that provision is unnecessarily restrictive. It is generally out of keeping with the broad manner of consultation exercise to which we have now become accustomed, such as the consultation process that was held last summer on the Government's proposals for tackling discrimination against disabled people.

The Disability Discrimination Bill will not establish a commission; it will establish a National Disability Council. The duties that we propose for the council would allow it to consult those persons directed by the Secretary of State and anyone else that it wished to consult. I have no doubt that it will wish to consult organisations of and for disabled people as well as those that represent employers, workers and providers of goods, facilities and services—when it considers that appropriate. Indeed, anyone who has an opinion on disability discrimination should be able to be consulted on such matters.
