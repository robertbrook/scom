New clause 2—Extension of levy—

"(1) In section 2(1) of the Film Levy Finance Act 1981, the words 'in respect of—

New clause 3— Levy on pre-recorded video cassettes—

"The Secretary of State shall by order establish a scheme to impose a £1 levy on all pre-recorded video cassettes sold in the United Kingdom, the proceeds of which shall be redistributed under the scheme to the makers of British films released on video cassettes, pro rata to their sales in the year in question, in the form of investment credits to be used for the production of British films.".

I indicated on Second Reading that I have no particular quarrel with scrapping the Eady levy. My JPEG 81 quarrel is with the Government's failure to put anything realistic in its place. It is worth reminding ourselves, particularly in the context of new clause 2, that three different kinds of levy have been proposed. I appreciate that we cannot discuss in detail one or two aspects of those levies and that amendment No. 39 relates to the proposal for a levy on films shown on television. But the three options which have been discussed and booted around in all conversations and debates on the subject are levies on films shown on televison; pre-recorded video cassettes and blank tapes.

In particular, we must look at the proposed levy for films shown on television and the two major reasons why such levies are being proposed. First, and most important, the levy must raise sufficient money to plough back into the promotion of British films. My hon. Friends do not regard the Government's proposals as sufficient for the promotion of British films. We want to see money raised from the use of British films and from films produced by the film industry. We also want to see the money raised from their use being ploughed back into further production. That is the first principle. Secondly, there is the principle of copyright—again I appreciate that we cannot discuss that in detail—and of having some return from the film industry, from the use of its products for those who are not part of the production of films.

I turn to the specific point about a levy on films on television. The hon. Member for Hendon, North said, in his able introduction to the amendment, that there were 4 billion individual film viewings on television each year in this country as compared with 66 million cinema admissions. That is an instructive comparison because it demonstrates clearly that the vast majority of film viewing, takes place in the living room rather than in the cinema. I accept everything that was said in our earlier debates about the value of big screen viewing and the great difference between viewing a film in the cinema and viewing it on television. But that does not detract from the important fact that far more people watch films on television in their living rooms than go to the cinema.

A breakdown of viewing between the channels shows that in 1983, 786 films were shown on BBC 1 and BBC 2; 350 films were shown on ITV's main channel and 380 on Channel 4, totalling about 1,500 films shown on television in that year.

It is important to remember that the buying-in price of those films is remarkably low and does not contribute significantly to the cost of production. The sale of films to television companies contributes around 6 per cent. to the cost of production. If the number of people who watch films on television is compared with the number who watch them in the cinema, the difference is extremely significant and makes for cheap and easy television for the television companies.

The Christmas period is approaching when that point will be amply demonstrated. Not only do films provide an easy mechanism by which television companies can use up time and not only are they a cheap form of doing so, but they are also popular with the public. The viewing figures for films—especially feature films—shown on television are consistently 82 high. It is clear that a contribution should be made by television to the films industry for that priceless asset which the television companies receive and use.

There is a lot of dumping in this country of American-made films for showing on television. When packages of films are bought, many are bought at very low prices and put some money back in the American film industry. That means not only that we buy and view American products, but that our homegrown British products do not get the attention they deserve from television audiences.

There is a very strong argument for some sort of recompense from the television companies and perhaps from television viewers, although the precise mechanism whereby a levy is introduced is a matter for discussion. I hope that the Government will respond in detail to that point if the amendment is accepted. Some recompense is required from the television industry to the film industry in respect of the large scale and, at the moment, very cheap use made of films by television companies. That is an important argument and should be considered.

New clause 2 takes us further than amendment No. 39, which I also support and hope to vote for. The new clause specifies that the revenue to be received from whatever mechanism the Secretary of State introduces for a levy on the use of films, should be a minimum of £10 million.

I should like to see a much higher figure, but £10 million would at least give some degree of security and a ground base for the promotion of British films and would approximately equal the amount raised when the Eady levy was first introduced. I look forward to hearing what the Minister says, and I shall try to catch your eye again, Mr. Crowther. if I am disappointed by his reply.

A further point which I wish to make involves the relative cost of programming on television. If we consider television and its own product—that is, its own material—we find that the average hourly cost is for ITV £100,000, for BBC 1 and BBC 2 f90,000 and for Channel 4 £35,000. But if we compare that with the average cost of feature films bought in by BBC and ITV we see—it must be remembered that most of them last for much longer than an hour—that for the BBC the sum is £6,000 and for ITV £7,000.

That demonstrates graphically the difference in cost to the television companies of buying in feature films, and shows that in terms of recompense made to the film industry for the talent, energy and effort which goes into the production of films, extremely low amounts are paid by television. The amendments and new clauses are pleading for—I hope this will not fall on deaf ears—greater recognition from the Government of the necessary tribute from television, and perhaps from other sources, to ensure that the film industry can produce films, thrive and flourish. I hope that the Government will respond positively.
