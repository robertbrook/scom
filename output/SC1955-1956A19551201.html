The Chairman
: Before I call the first Amendment, I must remind the Committee that there is a Financial Resolution and a Ways and Means Resolution. I do not propose to direct that they should be read, but if any Members of the Committee desire to see them, they will find copies on the Table.

I also want to call attention to the fact that adequate notice must be given of Amendments, as moving them without notice causes inconvenience. As a general rule, I do not intend to call manuscript Amendments.

Clause 1.
—(ESTABLISHMENT AND PRINCIPAL FUNCTIONS OF SUGAR BOARD.)

Mr. Frederick Willey
: I beg to move, in page 1, line 7, to leave out "appointed day" and to insert:

"first day of January, nineteen hundred and sixty."

Let me be frank. This is what is generally known as a wrecking Amendment. It will probably meet the convenience of the Committee if I explain briefly the Opposition's attitude towards the Bill.

Perhaps I might be allowed to stray near the bounds of order by congratulating the Minister on a speech which I heard him make yesterday. We all enjoyed it, and it impressed us very much. For reasons which the right hon. Gentleman will understand, I cannot say more.

As we explained on Second Reading, we oppose the Bill on three fundamental grounds. We think the opportunity ought to have been taken to remove the element of private capital from the British Sugar Corporation. Our attitude is that, if this is not done, we should prefer the 4 present arrangements to continue until my hon. Friends and I have an opportunity to do it.

We are also critical of the Bill because we believe—I know this is a matter which disturbs the Minister—that it disturbs Commonwealth relations. There is not such a confident feeling about the continuance of the Commonwealth Sugar Agreement as there was before the introduction of the Bill.

It is for that reason that we move our Amendment. By 1st January, 1960, the position would be quite clear. We should know then whether the Commonwealth Sugar Agreement was being continued, and, equally important, we should know the terms on which it was being continued.

Our third objection to the Bill is that we strongly object to the fiscal policy behind it, which has been pursued in other directions by the Government, of placing the burden of the aid to British agriculture directly upon the consumer. This is novel in so far as it is now affecting the 1947 price guarantees. This, again, is very disturbing to the British farmer. After all, the right hon. Gentleman is aware by now that there is great disturbance in the farming industry. In spite of the machinery of the guarantees, the farmers are losing confidence in the Government's intention to stand behind the guarantees. That is another contribution to the feeling of disturbance.

Here we find price support for maintaining British sugar production in this country—after all, in this case it is given largely on strategic defence grounds—placed not upon the Exchequer but upon the housewife when she goes shopping.

I want to correct an impression which might be gained by the casual reader of the OFFICIAL REPORT. The under-Secre-tary of State for Commonwealth Relations was reported as indicating dissent when I put forward the same argument about support for Commonwealth development and welfare. I gather that the Under-Secretary was merely nodding his head. No doubt he was carried away by the eloquence of my peroration; but he was not indicating dissent. It is accepted that there is a contribution which can be made in the way of Commonwealth development and welfare.

JPEG 5 We think it wrong that there should be support by means of a tax upon the housewife when she buys sugar across the grocer's counter. Aid of the character required should be placed squarely on the Exchequer. That is the fundamental political difference between the two sides.

We move the Amendment because we are confident that by 1st January, 1960, the Labour Party will be in a position of authority and responsibility in which it can ensure that this fiscal burden shall not be a permanent part of our legislation.

I move the Amendment with a slight degree of reluctance. I know that there are many hon. Gentlemen opposite who would like to get rid of the Bill. They do not want their own Government to put on the Statute Book a Measure which is largely Socialistic. They are very embarrassed in view of what they said during the General Election about physical controls, price controls, import licensing and the rest. Yet the Government are here seeking to impose conditions of that nature. This places me in some difficulty, because I do not particularly want to welcome support on that ground, although I should like the Government to accept the Amendment if they feel that the weight of opinion on the Government side of the Committee is sufficiently strong to justify their postponing the matter altogether until 1960.

Apart from our fundamental objections, we support the Bill for the reasons which cause hon. Gentlemen opposite to oppose it. If we have, as I believe we must, to support bulk purchase through the Commonwealth Sugar Agreement, then we have to reinforce that by a system of physical control. Again, if we accept an industry which is largely a monopoly—it is a very closely defined monopoly—we think it right and proper that the Minister should reserve to himself powers of price control.

I have taken this opportunity to inform the Committee that we shall not oppose many of the Bill's provisions in detail. Our objections to the Measure are on broad grounds, and broad political grounds, and it is for this reason that I move the Amendment.

Mr. Gordon Walker

Incidentally, it is not such an extraordinary idea that we should wait until a General Election so that the people can express their views one way or the other on a great and important issue like this which has many Commonwealth implications.

We are probably all agreed that the time has come to get rid of State trading in the precise form in which it has been going on and is going on in this respect at the moment. That does not mean that we are agreed about what should be substituted for the present system. I think that what the Government are proposing to put in its place is a great deal worse than the present system. There are much better ways of getting rid of State trading now, without all the consequences, both internally and in the Commonwealth, which will flow from what is proposed in the Bill. We could have a Sugar Board with real powers for purchasing sugar at large, and able, perhaps with the Corporation, to run the refineries and so on, and we should then avoid all the extreme complexities which arise in the Bill.

The trouble with the Bill is that the Government, for doctrinal reasons, are determined to restore a phoney appearance of private enterprise. The appearance will be accurate only in so far as private enterprise will make the profit, and not in that it will have to be enterprising. All the risks of private enterprise will be taken by the Board and the Cor- 8 poration, particularly with regard to imported sugar, but the profits will be made by private enterprise. There is also in the Bill a very large concealed subsidy element which will go to private enterprise. That is not the sort of picture of private enterprise that my hon. Friends and I have.

I am not altogether in agreement with my hon. Friend the Member for Sunderland, North (Mr. F. Willey) when he says that the Bill has Socialistic elements in it. It seems to me that all it has is some unnecessary physical controls. No Socialist wants unnecessary physical controls. We certainly say that physical controls must be enacted and enforced where necessary in the public interest, but the physical controls in the Bill result from the extraordinary doctrinal attempt to restore a non-existent, fictional free market.

If we had a Board with proper powers, we should not need to give the Minister the fantastic physical controls which I can quite understand worry hon. Gentlemen opposite very much. The controls are most extraordinary and most disturbing. All sorts of new things are made offences which are not offences today, and all sorts of new penalties appear. The whole thing could be done very much more simply. If it were done in a proper Socialist way we should not have all these physical controls, because the Board would be given general powers to carry on as other boards do and the Minister would not have the awful responsibility of deciding when, where and to what extent to enforce the provisions about new offences and penalties.
