[Mr. Ifor Davies in the Chair]

The Endangered Species (Import and Export) Act 1976 (Modification) (No. 2) Order 1978

10.38 a.m.

Mr. Robin Hodgson
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the Endangered Species (Import and Export) Act 1976 (Modification) (No. 2) Order 1978 (S.I. 1978, No. 1939).

I begin by thanking the Minister for allowing for a slight delay in the commencement of the Committee's proceedings. I also congratulate him on the advances that have been made in this modification order before us. The advances regarding the import and export of ivory, covered in paragraph 7, and of reptile skins, covered in paragraph 16, represent valuable improvements. It is thoroughly satisfactory that we in this country should be starting to take a more aggressive and responsible attitude towards the international trade in such articles.

My enthusiasm for what the Minister has done has been slightly tempered by the fact that it will be really effective only if it is enforced. It is in the enforcement provisions of most of these instruments and of the Act that most concern has arisen. If we are to be able to say a really sincere thank you to the Minister and his Department for what they have done in the order, that can only be when it is clear to us all that proper and strict enforcement of the provisions is being undertaken. None the less, nothing that I say will in any way detract from the fact that the instrument represents a step forward, and on that the Minister and his Department are to be congratulated.

I should like to make a number of points that fall broadly under three headings: first, the method by which the order has been made and its timing; secondly, some highly specific matters about the inclusions and exclusions covered in the instrument, and in particular the definitions of various categories of mammals and reptiles; and thirdly—bearing in mind how narrowly drawn the instrument is—I hope that I shall be allowed to stray a little wider in order to discuss some of the broader implications of the instrument and of those that have preceded it.

4 I know very well that not all the points I shall make will lie within the purview of the Minister. The Minister knows my view that there are other Departments of Government which have been extremely unhelpful and slack—" slack " is not too strong a word—in carrying out their undertakings and responsibilities under the Endangered Species (Import and Export) Act. I think that privately the Minister would probably support the view that his Department is not necessarily given all the assistance which it looks for from other Government Departments.

Therefore, when I am critical of what is being done under the Act, some of that criticism will quite unfairly appear to be aimed at the Minister's Department, whereas it should be aimed at other Departments, which we can never get at when we debate the matter. If the Minister feels that I am being unfair, I assure him that the criticism is because we cannot get at those Government Departments which are so often responsible for the real guts of the enforcement of the statutory instruments.

I do not seek to make any party political or national points, but to make an international point of the broadest and most important type. The phrase "space ship earth" has been used to describe the world, its inter-relationships and its ecosystem. We are discussing an implication of space ship earth. Strikes may come and strikes may go, but if, because we fail to draft and examine these orders sufficiently closely, we allow one or more species to be wiped out, there is no going back over that ground.

It is fair to say that, although the matter does not necessarily attract a tremendous amount of attention in the House, we are dealing with fundamental issues which will not just affect the world for the next four or five years, but which will affect the animal population of the world for ever. So when we go into details and find ourselves involved with Latin titles and with what may appear to be minor matters, the issues underlying them are extremely important.

I turn to the timing of the statutory instrument. The Minister knows my interest in the subject. When it was announced in the press in the middle of October that the order would be brought forward, I wrote to him to make a number of points and to ask what specific provisions would be included in it. I wrote to the Minister on 30 October, and on 20 November I received a letter from him which JPEG 5 covered in broad terms the points that he proposed to include in the order.

That was fine. But I was extremely disappointed to note that subsequently the order was made on 22 December, laid on 29 December and came into force on 19 January. I can only describe that as being less than helpful.

The Minister knows that I and other hon. Members have a particular interest in this subject. To make an instrument of this sort three days before Christmas, to complete it between Christmas and the New Year, and to bring it into force on 19 January, four days after Parliament has reassembled, does not seem to be the most helpful way of promoting a full and detailed discussion of proposals which many of us believe to be extremely important.

I was disappointed that this rather helter-skelter approach was taken in bringing the order into effect. I shall be grateful if the Minister can indicate some of the thinking behind the need for that timing, with the order being made just after the House had gone into Recess, and so on.

I turn to the specifics of the order. It is necessary for me to go into a little detail here and, unfortunately, it involves jumping about to various parts of the order. Paragraph 15, on page 4, deals with:

"Musk derived from any animal of the species Moschus moschiferus (musk deer)."

For the benefit of the Committee I shall expand on the reasons and on our concern.

Musk is used as a perfume fixative. An ordinary perfume placed on the skin quickly evaporates. It is, therefore, given additional body and, as it were, staying power by the addition of a fixative. Ambergris, which comes from the stomach of a whale, and musk are the two most commonly used.

The process by which musk is extracted is particularly horrible and cruel. In particular, although it is not included in the order, the civet cat is foully- ill-treated in an effort to get it to give its musk. Animals will secrete musk only when they are maltreated, angry or frightened. For example, the civet cat is kept in a small box about the size of the top of a desk. It is never let out and every three or four months it is poked with a stick and enraged in order to get it to secrete musk. The films of this procedure are particularly horrible.

The order deals with musk from deer. 6 The civet cat is not endangered and, therefore, does not fall within the purview of the order. However, musk from deer is used for similar purposes.

It is not clear whether the order will prohibit perfumes or other articles in which musk is suspended. It is all very well to say that musk is prohibited, but one rarely imports musk as musk. Perfume manufacturing in this country is small. Nearly all the trade in musk in this country is through perfumes and fixatives of one sort or another. I hope that the Minister will make clear whether the use of musk derived from the musk deer is likely to fall within the prohibition of this order.

Are we referring to musk in its distilled state or in its used state? That is important. If the Minister says that it is musk in the former category, in its distilled state, the paragraph is worthless, because imports and exports of it are negligible. However, a great deal of it comes to this country in the form of perfume. I await the Minister's comments on that.

I turn, secondly, to paragraphs 17 and 18 on pages 4 and 5. These paragraphs deal with the turtle. Paragraph 17 covers:

"The whole or any part of the bony shell and its covering".

Paragraph 17(2) relates to the claws, and paragraph 18 to "The meat and cartilage".

Nowhere does the order refer to turtle oil, which is in fact the most valuable part of the turtle. It is used in expensive soaps and perfumes. While it is quite useful and valuable to have a prohibition on the trade in the shell of the turtle, the question is whether this will cover the oil of the turtle.

For example, does the definition of "The meat and cartilage" include the oil, which is presumably partially suspended within that meat and cartilage? It is critical that we know the answer to this question for similar reasons, namely, whether this is to be prohibited and whether it will be prohibited in terms of the pure state or in terms of soaps and perfumes which may be made from it.

I understand that Her Majesty's Customs and Excise—that wonderful body of men—cannot identify turtle oil, or claims that it cannot do so. It may be useful if the Minister can say what steps he intends to take to ensure that it can.

I turn to the question of skins. Paragraph 9(3) relates to the raw skins of animals. It reads: JPEG 7

"Tanned or dressed furskin of a defined animal … assembled in plates, rectangles, crosses, trapeziums or otherwise."

I understand that last year there were problems over the imports of leopard skins. It is illegal to import leopard skins individually. But a loophole was found in the provisions by sewing the leopard skins together to make them into bedspreads. They were then imported as furnishings. If we now have a similar provision in paragraph 9(3), will this block that loophole? The words "or otherwise" seem to be the critical ones. Will the importers and exporters of animal skins merely find another way to get around this prohibition?

Obviously, there are all sorts of ways in which one could bring in leopard skins. They could be brought in as coverings on chairs, sofas and so on. Once it is here a sofa is dismantled and the leopard skin can be resold in its raw state. Undoubtedly that has happened in the course of the past 12 months. Her Majesty's Customs and Excise claims that it could not recognise leopard skins sewn together into a bedspread. Again, I ask the Minister to say what action he will take to ensure that the Customs and Excise takes a reasonable and common-sense analytical approach to this problem.

I turn to paragraph 16, on page 4, dealing with reptile hides. Obviously, this covers footwear, watchstraps, belts and so on. What is not clear is whether the provisions of paragraph 9(3) prevent the clever spotting of loopholes in the case of reptile skins, such as producing a chair covered in crocodile skin, sewing it together and dismantling it once it is inside this country.

Will the new prohibitions in paragraph 16(2) enable us to prevent unusual or useless articles covered with the skin in question being brought in, taken to pieces and then put to use in a way which is more profitable for the importer and exporter? I should be grateful for the Minister's comments on that.

Finally, with regard to the specific provisions, I should like to deal briefly with one exclusion. I believe that other members of the Committee wish to speak on this, so I simply say briefly that the great omission from the order is, of course, sperm oil. It is clear that we have to give the leather industry time to ensure that alternative products are available to replace sperm oil, which is currently of vital 8 importance to the industry.

Perhaps the Minister could say whether he is satisfied that speedy enough progress is being made towards the day from when, as I think all of us, including the British Leather Federation agree, it will be desirable and possible to prohibit the import of sperm oil. We are not asking him to introduce the prohibition now. I understand the industrial problems that would arise. But it would be helpful if he could put on record what progress is being made in this area, which has aroused great public concern, and say whether he feels that that progress is fast enough to enable a prohibition to be introduced in the near future. Perhaps he can give us some idea of the timing on that.

Those are the specific points that I wished to make. In conclusion, I return to two broad aspects.

In my opening remarks I mentioned the question enforcement. If the order is to be worth the paper it is written on, it must be enforced rigorously and universally throughout the country, because those who deal in endangered species are astute and clever, and are quite able to find ways round the more obvious points and ports of entry to this country. The profits are such that it is worth while doing that. We must therefore have strict and universal enforcement.

The problem here is divided responsibility for enforcement. As the Minister knows, I am highly critical of the Customs and Excise performance in this area and of its part in the United Kingdom's contribution to international treaties on this matter. When I wrote to the Financial Secretary last year about this, he replied—and, frankly, he hid behind the Minister's Department—in a letter dated 25 April:

"The Customs and Excise Act 1952 empowers Customs to permit transshipment of prohibited or restricted goods, but in exercising this power Customs are guided by the Department responsible for the prohibition or restriction concerned, in this case the Department of the Environment."

The ball was thus flung firmly back into the Minister's court. As I understand it, if he tells them what is to be prohibited and restricted, Customs and Excise officials will undertake those restrictions and prohibitions. I hope that the Minister will give us a clear undertaking this morning that he intends to sling the ball back into their court, so that those of us who feel strongly about this matter can bring the appropriate pressure to bear on the Customs and JPEG 9 Excise Department.

I understand that the Minister's Department has never yet issued any code of practice regarding this order or previous orders. The question is why such a code of practice has not been produced. When we were discussing turtles, I mentioned paragraph 17(2), which refers to:

"Anything made wholly or partly from the bony shell".

But people do not arrive carrying turtle shells. They arrive carrying bangles made of turtle shell on their wrists. Products of this type are not obvious, in that they are often of an ornamental nature and as such not immediately recognisable.

It would be helpful if the Minister were able to tell us this morning that his Department was producing a child's guide that would be available to Customs officers, outlining the more common forms in which such products are imported into this country around the wrists or neck, or state by what other means they might be imported. Customs officers would then be able to make a realistic attempt at enforcement. It is understandable that long lists of Latin names mean nothing to the Customs desk at Heathrow airport. It must be impossible to sort them out. But a child's guide would be most helpful.

I know that people have purposely brought prohibited articles through Customs at Heathrow. They have declared them and Customs officers have done nothing because they did not know about the matter. In many cases Customs officers are more interested in making sure that people have not brought in an extra bottle of Scotch than in ensuring that this country does not play its part in moving towards the wiping out of another species.

I hope that the Minister will take a tougher and much more realistic attitude towards Customs officers' duties and put the ball in their court by producing an informative guide that is available to them and to travellers generally. I know that he will have much support if he does.

Most people would be horrified to think that they were carrying a turtleshell bracelet which came from an endangered species. They would not know, so let us tell them. Let us inform the public, so that they may play their part in ensuring that this country keeps its hands clean in this matter.

Finally, there is the question of transshipment. The order deals with additions to the list of endangered species and for the 10 first time extends it to cover parts of animal shells, skins and so on, and that is most welcome. But the fact is that because Heathrow airport remains one of the busiest airports in the world, much trade goes through there. Many Third World airlines terminate at Heathrow, and it becomes a trans-shipment point for many animals falling within this category.

The Minister knows that there are loopholes there. One of these is the use of the temporary import licence. The question is how the order will affect that loophole.

The system that is, quite simply, a dealer will bring the animals in from abroad and leave them here for 28 days while he circulates other dealers, gets his auction over, marks up his goods and ships them on, having switched airfreight tickets meanwhile. The Minister knows that this happens on a large scale. There are several well-documented cases about it, even though it is kept as far below the parapet as possible. Blind eyes are turned all over the place. It is fair that we should ask this morning whether this order will also have blind eyes turned to it.

The United Kingdom is a signatory to the convention, and there is no doubt that the fact that goods have come from the United Kingdom in the first place is seen in their country of destination as a clear indication that there are proper import and export documents and restrictions relating to an animal. The United Kingdom is used for a washing effect, so to speak, which enables the dealer, first, to make a substantial profit and, secondly, to switch his sales of endangered animals to whichever part of the world offers him the best reward. If this statutory instrument is to be effective we must have indications that the trans-shipment provisions are to be closed up tightly.

I end as I began, by thanking the Minister for the steps that his Department have taken with regard to the order. It is a worthy step forward and one that I certainly applaud. But there are a number of specific points which make the order less satisfactory than it might otherwise be.

There are also several important general points which mean that this country—not because of the Minister's Department but because of other Departments—is not playing its full role in the protection of endangered species. The Minister must give a clear indication of the detailed and the general points relating to this order as to how his Department proposes in future to JPEG 11 tighten up the provisions, thereby ensuring that we do our bit—perhaps our little bit, but our bit none the less—to ensure that other species will not follow the many that have gone down the road to extinction.

The Under-Secretary of State for the Environment (Mr. Kenneth Marks)
: I am grateful to learn that the hon. Member for Walsall, North (Mr. Hodgson) will not oppose the order. In fact he expressed some pleasure at our having introduced it. As he said, it is already in effect.

As to the timing, as soon as we received the minimum list from the three countries, JPEG 17 I felt we should introduce an order as soon as possible, so that we could show at Costa Rica that it had worked, even if it were working for only two or three months. I had hoped that it would be before 22 December—at the end of November or the beginning of December—but a printing difficulty caused delay. There was no intention on my part to avoid parliamentary debate on the matter. Looking at the dates—22 December to 19 January—I must admit that the hon. Gentleman has a point, but I am sure he would not feel that I or the Department wanted to avoid debate on the matter.

The hon. Gentleman said a great deal about Customs and Excise. I had a feeling that he must once have been stopped at Dover and had the boot of his car emptied, or something.

Perhaps I should give the background to the order and explain why we are dealing with the matter by an order under the Endangered Species (Import and Export) Act. The Washington Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora—the Washington Convention to most of us—was signed in 1973 and ratified in this country in 1976. The Endangered Species (Import and Export) Act was passed later that year to replace temporary powers taken when ratifying.

The Convention has two categories of control of general importance. First, for species in danger of extinction because of trade there are restrictions which effectively ban commercial trade. For the second category—species threatened by or vulnerable to trade—there are controls designed to monitor but not restrict trade, with the intention that countries where 18 trade in a species is likely to endanger its survival will get early warning of the threat and take their own preventive measures.

The United Kingdom has only one species, the peregrine, which is listed as endangered on the international list, but we are one of the major consumers and processors of exotic leathers, furs and ivory, so our controls in support of the Convention are designed to ensure that imports and trade in wild life can be monitored and restricted by the Customs and Excise and by the police as required by the Convention.

Crucial in these controls is the listing of parts and derivatives of endangered and vulnerable species. Whereas live animals and whole carcasses are all controlled— except for domesticated species and a few very plentiful wild ones—parts and derivatives are treated differently and are specified in schedule 3 of the Act. This ranges from whale products to feathers, and was drawn up to ensure that all prime products were controlled in such a way that Customs officers could be expected to recognise that the specimen would be subject to licensing. It was a comprehensive but not exhaustive list, and even in that form we are satisfied that the bulk of trade in endangered species products was controlled on entry into the country.

In the Convention the requirement is to control listed species and their "readily recognisable" parts and derivatives—what is "readily recognisable" is not defined. Unfortunately, the result has been that in all too many countries no controls of parts and derivatives have yet been established. So, at the special working session of the Convention in Geneva in 1977, the United Kingdom, Germany and Switzerland undertook to prepare a list which would establish a minimum level of control which any Customs service could be expected to operate without specialist officers.

This "minimum list" was finished last summer and will be submitted to the Convention when it meets in March. The authors were forced to accept that recognition of whale meat or oil by species was not practicable and as, at present, only a few whales and dolphins are listed in the appendices it would be wrong to include whale products on the minimum list. The United Kingdom, of course, already restricts imports of virtually all whale products, and we believe those controls to be effective here.

JPEG 19 Another task undertaken by this country was preparation of a review of the conservation status of all cetacea—whales, dolphins and porpoises—for the Costa Rica meeting. When the review was finished in September the Scientific Authority for Animals, our statutory advisers under the Act, recommended that the United Kingdom seek the addition of some 11 species of dolphin and porpoises to the endangered, appendix I, list and all other cetecea not on appendix I to be included on appendix 2. This we have done.

There are many reasons why these proposals make good conservation sense. One porpoise or whale is as good as another for many commercial purposes. Especially apt to this discussion in justifying the listing of all cetacea is that by so doing the difficulty in distinguishing between products of species is largely removed. Customs control of whale and porpoise meat and oil should then be practicable. We shall be looking at a possible further modification after the Costa Rica conference. If our proposals are accepted we shall then argue for the inclusion of whale products on the minimum list, and we hope that all parties will undertake to control their use.

As I said earlier, the United Kingdom helped to draft the minimum list, which we support. We have gone further, and the order which the hon. Gentleman sought to annul—I should like to learn that he did not really, mean what he said—is our implementation of that minimum list. In many respects the order goes beyond that list, especially where there are problems with look-alike species. All reptile skins and many of their products are now controlled to ensure that the endangered and threatened lizards and snakes are protected. We have not introduced controls on items which are not in significant trade and which are not a reason for pressure on the species.

We have not yet made sperm whale oil subject to licence, as the species is not yet listed as endangered or threatened by trade. We maintain adequate trade statistics to show whether such a threat is developing. Incidentally, those statistics show that in 1970 we imported 14,341 tonnes of sperm whale oil. By 1974 it was down to 11,474 tonnes, and in 1978 it was 4,269 tonnes.
