Clause 127
INFORMATION ABOUT HOUSING REGISTER

Mr. Clive Betts
(Sheffield, Attercliffe)
: I beg to move amendment No. 408, in page 70, line 38, leave out

'other member of the public'

and insert

'person without the prior consent of the applicant'.

It is an important principle that information kept on housing registers, which is often sensitive for various reasons, should be exposed only to people who have a particular interest in having access to it. That must apply to the particular staff of an authority who administer the registration and allocation schemes. Such information should also be made available to those who are themselves registered. That is a fundamental principle.

I am concerned that the wording of the clause could at the very least be open to misinterpretation. Under subsection (2), the information about the housing register, "shall not be divulged to any other member of the public".

That is correct, in that it would exclude any members of the public other than the applicants from having information that is included on the register about the applicants. However, the provision implies that that information will be available to any other organisation or a member of an organisation.

Moreover, the provision could imply that a social worker who was not involved in the registration scheme as such, but who worked for the authority that was responsible for the scheme, would have access to applicants' files. Would local authority councillors have access to applicants' files? Are members of Parliament counted members of the public or could they have automatic access to the register? Could people who work in Government Departments have access to it because they are not members of the public? It does not seem that any of those individuals would be refused access to it under the clause.

646 Do the Government intend that any other member of the public should apply to civil servants acting in their official capacity for access to the information? We are worried that the wording of the provision could enable people in an official capacity, but who are not concerned directly with the registration scheme as such, to have access to it. That is why we have tabled the amendment.

The second of our concerns is that certain members of the public should have access to the applicants' records because the applicants have given their approval. Applicants may be ill or elderly and may want some information, but may not be happy dealing with it by telephone or by writing a letter. They may prefer to sign a statement asking a neighbour to collect certain information from the housing department or make representations to it on their behalf.

In those circumstances, it would be proper for neighbours or members of the family—acting on behalf of applicants—to discuss their cases with people in the local housing department. According to the provision, it seems that such people would be treated as any other member of the public and, thus, be excluded from having access to the information because no other member of the public in any capacity would have access to the information on the register in the applicant's name.

Indeed, how could citizens advice bureau staff gain access to an applicant's record, even if the applicant's permission had been given? It seems that they could not, because they would not be officials of the local authority or persons acting on behalf of a recognised governmental body. I presume that they would be treated as members of the public, who would be excluded—or would they? Those questions show that the clause could be open to misinterpretation.

We believe that the amendment makes it absolutely clear that the only people allowed access to information would be housing department staff involved with the registration scheme, the applicants themselves or people who had

"the prior consent of the applicant".

That is right; it is clearer, and everyone would understand the intention. I hope that the Minister will explain what the clause means, and say whether he would accept a change of wording, to clarify precisely who should have access to what can sometimes be very confidential information.

There is also the matter of cost. A family with several children will require larger accommodation and the price of that, certainly in the private rented sector, would be very high. That would have considerable implications for the family's ability to remain in employment if they became dependent on benefit, and for the public purse if it had to meet the high cost of privately rented accommodation.

Population projections suggest that there will be a continuing growth of smaller family units and single-person households, but in the absence of an adequate supply of suitable property for large families on low incomes, that group will have special difficulty in getting housing. For that reason it is entirely proper that such people should continue to be listed as one of the categories in need of reasonable preference in the allocation of housing.
