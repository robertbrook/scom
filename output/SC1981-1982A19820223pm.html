Those of us who want to see a reduction in the prison population, who believe that a reduction can be achieved without in any way endangering the public, naturally welcome the amendments. Those of us who favour lower maximum penalties start from what I think is now the well-established proposition, shared by both sides of the House of Commons, that long sentences do not produce any tangible benefit in either preventing crime or preventing re-offending.

In 1977 the Advisory Council on the Penal System's interim report about the length of prison sentences stated:

"we have come unanimously to the conclusion that a large number of sentences of imprisonment passed by the courts, especially in the short and medium term band of sentences, are longer than they need be, in the interests either of society or of the offender."

That was in paragraph 3 of the report. In 1979, the report of the May inquiry into the United Kingdom prison services quoted that statement by the advisory council, and went on to say:

"We have neither received nor have our inquiries disclosed any evidence that conflicts with this view. We are unanimous and emphatic in endorsing it".

That was in paragraph 3.64 of the May report.

Hon. Members will know that the advisory council undertook an exhaustive and thorough review of the effects of custodial sentences on young offenders specifically for its report " Young Adult Offenders ", published in 1974. In that report the council concluded that, for offenders of comparable background, time spent in custody could not be shown to produce any better results than supervision of that offender in the community.

A similar, indeed almost an identical conclusion, was reached by the Home Office in Research Study No. 35, " The Effectiveness of Sentencing", which analysed the results of a wide range of research studies from this country, the United States and other Western European countries, and summed up by saying:

"It can be concluded that there is no evidence that longer custodial sentences produce better results than shorter sentences.".

There already we have three eminently respectable, sensible, well-researched bodies or organisations, the advisory council, the May committee and the Home Office itself, all saying in quite clear and emphatic terms that shorter sentences are no less effective than longer sentences, that longer sentences are ineffective in terms of reducing recidivism or as acting as a general deterrent.

Indeed, on that particular point I accept that, when talking in terms of the sentences the courts may pass, it may be that they are affected by what they consider will be the deterrent effects of a sentence upon those who may be thinking about committing a similar offence; they may have in mind other potential offenders who they will deter by a 218 particularly punitive sentence from committing a particular crime.

If that is the case, it is only fair to point out what the Advisory Council on the Penal System's interim report " The Length of Prison Sentences " had to say in 1977 on the point. It said:

"Evidence about the effectiveness of general deterrents, however, is regrettably scant. Research findings are difficult to interpret and are inconclusive; the subject is in any event difficult to investigate by normal research techniques. All one can say with any degree of confidence is that nothing is likely to be lost by a general reduction in the length of sentences in the case of those offenders with whom this report is concerned."

By that remark it was referring to non-dangerous offenders.

We have some evidence, however, about whether punitive sentences have a deterrent effect. It is important to allude to evidence, because this is the argument that many people outside the House and certain hon. Gentlemen constantly adduce in favour of longer sentences. We will all acknowledge that the overwhelming evidence shows that longer sentences do not result in a lower rate of recidivism—that is common ground now amongst hon. Members on both sides of the Committee. Some hon. Members however, will probably advance the speculation that nevertheless longer sentences might be useful in deterring individuals from committing offences in the first place. As the advisory council's quotation which I have read indicates, that kind of research by its very nature is difficult to get hold of and is indeed scant.

We do have the conclusions of one very important and well-publicised study carried out by the Home Office research unit. You may remember, Mr. Dean, that in 1973 there was a widely-publicised case of a 20-year sentence being imposed on a 16-year-old boy mugger in Birmingham and 10-year sentences being imposed on his two accomplices. The results were followed up both in Birmingham and in six other urban centres by the Home Office research unit. The result was that there was no perceptible decrease in the number of robberies or assaults with intent in Birmingham or in the six other urban areas in the period after those widely-publicised and highly-punitive sentences were passed. Other studies both in this country and in the United States have indicated a similar conclusion, that the deterrent effect of punitive sentences has certainly not been established.

We know, then, that longer sentences have not been shown to be more effective than shorter sentences in preventing re-offending nor in acting as a deterrent. Therefore the argument for shorter sentences, particularly if one takes into account also the high cost of imprisonment and the overcrowding of our prisons, becomes overwhelming. The argument now for shorter sentences is accepted by the Magistrates' Association, by the Justices Clerks' Society and indeed by the Government. The argument has been advanced most eloquently and most notably by the Home Secretary and the Lord Chancellor. We all agree that there should be shorter prison sentences; we disagree on the methods that should be employed to achieve them.

JPEG 219 The Government insist that they should be achieved by exhortation and consent, that they will use the voluntary approach, constantly educating and exhorting the judiciary to pass shorter sentences, or to rely upon cases such as Upton v. Bibi to set guidelines for the lower courts. The Government can claim, as I have acknowledged, that this has resulted in a small reduction in the lengths of prison sentences. It should be emphasised, however, that there was only a marginal reduction in prison sentences imposed in 1980 compared with those imposed in 1979.

It is for that reason that the parliamentary all-party Penal Affairs Group proposed that legislation was essential if we were to see a significant reduction in the prison population. Like it or not, legislation is inescapable if we are to have any significant reduction in the present high level of people in custody. We need legislation, first, to produce an early release scheme and, secondly, to reduce, as my hon. Friend is attempting to do in these amendments, the maximum penalties that can be imposed by the court.

The Advisory Council on the Penal System, in its report " Sentences of Imprisonment—A review of Maximum Penalties " in 1978, set out a scheme for a reduction in the maximum sentences that could be imposed and differentiated between exceptional and ordinary offenders. The Penal Affairs Group believes that the effect of the scheme of the advisory council, if introduced, would have only a marginal impact on the prison population. This is why we would rather go further and have a new maximum for ordinary offences fixed at a point below existing sentences in practice and have a maximum, which the advisory council did not suggest, for so-called exceptional offences.

The Select Committee on Home Affairs has endorsed both the view of the advisory council and that of the Parliamentary all-party penal affairs group, signatories to the Select Committee's report on the prison service in 1981 included, amongst others the hon. It went on to say

"Such legislation should now be prepared, in full consultation with interested parties, since we would not wish to see such a measure brought before Parliament as a hurried response to a heightening of the penal crisis."

That is a quote from paragraph 87.

I must stress that legislation to reduce maximum penalties on the lines of that proposed by my hon. Friend in this amendment is not in itself an alternative to an early, supervised release of certain offenders, first, because the maximum penalties are not currently imposed anyway, so enacting new maxima would not in itself necessarily reduce the sentences actually imposed in practice nor reduce the prison population. Even if they did, the effect upon the prison population would take some considerable time to work through. There would be a considerable time lag between the imposition of any new maximum penalties and the effect on the prison population, whereas legislation for an early release scheme would have a substantial and immediate effect.

However, for the reasons that I have outlined, I believe that a reduction in maximum penalties both for young and adult offenders is desirable in its own right. Therefore I support these reasonable and sensible amendments.
