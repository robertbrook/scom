Amendment No. 217 makes two technical changes to the method of determining the percentage by which preserved pension rights must be revalued. Before the enactment of the Social Security Act 1985 there was no obligation on pension schemes to revalue the pension rights of those who left their employment before the scheme's normal pension age. Inevitably, the value of these rights would reduce over what could be 20 or 30 years between the date when the member left his employment and when the pension became payable. Schemes now have to revalue rights occurring from 1 January 1986 in line with price increases subject to a specified maximum which was intended to be 5 per cent per annum compound. This maximum figure was imposed to ensure that pension schemes which might JPEG 993 have no new members and relate to an employment which had ceased trading would be able to be adequately funded to meet their new statutory liability.

The amendment makes two changes. First, it brings the basis for determining the revaluation percentage specified by the Secretary of State by order into line with uprating order for state benefits. Technically, if a reputable body currently provided the Secretary of State with figures for the rate of price increases which were higher or lower than published figures, he might be obliged to use them. It could be in the interest of the scheme's trustees for one set of figures to be used and in the interest of scheme members for another set to be used. Either group could take proceedings if its set of figures were not used. The first amendment enables the Secretary of State to choose the increase in the general level of prices

'estimated in such manner as he thinks fit.'

This wording is identical to that for state benefit upratings.

The second change relates to the determination of the maximum cut-off point. The orginal wording was defective, resulting in a revaluation for one year in the 1988 order of 5.7 per cent. The defect resulted from the need to consider revaluations over a number of overlapping periods. The amendment will reinstate the 5 per cent maximum for future years and will ensure that schemes will not be faced with an obligation for which they are not funded.

Clause 21
ESTABLISHMENT AND FUNCTIONS OF WAR PENSIONS COMMITTEES

Mr. Bill Michie
(Sheffield, Heeley)
: I beg to move amendment No. 319, in page 16, line 21, at end insert—

"(2A) A committee may refer to a social security appeal tribunal any question arising from a complaint made to them regarding entitlement to or the amount of a war pension if in their opinion it would be appropriate for such a tribunal to determine that question, and the decision of the tribunal shall be binding on the complainant and the Secretary of State.".

998 The amendment would enable a war pension committee to refer a question arising from a war pension case to a social security appeals tribunal, where it is considered appropriate to do so.

At present, war pensions are governed by legislation separate from the main social security legislation. They are also administered separately in a way that, in terms of sympathy and understanding of the pensioner's point of view, is often more civilised than the treatment that many social security claimants receive.

War pensions committees are an important part of the administrative machinery. Among other functions, they provide a channel for complaints about decisions made by the Department on entitlement to pensions. If a committee is satisfied that a complaint is justified, or needs further investigation, it can make representations to the Department. This civilised, informal procedure does not always result in justice being done. In some cases, access to an independent tribunal is desirable.

I shall give the example of a case with which I have been dealing for many years. Mrs. Orme is a constituent of mine. Her husband died in November 1976 and she was awarded a war widow's pension in November 1986, backdated to June 1986. She asked for it to be backdated to the time of her husband's death in 1976, but that was refused.

Mr. Orme had been a prisoner of war in Japan during the second world war. He applied for a war pension on two occasions but it was refused, even though he was granted a full pension during his stay in Mossley Hill hospital in Liverpool for three months during 1956. However, that ceased when he was discharged.

In a letter to me dated 20 August 1987, Lord Skel-mersdale, Under-Secretary of State for Health and Social Security wrote:

"The authority under which the award is made provides that payment shall not normally be made for any period before the date of the successful application or appeal. The Secretary of State does have discretion to vary this but only in exceptional circumstances."

As I pointed out in my letter of 18 January 1988 to Lord Skelmersdale, schedule 3 to article 65 of the Naval, Military and Air Forces Etc. (Disablement and Death) Service Pensions Order 1983 provides that if the pension is not claimed within three or, in some cases, six months of the husband's death, it is payable from the date of the claim

"Except in so far as the Secretary of State may otherwise direct with respect to any particular case or class of case".

There is no mention of "exceptional circumstances" in schedule 3; the Secretary of State has unfettered discretion and there is no justification for limiting its exercise in that way. However, in his reply Lord Skelmersdale simply repeated his previous assertion that,

"the Secretary of State can only exercise his discretion to backdate claims if it can be shown that there were exceptional circumstances which prevented a claim being made earlier."

As it happens, Mrs. Orme's case was exceptional, but that is beside the point and I do not accept that those are valid grounds for refusing to backdate her pension.

Although there may be good reasons for not backdating some claims, for instance, claims for disable- JPEG 999 ment pensions that will depend on medical evidence that may be difficult to obtain, the problem does not arise in the case of a widow's pension. Once the cause of the husband's death has been established, it does not change over time. As it is accepted that Mrs. Orme is now entitled to a war widow's pension, there can be no doubt that she has been entitled to receive that pension continuously since her husband's death. The DHSS however, seems to have applied the same criteria to her request for backdating as it would in the case of a disablement pensions claim involving medical questions. That is unreasonable and unjust.

Schedule 3 to the order relating to service pensions gives the Secretary of State power to backdate payment in

"any particular case or class of case".

On receiving a request for backdating, therefore, he must consider whether the case falls within a class of cases in respect of which he had made a direction. If it does not, he must also consider whether he should make a direction. Lord Skelmersdale's letters to me showed that, in Mrs. Orme's case, the only question considered was whether it fell within a class of cases

"where there has been error or misdirection by a Government Department or where the claimant was prevented from acting sooner by illness or other good cause"—

in other words, whether it was covered by a general rule. As the circumstances were extremely unusual, it was essential to consider whether, apart from any such rule, Mrs. Orme's claim should be backdated. The Secretary of State failed to consider that question.

According to Lord Skelmersdale's letter of 5 October 1987, the DHSS refused Mrs. Orme's request for backdating on the grounds that

"failure to associate death with service has never been regarded as good cause for delay in claiming a war widow's pension".

In his letter dated 22 February 1988, Lord Skelmers-dale went further. He claimed that the only reason for exercising discretion was

"where the claimant was unable to claim earlier due to ill-health, or where he or she was misled by the Department".

That implied that no other reason would be accepted as good cause. The letter continued:

"This is in line with Social Security Commissioners' rulings … It is not accepted, for war pension or any other benefit, that lack of knowledge is good reason for not making a claim."

The reference to the commissioners' rulings and to "any other benefit" show that the intention is to interpret "good cause" in the same way as it is interpreted for the purposes of late claims for social security benefits. -It is an arbitrary and unnecessary restriction of the Secretary of State's discretion. Moreover, the interpretation placed on "good cause" is not in line with the commissioners' rulings. In my letter of 14 March 1988 to Lord Skelmersdale, I quoted from "Non-Means Tested Benefits: The Legislation", by Bonner et al:

"The Commissioners have long recognised that there are a wide variety of circumstances in which it would be unreasonable to expect a person to make enquiries about entitlement."

In the circumstances of Mr. Orme's death, it was clearly unreasonable to expect Mrs. Orme to make such inquiries.

1000 The problem is that, having decided to apply the legalistic test of "good cause", imported from social security law, the DSS refused to allow the interpretation of that test in Mrs. Orme's case to be referred to the chief adjudication officer for his advice. Lord Skelmersdale first denied that such a procedure was even possible in a letter dated 28 April 1988.
