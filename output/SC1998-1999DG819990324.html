Traffic Areas (Reorganisation) (Wales) Order 1999

4.30 pm

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Ms Glenda Jackson)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Traffic Areas (Reorganisation) (Wales) Order 1999.

The order's purpose is to create a new Welsh traffic area to replace existing arrangements, under which north Wales is part of the north-western traffic area and south Wales is a separate traffic area. It may help if I explain the reason for the change, who it will affect, and how.

For the purposes of administration of goods and public services vehicle licensing systems and the registration of scheduled bus services outside London, Great Britain is divided into eight traffic areas. Licences are granted by traffic commissioners appointed to those areas.

There have been a number of reviews into the licensing system and its administration. In 1991, the number of traffic areas was reduced from 11 to eight. In 1995, an efficiency scrutiny recommended that the number be reduced to six. The previous Government decided to close the traffic area offices in Cardiff and Manchester as a cost-cutting measure and to restructure the traffic area boundaries. They decided that the south Wales traffic area would be administered from Birmingham, and the north-west traffic area from Leeds. The traffic commissioner for the north-eastern traffic area was also appointed to the north-west traffic area.

The efficiency scrutiny stressed the need for modern information systems. In 1996, it was announced that restructuring of traffic area boundaries would be suspended while further work was done on computer systems.

It was clear when we took office that major investment would be needed to replace and modernise the obsolete information system and working practices. The work will take some time to complete; restructuring the boundaries could be some years away. Therefore, we decided to introduce proposals for a traffic area for Wales in advance of any wider restructuring.

The changes will affect operators, who will have to obtain a licence. A licence will be issued for operators in each traffic area in which they have an operating centre.

Mr. Nigel Evans
(Ribble Valley)
: The Minister mentioned new information technology. Can she say how money is being invested in it, from whose budget the money will come and when it will be fully operational?

4

Ms Jackson
: A study is taking place into the issues that the hon. Gentleman raises. The desired outcome is that such systems should offer flexibility. They will inherently provide a much better service, not only to operators but to traffic commissioners when they issue licences. I am unable to give figures now, but I shall inquire into the detail and write to the hon. Gentleman and all members of the Committee when I have the information.

I was saying that operators must have a licence for each traffic area in which they have an operating centre. They must specify centres and state how many vehicles will be kept at each.

They must also inform the Traffic Commissioner of registration marks and changes of vehicle.

The order makes detailed provision for dealing with licences affected by the boundary changes, to ensure a smooth transition and continuity of traffic commissioner functions and responsibilities. Broadly, the order provides that, on reorganisation, a former south Wales traffic area licence becomes a Welsh traffic area licence; a north-western traffic area licence, which specifies only operating centres in Wales, becomes a Welsh traffic area licence; and a north-western traffic area licence, which specifies operating centres in both England and Wales will be split, in consultation with the licence holder, to create a Welsh traffic area licence for the operating centre and vehicles based in Wales and an amended north-western traffic area licence for the operating centres and vehicles based in England. Where, as a consequence, an operator has two Welsh traffic area licences, those will be amalgamated in due course—we estimate within five years.

A consultation exercise was carried out with the industries concerned and a wide range of other interested parties in England and Wales. Most operators will be unaffected because they hold only one licence, which will fall within the new boundary.

Mr. Allan Rogers
(Rhondda)
: Under the new arrangements, if the traffic commissioner for the Welsh traffic area is the person who immediately before the reorganisation was traffic commissioner for south Wales, will road haulage operators in north Wales have to refer to Cardiff with respect to their licences?

Ms Jackson
: No. I shall be touching on that issue later in my remarks, but the administration will, as now, operate from the office in Birmingham.

Mr. Rogers
: My hon. Friend says that the administrative headquarters will be in Birmingham. Is that for North and south Wales?

Ms Jackson
: As I am sure my hon. Friend is aware, we are discussing an order that will make one traffic area for Wales, combining north and south Wales. At the moment, the separate north Wales traffic area is administered from the north-western traffic area, namely the office in Leeds. The south Wales traffic area is, I believe, administered from the office in Birmingham. The new all-Wales traffic area will be administered from the office in Birmingham.

JPEG 5 The effect on staffing in the traffic area offices will be minor—equivalent to two posts.

Mr. Evans
: Does the Minister not detect some irony in the fact that she is announcing streamlining to put matters under one-Wales auspices, but that the headquarters will be in Birmingham? What would she say to the suggestion that the headquarters for the all-Wales area should be based in Wales?

Ms Jackson
: I think that that issue will be examined after the study on the modernisation of the computer system to which I referred. What I find truly ironic is that the hon. Member for Ribble Valley (Mr. Evans) should have asked that question, in the light of the fact that it was a previous Conservative Administration who split up Wales in that respect.

The traffic commissioner for the west midlands and south Wales traffic areas will retain responsibility for the west midlands traffic area. His responsibilities for south Wales will be extended to all of Wales. If our proposal for a Welsh traffic area is approved, it is, as I have said, intended that it should be run from Birmingham, which already deals with the larger number of operators based in Wales. However, when a traffic commissioner decides to hold a public inquiry, for example, it will always be held locally—either by the commissioner or his deputies. One of the deputies for the Wales traffic area is a Welsh speaker.

The order amends the Traffic Areas (Reorganisation) Order 1990 by defining the limits of the north-western traffic area to exclude the former counties of Clwyd and Gwynedd and to include their areas in the new all-Wales traffic area. I commend the order to the Committee.

Ms Glenda Jackson
: I will briefly touch on some of the issues raised by hon. Members on both sides of the Committee. The hon. Member for Meriden spoke about restructuring. The hon. Member for Ceredigion (Mr. Dafis) and my hon. Friend the Member for Ogmore (Sir R. Powell) spoke about the siting of the office of the new Wales traffic area in Wales. As I have already pointed out, the Department is engaged in a wide-ranging study of the modernisation of the computer systems. It is hoped that these systems will ensure a much more efficient service to the operators and, indeed, from the traffic commissioner. Once that study is completed and a wider consideration of the system to be used has begun, we will examine the possibility of siting the office for the Welsh traffic area in Wales.

Concerns were also expressed about the additional burden that might be placed on hauliers by the creation of a Welsh traffic area, and the hon. Members for Meriden and for Brecon and Radnorshire (Mr. Livsey) referred to the fuel duty escalator. That was introduced by the previous Administration and was welcomed by this Government as a major contribution to ensuring that we meet our national and international commitments to reduce the amount of pollution in the air with the concomitant deleterious effect from greenhouse gases and global warming.

However, Opposition Members do the haulage industry no favours by persisting in the fallacious statement that, because costs in the United Kingdom are high, our 10 hauliers face undue burdens of competition. A report issued yesterday by KPMG shows that overall costs fore the haulage industry in the UK are the second lowest in Europe. Only Austria has lower costs, if a haulage company in this county were to set up in France, Belgium or the Netherlands, the overall costs would be more than £500,000 in France, more than £600,00 in the Netherlands and about £800,000 in Belgium.

Mrs. Spelman
: It depends which category of truck we are dealing with. Will the Minsiter give serious consideration to a transitional period while the study is being conducted for those who currently operate with one licence and will now have to have two?

Ms Jackson
: I had intended to come on to licences in response to a question from hon. Members.

I was referring to the fuel duty escalator. It is fallacious to say that costs in this country are overburdensome to our haulage industry and make it difficult for them to compete with companies on the mainland of Europe. That is disproven by the fact that haulage companies from the mainland are moving to the United Kingdom to gain the benefits of our lower corporation tax and social costs.

The hon. Members for Brecon and Radnorshire and for Meriden referred to restructuring and the hon. Gentleman mentioned inconvenience. Restructuring took place in 1995. In a cost-cutting exercise, the previous Administration split Wales into the south Wales and north Wales traffic areas. Northern Wales was subsumed into the north-west traffic area and was administered from Leeds. The south Wales traffic area was administered from Birmingham. We are creating, if the Committee agrees, a Wales traffic area. The two areas will be combined and the administration will be from one office, in Birmingham.

There will be no need to travel for routine traffic area business. Public inquiries to which operators are called, either for disciplinary reasons or to hear objections from residents to the granting of a licence, are held in Wales. Since the offices in Cardiff and Manchester were closed, there has been a total of 106 public inquiries involving operators and drivers in Wales, 97 of which were held in Cardiff, three in Manchester, three in Llandudno, two in Wrexham and one in Birmingham.

The hon. Member for Meriden was concerned about the fees and the number of operators who would be affected. About 1,707 good and 130 public service vehicle operators will have their licences transferred from the north-western traffic area to the new Welsh traffic area; 5,748 goods and 477 public service operators in south Wales will have their licences transferred to the new area, creating a combined total of 8,062 operators.

Fewer than 200 of those will need an additional licence or two Welsh traffic area licences—the latter will have to be amalgamated. The amount of vehicle fees payable by operators will not change. In cases in which an additional licence is needed, an additional licence fee will be payable in due course. The current licence fee for a five-year period is £250 for a goods vehicle operator's licence and £100 for a public service vehicle operator's licence.

I have touched on all the questions raised by Committee members, with the possible exception of that asked by my hon. Friend the Member for Rhondda. I apologise for JPEG 11 leaving him until last. My hon. Friend was anxious about the traffic commissioners, defining them as civil servants. However, the commissioners would object strongly to that definition. They belong to a statutorily independent public body and they are recruited through open competition.

My hon. Friend made an important point about haulage operators' potentially illegal activities, which was picked up by the hon. Member for Meriden. There can be no excuse for illegal activity based on the haulage industry's economic burdens and I am sure that the hon. Lady did not intend to justify such activity.

I can reassure hon. Members about the action that the Government will take with regard to operators who conduct illegal operations. Such action is additional to the measure, which will impact on such operators.

In the White Paper, "Integrated Transport Policy", we announced our intention to proceed with a detention scheme for illegally operated heavy goods vehicles. Detaining such vehicles will be the first truly effective sanction against unlicensed operators.

A practical scheme has been devised, the details of which have been discussed and agreed with industry. Enforcement of the rules on drivers' hours will be tightened by a Bill, which Earl Attlee announced in the transport debate that took place in the House of Lords on 27 January The Government will support that Bill, which is being drafted and which will shortly be introduced in the House of Lords. Our overriding priority is safety on our roads, whatever the form of transport.

12 The hon. Member for Meriden asked whether the creation of a Welsh traffic area was simply an exercise in political correctness. The previous Government gave an undertaking to create a Welsh traffic area office. This Government, however, have made that promise come true.

Mr. Rogers
: When the traffic commissioners' office was in Cardiff, there was fairly effective policing of people with heavy goods vehicle and public service vehicle licences. Some of us suspect that many firms with contracts for school buses, for example, are not up to standard. Will the Minister assure us that, under the reorganisation of traffic areas, such operators will be effectively policed?

Ms Glenda Jackson
: I can certainly reassure my hon. Friend that I will bring his concern——which is shared by other hon. Members—to the attention of the traffic commissioner.

I will touch on whether there will be concessionary fees for people who require two licences. The answer is no. The cost of the five-year licence will be £250, which is the equivalent of £50 a year for a heavy goods vehicle. Having a transitional period would increase costs to operators because there would be administrative complications. I commend the order to the Committee.

Question put and agreed to.
