Draft Race Relations (Northern Ireland) Order 1997

10.30 am

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Michael Ancram)
: I beg to move

That the Committee has considered the draft Race Relations (Northern Ireland) Order 1997.

The purpose of the order is to apply to persons belonging to ethnic groups who live and work in Northern Ireland similar protection to that which already applies under the Race Relations Act 1976 to those of us who live and work in the rest of the United Kingdom.

The absence of race relations has not been due to any lack of will on the part of successive Governments. We must acknowledge, however, that over the past few years, the political debate in Northern Ireland has, for obvious reasons, centred more on fair employment issues between the two communities. There was also the matter of proportionality. In recent years there has been a significant increase in the number of people in ethnic minority groups. However, the total number is approximately 0.5 per cent. of the population. It was only in the late 1980s that a public debate on the need for such legislation began. In 1995, the Secretary of State gave a commitment to introduce race relations legislation to Northern Ireland as soon as possible. This debate is evidence of that commitment.

I will describe the main provisions of the order. With the exception of the inclusion of the Irish traveller community, which has been defined as an ethnic group for the purpose of this legislation, the order follows the same general lines as the Race Relations Act 1976.

The order defines both direct and indirect discrimination and makes them unlawful. It also extends the definition of discrimination to cover the victimisation of an individual who has, for example, exerted his or her rights under the order. Important in the Northern Ireland context is the inclusion of the definition of "racial grounds" and "racial group". That has been necessary to distinguish between discrimination on the grounds of race and that on the grounds of religious belief or political opinion. In Northern Ireland, the latter is covered by the provision of the fair employment legislation, which is another reason for the delay in introducing this legislation.

Legal redress under the order lies, in employment cases, with the industrial tribunals and, in other cases, with the county courts. In line with existing anti-discrimination legislation, there is no upper limit to the level of compensation that can be awarded to a victim of racial discrimination.

The order places a duty on district councils in Northern Ireland to carry out their various functions with due regard to the need to eliminate unlawful racial discrimination and 2 to promote equality of opportunity between persons of different racial groups. In the equivalent provision of the Race Relations Act 1976, that duty is placed on local authorities, which have wider responsibilities for areas such as education, housing and roads. In Northern Ireland, central Government and public body functions which cover those areas are subject to policy appraisal and fair treatment guidelines, which ensure that issues of equality and equity are central to all spheres of governmental activity.

The order establishes a commission for racial equality for Northern Ireland to help enforce the legislation. It will also promote equality of opportunity and good relations between people of different racial groups generally. The intention is to appoint a minimum of five commissioners, one of whom will be the chairperson. The commission will advise the Government on the working of the legislation and will also be a principal source of information and advice for the public. Individuals who feel that they have suffered racial discrimination can seek assistance from the commission to obtain redress through the courts.

The introduction of race relations legislation in Northern Ireland serves to demonstrate the United Kingdom's commitment to human rights for all its citizens, and I commend the draft order to the Committee.
