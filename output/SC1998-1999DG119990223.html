Draft National Assembly for Wales (Disqualification) Order 1999

4.30 pm

The Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Alun Michael)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft National Assembly for Wales (Disqualification) Order 1999.

The Chairman
: With this we may also consider the draft National Assembly for Wales (Representation of the People) Order 1999.

Mr. Michael
: I shall speak to both orders.

My starting point for the National Assembly for Wales (Disqualification) Order is the aim to keep disqualification to a minimum. The original proposals would have been unfair to public employees, preventing national health service staff and others from standing for the Assembly and disfranchising 22 per cent, of the work force in Wales. I disagree fundamentally with that approach, and the draft order narrows down the field to the necessary minimum.

Section 12 of the Government of Wales Act 1998 identifies directly a number of persons and classes of person who may not also be members of the Assembly. In addition, it provides for an Order in Council to designate further offices and employments, the holders of which would also be disqualified from becoming members of the Assembly. The draft of such an order is now before the Committee.

There are no direct parallels to the National Assembly in the United Kingdom. One option would have been to replicate the extensive listing under the House of Commons Disqualification Act 1975. That would not have been an appropriate course of action. The Assembly will have very different powers and responsibilities from the House of Commons.

Mr. Allan Rogers
(Rhondda)
: May I congratulate my right hon. Friend on his great victory on Saturday? I am not sure to what he refers in the document before us. I wonder whether he could help me, as I am a little lost by his reference to section 12.

Mr. Michael
: There are two documents before us. I shall refer to the larger document second. The first lists the bodies whose members, and in some cases employees, would be disqualified from membership of the Assembly.

I have explained that the bodies listed are appropriate because the Assembly will have very different powers and responsibilities from the House of Commons. The justification for disqualifying people from election must be based on consideration 4 of whether their responsibilities as elected representatives are incompatible with their other responsibilities.

A consultation paper was issued in August 1998, with comments sought by 2 October. Its starting point was to identify executive bodies that would have a line of accountability to the Assembly, direct or otherwise. There are a variety of such bodies and I would be happy to go into detail, but I think that it is clear from the order what is intended.

The second key consideration is to preserve the impartiality of bodies that have a quasi-judicial function. We intend that the Assembly will be an inclusive institution, where the conduct of its business will be carried out in a far more transparent manner. That has important implications for the broadcasting interests and the coverage that they can be expected to provide across the whole range of the Assembly's functions. For that reason, the consultation paper proposed that broadcasting should be brought within the scope of the order's disqualification provisions.

I have set out clearly the basic principles on which the list has been constructed. Some 50 responses were received and they have been carefully considered. It was proposed that there should be universal disqualification for the staff of the bodies suggested as coming within the scope of the order. However, that proposal attracted considerable criticism and I agreed strongly that it would have been far too draconian. The staff of an organisation should not necessarily be placed under the same limitations as board members. The order therefore applies only to a limited restriction to certain classes of employee, mainly civil servants, preventing them from becoming Members of the Assembly.

Citizens should be debarred from political activity only when there is good reason. We should be aware that we are placing a limit on their democratic rights, and should restrict exclusions to the necessary minimum. That is the principle that I have sought to apply, and I believe that several members of the Committee will find that to be in accord with the views that they expressed in response to the original document.

Mr. Rogers
: Some have suggested that the National Assembly will be a glorified county council in that it will have administrative functions but no legislative or financial powers. Under the rather vicious local government legislation brought in by Lady Thatcher in the mid-1980s, members of staff of other bodies in Wales that spend huge sums of money, such as county councils, are not allowed to stand for election. Why will there be a difference between the staff of bodies that will be funded by the National Assembly and the people who work for local government?

Mr. Michael
: I think that my hon. Friend is referring to the position of people who stand at local government elections vis-a-vis the levels of responsibility above which political activity is debarred. That issue is different from the one with which we are dealing. We are considering whether members of boards, and also members of staff of JPEG 5 particular organisations, should be debarred from becoming Members of the Assembly. We are not discussing the staff of the Assembly; as civil servants, they will be covered directly. We are talking about the saff of the vast range of bodies, such as NHS trusts and health authorities, that will have a relationship with, and receive finances from, the Assembly.

The question is where we draw the line. Should some people be barrred from becoming Members of the Assembly because that would not be consistent with their role? When will we require someone to resign a role that is inconsistent with becoming a Member of the Assembly? Those issues arise normally—when people become Members of Parliament, for instance. As I said, I have applied the principle of using disqualifications when they are necessary because of a clear danger of conflict of interest. In accordance with that principle, I have narrowed down the range of individuals who will be covered by the disqualification order.

Mr. Rogers
: I fully understand the point made by my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Wales, but I am afraid that he does not understand mine. The chief executives of the Sports Council for Wales and the Arts Council for Wales, for example, are not members of the board but paid members of staff, and so might have direct and vested interests in any "ferret-in-the-sack" political arguments between bodies over the allocation of moneys. It is difficult to understand why an executive employee will not be disqualified when someone in a similar position in local government certainly would be.

Mr. Michael
: My hon. Friend misunderstands the relationship between the Assembly and the bodies that will receive finance from it, which is akin to the current relationship between Parliament and those bodies. In fact, that is exactly the relationship, because bodies that will be funded by the Assembly are currently funded by the Welsh Office. Therefore, parliamentary election ought to form the basis for comparison.

The order is relatively straightforward in its impact, as a result of extensive consultation since the original draft's publication last August.

Part I lists those 28 bodies in which all members are disqualified from membership of the Assembly. Part II identifies a further 43 bodies and offices, the members or post-holders of which cannot be members of the Assembly. In part II, a distinction has been made for employees who by virtue of their employment are ex-officio members of the board. I do not consider that it would be appropriate to apply disqualification in those circumstances. That relates to one of the examples that my hon. Friend the Member for Rhondda (Mr. Rogers) mentioned. The more obvious examples relate to senior managers in NHS bodies in Wales.

Many of the Welsh public sector bodies listed in part I and part II will have a direct link with Assembly and the funding dependency will be clear. Others operate in a quasi-judicial manner. On the grounds of preserving impartiality, members of the Audit Commission and the Commission for Local Administration in Wales will be disqualified, as will the health service 6 commissioner for Wales and the commissioner or assistant commissioner of the boundary commission for Wales.

There is also a range of social security bodies to which disqualification will apply. Although their remits fall outside the functions to be exercised by the Assembly, those bodies have a duty to decide appeals in Wales on matters that could directly or indirectly affect the Assembly's activities or expenditure or affect Assembly Members directly. For example, the bodies could deal with child support maintenance appeals or decisions that influence the respective responsibilities of local authority social services departments and the Department of Social Security.

The social security commissioner and the child support commissioner, who are analogous to High Court judges, are disqualified by the provisions of section 12 of the Government of Wales Act, which applies the House of Commons Disqualification Act. It is consistent to ensure that disqualification from Assembly membership is extended to the social security appeal and tribunal structure that is responsible for precedent-setting decisions, and has a deciding influence over the way in which a particular tribunal carries out its business. Similar considerations apply to other bodies listed in the order. There is a clear risk of actual or potential conflicts of interest and compromising impartiality if members of pension bodies are able to sit in the Assembly.

As was stated in the consultation document, I have decided that disqualification should apply also to board members of the training and enterprise councils in Wales and the career service companies in Wales, and to the board of the Welsh European programme executive. That will be given effect through the contractual relationship that exists between the Welsh Office and the training and enterprise councils and career companies. For the Welsh European programme executive, I will enter into an agreement with the company to preclude Assembly members from serving on its board.

The rules that will apply to the conduct of the elections to the Assembly will require all candidates, at the stage when consent is given to the formal nomination of their candidature, to confirm that they are not disqualified from becoming a member of the Assembly. That follows the same rules that apply to elections to the House of Commons. The elections to the Assembly will take place on 6 May, and the final date for the submission of nomination papers will be 8 April. Where people currently hold disqualifying posts or employment, the order will require them to resign before they give consent to their candidature as part of the formal nomination procedure.

Where a member of a public body has resigned that membership to stand for election to the Assembly and is unsuccessful, there is a discretion to reinstate that member to the body concerned.

Having dealt with that at greater length than I anticipated would be necessary, although I hope that I have satisfied hon. Members, I turn to the order that deals with elections—the National Assembly for Wales (Representation of the People) Order. I want to make it clear that the version of the order that we are considering today is that which was laid on 22 February, not the version laid on 15 February, which has been withdrawn. In the original version, a clerical JPEG 11 error deleted almost half schedule 5(53), making it defective. We have corrected the error and amended the drafting slightly to clarify the rules under which an Assembly regional ballot paper may be accepted. I shall explain that change later, and I apologise to hon. Members for any inconvenience that replacement may have caused.

The first elections to the Assembly will be on 6 May 1999. Section 11(1) of the Government of Wales Act 1998 allows the Secretary of State by order to make provision for the conduct of Assembly elections, including setting limits to election expenses of candidates and registered political parties and procedures for election petitions. The draft order draws heavily on provisions from the Representation of the People Acts 1983 and 1985 and includes material from statutory instruments made under those Acts and other legislation on the same basis. We have tried to make the rules consistent. We have also had to take account of two other developments which affect elections in the UK—the Registration of Political Parties Act 1998 and the recommendations of the Neill committee on the funding of political parties.

The order has been the subject of detailed discussion and consultation from the very start. To provide expert input, in December 1997, my predecessor set up an elections working group to advise my officials on the contents of the order. The working group comprised senior returning officers and elections administrators. The group members have reported regularly to their colleagues in Wales and fed back their views. We also issued a draft of the order to political parties, returning officers and a host of other organisations, such as the Wales TUC, and the CBI Wales. A copy was placed on the Welsh Office website. Therefore, there has been considerable consultation on the detail of the order.

Most of the order does no more than apply provisions that are equivalent to those in the rules for parliamentary or, occasionally, local government elections, with modifications because of the two-vote system. I shall explain where and why there are significant departures.

Articles 1 to 13 deal mainly with voting, registration, polling places and absent voting. Polling places for Assembly elections will be the same as those for parliamentary elections and the absent vote criteria are the same as those for local government elections, as the electorate is the same.

Mr. Michael

Articles 23 to 31 equate to rules in the Representation of the People Act 1983 for parliamentary and local government elections. Articles 32 to 36 define the arrangements for the appointment of election agents and sub-agents. They are based on standard provisions of the 1983 Act. There will be only one election agent for each party list.

Articles 37 to 65 deal with election expenses. Their provisions draw on the provisions of the 1983 Act that apply to candidates at parliamentary elections and on the recommendations that were submitted in the autumn of 1998 by the Neill committee on standards in public life in its report on the funding of political parties.

We have identified expenses limits at the constituency level and above. The proposed limits are in articles 46 to 48. We have followed without exception the Neill committee recommendations. For constituency elections, the same limits apply as to elections to the House, except that the figures have been uprated in line with inflation. Independent candidates at regional elections will have limits equivalent to the sum of the limits in all the constituencies in the electoral region. There will be no regional limit for registered political parties, but there will be a national limit of £600,000 for such parties at a general election.

Expenses incurred in the election campaign of a constituency candidate of a registered political party will not count towards the national total. In effect, expenses on a constituency campaign will be ring-fenced. The national expenses total for a party will be made up of all expenses incurred by that party that are not specific to an individual constituency campaign. The national total will include expenses on the regional campaigns and any expenditure of a general nature.

Article 66 makes provision for free mail shots. We have provided for one free mail shot per constituency, one for each independent candidate and one for each party list at a regional election. Suggestions were made, and Plaid Cymru specifically asked for parties standing at constituency and regional elections to be able to send a single leaflet to electors presenting both their list candidates and their constituency candidates in that region. That is provided for in the order, and the Royal Mail is amending its regulations accordingly.

Article 67 deals with broadcasting. Articles 68 to 158 deal with election meetings, disturbances, bribery, treating, election petitions and a range of miscellaneous provisions relating to forms and notices, and the relaxation of planning rules for Assembly election advertisements.

I am happy to respond to any points of detail, but I shall concentrate on schedule 5, which lays down the main body of rules for the conduct of an Assembly election. The provisions are based largely on the parliamentary election rules of 1983.
