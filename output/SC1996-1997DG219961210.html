Draft Criminal Justice (Northern Ireland) Order 1997

4.30 pm

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Sir John Wheeler)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the draft Criminal Justice (Northern Ireland) Order 1997.

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to consider the draft Criminal Justice (Northern Ireland Consequential Amendments) Order 1997.

Sir John Wheeler
: Copies of the orders were laid before the House on 20 November 1996.

The draft order is a substantial and wide-ranging measure that will update the criminal law in Northern Ireland in several important and diverse areas. It will implement proposals flowing from the 1993 discussion paper "Crime and the Community", and introduce a series of other provisions that will bring the law in Northern Ireland broadly into line with more recent changes in England and Wales. For ease of reference by practitioners and courts, the order will also restate existing legislation on deferment of sentence, probation, conditional and absolute discharge and community service orders.

It will be more helpful if I concentrate my remarks on those areas of greatest significance. I will, of course, assist the Committee with any other points that hon. Members may raise.

The introduction of a new statutory sentencing framework for financial, community and custodial punishments must be regarded as being of particular importance. While the Criminal Justice Act 1991 established such a framework in England and Wales, sentencers in Northern Ireland continue to rely on guidance from the Northern Ireland Court of Appeal. The new framework will, quite rightly, emphasise the seriousness of the offence.

The enhanced range of community penalties will offer the courts a more varied but no less challenging range of options for dealing with offenders within the community. Before imposing a community or custodial sentence, the court must consider the seriousness of the offence, or the combination of the offence and one or more associated offences.

Also, before deciding whether to impose a custodial sentence for a violent or sexual offence, a court must consider the need to protect the public from serious harm from the offender. The new framework will help to ensure fairness, consistency and clarity in sentencing decisions in the Northern Ireland courts.

4 In Northern Ireland, unlike England and Wales, there is at present no general system of post-release supervision of offenders. I turn briefly to two changes that the draft order will make in this area. First, a new sentence will be introduced for offences that are serious enough to merit imprisonment for one year or more. In those circumstances, the custody probation order will, with the consent of the offender, involve a period of custody followed by a period of supervision under probation of between one and three years. Secondly—I know that this will be particularly welcomed in Northern Ireland—the courts will be given the option of being able to require those convicted of sexual offences to be supervised on licence from the date of their release from prison until the expiry of the pronounced sentence.

I was most grateful to all those who responded to the consultation process on the draft order. After full consideration of all the points raised, I decided to make a small number of additions and minor changes to the published draft, and it would be appropriate to draw those to the attention of the Committee.

The draft order now contains three new provisions that will bring Northern Ireland further into line with Great Britain in matters relating to knives and other dangerous weapons. They will prohibit the sale of knives to children under the age of 16—I am particularly grateful to the hon. and learned Member for North Down (Mr. McCartney) for his views on the issue—and ban the manufacture and sale of flick and gravity knives. They will also make arrestable the offence of carrying an article with a blade or point on school premises. I have also made a small number of amendments to the published draft.

To ensure that pre-sentence reports are sought and obtained when necessary, I have decided that a court should give reasons why it is of the opinion that a pre-sentence report is not required.

In schedule 1 to the draft order, which provides for additional requirements for probation orders, I have removed the provision which disapplies the normal 60-day limit in relation to activities and attendance at a day centre where an offender has been convicted of a sexual offence. I am satisfied that the other additional requirements will provide the courts with an adequate range of options.

Some of those who responded during consultation were concerned about what would happen if an offender breached the probation element of a custody probation order. In such circumstances, the court would be able to revoke the order and deal with the offender as if he had just been convicted of the original offence. As the offender will have already served part of his sentence in custody, I agree that that would be unduly harsh. I have therefore amended the provisions to ensure that in cases of breach the court will be able to take into account the extent to which the sentence has already been satisfied by the time spent in custody.

In relation to the release of sex offenders on licence, concern was expressed about the extent of the powers given to the Secretary of State to revoke licences and recall offenders without recourse to any judicial process. I fully understand that concern and now JPEG 5 intend therefore that failure to comply with the conditions of a licence will be dealt with by the courts, which may impose a fine of £1,000 or suspend the licence and return the offender to prison for up to six months. Should the offender be convicted of a further offence during the licence period, the court will be able to return him to prison for all or part of that period.

The order as published did not apply the provisions on fines and financial circumstances to those aged under 17. The original intention had been to extend those provisions in forthcoming juvenile justice legislation, but on reflection I feel that it would be more logical for the draft order to include all age groups.

A number of consequential amendments to the Criminal Justice Act 1991 extend to the whole of the United Kingdom, and it is therefore necessary, by virtue of section 38(2) of the Northern Ireland Constitution Act 1973, to include them in the Criminal Justice (Northern Ireland Consequential Amendments) Order 1997 which was laid with the main order.

In conclusion, I believe that the orders contain a balanced package of measures which will make a welcome contribution towards enhancing the effectiveness and efficiency of the criminal justice system in Northen Ireland. I commend them to the Committee.

Article 53 sets out in clear, specific terms the description of a number of knives, such as flick knives or gravity knives. Despite the careful wording, this provision still seems rather general.

The hon. Gentleman asked whether I would recognise a combat knife if I saw one. Whether anyone in this Committee has been in armed combat with a knife, I do not know—I doubt it. If we asked members of the armed forces in this country or, indeed, in any other modern society, what knife they would use in armed combat, I suspect that they would not choose what is normally described as a combat knife, which is on sale in the shops. I have seen things there which seem to be far better equipped for cleaning up swedes, turnips and sugar beet than for combat.

An enormous amount of nonsense is talked about this subject, which appeals to the macho image that some young men have of themselves. It is in that context that the danger of any sharp implement lies. There is a shop in my constituency that sells knives. The biggest element to purchase them consists of members of the armed forces, and not necessarily from Northern Ireland.

I agree with the principle of trying to stop people buying these items and carrying them on the street. No sane person would want that. However, I run into considerable difficulty whenever I read the definitions in article 54. First, it provides that the article applies to

"any knife, knife blade or razor blade".

If my 15-year-old son goes to the chemist's for razor blades, which he needs because the hair is starting to grow on his face, the chemist who sells them to him is guilty of an offence. As the law is written here, it bears no relation to the use to which the razor blades are to 10 be put—it is an offence to sell them. I do not think that the Minister or the Government intend such innocent activity to be caught under the order. However, I suspect that under the letter of the law, such an activity will be caught, so the provision is nonsense.

The article does not say "gives" but "sells" to a person. If my son comes home and says, "Daddy, the chemist won't sell me razor blades", and I say, "Okay, son, here's one", I am not guilty of an offence, but my son has the article nevertheless. As far as I can see, the article does not even provide that it is an offence to carry razor blades down the street if I buy them from the chemist and give them to him.

Before we proceed, we need to think more carefully of the implications of the wording. What about "any axe"? There are all sorts of agricultural aids and implements. I know—I have had to use most of them for cutting hedges, and so on. There are bill hooks and various implements that go by different names in different parts of the country. They would all be very serious weapons if people wanted to use them as such. If someone went to a football match with a bill hook over his shoulder, the police would, quite properly, take a very dim view of it.

Why should it be an offence if I send my son to the local shop, which sells farm potatoes, to buy a billhook or an axe because the old one has worn out? It would be an offence for a 15-year-old boy to buy it and carry it home. Surely that is not the Government's intention with the legislation, which includes:

"any other article which has a blade or which is sharply pointed and which is made or adapted for use for causing injury to the person".

That is a huge barn door for an evil-intentioned person to get through. He could carry a screwdriver or a wood chisel. A wood chisel is not made or adapted for causing injuries to a person. However, what else would be more likely to do so? What about the Stanley knife—a perfectly legitimate piece of equipment, which any do-it-yourself addict might have. Although I am not opposed to the intention of the proposal, it is full of mines and pitfalls, which need to be explored in a different context.

I welcome the Bill of the hon. Member for Provan because it will give Members of the House the time and space to reflect on what we would like to do and how we could sensibly do it. It is not simple but extremely difficult, because there are so many perfectly legitimate uses that could be caught by the legislation as it is framed here and as it will be framed elsewhere.

I do not want to delay the Committee but I should like to say to its members—and, through Hansard, to the House in general—that we need to think through the issue and find definitions that work. Even if we defined the length of a knife blade, some people consistently and legitimately use knives with a longer blade than the normal pocket knife. The issue of sex offenders is a vast theme. I welcome the steps taken to deal with it, but it should be dealt with on a United Kingdom basis by a Bill. We should have the same laws and measures throughout the kingdom. Sex offending requires more examination that it has hitherto received.

: I anticipate that the provision will be sparingly used, for all reasons that the hon. Member for Maryhill has given.

There is no desire not to follow the spirit of the proposal, but I am sure that the hon. Lady and the other members of the Committee will agree that discretion is sometimes a valuable commodity in this type of legislation, particularly in the special circumstances of Northern-Ireland. There may be occasional cases where, peculiarly, the authorities believe that it is in the best interests of preserving life and the tranquility of a district not to give advance information. However, the hon. Lady made her point extremely well and I assure the Committee that the provision will be very sparingly used.
