The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Employment (Mr. Patrick Mclaughlin)
: I have listened carefully to the hon. Gentleman's comments. I do not, however, believe that the amendment would allow or, indeed, represent a satisfactory alternative to the fully postal ballot that we are proposing in the clause.

The amendment would provide trade unions with a choice of how to conduct voting in an industrial action ballot, and we believe that that is unacceptable. The amendment would not, however, change the provisions in a way which would reassure voters about the proper conduct of a workplace ballot, nor would it allow voters to cast their vote away from the particular pressures to which they might otherwise be subject if voting took place in the workplace.

I am sure that the hon. Gentleman would argue that a workplace ballot, supervised by the scrutineer, would be as acceptable in ensuring its proper conduct as a fully postal ballot. However, the function of an industrial action ballot scrutineer is not to supervise the ballot. His purpose is to observe and report to the union by whom he is appointed on matters specified in section 231 relating to the conduct of the ballot. The amendment would, therefore, alter the scrutineer's role in a way which has very marked and, I believe, wholly undesirable consequences.

The 1990 code of practice "Trade Union Industrial Action Ballots" and the 1987 Green Paper "Trade Unions and Their Members" referred to the possible merits of workplace industrial action ballots in certain circumstances. However, both references clearly implied that workplace voting in industrial action ballots is very much a second best option. We do not believe that it is right or proper to ask trade union members to settle for second best. That is why we are fully committed to implementing the clause as it stands. I believe that it will vastly improve trade union members' understanding of what is happening in their union and their view on how they should cast their vote, as well as enabling them so to do by a fully postal ballot. Therefore, I cannot recommend that the Committee accept the amendment.

"'226A. The trade union shall take steps to ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, that all employers whose workers are entitled to vote in the ballot have been informed that the ballot is taking place.'.".

The amendment applies to clause 15 which can be best described as the employers' charter. Once again in the Bill, the balance is wrong. We have constantly said that whenever there are two sides to a case, the Government's duty is to try and remain unbiased and fair in imposing duties and rights on each side. Once again, the bias is entirely against the trade unions, imposing on them duties but not conferring on them the rights that such duties should incur.

By throwing several stringent conditions before the trade unions, the Bill represents another attempt by the Government to try to allow employers to question the validity of the ballot and, therefore, to make the ballots and, as a consequence, industrial action far more difficult to pursue. Once again, the power of the trade unions is reduced. It is not a question of banning trade unions; even this Government would not be silly enough to do that, although there is their record at GCHQ. The Government are trying to neuter trade union power in two ways. First, they impose restriction after restriction that makes it difficult for trade unions to act. Secondly, they ensure that the unions' income is removed and that the costs are increased. That is a double whammy to undermine the trade unions' role. The clause would enable that to happen.

Why is it necessary to introduce this provision? The Minister spoke of the 1990 code of practice on balloting for industrial action which referred to good practice. That good practice is what we are considering today. The Government intend to damage the ability of the unions to take effective action. They set up one hurdle. When the unions got over that, they set up another, and so on. The Government believed that if there were ballots on industrial action, no one would vote for it. They were proved to be wrong, so they now want to impose full postal ballots. When that does not work, they will introduce other regulations to make effective action more difficult.

The hon. Member for Teignbridge (Mr. Nicholls), the Minister at the time of the 1990 code of practice, said that the law on industrial ballots was working well and that he could see no reason to change it. The Government have given no reason why a change is now necessary.

230 The clause has three subsections. Our amendment would delete most of it. The amendment deals with giving notice of a ballot. It must be a totally incompetent employer who does not realise that a ballot on industrial action is taking place which will affect him. When notice of a ballot on industrial action arrives one morning on the employer's desk and he asks his secretary whether there is any mail, to which she replies that there is going to be a ballot on industrial action, I cannot believe that he will then say, "I realised the world was flat, but I did not realise that there was going to be a ballot." That is not logical. If an employer does not know that that is happening he is not competent to be an employer.

The second part of the clause is concerned with what should be on a ballot paper. The provision would cause delay. It would also give the employer the opportunity to find reasons to delay industrial action. It is always the workers and their organisations that are wrong and the employers that are always right—every advantage should be given to the employers, but nothing should be given to the workers. We dispute that philosophy.

The most sinister part of the clause is concerned with giving information on the employees. We shall deal with that in another amendment. This amendment would remove all those requirements in the clause and would insert:

"The trade union shall take steps to ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, that all employers whose workers are entitled to vote in the ballot have been informed that the ballot is taking place."

That is being nice to the employers, on the offchance that there might be an employer who is on holiday in the Bahamas while his workers are slaving away for pittance wages and who might not know about the ballot. One or two trade unionists could fly out, at Government expense, to deliver the message. We are prepared to leave in that provision for the benefit of employers, but we want all the sinister provisions struck out.
