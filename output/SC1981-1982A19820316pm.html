Criminal Justice Bill
New Clause 14

4.30 pm

DISCRETIONARY PAROLE FOR SHORT-TERM PRISONERS

"In section 60(1) of the Criminal Justice Act 1967, the words ' twelve months' shall be omitted and the words ' six months' shall be inserted.".—[ Mr. Kilroy-Silk.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Robert Kilroy-Silk
(Ormskirk)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

This new clause, which I am moving on behalf of the parliamentary all-party Penal Affairs Group, would reduce the minimum period which has to be served before an offender can first be considered for parole from 12 months to six months. Its effect would be, firstly, to bring some short-term prisoners, who are currently ineligible for parole, into the parole scheme; and, secondly, to enable prisoners serving sentences of more than 18 months and less than three years to be considered for parole at an earlier stage in their sentences than they are at the moment.

Since its introduction in 1968, the parole scheme has shown that many sentences can be reduced in length without significant risk to the public. The scheme itself has demonstrated that a large number of offenders can quite safely be released on licence, on parole, into the community without in any way endangering the public significantly. In 1980, for example, 10,070 determinate sentence prisoners were considered for parole, of whom 5,077–50·4 per cent.—were granted parole, and the proportion of eligible prisoners who are granted parole at some point in their sentences is now 60 per cent., yet the proportion of those recalled for misbehaviour while on parole has always been relatively low. During 1980, 445 people on parole from determinate sentences were recalled to prison during their parole period and had their licences revoked. This represents 8·7 per cent, of those recommended for parole in that year. Fewer than half of these—183 out of the 445—had committed another offence while on parole; the others were recalled for breaches of their parole licence which did not involve the commission by them of an offence

Even this very low rate of re-offending while on parole licence must be seen in the light of the Parole Board's comments in its report for 1971:

"In all fixed term sentences the question before the Board is not whether a man should be released from prison—this is going to happen in any event—but when 594 is the best moment for release, and whether the risk of his re-offending is more likely to be reduced if he is placed under supervision and given statutory after-care, than if he is released at the normal time without any control over his future behaviour."

The Board expanded on this point in its report for 1977:

'Parole was introduced because it was believed that the early release on licence of selected prisoners, under the supervision of the probation service, would be of more value both to society and the individual offender than continued imprisonment. We continue to believe this to be the case. Without parole, and the support and supervision which it gives to a prisoner re-entering the community, some prisoners might drift back into circumstances which would eventually return them to a life of crime. The sanction to recall to prison reminds the prisoner that he is continuing to serve his sentence, albeit in more congenial conditions in the community, and helps to deter him from disobeying the conditions of his licence."

There is now considerable research evidence available which was summarised in the Home Office's " Review of Parole in England and Wales" published in 1981, that the combined effect of parole supervision and the threat of recall appears to reduce significantly the probability of an offender's being reconvicted.

At present, short-term prisoners—those who constitute the least danger to society—are not eligible for release on parole. This is because fixed sentence prisoners first become eligible for parole after serving a third of their sentence or 12 months, whichever is the longer. A prisoner who gains his full remission will be released after serving two-thirds of his sentence in any event; and this means in practice that parole does not apply to prisoners serving sentences of 18 months or less. This can result in great injustice, as Lord Justice Waller pointed out in an address to NACRO's Annual General Meeting held on 30th October 1980, when he said:

"The injustice is best illustrated by this example. Two men are convicted of a serious crime. They are of previous good character. One is the ringleader who gets three years, the other taking a relatively minor part gets 18 months. The ringleader gets parole and they are both released on the same day. It must appear unjust to the minor party. There is no parole available for him though, of course, if there were he would be extremely eligible."

Lowering the minimum period which must be served before release on parole, as the new clause proposes, would help to reduce, if not eliminate, the injustices referred to by, amongst others, Lord Justice Waller. It would not, of course, eliminate them completely because, wherever the line is drawn, there will be anomalies on either side of it; and it is for that reason that the parliamentary all-party Penal Affairs Group would prefer a scheme of automatic supervised release, which could be applied to prisoners serving sentences of any length, however short.

Although we shall return to that subject and debate it on another new clause, that course of action has now been ruled out by the Home Secretary and by the Minister in our previous proceedings in this Committee. Nevertheless, the lower the threshhold for parole eligibility, the less glaring will be the anomalies and the less serious will be the injustices.

New Clause 15
CONSIDERATION OF SOCIAL ENQUIRY REPORT BEFORE IMPOSITION OF FIRST CUSTODIAL SENTENCE

"A court shall not

  1. pass a sentence of imprisonment, or
  2. make a detention centre order, or
  3. pass a youth custody sentence
on a person who has not previously been sentenced to imprisonment or youth custody or detained in a detention centre, unless the court has considered a social enquiry report, that is to say a report about him and his circumstances made by a probation officer or by a social worker of a local authority social services department.".—[ Mr. Kilroy-Silk.]

Brought up, and read the First Time.

Mr. Kilroy-Silk
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a second time.

This provides that no court shall pass a first custodial sentence on an offender without first considering a social inquiry report. Like many other of the new clauses, it embodies a recommendation made by the parliamentary all-party Penal Affairs Group in our report, " Too Many Prisoners ". The purpose of a social inquiry report was first laid down in the Criminal Justice Act 1967, which stated that in accordance with the direction of a court, a probation officer preparing such a report could inquire

"into the circumstances or home surroundings of any person with a view to assisting the court in determining the most suitable method of dealing with his case."

Social inquiry reports include information about the social and domestic background of the offender which is relevant to the court's assessment of how responsible or culpable he was for the offence. They contain information about the offender and his surroundings, which is relevant to the court's consideration of how his criminal career—if one can so describe it—might be checked or thwarted. Several Home Office circulars over the years have stated that the reports should normally be based upon inquiries into the character, personality, and social and domestic background of the accused; his record at any educational, training or residential establishments; his employment prospects; and, where appropriate, his attitudes and habits as known to his most recent employer. Probation officers may also express opinions on an offender's likely response to probation and to other forms of sentences, and, if they consider it appropriate, may make specific recommendations for or against a particular course Of action.

Although section 57 of the Criminal Justice Act 1967 empowered the Home Secretary to make rules requiring courts to consider a social inquiry report before imposing a prison sentence on an offender who had not served a previous custodial sentence, no such rules have ever actually been made, and the Home Office has instead chosen to recommend JPEG 599 this practice to the courts rather than incorporate it in statutory rules. The Home Office has recommended courts, as normal practice, to consider a social inquiry report before imposing a borstal or detention centre sentence, and before imposing a prison sentence on an offender who has not previously received a prison or borstal sentence. However, information collected on a sample of offenders sentenced in 1971 showed that magistrates in fact neglected to obtain a social inquiry report on the majority of men receiving their first prison sentence, which suggests that, at the very least, the Home Secretary's advice on this point has gone unheeded.

Given the importance that everybody—probation officers, social workers, magistrates, justices' clerks, and indeed the Home Secretary himself—attaches to the importance of a first custodial sentence, and therefore to the need for the courts to have all the relevant information of the kind that is perhaps best presented in a social inquiry report before making such a custodial disposal, it is highly important that the evidence produced seems to suggest that courts are not in fact applying the recommendation made to them by successive, various Home Secretaries in numerous circulars.

I accept that the position has probably improved since the research was presented in 1971. However, the new clause would make quite sure that the practice of considering a social inquiry report is followed in practice before any custodial sentence is imposed on an offender who has not previously served one. If the recommendation or injunction is to be so important to courts, and if it appears that there is no real substantial reason of resources or administrative difficulty why a social inquiry report of this nature should not always be presented to the court before the imposition of a first custodial sentence, there seems to be no substantial reason why we cannot write this rule for the first time into legislation, and make it a statutory obligation upon the courts.

The argument for ensuring that this is done was succinctly expressed by the Justices' Clerks' Society in its evidence to the May inquiry:

"The first custodial sentence (of whatever type) can be a crucial point in an offender's life, demanding the closest scrutiny by every possible agency."

The implications of a first custodial sentence are potentially so serious, as has been acknowledged by all the commentators on the Bill, that the court should always have all the relevant information available to it before making such a grave and important decision. Moreover, a probation officer's assessment of an offender is particularly relevant in determining whether he or she might be a suitable candidate for the imposition of a probation order.

Ministers have stated repeatedly that they wish to reverse the current decline in the number of probation orders being imposed, and to see the probation order used as a full and effective alternative to custody. If that is what they really believe, the consideration of social inquiry reports in all cases where the offender is at risk of receiving a custodial sentence, particularly for the first time, would 600 be an important means of ensuring that the suitability or otherwise of an offender for a probation order disposal was brought on every single occasion forcibly to the attention of the court.

It is not just that we want to ensure that all the relevant characteristics of an offender are before the court before it determines on a custodial disposal, but also that we see the social inquiry report as one way of reversing the trend towards the decline in the use of probation orders, a trend which the Government, too, assert that they wish to reverse.

The value, in another context, of social inquiry reports as an aid to sentencing was demonstrated by Home Office Research Study No. 48, " Social Inquiry Reports: A Survey", published in 1979. This described the results of a sentencing exercise carried out with a sample of magistrates, which was designed specifically to assess the types of information which magistrates most frequently used when determining what kind of sentence to impose. The researchers identified 21 different types of information which the sample of magistrates most commonly used when making up their minds as to the sentence. They naturally took into account such matters as the offence, the offender's previous convictions, his attitude to his present offence, his family relationships; but also other things such as, ranked in order of importance, his employment, his parents or his partner, his personality, his response to previous sentences, his friends, his medical history, and so on. Some of that information would be available to the court in any event—the kind of information, for example, that one would expect to find in the police antecedence. Many of them, however, are social factors, about which by far the most satisfactory way of obtaining objective information would be by means of a social inquiry report.

JPEG 609 This new clause introduces into our discussion a totally new area of considerable interest. I hope that hon. Members opposite will regard it one one to which they can make a valuable contribution.

We already have in our law a system by which a court can defer sentence. It is a fairly recent innovation and I am not certain to what extent use is being made if it. That purpose of that power is totally different from that of new clause 17. The purpose is to give the courts an additional power in that, having decided that they will sentence an offender to a term of imprisonment, they would have the power to defer the commencement of that term until a later date.

Such power has not been used in this country;the power that the Bill now contains of enabling the courts to pass a sentence which is partly immediate and partly suspended is almost the reverse of it.The purpose of this power is to seek to ensure that some of the more drastic effects of imprisonment, particularly for a person who may be subjected to custody for the first time and for those sentenced to relatively short terms of custody, will be mitigated by a postponement of the inception of the term of imprisonment.The postponement would enable an offender to make provision for his family and his future employment; to tie up the threads that are inevitably broken during a period of imprisonment or other custody which is to take effect immediately. In addition, in cases where there is the possibility of an appeal it would give time for the transcript of the evidence to be obtained so that proper and full consideration of an appeal can be given before the offender starts to serve his term of imprisonment.

In far too many cases a person serves part of a term of imprisonment and then, as a result of an appeal, his conviction is quashed and it is decided that he ought never to have served that term of imprisonment. The new clause would be of considerable help in avoiding that situation.

There are many different reasons why a provision of this kind should be introduced into our penal system. Althought it would be an innovation in this country, it is by no means an innovation in the rest of the world. Systems of this kind exist in the United States, but coming nearer to home there is an excellent example of such a system, introduced in 1974 in the Netherlands. That is a system which is much wider than that suggested by the new clause. It is one which contains a call-up provision under which those who have been on bail before trial are not required to start their sentence immediately. They receive a call-up letter which requires them to report on a fixed day to a named prison. They can then apply for deferment of the call-up date, which is granted on reasonable grounds. There are sanctions in the system because punctual reporting in obedience to the call-up is a matter which is taken into account in deciding in what conditions the sentence should be served, for example, whether in secure or open conditions.

The Parliamentary all-party Penal Affairs Group obtained a good deal of evidence about the way in which this system worked in the Netherlands and 610 was satisfied that it worked well.
