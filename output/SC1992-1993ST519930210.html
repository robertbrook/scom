4.30 pm

Mr. Graham Allen
(Nottingham, North)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee has considered the Licensed Betting Offices (Amendment) Regulations 1993 (S.I. 1993, No. 51).

It is a travesty that a measure of such importance, which will affect the livelihoods of so many, should come before the House as a statutory instrument. That is an affront to democracy. However, this is not the time to discuss how to democratise Parliament.

The regulations relate to the extension of the hours of betting offices. The subject first came to my attention not because I have a long-term interest in the industry or in gambling, but when, out of the blue, many people who work in betting shops and betting shop managers telephoned me to ask what Parliament could do to help them. Their employers had stuck under their noses contracts extending their hours of work, with the accompanying threat that if they did not sign they would be dismissed.

That happened before there was an inkling in Parliament that the opening hours of betting shops were to be extended. There must have been collusion between the Government and the big three bookies to tip them off that the regulations were being introduced. Action was being taken before the will of Parliament was tested.

It is not surprising that when individuals were given the choice of the sack or working longer hours, most, reluctantly, signed the contract. But surveys carried out be people in the industry found that 97 per cent. of staff who work in betting shops said no to opening longer.

The Opposition feel that the thuggery of the big three bookies—Ladbrokes, Coral and William Hill—is completely unacceptable. I take no pleasure in nominating William Hill as the winner of bookies' bully of the year for insisting that individuals sign a new contract on pain of dismissal. The industry has struggled with its somewhat seedy image; it has dubious overtones and has been accused of intimidation in certain areas. This latest episode has done no favours to the image of the bookmaker or of racing. That is another reason why the thousands of people who earn a straightforward, honest and reasonable living from the industry should oppose the regulations.

There are lessons to be learnt for the Sunday trading issue. Many people currently working willingly at checkouts on Sunday afternoons for reasonable pay may, in a year or so, be forced by unscrupulous employers to work on Sundays. I shall not debate that now, but it is a warning to us.

The betting industry has been consistently anti-trade union; it has forced out the trade unions and even the 4 staff councils that were set up are in decline. We should beware of employers who gain the upper hand in that way and behave in an unrestrained, non-self-regulating manner. We should learn the lessons for the future.

How will the regulations affect staff? Many of those workers are women and if they are forced by the strong-arm tactics of the large firms to work late in the evening, their social lives and, above all, their family lives will inevitably be severely affected. I should like to read one of the many letters complaining about the treatment meted out to staff by the big bookies. We have all received letters from peiple who will be profoundly affected by the regulations if they are accepted. This letter is from The Racing Post of 22 January 1993:

"I am a betting shop cashier and have been told I will get the sack if I don't sign my new agreement to do night racing.


I told my boss that I was willing to work late one night a week but could do no more, as I have a handicapped son with cerebral palsy who cannot cook for himself. But the company expect me to work four or five nights a week.


As you see, 12 years' service means nothing and I am just one of many who will be looking for a job in April."

I can show the Committee many such letters from people who have given the industry long, loyal service. In these days of rising crime, staff and punters who leave betting shops late at night will face greater threats to their personal safety from muggers and other criminals. Betting shop staff often work in twos or threes, and late at night they will be a target for casual thieves and robbers. If people have a drink or two late in the evening and then go to the betting shop, that will be a recipe for further problems. With the streets quiet at night, staff going home after closing time will be very vulnerable to armed robbers and others.

I assume that many of the licensed betting offices were granted their licenses under a previous regime by local licensing authorities and magistrates committees. Premises were licensed on the basis of existing opening hours, which are now to be amended. What would have been the view of those licensing authorities had they realised that the hours would be extended until 10 o'clock? Can the Minister shed some light on that? I suspect that those licensing authorities would have been influenced by representations from local people and from the people who work in the industry and they would have granted fewer licences. Having established a foothold, betting offices are going further than expected.

5.45 pm

Mr. Devlin
: I am not a member of the Committee, and I am grateful to you, Mr. Martin, for calling me to speak. I have no interest to declare, not being a director of any business related to racing or having any other connection with it. I question the decision of the Home Office to extend the opening hours of betting shops from 6.30 pm to 10 pm. That follows an extensive consultation process, which has been going on since 1991 following the Rothschild royal commission. On balance, that commission was against evening opening. Its primary concerns were the potential for illegal betting, the incentive for excessive gambling and the damage to the greyhound racing industry. It found little public desire for evening opening.

The Government's consultation paper makes it clear that

"The large off-course bookmaking companies regard evening opening as an opportunity to generate additional profit."

Hitherto the Government's policy on gambling has had two main premises: first, that people should be allowed to gamble if they want to, and secondly, that demand for gambling should not be stimulated. Those seem admirably well designed to maintain a balance between individual liberty and the public interest and welfare.

The consultation paper cited the main objections to evening opening hours as:
  1. "the additional costs for (especially) smaller betting offices;
  2. the effect on hours of work for staff in betting shops:
  3. the effect on on-course bookmakers: and
  4. the adverse effect on greyhound racing."
On item 2, it was said: JPEG 11

"In relation to staff employed in Betting Offices, any changes to their conditions of service as a result of evening opening would be a matter for negotiation between them and their employer".

On item 4, it was said:

"This is a matter on which the Government would welcome the detailed views of the greyhound and bookmaking industries".

In October and November 1992, before consultation was complete, William Hill and Ladbrokes began to make wholesale changes of all employee contracts to cater for evening opening hours. Early that month, William Hill wrote to all its staff in these terms:

"The company believes that legislation will permit us to open our shops to cover evening racing and that the likely date when this change will be implemented will be April 1993."

When these letters were handed out, staff were told that if they did not sign they would have to "leave the industry". Ladbrokes has employed the same tactics with its staff. Revised, wholly unreasonable contracts are to come into effect on 15 April 1993, and staff were advised in November that

"failure to sign the revised contract after full consultation will regrettably result in those individuals being given notice to terminate their existing contract".

Coral has done the same.

I asked employees in the industry about consultation. I told them to write to their Members of Parliament and I tabled several parliamentary questions on 17 December about the consultation process. They were answered on 11 January, when I learnt, incredibly, that 824 representations were received of which 775 were against and 49 in favour; that three petitions of more than 2,000 signatures were received opposing evening opening; and that, of 199 representations from members of the public, only five were in favour and 194 were against. A survey of bookmakers by A. R. Dennis showed that of the 475 who took part, 439 opposed evening opening. Of 4,167 staff consulted, an overwhelming 4,046 opposed evening opening.

The National Association of Bookmakers has declared itself to be against an extension. In addition, the general purposes committee of the Association of Chief Police Officers advised the Home Office in late 1991 of its concerns about the safety of staff.

Against that background, let us consider the remarkable decision by the Home Office to allow evening opening. What are the reasons? What is the justification? As a result of my parliamentary questions, the strength of feeling against any extension is on the public record. Since I tabled those questions, I have received scores of letters from across the country complaining at the way in which people have been treated by the big three companies in their eagerness to prepare for this eventuality—in spite of the Home Office assurances given on 11 January by the Minister that whatever contingency plans Ladbrokes and others may be making, no decision has been or will be conveyed to them in advance of a public announcement. Why then did Ladbrokes, Coral and Hill go to so much effort and pain unless—as they say in letters to their staff—they knew that the decision was to be made in their favour?

On 19 January, the surprise announcement from the Home Office—more of a surprise to some than to others, it seems—stated:

"Over 800 representations and a number of petitions have since been received…In the light of the consultation process"—

12 Note, in the light of it, not in spite of it—

"I have decided that evening opening should be allowed until 10 pm on Mondays to Sundays…It is clear that the growth in the number of evening horseraces had created increased consumer demand".—[ Official Report, 19 January 1993: Vol. 217, c. 141.]

In fact, the number of evening meetings has increased only from 82 to 88 nights in the past five years.

Most impartial observers would think it strange that a consultation process, in which 97 per cent. of people were opposed to a plan was cited as the reason for approving it. When challenged, the Home Office relies on a Gallup poll conducted for the Betting Office Licensees Association—BOLA—which is dominated by the big three betting shop owners and not, perhaps, the most impartial body. That poll apparently found that 56 per cent. of punters were in favour of late opening.

Perhaps BOLA will explain why that 56 per cent. appeared in its circular letter to MPs as 72 per cent. Perhaps the association might also explain why it sent the survey back to Gallup for re-modelling. Perhaps BOLA will further explain why the study was denied to independent bookmakers who requested it. One gets the impression that there is a lot of explaining to be done. Is it time that Gordon Dennis of A.R. Dennis—the largest independent—resigned from the BOLA council in protest at the way in which the survey was massaged into shape? Will the Minister tell me whether he relied on the survey in making his decision and, if so, why it did not feature in the Home Office press release?

The recommendations of the Home Affairs Select Committee were cited in the Home Office press release. Yet again, there is a significant divergence. The Select Committee called for opening until 9 pm; the Home Office generously offered opening until 10 pm. The Select Committee called for compensation to support greyhound racing and the smaller bookmakers; no such schemes have been set. The Select Committee thought that the arrangement would be voluntary; in fact, it has turned out to be compulsory if people work for Ladbrokes or William Hill. To add insult to injury, those two companies claim that, because 98 per cent. and 99.5 per cent. of their staff have signed new contracts, that shows some level of support.

We are left with a decision which will create unfair and unnecessary hardship to employees of betting shops, which will damage greyhound racing and reduce the number of indepednent bookmakers—creating a triopoly of the big three who have done so much to secure the decision in their favour—and for which no credible justification has yet been given. The Government are right to say that it is a matter for the employer and the employee to negotiate in their contracts, but it is wrong to ignore a situation in which a decision may be used to bully and cajole people who have no bargaining power. The Home Ofrfice understands that point in relation to Sunday trading, so why ignore it here?

Under the new contracts that are being imposed, employees have to be available for work at any time they are asked. No extra money is to be paid for longer, or less sociable hours; no provision is made for meals; and no extra security is offered at night. William Hill's contract states:

"The company reserves the right to require the employee to work such additional hours over any five days as may be notified to them over and above their contractual hours."

JPEG 13 Ladbrokes can vary the number of days, shifts and times of working every week and need not even confine itself to any set number of hours in any one week. The effect on staff will be appalling. Most licensed betting office staff are female and worry about travelling home late. A non-driver staff member living in a village who cannot travel home by public transport after 7 pm was told that that was "tough" and that she would have to take a taxi at her own expense.

The greyhound racing industry, including the excellent Teesside park track in my constituency, stands to be ruined by the regulations. The afternoon opening of betting shops in 1961 caused a 15 per cent. drop in greyhound racing attendances in 1962 and 1963. That wiped out half the greyhound racing tracks. A similar drop in evening attendances is expected as a result of opening betting shops in the evening. The big three bookmakers own a handful of tracks. They will monopolise the sport by televising races from their own tracks. Greyhound racing authorities will not be able to control the number of races that are shown.
