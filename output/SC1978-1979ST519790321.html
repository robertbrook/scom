DRAFT CINEMATOGRAPH FILMS (LIMITS OF LEVY) ORDER 1979

10.30 a.m.

The Under-Secretary of State for Trade (Mr. Michael Meacher)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft Cinematograph Films (Limits of Levy) Order 1979.

I wish to stress at the outset that this draft order arises simply as a result of a general increase in the level of admission prices to cinemas and, above all, I am glad to say, from a most welcome upturn in cinema attendances. It does not represent any fundamental change of policy.

The order amends section 2(3)( b) of the, Cinematograph Films Act 1957. This Act gave statutory force to the collection of the levy on cinema seats—widely known as the Eady levy—which is a proportion of the admission price paid in respect of all public cinema performances, with certain exceptions. Cinemas are exempt from levy in any week when their takings do not exceed a certain threshold.

This levy is therefore not a Government aid, but is what might be called "trade" money. It is collected by Her Majesty's Customs and Excise and paid to the British Film Fund Agency, a body established under the 1957 Act. Out of the proceeds the agency, with the approval of the Secretary of State, makes grants to certain bodies connected with the film industry. These are the Children's Film Foundation, the British Film Institute, the National Film School and the National Film Finance Corporation, on behalf of the National Film Development Fund. The balance is then paid by the agency to the makers of eligible British films—or, in effect, so often to their distributor—in proportion to the current earnings of those films in the domestic market, in order to encourage British film production.

When the Act was passed, Parliament wished the levy to be neither so small as to be of little benefit to the industry, nor so large as to be an unreasonable burden. Consequently the Act specified that the estimated annual yield should be greater than£2 million but less than£5 million. 4 Section 5(1) of the Films Act 1970 provided that these limits could be varied by order made by statutory instrument. By 1975—I ask hon. Members to note this point—the true value of that£5 million laid down in 1970 was estimated to be about£13 million and it was therefore decided in that year to raise the upper limit to bring it a little nearer its original value, and it was increased to£7 million—as hon. Members will see, a modest increase when compared with the real value in 1970. This was said at the time to be sufficient to cater for any increases in the general level of prices over the following two years. But it is now necessary to increase the upper limit again, but with one important qualification to which I shall return in a few moments.

The levy year is a period of 52 consecutive weeks. Last year it ran from 25 September 1977 to 23 September 1978, and the total levy collected was about£6½ million—very close to the upper limit of£7 million. In the first 23 weeks of the current levy year the sum collected was approximately£700,000 above that of the same period last year. Clearly, therefore, if this rate of increase were to continue, the present upper limit of£7 million would be exceeded this year.

The enabling legislation provides that the Secretary of State may make a variation in the levy yield, but that he should first consult his statutory advisory body the Cinematograph Films Council, which includes representatives of all sections of the industry. The council's advice was that the present maximum yield should be raised and this advice has been accepted. The order therefore provides that the upper limit of the levy yield be raised, by amending section 2(3)( b) of the 1957 Act by substituting for£7 million the sum of£12 million. This raising of the ceiling will not increase in any way the rate at which the levy is collected from cinemas. I emphasise that point, because I know that there has been concern on it. It does not in any way increase the rate at which the levy is collected from cinemas.

It has been put to me that there are various other ways of dealing with this problem. It has been said, for example, that we could adjust the rate of the levy on each ticket, or raise the exemption threshold in order to produce a lower yield. In response to requests from the industry for relief to cinemas we have in fact made such adjustments two years running. In the JPEG 5 year 1977–78 and in the current year 1978–79 these adjustments were calculated to produce an estimated reduction in the total yield of£1 million. Despite these reliefs, the total yield has risen, partly as a product of higher prices, but, and most gratifyingly, as I mentioned earlier, because of a recent strong upturn in cinema attendances.

Alternatively, it has been suggested that we could suspend the collection of the levy when the£7 million ceiling was reached— not that one would want to approach that ceiling too closely, as the levy is collected fortnightly and the total is not known to us until several days later.

I do not think, however, that we should again tinker with the collection of levy in any of the several ways that have been suggested, because we do not know enough about the ability of cinemas, particularly the independents or small groups, to pay the levy at the present rate, nor indeed at any other rate. For this reason some months ago I put in hand a series of moves that will give us information about the levy that has never been available before. The important qualification to this proposed increase in the maximum levy yield that I mentioned earlier is that it is only an interim measure to cover the period while we collect and digest the information now coming to us, consult with interested parties and decide whether changes should be made in the levy system.

Perhaps I could indicate what these moves are. First, my Department is sharing with a number of trade associations the cost of a very thorough investigation by an independent firm of accountants into the ability of cinemas of different types in different circumstances to pay the levy. The accountants' report should be with us in the next few weeks and it would be quite wrong at this point to pre-empt the findings of this investigation by making any arbitrary alteration in the present rate of levy before their report has been most carefully considered by all the parties to it.

Secondly, as hon. Members will no doubt have seen, I have arranged for the publication of the levy distribution in respect of individual films, the details of which have up to now never been published. The first two such publications have now appeared covering the 14 weeks from 24 September to 30 December 1978 and subsequent publications will appear at 4 or 5-weekly intervals. The names of some of the films benefiting from levy 6 distributions have attracted attention in the press. It is all to the good that these matters be brought into the open, as there can be no intelligent debate about levy without this information.

In addition, the Interim Action Committee on the Film Industry is now considering the subject of levy and is to make a recommendation. I am also expecting to receive the views of the Cinematograph Films Council.

In the next few months therefore a lot of new material will be coming to hand, together with the opinions which can take these new facts into account. It is my intention to consider all this new information most carefully and to consult with interested parties. I expect our conclusions to be given effect either in a separate measure, or in a wider measure to give effect to the statement of policy which we shall shortly make on our future plans for the industry as a whole.

For these reasons I do not believe that we should engage at this time in any short-term fine tuning of the system, about which I have doubts on a number of grounds. I therefore seek as an interim measure—I emphasise that—this increase in the maximum limit of film levy to permit collection and distribution to continue meanwhile without interruption.

Mr. Bob Cryer
: I am grateful for this opportunity to discuss the order because I believe that Parliament needs to examine where the so-called Eady money has gone. The last annual report of the British Film Fund Agency available to Parliament, printed in July last year, does not give many details, but simply sets out its bare statutory requirements in a way that is not particularly helpful to Members of Parliament in their examination of the proposals as they are brought before the Committeee.

I ask my hon. Friend to ensure that future annual reports of the British Film Fund Agency, in explaining where the Eady money goes, should at least give some indication of why it is satisfied that the money has been distributed in the way authorised. It is not satisfactory to say that£499,675 was distributed to the Children's Film Foundation and that the agency was satisfied that the money was properly required for the activities of the foundation. It would be helpful to have a brief review of those activities. I believe that there is general agreement that the Children's Film Foundation provides a very useful and valuable purpose, but at the same time the information given is somewhat sparse.

I wish to say, in contrast, that my hon. Friend deserves congratulations on revealing some of the information about where the Eady money goes. This is a long overdue reform and it is quite staggering to think that the Eady money has been paid for so long without any individual allocations ever being divulged in the way authorised at present. It illustrates the fact that if one provides information, far from inhibiting discussion, it develops it and enables it to take place on a much better and more informed basis.

It is worthwhile making a brief comment about the list of recipients of the Eady money so far. There has been an amount of concern in the industry, expressed in 10 the correspondence columns of "Screen International" regarding the allocation of the Eady money to distributors and not producers. It is interesting that the allocation of the money has shown up some of the warped ways in which the automatic nature of the fund produces its allocation.

Concern has been expressed that the money goes to distributors instead of producers. It has been pointed out that some distributors engage in the finance of production as well as distribution. On the other hand, one commentator said in the edition of "Screen International" of 17 March:

"There remains, however, a significant number of cases where the distributor has demanded a share in Eady towards recovery of his costs as a condition for taking the film—this is a genuine abuse and I, for one, tried to persuade the BFPA to make a rule forbidding its members to enter into this type of agreement. However, it was the very type of independently-financed producer who is most vulnerable that refused such a restriction. These producers felt that inability to trade in their Eady entitlement might deny them distribution for a "difficult "picture. This area remains open to abuse and requires attention."

That is true. The Eady money was designed to assist independent British producers. It appears that in some instances, not unfortunately quoted in the letter, those very people are having to forego the Eady money because of pressure from the commanding position of the distributor. That is one point which deserves the attention of my hon. Friend.

My second point is that the automatic nature of the allocation of the funds produces, and has recently produced, some interesting illustrations of the sort of warping effect that I mentioned. For example, the short film "Hot Wheels" received£122,901 compared with the feature "Wild Geese", which received£138,984; and the feature "Midnight Express" which received£40,389 over the period for the publication of the first results. The reason that "Hot Wheels" received that amount of money was by virtue of the quirk that it was distributed with "Grease", which happened to be an enormously successful feature film.

One of the problems that arises is that the allocation of that money was made to a distributor, CIC. It wrote to " Screen International " to say that, although there was a large amount of money paid for a comparatively low-cost short, far and away outweighing the cost of feature film production, nevertheless that money was to be divided between Paramount—which JPEG 11 was to start on a short film production programme—and the British film producer of " Hot Wheels". It did not, however, say precisely what that division was to be. Quite clearly in that instance, the producer of a short film may not wish to cavil at any distribution, as it is the distributor, who decides that "Hot Wheels" goes out with "Grease" and who therefore receives£122,000, and not the producer. That relationship needs close examination.

Since the Eady money is designed to go to British film producers in order to sustain and retain an indigenous film industry, does the Minister think that the definition "a British film" needs tightening. Well-known American companies are named in the published list of fund allocations. Of course it is their British enterprises which receive the money, but at the same time we must recognise that the successive crisis in the British film industry has been largely due to the fact that we have depended too often on the investment of foreign capital which, for one reason or another, has been arbitrarily changed, withdrawn or deferred. The Eady fund is failing if it does not ensure that the money goes to a genuine indigenous British production.

I wonder, too, as the automatic basis of the fund produces allocations to all sorts of films, whether there is not a need to allocate the Eady fund on the basis of, for example, artistic merit. I know that that is vague and controversial. Nevertheless, in France, for example, there is a supplement to the automatic basis of their levy arrangements for films of particular merit. One could obviate criticism about leaving certain films to the vagaries of artistic merit, and thereby excluding some films entirely, by saying that, if certain films are of a worthwhile nature, they should receive a supplement. That would give direct financial encouragement to films of good quality.

My hon. Friend the Minister mentioned the investigation into the industry and the ability of independent exhibitors to pay the levy. We need to examine the position of the distributors in relation to exhibition. The aliance of exhibition and distribution means that two powerful organisations, EMI and Rank, in effect have complete oligopolistic control of exhibition in this country. Sometimes that power tends to be exercised in an arbitrary 12 way. It is worth remembering that what I regard as an extremely worthwhile film such as "Kes" nearly received no distribution at all. Indeed, it was given a West End distribution only by virtue of pressure and the very quality of the film.

That is true also of another British film, made many years before—" Saturday Night and Sunday Morning "That film has received many accolades, but it was regarded by the distributors as a tiny, eccentric, Left-wing film which could not possibly be given distribution. In the same way, "Kes" was regarded with concern by the distributors because it contained Yorkshire people speaking with Yorkshire accents. It was felt that "Kes" could not possibly be understood by the millions of people who live south of Potters Bar— indeed, south of Doncaster.

The Association of Independent Producers has suggested that the position of independent exhibitors might well be examined. Although the Eady money is to encourage independent production, independent production is meaningless without exhibition. There might therefore be merit in the suggestion, which the association has made, that cinemas that show films of merit and embark on a quality programme—dispensing, for example, with the " The Lusty Vicar " and other such productions, and concentrating on quality—might receive exemption from the Eady levy.

My hon. Friend has said that this is an interim measure. I understand that. But, once the interim measure is passed, as I assume it will be, if we are to sustain a British industry in the longer term we must look at all out options. I should have thought that exemption from the Eady levy for cinemas with distinct and defined policies of quality and merit might be worth considering. That in itself would help British independent production, because it would give exhibition when distributors might not be prepared to give it.

The increase in cinema takings is extremely welcome. But the industry is dominated by the two distributors and exhibitors, and that will not end until we take seriously the possibility of having one chain of cinemas, in either municipal or public ownership, to ensure that the oligopoly is at least partly owned in the public interest.

The publication of the report, which my hon. Friend has mentioned, on the ability of cinemas to pay will, I hope, enable us JPEG 13 to have even more informed discussion. Will the Minister ensure that the accountants' report is published? Some cinemas might say "Our financial arrangements are confidential". If that is the position, could I ask him to ensure that the general arrangements for the report are published, and that the confidential commercial arrangements are deleted from the report, so that we get the general position, and so that the report is not denied to Parliament and the greater part of the public because a dozen pages out of about 50 involve detailed commercial matters?

My hon. Friend by publishing the Eady allocation has contributed greatly to the informed discussion of the British film industry, and I hope that his examination can also make a contribution by publication as well.
