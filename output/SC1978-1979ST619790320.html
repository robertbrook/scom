DRAFT NORTH OF SCOTLAND HYDRO-ELECTRIC BOARD (COMPENSATION FOR SMELTER DEFICITS) ORDER 1979

10.30 a.m.

The Minister of State, Scottish Office (Mr. Gregor MacKenzie)
: I beg to move,

That the Committee have considered the draft North of Scotland Hydro-Electric Board (Compensation for Smelter Deficits) Order 1979.

The Committee will be well aware of the background to this draft order which is the third to be laid under the provisions of the Electricity (Financial Provisions) (Scotland) Act 1976.1 propose, therefore, to deal only very briefly with the history of the smelter supply arrangements. In 1968, the North of Scotland Hydro-Electric Board entered into a contract with the British Aluminium Company to supply electricity to the company's aluminium reduction plant at Invergordon. The electricity was to be provided by the Hunterston B nuclear power station which was being built for the South of Scotland Electricity Board at that time. The company undertook to pay an appropriate share both of the costs of constructing the power station and of the annual running costs in return for guaranteed supplies of electricity. In the early years, before Hunterston B was expected to be commissioned, the contract provided for power to be supplied to the smelter from conventional power stations. It was recognised that this arrangement would inevitably result in losses being incurred by the Hydro Board in the early stages, but it was expected that these would be recovered by the Board over the remainder of the contract period when the benefits of nuclear generation would be available. The Government none the less recognised at the time that the sums of money involved were very large in relation to the scale of the board's operations and therefore gave the board an assurance that, in the event of a substantial loss arising, consideration would be given to the position of the board's other consumers.

4 It transpired that a four-year delay in the commissioning of Hunterston B led to substantial losses for the board in the early years of the contract and because of restrictions on the output of the station making it necessary for part of the smelter's requirements to be met from fossil-fuelled power stations, it is likely that the board will continue to incur deficits on the supply of electricity to the smelter for the remainder of the period of the contract. The Government therefore took power under the 1976 Act to compensate the board for these deficits so that they would not fall on the board's other consumers.

The reasons for the continuing losses being made by the board under the smelter contract and the considerations which led the Government to agree to pay compensation for these losses were discussed at length during the passage of the 1976 Act and again during this Committee's deliberations on the two previous orders made under that Act. I hope, therefore, that the Committee will share my view that it would not be appropriate for me to take up its time this morning with a lengthy rehearsal of these matters. Instead I propose to concentrate on the draft order which relates to the deficit arising in the financial year 1977–78, and to outline the developments during that year which have a bearing on the compensation payment proposed in the order.

The Committee may have wondered why this order was not laid last summer when the size of the deficit incurred by the board in the financial year 1977–78 became known. The reason for the delay is that the Government have had to give lengthy consideration to the question of how much of the 1977–78 deficit should be reimbursed to the board at this stage. The deficit in the published accounts for 1977–78 is larger than it might otherwise have been in that the board has made charges to the British Aluminium Company under a number of heads which the company has so far refused to pay, because it takes the view that it is not liable for these charges under its contract with the board. The disputed charges relate to nuclear fuel reprocessing, the costs of repairs to reactor 4 at Hunterston and certain items on the capital account.

In view of these difficulties and certain other complexities in the 1977–78 accounts my right hon Friend the Secretary of State JPEG 5 commissioned a firm of independent consultant accountants to examine the board's smelter accounts for 1976–77 and 1977–78. The committee will recall that a similar examination of the deficits arising in the period to the end of the financial year 1975–76 was undertaken before these deficits were reimbursed.

The report by the consultants confirmed that, given the board's interpretation of its agreements with the South of Scotland Electricity Board and with the company, the charges to the smelter account and to the company had been correctly calculated. The consultants pointed out, however, that the interpretation of the contract between the board and the company was a matter for the parties to resolve, and that if the company were eventually to pay these disputed charges the final deficit on the smelter account for 1977–78 would be substantially reduced.

In the light of the consultants' findings my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State has decided that it would be improper, while negotiations between the company and the board are continuing, for the Government to pay compensation to the board for that part of the deficit which represents these disputed charges. The disputed items have increased the deficit in 1977–78 by some £3.5 million on the smelter revenue account and just over £500,000 on the capital account. The figures in question include associated financing costs, that is the interest incurred by the board in carrying these deficits on the smelter account during the financial year 1977–78. The result of the Government's consideration of the disputed charges, therefore, is that the deficit for which we propose to make compensation this year is reduced by some £4 million. Whether or not the Government eventually have to meet this sum depends on the outcome of the dispute between the board and the company. The Government are pressing the board to seek an early resolution of this dispute.

A further complication has arisen in that, in the course of the consultants examination, it was established that a sum of approximately £1.7 million which was paid to the board in compensation for the deficit incurred in the year ended March 1977—that is the sum paid under the previous order—also related to a charge to the British Aluminium Company for nuclear fuel reprocessing. As this charge was similarly in dispute it had not been 6 paid by the company. After consulting the board on the matter my right hon. Friend considers it right that a sum equal to the charge in question together with interest for the period since the sum was paid, should be deducted from the contribution which we would otherwise have intended to make towards reimbursement of the deficit arising on the smelter accounts in 1977–78. This has the effect of reducing by a further £2 million approximately the deficit which we propose to reimburse. Again this is a sum which the Government may eventually have to pay to the board, depending on the outcome of the dispute with the company.

The other factor which had an important effect on the size of the deficit for 1977–78 was the outage of reactor 4 at Hunterston. The Committee will recall the incident which took place in October 1977 when sea water entered part of the cooling system of the reactor. As a result of this incident insulation material in the pressure vessel annulus below the boilers was damaged and the reactor had to be shut down for the insulation to be replaced. The loss of production of cheap nuclear-based electricity from reactor 4 has inevitably increased the operating deficit on the smelter account, because of the need to provide make-up units to the smelter from higher cost fossil-fuelled stations. It is worth noting that the other reactor at Hunterston B has performed well throughout the year.

Finally, I turn to the actual payments specified in the draft order. The deficit recorded in the board's accounts for 1977–78 was £14.6 million. About £2.4 million of this sum is provision for future costs for which the board is not at present seeking compensation. The sum remaining is therefore approximately £12.2 million £1 million of which relates to capital contributions, with the remainder constituting the deficit on revenue account.

Deduction of the items in dispute— namely, just over £5.5 million on the revenue account, including the sum reimbursed in 1977, and the £½million on the capital account—brings the proposed payments down to about £5.5 million on the revenue account and £½ million on the capital account. As required by the 1976 Act, the board has been consulted about the drafting of the order and the Treasury has indicated its approval.

I invite the Committee to consider the draft order.

Mr. Hamish Gray
: This is the first occasion on which we have considered an order created under the Electricity (Financial Provisions) (Scotland) Act 1976 in which there is likely to be any degree of controversy. I say that because the British Aluminium Company, and probably also —for different reasons—the North of Scotland Hydro-Electric Board have doubts about this order.

I was a keen supporter of the policy of the Labour Government in 1968—before I came to this House—when they selected Invergordon, in my constituency, for the siting of the British Aluminium smelter. The British Aluminium Company smelter has contributed considerably to the present welfare of Easter Ross. It has provided about 900 jobs. It has settled well into what was almost entirely an agricultural community, despite the fact— as Mr. Tony Mackay, lecturer at Aberdeen University recently pointed out—that unemployment in my part of the Highlands is now greater than it was before the smelter was built. This is a matter of very great regret to me, and in no way would I lay that at the door of the Government of the day for adopting that policy. I believe that they were right at that time, and I welcomed the development, as I still do.

Having said that, I feel that we would be failing in our duties as Members of Parliament if we did not carefully scrutinise what that policy did, and what it has subsequently led to in terms of the spending of public money.

Today, we are being asked to consider the payment of a further £6 million. That 8 totals £56 million, which is a substantial amount of money and which we must scrutinise if we are to do our job properly.

My point is that there was something wrong with the original negotiations. The British Aluminium Company must be highly delighted with the way in which the participants in the negotiations conducted themselves.

It got itself a very good indeed, and I am delighted about that because my constituency is benefiting. But in examining the matter as a Mob Parliament considering the spending of public money, I feel considerable concern.

The reason for the deficits is that any shortfall which arose from the fact that 78 per cent, was not reached has to be made up from more expensive fossil fuel sources. That, again, is a serious matter, because it seems to me that this will be a recurring order. As far as I can see, the order will come before us year after year, and all the Committee and the House can do is approve it, becuase it is part of a contract which was fairly entered upon.

However, we have reached the stage when we must question the desirability of the Government entering into long-term contracts such as this without the most positive reassurance that the bargain will be to the Government's benefit. I do not suggest that they should have entered into a long-term contract which would make it impossible for the British Aluminium Company to continue. That would be equally ridiculous, but for a Government to have to face up to about £56 million in subsidy—a subsidy which is likely to be continued at the rate, at a modest estimate of perhaps £6 million a year—is a serious matter.

I have great sympathy with the British Aluminium Company. Why should the company now be faced with the cost, for example, of nuclear fuels processing? That was not part of the original contract. Why should the company be asked to pay additional sums becuase the Hunterston B power station has not lived up to expectations? We all hope that that situation is only temporary and that in due course JPEG 9 Hunterston will be an unqualified success. But we must face facts, and at present Hunterston is far from successful. That is why we have to meet today to discuss the spending of considerable sums of taxpayers' money in the form of subsidy to make up for the deficits.

I do not want to speak for too long because, as the Minister of State rightly said, we have had many opportunities to discuss these orders and the provisions of the Act under which they arise, but it is worrying that we face this recurring situation. We are responsible for all public bodies having public accountability, and it is to us that such bodies must look for guidance.

I hope that we shall agree to the motion this morning, I do not think that we can do anything else. But I alert the Committee to the fact that this matter gives grave cause for concern. Although I was a supporter of the original policy, with hindsight I doubt very much whether any Government would feel complacent or happy about what has ultimately developed in this project.

11.02 a.m.

Mr. George Younger
: It is clear that this order is to be an annual event for us for a long time to come. As all the speeches so 14 far this morning have emphasised, we are not discussing the order from the point of view of whether we should reject it— although I know that that is the technical position—because we are landed with it. The Government's calculations, as far as we can check them, seem to be acceptable, and so clearly we shall agree to the motion.

However, as each hon. Member has so far indicated, we must try to learn some sound lessons from this sad and sorry story. I begin by accepting at once that the intentions of everyone concerned have been thoroughly admirable throughout. The matter originated when all of us—the Government of the day, the Opposition and everyone else—were extremely anxious not to lose the opportunity of securing home-based aluminium smelting capacity. We wished to do what we could to make that possible, and we were all in favour of doing so by producing electricity for the smelter at the most reasonable cost. There I believe we all agreed, and to that extent we are all partly responsible for what has happened since.

Our comments should not, therefore, be seen as a criticism or witch hunt of those who did their best to implement our wishes if we point out that the whole matter has been a chapter of disasters, except in so far as we have, thank goodness, the highly successful smelter in the Highlands which, as my hon. Friend the Member for Ross and Cromarty (Mr. Gray) says, has been a tremendous boon to the area. That is the only good point about the agreement.

Bearing in mind that everyone was doing his best, this seems to be the classic case where the desire of everyone to achieve a result was allowed to distort the calculations, decisions and value judgments that were made in drawing up the agreement. The present problems about disputed costs give a splendid example of how such an agreement can go wrong.

It is always difficult to make decisions— particularly financial decisions—on very large matters where tremendous prizes are gained or lost according to the decision made. To take up a point made by the hon. Member for Edinburgh, Central (Mr. Cook) I think it is legitimate to say that there is a difference between making a difficult financial decision when, if it is wrong, the company will go under and, on the other hand, making the same decision free of such a sanction and with JPEG 15 only a very remote long-term consequence which will not affect the taxpayers of the day.

What is happening now is that the taxpayer—without realising it—is paying a great deal of money, as the my hon. Friend the Member for Ross and Cromarty (Mr. Gray) said, in order to pay for the mistakes in calculations made in 1968 and afterwards. It behoves us in Parlt, even though we all share some responsibility for those mistakes, to make this clear and try to draw the lessons from it.

I should like to consider briefly the matters at present in dispute, because they illustrate how impracticable the whole operation was from the beginning, except in its ultimate aim of attracting the British Aluminium Company's account.

There is the question of the repair costs to Hunterston reactor 4.1 doubt whether it was reasonable in the first place to base the whole agreement on the technology of a wholly unproven system. But, surely, even if it had been a reasonable decision, to bring into the issue the financial costs of an accident which took place as a result of human error some 13 years after the original agreement was made is an unacceptable extension of the original calculations. I am not a lawyer and I cannot make pronouncements on the matter, but common sense suggests that it is not quite fair to expect the British Aluminium Company to pay some part, not of the capital costs, or even of the escalating running costs, but of the financial results of an accident which was due to human error in the plant. That seems very extraordinary to me, and I cannot see how the original 1968 agreement could properly have been held to take that into account. That is a matter for the lawyers to sort out. It is, however, a lesson to us that such matters must be thought of in time in any similar future undertaking.

There is also the question of nuclear fuel reprocessing charges. I am not quite sure of the position—no doubt the Minister will correct me if I am wrong—but I believe that the cost of nuclear fuel had to be taken into account and that the escalation in its cost was included in that figure. But I am not clear whether the cost of reprocessing nuclear fuel was also specifically included. There is a difference—the Minister may know whether there is a difference in law—between the cost of the fuel and the escalation thereof, and the cost 16 of reprocessing fuel. Reprocessing is not necessarily the cost of producing the fuel in the first place although it is a commitment that anyone who produces and uses fuel eventually has to fulfil.

It seems to me that in that case, too, there was a lack of definition in the original agreement. Everyone knew that the fuel would have to be reprocessed. We should be told—if we can be told—whether reprocessing was included in the agreement.

There are also the disputed capital costs. The Minister will correct me if I am wrong, but I think that the provision was that the capital costs of Hunterston B would be taken into account up to the time when it was expected to be finished which was in 1975. In 1968, therefore, it was envisaged that it would be finished by 1975 and that the capital costs up till then would be taken into account and charged as part of the agreement.

One would not have had to be a particularly perspicacious prophet to decide, in 1968, that a guesstimate then of a completion date of 1975 was, to say the least, extremely unlikely to be fulfilled. After all, the completion of Hunterston A was delayed by an immense time by industrial disputes and so on, not to mention—as hon. Members have said this morning— that Magnox reactors had not been proved and were at the frontiers of technology.

Looking back, and being wise after the event, I am led by those three points to the conclusion that an agreement entered into on such an imprecise basis so long before it was to be in a current state—and this will continue for years yet—was, although all concerned had the best intentions and did the best they could, an extremely irresponsible piece of calculation at the time. I do not suggest that anyone should now be blamed for it, except that we are all partly to blame. But we must learn the lessons of that conclusion.

I want to raise one related matter with the Minister—the performance of Hunterston B. At our previous debate on this matter, on 20 July 1977, I asked about the corrosion that has been a feature of the Magnox reactors, and the arrangements made to deal with it at both Hunterston A and Hunterston B. The Minister could not answer at that time, but he kindly wrote to me on 8 August 1977, explaining the situation.

As the Committee knows, when Hunterston A came into commission, it was found JPEG 17 to be suffering from some corrosion within the reactor. As a result, the output of Hunterston A was considerably reduced to minimise the corrosion and to enable it to be monitored in the future. So far, so good, but at that time Hunterston B had been designed and was under construction. When the corrosion was discovered in Hunterston A, efforts were made to deal with the matter for Hunterston B.

The Minister of State's letter to me, dated 8 August 1977, made this clear. He said:

"The existence of this type of mild steel corrosion was recognised during the deisgn stage of the Advanced Gas-cooled Reactors at Hunterston B and appropriate modifications were made. It was subsequently found from laboratory tests, however, that somewhat similar corrosion could occur with alloyed steels at higher temperatures. "

I am not an engineer, still less a nuclear engineer. Neither am I a metallurgist. "from laboratory tests that somewhat similar corrosion could occur with alloyed steels at higher temperatures"

suggests to me some technical negligence. I am sure that these things are difficult to discover. But if the corrosion had been found ^within Hunterston A, and I were the expert responsible for trying to prevent the same trouble in Hunterston B, I would make sure that I did the laboratory tests on all the metals to be used on any part of the interior of the reactor very thoroughly before I allowed Hunterston B to go ahead.

A number of issues about the contract have arisen this morning. I am rather hesitant about going into all the fine details of the dispute becuase the Government are not involved. There is an explanation of why the charge for reprocessing should be made. It is a technical matter. I will give the explanation that I have been giving. Nuclear fuel differs from conventional fuels once used in electricity generating in that pure elements, once they go into the reactor remain there for several years. The South Board therefore operates an account which takes into consideration the actual and estimated future costs of replacing and discharging fuel for Hunterston B including the reprocessing. It aims to ensure that the operating charges for each year bear as equitable a share as possible of all fuel costs arising or expected to arise as a result of the operation of the station.

Spent fuel from Hunterston B is to be reprocessed by British Nuclear Fuels at the Windscale plant which is at present undergoing a major expansion. Although reprocessing on a signficant scale will not take place for some time the South Board is making charges on the ground that it believes—and I think this is right—that it would be unfair for reprocessing costs to be borne only by private consumers. So the charge for reprocessing made by the North Board to the British Aluminium Company—one which as I say has not been paid—relates to the tranche at Hunterston B assigned to the purposes of providing electricity for the smelter.
