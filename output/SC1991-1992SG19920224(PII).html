Mr. Dennis Canavan
(Falkirk, West)
: This debate is long overdue. It is interesting to recall that, not long ago, the Secretary of State for Scotland and other Ministers were telling us that the Scottish constitutional question was a non-issue and that people of Scotland were not interested in it. Times have certainly changed. The Prime Minister belatedly made a flying visit to Scotland in a desperate attempt to defend a status quo which even the Scottish CBI tells us is no longer tenable. All that the Prime Minister can say in response is that he will take stock of public opinion as expressed in the forthcoming general election.

The Prime Minister is at least 10 years too late. The real question facing the people of Scotland is not whether we want a Scottish Parliament, but when one will be established and how it will be established. Election after election and opinion poll after opinion poll have demonstrated that an overwhelming majority of the people in Scotland want a Scottish Parliament with legislative and economic powers.

How shall we deliver a Scottish Parliament? If we are to bring about such a radical constitutional change, it is only common sense to have the broad consensus of the Scottish people behind it. The Scottish Constitutional Convention has such a strength—its representation includes more than 80 per cent. of Scottish Members of Parliament and MEPs; more than 90 per cent. of Scottish local authorities; churches of various denominations; the Scottish trade union movement; and, despite what Ministers say, a useful input has been made to the convention by people experienced in Scottish business and commerce.

It is naive to imagine that we could deliver Scottish constitutional change of the nature that we seek without such broad support. I suspect that even the Government realise that, which is why they are trying to rubbish the SCC and trying to squeeze it out by polarising the debate between status quo and independence. If, by adopting such a strategy, the Government's aim is to save their own skins and to save the Union, they are playing with fire. The whole issue could backfire on them. They could risk the annihilation of their party, the Scottish Conservative and Unionist party, and that of the Union, which they claim to defend.

I have never argued that independence should not be considered as an option. If the Government ever offered me a choice between the status quo or independence, I would opt for independence. I would not be alone in so doing. Faced with such a decision, I believe that the majority of Scottish people would agree.

I have never argued that an independent Scotland could not be viable. The people of Scotland—indeed, the people JPEG 45 of any nation—are entitled to as much self-determination as they want. However, independence is not the only option: the home rule proposals of the Scottish Constitutional Convention are far more capable of achieving maximum unity and of delivering a Scottish Parliament in the near future.

It would be foolhardy for any political party to attempt to go it alone. I am sure that those realists within the Scottish National party do not believe, deep within their heart of hearts, that they will achieve a successful outcome at the forthcoming general election—that is, that they will win 37 seats out of the 72 seats. They do not really think that they will march victoriously along Princes street, occupy St. Andrew's house, remove the Union Jack and replace it with the saltire—and, hey presto, we shall all be free in 1993.

Life is more complex than that. I am sure that the realists within the Scottish National party know that, too. I am not making or expecting them—nobody is—to surrender their commitment to independence. However, I am urging them to face up to the realities of the position in which we find ourselves and to realise that what unites the people of Scotland on this issue is more important than what divides us. I ask them not to fall into the Tory trap of being divided and ruled by the Conservative party. Let us learn the lessons of the past.

Scotland is at the crossroads: it is facing an important general election on constitutional and other issues. The people of Scotland will soon be provided with a great opportunity. It would be tragic if that opportunity were lost because certain politicians, who claim to support a Scottish Parliament, decide for their selfish reasons or for those of their party that they would be better going it alone. They would be attempting to divide the people of Scotland when what we want is maximum unity. The convention's proposals, imperfect though they may be, are the best option available for uniting the Scottish people and for gaining maximum support for a Scottish Parliament. With the Labour victory at the forthcoming general election, the proposals will be turned into reality. I say to all members of the Committee who believe, in principle, in a Scottish Parliament that the convention proposals are worthy of our support.

The convention's proposals are fairly well-known by now and are ready to be put into action immediately after a Labour Government are elected to carry forward our plans for creating a Scottish Parliament. All the members of the convention agreed long ago that a Scottish Parliament would work normal day-time hours to a parliamentary calendar that matches the school holidays in Scotland. They agreed that childcare would be provided for elected Members and staff, and that help would be given for the care of other dependants. They agreed that Members would be full time and should not treat the great honour of being elected to the first Scottish Parliament for 300 years as a part-time interest in the time left over from more lucrative business pursuits, which is how many hon. Members treat their work at Westminster. It would be a full-time job and they would be paid a fair rate.

All those steps are necessary to ensure the highest possible participation by people whose responsibilities often preclude them from playing a more active role in their country's decision making. That exclusion is not only unfair to them individually; it silences the voices of those who need to be heard. The convention has unanimously agreed to those common-sense steps at the outset, while Westminster continues to deny us childcare facilities and to work hours that make family life impossible.

The choice is simple: open and accessible, or not; for all our people, or not. In the convention we have established a vital principle—no one should feel that a Scottish Parliament excludes them. We know that the Tories think that we have the best in the best of all status quo worlds. They are beginning to realise that a strain is being put on the Union, but they do not even listen to Tory women who tell them that an all-male Cabinet is not representative. As for the nationalists, we do not know what they think about these matters; they never utter a word about them. Perhaps today they will begin to do so.

The convention has agreed that steps need to be taken to ensure equality of representation for women. The reason is obvious. Just look around us—four women out of 72 is the highest proportion of women elected Members in Scotland that we have ever had. There have only ever been 21 women representing Scottish seats from all the JPEG 47 parties put together. We can either leave the matter to chance and end up with a Scottish Parliament with much the same composition as the Westminster Parliament, or we can seize this unique opportunity at the start to ensure equality of representation in a Scottish Parliament.

We have a strong commitment to nursery education and to childcare provision. We know the Tories' attitude to those issues. Far from improving the lot of working women, they have in many ways created the problems that Labour will have to put right.

How do the Tories see the future for women in Scotland under the status quo? The abolition of wages councils is easily foreseen, as well as cuts in health care standards and longer waiting lists for operations. There will be less chance of a second bite at training and education for women who have taken time out to rear their families. We can certainly foresee more children with asthma and bronchitis because the walls of their homes are running with damp. Perhaps the Scottish Nationalist party will break its self-imposed and uncharacteristic silence. I notice that Fiona Hyslop has recently become its Chief Security to the Treasury in waiting, following criticism that when the leader of the Scottish National party formed his Cabinet in waiting he managed to find only two women—he could hardly have left them out considering their position in politics.

If they agree with such policies—I am sure that they do because they are always so keen to present 48 their socialist credentials, at least in the west of Scotland—have they told Scottish capitalists and the Scottish restaurant and hotelkeepers who pay their work force low wages? Those people have got their share and they will be interested to know whether they can keep it.

Or is it one message for Govan and Garscadden and another for Banff and Buchan? Will the nationalists tell The Sun that it is not appropriate for a newspaper that supports a radical party to demean women? If they did, would The Sun take a blind bit of notice? No. The Sun has discovered that it sells far more newspapers in Scotland with its new nationalist packaging, but the basic product remains the same. Every trade unionist I know is aware of The Sun's record and wonders how a party can present itself with any credibility as being of the left when it has allowed itself to be connected with such a newspaper.

I have posed just a few of the more crucial questions that women would like to ask the nationalists and the Tories. I should like to have ranged more widely. As chair of the Scottish group I feel a duty to do so, but I also have a duty to use my time on behalf of the women of Scotland whose questions are ignored by the other parties that want to gain their support. It is high time that those parties told us where they stand on these issues. Perhaps we shall get some answers today.

We know the answer to one question. Women are supporting Labour. The latest poll of women voters put Labour six points ahead in the United Kingdom. They are not beguiled by Major's smile or by the Salmond smirk. They will vote Labour and get a Government who listen to women and include women.
