The whole object of the amendment and the new clause is to channel finance through a different method into the industry. It is, first, to deal with the effects of inflation. It is becoming more and more difficult, unless there is a more realistic pricing policy in farming, for sufficent profits to be generated by the enterprise to replace plant and equipment at new cost levels. Secondly, it is to deal with the whole problem of expansion, which we know is very dear to the Minister's heart. We have all read the White Paper " Food from Our Own Resources". That will require the generation of considerable amounts of capital which either will have to be provided by the industry itself, through a body such as the Agricultural Mortgage Corporation or, of course, through Government grants.

Thirdly—and by far the most important—is the whole problem of taxation to which these two pieces of evidence refer. It has not been sufficiently realised either by the industry itself—although it is now rapidly coming round to understanding what it all means—or by the Treasury—which is far more important A2 Image Col. 105 —what will happen when the full effects of capital transfer tax, on its own, begin to hit the industry. If there is to be a wealth tax—we know that it has been postponed for a year, but no one is under any illusions that it is the policy of the Government to bring it in at a later date—certainly agriculture cannot escape it. Its effects may be mitigated in one way or another, but certainly its effects on agriculture will be considerable. When those two taxes are added together, a farmer, or more particularly his successors in title, will be forced to sell parts of their farm in order to pay the tax. That is the point.

The method proposed in the amendment will allow a farmer to sell part of the interest in his farm but without selling the full title to it. Also—this is the most attractive part—it will give him an option to recover his title at a later date. The Government may say that that finance can be provided by the banks. We know that, under existing profit levels in farming, that is quite impossible because the banks will not lend on sufficiently long terms.
