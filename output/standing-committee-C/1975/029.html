"New Clause 11

DUTY TO NOTIFY COMPANY OF ACQUISITION ETC. OF VOTING SHARES

(1) In section 33 of the Act of 1967 (duty to notify company within fourteen days of acquisition etc. of shares amounting to one tenth or more of relevant share capital) for the words "one tenth" and "fourteen days" wherever they occur, there shall be respectively substituted the words "the prescribed percentage" and "three days".

(2) In the said section 33 as amended by subsection (1) above "the prescribed percentage" means 5 per cent. or such other percentage as may from time to time be prescribed for the purposes of that section by regulations made by the Secretary of State by statutory instrument, and different percentages may JPEG be so prescribed in relation to companies of different classes or descriptions.

(3) No regulations shall be made under subsection (2) above unless a draft of the instrument containing the regulations has been laid before Parliament and has been approved by resolution of each House of Parliament.

(4) In the case of a company which at the time when this section comes into operation, is one to which the said section 33 applies, every person who at that time is interested in shares comprised in relevant share capital of the company of a nominal value equal to 5 per cent. or more but less than 10 per cent. of the nominal value of that share capital shall be under an obligation to notify the company of the subsistence of his interests at that time and the number of shares comprised in that share capital (specifying it) in which each interest subsists at that time.

(5) Subsection (4) to (10) of the said section 33 (which relate to the enforcement and interpretation of that section) shall have effect in relation to subsection (4) above as they have effect in relation to subsection (2) of that section but as if in subsections (5) and (9) for references to three days there were substituted references to fourteen days and as if in subsection (5) for references to the prescribed percentage there were substituted references to 5 per cent.

(6) If regulations under subsection (2) above come into operation whereby the prescribed percentage for the purposes of the said section 33 is reduced, subsections (4) and (5) above shall apply in the case of a company in relation to which the regulations have effect as in the case there mentioned but with the substitution—

  1. for references to the time when this section comes into operation of references to the time when the regulations come into operation; and
  2. for references to 5 per cent. and 10 percent. of references to the reduced percentage and the percentage prior to the reduction.

(7) Neither this section nor any regulations made under subsection (2) above shall be construed as requiring the notification by any person of the occurrence of an event before the time when this section or the regulations come into operation; and subsection (1) above does not affect the time for fulfilling any obligation which arose before the time when this section comes into operation.

(8) For the avoidance of doubt it is hereby declared that where the relevant share capital of a company to which the said section 33 applies is divided into different classes of shares, references in that section and this section to a percentage of the nominal value of its relevant share capital are references to a percentage of the nominal value of the issued shares comprised in each of those classes taken separately.—[ Mr. Higgins]".

I do not believe that we have a sufficiently clear etiquette for the functioning of two-tier boards to be able to introduce them by means of legislation with any reasonable hope of success.

In the series of Companies' Bills that I have sought to introduce in the past I have tried to give more strength to the non-executive directors who are, in an embyronic form, the supervisory boards of the future. At present, although most large companies have non-executive directors, they are not given enough standing or authority to exert themselves when they see that something is going wrong. A non-executive director who is upset about something that is happening may resign—which is probably a too drastic step if he attracts attention to his resignation, and a futile step if he does not.

He may raise his point with the board, the managing director or the chairman of the company, but he will probably find himself in the minority. The reason why it is difficult to find strong and businesslike characters to accept the posts of non-executive directors is that the function that they are able to carry out in all too many companies is too weak and indecisive.

Apart from the non-executive directors there is in British law a supervisory element in the form of the auditor. Many people are amazed to discover that it was only with the passing of the 1948 Act that it became necessary for auditors to be qualified. The profession of auditing, which has been brought to a fine pitch of expertise, is of relatively recent growth. We need to add to the auditors and the non-executive directors something which will slowly take the shape of a supervisory board. I suggest that the non-executive directors and auditors should work together. That is a far more fruitful way of enabling the supervisory elements to become more effective.

In drafting the amendments I owed a great deal to the Institute of Internal Auditors, which provided me with a considerable volume of detailed and help- JPEG 49 ful information about the way in which the audit committees operate in North America, and especially in Ontario. About 10 years ago pioneering legislation was introduced into Ontario. I think it should be studied closely by the Department. That legislation has been so successful that it is being copied in other Canadian Provinces. It forms the basis of the practice in the United States.

I refer to the practice of requiring large companies to set up sub-committees of non-executive directors, that is to say subcommittees of the boards consisting of non-executive directors—with the special function of collaborating with the auditors in looking at the health of the company and bringing to the attention of the board points about which the auditors feel uneasy. By that means we are strengthening the supervisory function, but we are not doing so by setting up an upper tier. We are using a sub-committee of the existing board.

I do not think that it matters whether the supervisory function is exercised by an upper board on the German pattern or by a sub-committee of a unitary board in the way which has been so successful in North America. It is all-important that the supervisory function should be carried out effectively and that the people who engage in it should be able to make their voices heard.

If we are to be able to make our voice heard in the event of a dispute, it is necessary to refer to an authority outside the board room. The natural authority is the shareholders gathered at a general meeting. I have learnt a good deal from the reactions of directors of companies to the proposals that I included in my Companies' Bill. I proposed that the non-executive directors should be obliged to make a return to the shareholders, stating, in very much the same way as auditors, whether they have confidence in the management. That is to some, evidently, a frightening proposal. Many highly competent people with significant experience of the way in which joint stock companies operate are afraid of what might happen if non-executive directors were required to set themselves up as a board within a board, although still sitting round the same table as the executive directors.

In the proposals in new Clause 1 I suggest that the audit committee should 50 report to the board and not to the shareholders. The vestige which remains of my original proposal that supervisory elements should report directly to the shareholders is a much weaker version, to the effect that the audit directors should state whether they consider that the board has given due consideration to their findings.

There is plenty of room for debate as to the way in which an audit committee might function and the way in which it should report to the board and the shareholders. My formula offers a working basis for discussion. I hope that it will provoke interest among the Committee as to ways in which we should gradually move towards the fully-grown two-tier board.
