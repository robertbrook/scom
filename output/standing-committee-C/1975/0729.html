New Clause 18
REGISTER OF DISQUALIFICATION ORDERS

(1) The prescribed officer of any court which—

  1. makes an order, after the coming into operation of this section, that a person shall not, without the leave of the court, be a director of or in any way, whether directly or indirectly, be concerned or take part in the management of a company for such period as may be specified in the order or
  2. grants leave in relation to any such order which is so made,
shall, at such time and in such manner and form as may be prescribed, furnish the Secretary of State with the prescribed particulars of the order or grant of leave.

This subsection applies whether the order is made under section 188 of the Act of 1948, section 9 of the Insolvency Act 1976 or section 21 above.

(2) The Secretary of State shall, from the particulars with which he is furnished under subsection (1) above, maintain a register of such orders and of cases in which the court has granted leave.

(3) On the expiration of an order of which particulars are entered on the register, the Secretary of State shall delete from the register—

  1. those particulars, and
  2. any particulars of cases in which the court has granted leave in relation to that order.

(4) The register shall be open to inspection on payment of such fee as may be specified by the Secretary of State in regulations made by Statutory instrument.

(5) A statutory instrument containing regulations made under subsection (4) above shall be subject to annulment in pursuance of a resolution of either House of Parliament.'.—(Mr. Clinton Davis.)

The present situation of par value shares leads to a great deal of misunderstanding, especially among the public who subscribe to the shares.

At the moment in the case of a rights issue, we often read in a public document that the rights issue is being made of shares with a nominal value of 21/2p, but they are being issued perhaps at 125p when the market price is 150p. The only fact that may concern the potential investor at that time is the 10 per cent. or 15 per cent. discount below market value. To someone who is not accustomed to the jargon, it is reasonable for him to ask what is this business about a nominal value of 21/2p when he is asked to subscribe to shares at 125p. This misunderstanding becomes even greater when we consider the placing of shares in a new company, perhaps raising venture capital for oil drilling in the North Sea. Under such circumstances it is normal to issue shares for part payments. The situation may arise where shares are issued for 100p part value and the issue price is 125p. But the first part payment is 30p. The merchant banker, or whoever is behind the issue, puts in the issue document that the first part payment is to be 30p and, of that, 25p is part payment of nominal value and 5p is part payment of premium. Although I have been concerned in at least one such issue, I have never understood exactly how those behind the scenes arrived at the distinction between the part payment of nominal value and the part payment of premium. It is done as a purely arithmetical exercise so that at the end of the day the whole of the 125p—that is the issue price—will be subscribed. To the laymen it is confusing, indeed incomprehensible, to know why it is a 30p initial payment, of which 25p is the part payment of nominal value and 5p the part payment of share premium. Therefore there is a genuine background of misunderstanding and confusion which at the moment surrounds the whole issue of nominal values.

The question of the issue of shares of no par value has a substantial history in this country. Twenty years ago the Cohen Committee reported that: 498

"While there is, in our view much logic in the arguments in favour"

 of the issue of no par-values,

"there is considerable opposition … This class class of share has given an opportunity to the unscrupulous to manipulate accounts. We therefore refrain from recommending any change."

 That was the verdict of the Cohen Committee.

Subsequently in 1954 a committee under the chairmanship of Mr. Montague L. Gedge, Q. C. reported that the abuses to which the Cohen Committee referred formed part, in its judgment, of the early history of the system in the United States, but that they had long since been overcome.

Subsequently the Jenkins Committee looked at this question in June 1962. Although it did not consider that there was an immediate call for the issue of no par value shares, it took much advice and received many comments on this issue. I should like to read one or two of those comments.

The Society of Investment Analyists gave its evidence as follows:

"This Society can see many advantages of a purely practicat nature in favour of no par value shares—furthermore it would welcome any step which would prevent unsophisticated investors from deludipg themselves that the par value of a share had any bearing on its true worth."

 I believe that is an important point. Many people believe that the par value of a share has a bearing on its true worth. It does not. It is simply a balance sheet symbol.

The Association of British Chambers of Commerce, which also reported to the Jenkins Committee, said:

"Law should permit the issue of no par value shares but not insist that companies adopt the method."

 The Council of Associated Stock Exchanges was in favour of shares of no par value. The Chartered Institute of Secretaries gave evidence and said that it was desirable to amend the Companies Act 1948 so as to permit this. Therefore, at that time there was a substantial body of professional opinion building up in favour of no par value shares. Nothing has happened in the meantime.

I should like to summarise in practical terms the advantages of no par value shares as I see them. They represent a share of what is genuinely a fraction of the equity and they do not impart a notional total value. The par value of a share has JPEG 499 no bearing whatsoever on its true worth. No par value shares make it impossible to relate the dividend to a nominal capital and thus they avoid the source of misunderstanding and misrepresentation. I shall return to that point in dealing with the objections to no par-value shares.

I believe that with no par value shares, the company's distribution policy and the shareholders return on the money they have put into the company, will be seen in a much clearer prospective than in a situation of par value shares. It is the capital employed—and not the paid-up share capital—which is the true capital of the undertaking.

The shares of no par value would in certain cases facilitate the raising of additional capital. I should like to elaborate on the last point. Under Section 57 of the Companies Act 1948 the issue of shares at a discount below their par value is so hedged round with conditions—notably the need to call a general meeting of the company and to obtain court sanction for that move—that few companies have attempted to do it. Mr. John Loveridge
: I beg to move, That the Clause be read a Second time.

This relates to an important omission in the law. I was surprised to find that omission. The new clause aims to ensure that an auditor of any company shall not have any financial interest in the affairs of that company.

This matter came to my attention when I read a letter in The Times of 26th July from Professor Edward Stamp, Director of the International Centre for Research in Accounting, University of Lancaster. He said:

"…auditors must be like Caesar's wife. The rule must be immaculate, placing auditors above the possibility of suspicion".

 He went on to say:

"…the rules introduced last year by the English institute are not good enough because they do not prohibit the auditors of public companies from holding a financial interest in their clients".

 That also applies to private or non-public companies.

In this country there is not even a requirement for the auditor to disclose the amount of his holding or its value. I contrast that with the situation in Canada, where the Corporations Act makes the holding of shares, or a stake in the equity of a company, illegal. In the United States, the larger firms are prohibited by the rules of the Security Exchange Commission from holding shares in their clients' firms, for firms with stock exchange dealings and for any other firms with over $300,000 of debentures outstanding.

Further examples show that the rules of American Institute of Certified Public Accountants prohibit such holdings under Rule 101 of its code of ethics. Those tight rules help to ensure the probity of the auditors.
