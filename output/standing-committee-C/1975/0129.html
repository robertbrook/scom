I turn to some of the miscellaneous points made by the hon. Members for Westmorland and Holland with Boston (Mr. Body), beginning with the treatment of male hops on common land. Common land is no different from other land so far as occupancy is concerned. All common land is or can be "occupied" no matter what rights exist upon it. The occupier may be, say, the local authority, the National Trust or a private individual. They are responsible for any hops on their land and only they or the Minister in the exercise of his powers under the clause would have any right to remove male hops.

"the Minister may serve a notice … requiring him"

the occupier—

"to take … such steps for the removal treatment or destruction … for the purpose of preventing those plants from becoming or remaining an actual or potential cause of pollination in female hop plants".

Clearly, if for genetic or other special reason there is no risk of pollination, no notice need be served, but, in order to establish this, adequate powers of inspection must be provided.

Finally, I must return to the repeated suggestion of the hon. Member for Holland with Boston that the clause would make it an offence to grow male hons. That is not so. There are two offences under the provisions of the clause. Subsection (7) makes it an offence not to comply with a notice served by a properly authorised person. Subsection (10 makes it an offence to obstruct an official in execution of the powers given to the Minister. These are principles which are well established in legislation.

Mr. Strang
: The clause, relating to the power to obtain agricultural statistics, does not increase the burden of form-filling on farmers. It is a tidying-up operation and has been included in this Bill for three reasons.

First, the 1975 EEC Survey on the Structure of Agricultural Holdings—Directive 75/108—required more extensive coverage of the area of woodland 354 and other land, including unutilised agricultural land, than was normal under the June agricultural census. Further EEC surveys proposed for 1977, 1979 and subsequently are expected to impose the same requirement.

Secondly, it would be extremely difficult to have a wider coverage in some years than in others. Furthermore, such a situation would not encourage the return of accurate statistics. Consequently, the two relevant questions on the June 1974 census form were expanded in preparation for the 1975 Survey.

Thirdly, doubts were subsequently raised, while the directive was awaiting approval by the Council of Ministers, that too wide an interpretation might have been placed on the relevant sections of the 1947 Act. Hence it was decided to seek amending legislation so that the issue could be put beyond all doubt.

That is the important point which some hon. Members have not appreciated, although I tried to make it clear on Second Reading. The questions are already on the form, but there is doubt about whether there is a statutory basis for them.
