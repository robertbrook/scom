Sir Brandon Rhys Williams
: I had spoken for some time when we ended on Tuesday. I want to hear the views of the Minister and other colleagues in the Committee rather than dilate at great length on the detailed amendments I have suggested in connection with the audit committee and so forth, but I will round off my remarks if I may be permitted to do so.

The faults in the Bill as it stands are that auditing is seen as too restrictive and too historical in the terms of data produced. The audit role in the Bill as it stands is contracted on to an even narrower professional basis at the very moment when it should be expanded and strengthened. In my series of amendments I am seeking to weld the two supervisory elements in the company, particularly large companies, into a more effective force, namely non-executive directors and the auditors, by drawing up a routine by which they would be obliged and enabled to work together in the audit committee.

The audit committee is not just a brainwave of the moment but arises from studies I have been able to make of the ways in which audit committees are becoming standard practice in many companies in Canada and America. I am concerned with the problem of not just setting up another committee but giving it authority.

54 Firstly, this committee needs to have more facts as a matter of routine. I will not elaborate further on that because I hope to have the opportunity of saying more about it in connection with one of my later amendments. Secondly, we have to give the audit committee a statutory function which enables it to exert discipline within the firm. The suggestions I have made are thrown out as a talking point. I suggest that the audit committee should have a responsibility to report in detail to the board on the matters which it has considered, most if not all of which will be highly confidential.

I would like to make my position clear on the question of disclosure. In some ways the 1967 Act went too far regarding disclosure. We are not going to do anything more useful about the relatively inefficient or complacent company. I am not talking about fraudulent and scandalous firms, but the inefficient and complacent firm. I do not think it will be cured by being obliged to publish more data. The question arises—after disclosure what happens if medium-sized and smallish companies publish a whole lot more data? Few people will have time to analyse the data, and if they do they may get their interpretation wrong. Publishing more information may give people outside the firm a scent of what needs to be done, but in all too many cases the firm disclosing information will weaken itself vis-è-vis its competitors without doing anything to strengthen those forces which may be operating within the business to make it more efficient.

We need to encourage both medium and large firms to produce more data within the organisation for current analysis and estimation of future trends by the boards. The audit committee is the natural place for current figures and future estimates to receive thorough-going consideration. When this has been done the board must be acquainted with the findings of the audit committee.

As to the shareholders, if one were not to give some relationship between the board and the shareholders—between the auditors and the non-executive directors on the one side, and the shareholders on the other—one would be setting up powerless bodies which, in the event of a dispute, would be defeated because inevitably they would be either too JPEG 55 junior or in the minority, and so not able to make themselves felt. So somewhere in the legislation one has to include for the supervisory elements a power which enables them to exert themselves within the company when they feel they must. Therefore I am suggesting tentatively that the non-executive directors who are members of the audit committee should be obliged to make a statement to the shareholders rather in the same way as auditors now have to make to the shareholders statements on the balance sheet, confirming that they consider that the data produced by the firm have been properly produced and that the board has given due consideration to their report.

If the non-executive directors are not satisfied with the data they are getting, and if they do not think that the reports they are making to the board are getting due consideration, they ought to resign anyway. I would like to embody in statute that they should have to make the statements as a matter of course. I do not envisage that it would often arise that the non-executive directors who are audit directors would wish to qualify their report, but it puts them in a position to say in the course of the year to their fellow directors " Unless you pay attention to our anxieties and the reports we are making about the future of the company and its profitability and so on, then we shall be in a position where we will have to qualify our report." That would enable them to exert themselves fruitfully inside the business without having to tear the whole thing apart and excite public attention at the first stage of uneasiness or disagreement within the management.

I would like to touch on the whole question of why it is necessary to legislate at all in this area. There are many people, particularly in business, who think that the House of Commons produces far too much legislation and is choking business by an almost inscrutable volume of new legislation which affects business life, sometimes favourably and sometimes unfavourably, and which often seems to be unnecessary and irrelevant. Here is the Member for Kensington producing volumes of new clauses affecting companies and the way they operate. Why is it necessary to legislate? That is a real point.

56 I do not think that we have time for the natural forces of competition and reform and the other pressures on management to work their way through the joint stock company and carry us into better practice just through the processes of discussion, argument and competition going on all the time. I look at the present state of the British economy. Of course one has to recognise that there are acting against efficiency in joint stock companies many pressures which have nothing to do with faults cf organisation or management, or the shortcomings of audit procedure or anything like that. We need to recognise that. quite apart from questions affecting the British economy from outside, our company law has been stagnant while in other countries a great deal of progress has been made. Therefore I do not think we can wait for natural processes. We need to hasten the introduction of better practice by legislation.

I am only attempting in these new clauses to catch the largest companies. I should explain to my colleagues who may think that it is an error on my part that on the Notice Paper there are two amendments which seem to be similar. I am not offering them as alternatives. One is not simply a redraft of the other. One is to deal with the major companies in the way I have suggested in Amendment 40, and the other, No. 41, with large companies which are somewhat smaller. It is a modest way I have suggested in Amendment 41. My colleagues can see precisely what I intend by studying my proposals and I do not think it is necessary for me to enlarge upon them.

The major companies are those which I have defined as having more than £10 million of assets in the balance sheets or employing more than 2,000 people. The large companies are those with over £5 million of turnover or revenues or £2 million capital, or employ 500 people or more. Even if we were legislating for all those companies we would still only be catching a relatively small part of private enterprise. Therefore we are not initiating a tremendously cumbrous procedure which would be too burdensome or costly for small firms to carry out. I suggest we should have different procedures for the largest companies and the large companies, because companies JPEG 57 will move in and out of these categories, and one does not want them to have in the year of contraction to fold up completely an apparatus they may have set up; equally, a rapidly expanding firm should not be asked to go straight into an elaborate procedure which goes far beyond anything which it has begun to do in the process of growth. That is why initially I have suggested a modest form for the audit committee which in the largest company should become a full procedure.

I recognise that many people will say that all good companies do something of this sort anyway There is a great deal of truth in that, but they perhaps do not do it in a profesional and thoroughgoing way. When one thinks of the arguments there are about data processing and the collation of current information and the ability of management to make long-term future projections we have to realise that we are still in a grey area. Legislation would help to focus the minds of professional people and management on their responsibility, and encourage firms which are only moderately profitable and teetering on the verge of unprofitability of inefficiency to seek out the best practice and hasten to implement it themselves. I ask hon. Members to contemplate what it would be like in a very large company if it did not collate routine data about its current affairs and make forward estimates of the future profitability of the business.

If we look at what might happen in a large firm which totally ignores the routines I have suggested we have to recognise that the proposals I am making are not far-reaching. The Bill as it stands seems to me to be quite wrong, in its approach. We have news this morning of the French Government's major innovations in company law based on the important Sudreau report and we know that there are many changes in America, Canada and elsewhere.

: The first speech made by the Under-Secretary in reply was one of objection in principle. It was only when he recognised the feelings of the Committee that he quickly tried to make a somersault.

We are trying to make clear the responsibility that already lies on the directors under company law. I find it astonishing that the Under-Secretary should not wish to identify himself with that motive.

The hon. Member for Luton, West, had one objection to the drafting of the amendment, which was that a trivial reason could be given for a director's not being present and, therefore, not signing. So be it. If a trivial reason were reported, all the more reason why the shareholders would not wish to see that director remain on the board. If it were stated on the balance sheet that a director did not sign the balance sheet because he was in Monte Carlo, or at a TUC Conferenee, it would be up to the stakeholders in the business to decide the following year whether they wished to reelect him to the board. Therefore, the amendment as drafted could result in great pressure on directors to make certain that they were present at the board mseting when they had to sign.

The main reason that the Under-Secretary found, at the last fence, for objecting to the amendment was that no sanctions were imposed by our amendment on directors who did not sign, or on the company which failed to follow the new subsection (4). Subsection (2) of Section 156 (2) says:

"Any accounts so annexed shall be approved by the board of directors before the balance sheet is signed on their behalf."

In that subsection there is no sanction, no penalty. In the drafting of subsection (4) we have followed the pattern of the existing subsection (2).

The Under-Secretary has put forward a mere device for not accepting the amendment. I heartily commend the amendment to the Committee, and suggest to my hon. Friends that we take the matter to a vote.
