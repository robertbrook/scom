The points that he made were of interest, but I ought to point out what I understand to be the effect of the amendment. If I am incorrect he will he able to intervene. As I understand it, the amendment would have the effect of allowing a sheriff to disburse moneys, which under the provisions of the Bankruptcy Act 1914 and the Companies Act 1948 he must retain for 14 days, if within those 14 days the relevant increased monetary limit comes into effect.

Section 41 (2) of the Bankruptcy Act 1914 obliges a sheriff under an execution in respect of a judgment for a sum exceeding £20 to retain for 14 days the proceeds of sale of goods or money paid in order to avoid the sale. He then has to pay those moneys, less his costs, to the Official Receiver or Trustee if within the 14 days notice is received of a bankruptcy petition against the debtor on which a receiving order is made. The Official Receiver or trustee is then at liberty to retain those proceeds against the execution creditor.

Section 326 (2) of the Companies Act 1948 imposes a somewhat similar obligation on the sheriff in situations affecting companies if within the 14 days notice is received of the presentation of a petition for winding up. The liquidator in those circumstances is able to retain 158 the proceeds against the execution creditor.

It is my view that it would be inequitable for an execution creditor under a judgment for a sum of between £20 and £250 to have the benefit of the execution against the trustee or liquidator in cases where the execution sale took place or money paid to avoid such sale was received before the coming into force of the increased limit. The amendment would have retrospective effect.

I gathered that the hon. and learned Gentleman was putting this as a probing amendment, and I hope that the explanation I have given will enable him to withdraw it.

It seems in principle to be inconsistent with the case that he argued on Second Reading, which was that the court should have the guidance spelled out clearly. I thought that the further implication was that it was preferable to do it in the Bill rather than by rules.

Mr. Percival
: I did not intend any such inference. Even if I had, I owe it to everybody concerned to reconsider whether that is the best way to do it. I do want the court to have guidance in the form of legislation passed by the House of Commons which is binding upon it. The only difference is whether it should be in the primary legislation—in the statute itself—or in a statutory instrument made under the statute—that is, 206 in secondary legislation. I do not think I am departing in any way from any principle that I enunciated then. If I am, I can only say that what I am saying now is my considered opinion at this stage, and many of us have given a good deal of thought to all these things since we first began to consider them.

My second reason for suggesting that Amendment No. 42 is preferable to Amendment No. 44 is this. A field like this, where we are all groping our way forward, is one in which we are most likely to say after six or 12 months, " Oh good heavens, whydid we not think of that when we started? What a pity we did not think of that and put it in." We must accept that that is a likely eventuality when we are doing the sort of thing that we are here.
