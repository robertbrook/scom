New Clause 7
OPERATION OF NOTICE TO QUIT GIVEN BY REASON OF DEATH OF TENANT

"(1) In subsection (2) of section 24 (restrictions on operation of notices to quit) of the Agricultural Holdings Act 1948—

  1. for paragraph ( g) (by virtue of which the consent of the Agricultural Land Tribunal to a notice to quit cannot be required under subsection (1) of that section if the notice is given within three months after, and by reason of, the death of the tenant with whom the contract of tenancy was made) there shall be substituted—
  2. '( g) there had died within three months before the date of the giving of the notice to quit a person who immediately before his death was the sole (or sole surviving) tenant under the contract of tenancy, and it is stated in the notice that it is given by reason of the matter aforesaid.'; and
  3. after that paragraph there shall be added—
  4. ' In paragraph ( g) of this subsection "tenant" does not include an executor, administrator, committee of the estate, trustee in bankruptcy or other person deriving title from a tenant by operation of law '.
  1. This section shall apply to any notice to quit given after the passing of this Act."—[ Mr. Strang.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Strang
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

All Members of the Committee have been sent brief notes on the seven clauses which together constitute what is to become Part H of the Bill, together with a summary of the family succession scheme. Furthermore, there have now been two debates on the Floor of the House in which the general JPEG 759 principles of family succession, and the arguments for and against legislation on the subject, have been thoroughly aired. I do not believe there should be any need, therefore, to go over them once again. So I propose, with permission, to describe the provisions of each clause as we come to it, but in rather more detail than in the notes in hon. Members' possession.

Before I start to describe the provisions of new Clause 7 perhaps I might be permitted to explain the layout of Part II very briefly. New Clause 7 is a scene setter in that it corrects a minor and only infrequently encountered anomaly in the 1948 Act. The anomaly has not mattered until now, but it is necessary to correct it in the context of the new scheme.

New Clause 8 mainly covers definitions of those persons to whom the scheme will and will not apply.

New Clauses 9, 10 and 11 are the main procedural clauses, leading up to a decision by the Agricultural Land Tribunal either that someone is to become the new tenant of the holding or that the landlord is to regain possession.

New Clause 12 deals with the promulgation of a decision by the tribunal that an applicant is to have a new tenancy of the holding, while new Clause 13 enables the landlord or the tenant to refer to arbitration the terms of the new tenancy agreement and/or a fresh rent if they are unable to reach agreement on one or other or both of them.

Mr. Wiggin
: On a point of order, Mr. Costain. Will the Minister read his brief more slowly? This is complex stuff and we cannot understand him.

Mr Strang
: I am merely summarising the content. Hon. Members should have read the notes we circulated.

Turning now to new Clause 7, as the Committee will know, under Section 24(2)(g) of the Agricultural Holdings Act 1948 a landlord can serve an incontestable notice to quit the holding on the death of his tenant, when that tenant is the person who originally entered into the contract of tenancy, but as the law now stands a tenant who is not debarred from doing so can assign his tenancy to somebody else and leave the holding. The land will then be farmed by the assignee who becomes the tenant. But when the original tenant 760 dies it is his death which allows the landlord to serve his notice to quit, and it is the assignee and his family who will have to quit the holding.

This new clause removes this anomaly, because the provisions of the family succession scheme should clearly take etfect on the death of the assignee and not of the original tenant. Moreover, it is the close relatives of the assignee, as occupier, who should be entitled to claim to succeed when the assignee dies, and not the close relative of the original tenant when he dies.

This amendment avoids the need for elaborate special provision for what is believed to be a very small number of cases. It removes from Section 24(2)(g) the reference to the death of the tenant with whom the contract of tenancy was made, which means the original tenant, and refers instead to the death of the sole or sole surviving tenant under the contract of tenancy, which means either the original tenant or the assignee if the tenancy has been assigned. So the landlord will in future serve his notice to quit on the death of the occupier of the holding whether he be the original tenant or the assignee.

The reference in the new provision to the

"sole (or sole surviving) tenant"

is required because of the possibility that a joint tenancy exists. In such a case the interest of a deceased joint tenant in the tenancy will vest in the remaining survivor or survivors till only one is left holding the tenancy on his own, when the provisions of the family succession scheme will apply. I should add that it is not possible for one of the joint tenants to assign his tenancy, so no confusion on this score is likely to arise.

Paragraph ( b) of the new clause is, I think self-explanatory. It ensures that the landlord cannot serve an incontestable notice to quit on the death of a sole executor or administrator of an estate.

Having given that explanation of the new clause, which is fairly technical and which does not bring us to the substance of the Government's family succession scheme, I commend it to the Committee.
