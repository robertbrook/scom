Motion made, and Question proposed,

That during the proceedings on the Agriculture (Miscellaneous Provisions) Bill the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock.—[Mr. Bishop.]

Mr. Michael Jopling
: Concerning the Sittings Motion moved by the Minister, I am sure that Opposition hon. Members see no objection to the arrangement proposed. However, at this stage of our deliberations it would be helpful if the Government told us their intentions. When I last appeared as a speaking member, if I may put it like that, of a Committee dealing with an Agriculture Bill, I remember getting into some difficulty with my Front Bench by saying that these Agriculture Bills, which have a habit of going to Committee at this time of year, were annual jamborees that lasted from muck-spreading to seed-time. I hope that our deliberations on this Bill will not last too long. There is not as much in this Bill as in past Agriculture Bills which lasted through many sittings.

The main factor governing the length of Committee time needed, however, depends on the Government's attitude to various amendments that might be put down. One amendment, tabled in the name of the hon. Member for Durham (Mr. Hughes) and supported by other Labour Members, proposes alterations to the landlord and tenant law, by seeking to amend the provisions of the Agricultural Holdings Act 1948.

The Government may feel that the Bill is a good and suitable vehicle for amending the Agricultural Holdings Act. I suggest that at this early stage it is important for us to know the Government's intentions. If over the next few weeks we are to have odd amendments or new clauses appearing on the Amendment Paper which will cause major changes in, say, the landlord and tenant law, I submit that we should know now. During this past week I know that a number of representations 4 have been made to us concerning other potential amendments or new clauses seeking to alter this sector of the law. If we are to use the Bill as a vehicle for amending this section of the law, a great many amendments will doubtless be tabled by hon. Members on both sides of the Committee.

I think that one of my hon. Friends is already aware of 20 such amendments. I am bound to say that I do not think that this would be a particularly fruitful or sensible exercise. if the Government embark on an exercise that will lead to many discussions about changing the form of the law in relation to landlord and tenant, then there ought to be a great deal of consultation and thought between the Government and interested parties.

I am aware that hon. Members opposite take a particular view over the matter on which there is a new clause on the Amendment Paper. Their new clause is in a form which would not be acceptable to the National Farmers' Union which started off this matter.

Mr. Keith Stainton
: Hereditary privilege!

Mr. Jopling
: My hon. Friend the Member for Sudbury and Woodbridge (Mr. Stainton) makes a comment and no doubt may wish to say something in a moment.

If we must embark on this exercise, it should not be done by amendments appearing from the Government back benches, from the Opposition back benches, or even from the back benches on both sides. It would be more sensible if, in view of the situation and in view of the ideas already in the heads of people outside this House, the Government told us their ideas for changing the law in relation to landlord and tenant. It is important that the Government tell us today their attitude to these matters. It would be best if the Government were to say that, apart from the four clauses which are already in the Bill which deal with harassment of tenants, they will resist any other changes to the landlord and tenant law.

I am not entering into the merits of Government back bench amendments or into the merits of any other amendments that may be tabled. But it would be a foolish and fruitless exercise if we were to try to amend a major part of the law JPEG 5 affecting the agricultural industry without wider consultation and preparation. I fear that, unless the Government are prepared to give an undertaking at this stage, the Bill might develop in this way, and we may find ourselves sitting for a great many more mornings in the weeks to come than would seem likely on this first morning of our consideration of this Bill.

Mr. Norman Buchan
: One knows the customary method of opening these Committees when general skirmishing takes place on the Sittings Motion. Like a game of chess, it is a pawn in advance, and it sometimes returns to its original position. There has been a difference in this case, however. I cannot recall a case ever being made in a debate on a Sittings Motion, seeking a comment or reply on amendments placed on the Amendment Paper before the Committee actually sat. It is a highly improper request for the hon. Member for Westmorland (Mr. Jopling) to make of the Minister presenting a Sittings Motion, pre-empting, as it does, the rights of the Committee to discuss any question on the Amendment Paper, or to make any such decision when the point is reached.

It is not only highly improper that the hon. Member has put it forward in this way, but it would be highly improper for the Minister to give an answer before the case had been deployed. For all I know, the Minister will wish to deploy the case for this amendment. It is not unusual or unknown for back bench amendments to be tabled with the full backing of the Government. It is not unknown, especially to the Opposition party, which one recalls once found a highly organised system of putting down Parliamentary Questions to be answered by the Minister. Therefore, it seems highly improper to do all of these things.

The hon. Member raised the question of taking from muck spreading to seed time. I do not know which seed he had in mind, but it sounded remarkably like rape. There are problems about the definition of rape. If there is a belief in the willing complicity of the partner, rape is no longer held to be an offence. In this case, for all we know there is a willingness on the part of the Government. Therefore, the hon. Gentleman's argument again would not hold water. Behind this attempt to rape from the Amendment 6 Paper an amendment which has been rightly put down there is an unacceptable motive.

I have known the hon. Gentleman for many years, and a nice gentle chap he is. But behind the gentleness there were certain teeth being shown. The teeth, of course, are the extraordinary proposition that, unless the Minister gives an answer on the amendment before it is due to be discussed, the Opposition will put down a whole series of amendments in order to prolong the time of the Committee.

This is very serious. This is a threat to the rights of the Committee and to the House of Commons. I have never before heard the proposition advanced that a decision must be made in advance of discussion. If that decision is given before the discussion, it is not only a denial of the right to reply; it is also a denial of free speech.

It seems to me, if not unparliamentary in the technical sense, certainly a highly improper method of proceeding that the hon. Gentleman should demand a rejection—this is what he is asking—not a reply, not an answer or a commitment, but one answer, namely, a rejection before a case has been put. If the rejection on the question relating to the clause on security of tenure is not made, the Opposition will put down JPEG 7 a number of unstated amendments, not dealing with the security of tenure of tenant farmers but on other topics. [Hon. Members:" How does the hon. Member know?] That is what is known as an intelligent guess.

The argument was that we should not bring in a measure of this dimension by amendment in the middle of a Committee. With respect, that is precisely what happened the last time that there was a discussion on the proposition of security of tenure. It was brought in as an amendment in the middle of a Committee stage on security of inheritance for the tenant farmer in Scotland. Not only was it brought in during the middle of a Committee stage but—since it was I who proposed it and the Committee was convinced by my argument—it was in fact carried among those who voted for the measure on exactly the same lines as this clause. Indeed, it was brought in later because it was not on the Notice Paper when the Committee started.

Therefore, if there were any merit at all in the hon. Member's argument, which in view of what happened there is not, it would be of less merit on that occasion.

However, it went through the Committee. No such arguments were deployed and among those who supported it was the present Minister of Agriculture.

How far can we go in demanding that a Minister of State answers on behalf of the Minister for Agriculture, who has himself voted for precisely this proposition in relation to one part of the United Kingdom? One may be all for devolution, but it is somewhat monstrous to allow one section of the community to profit from this measure, in the way proposed here, but not to allow other parts of the United Kingdom to do so.

I hope that the Minister of State will not succumb to these veiled threats, this kind of Loch Ness monster that we have seen appearing from the other side—this time wasting, this muck spreading disease. The hon. Gentleman is threatening to prolong the sitting right into the grain harvest. If there is any time wasting, it will not be by the Government side. We promise that when we come to this clause we shall make short speeches and have the measure passed within the day with no problems. We shall not waste time.
