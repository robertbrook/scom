Question again proposed.

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Gavin Strang)
: The Minister of State has to be elsewhere on important Government business, so I shall continue the remarks he was making in reply to the debate on Amendment No. 24.

I am sure the hon. Member for Holland with Boston (Mr. Body) will recognise that the amendment will have the effect of increasing the costs of designation and confuse those whose interests may be affected, since designation orders might be made for a number of contiguous areas. For example, if one decided to cover 27 square miles, a number of orders would be needed under this amendment, whereas it could be covered with one order, given the flexibility of the clause. Moreover, to protect female hops adequately at any one point could conceivably require a quite substantial area, equivalent to about 28 square miles.

The hon. Member for Holland with Boston expressed his concern and the fear of some hop growers that the clause could give the Minister powers to prevent the growing of male hops throughout the country without coming back to the House for further authority. I can assure him that these fears are unfounded. Subsection (4) provides that any order made under this clause will 298 be subject to negative resolution by either House. Moreover, subsections (2) and (3), taken together, provide for those concerned to make their views known.

The hon. Member expressed his understanding that the clause was intended to provide for the experimental growing of seedless hops. That is not so, as my right hon. Friend made clear in the Second Reading debate, when he said:

"… this is not an experiment but a commercial proposition".—[OFFICIAL REPORT, 1st December 1975; vol. 901, c. 1332.]

That is the light in which these proposed measures must be judged. The Hops Marketing Board has carried out experiments in the past but now feels, with many growers, that it would like to proceed, perhaps with caution, into commercial operation. Therefore, it sought our help, which is provided for in this clause, for the benefit of the industry generally.

Concern has been expressed about the effect of this measure upon the taste of beer. Male hops are not used in brewing. The taste of beer attributable to hops is dependent upon the variety used and is unaffected by the seed content. Some hop varieties are better grown seedless. and the growing of such varieties will be helped by this measure. Of course, other varieties will continue to he grown.

In the light of these remarks, I hope that the Opposition will feel able to withdraw the amendment.
