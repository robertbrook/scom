AGRICULTURE (MISCELLANEOUS PROVISIONS), BILL

Clause 15
RECOVERY OF FARM CAPITAL GRANTS MADE BY REFERENCE TO FALSE OR MISLEADING INFORMATION

Question again proposed.

The Minister of State for Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. E. S. Bishop)
: I was unable at our last sitting to complete my reply to the hon. Member for Holland with Boston (Mr. Body) about the coverage of our recovery power.

In c. 708 of the proceedings of our last sitting 1 reminded the Committee that there was a precedent for the situation in the Farm and Horticultural Development Regulations 1973. Indeed, we do not need to go further than Section 29 of the Agriculture Act 1970—the section which this clause amends—to find a precedent, which is what the hon. Member for Holland with Boston was asking for. We do not even need to go beyond the 1970 Act to find a precedent. This section gives the Minister the power to recover the whole of a farm capital grant where a false or misleading statement is made in connection with the initial application for approval of the proposed work. I hope that the hon. Member is following me.

Mr. Richard Body
: I am. I am listening very carefully.

Mr. Bishop
: This is a very important point.

Mr. Body

Mr. Bishop
: I make this point because it is crucial. We already have powers in the 712 1970 Act to proceed in this way in relation to the initial application. We are seeking to extend it to the other aspect where the falsity relates to the subsequent claim for payment of grant. No new principle is involved. The principle and the precedent is already before us.

I have explained that our usual practice will be to use the power only in cases where there has been a successful prosecution for fraud or false statement. We would also use it sometimes when no prosecution had taken place if there were ample grounds for believing that falsity had occurred—for example, following an admission of the applicant. Again, in a case where a claim involving a number of items was tainted, we might not seek to prosecute in respect of all of them. If the prosecution was successful we would consider that we had a right to recover grant paid on all the items in the claim. The extent to which other items were affected would be one of the questions which the Minister would wish to be covered by the report of the appointed person.

I should like to say a few words about that—it is the subject of this Amendment and Amendment No. 123. The amendments deal with the status of the inquiry to be set up when representations are made against the Minister's decision to recover grants under his various capital grant schemes where approved conditions were broken, the work badly done or the applicant had given false or misleading information. Basically, the matter to be considered at such hearings is, in the light of circumstances, the extent to which grant already paid can justifiably be recovered.

The inquiry will be essentially to determine the facts of the case having regard to all the relevant agricultural considerations. Matters of law are not involved. It would therefore be inappropriate to set up a legal type inquiry, as is implicit in the amendments, especially as the " appointed person " is called on not to make a decision, but to report the facts to the Minister with, if he so wishes, a recommendation.

Moreover, it seems to me that the appointment of a person to hold such an inquiry should rest with the Minister charged with the responsibility for administering all matters relating to the grant schemes involved and to whom the report will be addressed.

As Section 29(4) of the Agriculture Act 1970 now stands Ministers have the power to recover the whole grant only where a false or misleading statement has been made in connection with the initial application for approval of the proposed work and not where the falsity relates to the subsequent claim for payment of grant.

"the person by whom the application for that approval or for the making of any payment was made gave information on any matter relevant to the giving of the approval or for the making of the payment which was false or misleading in a material respect."

This merely gives power to the Minister on the second part of the approval procedure as well as the first part.

New Clause 6
LETTING OF AGRICULTURAL LAND

"Where any land is let to a person for 746 use as agricultural la-id for a term of seven years or less, and before the agreement was entered into the letting was approved by the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food, the letting shall terminate on the expiration of the term of years for which it was granted notwithstanding the provisions of section 3 cf the Agricultural Holdings Act 1948".[ Mr. Wiggin.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Wiggin
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

12.15 p.m.

This clause is designed to enable land to be let for a short period—in proper cases, for more than one year—with the Minister's approval. I should tell the hon. Member for Renfrewshire, West (Mr. Buchan) that in no circumstances can this new clause be interpreted as not benefiting both tenant and landlord equally. There is no case for suggesting that this is a biased new clause, but it deliberately breaches the existing law—I make no bones about that—for a matter of mutual convenience.

At present a letting, or a licence to occupy, agricultural land either for a period of less than a year or for a term of two years or more, will not end at the expiration of the term but will be converted by the statutory magic of the Agricultural Holdings Act 1948 into a protected tenancy from year to year under the Act. An exception to this is provided by section 2 of the 1948 Act whereby the letting or licence is not converted into a protected tenancy if the Minister's approval is first obtained. Section 2 is so worded, however. that the Minister cannot approve a letting when it is a letting

"for an interest less than year to year",

though oddly he can approve a mere licence to occupy for a longer period, which is anomalous and unsatisfactory.

By section 3 of the 1948 Act a tenancy for a fixed term—that is a term of years—of two years or more shall not end automatically at the end of the fixed term, but will continue as a protected tenancy from year to year—that is, terminable by notice to quit, but only if and when the Act allows it.

I understand that some judicial rulings on this interpretation have left an area of uncertainty, but the purpose of the new clause is to allow greater flexibility in short-term situations.

JPEG 747 If the Minister is prepared to accept the spirit of the new clause, I should be prepared to accept an amendment to delete

"the Minister of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food"

and to put in the Crown Court, a magistrates' court or some other more readily available body which could judge from outside. It has been suggested that, because of the machinations of bureaucracy, the Minister may not be very quick to give his approval. I should be happy, if the principle is acceptable, to see a different new clause to the same effect—that, where tenant and landlord are in agreement on a term of years, but not more than seven—that would apply not to lifetime tenancies, but to some odd field or smallholding leading to the long-term reorganisation of an estate—they could both go to the court or to the Minister to approve the agreement. I think that, although it breaches the law, it is a reasonable proposition which will be to the mutual benefit of all affected parties.
