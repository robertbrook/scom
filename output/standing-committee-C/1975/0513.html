The Under-Secretary of State for Trade (Mr. Clinton Davis)
: At the last sitting I was anxious to put on record, for the benefit both of the Opposition and of people outside the House who are interested in the Bill, the Government's thinking about the issues contained essentially in New Clause 4. It may be helpful, particularly in view of the motion which I propose to move later—which will enable the Committee to stand adjourned so that further consultation can take place with the Opposition and the professional bodies—if I were to enlarge upon what I said last time and cover some of the other matters which we are considering in this group of amendments.

Having stated the purpose of New Clause 4 and having gone through the 264 various subsections, may I turn to the question of the automatic review procedure which is essentially set out in Clause 6.

The first point I want to make is that this procedure is additional to the existing procedure for discharge. Therefore, before the five years has expired from the date of the order of adjucation, the bankrupt will still be able to apply for his discharge under the existing procedure. It follows that, where the court has made an order under new Clause 4, that is to say for absolute discharge on the fifth anniversary of the order of adjucation, unless the order is subsequently rescinded it will be necessary to provide for a review for the purpose of discharge. Hence we have Amendment No. 52 about which I need say nothing more.

Under Clause 6, the Official Receiver will be obliged to apply to the court to review an order of adjucation for the purpose of discharge within 12 months after the fifth anniversary of the date of the order. At present, something like one JPEG 265 in three bankrupts apply for discharge. Therefore, on average, two-thirds of all the orders of adjucation made in any one year will fall to be reviewed within the appropriate 12-month period. The court will have to fix a day for hearing the application to review, and a notice will have to be given only to the bankrupt not less than 14 days before the hearing. n that regard the situation will be somewhat different from the application for discharge where a notice has to be sent to the creditors, although I would add in parentheses that in such cases—that is to say, applications for discharge—the creditor is constrained in his opposition because he is limited to the grounds set out in the Official Receiver's report, unless the creditor has given notice in writing that he wishes to enlarge upon that.

For the purposes of review after five years we thought it unlikely that creditors would have grounds of opposition additional to those of which the Official Receiver would be aware and which would therefore be included in his report. So we took the view that the expense of sending out notices to creditors was, in those circumstances, without justification.

The court will also be obliged under Clause 6(4) to take into consideration on the application for review, the Official Receiver's report on the bankrupt's conduct and affairs. As is the case in existing law, the report represents prima facie evidence of the statements contained in it.

10.45 a.m.

The other important feature of Clause 6(5) is that the court will be able to hear an application to review, even in the absence of the bankrupt. The reason for that is not to cause any injustice. The situation that is likely to arise in a number of cases is that the bankrupt may simply elect not to attend the hearing because the application is to be made by the Official Receiver. The bankrupt will, of course, receive notice, but he may decide not to attend. Our intention is that all bankruptcies coming within the scope of Clause 6 should be reviewed, and therefore this provision is essential.

The powers provided by subsection (6) are different from those which the court now has when dealing with an application for discharge. Under Section 26 of the 1914 Act, the court has the general power to 266

"grant or refuse an absolute order of discharge or suspend the operation of the order for a specified time or grant an order of discharge".

subject to conditions, relating perhaps to after-acquired earnings, or income for profit. If the bankrupt committed a misdemeanour in relation to his bankruptcy or any of the " facts " under Section 26 are found proved, the court must refuse a discharge or suspend for such time as it thinks appropriate, or until a dividend of at least 50p in the pound is paid to creditors, or require that the bankrupt, as a condition of discharge, consents to judgment.

Under the new provisions on the application for review the court's power will be the same for all bankruptcies. Consequently, it may grant or refuse an absolute order for discharge. It may suspend for such a period as it thinks fit, or until a dividend of 50p in the pound is paid to creditors, or it may require the bankrupt to consent to judgment as a condition of discharge.

Therefore the court will be able to grant an absolute order of discharge in any case, if it thinks it appropriate so to do. But it will have no power to attach conditions other than those under the second and third points which I have just made—that is to say, suspending for such period as the court thinks fit, which will be when a dividend of 50p in the pound is paid to creditors, or requiring the bankrupt to consent to judgment as a condition of discharge.

Nor will the court have power to impose different conditions concurrently as under the existing law. These limitations are necessary in the case of application to review, since five years will have elapsed since the order of a judication will have been made. The bankrupt is not making the application. The review is being undertaken automatically and will often be heard in the absence of the bankrupt.

Lastly, ar far as this aspect of the matter is concerned, the new provisions would enable some of the burden that now falls upon the judges to be eased by empowering registrars to hear all applications for review, whether or not they are opposed. Of course, at present only a judge may hear the opposed applications.

I turn to the Opposition's New Clause 3, which provides for automatic discharge five years after the conclusion of the public examination, unless the bankrupt has already obtained his discharge, or remains JPEG 267 undischarged from an earlier bankruptcy, or the court has entered a caveat preventing automatic discharge from becoming effective.

Clearly, this seeks in most material respects to follow the Blagden Committee representations, save that Blagden spoke of two years and the Opposition amendment refers to five.

On that position the Opposition have tabled another amendment to remedy a defect which appeared in their earlier proposals.

We considered very carefully what Blagden had to say. n a review of the position leading up to a Bill of this kind it is important that not only my officials and those outside the House who are interested in the matter, but also the Opposition, should pay due regard to an important report of this kind. But we should have regard to some of the practical difficulties which may not have emerged in the debate outside the House thus far. There would be immensely difficult decisions confronting both the Official Receiver and the court about whether to make application for a caveat on the one hand, or the court should enter the caveat on the other. The amendment provides that a caveat may be applied for or entered either on the adjournment for the closing of the public examination or at any time during the five years.

I want to deal first with the situation on the adjournment for the closing of the public examination. At that point, the only evidence that will be before the court is that which will have been given orally at the public examination, whereas on a normal application for discharge the evidence is summarised by the Official Receiver in his report. When one has regard to the sort of case that might be expected to arise, in which the public examination will still be held following the power to dispense under Clause 5, it is unlikely that either the Official Receiver or the court could decide this issue on the oral evidence. I think that it would be necessary for the public examination transcript to be made available.

This would be necessary for the Official Receiver, because he would have to determine whether to apply for the entry of the caveat It would be equally important for the court, in determining whether a caveat should be entered.

How could the court predict, in the 268 more difficult cases, what it might wish to do five years ahead? How could the Official Receiver predict what the court might do in such circumstances? The Official Receiver, because he entertained some doubts about the matter, might feel driven to make an application. n those circumstances, is it not almost certain that the application would be faced with an appeal against it?

In nearly every case the Official Receiver would have to wait for the transcript of the public examination and then prepare a report before deciding whether to apply for the caveat to be entered. He would be required to give notice to the debtor, who would probably oppose the application, and a full court hearing would follow.

If, on the other hand, the Official Receiver decided that he did not wish to apply for a caveat, is it not right that creditors might feel that they should be entitled to challenge that decision? Would that not result in another court hearing?

The amendment also provides for a creditor who has proof to apply at the stage about which I am talking for a caveat to be entered, even though the Official Receiver or the court would hardly be in a position to decide on the issue of the caveat. That might well represent a charter for vindictive creditors. In these circumstances the court would have virtually no alternative but to adjourn for the transcript to become available and the Official Receiver's report to be prepared. Therefore the burden on the Official Receiver and the court would be substantially increased.

The automatic discharge procedure, as set out in the amendment, is likely therefore to be more cumbersome, time-consuming and costly than the review procedure which we have set out in Clause 6. The same purposes can be achieved under New Clause 4 without the disadvantages of New Clause 3.

The differences between the two are fundamentally these. First, although the court is given a power in New Clause 4, when making an order including the public examination or dispensing with it, to order also that the bankrupt be given an absolute discharge on the fifth anniversary of the order of adjudication, there is no burden placed on the Official Receiver. The advice I have been given in this respect is probably right. It is a burden JPEG 269 that should not have to be borne by the Official Receiver.

Secondly, under New Clause 4 it is likely that the court would make an order for automatic discharge only when there is no evidence of gross misconduct, that is to say in the more straightforward cases. That represents a much simpler task than that imposed under New Clause 3, which requires the court to decide on detailed and lengthy oral evidence whether the caveat should be entered.

Thirdly, under New Clause 3 where the caveat is entered, the bankrupt is essentially left to his own resources, since the only way in which he then will be able to procure his discharge is to apply under New Clause 3, replacing Section 26 of the 1914 Act. If he fails to apply, he remains a bankrupt. Therefore there is the same old problem, to which I have referred earlier in the debate, of the standing population of undischarged bankrupts. This would perhaps be a prolongation of that problem.

Under Clause 6 and New Clause 4 every bankruptcy will be reviewed if no automatic discharge is granted or the order of adjudication is not otherwise disposed of. That sets out our case in relation to the substance of the matter.

The Opposition have tabled a number of amendments to New Clause 4. It would be helpful if I put on the record the Government's view of them. I feel, however, that I would wish to have a further look at the points that are made, because the amendments appeared rather late in the day. I blame nobody for this.

Amendment ( a) to New Clause 4 would have the effect of requiring a court, when considering whether to make an order directing that the bankrupt shall have an absolute discharge on the fifth anniversary of the date of the adjudication, as is provided for in New Clause 4, to have particular regard to the answers given by the debtor at his public examination instead of having particular regard to the criteria which have been provided in the amendment to New Clause 4, namely any facts stated in Section 26(3) of the 1914 Act or whether the bankrupt has committed any offence under that Act or any other events connected with his bankruptcy.

11 a.m.

It would also make it mandatory for the court—instead of providing it with a 270 discretion as we do under the amendment to New Clause 4—when it makes an order dispensing with the debtor's public examination, to make an order directing that the bankrupt should have an absolute discharge on the fifth anniversary of the date of his adjudication providing that the requirements of subsection (2) have been complied with.

Amendment ( b) to New Clause 4 would have the effect of requiring the court at the time it makes an order concluding public examination or an order dispensing with it—when it is considering whether to make an order directing that the bankrupt shall have an absolute discharge under the provisions of amendment to New Clause 4—to have particular regard to the answers given by the debtor at his public examination or in the report of the Official Receiver on the bankrupt's conduct and affairs in cases where the public examination has been dispensed with, instead of having particular regard to the two criteria provided in New Clause 4.

The terms introduced by the amendments could be said to lack the degree of precision of the two criteria which are laid down in New Clause 4. They might lead to a state of uncertainty and increase the variation of treatment of similar cases by different courts.

I do not make too much of these points, because something might also be said along the same lines about the terms set out in New Clause 4. I do not think there is any essential difference between the two propositions in practice. I cannot see any reason, although I shall wait to hear from the hon. and learned Member for Southport (Mr. Percival) when we are discussing this later, why his solution and his form of words should be preferred to those which appear in New Clause 4.

Where the court makes an order dispensing with the public examination under the provisions of Clause 5, it is very probable that it would also make an order directing that the bankrupt be automatically discharged under New Clause 4. I think it would be undesirable to make this latter order mandatory as is provided in Amendment ( a)to New Clause 4, in other words to remove every possible discretion from the court, even if the situation is an improbable one in practice. The public examination may have been dispensed with under the existing provisions of JPEG 271 Section 15(10) of the Bankruptcy Act 1914 because the debtor was unfit to attend. The circumstances of the case, however, may be such that an order directing automatic discharge might be inappropriate and I would ask the Opposition to take that point into account in due course.

Amendment( b) to New Clause 4, insofar as it provides for the court to have particular regard to the report of the Official Receiver about the bankrupt's conduct of affairs in cases where the public examination has been dispensed with, is unnecessary, except in cases where the order to dispense has been made on the application of a friend or relative of the bankrupt on the ground that he is unfit to attend. n all other cases the Official Receiver will already have filed such a report for the purposes of his application to dispense with the public examination. All the relevant circumstances of the case to which the court will have to have regard will be included in his report. I would add in this respect that it may help the Committee to know that it is intended that the rules will be amended to provide for the making of such a report and for it to be prima facie evidence of the statements which are contained in it.

Amendment ( c) to New Clause 4 would have the effect of preventing an order made by the court directing that the bankrupt be absolutely discharged on the fifth anniversary of the date of adjudication from becoming operative if the bankrupt has applied for his discharge before the fifth anniversary.

Under the provisions of New Clause 4 the longest time that a bankrupt is able to remain undischarged, once the court has made an order directing that he be given an automatic discharge and that order has not been rescinded, is five years. He can, of course, obtain his discharge earlier if he applies for it and is granted it under Section 26 of the Bankruptcy Act. But if he applies for his discharge and the court refuses the discharge or suspends it for such time as would extend beyond five years—these are both somewhat unlikely events—the order for automatic discharge would still operate and the discharge would still be effective on the fifth anniversary of the date of the adjudication.

Under the amendment it would be possible for the bankrupt, notwithstanding the fact that an order may have been made directing that he be given an automatic 272 discharge, to remain undischarged for longer than five years indeed, even for life, because if he applies for his discharge during the five years an automatic discharge order ceases to operate. Accordingly, if on the bankrupt's application under Section 26, the court suspends the discharge—so that it does not become absolute until after the five years have expired—the bankrupt would remain undischarged for that extra time.

Similarly, if the court, on the bankrupt's application, refuses the discharge, he would form part of this new standing population of undischarged bankrupts to which I referred earlier in another context.

As it is unlikely that the court would, on a bankrupt's application under Section 26, either refuse the discharge or suspend it beyond the five-year period in cases where an order had earlier been made directing that the bankrupt be automatically discharged, the amendment is unlikely in practice to have any real effect. In all other cases—that is to say, where the bankrupt applies for and is granted earlier discharge than he would have had under the order for automatic discharge, the effect is the same as under new Clause 4 and this amendment.

I turn to the penultimate amendment in this group—Amendment ( d) to new Clause 4—which would have the effect of restricting the category of persons who have been adjudged bankrupt more than five years before the coming into force of the clause not having had the adjudication now—and who would be granted an absolute discharge on the relevant date, that is the coming into force of the clause or the tenth anniversary of the date of adjudication, whichever is the later. The group is composed of bankrupts who have not applied for discharge before the relevant date; bankrupts who have applied for and were granted an absolute discharge; and bankrupts who have applied for and were granted the conditional order of discharge providing that order was made more than five years before the relevant date.

I would say in relation to that, first, that under new Clause 4 all bankrupts having been adjudged bankrupt more than five years before the coming into force of the clause, not having had the adjudication annulled and not having been granted the discharge which had therefore become effective, will be granted an absolute discharge on the relevant date, that is JPEG 273 when the clause comes into force or on the tenth anniversary of the date of adjudication whichever is the later.

Precisely the same result would flow from the amendment to New Clause 4, in so far as the effects in the first of two categories to which I have already alluded are concerned. That is fairly clear. The result would also be the same in so far as the third category is concerned—that is bankrupts who have applied for and been granted a conditional order of discharge, providing the order was made more than five years before the material date. That would be the case irrespective of what conditions the court might have attached to the discharge granted on that application.

If the bankrupt had applied for his discharge within the five years preceding the relevant date the automatic discharge provision would not apply. This is the only difference which the amendment would make. It might have some unsatisfactory consequences. or indeed, even curious ones. For example, if a bankrupt applied for a discharge five years and one day before the relevant date, and had the discharge granted, but suspended until the dividend of 50p in the pound had been paid, he would automatically be discharged on the relevant date, notwithstanding that the condition had not been fulfilled. If, however, he had applied for his discharge five years less a day before the relevant date, and the same order had been made, the automatic discharge procedure would not apply. He would remain undischarged until the condition was fulfilled.

It would be even more curious, perhaps, if a bankrupt had applied for his discharge within the five years preceding the relevant date, and had been refused. He would automatically be discharged from the relevant date because he had applied and no absolute order of discharge had been made. Such a bankrupt would therefore be in a better position than one who had applied at the same time, and had his discharge granted subject to a condition which had not been fulfilled by the relevant date.

Finally, Amendment ( a) to New Clause 3, is to remedy the drafting error which appeared in New Clause 3 and to which I referred on 11 th May. I do not think I need to add anything to that.
