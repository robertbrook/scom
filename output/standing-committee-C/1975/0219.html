The clause is perhaps more important than a casual glance may suggest, because it imports a new principle. The clause amends Section 29 of the Agriculture Act 1970 and gives the Minister the power to recover a grant that has been paid, if the Minister himself considers that the applicant for that grant JPEG 707 has given false or misleading information.

I find the distinction between " false " and " misleading " somewhat disturbing. We are not talking about fraud in the clause. I make it plain that I am not suggesting any easy life for those who are guilty of fraud upon the Minister. If the information is false, the Minister has a remedy in the proper place, namely, in a criminal court, for the offence of obtaining money by false pretences. Therefore, what we are concerned about this morning is the ground of obtaining a grant by means of providing misleading information. The question is: misleading to whom? It is misleading to the Minister. The importance of the phrase is that the test is subjective and not objective. The test must be subjective and must relate to the mind of the Minister, whereas if the information is false the test is objective, meaning that the facts can he ascertained and proved in a court of law. It follows that if the facts given to the Minister are considered by him to have misled him, he can go ahead and recover the money.

This is a serious matter, and my first question to the Minister is: is there a precedent? The nearest reference I can find are the penalty provisions set out in the Income Tax Act 1952, but I do not think that anyone could seriously argue that there is any true analogy between failing to provide proper information to the tax collector and providing information to the Minister which he himself may consider to be misleading. Therefore, I propose this amendment in the hope that the Minister will allow a legally qualified person to be appointed to decide whether the grant should be returned to the Minister, or whether it might be retained by the applicant. I believe it would be quite wrong for somebody who is not legally qualified and who cannot understand the difference between a subjective and an objective test to decide a matter as grave as this.

I am not exaggerating when I say that the rule of law itself is infringed by this provision. We sometimes talk rather glibly about the rule of law as if it means obedience to the law as it has been provided by this House. That indeed is not the true rule of law in my submission. The rule of law has one essential principle, and that is that at the time when a person embarks upon 708 a course of action, or acts in any way, he should know at that moment whether his action is going to be within or without the law. That is at the heart of the rule of law, and I regret to say that this position is in breach of that principle for which we have fought dearly in this House for a very long time.

I therefore hope that the Minister will give an assurance that there are reasonable precedents for this and, above all, that he will agree that there should be a legally qualified person to decide whether the Minister should have this money or not.
