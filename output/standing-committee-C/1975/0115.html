One of the major issues of this whole clause is consultation—consultation with the various sectors within the meat and livestock industry and with the various industries and interests within the meat and livestock sector. From the number of amendments tabled to the Bill, and JPEG 153 in particular to Clause 3, one can see that there is considerable concern and interest in the Bill. There is a widespread belief that, although the Ministry wants the consultation between interested parties within the meat and livestock sector to be improved in relation to consultation with the Meat and Livestock Commission, the Bill as it stands will not substantially improve the consultative arrangements.

The message I have received from a large number of organisations, of which I have mentioned some—the Smithfield Market Fresh Meat Traders' Association, the Federation of Fresh Meat Wholesalers, the Meat Manufacturers' Association, the National Farmers' Union, and others—clearly indicates that the industry would like further amendments to the Bill to ensure that participation with the Meat and Livestock Commission is really meaningful consultation, not just something that is done in order to meet the basis of some clause or subsection in a Bill, with no real meaning behind it.

The proposed new Section 1A requires the Commission to consult organisations if it appears to the Commission that such consultation is called for. The Bill does not provide in detail for the mechanics of consultation. More important, seemingly, it cannot lay down how the MLC is to judge when to consult. in practice, it could mean that matters important to trade would not be the subject of consultation. It is for that reason, among others, that we require various amendments to be made.

There is a great deal of feeling within the trade and by interests within the meat and livestock industry about levy increases, future programme, capital expenditure, and numerous other matters. The application to Parliament by the Meat and Livestock Commission for an increase in the maxima of levy at the point of slaughter was debated in April last year. Arising from that debate on a Statutory Instrument, it was conceded that an examination of the MLC by officials of the Ministry should take place. The results, of course, would be reported to the appropriate Ministers.

I am sure the Minister of State will he aware that evidence was given orally and in written form. A number of important points were made by the interests 154 represented. They said—again, with full justification, in my opinion—that no consultation had taken place with the industry about the establishment of the carcase evaluation unit at Blisworth and the cattle improvement units at Sutton Bonnington and Ingliston. They also commented on the paper loss on equities investment put against the general account. That was one of the arguments put forward by the MLC for a levy increase. Numerous other matters were put forward, which I believe fully justify their case in stating that there was insufficient negotiation and consultation at the right time by the MLC with the interests within the meat and livestock industry.

In saying all that, I hope that the Minister will not assume that we are totally critical of the functions and activities of the MLC. Far from it. I much appreciate the work and progress that has been made by the MLC since it was formed.

We on this side are critical of the increase of 31 per cent. after netting of income in the general livestock and improvement services. Surely that calls for a complete reappraisal and study of the services. We are also particularly critical of the on-farm testing and recording, which is up by some 90,000, an increase of 40.7 per cent. net. Surely the Minister would agree that the majority of the Commission's services should be cost effective and self-financing by these services and what the services are charging. Where carcase classification is employed primarily to authenticate weight, this charge surely should be on levy income.

The industry also feels some concern that university grants are not only too high—the total cost, I think, is about £196,000—but are leading to overlapping of research.

These are just some of the matters that have been brought to my attention in the representations made to me by various sectors of the industry which are concerned that, while we are debating the clause, the Government should try to ensure positive and meaningful consultation. Consultation and negotiation for their own sake are hopeless, do not help the industry, and do not help the relationship which I believe is important between the MLC and the meat and livestock industry.

In regard to the first amendment, one realises that the consumer's voice is inadequately expressed at any official level. It is almost impossible really to bring together a committee of people who can adequately represent the views of consumers. In regard to meat, there are millions of consumers. I find it difficult to see how this Consumers Committee can be valuable, given the fact that the consumer has the last word in the meat trade. Those of us who produce meat always have to remember this. That is why I come back to an old saying. Forgive me, Mr. Costain, if you have heard it many times before. The price mechanism whereby the consumer decides these matters and votes constantly daily and weekly is like a continuous General Election. It is not once in four years when the housewife casts a vote and says " I shall always buy pork " or " I shall always buy beef and it will always be at this price". It is a continuous General Election when she is voting week by week, casting a vote for one kind of meat in preference to another, or abstaining from voting—we know how effective an abstention from voting can be—when she decides to buy no meat in that week at all, as many housewives are now doing.

The votes cast week by week by the consumers in their millions are recorded by the returning officers who are the butchers, the wholesalers in the meat industry, the meat manufacturers and, ultimately, by the farmers. They, at the end of the day, are the returning officers in this process of a continuous General Election in which the customer is day by day expressing his orher preference JPEG 159 for the kind of meat that is wanted on the dinner tables in Britain.

I support the amendment wholeheartedly, but in doing so I cast doubt, once more, upon the value of what the Commission is doing. It has been a most expensive beast, being fed liberally upon the levies that we have produced. I for one—this view is shared by every other member in the organisation of which I am the national chairman concerned with meat production—believe that we could have invested the money we have given by way of levies far better in such a way that housewives would have had a better bargain than they have had.
