Clause 1
INCREASE OF MONETARY LIMITS RELATING TO BANKRUPTCY AND WINDING UP

Mr. Higgins
: I beg to move Amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 11, leave out subsection (2).

As you will have gathered from the preliminary remarks, Mr. English, the objective on both sides of the Committee is to improve the Bill as much as possible. 4 There is little partisan controversy on it between the two sides of the Committee, and there may be comparatively little of political controversy. On the other hand, contrary to initial expectations, quite a lot of it may be highly controversial in its technical aspects.

I remarked on Second Reading that, given the pace of reform in this area, it was rather surprising that we had got away from the concept of the debtors' prison. Certainly a number of committees have considered the law of bankruptcy and insolvency over the years, but successive Governments have not seen fit to legislate on this matter. So we now have before us a Bill which makes a number of changes, which we shall consider in detail on the specific amendments which we have tabled and in the debate on "clause stand part" at each stage. I hope that we shall manage to achieve considerable changes in the Bill. I understand that the Government have now had consultations with a number of expert bodies on the subject, and we shall look forward to hearing the Government's conclusions on this matter.

I turn now to Amendment No. 1, which seeks to leave out subsection (2), which states:

"The Secretary of State may by regulations increase or further increase any of the sums specified in the provisions amended by subsection (1) above."

The main reason for tabling the amendment is that we feel that it is important that executive discretion should not be unfettered in making changes in these limits. There are a number of aspects of insolvency law where I understand changes can be made by bodies in no way accountable in their decisions to Parliament. If these sums can be altered by order, that is probably undesirable.

We shall listen with interest to the Under-Secretary's reply to the points which we shall raise. There are subsidiary amendments on the Notice Paper at a later stage concerned with the form of any change made by statutory instrument.

The first question we have to consider is whether it is right that the Government should have power to make amendment by order in any case. We all know very well the difficulties of parliamentary time which are arising now and have JPEG 5 arisen for some time. The chance of the House adequately debating changes made by statutory instrument is now extremely low. None the less, these limits are important, and we shall be debating the specific figures. The question, therefore, is whether it is right and proper that the Government should be able to alter them, probably in practice without any further debate whatsoever.

The figures which it is proposed should be amended by statutory instrument cover a variety of different limits, and the arguments which apply to one may not necessarily apply to another. Certainly the main limit, that, for example, of the amount for which one can go bankrupt, is at present£50. It was originally set in 1869, and has not been altered since. Yet we are now asked, in the subsection which is the subject of our amendment, that the Government should in future be able to change it more or less at whim, and at very short notice.

It is a little curious that, having left the limit the same since 1869 until the present day—more than 100 years—the Government should feel it necessary to have this power. It is true that the rate of inflation has changed considerably in the last 100 years or so, but it is not only inflation which is involved in this aspect of the question. If that were so, no doubt one could consider, as on Finance Bills and other measures, the possibility of having some form of indexation which would take account of that.

It is not simply the effect of inflation, altering the monetary yardstick, which is relevant in this context. In nearly all these limits there is also an important balance to be struck between creditor and debtor. Obviously the balance between the two is radically altered depending on the limit. If the limit were sufficiently high, it would be extremely difficult for those seeking to recover their money to do so. Conversely, if it were very low, that might be unduly onerous on those who were operating in the normal course of business, or ordinary individuals.

There are, then, both the inflationary aspect and the question of the balance. The question is whether the Government should be able to do this by order. As I say, in practice it is difficult by way 6 of parliamentary debate on the merits of the case. Given that it has not proved necessary to make these changes very often in the past, we at least ought to hear a substantial argument in favour of doing so in future.

The other point arising is this. We were under the impression on Second Reading —perhaps the Minister would confirm this—that the Government, having had three Committees, in 1908, in 1924 and in 1957, had finally decided to carry out a long-term review of this subject. If that is so, this is obviously in some sense a holding measure, at any rate so far as the limits are concerned. It also makes significant changes in the present procedures, to which we shall come later.

Even if the review is likely to produce a more radical reform of the law on insolvency, it raises the question whether in the interim the Government need this power to alter the limits. It is surely arguable that in these circumstances one would be better not to have this power.To some extent, it is an incentive to the Government to get on with the job of carrying out the long-term review which has been so long delayed.

The final point I wish to make in opening is that obviously various interests are involved, and the balance is not only between the oreditors and debtors; it is also a question of striking a balance between the so-called insolvency service, and the cost of that service, and the burden which is placed upon it by the number of bankruptcies which take place.

We are all aware of the tremendous increase in the number of bankruptcies which has taken place recently. Indeed, some of the longer term surveys also suggest that the trend has been significantly upwards for a considerable time. The danger is that at some stage, perhaps not very far forward, the Government, in seeking understandably to curtain public expenditure, may, as indeed they do in the Bill, feel that the limits should be manipulated—perhaps thatis putting it too emotively but, at any rate, should be altered—in such a way as to limit the service to the resources which are available. On the whole, they are extensive resources and resources which involve a number of people with a considerable degree of expertise.

JPEG 7 Again, is this a matter which ought to be done by statutory instrument? The Government have put this change forward in the Bill and quite openly say that the purpose of the Bill is to cut down the burden of administration and reduce public expenditure. We are certainly sympathetic in principle to that aim. None the less, one must also take account of the effect which it is likely to have on the people concerned, whether they are trying to obtain their money or whether they are those who, certainly in the individual case and also in the firm's case, are going through what is inevitably a traumatic experience.

Our main point, therefore, particularly because we understand that there is a long-term review in hand, is that this is a subsection which could reasonably be deleted. If it is, one would not need to concern oneself with the amendments which subsequently appear on the Notice Paper to limit the regulation-making powers.

I hope therefore that the general amiable spirit which has characterised the Committee so far, although we have not been sitting very long, will prevail and that the Minister will feel able to accept the amendment.
