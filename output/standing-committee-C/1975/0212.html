Mr. Jerry Wiggin

"and (ii) by substituting such longer period of time for the time specified in the notice in which the work is to be done as he thinks reasonable in all the circumstances; or".

It would, perhaps, be helpful if I were to repeat what I said about the amendment. I said that the amendment was part of the philosophy of the amendments we have tabled on the clause, and is in line with the thoughts that I propounded in proposing the first few amendments on Tuesday.

I pointed out that, once the period which a tenant has been given to comply with a notice to remedy or to do work has expired, the landlord is entitled to serve a notice to quit where the tenant has failed to do so.

As the law stands, and in this respect Clause 11 would not alter the position, it is possible for the tenant to oppose the notice to quit, even if he admits he has failed to comply with a notice to remedy on the grounds that the notice gave insufficient time in which to do the work or to remedy the breach.

In the interests of fairness between landlord and tenant, it is more equitable that an arbitrator should judge the reasonableness of the time specified, presumably after having seen the condition of the farm, soon after the service of the notice. It must therefore follow that the tenant would be debarred from challenging the resonableness of the time at a later stage.

546 At present Article 13 of the Notices to Remedy and Notices to Quit Order gives the arbitrator powers to extend the period for complying with a notice, but this power can be used only at the arbitration on the notice to quit. It is not intended that the amendment should take away the protection from the tenant. However, it is a matter which certainly should be looked at, and if the Government are proposing any sort of review of the Order, the purpose of the amendment is to draw their attention to the situation in the hope that the amendment, if not acceptable today in its present drafted form, will be subsequently written in to the new Order.

This is, of course, a highly technical and legal point, but I am sure that the Minister appreciates that the amendments we have tabled follow a certain line of thought which I think he will agree is designed to bring greater fairness into the arbitration procedure. I hope and believe that both sides of the Committee will accept that it is my intention to try to make what is a crucially important matter in the life of a tenant, and in some cases the life of a landlord, be seen to be as fair as the British people can make it.

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Gavin Strang)
: It might be helpful if I simply explained at the outset that this is not so much an anti-harassment amendment as an amendment to the general procedure for arbitration on notices to do work. Its effect would be to enable an arbitrator on a notice to do work to extend the time allowed if he considers it reasonable to do so.

I should, however, like to make the point, particularly to my hon. Friend the Member for Brecon and Radnor (Mr. Roderick), as I have already explained in reply to an earlier amendment, that an arbitrator can extend the time at the first arbitration on the tenant's request if he considers it reasonable to do so. His powers to do so are set out in 552 Article 6 of the Agriculture (Notices to Remedy and Notices to Quit) Order 1964.

It is, however, necessary under the terms of Section 24(2)( d) of the Agricultural Holdings Act1948, for a landlord to specify a reasonable period of time for doing the work when drawing up his notice. If the reasonableness of the period so specified is challenged by the tenant, this question, and hence the basic validity of a notice, must be settled by the arbitrator one way or the other. But what an arbitrator cannot do is retrospectively to make valid a notice in which the landlord originally specified an unreasonable period of time by substituting a different period.

I should add that it is not possible to ensure in all circumstances that the question of reasonableness of time is settled at the notice to do the work stage. A tenant may not go to arbitration at all at that stage. Therefore, he cannot be deprived of the right to raise the question on an arbitration on the notice to quit. In practice, however, most tenants are likely to require arbitration on the notice to do work itself, in which case a challenge by the tenant on the question of time must be made at that stage also. If not then made, it cannot he raised later, which is dealt with in Article 5(3) of the Agriculture (Notices to Remedy and Notice to Quit) Order 1964.

This is a highly technical matter, because, frankly, the powers are there for the arbitrator to modify the time. The important factor which concerns us—I dealt with it at some length in earlier proceedings—is that we must recognise that there will be circumstances where, due to unforeseen events, the tenant will be unable to complete all the work. I explained that matter. The hon. Member for Norfolk, South-West (Mr. Hawkins) was not here at the time, but I am sure that he has plenty of experience of these matters. A contractor may not have turned up, or a serious illness befell a tenant, and the tenant may have been unable to comply with what seemed a, reasonable time given.

It is a fairly technical matter, and I can tell the hon. Member for Norfolk, South-West that we have had a great deal f help from the association of which the hon. Gentleman is a member, the Central Association of Agricultural Valuers. We consulted the Association JPEG 553 on the amendments and its advice was invaluable. It appears that the amendment is designed to help the landlord, but, in my view, there is little scope for the operation of it and, accordingly, I do not consider that it is necessary. Therefore, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will feel able to withdraw it.
