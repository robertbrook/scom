: I hope to be very lavish in dealing out sympathy today, but at the end of the day I do not know whether hon. Members opposite will be satisfied that my sympathy is enough.

I assure the Opposition and the Committee that all matters which we discuss are carefully considered between sittings and amendments are given a great deal of thought. Indeed, if one can possibly accept the line that is put forward, one seeks to do so.

However, there are occasions, such as now, when one must be sensitive to the facts of the situation and have the interests of the industry at heart. The purpose of the amendment is to give each relevant organisation the right to bê JPEG 203 consulted when it considers the interests of the class of persons it represents to be affected by the commission's activities. As I said last week, this is an amendment which would be inserted in a clause which says:

"If it appears to the Commission that any matter arising or likely to arise out of the exercise of the Commission's functions has or is likely to have a substantial effect on the interests of one or more classes of persons mentioned … the Commission shall consult."

The Commission, therefore, has the duty laid upon it by Parliament to ensure that there are consultations in any areas where its functions are likely to have a substantial effect on the interests of those concerned.

Last time we met hon. Members expressed concern that the opening words of the proposed new Section 1A of the 1967 Act, which I have just quoted, place upon the Commission the responsibility for identifying matters on which it must consult, and whom it should consult about these matters. This would place on the Commission the same obligation towards the relevant organisations as at present applies to the statutory Production and Distribution Committees. This obligation must rest with the Commission which alone is in the position to identify where its activities are likely to affect such interests. The Commission, doing the job in the very able way we know it does, is in a position to be sensitive to those interests which might be affected by any of its workings or policies.

The hon. Member for Macclesfield (Mr. Winterton) said that some industry organisations were concerned that the provision in the 1967 Act for the Production and Distribution Committees to submit proposals on the exercise of the Commission's functions, or to make representations on matters affecting the interests of producers and distributors, were not similarly spelt out in the arrangements envisaged in the Bill to introduce direct consultation with relevant organisations.

I am sure that this arises from a misunderstanding of the new situation which this clause would create. So long as this consultation with interested parties has to be conducted through committees created by statute, it is also necessary to provide for the rights and the obli- 204 gations of those bodies by statute. In the new situation there is no need to legislate for this right. The designated organisations will be free to make representations to the MLC and, because there will be a statutory duty on the Commission to consult those organisations, they would be, if anything, in a stronger position than they were previously. That is because the two statutory committees will disappear and the organisations—I believe there are about 40 at the moment—will all have contact with the Commission.

Mr. Bishop
: I am glad that the hon. Gentleman raised that question, because I was about to come on to that point.

Indeed, nothing in the proposed clause as it stands would exclude the right of any interested person or organisation—whether for the time being designated or not—from making its point of view known to the Commission, either privately or publicly. So there is this flow between the Commission and the organisations which may be concerned. On that score alone the amendment is inappropriate and unnecessary.

Another point I wish to make is that the MLC is set up as an independent body to do a job which embraces a wide range of functions. There would be a danger in placing an obligation on the Commission to consult an organisation—whenever that organisation wished—on each and every matter which it chose to claim might affect its interests. It could enable any organisation wishing to hamper the development of the MLC function to paralyse MLC progress in any given sphere—possibly to the detriment of wider interests of others in the industry who would stand to benefit from the activity in question.

With no statutory committees standing between them and the Commission, the producers and distributors organisations will be given much more freedom and it would be counter-productive to JPEG 205 make statutory provisions for their rights in this legislation.

I would emphasise again that the answer to the question "Should an organisation submit papers on certain matters, would the MLC be bound to consult?" is "Yes," if the matter in question could truthfully be said to have a substantial effect on the interests of the class of person that the organisation represents.

Therefore, the amendment is quite unnecessary. I appreciate the concern of hon. Members on this matter, but in the debate on the MLC levy on 16th April my hon. Friend the Parliamentary Secretary said:

"The MLC has also suggested that the consultative arrangements with the meat and livestock industries could, with advantage, be reconsidered. Ministers are, therefore, asking officials, in consultation with the Meat and Livestock Commission and with the trade associations, to examine further the views which have been expressed and to report to the Ministers on the results of their discussions."—[OFFICIAL REPORT, First Standing Committee on Statutory Instruments, 16th April 1975, c. 9.]

In Clause 3 we are discussing what has happened since to strengthen the consultations between the Commission and those represented. It has been indicated that the Commission and those it represents are satisfied with these arrangements. I feel confident that the new arrangements whereby the MLC will be in constant touch with the many designated organisations will improve the structure of consultation, which was the basis of criticism at the time of the last levy debate and has been since. So there will be a two-way flow between those concerned and, at the same time, there will be none of the hindrances which might come about and which, I am sure, would come about if the amendment was accepted.

I hope, therefore, that after this very full debate the mover of the amendment will see fit to withdraw it.
