Mr. Higgins
: We come now to the first clause of the Bill and the general principles embodied in it. We have discussed already some of the aspects which affect it. The Under-Secretary will be well aware that we are in favour of measures to ensure that the accounts which companies are, by law, required to produce are, in fact, produced on time.

Certainly it is difficult under the present arrangements for the Government and their officials to ensure that such accounts are produced. Indeed, there are great problems in pressing companies and their directors. There is no difference between us on that point.

I stress that we on this side of the Committee want to improve the Bill and ensure that companies operate within a sensible framework which can be properly enforced. That having been said, we have very considerable concern about the way in which the Bill has been drafted. This clause is related to 11 other clauses, all concerned with the particular point about the

"Duty to prepare, lay and deliver accounts by reference to accounting reference periods".

106 There is here a considerable difference from the existing law under which companies are operating, and it is the existing law with which they have become familiar over a period of years.Although some companies may delay in producing their accounts for one reason or another, not least in the case of small businesses because of the need to concentrate on the actual running of the business, they have all become used to the existing system. Therefore, a change in the law, although not tremendously onerous for the large company employing accountants, solicitors, and so forth—it is just one of the occupational hazards of modern life—isvery important indeed for the small business. 108 The central aspect of this clause is the change to accounting reference periods. As the preamble to the Bill says:

"Clause J requires the directors of every company to prepare accounts in respect of each accounting reference period, normally one year."

As a result of this experience, he now answers every question on a questionnaire whether or not it really relates to his firm.

For example, he is often asked questions like "How many nails do you use?" He replies that he uses 20 lbs. one month, JPEG 109 and 25 lbs. the next. He is also asked "How much paint do you use"? He answers that as well. But, in actual fact, his firm uses neither nails nor paint. However the official Department is happy because the questionnaire has been filled in; and he is no longer fined. So the game goes on.

Clause 1 perpetuates the kind of difficulties experienced by small firms in having to deal with so much administration. The document circulated by my hon. Friend the Member for Worthing (Mr. Higgins) shows the veritable web of obligations which are placed on com- panies by this Bill. Clause 1 alone involves eight cross-references to other clauses, and I pity the poor working director or part-time company secretary who has to try to work these out in relation to a small manufacturing company, like the ones in my constituency.

It is inevitable that small firms will find this Bill an additional burden, and we should consider their problems very carefully.

It may be that some members of the Committee will consider that the Bolton Report definition of small firms is not ideal. At least it has the advantage of giving us a basis for trying to distinguish between them and the large corporations which have the apparatus necessary to deal with the demands of the Companies Acts. At the same time, we can realise and recognise the very real decline of small firms, and that presents a real danger to the economy. That danger can be easily understood since small firms like the one I have described are key suppliers of parts to the assembly industries, key suppliers of highly specialised machine tools in export markets. The upturn in trade depends very much upon the efficient manufacturing capacity of these firms and the contribution they make to the economy. The ability to innovate and succeed in manufacturing industry depends to a great extent on working directors of small firms being able to concentrate on manufacturing rather than on form filling and administration.

Many of the firms in my constituency are typical in this respect. There might be one, two or three directors of the firm who spend their day on the shop floor engaged in manufacture primarily for the 110 export market. They have no company secretary or office available to deal with the requirements of the Bill. It is, therefore, vital that the provisions of the clause—backed up as they are by the penalties in Clause 4—do not constitute the straw which will break the back of the small firm. Surely it is unreasonable to expect small firms to go to the expense of producing accounts in some printed form, the nature of which we do not yet know. It was for that reason that we thought it would be a good idea to specify the form in which the accounts should be produced. That is why we do not want to leave the method of reproduction to be determined by means of regulation, and that is why we propose that the requirement to produce accounts in a legible form should be sufficient. That is why we shall return to this matter on Report, as no doubt the Government will, too.

The Government must say what real advantage there is to the nation in compelling these small firms to provide more information in some printed form. The Bolton Report defines the small firm, first, as one with a relatively small share of the market. A second essential characteristic is that it is managed by its owners or part owners in a personalised way and not through the medium of a formalised management structure. That characteristic should be taken very much to heart by the Department in considering how the Companies Acts should apply to this type of enterprise. A third feature is that the company is also independent in the sense that it does not form part of a larger enterprise, and that the owner-managers are free from outside control in taking their principal decisions.

These three factors constitute what the Bolton Report called an economic definition. But it felt obliged also to use a statistical definition, and this was set at the 200-employee upper limit for manufacturing, and a more or less arbitrary series of definitions were adopted for other trades. The definition on page 3 of the Bolton Report provides a useful basis on which the Government could consider making exceptions and distinctions for the smaller type of firm. Behind that definition lies the seed corn of British manufacturing industry, the major servicing firms of tomorrow. Collectively their economic contribution is enormous. Individually it might be minute, so why is so much information needed? Is it to JPEG 111 assist their creditors? Surely not. The creditors who may need information about small firms are able to obtain it now and would in any case require much more up-to-date information on the trading position of the company than would be represented by the production of these accounts.

There are a large number of small firms. The Bill therefore imposes twin burdens. t imposes the burden on companies of having to file, and upon the Registrar of having to check up that they have done so. What, I wonder, will that cost the country; and how many extra staff will be required for the purpose? Why is it that these small firms are to be called upon to assemble information which in many cases will never be looked at, but which in some cases will be looked at very carefully by our foreign competitors. I refer of course to the danger presented to the single-product company in having to file this type of information.

I therefore ask the Under-Secretary in all seriousness to have regard to the position of the small company. I know that he admires it and understands the contribution it makes to the economy. I realise that to make distinctions and definitions requires innovation, but, knowing the Under-Secretary as I do, I am certain that that is not beyond his powers. It is, therefore, very important in considering the clause that we pay regard to the real problems being expressed, not in the board rooms but on the shop floors of the small firms which are the manufacturing seed corn of our industry.
