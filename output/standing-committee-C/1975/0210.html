The hon. Member for Macclesfield (Mr. Winterton) asked me what instances we foresaw in the future in which the powers conferred in the clause might have to be invoked. Another point, raised by the hon. Member for Westmorland (Mr. Jopling), 494 concerned the power to make provision for the use of illegal methods of destruction. The hon. Member expressed the hope that this would not mean we were contemplating the use of a method such as a gin trap. Certainly it was not the gin trap that was in our minds when this provision was included in the Bill. We were thinking more of certain poisons which are prohibited for use by the general public, but which might be the only practicable way of doing what has to be done in a particular situation.

The use of hydrogen cyanide gas against badgers is an example of this. Hon. Members will notice that the subsection requires the Minister, in making use of these powers, to pay particular regard to the need to avoid unnecessary suffering in the wild species concerned. Clearly our first thought in all cases would be to carry out the necessary work of destruction by methods which were already legal, but the power in subsection (5) enables us to use other methods if it is preferable to do so.

Another point, raised by the hon. Member for Norfolk, South-West (Mr. Hawkins), was the need to remove the source of infection of the wild species. This is absolutely right, since there can be little point in taking action against a wild species if that species is continually being reinfected from another source.

But our whole approach to the problem with which this clause and Clause 10 are concerned is that we should make a two-pronged attack on the disease, under the ordinary Diseases of Animals Act powers in relation to farm species and under these powers in relation to wild species. Indeed, the case for this clause rests on the assumption that it is possible to prevent further spread of the disease from farm livestock to wild species by action under the Diseases of Animals Act. If the circumstances were such that the disease in the farm species could not be contained, on the face of it there would be no case for taking action against the wild species.

I hope that with these explanations of the points raised the Committee will agree that the clause should stand part of the Bill.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 9, as amended, ordered to stand part of the Bill.
