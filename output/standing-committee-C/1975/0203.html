POWER TO REGULATE EXPORTATION OF ANIMALS IN THE INTERESTS OF THEIR WELFARE

The Chairman

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Gavin Strang)
: Before we adjourned last Thursday, hon. Members raised a number of points. I believe that some of them were based on a misunderstanding of the Government's position, and I hope, Mr. Costain, that you will allow me a little time to explain why I think these amendments should not be made.

There are, of course, differing views as to whether we should export livestock for slaughter at all, but it is common ground that, if we are to exportlivestock, we have a duty to ensure, as far as is possible, that they are properly treated. We helped to formulate and strongly supported the Council of Europe Convention on the Protection of Animals in International Transport. We also welcomed the EEC directive on humane slaughter.

We need information on the proposed destinations of the exported farm animals, the arrangements for transporting them there and the treatment which they will receive on arrival. We need this in order to comply effectively with 392 present and any future obligations, and also in order to make it possible for our veterinary staff to co-operate with their opposite numbers abroad inensuring that the welfare regulations are observed in practice. I am sure that no member of this Committee would wish shipments to be permitted in circumstances where we could not be assured of the welfare of the exported animals.

The hon. Member for Holland with Boston (Mr. Body) referred to the possibility of animals being sent to North Africa. We do not allow the export of animals for slaughter to any countries where humane slaughter is not practised.

The hon. Member for Holland with Boston is not yet with us, but I assure the Committee that there is no question of our departing from the assurances that my right hon. Friend gave in the debates in the House on this matter.

We have no evidence that any animals shipped from this country have been slaughtered in any non-European country for many years. The hon. Member for Holland with Boston said that he had heard suggestions that, if the clause were passed without an Order being made, the protection now afforded to exported animals would be reduced. This is not the case. Effective control overthe welfare of exported livestock is already being exercised by means of administrative arrangements under The Exported Animals Protection Order 1964,and by licensing control under The Export of Goods (Control) Order 1970 administered by the Department of Trade.

These arrangements are working satisfactorily, and will continue to operateuntil such time as Ministers make Orders under Clause 8. It is hoped that this clause will enable the introduction of a new control system, operated exclusively by the Agriculture Departments, which would be simpler for exporters and offer comprehensive protection to the animals concerned. The emphasis should, I think, be on a proper consultation with all concerned to get the best control system, and I would not wish these essentials to be sacrificed in order to comply with a fixed time limit.

It is the Minister's firm intention to make an Order as soon as possible. However. present licensing controls operated by the Department of Trade are on a United Kingdom basis, and we wish to continue with a uniform export policy JPEG 393 for the United Kingdom as a whole. Hence we would prefer not to make an Order covering Great Britain until corresponding legislation is available in Northern Ireland.

I am sure that all hon. Members will agree that we cannot afford to be complacent about the welfare of exported animals. Although, as I have said, thepresent arrangements are working satisfactorily, I would not wish to miss any opportunity which might occur in the future to introduce further safeguards, or to correct any shortcomings which may become apparent.

The Clause as it stands will enable Ministers to do this. Amendment No. 62,if carried, would limit Ministers' powers for the making of Orders to a period of 12 months from the passing of the Act. I am sure hon. Members will see that this limitation could prevent Ministers from introducing new welfare controls which might be required in the future as a result of changes in the pattern of trade, or of new developments in transport, or new international agreements.

My hon. Friend the Member for Brecon and Radnor (Mr. Roderick) indicated inhis speech in the last debate in the House of Commons, when the House votedto resume the export of live animals on the basis of assurances given by myright hon. Friend, that although he voted for that he subsequently—if not regretted it—wondered whether he had taken the right decision.

There has been one incident. My learned Friend might be thinking in particular of the French incident which was featured on a BBC television programmeI saw that programme and it was very interesting. I hardly ever see television, but it so happened that I managed to get back to Scotland in time to see that programme one Thursday night.

I should explain that we have been able, following that incident, to introduce, on an administrative basis, a further tightening of the situation and an addition to the information which we obtain from these exporters. To some extent, this legislation will enable us to put this on a more effective statutory footing when the Order is made. We have found that the French authorities have responded to our representations 394 and we are building up a very effective relationship between the veterinaryofficers representing the Ministry in this country and in other countries,particularly France, to where these live exports are made.

Nothing I have said detracts from my hon. Friend's observation that where at all possible we favour the export of carcases rather than live animals. There is no doubt that that is the view of the majority of British people, and of the House of Commons.

Turning now to Amendment No 67, which deals with the question of what information exporters should be required to give, we must be in a position to obtain all the relevant details, but we should like to be able to do this without asking for more information than is necessary.

The Clause as it stands will enable Ministers to make Orders to protect thewelfare of any type of animals. In any Order designed to protect the welfare of exported food animals, it will be necessary for the exporter to declare the final destination of the animals and the arrangements for conveying them later.

However, this Clause may later be used to make Orders to control the exportof other species of animals in different circumstances. In some cases—for example, horses going abroad for racing or for exhibition—it might not be necessary to insist that the final destination is stated. For this reason, I would prefer that Ministers should be given discretion to decide on exactly what information is appropriate in relation to particular Orders.

This Clause is drafted in the usual discretionary form appropriate to an enabling Act. I hope that theCommit-tee will agree to pass it as it stands, thereby giving Ministers continuing powers to make Orders to give proper protection to the welfare of exported animals.In return, I can assure the Committee that the first Order under this Clause will he made as soon as possible, bearing in mind the consideration whichI have mentioned.

Indeed, I should like to go further. I have carefully considered this matter since the last meeting of the Committee and I am happy to assure the Committee that the Government will ensure that the Order is made within 12 months—which JPEG 395 meets precisely the point which has been made by the hon. Members on both sides.
