NIGHT ASSEMBLIES BILL

Clause 4
OFFENCES

10.30 a.m.

Mr. Jerry Wiggin
: I beg to move Amendment No. 91, in page 3, line 16, at end insert:

"or receiving any payment for any right to make records, films or sound or television broadcasts of the assembly or any part of it, or for the right to supply goods or services to persons attending the assembly".

The Chairman
: With this Amendment we can take Amendment No. 85, in page 3, leave out lines 23 to 31 and insert:
  1. "If at a night assembly section (Control of night assemblies by local authorities) (6) of this Act is not complied with, any person promoting the assembly or being concerned in its management shall be guilty of an offence and liable on summary conviction to a fine not exceeding £400.
  2. Where a person is charged with an offence under subsection (1)( b)above as being such a person as is mentioned in subsection (2)( b), ( c) or ( d), or is charged with an offence under subsection (3), it shall be a defence for him to prove either—
    1. that he had no responsibility to the managers or promoters of the night assembly for seeing to the matters to which the charge relates; or
    2. that, having such responsibility, he used all reasonable diligence in discharging it.
  3. Proceedings for an offence under this section shall not be brought except by or with the consent of the Director of Public Prosecutions, or by the local authority."

Mr. Wiggin
: Clause 4 deals with the offences that might be committed, in terms of fines and also the persons who might be liable for prosecution.

It is right to point out that if the local authority or whoever presses the prosecution under the Bill takes the matter to a magistrates' court and a person is found guilty on summary conviction, the limit to the fine would be £400. One imagines that it would be for the minor offence—the smaller crime, if one could use that word—but it would deal with a smaller 262 movement away from the purpose of the Bill.

If, however, promoters are determined to try every possible means to get round the Bill's provisions and avoid great expense as a result of their activities, they can be fined on indictment, in which case there is no limit on the amount of the fine. I believe that that is the right way of dealing with penalties.

I now turn to the Amendments, and the way in which we have re-grouped them. We are concerned that semi-innocent parties who—although perhaps being liable in the first instance under the original Bill—have taken reasonable precautions to defend themselves by finding out what is going on shall not find themselves liable to conviction under the Bill.

Those people covered in subsection 2( e) and ( f) in the original Clause 4 have been moved up to come in with persons under subsection 2( a). The persons whom we are after are not the members of the camera crew, or persons selling hot dogs—a casual reader might come to that view—but the promoters or the persons employed by or contracted with the promoters to sell the festival rights to film or catering companies, or whatever it might be. It is clear that such persons are likely to have a deep financial interest in the organisation of the assembly, and it is right and proper that they should be coupled with the persons promoting the assembly.

At the request of the Opposition, we have added the necessary defences, and I think that the new subsection (4)( a)—

"that he had no responsibility to the managers or promoters of the night assembly for seeing to the matters to which the charge relates: or ( b) that, having such responsibility, he used all reasonable diligence in discharging it."

covers extremely well those people in subsection 2( b), ( c) and ( d) who might, quite innocently, be prosecuted for something for which they should not have been prosecuted.

During the debate at our last Sitting the question of the occupier of the land was referred to. I am cognisant of the criticism made by the Opposition, in particular the hon. Member for Erith and Crayford (Mr. Wellbeloved). However, it seems to me that, irrespective of anything in the Bill, an occupier has some responsibility for ensuring that his land is not used for unlawful purposes. I speak as a tenant farmer who has a substantial JPEG 263 responsibility for what goes on on my land, and a responsibility to my landlord. Therefore, there is nothing new about placing on the occupier the responsibility in terms of occupation of land. That is a well-established point in law.

The Clause makes the occupier liable in respect of any failure to make sure that his land is not used for unlawful purposes. It could be argued that an occupier may be in legitimate doubt about the lengths to which he is required to go. For that reason he might be reluctant to agree with the promoter in the first instance. That argument carries things a very long way. Having accepted that there is responsibility on the occupier of land, it does not seem to me that the responsibility laid on the land occupier in the Bill is any worse than that laid on an occupier of land in many other instances.

I am open to listen to the arguments on the point, but for the moment I should like to leave my Amendments Nos. 91 and 85, as shown, on the Order Paper. I have not, however, closed my mind to the possibility of making some concessions, if the arguments for doing so are good ones.

Mr. Arthur Davidson
: I listened with great interest to what the hon. Gentleman said. His Amendments go some way towards meeting the strenuous objections that we raised to the Clause as originally drafted, which, in our view, might well involve many innocent people who play no part in the staging or promotion of an assembly.

Two specific offences are involved. The first is the putting on of an assembly in contravention of the Bill, as when a promoter completely ignores the necessary procedure and deliberately puts on an assembly for which permission has not been obtained. We all agree that that would constitute a serious offence, and should be treated as such.

The second offence is a totally different one. It arises not from putting on an illegal assembly but from non-compliance with a requirement that a local authority may have imposed. In this connection, I am not satisfied that anyone other than the promoter or a close associate should be caught by the provisions of the Clause. I therefore give notice of my intention—I am sure that I will have the support of my hon. Friends in this matter—to table an appropriate Amendment on Report.

264 I am aware of the seriousness of the contravention specified in subsection (1) ( a), but I am not sure whether there should not be at least some form of statutory defence for such a promoter, based on the degree of his awareness whether he was acting in contravention of the Bill. At present there is no such provision.

I agree that there may well be a common law defence and that if a promoter were brought before the court the fact that he had no knowledge that he had acted in contravention of the Bill would weigh with any reasonable court in reaching a decision. Nevertheless, as at present drafted—and even as amended—the Clause mentions several categories of person other than the promoter, such as a person giving notice of the assembly, the occupier of the land, or a person receiving payment for the right to attend.

The drafting is extremely confusing. Several lawyers to whom I have shown the Bill have assumed, as was my first impression, that the word "receiving" was a mistake for "making". I am assured that that is not the case, however, and that people such as musicians, comperes, and ice cream or hot dog vendors will not be caught by the Bill. I should be happy to receive the hon. Gentleman's assurance that no person who innocently attends an assembly—certainly no one who has paid admission money in order to attend—could be caught by the terms of the Clause.

In addition, I should be grateful if the hon. Gentleman would explain more fully what is meant by:

"any person receiving any payment for the right to attend the assembly;"

which I still find confusing, despite the hon. Gentleman's explanation. However, I now understand the phrase.

"any person receiving any payment for any right to make records, films"

I understand that the desire is to involve the person who, for example, lets out the right to make television films, and who is part and parcel of the promoters. I understand that provision, since a promoter might well be a company with television interests. I presume that the reference to

"any person receiving any payment for the right to supply goods"

comes under the same category.

My hon. Friends and I would like the situation to be carefully examined, in order to ensure that those who play or JPEG 265 sing at a pop festival, or sell necessary services—and certainly those who attend the festival, who cannot be expected to know whether necessary provisions have been contravened—should not be caught by the Bill. After all, heavy penalties are involved. It would be outrageous if innocent people were involved.

Gentleman of my intention to pursue this matter on Report, with a view to tabling an appropriate Amendment to Clause 4. Mr. Wiggin
: The hon. Gentleman has discussed this matter and, as I pointed out in my earlier remarks, we have done everything in our power to meet him.

I am certainly at one with the hon. Gentleman on the underlying intention of the Amendments. I think there is no disagreement on either side that under the terms of the original Clause certain innocent people might have been penalised. I shall look forward with interest to the hon. On the question of giving an assurance regarding those who attend an assembly, I can only reaffirm my earlier remarks. It must be stressed that persons who purchase either the right to supply goods or services or the right to make records, films, and so on, are nowhere covered in subsection (2) and are not subject to any penalty.

The same is true of persons who attend the assembly as listeners or performers. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will take that as spelling it out abundantly clearly. He has said—and he is completely right—that those who sell the rights to attend are, to all intents and purposes, promoters or partly promoters. If one individual promoted these things—an individual person or an individual company—the whole problem would be much easier. The hon. Gentleman knows full well that the groups involved are often somewhat diverse. The purpose of spreading the Clause widely is to make sure that the people who receive 266 the money are the people who are liable to see that the terms of the Bill are carried out.

Clause 5
APPEALS

Mr. Wiggin
: I beg to move Amendment No. 86, in page 3, line 33, leave out from "may" to end of line 41 and insert:

"appeal to the Secretary of State.


(2) An appeal under this section shall be by notice to the local authority and to the Secretary of State which must be given within fourteen days of the date on which the notice under section 2 was given.

  • (2A) Before determining an appeal under this section the Secretary of State shall, if either the applicant or the local authority so desires, afford to each of them an opportunity of appearing before, and being heard by, a person appointed by the Secretary of State for the purpose.
  • (2B) On an appeal under this section the Secretary of State, having considered the report of any person appointed under subsection (2A) above, and any expression of opinion by that person as to the suitability of the site proposed for the night assembly or, as the case may be, as to the requirements which ought to be imposed in connection with the assembly, shall either—
    1. dismiss the appeal, or
    2. quash the notice appealed against and substitute for it any other notice which he thinks the authority ought to have given;
    and where he acts under paragraph ( b) above the substituted notice shall be deemed to have been given by the authority under section JPEG 267 (Control of night assemblies by local authorities) of this Act".

    • Mr. Wiggin
    : In many respects I look upon this as one of the most important Clauses, if not the most important Clause, since it provides the appeals procedure that has disturbed many people outside the House. They have been afraid that the provisions of the Bill could be unjustly used against an innocent promoter to prevent the holding of a pop festival. The reasons could be blind prejudice, dislike of noise or of large numbers of people, or any other reason, none of which would stand up in a court of law.

    Hon. Gentlemen know that under the original appeals procedure proposed in the Bill, an appeal would be heard by a nominee of the Secretary of State. The Amendment alters this, so that the appeals procedure will resemble that of a planning appeal. An inspector will be dispatched within 14 days of the date of the requirement for the appeal to listen to evidence from both sides. He will then report to the Secretary of State, who will issue his ruling. He will be enabled to dismiss the appeal altogether or to quash the notice and rewrite it. This removes the possibility that an eccentric inspector might be appointed. The sieve that will now be applied to an appeal under the Bill will ensure that no major injustice can arise.

    There are many who feel that a court of law, for example, the Crown Court, might be a more suitable place for this type of appeal. The Crown Court will be both slow and, more important, expensive, for hearing appeals against unreasonable requirements, or against requirements made by local authorities under the Bill. The procedure proposed is quick and cheap. It has been proven in other areas, and I believe that it is ideally suited for this purpose.

    The question of magistrates' courts has been raised. I mentioned it in the Second Reading debate. In the light of present thinking about pop festivals I doubt whether the local magistrates' court would be the right place to hear an appeal. I am in no way casting aspersions on magistrates, who would do their best to adjudicate fairly, but there would inevitably be enormous pressures upon them. Therefore, I reject the idea of the magistrates' court as the place for an appeal.

    268 Hon. Gentlemen opposite have asked for an assurance about the grounds for appeal against the adjudication of the local authority. As I read the Bill, whether there be a refusal to hold the festival in the first instance on the ground that the site is unsuitable; whether the conditions that the local authority lays down are too onerous, or whether the security to be deposited is too high, the inspector and, subsequently, the Secretary of State, may rule. All the criticisms that an unreasonable condition can be imposed are, therefore, completely removed. After a few appeals have been heard it is to be hoped that these matters will be resolved without having to go to appeal. The Clause is a protecion for both parties—certainly for the promoters—and I believe that we have reached a satisfactory conclusion.

    I am grateful to hon. Gentlemen opposite for their help, because they may have felt that this arrangement was more onerous than that which was first introduced in the Bill. It is better, fairer and more likely to lead to correct decisions.

    Mr. Davidson
    : I need not speak at any great length on the Amendment because the Committee is basically in agreement. As originally drafted we regarded the Bill as a menacing attack upon civil liberties. I was of the view, as were all my hon. Friends, that because the Bill was dealing basically with civil liberties—with the right of assembly—the proper authority to deal with an appeal was the Crown Court, which has always been the arbiter of matters concerning right of assembly.

    However, as the hon. Gentleman has given way on so many points, I think that my hon. Friends now concede—I certainly do—that in the circumstances an appeal to the Secretary of State would probably be the cheapest and most effective way of dealing with the matter. I should like to hear from the Minister—either now or on Report—exactly how his inspectors will be instructed to deal with appeals because the way in which the inspector reports and the Secretary of State uses his discretion is most important. Indeed, the success or failure of the Bill depends upon the way in which the appeal procedure is carried out. One cannot underestimate it.

    I should like an assurance from the Minister that his inspectors—and certainly JPEG 269 the Secretary of State—will reject any capricious use of power by a local authority; will turn down measures which have been imposed by local authorities which are in contravention of the code, which are unnecessary, and are the product of a bigoted or intolerant council, or a council under pressure from bigoted and intolerant biased local people—and there are such people.

    The success or failure of the Bill depends not only upon the way the Secretary of State uses his discretion; it also depends how binding the code of conduct is on local authorities, the inspector and the Minister. The code of conduct should not be something to be casually referred to by a local authority, which may say: "Yes, we will have a look at it," with no intention of abiding by it. We should apply much more stringent measures in the code of conduct. It should be binding on a local authority to consult. It should be equally binding upon the inspectors and the Secretary of State. If the Secretary of State does not use his discretion to reject onerous conditions, if they became precedents, local councils will be encouraged to turn down applications and effectively stop pop festivals or other assemblies being put on. The object of the Bill will have been defeated.

    I am satisfied with the procedure laid down, but I should like an assurance from the Minister as to the way in which the inspectors and the Secretary of State are to carry out their duties.

    The Minister for Local Government and Development (Mr. Graham Page)
    : There are two points about the procedure on appeal. First, there is the machinery. Obviously, one must set up a form of appeal that will be expeditious. The whole process of appeal would be defeated if the parties wishing a hearing were delayed for any length of time. The Secretary of State will allot this duty to inspectors, who will be ready to undertake inquiries at short notice. Normally our inspectors are booked up many months ahead for planning inquiries and so on, but there will be a deliberate arrangement that the inspectors to whom this duty is allotted will be available for hearings to be fixed quickly, so that decisions may be made as soon as possible.

    Secondly, there is the consideration of 270 the cases that come before the inspectors. The inspector and, eventually, the Secretary of State, will decide each case on its merits. At this stage I do not intend to lay down rules about it. We shall be drawing up a code of practice and, as the hon. Member for Accrington (Mr. Arthur Davidson) said, it is the code to which the local authority, the inspector, and the Secretary of State will refer.

    The consideration will be along these lines: first, the inspector will look at the Bill, which in new Clause No. 8—which will become Clause 2—lays down certain statutory requirements, such as that the site is suitable. A local authority should look at an application from that point of view, and not from some capricious point of view, such as the colour of the eyes of the applicant. Its consideration must relate to the things that are set out in new Clause No. 8. Obviously, as in any other case of this kind, the inspector and the Secretary of State would reject any capricious use of the powers, because that would not be in accordance with the statutory duties placed upon local authorities.

    With regard to reference to the code, the hon. Gentleman used the word "binding" several times. I am reluctant to use that word when we are not making conformity with the code a statutory obligation. We wish the code to be flexible and suited to the occasion. To begin with, it will to some extent be experimental, and we shall learn to modify it by experience.

    But there is no point in having a code unless it is a code. It is a document that goes further than guidance. We could have chosen to ask the Committee to accept the idea of a departmental circular setting out the way in which the Secretary of State would wish assemblies to be organised. I look on a code as something one step further than merely guiding a person to reach a decision. It is something to which that person must give serious attention. Only if there were some exceptional reason, which would be disclosed in announcing the decision, would there be any suggestion that the decision ran contrary to any item in the code.

    I think that that is as far as I can go without prejudicing the procedure under which both sides make their representations to the inspector, and the Secretary of State eventually decides on the recom- JPEG 271 mendations that he receives from the inspector.

I hope that in practice we shall be able to reduce many of the burdens that we place on inspectors in their extremely thorough recording of evidence, and of how they reach their findings and conclusions in planning decisions. We must balance the need for speed with the need for full examination of the case.

We originally thought that we could deal with appeals without the need for a political decision by the Secretary of State, by appointing an inspector whose own opinion, without any reference to a Minister, would be decisive. The inspector will decide the facts, setting out what he finds in his conclusions and recommendations, and the Secretary of State will then apply his mind to those recommendations, using the code as a background. We will learn by experience how that works out. We have set out the framework on which to build up a reasonable set of precedents. After about 12 months both sides will know the way in which an inspector will apply his mind to these inquiries.

    LOCAL AUTHORITIES AND STATUTORY UNDERTAKERS

    The Chairman
    : With this Amendment it will be convenient to take the following:

    No. 35, in page 4, line 10, leave out "district" and insert "county".

    No. 95, in page 4, line 13, at end insert:

    "( c) in relation to a night assembly held on land partly in the area of one county and partly in that of one or more other counties, a joint committee of the county councils in which any part of the land is situated."

    No. 94, in page 4, line 14, leave out from beginning to end of line 22 and insert:

    Mr. Maddan
    : Amendment No. 34 has been grouped with other Amendments, which I hope does not mean that we are going to have shades of Standing Committee D on local government reform. It is an attempt to determine in the context of the Bill, and not in a doctrinal way, which is the better authority—county or district—to be primarily involved in fulfilling duties laid upon the local authority by the Bill.

    The Amendments deal with the first three subsections of this Clause in the Bill as it was originally printed. The primary authority should be the county, but it should have power to delegate its function, with or without restriction, to a district if it thinks it appropriate. For instance, if there is a suitable open space in the City of Bristol and a pop festival 276 is being promoted there, the district council might be thought to be a suitable authority to exercise these functions, but, on the whole, primary responsibility should be pinned on county authorities.

    Another reason for that choice is that it lessens the chances of there being a festival on a site overlapping local authority areas.
