Clause 1
POWERS OF COURT ON HEARING COMPLAINT FOR AFFILIATION ORDER AND ON APPEAL

The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Mark Carlisle)
: I beg to move Amendment No. 1, in page 2, line 1, leave out subsection (4) and add:

The following enactments are hereby repealed:—

  1. in section 44(3) of the National Assistance Act 1948 and section 26(2) of the Children Act 1948 the words "in addition to the evidence required to be heard by section 4 of the said Act of 1872;
  2. in section 24(3) of the Ministry of Social Security Act 1966 the words "in addition to the evidence required to be heard by section 4 of the said Act of 1957; and
  3. in section 30(3) of the Maintenance Orders (Reciprocal Enforcement) Act 1972, paragraph ( b) and the word "and" immediately preceding it.

This is one of a group of Government Amendments, the purpose of which is to make alterations in the Maintenance Orders (Reciprocal Enforcement) Act, 1972, which received the Royal Assent on 23rd March. The purpose of that Act was to enable the United Kingdom to give effect to the 1956 United Nations convention on the recovery abroad of maintenance, and it enables a person abroad to apply for maintenance from a person in England in accordance with the law of England.

The law in this country at the moment requires a mother of an illegitimate child to give evidence in person before the court. Clearly, to have retained that re- 4 quirement in the Maintenance Orders (Reciprocal Enforcement) Act would have breached the whole purpose of the Act, which allowed a mother to enforce an order in this country. Therefore, Section 30(3, b) of that Act specifically provided that an applicant's written evidence could be given before the court and that it would be treated as if it had been given by her in person, thus avoiding the effect of Section 4 of the Affiliation Proceedings Act, 1957.

The whole purpose of the Bill is to remove Section 4 of the Affiliation Proceedings Act, 1957, so that in future an order may be made in our courts without the mother giving oral evidence. Therefore, the provisions of Section 30 of the Maintenance Orders (Reciprocal Enforcement) Act, 1972, will become unnecessary. The effect of the Amendment, by adding the new paragraph ( c) to subsection (4) of the Clause, is to delete that provision from the 1972 Act.

: You kindly agreed, Miss Quennell, as the Bill had not had a Second Reading debate, that I might say a few words about its principles.

First, I wish to thank the hon. and learned Gentleman for the help that he and his Department have given me. It is a rather small Bill because I was thirteenth on the list and thought that I could not bring in the major Bill that I should have wished to introduce to deal with the guardianship of children.

The purpose of the Bill is humane: to stop discrimination against a small section of the population, namely, mothers of illegitimate children. By repealing Section 4(1) of the Affiliation Proceedings Act, 1957, the Bill will eliminate the need for the mother of an illegitimate child to give evidence in person in affiliation proceedings, unless the case is defended.

Secondly, it will extend from one to three years from the date of the child's birth the time in which the mother may initiate affiliation proceedings. This will make her equal to the mother who re- JPEG 5 quires maintenance for a child. At present the State has the longer period—up to three years if the child is receiving assistance—in which to take this action.

Thirdly, at the discretion of the court, it will make the proceedings for the revival, variation or revocation of an affiliation order domestic court proceedings for the purpose of the Magistrates' Courts Act, 1952. The Bill brings these proceedings into line with similar proceedings relating to matrimonial orders and it will restrict the publicity and exclude the general public, which is highly desirable in this type of case.

The Affiliation Proceedings Act, 1959, consolidated the law in respect of illegitimate children. It is interesting to observe that provisions of this sort have been in existence ever since the time of Queen Elizabeth I. They were followed by the Poor Law (Amendment) Acts, 1834 and 1844, consolidated by the Bastardy (Amendment) Act, 1872.

For many years, there has been a strong element of criminal law in affiliation law. Until the Bill is passed, the mother of an illegitimate child will still be at a disadvantage compared with the mother of a legitimate child. However, medical advances, for example, the availability of blood tests, had now almost entirely eliminated blackmail.

Let me give some examples. At present, the mother must give evidence in person. That rule rests upon the words in Section 4(1) of the Affiliation Proceedings Act, 1957:

"… the court shall hear the evidence of the mother (notwithstanding any consent or admission on the part of the defendant) and such other evidence as she may produce …".

Sometimes the mother has died and so the complaint cannot be made for it has been held that the magistrates have no jurisdiction to make an order in such a case. That may help the hon. Member for Pontypool (Mr. Abse) with some of the issues that he wishes to raise.

The absolute rule can cause hardship and may cause unnecessary difficulty to the mother. If the mother, having made a complaint, becomes seriously ill or temporarily of unsound mind, the substance of the complaint may be uncontested for the man alleged to be the father may be willing to admit paternity. It has been suggested to me that in as many as 75 per cent. of affiliation cases the issue of the paternity is not contested which makes 6 it easier for the mother. In the 25 per cent. of cases where the defendant is not prepared to admit paternity, it seems unnecessary to require the mother to be present in court to give evidence.

With those few words outlining the purpose of the Bill, I hope that it will meet with the Committee's approval.

Clause 2
TIME FOR APPLICATION FOR SUMMONS

Mr. Carlisle
: I beg to move Amendment No. 2, in page 2, line 15, at end add:

(2) In section 30(3, a) of the Maintenance Orders (Reciprocal Enforcement) Act 1972 (payment under the law applied by a court outside the United Kingdom of money towards the maintenance of a child) for the words "twelve months" there shall be substituted the words "three years (or twelve months, in the case of a complaint under the said Act of 1924)".

It may be convenient also to discuss Amendment No. 4, which is consequential.

The Amendment makes an alteration to Section 30 of the Maintenance Orders (Reciprocal Enforcement) Act, 1972, and is consequential upon Clause 2 of the Bill.

As I explained earlier, Part II of the JPEG 11 Maintenance Orders (Reciprocal Enforcement) Act, 1972—in which the hon. Member for Newark (Mr. Bishop) and I were both involved during its short passage through Committee—gives effect to the 1956 United Nations Convention on the Recovery Abroad of Maintenance. In order to do this it enables a person abroad to make an application for maintenance from a person in England in accordance with the law in England. At the moment, to be in accordance with the Affiliation Proceedings Act, 1957, that application had to be made within the time limit provided by that Act, namely, within 12 months of the alleged father's return to England, within one year of the child's birth, or at any time if the alleged father could claim maintenance for the child within one year of its birth.

Accordingly, for the purpose of an application under the 1972 Act, the mother with an overseas order is enabled to take proceedings through an affiliation order by providing in Section 30(3, a) of the 1972 Act that the applicant shall be able to meet the time limit specified in the Affiliation Proceedings Act, 1957, if she can prove that the defendant has within 12 months of the birth of the child paid money for its maintenance in pursuance of the requirements of the law applied by a court outside the United Kingdom.

Clause 2 extends the time limit in the Affiliation Proceedings Act from 12 months to three years. It therefore becomes necessary to amend Section 30(3, a) of the 1972 Act to refer to the revised time limit. The Bill does not alter the substance of the law of Northern Ireland, and the reference to the twelve months' time limit is therefore retained for applications against men in Northern Ireland.

Amendment agreed to.

Clause 2, as amended, ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Clause 3
PROCEDURE ON APPLICATION FOR, OR FOR VARIATION, REVIVAL OR REVOCATION OF, AFFILIATION ORDER

11.0 a.m.

: I beg to move Amendment No. 3, in page 2, line 32, leave out subsection (3) and add: 12

(3) Section 5(1) of the Legitimacy Act 1959 section 24(8) of the said Act of 1966 and in section 17(3) of the Maintenance Orders (Reciprocal Enforcement) Act 1972 the words "or section 5 of the Legitimacy Act 1959" are hereby repealed.

This Amendment alters Section 17(3) of the Maintenance Orders (Reciprocal Enforcement) Act, 1972, which is consequential upon the repeal, by this Bill, of Section 5(1) of the Legitimacy Act, 1959.

Section 17 of the Maintenance Orders (Reciprocal Enforcement) Act, 1972, makes provision for certain proceedings under that Act in magistrates' courts in England and Wales to be domestic proceedings within the meaning of Section 56 of the Magistrates' Courts Act, 1952. Proceedings under the 1972 Act can be taken in respect of the variation and revocation of affiliation orders. Section 5(1) of the Legitimacy Act, 1959, specifically excludes proceedings for the variation and revocation of an affiliation order from those which it describes as "domestic proceedings". However, since Clause 3 repeals Section 5(1) of the Legitimacy Act and, therefore, they can in future be treated as domestic proceedings, there is no longer the necessity to have the reference to that Act in the 1972 Measure.

11.30 a.m.

In affiliation proceedings, the practice is quite different. Children are born as the result of the activities of a man and a woman. It is necessary to have legislation which corresponds to the needs of unmarried fathers as well as to the needs of unmarried mothers. It is easy when dealing with matters relating to unmarried mothers and their children to forget the father, but births do not take place by immaculate conception. Therefore, it becomes necessary to ask ourselves whether our courts are equipped to do all they can to give a child two parents.

No maintenance order is the equivalent of a father, particularly a natural father. As has already been revealed this morning, in 75 per cent. of the affiliation orders that come before the courts paternity is not contested. It is true that a considerable proportion of that 75 per cent. must consist of men who are married and thus unable to marry. However, a fair proportion, from any analysis that I have seen, are single men.

I do not want to appear totally un-romantic, but it is not the general practice of men to sleep with women if they do not like them. This means, again from my experience in former days of this type of proceeding, that it is a fact that the unmarried father has been consorting with the girl for a long time: they will have been going around together.

One of the most important pieces of evidence that occurs and recurs in adducing proof of the paternity of the man is the fact that not only was he in regular association with the woman over a long period, but when the child was born, he visited the hospital. The father is often turned away by more punitive nurses who are so sympathetically identified with the unmarried mother that they regard the man as someone who should be punished and banished for having put this woman into this terrible position. Nevertheless, the sending of flowers and gifts takes place frequently in cases of this kind and becomes the substance upon which the lawyer is able to make out his case to 22 show corroboration of the allegation that the mother had had intercourse with the man.

One often sees a pattern in this type of case where there are feelings of some guilt or concern expressed by way of gifts or flowers, or even by sending clothes to the children. The parents of both sides are often lining up. The parents of the girl say the boy is no good because he has seduced her and abandoned his responsibilities; the parents of the boy say that the girl is no good because she allowed him to sleep with her. So one sees these family battles which naturally operate significantly upon immature young men who, faced with the unexpected situation of prematurely becoming fathers, shrink from taking the ultimate responsibility because they are not encouraged to do so. On the contrary, they arc discouraged by the parental group surrounding them.

It is important for us to recognise that it is difficult for men to anticipate their own parenthood. Women can and often do so. My own little daughter used to play with her dolls and long before she reached teen age she had anticipated, as it were, her motherhood. However, I do not think that it is the general experience of men that they are able to anticipate their parenthood and understand it until they become fathers.

Judging by the reactions of the men who are brought before the courts on issues of this kind, I would say that there are considerable numbers of men who are full of ambivalence and guilt about their reaction in yielding to parental and other pressures not to marry the girl. People may say that they should not be forced to marry, but, judging by the statistics of women who get married while they are pregnant, it is clear that there are large sections of the population who have premarital intercourse and then marry successfully.

As matters stand, although in domestic proceedings the court may often actively intervene to bring together the husband and wife, there appear to be no powers to bring together the unmarried father and the mother. I am aware, too, that some courts believe that they have no power to bring in the probation officer in cases of this kind.

I am persuaded from my experience of years ago that a solution to the problem of the immature unmarried father could be helped forward if he had some social work support which enabled him to get over his first feelings of apprehension and was able to face up to the responsibilities of parenthood.

I am anxious, therefore, in moving the Clause to know whether the matter could be looked at to see whether there are any powers to bring in the probation officer in the circumstances I have outlined. If there are, I should like to know whether the courts could be encouraged to use them. Many courts do not believe that they possess them. We want to make our courts healing posts rather than punitive agencies. It is with that in view that I put the new Clause before the Committee.
