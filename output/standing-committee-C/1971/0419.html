I beg to move Amendment No. 76, in page 1, line 10, at beginning insert:

"Subject to the provisions of this Act".

This is a formal paving Amendment to new Clause No. 9 which I tabled in response to the legitimate complaints that four months' notice can, under certain circumstances, be a grossly onerous burden on the promoter of a night assembly, and can, on occasions, preclude him from holding it at all. Perhaps this is not the appropriate place to go into full reasons as to why we originally put down four months. I felt that two months was about right for the actual physical process, from the date of permission being given to the date of the assembly being held, to carry 230 out the requirements of the local authority.

In my anxiety to see that the appeal procedure could properly be carried out, and without putting into the hands of the local authority the weapon of delay, I felt it necessary to add a further two months to the period, so that the appeal procedure would be properly carried out and resolved and full time given for the organisers to meet the requirements.

However, it seems that having adopted that view, if the promoter of the assembly is prepared to concur with the requirements of the local authority, there is no reason for the two months' delay for the appeals or, indeed, for any delay if the promoter feels able to fulfil the requirements in the shorter period. The new Clause says that if a promoter does exactly what the local authority requires and selects a suitable site, he shall be allowed to go ahead and the four months' notice which appears elsewhere in the Bill will not be required.

This will be a help to promoters who claim already that they concur with the local authority's requirements and to promoters who in the future will learn to do so. When the Bill becomes law it is to be hoped that most promoters will take advantage of this concession. There seems to be no reason why they should not, and if co-operation between them and the local authority begins to build up, this gives them the opportunity to exercise their rights to hold a pop festival or a night assembly of any sort at whatever notice is convenient to them and the local authority.

The new Clause was introduced at our instigation and I hope that hon. Gentlemen opposite will feel that it is both a sensible concession and a useful addition to the Bill.

Mr. Wiggin
: The Clause is to do with the recovery of expenses by the authorities concerned. There are some complicated legal definitions—what is an authority, what is a local authority—which are germane to what we are discussing.

Amendment No. 82 is to prevent expenses incurred by a police authority from being recoverable. This seems appropriate since all other matters relating to public order of night assemblies are outside the Bill. Police authorities as constituted by the Police Act, 1964, are not local authorities, and the insertion of "local", as proposed in the Amendment, will produce the desired effect. A casual reader of the Bill would not have reached that conclusion.

A local authority might incur two kinds of expenses in the event of a night assembly being held in its area. The first is the expenses incurred by local authorities and statutory undertakers in the pursuance of JPEG 257 their statutory functions, and the second would be expenses incurred at the request of the promoters. For example, the promoters might ask for a road, or an entrance, and they would expect to pay. I believe that to be outside the terms of the Bill. It is a normal business contract and it is not my concern.

In the first instance, however, where a local authority says that it will require something to be done, and does the work on behalf of the promoters, that is fulfilling an obligation as a result of a statutory requirement.

Amendment No. 84 deals with the first of my two cases and would ensure that only:

"expenses necessarily incurred … for the purposes, or in consequence, of the night assembly…"

would be recoverable. Expenses incurred at the request of the promoters would be recoverable under Clause 3, but it should be noted that security for them could not be taken under the provisions of new Clause No. 8. The local authority could ask the promoters to pay in advance, or otherwise treat the matter as a normal business deal.

There are no other major points. The matter is technical. The question of the police has bothered many people, but if we were to introduce the extra costs of policing for large assemblies, all kinds of questions would present themselves—football matches, race meetings, daytime assemblies and so forth. By redrafting the Clause, the matter is now clarified.
