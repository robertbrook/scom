Mr. Patrick Cormack
: I fully appreciate the defects of this Bill, since I have not had expert outside advice on its drafting, 4 and I also appreciate that it is unlikely to proceed beyond this morning's Committee stage. At the same time, the Committee affords an opportunity to debate a topic of considerable interest and importance, and one which has excited a great deal of attention throughout the country since I first attempted to introduce a similar Bill in May of last year.

This Clause seeks to amend the provisions of the Civic Amenities Act, 1967, by requiring local authorities to designate conservation areas for the purpose of ensuring the protection of the environs of every cathedral and grade A church building.

Subsection (1) deals with about 2,000 grade A ecclesiastical buildings and 42 Church of England cathedrals. Thirty cathedrals, plus Westminster Abbey, are already in designated conservation areas, but it appears to me and to many other people that all our most important ecclesiastical buildings should be protected as far as possible by having their environs so designated.

The Clause would also require local authorities to consider requests to designate conservation areas in the vicinity of all places of worship of intrinsic architectural and historic importance.

Our great heritage of religious buildings—cathedrals, churches, chapels—contributes much to the beauty of the landscape and the dignity of our times. They are admired and enjoyed by those of all faiths and of none. It would seem, therefore, that stronger legislative protection is necessary for them. So often they are the focal points of town and country scenes. Perhaps I may quote Ruskin, which would be entirely appropriate in a debate of this nature. He said:

"Great nations write their autobiographies in three manuscripts: the book of their deeds, the book of their words and the book of their art and architecture; not one of these books can be understood unless we read the two others but of the three the only trustworthy one is the last."

Commenting on this in that famous series, "Civilization", Lord Clark said:

"On the whole I think this is true. Writers and politicians come out with all sorts of edifying sentiments but they are what is known as declarations of intent. If I had to say which was a truer commentary on society, a speech by a Minister of Housing or the actual buildings put up in his time, I should believe the buildings."

That is no reflection either on my right hon. Friend the Minister responsible for JPEG 5 housing or my hon. Friend who is present this morning.

We are concerned here with a vital part of the fabric of our civilisation. The provisions of the Clause seek to combat the unthinking destructive acts of those planners, developers, and industrialists, who so often have neither a regard for architectural beauty or harmony nor a sense of history.

In subsection (3) I refer to the Historic Buildings Council and the Historic Churches Preservation Trust. That is in connection with the provision that would require local authorities to consider requests to designate conservation areas in the vicinity of places of worship of intrinsic architectual and historic importance. It is important that we should not be seen to be proposing some sweeping change without building in proper and adequate safeguards and precautions. It is because of the accumulated wisdom and experience of the Historic Buildings Council and the particular knowledge and experience of the Historic Churches Preservation Trust that I write them into the Bill at this stage. I fully appreciate and acknowledge that this formula may not meet with the approval of the Government, and I should be happy to consider alterations.

When I first sought to introduce a Bill of this nature in May, 1971, I was inundated with letters from all over the country. It would bore the Committee beyond endurance were I to quote vast numbers of these. They not only told stark, and very often tragic tales, of churches falling into disrepair, and of the loving efforts of devoted congregations being frustrated, but also terrible tales of beautiful village and town centres, and lovely areas of the countryside, being despoiled because of the insensitivity of planners.

I came across one example of this—a church which I attended in Lincolnshire for many years. We woke up one morning literally to the sudden sound of the bulldozer, and with piles of bricks being deposited within a matter of feet of the chancel wall of the lovely Norman church, with its Saxon tower. The Vicar telephoned me, and we discovered to our absolute horror that planning permission had been given for a bungalow to be erected on this piece of land. We founded a preservation society within 24 hours. We raised £1,000—it was not at today's 6 land prices—within a week and we bought the land and saved the church. That was a success story. So often the stories are not so successful—the ending is far from happy.

I would like to refer to another example from the Midlands—from Coleshill, in the constituency of my hon. Friend the Member for Meriden (Mr. Speed). I mention this particularly because the Vicar not only wrote me several letters but asked me to go to see him. Together with my hon. Friend the Member for Meriden we spent a Monday morning looking at the lovely church and flats which had been put up obscuring its environment. "They shout down the church", says the vicar.

To quote from the local newspaper:

"Council flats being built in front of Coleshill Parish Church were in 'violent contrast' to the church buildings, Canon Frank Morgan, the Vicar, said."

He added that:

"the reddy-blue bricks are reminiscent of Victorian institutions and the black mortar is like that used in prisons."

This lovely church, which was surrounded by large Georgian houses, is now completely cribbed, cabined and confined by rather ugly, gaunt blocks of flats. My hon. Friend the Member for Meriden could do nothing about that, nor could I, or anybody else. It was a graphic illustration of something that could have been prevented had the law had more teeth.

It is because examples like Meriden can be multiplied throughout the country that we write the Clause into the Bill. No one would suggest that the Civic Amenities Act, 1967, is not a splendid piece of legislation. No one could suggest that we share no regard for these important matters, but we are dealing with matters of national importance. On a local front, when there is a contest between conservationist and the developer, it is often the developer who wins, because he has the big financial battalions on his side. A brief discussion of the Clause should give us a chance to sound the tocsin.

The present law requires local authori- JPEG 17 ties to determine which parts of their areas are of special architectural or historic interest, the character or appearance of which they consider it desirable to preserve or enhance, and to designate them as conservation areas. They are given a free hand in the matter and do not need the consent of the Secretary of State. About 1,700 areas have been so designated in England. Designation is an earnest of the local authority's intention to pay special attention to the character of the area when exercising any functions under the Town and Country Planning Act, 1971, and the Local Authorities (Historic Buildings) Act, 1962. It draws the attention of other authorities and the Government to the importance attached by the local authority to the preservation and enhancement of that area.

The Clause requires a local planning authority to designate a conservation area even if nothing in the area other than the church itself was of special architectural or historic interest. It seeks to interfere with the freedom of local authorities—which are directly elected bodies—and their discretion to manage their affairs. The provisions of the Civic Amenities Act. now incorporated in the Town and Country Planning Act, 1971, give local authorities the freedom to work out their own programme of designations. My hon. Friend the Member for Sudbury and Woodbridge produced evidence that the Committee must seriously consider with regard to that aspect.
