[Mr. ALAN FITCH in the Chair]

NATIONAL HEALTH SERVICE (FAMILY PLANNING) AMENDMENT BILL

10.30 a.m.

Resolved,

That during the proceedings on the National Health Service (Family Planning) Amendment Bill the Committee do meet on Wednesdays at half-past Ten o'clock.—[ Mr. Whitehead.]

The Chairman
: Before I call the first Amendment, I remind the Committee that there is a Financial Resolution in connection with the Bill. Copies are available in the room.

I also call attention to the fact that adequate notice should be given to Amendments. As a general rule, I do not intend to call manuscript Amendments. My provisional list of selected Amendments is available on the table.

Clause 1
PROVISION OF VOLUNTARY VASECTOMY SERVICES

Mr. Leo Abse
: I beg to move Amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 11, leave out paragraph ( a) and insert:

( a) after the words "giving of advice on contraception", there shall be inserted the words "and voluntary vasectomy (provided that the arrangements made for giving of advice on vasectomy shall include either the advice of a psychiatrist or advice given by a counselling service approved by the Secretary of State), and after the words "seeking advice on contraception", there shall be inserted the words "and voluntary vasectomy".

 I shall begin with a comment made by my hon. Friend the Member for Halifax (Dr. Summerskill) on Second Reading when she wisely pointed out that this Bill is

"… based to some extent on knowledge of anatomy, physiology, psychology, psychiatry and surgery,"

 and that

"… it is difficult for hon. Members and the public to appreciate the results of the operation or the method of the operation. … "—[OFFICIAL REPORT, 21st January, 1972; Vol. 829, c. 871.]

 The admonition given by the hon. Lady was one which could be addressed

4 to the House and to the Committee. Quite clearly, we are dealing with an extraordinarily delicate matter. We are presuming to say that there should be State and local authority intervention so that those who wish can decide, for ever, to give up their parenthood. There can be few more serious options open to any human being. Parenthood is the opportunity afforded to large numbers of people to reach a higher degree of maturation. It is through parenthood that we have our links with past and future.

Sir George Sinclair
: I quite appreciate the point the hon. Member is making, but surely, it is nothing new. This option has been open to and taken by women for many years. The choice is open now to men and women.

Mr. Abse
: I suggest that the novelty of the situation is twofold. First, before a woman is sterilised there are usually other overwhelming medical reasons or she already has a considerable number of children. If the hon. Gentleman is suggesting that it is medical practice to select women who are unmarried, who have no children, and to sterilise them for non-medical reasons—

Sir G. Sinclair
: The hon. Gentleman knows that is not my intention.

Mr. Abse
: If he is suggesting that, I take his point, but he is not. He is confusing the Committee. How over-anxious the Committee is when I start to declare what I should have thought is a truism, that parenthood is of great value, that it links us with the past, with eternity, and with the future! When I say that, what anxiety it arouses!

Mr. Douglas Houghton
rose—

Mr. Abse
: We are going to have a rumbustious Committee. I make such non-controversial comments on which I should have thought there would have been general agreement, yet they arouse the distinguished Chairman of the Parliamentary Labour Party and also the idiosyncratic Member of the Tory Party to intervene.

Mr. Geoffrey Finsberg
: The only anxiety on my part is that the hon. Gentleman should not seek deliberately to confuse the issue in his almost Oxford Union Debating Society speech.

Mr. Abse
: If I can make an Oxford Union speech in two minutes, then I do far better than when I speak in the Oxford Union. Hon. Gentlemen should contain themselves. Perhaps when they have heard what I have to say, they will not be so nervous as they so demonstrably are.

I was saying that this is a fateful decision. It would be a fateful decision if a woman took it, as well as a man. Surely, nobody disputes this. Surely, nobody disputes that when women do take this decision, we find that it is done when women either have compelling medical reasons for it to be done or when they have a considerable number of children.

I am addressing myself to a time when, as yet, beyond the fact that the man must be 30, there are no such checks and, beyond that, it is suggested that a man should be able to make this fateful decision without the necessity—as the Bill stands at the moment—of any discussion or guidance of any kind. I know that certain hon. Gentlemen take a somewhat anarchic view, such as the right hon. Member for Sowerby (Mr. Houghton) who already, without anticipating his Amendment, has indicated quite clearly by it that he does not even want the 30 years of age provision. He believes so passionately, in this connection, in the freedom of the individual—

Mr. Houghton
: May I ask my hon. Friend not to anticipate my Amendment or my speech on it. At this moment he is moving his own Amendment. Can he not stick to that?

Mr. Abse
: As the Bill stands, the only check is the 30 years of age check. I have to take into account how I can suggest to this friendly Committee that there is a need for wise counselling before the fateful decision is taken. If the right hon. Gentleman thinks that I should ignore the impossibility of even a youngster of 18 having this operation, that is not what he is doing by putting down the Amendment. Clearly, I have to take that into account as a possibility. Is it being suggested, either in anticipating the right hon. Gentleman's Amendment, as I am, or, taking the sponsors' existing position—

The Chairman
: Order. The hon. Gentleman must not anticipate anybody's Amendment other than his own.

6

Mr. Abse
: I have to take into account the state of the Bill. With great respect, Mr. Fitch, I have to take into account the fact that I am asking the Committee, in the light of the age of the man, whether there should be counselling or no counselling. It would be quite wrong not to take account of the fact that there is clearly a view among some hon. Gentlemen that they could oppose or support counselling in the case of people who are even younger than 30 years of age.

In the usual course of events I should have thought that nobody would have a vasectomy without having counselling. I do not doubt that through the existing agencies, such as the family planning bodies and the Simon Trust, there are some forms of counselling. If anyone has taken the trouble to investigate and discuss with those who are engaged at the moment in vasectomy, through those or other agencies, they will find an extraordinary amount of criticism of the standards and criteria of counselling.

We have to accept that all sorts of disasters could result from this operation, although many benefits could come from it. It could be a means of ending or creating intolerable marital situations. It could mean that people could have greater control over the planning of their parenthood, but, because of the irreversible character of the operation, it could also bring them the most appalling distress in the long run.

We do not have to take into account the obvious benefits. In supporting the Second Reading of the Bill, I accepted the principle of the Bill that there must be circumstances in which, as a measure of last resort, as my hon. Friend the Member for Halifax (Dr. Summerskill) pointed out, vasectomy is a socially and personally beneficial operation. However, our task in this Committee is not to start zealously campaigning for this as part of a population programme. Our task is to examine the Bill so that, as far as we can, we avoid any of the difficulties of the minority who, for the wrong reasons or without sufficient forethought or because of unexpected later events, find themselves in deep trouble because they have had an operation that is irreversible.

In The Times this morning there is a letter from a research doctor, seeking to reprimand my hon. Friend the Member for Halifax, because in the debate she JPEG 7 emphasised the irreversible character of the operation. This doctor has suggested that in 50 per cent. of the cases it is irreversible, but he does not deal with the point, which the literature indicates, that even if the operation could be reversed, fertility problems may still remain. The reproof in The Times is ill deserved. We cannot act on the assumption that perhaps it could be reversed.

Mr. Phillip Whitehead
: Who has acted on that assumption in introducing this Bill? Every speech made in support of the Bill has been on the assumption that the operation is regarded as irreversible when people go for the operation.

Mr. Abse
: I am obliged to my hon. Friend; I know he has taken that point of view. However, when, on the morning of a Committee, one sees a letter of that kind reproving the hon. Lady, then, when presenting my argument as to why there is a need for counselling, I think I am entitled to draw the attention of the Committee to the letter and to warn them that whatever may be said there, it would be quite wrong of us to act in the individual case except upon the presumption that it is irreversible.

However, the letter has a greater significance to the question of counselling than the misplaced reproof to the hon. Lady. It is demonstrating that there are a considerable number of people who, quite obviously, have changed their minds. One would not know of this matter at all. The Nuffield Institute would not have had the research which is available to indicate that a certain percentage of people have tried to reverse the operation, if it were not for the obvious fact that there are people who have changed their minds and have sought, either successfully or unsuccessfully, to undo what they originally did.

10.45 a.m.

Let us examine the situation in which no guidance is given. A young man who marries and has two children, may find afterwards that there is great marital trouble within the family unit. People react to marital trouble in different ways. When things are rocking within a marriage, it is sometimes believed that having another child will bring the parties together. Equally, however, when things are 8 wrong within a marriage, when the frigidity of the woman is said to arise from her fear of another pregnancy, it is possible, as anyone who has read case histories on this matter will know, that she will try to persuade the husband that a vasectomy is a way to attain a degree of freedom in their relationship which could allow the marriage to be healed.

Persuasive as such a suggestion may be, it is usually as foolish to believe that marital troubles can be healed by having another child as to believe that they can be healed by renouncing parenthood for ever. It would be unwise if a person in such marital stress obtained a vasectomy when what was, in fact, required was far more subtle marriage guidance counselling than that offered by a surgeon's knife.

Mr. Finsberg
: Do I gather that the hon. Member is submitting as the basis of this part of his speech that people might subsequently regret having had the operation? I wonder whether he has had an opportunity of digesting the Simon Population Trust report. It did a survey of 1,004 men who had had the operation and asked them if there were any regrets. Six of the men said, "Yes", which is 0.6 per cent. of the survey and of the respondents' wives 14 said "Yes", 989 said "No", 1.4 per cent. I hope the hon. Member will agree that what he has been saying is substantially contradicted by the Simon Trust which, after all, is recognised by most people to be a highly reputable body.

Mr. Abse
: I do not think that even the Simon Trust research in any way contradicts what I said. The percentage of women who regret it, on the basis of that report, bears out my contention. The Simon Trust are clients in their own cause. As shown by the independent inquiry, 16 per cent. of them, according to the most objective tests that could be made, were psychologically disturbed as a consequence. I accept the fact that the overwhelming majority appear to be satisfied, although it is pretty late not to be satisfied. Most people can make a virtue out of a fault when they know that the fault is irremediable, and this must also be taken into account. One must be quite fair about it.

But even allowing for the figure of the Simon Trust or, what I find much more JPEG 9 satisfactory, an independent account where there certainly was no preconceived view, and which went as far as 16 per cent., surely both those figures emphasise the need for screening, and effective screening. Remember that the Simon Trust have screening, and even when they have the screening of the type that they have, it is quite clear that a substantial percentage of the women in particular—and, in the case of the other report, of the men and women—felt most dismayed at a later stage.

Therefore, since this is the case—whatever our preconceived views may be upon policies, or whatever our views may be upon the desirability of more rather than fewer people having this operation—should we not say that if it is at all possible we want to make certain that those who have it have pondered and considered hard and well before the decision is taken?

I mentioned in the House a personal case which came to my attention and which I found extraordinarily distressing. I will mask the facts so that the parties cannot be identified, but, beyond that, what I say will have verisimilitude with the actual situation. A man in his early thirties came to me professionally in order to start an action against a hospital because he claimed that his child had died in hospital as a consequence of the negligence of the doctors and nurses who were treating his child. When I examined the facts I could see, from what he told me and from further inquiries which I made, that there was no substance in the allegation, that the dedicated doctors and nurses had done more than one could demand of any human beings in order to save the life of that man's son.

The man had two children; the son had now died. When he came to me in his desire to make a scapegoat of the hospital, he did not tell me of his recent past. The recent past was, as I found when the unfortunate man had broken down and a catharsis took place, that he had had a vasectomy in comparatively recent months before his son had died. I regret to say that it was quite clear to me that there had been the most perfunctory guidance given to this young man. The position was, quite inevitably, that he felt, at least unconsciously, his own guilt. He believed, being a simple man, that retribution had fallen upon 10 him. Perhaps even more sophisticated men would have felt the same thing. But now he had lost his son, and he had lost for ever the possibility of having a son. People who feel a sense of guilt often try to project on to others their responsibility. Naturally, therefore, as so often is the case, he wanted to become litigious.

Such a situation is not one which we should like to see occur, even if we wish to provide vasectomy. I do not suppose, whatever safeguards we put forward, that we can deal with every contingency in the future. But certainly when families are small, when they are young and when men are young, the realities of the situation must be presented to them so that they understand the natural hazards of life. If they make the choice, they should do so with a very clear understanding.

Again, we must accept the fact that the overwhelming majority of divorces take place between people who either have no children, where the matter is not relevant, or who have one or two children. If one examines the statistics, one finds that this is the position. Since that is the reality of the situation, we must accept the fact that we can anticipate, if vasectomy is proferred, that there will be drawn out of that section of people whose marriages are unfortunately going to break down, a higher proportion of people with small families than we may have otherwise expected. If a young man has a vasectomy while he is married, he is faced with the position that in the future, if his marriage breaks down, he can have no second family. If he remarries, his wife will be barren. That is an awful thing.

Mr. Whitehead
: Will my hon. Friend not agree that such a man and wife are in the position of the one couple in 10 who are naturally infertile and who must therefore get a family by adoption? This is not necessarily the worst fate in the world.

Mr. Abse
: I am sitting on a Home Office Advisory Committee dealing with adoption, and I must say that I find that remark of the sponsor extraordinarily naive. Nobody who has delved into this problem has failed to discover the extraordinary yearning that comes from a large number of women in this country who regard children not as mistakes but as blessings. To suggest that it does not JPEG 11 matter whether some wives are involuntarily to be deprived of the blessings of motherhood by any action of ours, since they can adopt children, shows a degree of insensitivity which I am quite sure my hon. Friend did not intend to convey.

Mr. John Biggs-Davison
: Is it not also a common experience, and I am sure it is the experience of hon. Members in this Committee, that persons with an intense desire for children are often extremely reluctant to adopt them? Unfortunately, this is the case.

Mr. Abse
: This is the case. It is an extraordinarily difficult matter. Many men cannot take this situation, because the child remains a constant reproof to them for their lack of potency. I do not understand how my hon. Friend can be so casual in suggesting that it does not matter and that the now sterile man and the barren woman are only in the same position as those who are sterile by the law of nature.

Mr. Whitehead
: When I say that I was the adopted child of, as he puts it, barren parents, I think my hon. Friend will agree that he is perhaps unwise to say that I am being casual or frivolous. Adopted children can be a very great blessing.

Mr. Abse
: I was not suggesting that my hon. Friend was frivolous. I had observed that he had commented that he was an adopted child. Each of us, when we come forward with propositions of this kind, must also have an insight into our own motivations as far as possible. I will say no more. This applies to us all, and there is a dynamic which often operates against us all. There are also patterns of behaviour among us, springing from our cultural and individual backgrounds. The hon. Member is not exempt from that because he has had the good fortune of having secure adoptive parents.

It is quite possible that people would like to change their minds, and the question for any reasonable person to ask is how we can provide in the Bill a screen so that the choice is fully understood and appreciated and the deepest needs are taken into account. At the minimum, a psychiatrist should be available. Psychiatrists are held in high or low esteem 12 according to one's prejudice. One could talk in terms of much stronger safeguards than one psychiatrist, for they have the fraility of every man and woman. I have contented myself, in an endeavour to be realistic, with suggesting that there should be a psychiatrist.

Mr. Laurie Pavitt

Gentleman if I were to tell him that I received a letter from the Royal College JPEG 13 of Psychiatrists supporting the Bill as it stands, but recommending that such an operation should not be carried out except on the recommendation of at least two medical practitioners, one of whom should be the family doctor and the other preferably a psychiatrist.

Mr. Abse
: I am much obliged for the sponsor's frankness and helpfulness, but it is only what I would have expected. I regret that such a responsible suggestion is not in the Bill. If I had had the letter, I might have slavishly followed the suggestion of the psychiatrists, to whom, after all, one should go to ask about it.

An issue of such importance, touching upon the lives of a large number of the living and the unborn, should not be left to a letter passing to a well-intentioned hon. Member putting forward a Bill which in principle is needed. The Committee is entitled to ask what advice has been taken and, if it has not been taken, whether advice will be taken and what memoranda are in existence which the Committee can study. It is not good enough, although I welcome the sponsor's frankness, that it is presented in the form of a letter which is short and without adequate depth. I am sure that thinking has gone into it, but we are entitled to have some of the ratio decidendi which allowed the writers of the letter to come to such a conclusion.

To come back to the point which the hon. Lady made, in my opinion it would be highly desirable that the psychiatrist should screen an applicant. I have put in an alternative which is that it should be given

"by a counselling service approved by the Secretary of State".

 Perhaps the Secretary of State does not like it. We know what happened in the case of the Abortion Act when the Secretary of State agreed in the first instance, through his spokesman in this Committee room, to accept the fact that he might have to have some jurisdiction over the type of doctor involved in determining whether an abortion should or should not take place. The attitude was abandoned under the pressures of the Committee, the House and outside groups.

Can it be suggested that the Secretary of State could vest such a serious matter in the hands of a local council to decide whether the guidance counselling is or is not up to the standards required? Is it 14 seriously suggested—I hope it is not—that the counselling can be determined by people who are not fanatics but extraordinarily zealous in pursuing this particular form of family planning, that they can decide who shall do the guidance counselling and what should be the qualifications of those who do it? Will it be a counselling of the kind that can be very patchy, as in the case of some marriage guidance councils? I hope the Secretary of State will not retreat from the position that we in the House should be able to ask him questions if we were in any way concerned that a counselling service set up to meet the requirements of the Bill was slight, inadequate or badly staffed. Unless we have the opportunity to turn to the Secretary of State to remedy any blemishes of this kind, the House will, by passing the Bill, have forfeited its major opportunity of supervision of an irrevocable and serious operation.

I do not want to indulge in hyperbole, but I hope the anxiety originally expressed by hon. Members when I began to speak is to some degree allayed. I presume that they are rational, intelligent men who are as concerned as I would be in making certain that people who should not have vasectomies do not have them. I act on the assumption that in their zeal for population programming, they are not indifferent to who has the operation as long as somebody has it.

Although the voguish population lobby showed itself strongly in the course of the Second Reading debate, I presume that here we are dealing with the individual personal problems of people and families. I presume that we are talking about family planning. When I was assisting in putting the Family Planning Bill through, I encountered many different attitudes, and I expect that they still exist. There were those who wanted family planning and those who wanted birth control. There were those who thought that family planning meant planned parenthood, and those who, either consciously or unconsciously, did not seem to want to have children at all.

Mr. Abse
: These are the dangers of rushing into vasectomy. I am not trying to wreck the Bill, but the Government spokesman indicated that it may be desirable for this issue to be considered some years hence when we have integrated the health services and the involvement of all the medical people concerned in the issue will be easier.
