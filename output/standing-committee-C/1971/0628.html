clause Clause 1
title MINIMUM ANNUAL HOLIDAY PERIOD

Question proposed, That the Clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Denis Howell: Clause 1 deals with annual holiday entitlement. This subject was discussed in only a cursory manner on Second Reading, when attention seemed to be focused mainly on early retirement. Nevertheless, it is a matter of considerable importance.

On Second Reading the Minister was good enough to concede that in questions concerning industrial negotiation the movement towards extended holidays was attaining greater significance. That is true. As president of my own union, APEX, I have myself been negotiating this year and in every one of the claims an increase in the amount of holiday entitlement has been insisted upon. There is undoubtedly a desire throughout the country for people to have more leisure. It is also significant that many trade union leaders now pay more and more attention in their speeches to this aspect of the industrial situation. This is due in a large measure to the increasing levels of unemployment, or what on Second Reading I called the increasing technological unemployment.

The Minister told us on Second Reading that there was no evidence at that time—February last—that unemployment was due to structural or technological factors: it could be that it was due to a stagnating Col. 4 economy. He went on to outline the steps which the Chancellor had taken and which he told us must be seen through before an effective judgment could be made as to whether the increase in unemployment was due to economic or to technological factors and changes—automation, greater efficiency and all that goes with it. That was four months ago.

We have now had the effects in the economy of a whole year of the Chancellor's last Budget but one when he put substantially increased amounts of money into the economy. In this year's Budget he has done the same thing; he has put an additional boost into the economy. Economists estimate that in a period of 12 months covering two Budgets, the economy has had a boost of over £2,000 million. Yet no one would claim that the graph of unemployment shows a downward trend; although the numbers of unemployed this month are reduced, it is a marginal reduction compared with what it ought to be in the middle of the summer, and there cannot be any doubt for much longer that the case for saying that we have a great deal of technological unemployment is well made out.

That leads to the question what the Clause poses—whether as a nation, we are now earning the right to more leisure time because we can produce in a shorter working week and a shorter working year the goods that society needs to sustain itself. I have said before that there is no virtue in work for its own sake, although every man should have a worthwhile job, for not to have is a soul-destroying business. Nevertheless, the technological revolution of our society is not being accompanied by any social revolution which enables us to take advantage of the situation we have ourselves created.

One of the first ways in which we can do that, and therefore help the unemployment situation, is by increasing the amount of the annual holiday. But I do not rest the case for the Clause on that alone. I emphasise again the modern drudgery of the treadmill society in which so many of our industrial workers have to compete. They are working day after day and week after week in the same factories, pulling the same levers, pushing the same knobs, doing the same menial jobs. That is one of the by-products of automation, and it is clear to me that in those circumstances there is a case for the Image Col. 5 industrial worker to enjoy the same kind of holidays that many white-collar workers enjoy, particularly in the Civil Service, local government, the teaching profession, and so on.

I see no logic in a situation where a man has to go to work early in the morning, at 7.30 to 8 o'clock, and is expected to have only half the holiday taken by people who are employed in rather more pleasant pursuits for a smaller amount of time per day, per week, and per year. Moreover, there can be no doubt that in the provision of holidays this country lags far behind many others.

This is not the place to debate the pros and cons of entry into the Common Market, but it is fair to say in passing that one of the tests of the Government's good intentions is comparability of benefits when we go into Europe. We have heard much about comparability of the burdens which the British nation will be called upon to bear. I suggest to the Minister that he should give evidence of his Government's good faith—I am sure I carry all my hon. Friends with me, whatever views we have on the Market—by coming forward at this stage and welcoming proposals which would put us in line with the practice in most Common Market countries.

France and Luxembourg already have written into their law provision for four weeks' annual holiday. That was conceded by the Minister on Second Reading. As I said then, a fascinating proposal is already going through the German Parliament to give four weeks' annual holiday and to link that provision with a gradual retirement provision, much more beneficial than anything we have thought about so far in this country.

The case for four weeks' annual holiday after one year's service has been more than adequately made out. The only objection I can see to this proposal ought logically to come from the Opposition side of the Committee. If I am right in saying that trades unions are negotiating annual holidays more and more as a normal part of their negotiations, the people who in the end will be left without four weeks' annual holiday would be non-trade unionists for whom no one negotiates.

It has always been part of the philosophy and principle of the trade union movement that, much as we resent and regret the failure of people to join the Col. 6 union which is negotiating for them, nevertheless it is right to have minimum national rates of pay and minimum national holiday entitlement for all workers, whether they happen to be members of a trade union or not. We are now sufficiently advanced along the road towards the enjoyment of the greater leisure facilities which modern society holds out for us to be able to say that this provision is not a day too late in being put on the Statute Book, and I recommend the Committee to agree to the Clause.

I was told in elementary economics classes that some people prefer boring jobs—"The girls can do this almost with their eyes closed and meanwhile talk about their boyfriends, prospective and past, and they do not find it boring". But undoubtedly a lot of men and women do find their jobs boring. I believe that one reason for many of the strikes in the car industry is precisely that. If one were to research into the reasons for strikes, one would find this factor. At the slightest excuse, workers say, "For God's sake let's have two or three days off this damned production line". It is a major problem that we must face in modern society.

I refer to another extremely unpleasant job that ought to be compensated not simply in money terms but in time off the job. I recall the strike of the local government employees. One would imagine that those fellows had fairly good jobs compared with the job of a dustman, or even worse, the job of the fellows who Image Col. 9 go down the London sewers. I remember an interview when one of those fellows spoke about the danger of drowning and about removing blockages—I dare not tell the Committee what the men have to remove to prevent blockages in the London sewers.

I see no reason why we should not attach as much importance to the jobs of such men as to the jobs of highly qualified professional people like doctors, dentists and others who find job satisfaction in their work. They are no more important for the smooth running of our society than dustmen or sewer workers or coalminers.

Many years ago I visited West Africa, and I make the point to show that where the climate is congenial, a great premium is put on leisure for the workers. I went down a coal mine in Enugu in Eastern Nigeria, where one walked into a hole in the hillside. It was what we would call a drift mine. The humidity in that pit was 100 per cent.; one stood still and the sweat ran down. Those men went in bare-footed, wearing nothing.

I asked what the wages were. The manager was a Scotsman and the deputy manager a Welshman, or the other way round—anyway, they were foreigners. He said that the wages were about £6 a month. I said, "That must be the cheapest labour in the world". He said, "On the contrary: it is the most expensive". I asked, "How come?" He said, "You have seen the conditions. In 100 per cent. humidity you stand still and the sweat pours off you. These fellows work two days a week. They come in from the glorious sunshine and they go into the bowels of this pit to dig coal in their bare feet, sweating like pigs, and they make enough in two days to keep them. The rest of the week they enjoy the sun, go into the forest and live off what grows there". So they put a high premium on five days' leisure and two days' work. In our great civilised society we want to produce more and more cars to pollute the atmosphere but we do not build roads. The number of cars that stand in garages for 20 hours a day must be fantastic. We go on producing consumer goods in shocking, boring conditions, and yet do not provide the facilities to enable people to enjoy them, either in terms of leisure time, or of the provision of the services—building the roads, building decent cafés and hotels to service the travelling motorist.
