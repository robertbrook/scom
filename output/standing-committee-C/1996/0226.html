All my amendments are drafting amendments to give the Bill greater clarity and they have been tabled in response to widespread consultation that I have undertaken with the Department of Trade and Industry officials, the DTI-led Taskforce 2000 and the top 100 companies.

The use of the word "audit" has caused some confusion because, first, it is equated more with the audit of financial accounts as opposed to the audit of millennium compliancy of the computer systems of companies.

Secondly, the word "audit" could imply that an independent third party would be required to examine the computer systems of companies, which is not necessarily the case. Therefore, the word "assessment" is a more appropriate word to use in the Bill; it is more flexible and it could mean an internal assessment, an external assessment or a detailed or cursory appraisal. I commend the amendment to the Committee.

Mr. David Atkinson
: I have no intention of delaying the proceedings, but I shall mention two developments that have occurred since the House gave me leave to present the Bill last December. They further justify the need for legislation to deal with the millennium problem.

This year, for example, is expressed as "97". In the year 2000, the computer may interpret the double digit "00" as referring to the year 1900, or it may not recognise it at all. It may invent a date, perhaps when the software programme was first applied, or it may just shut down.

Mr. Ian Taylor
: The size of the problem, therefore, can rapidly be understood. Its impact on any one company is less easy to appreciate. Some companies are heavily computer-dependant, some much less so. Legislation is therefore a slightly blunt instrument. The Department of Trade and Industry has attempted to lead an awareness campaign. I take issue with my hon. Friend on the matter: I chaired the press conference to which he referred as a result of the PA Consulting survey. I entirely endorse his anxiety that only 9 per cent. of companies surveyed completed their audit. The survey showed that 65 per cent. of the organisations surveyed had started their audit, which is a considerable increase on the previous year, and that full or partial awareness among senior management reached 93 per cent.

I shall put the matter in a different context and deal with the worries of the hon. Member for Linlithgow and my right hon. Friend the Member for Northavon. Many companies have failed to tackle the matter, first, because of lack of awareness of the problem and, secondly, because the chief executives have failed to get a grip of a problem that is not in essence a problem of technology but of management decisions and resources.

During the past 15 months and more I have constantly raised the issue in the House and at meetings in the European Union with the European Community Telecoms Council I have lobbied the Commission, had bilateral discussions with the American Government and considered whether we should raise the issue in the Organisation of Economic Co-Operation and Development and elsewhere. At every stage I stressed the fact that the matter should be dealt with by chief executives if they obtain the JPEG 15 necessary management resources. My worry is not about the statistics but about the preparedness of companies to make the necessary resources available. When companies start to assess their problem, they first discover that it will take them longer and, secondly, that it will cost them much more money than they expected.

Representatives of Morgan Stanley International, who came to see me the other day, gave me permission to reveal the following figures: the bank's New York partners had calculated that it would cost £55 million to put in order its own operations in respect of its trading ability and its ability to continue to be a computer-based trading operation. It has now employed 200 people specifically for the task at hand. That is one bank in one situation, and it is very concerned about the ramifications elsewhere.

The solution is not so much at hand as a continuance of the awareness campaign using every available weapon. My officials have talked with the Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales about drawing up rules that may well be applied by auditors throughout the country in asking directors whether they have made a proper and fair representation of the liabilities within the company, and therefore provision within the accounts for correcting this problem. I have intimated to the stock exchange that I think that the directors who have not made clear statements to their shareholders may well be in breach of stock exchange listing requirements. It is not for me to say whether they are or not; I am raising the question as a point of discussion.

I have issued a whole series of challenges to the industry, but I will not take time in this Committee to go into them. Anyone who is interested in further information can contact Taskforce 2000, which my Department has set up and funded. I am delighted to say that industry is now sharing in the costs of funding Taskforce 2000, which was always the original intention. Some disparaging remarks have been made about the amount of money that we have put behind that body. In my judgment, taxpayers' money is not intended to solve the problems of the private sector, but it can well be used to promote awareness of the problem and to encourage—through Taskforce 2000 and the work that is now being done in various areas—ways of tackling the problem. Incidentally, the business links system is now gaining the expertise needed to distribute to companies across the country—particularly the smaller companies that my hon. Friend the Member for Reading, West mentioned—so that they can grapple with the problem.

One key point that comes across in the trawl of the largest companies carried out by my hon. Friend the Member for Bournemouth, East, is that whether or not they are in favour of legislation, they are all fairly well aware of what is happening. They are keen. That is 16 precisely why the CBI, which is part of Taskforce 2000 and is not changing its publicly identified rules, has said that it considers that further support for the development of initiatives such as Taskforce 2000 is a more effective way of dealing with the problem than legislation.

The supply chain has become crucial. Any company in a supply chain—my hon. Friend the Member for Bournemouth, East specifically mentioned this—will not be happy to deal with another part of that supply chain if it is not satisfied that that other company has made its systems compliant, and—here I stress something that never seems to get stressed—compliant in a way that is compatible with others in the supply chain.

There are various ways of dealing with this problem. There is no point in dealing with the problem and then finding that one's solution is not compatible with someone else's. The Bill does not help in that respect, which is why we are encouraging every supply chain to look at that problem. We are beginning to make breakthroughs. BT, for example, has said that it will not deal with a supplier that cannot satisfy BT that it has dealt satisfactorily, and in a compatible way, with the problem. Visa has indicated the same. As it often issues credit cards well in advance, Visa already has difficulties with going beyond the year 2000.

The hon. Member for Linlithgow raised several concerns. I assure him that the Government take the matter seriously. We have made it quite clear that we will have reviewed our systems by October of this year and will have run trials and corrected our systems by December 1998. I have issued a public awareness warning that it is implausible to believe that the Government, having corrected their systems, will contemplate dealing on an open basis with any computer system that cannot guarantee to the Government that it is compliant with the year 2000.

There are some severe warnings for companies to act immediately—in their severity, they go well beyond anything in the Bill.

I hope that I have explained as succinctly as possibly why the Government are not backing the Bill, but also why we warmly welcome all the efforts of my hon. Friend the Member for Bournemouth, East as part of the awareness campaign on the issue.
