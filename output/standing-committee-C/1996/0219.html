Mr. Don Foster
: I hope that the Committee will disagree to clause 2 and pass new clause 1.

The new clause is at the heart of the matter; it is the most critical part of our debate. The new clause makes the Bill somewhat different from that which we discussed on Second Reading. Clause 2 imposes a duty on councils to draw up road traffic reduction plans containing targets for reducing traffic volume or rates of traffic growth. The new clause would place upon councils the duty to prepare a report. I am always surprised by the fact that the Department JPEG 11 appears to be unhappy with using the word "plan" in such circumstances, and insists on the word "report". Nevertheless, there seems little difference between them, and I am happy to accept the use of the word "report".

The new clause will place on councils a duty to prepare a report examining existing and forecast levels of road traffic in their area and to consider whether to set targets to reduce them. It will also oblige them to publish their reports and submit copies to the Secretary of State. Finally and importantly, it makes it clear that the Secretary of State may issue appropriate guidance.

Mr. Watts
: The Government's main concern with clause 2 is that in its adoption of a rather inflexible and prescriptive approach, which would require all principal councils to draw up road traffic reduction plans and to set targets for reducing traffic levels or rates of growth, it fails to recognise that circumstances may differ from one part of the country to another and, indeed, within a single authority's area of responsibility. The needs of a largely rural authority 12 may differ from those of an urban one and the case for having targets relating to a specific conurbation may be quite different from that for the whole of a shire county's area.

It is important to recognise that in some locations other highly worthwhile initiatives may result in traffic growth. I am thinking specifically of places where regeneration programmes are under way—I mentioned one example on Second Reading. Local authorities have to balance the damaging effects of traffic against other considerations, such as the speed at which viable alternatives might come on stream and the need to promote good accessibility and foster economic growth.

The new clause places a duty on local authorities to review traffic levels on local roads in their area and to forecast growth. When they have that information, they can consider whether targets for reducing traffic levels or traffic growth have a useful role to play in their area.

I should stress that, in giving local authorities the option of deciding whether to set targets, we do not intend that they should take the issue lightly. It will not be acceptable, for two reasons, for an authority simply to say it does not agree with such measures and that it will not set targets. First, subsection (5) of the new clause would make it clear that authorities would be obliged to explain in their reports their reasons if they decide not to set targets. Secondly, I expect to sound a suitable cautionary note in the guidance to be issued under subsection (6) of the new clause.

Much of the detail under clause 2 could be covered more usefully in guidance notes. As the hon. Member for Bath mentioned, members of the Committee have already seen a rough draft of them. Covering such issues in guidance notes, rather than in the Bill, does not mean that we regard them as less important than other measures. Indeed, subsection (6) of the new clause makes it clear that local authorities

"shall have regard to any guidance when preparing a report."

 In particular, the guidance will make clear the importance of local authorities consulting on the setting of targets. The new clause would retain the obligation for local authorities to publish their reports.

Another crucial advantage of that approach is that it will allow us time to consider with local authority associations the detailed mechanics of how it will work. It would be a mistake to take an over-prescriptive approach in the Bill, only to risk finding that we had imposed in legislation something that is not workable. The new clause would also place a duty on authorities to submit their reports to the Secretary of State who will, in turn, lay them before Parliament.
