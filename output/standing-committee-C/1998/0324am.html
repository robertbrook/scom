Question proposed, That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

Ms Debra Shipley (Stourbridge): This is one of the most important clauses in the Bill, and rightly so, because it has been the focus of a great deal of the Committee's attention. Its purpose is to specify the criteria and procedure for the inclusion of names in the new statutory Department of Health list.

Subsection (1) provides that, while child care organisations must refer eligible names, it will be permissible for other organisations also to make references. All references will be treated similarly, regardless of whether they are compulsory or permitted.

Subsection (2) provides that individuals eligible for listing who are to be referred by a child care organisation, or who may be referred by any other organisation, are those who have been dismissed, transferred or suspended because they have harmed, or put at risk of harm, a child, or who would have been dismissed, or considered for dismissal on such grounds, had they not resigned or retired. The Committee will recall its decision to amend subsection (2) to omit reference to incompetence from the relevant grounds because it seemed to vague a concept to justify listing.

Subsections (3) to (7) set out how references are to be received and determined, including provisionally. They lay down a system of procedural fairness appropriate for the seriousness of the issues at stake. Thus, only after inviting observations from relevant individuals and their employers, and considering all the relevant information before him, will the Secretary of State be obliged to confirm an individual's inclusion on the list if he is of the opinion both that the referring organisation reasonably considered the individual to be guilty of misconduct—whether or not during Col. 88 employment—and that the individual is unsuitable to work with children.

Clauses 2(2)(d) provides that, where an organisation suspends or provisionally transfers an individual while investigating allegations of misconduct, the name should be referred to the list and included provisionally, pending the conclusion of the employer's investigations. Subsection (5)(b) and subsection (7) make necessary amendments to the clause to deal with these referrals.

Subsection (8) applies the effects of subsection (2), with appropriate modifications in the case of references for employment agencies.

I am sure that members of the Committee will agree that the fullest attention has been given to these important provisions. I hope that the clause will stand part of the Bill.

Ms Shipley: This clause, which has been amended by the Committee, establishes the tribunal, which now has two purposes. The first is to decide appeals arising under clause 4 in relation to the Department of Health list, or regulations made under clause 6 in relation to Department for Education and Employment List 99. The second purpose, acting instead of the Secretary of State and having granted leave, is to determine cases provisionally on the Department of Health list where the listing has been for more than nine months or, in cases where there are relevant civil or criminal proceedings, six months after those proceedings are terminated in ways defined in clause 4(5).

The clause achieves those purposes in three main ways. First, in subsections (2), (3) and (4) it confers a power to make regulations about the proceedings of the tribunal. Secondly, subsection (5) establishes offences triable only in magistrates courts and restricted to three specific areas of the regulation-making powers for the purpose of supporting the integrity of the tribunal's procedure. Thirdly, subsection (6) gives effect to the schedule to the Bill, which concerns the constitution and management of the tribunal.

Ms Shipley: The clause makes it possible by an order under the affirmative resolution procedure to extend the protection of the Bill to adults suffering from mental impairment, as defined in clause 12(1). In that case, "mental impairment" is defined

Although I cannot say now exactly when it will be possible for the Secretary of State to lay the necessary order, there can be no doubt that that is a highly desirable provision of the Bill.

Mr. Hammond: I sympathise with the hon. Lady's intentions in including this provision in the Bill. She spoke passionately on Second Reading about her Col. 128 reasons for deciding to include it. In some respects, it sits slightly uncomfortably with the rest of the Bill, which focuses on the protection of children.

I accept that adults who are severely mentally incapacitated have, in some respects, the characteristics of children, particularly in terms of their vulnerability to abuse by others. The mood throughout the House on Second Reading was that it would be desirable to extend the principles behind the Bill to ensure the protection of vulnerable adults, wherever possible. I am mindful, however, that the Government have their own proposals for protecting vulnerable individuals, young or old, within society.
