The Attorney-General (Sir Lionel Heald)
: I am obliged to the hon. Member for Brierley Hill (Mr. Simmons) for putting his inquiries about this Clause so clearly. Perhaps I may start by dealing JPEG 1237 with his last point, which is very important. I can say definitely that it is intended—Amendments will be moved later with regard to this matter—that the power of the Central Land Board to exercise its discretion shall be subject to appeal to the Lands Tribunal. In addition, I shall be able, in a few minutes, to make it clear why it is necessary that the Board should have that discretion. There will be some complicated cases where only part of a development charge payment comes into question, or where only part of the land comes into question. It is not possible to lay down any mathematical or arithmetical rules, and the Board will have to make the necessary adjustment. I think that the hon. Member's point will be safeguarded by the right of appeal.

Regarding the Clause as a whole, the key to it is this. As one can see, by reference to the marginal note on page 5 of the Bill, this Clause deals with a case where provision has to be made for payment where there has been development charge incurred by a claim-holder or his predecessor in title. In certain circumstances that would result in a man getting paid too much. That is why, when we come to subsection (5), it says:

"Where apart from this subsection"—

that means to say if subsection (5) was not there—

"a payment would be payable by virtue of this section by reference to a development charge, and by reason of the payment of that charge"—

certain things happen.

Then provision will be made for the Central Land Board to

"reduce or disallow the payment as the Board may determine to be appropriate…."

This machinery will prevent a man receiving more than he should. One has to go back to the 1947 Act as it stands before amendment. (Under Section 22 of that Act provision is made for the case where planning permission is revoked or modified. If that is done no compensation is payable in respect of depreciation of the value of the owner's interest unless development charge has been paid in connection with the permission, or, under Part VIII of the 1947 Act, none is payable at all.

If development charge has been paid then, under Section 22 of the 1947 Act, 1238 the owner will have received compensation in full for the whole of the damage to his interest. That damage will include the money he paid out in development charge, which was wasted because he could not take advantage of it. In that case the owner will already have, in effect, received back the development charge, and if he gets it again under this Clause he will be getting it twice over.

Therefore we say that this is a case where, apart from this subsection, the owner would get X plus Y We do not wish him to have Y so we have to make provision for Y being taken away, and this is the way that is done. Therefore, paragraph ( a) deals with the case which I have already mentioned, where a man has already received the equivalent of his development charge back by virtue of Section 22 of the 1947 Act.

Paragraphs ( b) and ( c) deal with different cases. They deal with a case where the land in respect of which development charge has been paid has been compulsorily acquired or sold under the threat or shadow of compulsory purchase powers. Exactly the same point arises there because, under Part V of the 1947 Act, when an interest in land was compulsorily acquired the compensation payable disregarded any value attributable to the grant of planning permission for any development beyond the existing use except in the same two cases; that is to say, where development charge had been paid in respect of the development, or where, by reason of Part VIII of the Act, none was payable at all.

There again, if development charge has actually been paid the value attributable to the permission to develop is included in the existing use value compensation, and the result is that, in effect, the man has already had compensation in the shape of repayment of development charge, or the equivalent. Therefore it is obviously right for that reason that the previous owner should not be entitled to claim under the Bill on the ground that he has paid development charge, because he has also had back the equivalent of it.

At the same time it is right that he should still be paid for the development value of the land, and that he will obtain under Clause 6, which we shall be shortly discussing. Under that Clause he will get the claim holding value or so much of it as attached to the land JPEG 1239 bought. The hon. Member rightly says, "Yes, that is all very well, but why should the Central Land Board have the power to do what it thinks fit?" The reason is that in some cases planning permission may not have been revoked; it may be that it has been modified. In that case there must obviously be some sort of proportion sum or apportionment.

In other cases it may be only part of the land which is affected; only part of the land on which charge was paid may have been bought and, therefore, in that case the owner can be said to have been compensated for only part of the relevant charge payment, and someone has to make an adjustment. That has been dealt with by providing that the Central Land Board shall have the discretion to do what is right. We entirely agree that it should not be left in that way, and that there should be an appeal to the Lands Tribunal, which will have the right to put right anything which has gone wrong.

I hope that I have explained the matter. I think one can summarise it by saying that it is helpful, in considering this Clause, to remember that this is a sort of clearing-up process, as it were, because if the matter is taken on too broad lines we shall find that people will be getting too much. In some cases in this Bill provision is made in respect of people who may be getting too little, but this is a matter to deal with those who may be getting too much. The language is rather tightly compressed, but I hope I have managed to explain that this is a case where, if subsection (5) were not in the Bill, payment would be made by virtue of the Clause; that is to say that the main part of Clause 4 would result in too much being paid. Therefore these complicated provisions are inserted in order to cover those cases where a man has already, in effect, been compensated in respect of his outlay on development charge or its equivalent, or part of it, and provision is made that he is not to receive payment in respect of it again.

I think it would be better if I left the matter in that way, but if there are further details which I can provide, I shall be glad to do so.
