Mr. DAVID GRENFELL
: I beg to move, in page 6, line 28, to leave out from the word "relates" to the word "and" in line 29.

We now come to what is, perhaps, the most important part of the Bill. The object of the Bill, as we are told and as we readily accept, is to limit the flow of goods on to the British market, in order that producers at home may have a reasonable price for the goods which they produce and market in their own country. The first means by which that end is to be attained is by the issue of Orders limiting imports from foreign countries. The second means is by agreements between this country and other producing countries for determining the amount of agricultural products of various kinds to be imported. The sole object of that limitation of imports has been described to us as the encouragement of home production by raising the average level prices and giving more or less security that commodities produced by farming operations in this country will find an assured market.

We who criticise the Bill said downstairs, and we say here, that there is a feature in the Bill which does not correspond with the object I have just described. That object is not, solely, to give encouragement to the home producer but to enable him to increase his production and his remuneration and profit from his industry. In this Clause however, we come to a most objectionable feature of the Bill and that is a provision directed actually towards the re- 332 duction of home production. In this Clause we find it stated that one of the intentions is to permit the production of secondary products only on licence. This production is to be under schemes set up by producers coming together for the purpose of dealing with the secondary products of their respective branches of the industry. The conditions attaching to these licences are described in the Clause. The Clause sets out that the development board is empowered to grant a producer's licence:

"subject to such conditions as the board, having regard to the interests of the persons registered as producers under the related marketing schemes, think necessary for promoting efficient production of the said product in the premises to which the licence relates."—

We go so far without offering any Amendment to the proposal in the Bill, but then the wording of the Clause proceeds:

"or for preventing or reducing excessive production of that product."

That is where we beg to differ from the Government. We wish to test the Government and their supporters on this point. They say that the object of the Bill is to give more employment, more profit, and more security to those engaged in agriculture at home. As a means of finding the additional employment there is the assured market. There is, further, the part of the Bill upon which we are now engaged, which relates to the setting up of industries for the manufacture of secondary products. Those products are not enumerated in the Clause, but we know that milk schemes and bacon schemes are in operation, and that probably there will be butter schemes and factories for the production of bacon, milk, and butter products, as well as other commodities. But in this Clause, the Minister and those who support him appear to lack confidence in their own Bill, because they say in effect, "We are prepared to provide machinery, not for increasing the production of the secondary products of home agriculture, but machinery which is possibly going to limit and restrict the production of those commodities." Indeed they say so in terms. They say that the development board shall issue licences subject to the need "for preventing or reducing excessive production." A licence may be denied or withdrawn on the ground that if it were given too much work might be JPEG 333 done at home; too many people might be given employment; too much capital might be employed, and there would be a danger of too much production of bacon, butter and milk products. Yet I am sure that hon. Members opposite all agree that those are necessary home industries if agriculture is to derive the full benefit from this or any other organisation scheme.

I do not see anyone blushing, but I can hardly imagine any representative of agriculture defending this part of the Bill in his own constituency or anywhere else in the country. How can anyone who claims to represent agriculture, and whose ambition it is that agriculture should be revived and fostered and built up into a powerful industry, face his constituents, or people who might be identified, by means of these secondary industries, with the productive side of agriculture, to defend a system of licences which commences by saying that under certain conditions, because of excessive production, or what might be termed excessive production, these industries are not to be permitted to be established in this country. It is most startling to find a provision like this in a Bill. I represent a mining constituency, and I know that, as soon as the Minister rises to reply, he will tell me that I represent an industry in which already restriction is imposed. That is true, but the cases are not analogous at all, and I am assisting the Minister and the Committee by meeting that argument before he submits it. It is true that there is a form of restriction on the production of coal in this country, not because anyone wants to interfere with the supply of coal for domestic consumption, but in order to prevent this industry—which is an exporting industry solely—

Under Sub-section (2) there is one point with which I should like to deal relating to the provision which enables a development board to buy and sell property. I may be wrong; if so I am sure the Minister will correct me. Does this provision ensure that producers will bear part of the expense of rationalising an industry in which they have no share? The producers under a marketing scheme are to form a fund out of which certain things are to be done. The primary producer has no share whatever in any of the profits that are made by the secondary producer, but if compensation is to be paid to the secondary producer, if losses are made in selling a property, then he is to put into the pool part of his resources in order to enable compensation to be paid. It seems to me that you are asking the primary producer to subsidise, or pay off, if you like, the secondary producer. I can imagine that the producer of pigs may have to bear the expense of rationalising the bacon-curing industry. If there is excessive production or there is inefficient production, and under this Clause a place is to be closed down, and the secondary producer is to be compensated, you call upon the producer of pigs to do it through the pool. Surely, you cannot say to an efficient producer of pigs that he shall bear part of the cost of compensating an inefficient producer of bacon.

Under Sub-section (4) tremendous powers are given to the Development Board to grade, pack, store, adapt for sale, insure, advertise or transport an agricultural product. Under the principal Act of 1931 the purpose of a marketing scheme was to deal with sales, while the purpose of a development scheme is really to deal with production. Are you not delegating the powers of the Marketing Board in this particular scheme to the Development Board? Under the Amendment that will not have to be done, although I may be wrong in my interpretation.

Another objection on this point is that whereas there is a consumers' committee to study the workings of the marketing board there is no provision for a consumers' committee to study the workings of a development board. If a marketing board decided that it was inconvenient to have an inquiry by a consumers' committee, they might hand over their powers of sale to a development board, which would not have to submit itself to an inquiry from the consumers' committee. I should have liked to develop that point, but time is going.

There is a danger that the powers granted under a development scheme regulating one product may interfere with the production of raw material which may be the raw material of numerous and other and possibly equally or more important secondary products not under a scheme. We are not certain that there will be schemes for all secondary products. Supposing one development board through their workings of marketing boards are regulating a certain kind of raw material which is the primary product for one secondary product, it will be difficult if such control interferes with another firm using that same raw material but producing another secondary product. I may not have made my points clear to the Committee but they are clear to me.
