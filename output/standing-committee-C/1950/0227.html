Mr. Richard Law
: I beg to move, in page 15, line 4, at the end, to insert:

"calculated proportionately according to the gross value of white fish landed but."

We have now come to the most disagreeable part of the Bill. So far we have been discussing the great benefits which we all hope—some of us with more conviction than others—will accrue to the fishing industry from the operation of the White Fish Authority. We now come to the less agreeable question of how the benefits are to be paid for. It is a principle of this financial arrangement that there should be a general levy on the white fish industry. Certainly we have no quarrel with that principle, and the Amendment does nothing to interfere with it. It is not the purpose of the Amendment to interfere with that general principle nor to vary the amount which would be raised by the levy; it is designed simply to change the incidence of the levy.

It is a principle of taxation which is generally accepted—the levy is, in effect, a tax on the white fish industry to pay for the benefits which we hope will come to it—that those with the greater money incomes contribute proportionately more in taxation than those with smaller money incomes. As it stands, the Clause utterly denies that principle. Under the Clause those with the smaller money incomes would pay the greater amount of tax and those with the greater money incomes would pay the smaller amount of tax. It is easy to see how this happens, but, on the face of it, it seems quite reasonable that there should be a general levy of a penny per stone. It would be reasonable if the value of fish was uniform and if the different kinds of fish had the same value, but the values of fish differ enormously, and in practice under the Bill 1116 the result of the difference would be that the section of the fishing industry with the heaviest landings, which would therefore pay the heaviest amount of tax, has the smallest money income with which to pay that tax, and vice versa. In 1950 the distant water section of the industry landed a little more than seven million hundredweight of fish. The value of that was £12,500,000 and the tax paid was £236,000. The near and inshore water sections landed a little less than seven million hundredweight, of a value of not £12,500,000 but £19,500,000, and the tax payable was not £236,000 but £227,000. In other words, the near water ports, with an income greater by £7 million, will pay less taxation than distant water ports with a smaller income. I realise that many hon. Members of the Committee, and a number of my hon. Friends, will not approve of the Amendment as a matter of expediency, but I do not see how they can disapprove of it as a matter of justice because the Clause seems to work a real injustice as it is framed.

How is the levy to be raised; at what stage will it be paid, and who in the first instance will pay it? It must be paid by either the producer or consumer of white fish. If it is paid by the producer it will be paid by the fishermen, and that means that the long distance fishermen will have to pay a greater amount than the inshore and near water fishermen. If on the other hand, it is paid by the consumer, it means in effect that the consumer at the fried fish shop will have to pay a levy in order to subsidize the customer at the Ritz Hotel.

My hon. Friends who represent near water ports ought to reflect on the effect which the Amendment would have on foreign imports. It is often thought that near water men are affected by competition from the distant water sections of the trawling industry, but I do not think that is so, because the various amounts of fish which they respectively produce are in no way competitive. What does affect the near water men are importations of boxed fish. According to such calculations as I have been able to make, the Amendment would double the amount of the levy on the importation of boxed fish, and that is a point which hon. Members representing near water ports would be very wise to consider before seeking to JPEG 1117 reject the Amendment out of hand. I hope very much that the Committee will give full considerations to the arguments which I have put forward. I am sure they must feel that the present arrangement under Clause 15 is inequitable. I hope the Minister will be able to tell the Committee that he will accept this very modest and sensible Amendment.

On the other hand, the large trawlers which go to the far waters have a regular source of supply. As yet there is no over-fishing in their areas, and I hope there never will be. They have a tremendous amount of reserve, and, broadly speaking, they can dispose of their fish, in view of 1118 its quality, at very reasonable prices. At least, they could. Let me rather say that the fish is landed at reasonable prices. As to the value of a single near water or inshore catch, sometimes a couple of soles will make the price of a stone of cheaper fish. In view of the variable character of the catches, the instability, and the danger of coming in empty, it is extremely unfair to mulct these men of a levy on the value of their catch.

I hope the Committee will resist the Amendment. It puts a premium on low quality, whereas the Bill is designed, as it has always been explicitly stated, to provide good quality fish at a reasonable price with an adequate return to the fishermen. The Amendment would militate against that desire. We have heard the words "justice" and "equity". We all agree with the tendency these days. that those most able to bear the burden should carry it. In this case those who, have large regular sources of supply and can make reasonable returns on their capital should not try to pass that burden to the smaller men. For that and other reasons, I hope the Committee will reject the Amendment. I cannot see how the Amendment would be implemented. Although there is, on the face of it, some sort of rough justice in the case put by the right hon. Gentleman, I am sure that those of us who have at heart the interests of the smaller men will, on reflection, reject the Amendment.

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture and Fisheries (Mr. George Brown)
: I hope it will help the Committee to make some progress if I intervene at this stage because as far as I have heard the arguments they are based on a misconception of what the Clause, if left alone, does. If one enters the discussion as to who are the poor in this matter of the industry, the distant water section or the near and inshore fishermen, one would get into an argument which seems to depend upon the constituency for which one sits rather than, necessarily, the facts of the case. That shows how unwise it would be to try to legislate in this Committee in this way and to try to decide where the balance of need lies.

The view we have taken is that we should set up a strong Authority, independent of these considerations which must unhappily affect Members of Parliament from time to time, and leave them, subject to general guidance, to get on with the job and to decide where the incidence ought to lie. The right hon. Member for Haltemprice (Mr. Law) and the hon. Member for Hull, North (Mr. W. R. Hudson) have taken the view that if the Clause is left alone it means necessarily that the levy must be raised on a tonnage basis. I am advised that that is not so at all.

The effect of putting in this Amendment would be to tie the hands of the 1120 Authority and to tell them in advance how the levy is to be raised. The effect of not putting in the Amendment but leaving the Clause as it stands is to say in subsection (2, a) what the limit in terms of money of any levy raised up to a limit of one penny a stone shall be—in the present state of things—£430,000 a year, and subsection (3) says:

"Any levy imposed by the Authority under this section shall be payable by such persons engaged in the white fish industry, in such proportions and at such times as may be prescribed."

It leaves the Authority the job of deciding what in the light of all the circumstances is the most equitable way of raising the levy having decided to raise it within the general limit we put on them. I feel we would be much better advised to leave it for them to work the thing out than to come forward with a regulation under Clause 5 as they will have to do. They may decide that so much per stone is the right way, or they may take some other view. When they come forward with a regulation there will be a chance for Parliament to discuss it if it wishes to do so, but the decision will be arrived at in the first place by folk not subjected to these pulls who can take a better and wider view of the matter.

Therefore, I do not propose, if I can help it, to get involved in the argument which has been launched. I would only say that I think the argument is not so much one sided in favour of ad valorem as hon. Members opposite suggest, but there is quite a lot to be said for the other view. Those men who at the moment may be thought to be well away and who catch fish in great quantities, may feel it unfair that this has happened, but it is the disposal of their fish which raises considerable difficulty. It may be that they would get the most benefit out of the Authority, and in that case those working in the near water and inshore should have to pay. I do not say I hold that view but I merely say it is a different argument which, in certain circumstances, can be put forward.

I hope that, having given air to the views of constituents of right hon. and hon. Gentlemen who sit for various ports, the Committee will feel that at this stage we are well out of this particular argument and that it is best to give the Authority the over-riding limit and, within JPEG 1121 that limit, to allow them to take all considerations into account and leave them untrammelled so that Parliament can look at the regulation when it is brought forward.

Mr. Erroll
: I beg to move, in page 18, line 9, to leave out subsection (6).

Scattered through this Bill there are and must necessarily be a number of penalties for offences committed in contravention of the provisions of the Bill. Clause 18 contains some general provisions about offences. I am particularly concerned about subsection (6) and, in the Amendment which I have moved, I seek to delete it.

The purpose of this subsection is to secure, more rigorously perhaps than is fair or just, the execution of penalties described in earlier Clauses. The Clauses in which the penalties are specifically outlined state that where a person is convicted he will be liable to fines and, in some cases, imprisonment. The difficulty arises, of course, where the guilty party is not a person but is a body corporate—that is, a company. This subsection seeks to carry the penalty from the body corporate which has been proved guilty to the individual or individuals within that body corporate so that they may be imprisoned.

The point of particular issue here is that the penalties in the earlier Clauses are both fines and imprisonment. It is easy to fine a body corporate, but it is not possible to put a body corporate into prison. It is, therefore, necessary for the prosecution to proceed against one or more individuals within that body corporate. It has chosen what I think is a particularly unfortunate way of doing so for, by means of this subsection, the prosecution will be able to say that all members of the body corporate, as defined in the subsection, can be deemed guilty until, more or less, they can prove their innocence.

In effect, the prosecution is able to say, "Your company is guilty and, therefore, you are all guilty unless you can prove your innocence, unless you can prove that the offence was committed without your consent or connivance and that the individual so charged exercised all such diligence as he could to prevent the commission of the offence.

This is reversal of the tradition of British justice which assumes that all men and women are innocent until their guilt is proved. The reversal of the tradition of British justice is particularly 1134 serious in this case, because a prison sentence is involved.

It is not merely a matter of paying a fine. Here a man who may be liable to imprisonment has got to prove his innocence to avoid being sent to prison, which seems a most unreasonable provision to find in a comparatively non-controversial Measure such as this.

In moving the deletion of this subsection I have no desire to shield the guilty, but I do feel that by requiring the defendant to prove a negative the burden of proof may well become too heavy and result in the conviction, not only of the guilty but also of the innocent It is perfectly possible for the prosecution to secure the conviction of an individual by the normal processes of the law. The fact that it may be more difficult to do so because they cannot assume him guilty is surely no reason or justification for the inclusion of this reversal of the standard practice in a Bill of this kind.

I dare say that whoever replies for the Government will cite a number of precedents, and there certainly are a number of so-called precedents in earlier Acts. In, for example, the Dangerous Drugs Act, 1918, there was some justification for the reversal of the principle because the public interest was rightly deemed by the Parliament of the day to be so great as to justify the reversal. The same might apply to, for example, the Prevention of Corruption Act, where the fact that a civil servant had received money from an individual was in itself. such an unusual occurrence as to merit the reversal of the onus of proof.

The Solicitor-General for Scotland (Mr. Douglas Johnston)
: As the Committee 1136 knows, and as the hon. Member for Altrincham and Sale (Mr. Erroll) said, in our law a company is a person separate from that of its constituent members or directors or other servants. That separate persona of the company gives rise to very great difficulties on occasion. One notable judge said that a difficulty was that a corporation had neither a soul to be damned nor a body to be kicked. The result of that is that the criminal law has in many cases great difficulty in proceeding against a company. It is true that where the company does an overt act, an act of commission, one can always proceed not only against the company but against the servant of the company who commits that act. That does not, of course, apply where the act is not an act of commission but is an act of omission.

This is not a new provision. The law has for a number of years taken two steps to overcome the difficulty. It has placed responsibility for certain acts upon servants of the company, such as directors, the secretary and other officials directly. Examples of that can be found in the Companies Act. The Act says, not that a company shall do so and so, but that the company and the directors and the secretary shall do so and so. The other way the law has got over these acts of omission is to say that if the company does not do such and such an act or commits an offence of any kind, then the directors, the secretary and other officials of the company shall be guilty unless they prove to the contrary. I emphasise that this Clause and all the other Clauses say that the guilt of the company must be proved before the Clause comes into operation.

There are much older examples than those given by the hon. Member for Altrincham and Sale. For example, Section 75 and other Sections of the Coal Mines Act, 1911, provide that if certain regulations are not observed the owner, the manager, the agent and other persons shall be liable unless they prove their innocence. There is the same thing in the Merchant Shipping Act, where the loading provisions provide that the owner shall be liable unless he proves that he was innocent. There are other examples in more recent Acts.

I suggest it is perfectly reasonable not only that this impersonal person, a company, should be liable for its deeds and JPEG 1137 misdeeds, but that those responsible for the government of the company, namely, the directors and others, should be liable also. It is a pure fiction of our law that a company is a separate persona. On these grounds I suggest that the Amendment, if it is to be pressed, should be negatived.

I see your difficulty, Mr. Butcher. You have suggested that the long title might have to be amended in order to make it quite clear that it is competent for us to insert the Clause into the Bill. We had understood that there was some good precedent for this action in the Act of 1938, but I accept your advice and we shall amend the long title if this Clause is added to the Bill.

The purpose of the Clause is to amend the 1907 Act which provides that there shall not in any circumstance be more than one whaler attached to any whaling station in Scotland, unless the whaling station existed before 1907 and had more than one whaler at that date. The reason for that limitation which applied to Scotland, and to no other country in the world so far as I know, was that certain herring fishermen, in particular the Shetland fishermen, believed that whaling was seriously detrimental to herring fishing.

Although inquiries were made at the time by an advisory committee and although a good deal of scientific research has been made, and the fears expressed from time to time since 1907 have never been seen by the scientists to be at all justified, the limitation was imposed in 1907 and has continued ever since. We have never had many whaling stations in Scotland, and there were none at all from 1929 until a new company revived operations at the whaling station at Bunaveneader in Harris in June last year. They have pointed out that they are quite unable to carry on their activities while the limitation im- JPEG 1149 posed in the 1907 Act continues. We think that they have made out a case for additional whalers.

This Clause merely provides that, after making proper inquiries—"due inquiry" the Clause says—the Secretary of State will be empowered to permit any whaling company with a whaling station to use more than one whaler in connection with that station, up to a maximum of four. I understand that four is about the normal number used by whaling stations in different parts of Europe. We think it is a fair ceiling to apply in this case. In any case, it is obvious that we must have permission for the company to use more than one whaler.
