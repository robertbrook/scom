The Lord Advocate
: The first point by the hon. and learned Gentleman the Member for York (Mr. Hylton-Foster) was that the Clause is not necessary. The reason for its inclusion is that it forms part of the pattern of this procedure, and provides, in the event of a conviction "by court-martial being quashed, that the accused person shall not be liable to be tried again for the same offence by court-martial or any other court.

If we look at the previous part of the machinery we find that statutory provision is already made for this plea of autrefois acquit in relation to an actual court-martial itself. The Committee will recollect that the position is that on trial by court-martial autrefois acquit by another court may be pleaded. That is provided for in Section 157 of the Army Act, and Rule of Procedure No. 36. On the other hand, on a trial by court-martial autrefois acquit by a competent civil court may be pleaded. That is provided in the Army Act, Section 162 (6), and Rule of Procedure No. 36.

Conversely, on a trial by a civilian court autrefois acquit by court-martial cannot be pleaded, and we find that in Section 41 (a) and 162 of the Army Act. JPEG 1251 Therefore, within the code we already have a statutory provision for this plea, and since we are now setting up an appeal court it seems reasonable, having regard to that background, that we should also make statutory provision for this plea of autrefois convict in relation to this appeal court. I think that disposes of the argument on the ground that it is not necessary.

I feel that there is some misapprehension on the second argument that the Clause as it stands fails to give protection to the accused men. Manifestly, this is one of the cases where we like to have uniformity—a uniformity between the civil criminal law and the military criminal law. I gather that some misapprehension may have arisen by a passage in Archbold's Criminal Law, which deals with this question. The learned author, considering the fact that under Section 33 of the Interpretation Act, 1899, these words are used:

"The offender shall not be liable to be punished twice for the same offence"

suggests that Section 33 of the Interpretation Act would have been clearer if for the word "offence" there were substituted the words "act or omission." That, in fact, is what is proposed by this Amendment.

That suggestion by the learned author of Archbold's Criminal Law was expressly disapproved by Mr. Justice Humphreys in the recent case of Rex v. Thomas, which is reported in 1950 I King's Bench, and there the learned Judge said:

"It is not the law that a person shall not be liable to be punished twice for the same act. It has never been so held in any case, and it is not so provided in the Interpretation Act."

In those circumstances, we get back to what is the common law in relation to this particular plea. The common law is that a person should not be put twice on trial and be convicted for the same offence, and there is plenty of authority for that proposition. That being the common law, we merely wish to retain it in relation to the plea as affected by the actions of this new appeal court. Manifestly, it would be very invidious indeed if we accepted a form of words which might result in the interpretation of the law being different for this form of procedure than it would be in the civil-criminal courts. For these reasons, I ask the Committee to reject the Amendment.

The petitioner may put it forward on the aground that something went wrong in closed court. One does not know. As my hon. and learned Friend said, that is a very novel power; but of course, the Court of Appeal is a novel institution for courts-martial. What we are concerned with is the circumstances in which any court of appeal desirous of doing justice, as all courts of appeal are, is likely to want to use that power. Before we give that power, we should ask under what circumstances it is likely to be used. As my hon. and learned Friend the Member for York (Mr. Hylton-Foster) said, it is a novel power because it is not a power concerned with sentences. A Court of Appeal is not concerned with sentences. To use the analogy of the civil courts, it is a power to obtain a report from a member of the jury as to what went on in the jury room. That is a very novel power.

I put forward, because I think it might be helpful to the Committee, the sort of circumstances in which possibly the Court of Appeal might find it desirable to ask for a report. The Lord Advocate may be able to confirm that the only time when a Court of 1276 Appeal may want a report from members of the court-martial is possibly when some suggestion comes before the Court of Appeal about something going wrong in closed court. I refer to a closed court because if it was something going wrong elsewhere—if witnesses were not sworn or something like that—then obviously a report could be obtained from the Judge Advocate. If we are to get a report from a member of the court-martial about what happened in closed court, this is where we come to the crux of the position.

It is true that the rules could provide for getting a report from all members of the court-martial. Equally, they could provide that a report could be obtained only from certain members of the court-martial. The answer may be "Well, have another Prayer." I would rather get the matter straight and avoid the necessity for having another Prayer, particularly when one cannot amend the rules. I think that the argument of the Lord Advocate indicated the fallacy here. Suppose there is a need for the report in the circumstances I have indicated. The Lord Advocate said, "If it is a point of not very great importance and if you ask the president, the matter can be settled without controversy." I think I have quoted him correctly. That is just the danger. It may appear to the court, having merely asked the president, that they have settled the controversy. The president may say one thing, but another member of the court, if he was asked, may say, "As a matter of fact, that is not a point which may be settled without controversy for this, that and the other reason."

That is the danger if we are to select one person, one member of the court, no matter who he is, to answer a question from the Court of Appeal. One might get an answer which does not correctly represent the views of the court-martial about what happened. We suggest that power should be kept to ask the Judge Advocate about anything that takes place while he is present. Then we say that when the Court of Appeal, intending to act upon information received, asks for information about what took place in closed court when the Judge Advocate was not there, it is not sufficient to ask for the opinion of only one member. What we want to lay down as a general principle is that when the Court of Appeal JPEG 1277 ask for the opinion of members about what happened in closed court they should get the opinion of all the members who are available and who can express an opinion.
