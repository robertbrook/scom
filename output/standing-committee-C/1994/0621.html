I understand the concerns of hon. Members about those categories of people but I should say that clause 1(1)(a) provides for a defence of reasonable excuse, as was mentioned by some of my hon. Friends who are concerned that the amendment goes beyond what is required.

The defence of reasonable excuse will, in many cases, provide an appropriate safeguard, although it would, of course, be for the courts to decide whether a person had a reasonable excuse in the light of the circumstances of the case. Questions were asked about whether particular circumstances would amount to reasonable excuse for failing to remove dog droppings from land. As my hon. Friend the Member for Blackpool North said—and as as I have repeated—it would be for the courts to decide whether there were grounds for reasonable excuse.

Hon. Members may have thought that the term "reasonable excuse" was not unfamiliar and that it may already be on the statute book. A computer search revealed that the phrase "reasonable excuse" has occurred 957 times in more than 400 statutes and statutory instruments during the past 150 years. I suggest that the words are familiar to the courts and, therefore, should not give rise to insurmountable difficulties in interpretation.

The interpretation of the phrase necessarily depends on its context. There are numerous cases on its meaning under various statutes, such as reasonable excuse for failing to give a blood or urine sampler under road traffic legislation—I am sure that no Committee member would be in that difficulty. There are also reported cases of reasonable excuse for not making a child attend school; for failing to give the invoice required by statute on a sale Of fertiliser; for non-vaccination of a child; for desertion of a 40 spouse; for non-production of a receipt for rent; for carrying a firearm or offensive weapon, and for failure to comply with a local authority nuisance order.

Those are examples of cases that have come before the appeal courts. Magistrates may well have had to consider similar examples. They may also have had to consider reasonable excuse in relation to dog droppings because the phrase appears in some of the byelaws on the subject. Magistrates have been using their experience and common sense for a great many years to reach sensible results in a wide variety of situations, and I am sure that they will be able to take dog droppings in their stride, if the Committee will excuse the phrase. I hope that the Committee will forgive me if I decline to speculate on what their decisions might be.

With the exception of amendment No. 5, I am not convinced that the amendments before us are technically correct. Amendment No. 1, taken with amendment No. 2, would mean that the words "a person" appear twice in the categories added by amendment No. 2.

More importantly, I am advised that amendment No.2 is defective because the words

"while being used for sporting purposes",

or any of the other purposes mentioned, relate to the person, not to the dog. Hon. Members will recognise that we are dealing with the dog and its activities, rather than the person's activities. Hon. Members might be tempted to take that opportunity for sport of another form presented by a play on words, but I am sure that they will resist it.

I am afraid that amendment No. 2 is not acceptable. Nevertheless, I am sympathetic to the idea that there should be some exclusions, especially for clearing up after working dogs. I am attracted to amendment No. 5 as it would enable the Secretary of State to prescribe exceptions by regulation. Of course, we would consider the views of the Committee when drafting such regulations. We would consult widely before bringing such regulations before the House.

I note that some concern has been expressed by the National Farmers Union. Some time ago, as a farmer's assistant, I shepherded using dogs, so I can imagine that the situation is extremely difficult. Before I left the Antipodes—I mention this cautiously after the interesting game on Sunday—my last task was driving approximately 5,000 to 7,000 sheep along a road using four or five dogs. Of course, roads can be designated under the Bill. The technique used in the Antipodes involves the use of a hunt-away dog, which the farmer, who is at the rear of the flock, sends ahead of the sheep to turn them. Often the dog is out of sight and both it and the farmer are working hard. So, obviously, the farmer's attention can be drawn away from that dog to his other dogs which work behind him. If they were my dogs, they would be behind me behaving themselves. The farmer would be unable to see whether the dog had utilised the opportunity to plant a dropping on the highway. As one can imagine, he would have his hands full JPEG 41 dealing with 7,000 sheep and a hunt-away dog, especially if it were in training.

I do not know whether that technique is used in this country, but I can understand why the NFU is especially concerned. I am sure that if there are any members of the farming community among us they could say whether a hunt-away dog system is used in this country. Nevertheless, we should recognise that a farmer using dogs to drive a large and potentially difficult flock or herd—whether it be of cattle or sheep, or the llamas and deer that have been mentioned—would face considerable problems. Therefore, amendment No. 5 is eminently suitable.

On that basis, I hope that, after considering all the points made both for and against the amendment, and hon. Members will accept amendment No. 5, my hon. Friend the Member for Blackpool, North will withdraw amendment No. 1.
