Mr. Hague: It is a pleasure to move amendment No. 6 and, in so doing, to welcome you to the Chair, Mr. Maxton. I hope that you will enjoy your tenure, as did Mr. Hogg last week.

This large group of amendments relates to the Bill's concept of a qualified disabled person in part III, which deals with employment. The amendments would remove the concept of a "qualified" disabled person from the Bill's employment provisions. Like new clause 3, it reflects a fundamental difference between the approach that the Bill takes on the issue of discrimination in employment and the approach that the Government favour.

In the Bill as currently drafted, an employer's only justification for discrimination would be that the person Col. 102 could not perform the essential functions of a job. This concept has been borrowed from the Americans with Disabilities Act's "qualified individuals with disability". The ADA further defines this as

"an individual with a disability who meets the skills, education, and other job-related requirements of a position held or desired, and who, with or without reasonable accommodation can perform the essential functions of the job".

To be protected by the ADA, a person must not only be an individual with a disability but must also be "qualified". An employer is not required to hire or retain an individual who is not qualified to perform the job.

The Bill gives us no insight into what an "essential function" of a job might be. It is not clear how the Bill's sponsors would establish what could be an essential function if cases were taken to industrial tribunals and the terminology were argued over. I assume that the thinking behind the clause is that an employer might deliberately add spurious marginal duties to a job specification, and then argue that a disabled employee was unable to perform these duties and so should be dismissed or not taken on.

I agree that the issue of performance of marginal duties needs to be addressed. However, the provision to do so already exists within the Bill. An employer must consider the duty to make reasonable adjustment. If an employee cannot perform certain duties and these are marginal duties, it may well not be reasonable to require him or her to do them, and they can be dropped or reassigned in line with the reasonable adjustment provision.

In the Disability Discrimination Bill—the Government's Bill—employees and applicants are protected in a much simpler way. The Government Bill recognises that there are a number of circumstances in which an employer may have good reason to treat a disabled person less favourably. One such circumstance is where the disabled person is unsuitable for the employment. Another is where the disabled person is less suitable than the person who is given the employment; or where me nature of the disabled person's disability significantly impedes, or would significantly impede, the performance of any of his duties. However, an employer must first consider the duty to make a reasonable adjustment. That could mean reassigning marginal duties to another employee.

That framework, as I have described it, is much easier to work in practice. We are trying to achieve the same thing—in the Disability Discrimination Bill and the Civil Rights (Disabled Persons) Bill—especially in respect of employment. However, the Bill is creating too complicated a system, which will give rise to grave doubts about what essential functions may be. The concept of qualified disabled persons is seeking to create a class of people who could be equated, for example, with an ethnic group in race relations legislation. In this context, that is a complex and ambiguous concept. The framework set out in the Government's Bill is a fairer and more satisfactory way to proceed, and that is why I want to highlight this issue through the amendments.
