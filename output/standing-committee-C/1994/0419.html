Dr. Michael Clark

: I thank you, Mr. Hill, for taking the Chair so soon after our return from the Easter recess. I also thank my hon. Friends and colleagues who have come here this morning to try to ensure that the Bill completes the Committee stage and proceeds to Report.

You will recall, Mr. Hill, that on the Wednesday before the Easter recess my hon. Friend the Member for Epping Forest (Mr. Norris), the Minister for Transport in London, summarised and explained the amendments to clause 2. During that debate, my hon. Friend the Member for Batley and Spen (Mrs. Peacock) asked about non-British driving licences and tests and I want to give a fuller answer to her questions and those from other hon. Members.

The rules on exchangeability and validity of licences from other European Union countries will change radically on 1 July next year when the second European directive on driving licences comes into force. On the same date, the provisions of that directive will be extended to non-European Union countries in the European Economic Area. The Bill has been drafted to reflect the new rules because it is impracticable for the Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency to introduce the provisions of the Bill in advance of the major changes required to implement the second European directive.

The Bill applies only to holders of British driving licences. The word "licence" is construed in the same way as in section 108 in part III of the Road Traffic Act 1988, which defines a licence, except where the context otherwise requires, as

"a licence to drive a motor vehicle granted under this Part of this Act."

The Department of Transport considered the possibilities of applying the provisions to holders of licences of other European Economic Area countries, but was advised that it would not be possible to revoke a licence granted by an authority other than another European Economic Area state, and nor would those states be obliged to recognise such revocation were it to take place.

However, a licence granted under Part III of the 1988 Act is not necessarily granted only to people who have passed driving tests in Great Britain. It may be granted in exchange for an equivalent licence issued in Northern Ireland, the Channel Isles, the Isle of Man, Gibraltar or in 36 other European Economic Area states, or on evidence of passing a test in Northern Ireland or any of those states or territories.

In all the cases mentioned, a British licence granted in exchange on or after 1 July next year will show the date of the holder's first becoming entitled to a full licence—that is, the date on which the probationary period begins in accordance with clause 1(1) of the Bill.

Under the second European directive to which I referred earlier, a licence may be issued only by the authority of the applicant's country of normal residence which, with some exceptions, means the place where he lives for at least 185 days in each calendar year. Hence, a British licence cannot lawfully be granted to a non-resident in terms of the directive for use as a flag of convenience.

Dr. Michael Clark

The Chairman

Dr. Clark
: The main part of the Bill covers the straightforward case of a qualified driver who has exchanged his or her provisional licence and test certificate for a full licence and is driving under that licence. Schedule 1 modifies those provisions to cover a qualified driver who has not yet exchanged a test certificate and still holds a provisional licence, although a test certificate as well. Section 89 of the Road Traffic Act 1988 allows a driver to obtain a full licence at any time within two years of passing the test. However, in the interim, he is permitted to drive free from the conditions attached to the provisional licence—that is, he need not display L-plates and, if a car driver, need not have an experienced driver sitting beside him. He has passed a test and although he is still technically driving on a provisional licence, the test certificate serves as proof of his entitlement. If he were asked by a policeman to produce his licence, he would be advised to produce the test certificate also. If he did not, he might have to defend a charge of driving otherwise than in accordance with his licence. It seems most odd that anyone who passes a driving test does not immediately send off the application for a full licence, but the Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency informs me that it receives quite a lot of late applications. If the application is made after the two-year deadline, the applicant must pass another test. That happens more often than one might suppose.

44 All that relates to schedule 1 as drafted. The schedule does not cover another group of drivers of which I was not aware when drafting the schedule—those who pass more than one test for more than one class of vehicle. I am looking at the hon. Member for Crewe and Nantwich (Mrs. Dunwoody) while I explain the reason for there being so many admendments and specifically the three amendments to clause 4. Loopholes in the Bill, some of which the hon. Lady tried to find in clause 2, were found in time to correct them in clause 4. They were covered in the amendments before us and I hope that the hon. Lady will give them an easy passage.

Someone who has passed two tests may send in his test certificate and obtain a full licence on the first occasion, but on a later occasion he may not get round to upgrading the licence and might hold a test certificate as well as a full licence. The first question to ask is whether it matters. In some cases it could matter a great deal—for example, if someone with a full motor cycle licence then passes a test to drive a car but does not exchange the certificate. Motor cyclists are always punctual at trading in their provisional licences because they are valid for only two years, but those for cars are valid for longer.

If a driver has his licence revoked with six penalty points, the court will return the licence to the Secretary of State, but is not obliged to inspect the test certificate because he has a full licence; it may not even know that he has a full licence; it may not even know that he has an extra test certificate. The all-knowing computer in the Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency would know about it, but the agency could do nothing about it. The driver might then obtain a provisional licence but he may by then not be interested in motor cycles. He may realise that he still has the unclaimed certificate for passing his car test and could send off that certificate to the agency and apply for a full licence. Having held back his pass certificate and having got a new provisional licence, he puts the two together, sends them off and attempts to obtain a full licence for a car, to which he is not entitled. The agency staff check the computer records, realise what he has done, read the small print and conclude that they have no grounds on which to refuse the application. He is not prevented from obtaining the licence under clause 4 because he never held a full car licence. He is not prevented by schedule 1 either because his licence is not a simple provisional licence. He has slipped through the net and can have the licence he wants without taking a re-test. That is clearly unacceptable and I am attempting to stop it by means of the amendments.

I had hoped that the courts and the agencies would apply the main part of the Bill and schedule 1 concurrently to achieve the result that we want: unfortunately, however, life is not that simple so amemdments were clearly needed. I had not anticipated that the process would be so long and so complicated, but the whole structure of the Bill as originally written is affected, so there are more complications than we would have wished.

I had better explain the mechanics. Amendment No. 27 gives the schedule a new title. The schedule is to be divided into five parts: part I defines the scope; part II requires all test certificates to be produced to the court; part III is similar to the present provisions for drivers who still have provisional licences; part IV, which is entirely new, is similar in effect to part III, but deals with drivers who have full licences and test certificates; part V again deals with JPEG 45 both those groups, covering much the same ground as paragraph 5(5) and paragraph 6 in the Bill as printed, but modified to take the new group of drivers into account.

The procedures for the new group of drivers combine those in the main body of the Bill and those in the existing schedule 1. It may not be altogether clear how the amendments to clause 4 fit into that pattern, but those, too, reflect the fact that a person may be entitled to drive some vehicles as a full licence holder and others under provisional licence conditions. The fact that a single piece of paper may serve both functions is not taken into account in the existing wording of clause 4 and is a potential source of confusion.

It may help the Committee if I give an example. Suppose a young driver passes the car driving test and obtains a full car licence, which allows him also to ride a motorcycle as a learner, as I did as a young man of 17½. It was a long time ago and my long-term memory is fading, but I just remember it.     Dr. Michael Clark
    : Forgive me for rising again, but it is the last time I shall do so in this Committee and is, therefore, a pleasant duty. I thank you for the patience that you have shown in chairing the Committee, Mr. Hill, and for the way in which you have assisted me. This is the first time that I have promoted a private Member's Bill and, after the Easter recess, we all needed your help this morning.

    When I started, I thought that it would be simple to introduce a Bill that would ensure that someone who had got six penalty points during their first two years of driving would have their licence revoked, and that would require them to take a test before continuing on their sweet and merry way. Complications grew as one spent more time considering the matter. We thought everyting was fine when the first draft was prepared, but then realised that it was not. Members of the Committee have pointed out other potential—one hopes, inconsequential—shortcomings. The Bill proved to be more complicated in its drafting and Committee stage than one had hoped.

    I thank the sponsors of the Bill, most of whom have served on the Committee, and those who spoke on Second Reading and who may have a chance to speak again. Principally, I thank those who came to both of the Committee's sittings. There has been a high percentage turnout; clearly, members of the Committee believe that the Bill is worthwhile which is why they have given up their time to be here. I am grateful to all of them.
