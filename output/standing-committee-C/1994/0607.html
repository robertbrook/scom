Civil Rights (Disabled Persons) Bill

Clause 12
PROVISION OF GOODS AND SERVICES TO A QUALIFIED

DISABLED PERSON

10.30 am

Question proposed, That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

Chairman
: With this we shall consider new clause 2— circumstances in which discrimination is justified—

'.—(1) For the purposes of section 12 above, a provider of goods, facilities or services is justified in discriminating against a disabled person only if—

  1. in his opinion, one or more of the conditions mentioned in subsection (2) are satisfied; and
  2. it is reasonable, in all the circumstances of the case, for him to hold that opinion.
  1. The conditions are that—
    1. in any case—
      1. the discrimination is necessary in order not to endanger the health or safety of any person (which may include that of the disabled person); or
      2. the disabled person is incapable of entering into an enforceable agreement, or of giving an informed consent, and for that reason the discrimination is reasonable in that case;
    2. in a case falling within section 12(2)(a), the discrimination is necessary in order for the provider of goods, facilities or services to be able to provide the goods, facility or service to other members of the public;
    3. in a case falling within section 12(2)(b),(c), (cc), (d), (e) or (f), the discrimination is reasonably necessary in order for the provider of goods, facilities or services to be able to provide the goods, facility or service to the disabled person or to other members of the public;
    4. in a case falling within section 12(2)(cc), the difference in the terms on which the goods, facility or service is provided to the disabled person and those on which it is provided to other members of the public reflects the greater cost to the provider of goods, facilities or services in providing it to the disabled person.
  2. Regulations may make provision as to other circumstances in which, for the purposes of section 12, a provider of services of a prescribed description (for example, insurance services) is to be taken to be justified in treating a disabled person less favourably than he treats, or would treat, other members of the public.'.

The Minister for Social Security and Disabled People (Mr. William Hague)
: Given the length of time the Committee has spent debating clause 12 I do not intend to raise many points about it now. We have discussed a large number of amendments and I should like to refer briefly to new clause 2 and my belief that any anti-discrimination measure would need such a clause.

New clause 2 would help us to face reality and admit that there will be a few occasions when a service provider would be justified in treating a disabled person less favourably than he would any other member of the public. Those instances will be rare and the tests which service 238 providers would have to pass successfully for new clause 2 to be evoked as a defence are severe. But the clause is none the less vital to the success of the legislation and we would be misguided if we thought that the Bill could do without a clause of this nature.

The Bill's advocates may argue that all is taken care of by the Bill's definition of a "qualified disabled person". We have debated that at considerable length and so I shall not go over it again. A "qualified disabled person" would have to meet

"the essential requirements for the provision of goods, facilities and services".

There is some notion of justified discrimination within that. A person who did not meet the essential requirements would presumably not be a "qualified disabled person". We cannot necessarily have confidence that the courts would interpret that formula in the manner intended. No doubt the proposed commission would issue a code of practice to clarify matters. But even assuming it produces a clear code which meets with wide acclaim, what happens if a court decides that the words in the Bill mean something else? Courts have been known to take surprising decisions—they certainly surprise policy makers from time to time—and successive Governments over the years have had to ask Parliament to pass amending regulations made necessary because of an unexpected court decision.

That brings us to the problem that I have often referred to in our debates—the absence of regulation-making powers. Since I announced the Government's proposals to the House in November I have continually emphasised how important it is to maintain the credibility of the legislation in the eyes of those employers and service providers who will be asked to implement it. If the aspirations of disabled people, shared by all of us in this Room, for full participation in society are to be met, it is vital that the reasonable limitations on what can and should be required of business are recognised.

A clause such as new clause 2 is necessary. I have spoken about it on many other occasions during our debates and so I shall not move it formally when we come to that point in our proceedings. I should like to ask questions about one subject covered by clause 12 that we have not considered during our debates on the amendments. Clause 12(1) uses the words:

"in relation to goods, facilities or services".

That is presumably distinct from saying in relation to the provision of goods, facilities and services which is the alternative formula.

Should it be assumed from that wording that the manufacture of goods is covered as well as their sale or delivery? The provision of goods, facilities and services seems to mean their sale or delivery, but the proposal refers to goods, facilities and services, not just to their provision. Does the hon. Member for Derbyshire, North-East (Mr. Barnes) intend the measure to cover the JPEG 239 manufacture of goods and if so, to what extent? Would instructions have to be in an accessible format or on a particular kind of packaging? Would design features of manufactured products—buttons, switches, displays, dials and so on—have to be accessible? If that is so, how does the hon. Gentleman propose to deal with manufactured goods made in other countries but sold in British shops? Would they be able to stock foreign goods that failed to comply with the requirements of the clause? If not, how would that square with international agreements on free trade and competitiveness? We had to consider that tricky issue in our debate on the Disability Discrimination Bill and I would welcome comments on the matter and clarification.

'(1C) Nothing in section 12 makes it unlawful for a provider of goods, facilities or services to fail to take any steps which would cause him to incur expenditure exceeding the prescribed maximum.

Mr. Hague
: The amendment would protect service providers from being obliged to make changes that would alter the fundamental nature of their business. The Bill provides that a service provider must not discriminate against disabled people by failing to make reasonable modifications to rules, policies or practices.

Clause 12(2)(e) obliges a service provider to remove architectural, communication and transport barriers when such modifications will not impose undue hardship on the business. Under Clause 12(2)(f), the service provider has a duty to supply auxiliary aids and services when that will not impose undue hardship on the business. The Committee knows my objections to a test of undue hardship, and also that I support the intention of the provisions. The Government's disability discrimination measure contains parallel provisions.

The test of undue hardship must be considered with the provisions of clause 14(1), which contains the Bill's criteria for determining the reasonableness of any modification. The test does not refer to the effect of a modification on the nature of the service. Therefore, the Bill may require a service provider to make changes that would fundamentally alter the business. That could lead to ludicrous circumstances in which, for example, the proprietor of a nightclub would be required by law to increase the level of lighting to meet the needs of a visually impaired person. We agree that a proprietor of a nightclub should not be allowed blatantly to refuse entry to a disabled person. But a requirement to turn up the lights would change a fundamental feature of the business and would spoil the enjoyment of other customers.

Amendment No. 60 introduces a subject familiar to those of us who discussed the Disability Discrimination Bill. It would introduce the power to set a financial limit on the level of expenditure required to comply with the right of access. The power to set a financial limit on the cost of meeting one's duties under the legislation is essential if businesses and service providers are to have confidence in the Bill. Service providers will reap the rewards of better access through increased custom, and when the Disability Discrimination Bill becomes law, we must all join in persuading them of that. Nevertheless, it is important for service providers to know that there is a limit, within any given time period, on the expenditure in which they are likely to be involved.

It my be claimed that the concept of undue hardship is a sufficient safeguard for traders' interests, but I am not persuaded that it would be or that service providers would be convinced that it would be. The ability to set a financial cap, as set out in amendment No. 60, would give a certainty to business that a phrase such as "undue hardship" cannot provide. In previous sittings I gave examples from the United States of firms which appear to have been driven out of business because of the enforcement of the Americans with Disabilities Act and the uncertain concept of undue hardship.

The amendments would incorporate an ability to set a financial limit, in the same way as in the Disability 246 Discrimination Bill. That could be calculated by reference to a variety of situations. It could encompass aggregate amounts of expenditure incurred in different cases, so that a service provider could consult a local access officer, plan a series of improvements to access over a period of time and be reassured that just because elements of that plan did not come up to the financial limit, he would not be required to make further alterations. We must account for the wide variety of modifications that might be considered; for example, installing a ramp, widening doors or removing obstructions from corridors or shopping isles.

The provision would be a fair way of ensuring that the service provider who sensibly planned ahead would not run the risk of having greater liabilities than the service provider who chose to delay taking any action until forced to do so by the threat of legal action. For similar reasons, it could also cope with different periods. The provision might also be a way of allowing a service provider to make an earlier start to improvements without necessarily waiting for a particular date when the provisions became operative.

It is our plan in phasing in the Disability Discrimination Bill to set out clearly what will be a service provider's obligations and then to allow time for him or her to incorporate those in, for example, planned schedules of refurbishment of premises. One of the clearest messages that I brought back from my studies of what is happening in the United States is that service providers and employers can be persuaded overwhelmingly to support antidiscrimination measures for disabled people and will be prepared to make adjustments, but they need to know what will be required of them and to know that the costs will not be unreasonable.

The provision can also cope with the problem of different services, thereby allowing traders in certain types of business to be treated differently from others. Following consultation on the Government Bill, it became clear that it would be inequitable for a provider of a computer support service for, for example, who may have no premises open to the public, to be under the same obligations as a major chain of high street retailers. It would ensure that a person who rented out stalls in an indoor market could take the necessary steps to ensure that the premises met the requirements of the legislation. An individual stallholder could thus have confidence that no further physical alteration to the premises would be required.

The flexible approach that I propose could deal with such situations; the Bill as drafted offers nothing to help service providers understand their obligations other than JPEG 247 the woolly notion of reasonableness in clause 14(1), with no reliable, legally binding mechanism to provide a clearer picture in the future.

The new clause would ensure that insurance companies will continue to be allowed to consider actuarial data, or other data from a source on which it is reasonable to rely, when assessing the insurance risk of a disabled person without being accused of unlawful discrimination.

Before I explain the detail of the new clause, it is important that the Committee understand the background. The United Kingdom insurance market is highly competitive. In that market, there is considerable variance in the degree of cover provided, rate of premiums charged and policy imposed.

Insurance is the business of selecting and calculating risks and setting premiums to correspond to those risks. When disability has no effect on the risk, it should be disregarded in the calculation of the premium and in setting policy conditions. That is why we have included coverage of financial services in the Government's Disability Discrimination Bill. However, disabled people, like others, can have a medical condition that, in the case of life insurance, can affect mortality. Insurers must be able to take those factors into account in setting premium rates and policy conditions.

In other words, discrimination is, to some extent, the basis of the insurance industry. Although it is right that insurance companies must be allowed to discriminate, we should not be prepared to let them discriminate unfairly. That is what the Government Bill will prevent and what the hon. Member for Kingswood said was his objective when he sponsored his Bill last year.
