Amendment No. 6 would provide for the exemption of part VIII of the Charities Act 1993. Part VIII deals with provisions that relate specifically to charitable companies and, in particular, their winding up under section 63 of the Act. It also includes section 64, the alterations of objects clause, and section 68, which provides for charities' status to appear on correspondence. Those specific provisions in relation to charitable companies may need to be excluded from the Bill, because otherwise each charitable company will not have to be dealt with separately and the corporate identity of each charitable company that is part of a conglomeration of charities may be lost. The strict requirement of corporate status is a safeguard that we should throw away only with great reluctance and after the most careful consideration.

It is worth pausing to reflect on the structure of certain charities. It is often the case that a charitable trust controls a separate charitable company. In fact, the charitable trust and the charitable company will often have the same individuals involved as trustees of both. Charities in that situation regard the provision as an important safeguard for their own success.

We thus return to the way in which the Charity Commission will exercise the powers that the Bill will confer on it. Will it force people to move to a status that they do not seek? That would be a retrograde measure that the Committee should not endorse. Perhaps I might give a practical example of how that works for a charity. We do not want a debate about the political involvement of charities, but many charities exist to spread a message, whether that be educational, religious or about third world development issues.

As members of the Committee will be aware, I come from a public relations background. I know that in the modern world, spreading the message is an expensive process that can involve a variety of techniques.
