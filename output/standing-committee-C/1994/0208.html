10.30 pm

Mrs. Diana Maddock
(Christchurch)
: I beg to move,

That, during its consideration of the Home Energy Conservation Bill, the Committee do meet on Wednesdays at half-past Ten o'clock and at half-past Four o'clock.

Given the agreement in principle on Second Reading and given that I have been in regular contact with the Minister, I see no reason why the Committee should not proceed swiftly. Unless Committee members are difficult, I do not anticipate that we shall sit next Wednesday afternoon—I sincerely hope not. I ask for the Committee's co-operation.

An Energy Conservation Bill was first introduced by the hon. Member for Ceredigion and Pembroke, North (Mr. Dafis), and I am grateful for his continued support for the Home Energy Conservation Bill. There was wide support, so another Bill was introduced by my right hon. Friend the Member for Berwick-upon-Tweed (Mr. Beith), who faced a rather different climate of opposition tactics last year. However, despite that, support for the Bill has grown—especially outside the House—which is one reason why I hope that the Bill will be successful.

There are many reasons why we are debating this important Home Energy Conservation Bill. More than 700 councils, controlled by all political parties, support it. Since Second Reading, even more councils have supported what we are trying to achieve. Few Bills have had such support from elected councils.

In addition, there has been exceptional support not only from a wide range of voluntary organisations and environmental groups, which one would expect, but from many other groups which support people whom I describe as the fuel poor, and also from agencies that are responsible for looking after the elderly—Age Concern and Help the Aged, for example. The Trades Union Congress, Mencap, the Royal Society for the Protection of Birds, and many other groups support the Bill.

Of course, the Bill is as necessary as ever. It will impose a duty on local authorities to survey the energy efficiency of residential properties in their areas and to produce reports to enable the Secretary of State to act accordingly. We must correct the current lack of coordinated information on home energy efficiency across the country. That is widely felt, especially by environmentalists and those who work in energy conservation. Many such people believe that the main obstacle to a comprehensive national energy conservation strategy is the very fact that the measures in the Bill have not been enacted before. The Association for the Conservation of Energy has called the Bill the most important for energy conservation since the second world war. I am very grateful for such strong support.

The Bill is as necessary as ever, especially for people who, as we sit here, live in cold and damp properties. That has not changed.

The general principles that have guided the Government in tabling amendments are those that I set out on Second Reading. We have to ensure that any new duties placed on local authorities are the minimum necessary to achieve the Bill's aims, and that, wherever possible, central and local government are given the opportunity to build upon existing procedures and information in complying with the Bill. Additional costs must be kept to the minimum, and I am sure that the Committee will agree that that is a sensible approach. It is much better that resources should be channelled into practical energy efficiency measures than into layers of bureaucracy. At the same time, we want there to be real benefits from requiring local authorities to take a systematic look at what can be done in their areas and from having information gathered together at the centre to inform and influence Government thinking.

The Government's concerns about the Bill, naturally, have meant that we have had to look closely at the content and purpose of the proposed energy conservation reports. Our aim has been to preserve the fundamental concept that a local authority should draw up a report setting out the measures which it considers will result in an improvement in the energy efficiency of residential accommodation in its area, but the requirement to adhere to a rigid target requiring considerable savings was difficult to accept. Not that considerable or, to use the word in my amendment, significant savings are not desirable—of course they are—but there is concern that such a target could have an adverse effect on better-performing authorities and that there may be real difficulties in adequately defining a numerical target. I am sure that we shall have considerable discussion on that matter. That is why it is appropriate that we should accept the sittings motion. It is important that consideration of the proposal should be speedy and sure.

We also felt that there was a need to reconsider the Secretary of State's functions in relation to the reports. Again, the general concept of the provisions as drafted was broadly in line with an approach that the Government 6 would want, but, in the Government's view, it is desirable to allow some flexibility in setting timetables for submitting reports—for example, to take into account forthcoming local government reorganisation. We have also sought to provide for reports to be updated from time to time, either at the initiative of the energy conservation authority or at the direction of the Secretary of State.

One important new element in the Bill is a provision for guidance to be issued by the Secretary of State. Again, that is a practical measure to make the Bill work better. We felt that a form of guidance would be necessary and worth while to ensure a broadly consistent approach by local authorities, to ensure that reports were presented in such a way that informed decisions could be taken by the Secretary of State, and, most important, to suggest to authorities that are not as advanced as others the steps that they really should be thinking about.

The amendments in my name also provide that Northern Ireland would be included in the Bill. I am sure that that will be welcome to the Committee—many hon. Members suggested that on Second Reading. My remarks show that the Government approach the Bill constructively, and I look forward to participating in our discussions on that basis.

Some people will say that the Bill is not greatly different from the Energy Conservation Bill that was presented by the right hon. Member for Berwick-upon-Tweed, but that is an over-simplification—many changes have occurred to take on board the various criticisms that were made, and I welcome that. This Bill will be less rigid, more clearly defined and more workable than the previous Session's Bill, although delivering the same broad aim of reducing energy consumption in the home. If the Bill emerges from Committee in the form that the Government would like, its emphasis will have shifted to complementing existing powers of central and local government rather than to imposing new duties for detailed inspections and complex plans.

The Home Energy Conservation Bill has the potential to produce a cost-effective assessment of energy use and to increase public awareness of the problem of energy inefficiency. If it can deliver real and significant savings to the benefit of householders, local economies and the national and global environment, the nation's debt to the hon. Lady will be considerable. I look forward to participating in today's debate and in future debates with the constructive approach that the Government have already demonstrated.

    Mr. Jones
    : The amendments deal with definitions. Amendment No. 1 is a technical one concerning Great Britain. It will be welcome, as regards Northern Ireland, as it provides how the Bill shall apply there. It amends the definition of energy conservation authority so as to give the responsibility for preparing reports under the Bill's provisions in each part of the United Kingdom to the authority responsible for housing. The change as regards England, Wales and Scotland is one of drafting, not of substance. "Local housing authority" for England and Wales, and "local authority" for Scotland, are the terms used in the relevant legislation to indicate the authority responsible for housing. This terminology is clear and has the advantage that it will automatically take account of changes due to local government reorganisation.

    In Northern Ireland, it is proposed that the Northern Ireland Housing Executive should act as the energy conservation authority. The administrative arrangements for delivering local authority functions in Northern Ireland differ from those in the rest of the United Kingdom. Functions such as housing, roads, planning and social services are carried out by central Government Departments or by statutory bodies rather than by local authorities. It would be quite inappropriate to give district councils in Northern Ireland responsibility for preparing reports containing proposals for energy conservation measures for residential properties in their areas. Northern Ireland district councils do not have any housing functions and if, as proposed in the Bill, the functions of energy conservation authorities are given to local authorities responsible for housing in the rest of the United Kingdom, it seems logical that the energy conservation authority in Northern Ireland should be the Northern Ireland Housing Executive—the sole public housing authority in Northern Ireland.

    Besides being responsible for energy conservation in its own stock of around 160,000 dwellings—some 27 per cent. of the total housing stock in Northern Ireland—the Housing Executive has in the past acted as an agent of the Northern Ireland Department of Economic Development in paying insulation grants to low income families in the private sector under the former homes insulation scheme. It also has a statutory duty to examine house conditions across all tenures in Northern Ireland and does so every five years by means of a house condition survey which is increasingly paying attention to the energy efficiency of stock. I mink that we can take it as read that the Housing Executive is the appropriate body to carry out the duties of an energy conservation authority in Northern Ireland.

    Amendment No. 4 is consequential and provides that references to the area of an energy conservation authority shall be a reference to the area of the authority prescribed as the energy conservation authority, or to Northern Ireland as a whole in relation to the Northern Ireland Housing Executive. There are such references, for example, in the existing clause 2 and new clause 1, which I shall be suggesting will replace it.

    I commend the amendments to the Committee.
