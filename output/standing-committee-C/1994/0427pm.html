I was in mid-speech and speaking to amendment No. 5, which addresses the question of the definition of the organisation of disabled people. Those are the organisations to be consulted by the commission in the course of its work. I moved the amendment because I cannot see what advantage a restriction such as the one posited by the provision would bring to the quality of information gained by the commission, or hence to the usefulness of the codes of practice and guidance that might ensue.

I am sure that the hon. Member for Derbyshire, North-East (Mr. Barnes) would recognise the value and experience of bodies representing disabled people which perhaps do not have a majority of floating members who are disabled or whose controlling body does not contain a majority of disabled people. I have in mind bodies such as Scope and Mencap which do not meet that strict definition. The Bill appears to put them into a different category, which troubles me.

I wonder whether the Bill's supporters could tell the Committee either now, or in writing at a later stage in our proceedings, how many organisations of disabled people meet the definition of organisations which have to be consulted by the commission on draft codes of practice. Would I be right in thinking that some organisations which we all value greatly such as Scope and Mencap would not meet the definition? Would the commission that is envisaged need to maintain a register of organisations that met the definition? If so, how would it be done? I have a number of points to raise.
