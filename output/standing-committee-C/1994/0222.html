Mr. Jamieson: It is a great pleasure for me to introduce the Bill to the Committee having had such widespread, cross-party support on Second Reading. It is only the second time I have served on a Committee under your chairmanship, Mr. Hill. I remember from the previous occasion your firmness, fairness and good humour. Although I hope that this Committee will be brief, I am delighted to serve on it under your watchful eye. Col. 4 It is unusual for a new Member to present a Bill in Committee, especially in his first term. I am pleased that the Committee contains many Members who spoke so strongly in favour of the Bill on Second Reading. I was given one word of caution about the Bill. An older Member told me that it is rare to get wide, cross-party support, and he reminded me of two other issues for which there was such support: the exchange rate mechanism and the Child Support Agency. I hope that during our deliberations we can find something to disagree on, so that this Bill does not go down the same road as those other two matters.

I hope that the Bill will continue to receive such wide, cross-party support, that its provisions will survive for many years and that we can together create a new law of lasting value.

Just yesterday, the Select Committee on Education produced a report on "Safety in Outdoor Activity Centres". Written evidence from more than 90 people and oral evidence collected by the Committee showed overwhelming support for the measures proposed in the Bill. It is extremely unusual for a Select Committee to report on a Bill that is before the House. I am pleased that the document launched yesterday by the Select Committee gives us such widespread support.

That report serves to remind us how many parents, teachers, governors, local education authorities and children support the Bill. They are all relying on the Committee to get the Bill on to the statute book in a form that is workable and will achieve the Bill's aim of making outdoor activity centres safer for children. I therefore hope that we shall make progress as rapidly as is compatible with proper consideration of the Bill.

Amendments Nos. 45 to 48 on the selection list deal with the Health and Safety Executive and its role in the Bill. As it stands, the Bill enshrines the requirement that the Secretary of State should consult the Health and Safety Commission. The amendments go beyond that. Amendment No. 45 gives the Health and Safety Commission a very full role, in particular in making nominations to the licensing body. That is appropriate because the Health and Safety Executive was charged, under the Health and Safety at Work, etc. Act 1974, with the task of advising the Government on safety matters. It is therefore proper that the Health and Safety Commission should recommend the licensing body to the Secretary of State.

The other amendments are consequential to amendment No. 45. Amendment No. 46 takes out "Secretary of State" and inserts "Health and Safety Commission" and, with amendment No. 45, it empowers the HSC to make recommendations about the nominations to the licensing committee. Because the HSC will nominate the licensing body, it will also give it guidance and will be required to consult beforehand.

The other amendments in the list follow in logical consequence. If we left the Bill as it stands, the HSC would nominate candidates and make recommendations to the Secretary of State; he would then be able to go back to the HSC and consult it over matters that it had already raised, and we would not want that. The subsequent amendments therefore follow on consequentially from amendment No. 45.

Mr. Mark Wolfson (Sevenoaks): I should like to challenge Mr. Jamieson's suggestion that the amendments would give the Health and Safety Executive a full role in nominating individuals to the licensing authority. In fact, they would give it a dominant role, which would be regrettable. The purpose and role of the HSE is to minimise all possible risk, especially in work situations. That is quite right and proper and I make no complaints about the HSE having that role.

In this situation, however, there is a danger that if such a responsibility were given to the HSE and not left to the discretion of the Secretary of State, the HSE's intention would be the minimisation of all risk. Yet, as I said on Second Reading, with strong backing from some of the best practitioners in the outdoor activity industry—the safety record of which is first class—there is a real danger that one could confine those activities: their adventurous side—the excitement and, therefore, the thrill and the attraction—as well as the opportunity for developing young people, which is provided by allowing what I would call an acceptable risk, could be squeezed out altogether, simply because the HSE has a specific and rather different role in its industrial and commercial settings. It is on that basis that I think that the measure could well be counter-productive and place an unnecessary constraint on the licensing authority's outlook and approach.

I want to deal carefully with the issue as I appreciate that certain members of the Committee will tell me that that is what the Bill is about: it is to ensure safe activity, which is absolutely paramount. They may ask who am I to risk any other lives after Lyme bay. If any hon. Members thought that, they are totally out of line with my thinking on the issue.

I return to the point, again made on Second Reading, that the industry's record is very good. The Bill deals with a few cowboy institutions. The whole inquiry and court case on Lyme bay demonstrated that that operation was totally out of line by any decent existing safety standards.

We should not, therefore, use the Bill to place an unnecessarily heavy hand or an unnecessarily constraining framework on the work of the centres, which need to provide some element of risk, perhaps even occasional danger, provided that the whole concept of the staff and the organisation in those centres is one of a culture of safety first. All of that was missing in Lyme bay where there were unqualified, ill-equipped staff, carrying out activities which, even to a sensible lay man, would have appeared wrong. That is the burden of my point. I do not want the HSE to be given quite such a dominant role as the amendment would provide.
