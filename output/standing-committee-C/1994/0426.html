Civil Rights (Disabled Persons) Bill

10.30 am

Mr. Harry Barnes
(Derbyshire, North-East)
: I beg to move,

That during proceedings on the Civil Rights (Disabled Persons) Bill the Committee do meet tomorrow at half-past Ten o'clock and at half-past Four o'clock.

It is a pleasure to move the sittings motion with you, Mr. Hogg, in the Chair.

I have moved the motion because the Bill has been pushed into a corner. Outside of a procedural coup, Friday is the last practical day for Report stage and Third Reading of the Bill. The Bill must be out of Committee by close of business on Thursday, but the Minister has tabled 111 unstarred amendments and two starred amendments. The only chance of the Bill being discussed on Friday is if the sittings motion is accepted so that we can deal with the amendments by the close of business on Thursday.

Why are we in this predicament? Changes in the business of the House mean that only five days are left for the Report stage of private Members' Bills. That produces problems, as we know from experience with the Bill before us, which must be reported back to the House. The Government have decided that only Standing Committee C is available for private Members' Bills. This is the fifth private Member's Bill this session. The four earlier Bills have made slow progress, yet all those Bills were either Government Bills or were supported by the Government and the Opposition. We expected to reach the Civil Rights (Disabled Persons) Bill before now.

None of the previous Bills had extra sittings. A sittings motion was passed on the Home Energy Conservation Bill, but that proved to be unnecessary because that Bill was dealt with in two sittings. The Activity Centre (Young Persons Safety) Bill had three sittings and the Proceeds of Crime Bill had three sittings, which was surprising because it was promoted by the hon. Member for Exeter (Sir J. Hannam), who is joint chairman of the all-party disablement group and was aware of our need to move forward. The third sitting of that Bill in Committee lasted a mere 18 minutes, which was a serious waste of opportunity for which we would have been very grateful.

The Road Traffic (New Drivers) Bill, which was promoted by the hon. Member for Rochford (Dr. Clark), was in Committee for two weeks. Although it is a private Member's Bill, it should be considered to be a Government Bill. When I was lucky in the draw for private Members' Bills, I received a letter from the Minister for Transport in 4 London asking whether I would take on that Bill. It stated:

"The attached papers describe a Bill designed to improve the safety of new drivers by requiring those who offend to re-take their test."

An extra problem has arisen from the Road Traffic (New Drivers) Bill because the promoter of the Bill chose to take the Report stage this Friday instead of last Friday, as would have been his right, had he so wished. That has seriously annoyed organisations for the disabled, which have realised that, even if we manage to reach Report stage by Friday, being number two on the list will make it more difficult for us to make progress if there is a great deal of talking on the Road Traffic (New Drivers) Bill. That is why 15 disabled people—reflecting the 15 attempts to put this legislation on the statute books—have set off on a march from Rochford to London. They are due to arrive here on Friday, when we hope that the Bill will be dealt with in the House.

The remaining impediments to progress are the amendments in the name of the Minister, 22 of which relate to the word "qualified", which reduces the number that we have to deal with separately. Others are technical, covering various slips in the drafting of the Bill. For example, there are two amendments which would change the words "Great Britain" to "United Kingdom", which would be a correct technical adjustment because the Bill applies to the whole of the United Kingdom.

The bulk of amendments represent an attempt to turn my Bill into the Government's Disability Discrimination Bill, which has already been through the House. Why has that been done? The Government have their own Bill, so there is no need to turn this one into something that looks like it. A second Bill that is a carbon copy of the Government's could not proceed on the Floor of the House for procedural reasons. "Erskine May" is quite clear that there cannot be two such similar Bills before the House, although there was a ruling ahead of the Second Reading of the Disability Discrimination Bill that permitted this Bill to go forward, even though the Government's Bill had already done so, because of specific differences between them.

All that can be said in favour of the bulk of the amendments in the Minister's name is that they are in his name.
