    In civil proceedings, one begins by issuing a generally endorsed writ, although it is permissible to serve a writ endorsed with a statement of claim.     That is the broad outline of the argument. The prosecutor should have to tell not only the court but the defendant that he or the prosecution service has a case to put against the defendant. That is entirely sensible and a matter of natural justice. The man against whom an order may be made should be informed at the earliest possible time that it is to be made.

    Subsection (1B) goes further. It states:

    "Where the court decides, in accordance with subsection (1)(b) above, that it would be appropriate for it to proceed under this section without a written notice from the prosecutor, it shall as soon as practicable provide to the offender a statement summarising the reasons for its decision."

    The prosecutor or the court may decide that there is an appropriate prima facie case. In either case, the defendant must be informed, and with sufficient clarity so that he is made aware of the main facts upon which the prosecutor intends to rely. Those facts include the nature of the proceeds that the defendant is alleged to have amassed and their whereabouts and value.

    Subsection (1C) would provide:

    "The court shall not take any action in pursuance of a written notice from the prosecutor under subsection (1) above until the offender has acknowledged receipt of a copy of the notice; provided that after the period of fourteen days has elapsed beginning with the date of sending of a copy of the notice to the offender, the court may presume, in the absence of any communication from him, that he has received the copy of the notice."

    In many cases, the recipient of the notice would be in 12 prison or would be represented by solicitors, so there would be no difficulty in serving the notice so as to ensure actual rather than constructive knowledge of it. In other cases, however, defendants might have absconded or deliberately absented themselves. The court should not for that reason be precluded from inquiring into their fortunes. That is why the amendment would introduce the saving provision to allow the court to proceed in the absence of offenders or any communication from them.

    Such a way of going about things is not new or strange. It is used in civil proceedings. If a defendant does not acknowledge service of a writ, the plaintiff may enter judgment in default of acknowledgment. If the defendant acknowledges the writ but fails to respond to the statement of claim, the plaintiff can proceed in default of defence. I am not introducing into the Bill a procedure that is alien to the law. My amendment would introduce clarity, more obvious justice and openness—three characteristics that I hope hon. Members would welcome.

In the Bill, we are in the difficult position of wanting to create effective provisions to amend part VI of the Criminal Justice Act 1988, while ensuring that adequate safeguards exist in respect of trivial cases.
