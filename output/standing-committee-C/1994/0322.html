Proceeds of Crime Bill

clause Clause 2
title CONFISCATION RELATING TO A COURSE OF CRIMINAL CONDUCT

10.30 am

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Sir John Hannam (Exeter): I was much impressed by hon. Members' scrutiny of the clause at our previous sitting. The clause is the heart of the Bill. It attempts to strengthen substantially the courts' powers under part VI of the Criminal Justice Act 1988 to enable them to deal more effectively with offenders who amass proceeds from persistent offending. The courts' existing powers are extremely limited and, with your permission, Mr. Hill, I shall explain why.

Under part VI of the 1988 Act the courts are empowered to confiscate only the proceeds of any offences of which a defendant stands convicted in the current proceedings, plus the proceeds of any offences taken into consideration by the court in those proceedings. Unfortunately, all too often the offences of which a defendant has been convicted represent only a small proportion of those actually committed and a confiscation order merely scratches the surface of a criminal's true benefit. In theory, the provision enabling the courts to confiscate the proceeds of offences taken into consideration may enable them to confiscate proceeds more realistically aligned with a defendant's true benefits, but in practice offences are taken into consideration only if a defendant consents.

Criminals, as we know all too well, are aware that the more offences they allow to be taken into consideration, the more they are likely to have to pay under a confiscation order. It is hardly surprising that cases that are clearly appropriate for confiscation orders cannot be prosecuted satisfactorily at present. I have no doubt that those difficulties are largely to blame for the lamentable statistics on the amounts that have been confiscated under part VI of the 1988 Act.

The shortcomings of part VI of the 1988 Act are particularly obvious and acute in the sort of economic crimes that are committed by professional criminals. Two such crimes which worry hon. Members and the general public are counterfeiting and the production of pornography. In such offences the criminal's profit accrues not from the commission of one major offence but from the repeated commission of the same offence, which may be relatively minor—for example, the production of one video. That same offence is committed thousands, tens of Col. 44 thousands and sometimes millions of times. Under existing provisions, the courts may confiscate the proceeds of a single major offence but have no powers to deal with the sort of crimes that consist of repeated commission of the same relatively minor offence. For the entire proceeds of a course of such offending to be confiscated, each offence would have to be charged and convicted separately, which is wholly impractical, or each offence could be taken into consideration by the court, but that does not happen in practice.

The solution is to allow the courts to make assumptions about the illegal origin of the defendant's property similar to those in drug trafficking legislation. The court should be empowered to make an assumption that a defendant's property has come from crime. The assumptions should, of course, be subject to the rebuttal by a defendant, and clause 2 allows for that. Once it has made such assumptions, the court should be able to order a defendant to pay an amount equal to the sum total of the proceeds exposed by the making of the assumptions. In keeping with the legislation generally, the court should not be able to order a defendant to pay more than the amount of realisable property available for confiscation where that is less than the amount of the proceeds revealed by the operation of the assumptions. Those are all sensible safeguards.

I have no difficulty with the principles underlying the making of such assumptions, whether in the drug trafficking legislation or in part VI of the Criminal Justice Act, 1988 as amended by this clause. It seems to me—and, I am sure to all members of the Committee—entirely reasonable to make defendants account for the lawful origin of their property where they have been convicted of crimes from which financial benefit is derived. The power to make assumptions is particularly important in respect of the sort of repeated offending that I have just described, because frequently no trace will remain of individual offences and the true extent of the offending will be peculiarly in the knowledge of the criminal.

Having stated the principles behind the amendments, I must add that the power to make such assumptions can have far-reaching effects and may result in the confiscation of the whole of a defendant's property. Part VI of the 1988 Act applies to crime generally, including a few highly lucrative summary offences. Great care must be taken to ensure that the wide-ranging power to make assumptions is brought to bear only in appropriate cases. For that reason the court will be able to make the assumptions only where written notice is tendered by the prosecutor.

In addition, the court will have a discretion as to whether or not it makes the assumptions in the case of a particular defendant. I am aware that there are doubts in some quarters as to whether it is appropriate to grant the courts such a discretion, bearing in mind the recent amendment to the drug trafficking legislation depriving the courts of the same discretion under those provisions. It is a question of striking the proper balance between the extreme nature of drug trafficking offences and the general nature of the offences covered by the Bill. That is a necessary safeguard, given the wide range of offences to which part VI of the 1988 Act applies.

Image Col. 45 Further safeguards are built into subsection (5). The court will have a general power not to make an assumption where it believes that there would be a serious risk of injustice. Specific provision is made to avoid the risk of double accounting, and the court will have the power not to make an assumption in relation to property taken account of in a previous confiscation order. Those are all sensible safeguards.

I come finally to the question of triggering offences, which featured so prominently in last week's debate. The purpose of clause 2 is to attack the proceeds of persistent offending. I have no desire to see the power to make assumptions used in trivial cases, and I am sure that all members of the Committee share that view. One must tread warily in these matters and I am anxious that these new powers should not fall into disrepute. However, some hon. Members drew attention to some anomalies and pitfalls in the clause as it is drafted. It is a question of striking the correct balance and I intend to reflect carefully on the points that were raised. I shall draw my conclusions before Report on whether we have identified the correct threshold, or whether that needs to be adjusted. In winding up last week's debate I expressed some sympathy for a revision of that threshold.

I conclude by recommending that clause 2 stand part of the Bill.

clause Clause 4
title PROVISION OF INFORMATION BY DEFENDANT

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Sir John Hannam: Clause 4 is closely linked to clause 3. Whereas clause 3 deals with the provision of information to the court by the prosecutor, clause 4 deals with the provision of information to the court by the defendant. It will ensure that the court's ability to make a confiscation order under part VI of the Criminal Justice Act 1988 is not impaired by a lack of disclosure on the part of the defendant. The High Court already possesses the power to order the defendant to provide information about his assets in cases where, for example, a restraint order has been made by the court. Clause 4 provides the Crown court and the magistrates court, as the case may be, with a general power to order the defendant at any stage to provide information to assist it in carrying out its functions under part VI of the 1988 Act. The information required could include details about the extent of the defendant's benefit from crime or the amount of realisable property available for confiscation.

It is, of course, important that confiscation hearings should not be unduly delayed when additional information is being sought. Clause 4, therefore, allows the court to set a time limit for provision of the information that it requires. If the defendant fails to provide this information within the time set by the court without reasonable excuse, the court will be able to draw adverse inferences from his failure to respond.

New clause 2 would amend paragraphs 10, 20 and 30 of schedule 4 to the Prevention of Terrorism (Temporary Provisions) Act 1989, and section 9 of the Criminal Justice (International Co-operation) Act 1990. The amendments would make it clear that the new clause is to come into effect on Royal Assent being given and that it will apply throughout the United Kingdom. The background is as follows.

Schedule 4 to the prevention of terrorism Act 1989 enables an Order in Council to made for the enforcement of external orders for the restraint and forfeiture of terrorist funds. That differs from the scheme applicable to the Image Col. 81 enforcement of external drug trafficking and all crime confiscation orders in one important respect. Under terrorism legislation, property is frozen in this country by the enforcement of an external restraint order. Under drug trafficking and other crime provisions, the prosecutors must apply for a domestic restraint order to the High Court.

Although the terrorism provisions for enforcing external restraint orders are an effective means of freezing property in the United Kingdom, it has become apparent that circumstances may arise in which it would be useful to have a power to apply to the High Court for a domestic restraint order. I think particularly of the early stages in a case where there was then no external restraint order but where it was desirable for the authorities to freeze property here, perhaps even before proceedings were instituted. Alternatively, the external restraint order may in some cases have to be served on those affected by it as soon as it is made abroad. Under those circumstances, the possible dissipation of the funds is obvious; but that could be forestalled by a timely application for a domestic restraint order. Under new clause 2, it would be possible to make such an order, as it already is possible in drug trafficking and all other crime legislation.

There is also doubt about whether it is possible at present to obtain a domestic restraint order at an early stage, in anticipation of an external forfeiture order generally—that is, an order for the destruction or other disposal of items used or intended to be used to commit offences. New clause 2 therefore also amends section 9 of the Criminal Justice (International Co-operation) Act 1990 to make that possible.

The provisions will provide more flexible powers for enforcing external forfeiture orders. I commend them to the Committee.
