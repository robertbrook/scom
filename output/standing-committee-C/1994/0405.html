Dr. Michael Clark (Rochford): With your permission, Mr. Hill, before I deal with the detail of clause 1, I wish to say how grateful I was to the House for giving such a warm reception to the Bill on Second Reading on 3 February. More than two months have passed since then and that is sufficient time to discover all sorts of small, technical blemishes and, indeed, one or two bigger ones. The amendments tabled are a result of that.

A glance at the amendment paper would suggest that my hon. Friend the Member for Epping Forest (Mr. Norris)—the Minister for Transport in London—and myself have done little else than consider amendments over the past eight weeks. I thank my hon. Friend the Minister for the assistance that he and his Department have given me in putting the Bill into proper shape. I am as convinced now as I was two months ago that the basic policy in the Bill is right and that the basic procedures will work.

I do not have too demanding a task in this stand part debate. Clause 1 is an introductory clause, defining the Bill's scope. It is indispensable for understanding what follows. For the most part, the Bill is simple enough to understand. On the day on which a driver first passes a driving test in any class of vehicle, he or she becomes a "qualified driver". The two-year probationary period described in the Bill starts from that date. How the driver behaves during those two years is the subject of the Bill.

If at any time during that period the driver's licence carries six or more penalty points, the provisions of clauses 2 and 3 come into effect. The driver is obliged to surrender his or her licence and is allowed to receive in exchange only a provisional licence and to drive under the conditions applying to learner drivers. For all learners, that means displaying L-plates and for a car driver it means being accompanied by an experienced driver.

Obviously, motor cyclists do not need to be accompanied, but they will be restricted to lower-powered motor cycles as are appropriate for learner drivers. Moreover, their provisional licence is valid for only two years. They are then on and off the road for one year altogether before they can renew it. Therefore, for someone used to full-driver status, it can be quite a Col. 4 humiliation to revert to being a learner driver, but it is much less so than being disqualified totally. They are given the chance to improve and they can continue driving during that period provided that they abide by all the requirements of a learner driver.

I should explain that because the probationary period begins on the first occasion of passing a test, it is not affected by any subsequent tests passed. Therefore, a driver who passes a motor cycle test in January 1996 and a car test in July of the same year will remain probationary only until January 1998—that is two years after passing the motor cycle test, which is the first test to be passed.

The Bill will apply only to holders of British licences. However, British licences can be granted on the strength of certain driving tests taken outside Great Britain. The Bill needs to take them into account, too. Licences from Northern Ireland, the Channel Islands, the Isle of Man and Gibraltar are readily exchangeable for British licences if a resident of one of those places moves to the British mainland. All those administrations use similar driving tests to ours. Under Community law, licences from all other member states are also exchangeable if the holder moves to an address in Great Britain.

Three of them have joined the European Union, leaving only Norway, Iceland and Liechtenstein outside. Subsection (3) of the clause is a necessary technicality because Liechtenstein has not yet ratified the EEA agreement, though it intends to do so when it has made the necessary changes to its legislation. One does not often see cars from Liechtenstein on our roads, but we obviously need to be clear whether they are covered by the scope of the Bill

The point of all that is that drivers begin their probationary period on the day they pass their test, whether they took it in Great Britain, Northern Ireland, the offshore islands or in the various other European countries I have mentioned.

I should reply again to the point made by my hon. Friend the Member for Batley and Spen (Mrs. Peacock), because I may have misled her. Looking at my notes, I see that the probationary period starts from when a driver passes a test, whether it was taken in Great Britain, Northern Ireland, the offshore islands or in various other European countries I have mentioned. However, my hon. Friend specifically mentioned the Republic of Ireland and, therefore, my answer was right. A different set of Image Col. 5 circumstances would have applied had it been Northern Ireland.

Equally important is the fact that the probationary period will end on the second anniversary of passing the test.

One question that may spring to mind is how the police and courts will know the date on which a driver passed a driving test in a foreign country. The answer is that all the countries' licences, either now or by 1 July 1996, will conform to the Community model and will show the date of entitlement in a prominent place. That is true even of licences from the Channel Islands and the Isle of Man, although they are not part of the European Union. The EEA countries are also conforming to the model as part of their treaty obligations. When the holder of a licence from any of those countries exchanges it for a British licence, the date will be transferred to the British licence.

Ideally, I would have liked to extend the Bill to other countries with licence exchange agreements with Britain, for example Australia and New Zealand. Many of us would feel that people from those countries were rather more likely to drive on our roads than people from Iceland or Liechtenstein. But I was advised that that would be too ambitious because those other overseas countries do not have the same sorts of licence, and when the licence is exchanged for a British licence it would not be possible to record the date which would commence the probationary period.

It would be a little unfair to drivers from Australia and New Zealand if they were made to start a probationary period from the date they obtained a British licence, taking no account of the experience gained in their own countries. The only alternative was to leave them out altogether. The clause as it stands is therefore about as comprehensive as it reasonably could be.

I am a keen supporter of the Bill and I spoke on Second Reading. I should like to say a few words about the clause because it establishes the important new principle of probationary driver. It is an excellent idea and the other clauses are built on it. It brings into effect extra sanctions on probationary drivers for breaching road traffic laws.

One or two points about the clause have been put to me since the debate on Second Reading. One issue is whether a probationary period of two years is right. Some people say, "Why two years? That is an arbitrary figure." That is correct. It would be possible, without altering the principle or the effect of the rest of the Bill, to have a probationary period of one year, three years, or some other arbitrary figure.

I would defend the two-year period for probationary drivers. One year is too short. If one of the purposes of the probationary period is to establish experience, one year is too short. A longer period than two years, while it might have a practical effect in that it would further constrain drivers of limited experience, would be too onerous and would run the risk of making a mockery of the term "probationary period". For those reasons, despite misgivings that have been expressed to me, I think that two years is about right.

One misconception that has been created by the probationary period, which was evident even on Second Reading, is that the clause is aimed at young drivers. That is not the case. The probationary period will apply to drivers of any age. While much attention has focused on the problem created by some young drivers, the question is not simply one of age—provisions covering age could have been written into the Bill if it had been so desired—but of inexperience. We focus on young drivers because these days the majority of new drivers happen also to be young.

I can remember in the not too distant past—or perhaps it is rather distant—when I was a child, a large proportion of new drivers were quite old. I remember the headmaster of my school qualifying when he must have been 55 or 56. Within a few weeks of his qualification he bought himself a brand new Rover and within a few more weeks Image Col. 7 the car was dented in many places. It was a great joke in the school that every time the headmaster arrived in his car it was bashed somewhere else.

The point is that a driver who is newly qualified is, by definition, a much greater risk. That applies to elderly as well as young drivers. It is right that the clause should define a probationary period in terms of a period of time elapsing after qualification rather than being related to age.

Another point that has been raised with me by some of my constituents, but which I have heard before, is whether the probationary period should be marked by more than a definition. In other words, a driver in his probationary period should carry some form of mark on his vehicle. One of my constituents said that a probationary driver should carry a green "P" on the front and back of his car. That puzzled me at first and I wondered what she was talking about, but then I realised that she was talking about a P-plate. That would indicate clearly to everyone that that driver was in a probationary period. I wonder if my hon. Friend the Minister will refer to that, as it has been widely discussed.

The clause is significant in that it establishes a new class of drivers, who are fully qualified but who are not to be treated the same as other qualified drivers in law. The new category could be described as a halfway house. Therefore, the Bill establishes an important new principle. It is demonstrable from the accident figures that the chances of a driver's involvement in an accident in the proposed probationary period are far higher than those of a more experienced driver. That is the nub of the Bill: the theory on which it is based. The theory has been shown to be statistically accurate. A quarter of all fatal accidents occur when one of the drivers is newly qualified.

The rest of the Bill covers drivers with convictions. Forty-two per cent. of young drivers with convictions have been involved in accidents. That is a phenomenal statistical indicator of the risks.

Other reasons for a probationary period include experience. Good driving is a result of a combination of factors: techincal competence—the physical ability to control a car, experience, which is acquired only through time and familiarity with different circumstances in driving, and maturity. Maturity and experience are linked. Those three important factors underline the need for a probationary period as a yardstick by which other decisions can be made.
