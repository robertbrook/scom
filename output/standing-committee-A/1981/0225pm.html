Mr. Davidson
: The purpose of the amendment is to ensure that before magistrates specify requirements in a supervision order under clause 19(3), they not only consult supervisors as they are obliged to do but reach agreement with them as to what specific requirements are included.

The Committee will be almost over-familiar by now with the White Paper on Young Offenders and will know also that the White Paper proposed to give the courts specific power to order a juvenile offender, under supervision, to undertake a programme of specified activity which has been agreed between the supervising probation officer or social worker and the court as being suitable for that particular child or young person.

Amendment No. 43 will ensure that the Bill more accurately and closely reflects that intention. Two possible consequences, both of them unintended— certainly by the Government—could result if the agreement of the supervisor to the inclusion is not added to the Bill.

I fear that an atmosphere of mistrust is growing between the magistrates and supervisors and also that the limited community resources are not efficiently and sensibly used.

I accept that in most cases, consultation between the magistrates and the supervisor will be good in the proper sense of the word " consultation". The Minister will no doubt say, as he said when 288 replying earlier that it would be a foolish magistrate who would go against the wishes of the supervisor. However, it is possible that some magistrates may not indulge in proper consultation, but merely token consultations. Indeed, even if there were proper and fairly elaborate consultations, it is posr sible that the supervisor's recommendations could be disregarded. As the Minister well knows, consultation is a very vague word.

The Committee will understand that for a supervision order to be carried out effectively, the supervisor ought to be enthusiastically and energetically committed to the recommendations of that order.

My fear is—and certainly the fear of NACRO and one or two other bodies—that if the Bill is enacted without the necessity for the supervisor's consent, the supervisor might be faced with having to implement, carry out and enforce requirements of the order with which he does not agree, for which he has half-hearted enthusiasm and for which, ultimately, the local authority will have to take financial responsibility.

As a result, as I said, an atmosphere of distrust between magistrates and supervisors would develop, and magistrates' confidence and faith in the ability of social service departments and probation officer services to provide administrative supervision in the community for juvenile offenders, would be severely dented.

The second danger is that it might lead to inefficient use of community facilities and resources, as well as to a use of those resources that is not the most appropriate and helpful for the young offender, and in the long run for the wider community. The purpose of the Bill is to develop, as the Minister knows, a number of different community facilities for dealing with children in trouble. That is the purpose of the clause. These would range from programmes structured primarily as a true alternative to custody, which would be more appropriate and would be geared to the serious and persistent offenders. There would also be more relaxed programmes that would be more relevant, and specifically geared, to minor offenders. There is a danger that magistrates, if consultation is only to be required, might use those projects in which they have true faith—which are more suitable for those designed for one type of offender, namely, the more serious type of offence— for juveniles who would not otherwise be sent to custody. Magistrates might do this because they were not sufficiently aware, or they did not know about, other programmes and projects that would be more appropriate for dealing with a juvenile who has committed a minor offence.

In addition, if a juvenile who is sent on what I might call the stiffer regime re-offends, he might well automatically be sent to formal custodial treatment, which he might not have been sent to if he had offended against a less harsh programme more suited to a minor offender.

That is the purpose of both the amendments, but so that I do not have to raise the matter in the JPEG 289 "clause stand part" debate, let me refer to something that is a mystery to me? What if the supervisor is not known at the time of the making of the order? How does one consult with a supervisor if it is not known who it will be?

I imagine that this applies equally to the terms of the Bill and to the terms of my amendment, because the person who actually draws up the report in court is not necessarily—frequently he is not—the person who will carry out the supervision. In those circumstances I should be grateful if the Minister would clear up what seems to be, on the face of it, a serious anomaly in the Bill.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Patrick Mayhew)
: The hon. and learned Gentleman rightly spoke of it being common ground that a strengthened supervision order, pursuant to the provisions of this clause, should be made only after consultation with the officer, whether he is a probation officer or a social worker, who will be the supervising officer. It is common sense that that should be the case. The nub of amendment No. 43 is whether consultation is enough—'that is, the obligation to consult—or whether it should be a condition precedent of a supervision order under the clause that the supervising officer shall have agreed to the terms of the supervision order.

I did not express myself in the precise words attributed to me this morning by the hon. and learned Gentleman, at least I hope that I did not. He said that I had expressed the opinion that it would be a foolish magistrate who went against the wishes of the supervisor. I do not believe that I said that. What I said was that if there is a major difference of opinion between the supervisory officer and the magistrates when a supervision order is contemplated, it would be questionable whether a magistrates' court should proceed with a supervision order in those circumstances. Consultation is the important point. I am sure that we would be unwise to go further than the statutory obligation incorporated in the Bill to consult before making a supervision order.

The reason is that it is for the courts to decide in their discretion what sentence or order meets the justice of the case overall. Of course, we give directions and guidelines. We say, most notably, that in the case of a young offender they shall not make a custodial order unless satisfied that no noncustodial order would be appropriate, but to go as far as the amendment, which requires the consent of the supervising officer, would be to place the supervising officer over and above the courts. That would not be right.

We are well aware of the argument put forward by the local authority associations that this provision could create an area of local authority expenditure over which they have no control. If the court could include a requirement in a supervision order only with the consent of the supervisor, the supervising services could control their expenditure. To his credit, the hon. and learned Gentleman did not advance the expenditure argument in support of his amendment.

290 This would not be a realistic arrangement. We have already acknowledged that this provision could result in a financial burden on local authorities. That is why my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Social Services gave them an undertaking that they will be provided with additional resources to meet any additional expenditure arising from the implementation of the legislation.

The Bill already includes the provision that the courts should consult. That gives the supervisor ample opportunity to advise the court about the suitability of the requirements that the court proposes to make. It also allows the supervisor to explain to the court if the facilities involved are unduly expensive. The courts are likely in general to be grateful for advice of this kind and to act in accordance with it. They are not divorced from the areas which they serve. I do not think that the supervising services can ask for a great deal more.

The fears that underlie the amendment are exaggerated. The Government can establish that they have already recognised the concern of local authorities and made careful allowance for it, but to go as far as this amendment is to put the cart before the horse. We must insist that the magistrates or the higher courts should be the bodies that exercise judicial discretion to make use of such orders and disposals as Parliament thinks it right to make available. It must not be a condition precedent that they have the consent of this person or that, however dedicated or experienced in his own sphere. The point is that the spheres are limited and do not extend to the making of judicial decisions. The court in making a supervision order will need to decide on the supervisor—that is, the probation officer or social services worker—and will then be required to consult, possibly adjourning to do so. That is the answer to the point made by the hon. and learned Gentleman.

4.45 pm

Obviously, if there is an obligation to consult, one cannot consult until one has decided who the person is to be.

The second amendment would have the effect that the strengthened supervision order could be used only as an alternative to the three sentences specified in the amendment, that is,

" a detention centre order or a care order in respect of a child or young person or … a youth custody sentence."

I see entitrely what lies behind this and it is a perfectly proper consideration—to ensure as far as possible that the strengthened supervision order introduced by the clause is used as a serious alternative to a custodial sentence or a care order.

However, there are difficulties about legislating in this way. The amendment raises the same kind of difficulty as some of the earlier ones which sought to provide guidelines for the sentencing practice of the courts. There is some doubt whether it would be at all effective as there is no criterion against which it could be judged whether or not the courts would otherwise have passed a custodial sentence or made a JPEG 291 care order. It is not desirable to include in legislation provisions that do not add to the substantive effect of what one is saying.

The Government are making provision to strengthen the confidence of the courts in the supervision order, partly in the hope that the courts will then use it in some cases instead of a custodial sentence. The new supervision order has a proper function as a disposal in its own right and not solely as an alternative to custody.

The same may be said of each of the other sentences, particularly the care order. They cannot be seen as sentences that should be used only in the absence of an appropriate provision for supervision.

The difficulty may be put in this way. If, as the amendment proposes, the new supervision powers are to be used only where the courts would otherwise have imposed a care or a custodial sentence, why have those sentences at all? The point is that they each occupy a different position in the sentencing tariff and cannot be regarded as direct and exclusive alternatives to each other. They are not interchangeable.

The approach the Government have adopted is to strengthen non-custodial disposals and to make their range wider, and to try to put custodial sentences slightly further from the immediate reach of the courts. If I can express it in this way: we want to stretch the tariff rather than to compress it, which would be the effect of the amendment.

Clause 1 has been carefully framed so that any decision to impose a custodial sentence on a young offender should be unequivocal and inevitable. The court can impose such a sentence only when it is satisfied that no other method of dealing with the offender is appropriate. Other methods include a supervision order under the provisions of this clause.

The new supervision order could be very powerful—that is our intention—but it cannot be regarded as being as drastic or serious a measure as a custodial sentence. The supervision order is an important disposal in its own right, as well as being a measure that can be used instead of custody. We want custodial sentences to recede from the immediate focus of the courts and regain their true perspective as an ultimate sanction.

Many of us in the Committee agree that custodial sentences for young offenders have increased in frequency because there have been areas of the country where there was insufficient confidence in the adequacy of supervision orders on the part of the courts. On the other hand, this amendment would serve to blur the strong distinction which we wish to maintain between community-based disposals and custodial disposals. So, although I wholly understand the objective that lies behind the amendment—which has been well expressed by the hon. and learned Gentleman—I am not able to recommend that the Committee accepts it.

Mr. S. C. Silkin
(Dulwich)
: I listened with great care to what the Minister said. It seemed to me that he was not really addressing himself to the thrust of these two amendments. I put my name down 292 to the second one—amendment No. 44—because on the whole I prefer it to the first.

The Minister spoke in terms of fettering the power to make a supervision order. That is not what the amendments are concerned with, if I have correctly understood this rather complex clause. As I understand clause 19, there are two parts in it. The first part, in giving power to make a supervision order, gives the court power to require the supervised person to comply with whatever directions within a certain compass were made by the supervisor. It leaves the way in which the supervised person is dealt with very much within the discretion of the supervisor. Just as we would not want to fetter the powers of a court any more than is essential, so also once a supervision order has been made we would not want to fetter the supervisor's powers any more than is necessary. He will exercise them according to how his charge behaves, taking into account the circumstances of the particular case.

The clause then goes on to permit the court itself to make specific requirements. I suppose it was intended that the courts would exercise that power only in very rare cases, that is to say, in those cases where it takes the view that it is insufficient simply to give the powers to the supervisor and that it is necessary to add the court's own directions to those powers, overriding the general discretion of the supervisor.

It is to that second situation, as I understand it, that my hon. and learned Friend's amendments are directed. Therefore, they do not bite upon the general power to make a supervision order. Surely, in that second set of circumstances the court would include its own requirements in such rare cases, because in any normal case it would be wholly sufficient to leave the discretion within the hands of the supervisor.

That is why I favour amendment No. 44, which makes it quite clear that this second power—this much more stringent power which the court would exercise—will be used only in the rarest circumstances, where otherwise it would have felt itself compelled to have a custodial order against the offender. That seems to me to be a perfectly logical and reasonable fetter on the discretion of the court, just as the clause already imposes certain fetters on the power of the court to make requirements.

This additional fetter would in my view be highly desirable. It would make it quite clear that it is indeed in those circumstances that the court should impose requirements of this kind and that it ought normally to leave these matters to the supervisor.

It seems to me that unless something of that kind is written in to the Bill, the court will have no guidance as to the circumstances in which it would use the ordinary powers in subsection (2) and in which circumstances it should go to the extreme length of using its powers, including requirement of an order.

I hope that the Minister will address himself to that point, rather than to the general supervision order power, and that he will consider the matter again in that light.

Mr. Robert Kilroy-Silk
(Ormskirk)
: I welcome the proposals in the clause, which empower the courts to impose supervised activities requirements on juvenile offenders, as yet another important step forward in giving the courts greater confidence in using intermediate treatment schemes as a genuine alternative to a custodial sentence, particularly at the heavy end of the delinquent spectrum.

The Minister, of course, has constantly made references, though he has not yet produced the evidence, for the assertion that the magistracy in particular seems to be losing confidence in alternatives to custody. The clause, and the proposals in it, should help to restore that confidence, if the Minister is correct in his assertion that it has been eroded.

Indeed, the development of intermediate treatment schemes and alternatives to custody of this type should, I submit, be central to any policy for juveniles or young offenders, and should be a far more prominent part of the Bill than they are at present. Their aim, which presumably we all share, is to reduce the level of juvenile delinquency, and the level of offending. There is some evidence to suggest that intermediate treatment schemes can play an important and constructive part in reducing the level of offending among juveniles. It helps, for example, in that many of them deal with constructive acitivites, that help to develop social skills in what may be inarticulate, and somewhat deprived and often disadvantaged juvenile offenders.

They help with counselling, and bring them into better relationship with mature adults. In particular they try to ensure that parents also are involved in supervised intermediate treatment schemes, so that they also accept at least some sense of responsibility both for what their child has done and for the supervised activities in the intermediate treatment scheme.

All those seem very desirable objectives, which we all ought to support, and they are achieved at considerably less cost than the custodial alternatives. The cost varies from £100 or less per child per year in the less sophisticated schemes, up to as much as £2,000, or £10,000 per child per place in the more intensive intermediate treatment schemes.

Before Conservative Members are, however, frightened off, the whole concept of intermediate treatment by the £4,000 per place per year figure, it is worth reminding them, and the Committee, that it costs something like £10,000 per year per place in a local authority community home of education. The figure for borstal is £7,823, and a detention centre place, at the lower end, or minimum standard of the spectrum, costs as much as £6,505 per year.

In spite of that, and of what I have said, that intermediate treatment can be highly sophisticated, and clearly relevant to the individual juvenile's needs. In spite of the fact that it is considerably cheaper than the custodial alternatives, it is rarely used nationally as a direct alternative to custody. Part 296 of the problem with developing intermediate treatment is that perhaps it is not used as often as it should be as a direct alternative to a custodial placement. For that reason, the all-party Penal Affairs Group recommended in its report, " Young Offenders —A Strategy for the Future ", in paragraph 61, that

" there should be a range of intermediate treatment catering both for young offenders and those at risk. Within this range, greater efforts should be made to provide intermediate treatment for juveniles who would otherwise have been committed to custodial and residential establishments."

At present, when a court makes a supervision order, it can confer on the supervisor the power to direct the young person to attend an intermediate treatment facility. However, at present it is for the supervisor himself to decide whether, and to what extent, the individual will attend the intermediate treatment scheme, and to what extent the facilities may be used. The White Paper, " Young Offenders ", summarised the present position fairly in paragraph 49, when it said:

"Generally, except where there is very close liaison, the magistrates do not necessarily know what, if anything, will happen under the order. They might for that reason understandably hesitate to make a supervision order, particularly in the case of a serious or difficult offender ".

It is for that reason that the Magistrates Association has now been advocating for a considerable time that legislation should be introduced actually to direct that the child will have to undergo intermediate treatment, and to require that the supervisor ensures that that recommendation is carried out in practice.

In its report, the all-party Penal Affairs Group endorsed the recommendation of the Magistrates Association. In paragraph 67 we said:

"We recommend that, before imposing an intermediate treatment requirement, the court should satisfy itself that the programme envisaged by the social worker or probation officer is appropriate. We further recommend that intermediate treatment requirements should be mandatory and their implementation should not be left to the supervisor's discretion. However, discretion should be given to the supervising officer to modify the detailed content of the intermediate treatment programme in the light of changing circumstances."

The provisions of this clause are substantially in line with our recommendation and, of course, for that reason if for no other, I welcome them. I consider that the usefulness, however, of these provisions will be limited unless much greater financial commitment is made to local authorities, the probation services and the voluntary organisations, to develop intermediate treatment schemes. They can be developed. They can be used by the courts, only if that finance is made available. Perhaps I will have the opportunity, if I catch your eye, Mr. Dean, to develop that argument in the " clause stand part " debate on clause 20.

Incidentally, the hon. and learned Gentleman will acknowledge that while he and I and everyone else say that there ought to be a shift from custody to non-custodial alternatives in the community, and while we accept that intermediate treatment is, if not the most important, at least one of the most important alternatives, it is receiving only a derisory amount of financial support from the Government. I should now like briefy to give an example of a particularly intensive intermediate treatment scheme that has gained a great deal of confidence from magistrates in a certain area. I am sure the Minister knows about this. Having described that scheme I shall ask him certain questions about the extent to which the approach I want to describe, which is in line with clause 19, might also be applied throughout the country, and in particular to those of an older age group than the under-17s.

The scheme I have in mind is the Medway Close Support Unit for juvenile offenders, run by the Kent Probation and After-Care Service. I notice that the Minister acknowledged the fact that he is familiar with the scheme. It is financed by the Kent Social Services Department. The unit provides a form of intensive intermediate treatment for juvenile offenders who in every case—I emphasise the words " in every case "—would otherwise receive a custodial sentence.

That answers the point made during our first debate by the hon. Member for Nantwich (Sir Nicholas Bonsor), who made the valid point that we cannot always compare the recividist rate for custodial treatment with those for community service orders or other community-based facilities. He made the point that often the community-based facilities do not take the more delinquent offenders. Here we have an example where they do. In that context, the matter is important and relevant.

The children attend the unit for a 14-week period. On weekdays they go to school or work during the day, and if for some reason they are not attending school or work, they attend the probation service's day centre, which is located in the same building as the close support unit.

At the end of the school or working day, the evening is occupied with positive activities. It may be school homework, handicrafts or sports, either in the building or in supervised excursions outside. These activities in the evening continue until 8.30 p.m. when the young people go home and the unit staff make visits later in the evening to some of the children's homes to ensure that parents as well are properly and fully informed about what is happening, that they are involved in the work both of the unit and of the child and know full well of the children's progress. On Saturdays between 9 a.m. and 4 p.m. the children carry out work of value to the community or again involve themselves in supervised leisure pursuits.
