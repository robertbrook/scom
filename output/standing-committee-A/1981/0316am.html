New Clause 9
SENTENCING FOR MURDER

"In section 1(1) of the Murder (Abolition of Death Penalty) Act 1965, the word 'sentenced' shall be omitted and the word 'liable' shall be inserted".— [Mr. Kilroy-Silk.]

Brought up and read the First time.

Mr. Kilroy-Silk
(Ormskirk)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

This new clause would make life imprisonment the maximum sentence for murder, not the mandatory sentence for murder as it is at present. This, as the Committee will know, was a recommendation of the Butler committee on Abnormal Offenders in 1975. It was a recommendation of the Advisory Council on the Penal System in its 1978 Report. It was also a recommendation of half the members of the Criminal Law Revision Committee, which on that particular occasion was fairly evenly divided in 1980.

It is worth recalling when we are debating this subject that when the Bill abolishing capital punishment and substituting mandatory life sentences was debated in another place, Lord Parker proposed an amendment very similar to the one we are discussing here today. The clause that he then proposed gave judges complete and unfettered discretion to determine the sentence to be imposed upon murderers. He argued that giving judges that kind of discretion—total discretion—to determine the length of sentence that would be imposed would enable them to mark out the seriousness of any par- 560 ticular offence with the length of sentence imposed while at the same time being able to show mercy in appropriate cases.

The amendment that he then proposed was supported by all the Law Lords in the debate. It was actually carried in Committee, but unfortunately the whole thing was changed in what would appear to have been a fairly unsatisfactory and unhappy compromise on Report, when it was agreed that the life sentence should be mandatory, but that the judges would have the power to be able to make recommendatons as to the actual length of time an individual would serve in custody.

I accept that murder is an extremely important and grave offence. It must always be treated as a grave offence, but we also know that murders vary enormously one from another. There are those that are calculated, cold-blooded, premeditated and well-planned and which are properly to be despised and the offenders punished accordingly. But there are also those committed in what might be described as exceptional circumstances of great stress—perhaps great emotional turmoil.

They are quite different circumstances, and the individuals committing the murder are also themselves quite different and operating from totally distinct and separate motives. Unfortunately, at the moment judges do not have the ability to reflect in their sentences the varying degrees of culpability of the murderers committing the offence. The Butler committee pointed out that some manslaughters may be far more serious and culpable than some murders which may have mitigating circumstances. It quoted the words of Lord Kilbrandon in the case of Hyam, where he said:

"It is no longer true, if it ever was true, to say that murder, as we now defiine it, is necessarily the most heinous example of unlawful homicide."

Those members of the Criminal Law Revision Committee who supported this view in the committee's report "Offences Against the Person" published in 1980, pointed out that:

"Many of those convicted of murder are much less of a danger than persistently violent robbers."

In the case of a persistent armed robber, the trial judge has complete discretion in the sentence he chooses to pass. In the case of a killing however, he has no discretion unless diminished responsibility or provocation are proved. As the Criminal Law Revision Committee's Report went on to say in paragraph 54:

"there are, of course, some killings under stress not amounting to provocation, where in spite of the psychiatric evidence the defence of diminished responsibility fails. In such a case or in cases where an accessory to murder has played only a small part a life sentence is undeserved and unnecessary for deterrence."

The Advisory Council on the Penal System argued in very similiar terms in its report " Sentences of Imprisonment—A Review of Maximum Penalties " published in 1978. It pointed out in paragraph 244 that murder:

"is not a homogeneous offence, but a crime of considerable variety. Instead of automatically applying a single sentence to such an offence, we believe that sentences for murder should reflect this variety with correspondingly variable JPEG 561 terms of imprisonment or, in the exceptional case, even with a non-custodial penalty. This is primarily because we do not think that anyone should, without specific justification, be subject to the disadvantages which we see in indeterminate sentencing."

Mr. Kilroy-Silk
: I do agree with the quotation I have just read out. The mandatory custodial sentence for rape clause was withdrawn by me after having reconsidered it and taken advice from my colleague, but in any event there is a distinction to be drawn between on the one hand saying, as we do for many offences, that a sentence of imprisonment should be imposed and saying here that in all cases and all circumstances—however much they may vary, however much to blame the individual committing the offence may be—the sentence shall be life. One can even maintain support if one wished to continue for a mandatory prison sentence to be imposed for rape offenders and at the same time support new clause 9 which removes the mandatory element of life sentences for murder. I see no inconsistency in supporting the two.

The Advisory Council whose report I just quoted also argued that efforts to alleviate the harshness of the mandatory life sentence had led to very serious complications in legal proceedings for which it believed there could be no proper justification. It meant by this the special defences of provocation and diminished responsibility which can reduce a murder conviction to manslaughter.

The Butler committee went so far as to state that in order to enable a trial judge to show humanity in some cases, medical witnesses sometimes have to " stretch their conscience " in testifying as to whether one of the special defences applies.

Another serious disadvantage results from the use of life sentence indiscriminately in all cases of murder, whether they really call for it or not. It dilutes what has been referred to as the " awe-inspiring " nature of this sentence. As the Criminal Law Revision Committee put it in paragraph 55:

"As long as the life sentence is mandatory for all kinds of murder its imposition cannot mark the heinousness of any particular offence, nor can it appear to provide appropriate retribution."

It has been argued in support of the mandatory life sentence that a trial judge cannot be trusted to decide when it will be safe to let a defendant out. That argument is inconsistent with the fact that some mentally unstable defendants have their convictions reduced to manslaughter on the grounds of diminished responsibility and in these cases the trial judge does have a discretion. If judges can be trusted with discretion in respect of disordered killers who are often, by definition, of the most unpredictable kind, they can surely be trusted to sentence relatively normal people who may have killed in circumstances of great emotional stress and great psychological upheaval.

562 Again, it has been suggested that if the mandatory life sentence were to be abolished, as is suggested by new clause 9, some judges would pass inordinately long determinate sentences in an effort to ensure that prisoners would not be released too soon. But if the courts took up that option, if they passed sentences which were inordinate or severe, presumably, that is something that could, and would, be rectified by the Court of Appeal. Therefore, there does not seem to me to be any substantial argument against the introduction of new clause 9 and the ending of mandatory life sentences for murder. Indeed, there was no powerful argument when this was first considered when capital punishment was being abolished and this was being debated in another place.

I am not saying, and perhaps I should make this quite clear, that the offence of murder is not a serious and important offence because, clearly, it is serious and important and we have to regard it as such. I am not saying that in some cases life sentences are not appropriate because, clearly, they are appropriate in many cases of murder. Nor am I saying that in some cases a life sentence is inappropriate, because, again, clearly, a life sentence for some murderers, or a particular murder, is appropriate and may indeed be the only sentence that any sensible and reasonable person could impose at that particular time in those circumstances. I am saying that it is not necessary, nor is it desirable for all murderers, in all circumstances, at all times to be given a mandatory life sentence. I hope that the Minister and the Committee will agree with me.

There are, then, many safeguards as far as the convicted murderer is concerned, to ensure that there is a possibility of his being let out when the time is safe to do so, and when the public can feel satisfied that he is no longer a public danger. My hon. He argues—an argument I can understand—that in the case of somebody who has been found to be suffering from diminished responsibility, the judge by its very nature is dealing with somebody who is suffering from a mental disorder.

There is, however, a great difference between the judge's deciding in the case of somebody suffering from a mental disorder and thereby found to be not fully responsible for his actions at the time of committing the offence, and somebody whom the jury has found to have murdered in the full knowledge of what he is doing, and where the intent is fully there. In such a case a difficulty is presented to the judge of deciding when that person is likely, as far as the public are concerned, to be no longer a danger—in other words, no longer to present the likelihood of killing again. The two situations are very different.

It is true that in other cases such as rape, robbery and grievous bodily harm with intent, life imprisonment is a possible penalty. In fact, it is very seldom imposed in these cases. Usually the penalty is six or seven years. That is the average sentence for crimes of that type. It would be rightly regarded by the public as completely inadequate in the case of murder. So I do not think that one can compare the two and say that because the judges can use their discretion in the case of rape or grievous bodily harm, they can similarly use their discretion in the case of murder. I do not think the public would take kindly to that.

My hon. Friend said that if one of the arguments against getting rid of the mandatory sentences was that judges would be tempted to pass very heavy sentences, such as 30 or 40 years, that would be all right because the Court of Appeal could deal with that by reducing the sentence if necessary. That is true, but what the Court of Appeal cannot do—and this has been proved to be a matter of great concern to the public in the recent rape case—is to increase a sentence if it thinks that the sentence is inadequate.

In a case of murder where a low sentence has been passed because a judge has used his discretion, and perhaps used it wrongly—as everybody will agree 564 the judge in the now notorious rape case did—there would be a public outcry. There would be demands that the law should be changed to allow the Court of Appeal to have the power to increase the sentence. That is a separate argument. Some members of the Committee may feel that that should be done, but it would not be done by passing the amendment. The law would have to be amended in many other respects as well. I cannot see how the discretion of the judge to award a low sentence if he felt justified in doing so would not in some circumstances lead to some public outcry and some public outburst of rage and anger.

I am, of course, aware of the arguments of the Butler committee, the Advisory committee, and of those on the Criminal Law Revision Committee who argued for a change, but it seems to me that on balance the present system give enough flexibility To my mind the public would not understand how any offence of murder did not merit life imprisonment. That seems to me to be axiomatic and I do not think it would be right to change it.

Anyone reading the Criminal Law Revision Committee's report—and I recommend that it should be read—will see clearly from the tables at the back that contrary to popular belief, most prisoners who are serving life sentences serve for a very long time indeed—10, 15 or 20 years in some cases. They certainly serve for far longer than the public are frequently led to believe is the case. There is a belief that nine years is about par for the course, but the tables give the lie to that, and I recommend that the Committee should study them carefully.

A man had been sentenced by Lord Salmon to life imprisonment following a trial for murder, a trial which took place over a day-and-a-half. By that time everybody—the local review committees, the prison authorities, the psychiatrist^—who had anything to do with this man said that he ought to be released because by that time it was perfectly plain that he would not be a danger to the public and that he might very well deteriorate if he remained in prison any longer.

This new clause would reverse the decision of the Divisional Court in 1980 in what has become known as the Nottingham Justices case. This was a test 576 case brought by a group of Nottingham solicitors who wished to challenge a policy decision made by the Nottingham magistrates that, on or after a defendant's third successive application for bail, they would refuse to hear arguments in support of a bail application unless there were new circumstances since the last remand hearing. The Divisional Court held in that case that the practice of the Nottingham magistrates was correct.

As members of the Committee will be aware, the Bail Act 1976 provides that a defendant should be granted bail unless one of the exceptions in schedule 1 of the Act applies—namely, that if granted bail he is likely either to abscond, commit offences, interfere with witnesses, or otherwise in some way obstruct the course of justice. In the Nottingham Justices case, Lord Justice Donaldson said:

"The court will have been under an obligation to grant bail unless it was satisfied that a Schedule 1 exception was made out … on the next occasion when bail is considered the court should treat as an essential fact, that at the time when the matter of bail was last considered, Schedule 1 circumstances did indeed exist. Strictly speaking, they can and should only investigate whether that situation has changed since then."

In a recent article in the Criminal Law Review 1981, Mrs. Mary Hayes, a law lecturer at Sheffield University—herself a magistrate—pointed out that the decision of a court in a remand hearing is far from being a straightforward finding of fact. She said, on page 22:

"The court does not make a finding about what the accused has done, it makes a prediction about what the accused may do—that is, he will fail to surrender to custody, or will commit an offence while on bail or will interfere with witnesses … These grounds embrace objective facts and a subjective evaluation of the facts."

As we know, bail decisions are a subjective and evaluative exercise. They inevitably involve an element of informed guesswork as to whether the defendant is likely to turn up in court or whether he is likely to commit a further offence or whether he is likely to obstruct the course of justice, and so on. One set of magistrates is just as likely to reach a different decision than three of their colleagues sitting on a case in similar circumstances.

However, the effect of the Nottingham Justices decision is that some defendants who might have been granted bail on a third or subsequent appearance cannot now be considered for bail and that, I submit, is contrary to Parliament's intention in providing for remand hearings to take place every eight days, presumably on the assumption that circumstances can change, that these circumstances should be taken into account and that the opportunity should exist for a defendant to be able to make an application on successive separate occasions for an end of the remand in custody and, therefore, for bail.

: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The new clause reduces the maximum periods of imprisonment which the Crown Court can impose for fine default to the same levels as those which a magistrates' court can impose for failing to pay a fine of a similar amount.

In 1977 the Criminal Law Act reduced the maximum periods of imprisonment which magistrates can impose for fine default. Section 59 laid down a 586 scale of maxima which was later consolidated in schedule 4 of the Magistrates' Courts Act 1980. According to this scale, magistrates' courts may impose up to seven days' imprisonment for failing to pay a fine of up to £25: and up to 14 days' imprisonment for failing to pay a fine of between £25 and £50. There is then a graduated scale up to a maximum of 12 months' imprisonment for failing to pay a fine exceeding £5,000.

The effects of this reduction in the maxima in the Criminal Law Act were striking. It resulted, first in a marked reduction in the average length of sentence served by fine defaulters. For example, in the two years up to September 1977, when the new scale of maxima came into force, only about 40 per cent, of those received into prison for fine default had been sentenced to terms of one month or less; but ever since then the proportion of fine defaulters serving one month or less has been consistently over 70 per cent, of those received into prison. In consequence, although the number of fine defaulters received into prison has continued to rise, the average daily number in prison has fallen. For instance, on 30 June 1976 the number was 1,123, and on 30 June 1977 it was 1,069. This fell to 826 on 30 June 1978, 889 on the same date in 1979 and 892 on 30 June 1980. This shows a striking and significant reduction in the daily average prison population of fine defaulters.

However, the maxima laid down in the Criminal Law Act, and now contained in the Magistrates' Courts Act, do not apply to fines imposed by the Crown court. Although fines imposed by the Crown court are enforced by magistrates' courts, the period of imprisonment for default is fixed by the Crown court at the time it imposes the fine. The only statutory restriction on the length of such periods is an overall maximum of 12 months. According to the NACRO working party on fine default, chaired by Lady Howe, in a report published last year

"In practice, the size of fines and default terms imposed by the Crown Court often far exceed the maximum specified for magistrates' courts' fines."

Clearly, that is both illogical and unjust, There is no reason why someone who has failed to pay a fine of, say, £100 imposed by a Crown court should be liable to anything up to 12 months' imprisonment, when if he had been fined precisely the same amount by a magistrates' court he could not be imprisoned for more than 30 days. This results in defaulters fined by Crown courts serving unnecessarily long terms in prison, with all the resultant waste of the prison service's resources which that involves, as well as the injustice to the individual.

The new clause would remedy that source of injustice and would make a modest but, I submit, a useful contribution to reducing the average daily population of local prisons. In 1980, there were 1,137 receptions into penal establishments of prisoners held under the Immigration Act 1971. These prisoners fell into several different categories. Some were awaiting the making or carrying out of a deportation order, either after the court had recommended that they be deported or after the Home Secretary had decided to use his own powers to make a deportation order against the person who has either overstayed or committed an offence without the court recommending deportation. There are also those awaiting removal, not deportation, because they are alleged to have entered illegally into this country in the first place. And, again, there is a small number of people who have been refused entry and are also held in prison awaiting removal from this country.

The average length of time which these Immigration Act prisoners spend in custody is a little over 50 days, but in some cases the periods for which they are detained are considerably longer. The latest breakdown that I have been able to obtain is for prisoners discharged in 1978. In its report, "The Reduction of Pressure on the Prison System", published in 1978, the Select Committee on Expenditure pointed out that in most cases where a court recommends that a deportation order should be made on an offender, the sentence itself is a non-custodial one. It said:

"The result however is the detention of the person for weeks, maybe months, awaiting the Home Secretary's decision whether to make a deportation order. We think it right that courts should be asked to consider more carefully than they sometimes do whether or not it is necessary to make a recommendation for deportation. The courts should be JPEG 589 reminded that in less than half cases where a court has recommended deportation in recent years has a deportation order actually been made."

That is from paragraph 101.

When a court recommends someone for deportation, unless it specifically states that he should not be detained in custody he is automatically sent to prison for an indeterminate period while the Home Secretary makes up his mind on the question of deportation. In the case of defendants accused of ordinary criminal offences, Parliament has gone out of its way in the Bail Act, which has been referred to on previous clauses, to enact a statutory presumption in favour of bail; but, in the Immigration Act cases, Parliament has gone the other way by stipulating that the accused will be kept in custody pending the Home Secretary's decision about deportation, unless the court otherwise directs. The presumption therefore is entirely contrary to that entailed within the Bail Act.

That is from paragraph 108.
