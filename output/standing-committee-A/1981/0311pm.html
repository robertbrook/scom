Mr. Dubs: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

I make it emphatically clear at the outset that the new clause does not seek to legalise the possession of cannabis. What it would do is remove the penalty of imprisonment on summary conviction for the simple possession of small amounts of cannabis for personal use. It would not affect the existing penalties for the separate offence of the possession of cannabis with intent to supply other people with the drug. It is merely a measure affecting users of cannabis, and would not affect in any way the pushers of cannabis. It is based upon a recommendation made in a report " Too Many Prisoners " produced by the parliamentary all-party Penal Affairs Group.

In 1979, 329 people in the United Kingdom were sentenced to immediate imprisonment for the simple possession of cannabis. I know that over the years the courts have become more liberal and that the proportion of those found guilty of possesion of cannabis receiving custodial sentences has been declining. In The Sunday Times of 1 February 1976 there was an article by a Mr. Peter Watson, who detailed the findings of an unpublished survey by members of the Home Office research unit of the views of magistrates on the relative seriousness of different offences.

Image Col. 547 According to that survey, magistrates did not regard the smoking of cannabis as a very serious offence. They thought that it was roughly equivalent to creating a disturbance or stealing small amounts. They regarded it as considerably less serious than driving a car while disqualified. That was in 1976, and if anything in the intervening years one would expect that the views of the magistrates would have become more liberal on this issue, but unfortunately views up and down the country are not uniform. If one were to come up in court for the possession of a small amount of cannabis one might be likely to get off more lightly in London than in other parts of the country. The practice of the courts is uneven with regard to the sentences they impose for the possession of this drug.

However, the parliamentary all-party Penal Affairs Group was emphatic in its view that one could not justify a custodial sentence for the simple possession of cannabis. That group felt that in a society where most adults drink alcohol, or smoke tobacco, it would be wrong to imprison people for the use of a drug which appears from the evidence that we have available to be no more dangerous than tobacco or alcohol, and possibly it might even be less dangerous.

There is a counter-argument that the existence of certain socially-approved evils is no reason to create yet another one, and I do not disagree with that proposition. The only purpose of the new clause is to prevent the imprisonment of cannabis users, not their conviction or their being fined for having this drug.

A number of bodies and organisations have come out in support of the view that cannabis possession should not be an imprisonable offence. The Advisory Council on the Misuse of Drugs had much to say about this in a report which formed part of the council's " Review of the Classification of Controlled Drugs and of Penalties ", published in 1979.

Paragraphs 3.14 and 3.15 stated that:

"there was no compelling evidence that occasional moderate use of cannabis was likely to have detrimental physical effects on individual users . . . The available reports on the effects on the health of the individual of what is deemed to be long continued (a matter of years) heavy use (daily consumption of several ' joints ') of cannabis contain no evidence of consequential harmful effects on physical health and contradictory reports on the mental state and the intellectual capacities of the subjects examined. The sub-committee concluded that a casual relation between chronic use of cannabis and mental impairment was by no means proven."

Where the use of cannabis causes the most apprehension is in its effect on impairing the ability to drive a motor vehicle. Sadly, there are no tests to examine motorists on whether they are under the influence of cannabis comparable with those on whether motorists are under the influence of alcohol. So there is a danger that the widespread increase in the use of cannabis and its legalisation would have a damaging effect in that it would be liable to lead to an increase in the number of motor accidents, which I should not wish to happen.

However, I do not believe that one can simply say that that would follow from making possession Col. 548 no longer an offence attracting a custodial penalty, which is the purpose of the new clause. It is not appropriate to go into all the other arguments about cannabis. I do not believe that cannabis should be totally decriminalised, but there is a reputable body of evidence to suggest that it is not appropriate to have imprisonment as a punishment for the possession of small amounts of cannabis. I therefore hope that the Committee will adopt the new clause.
