Criminal Justice Bill

Clause 20
PROVISION OF SUPERVISION FACILITIES

10.30 am

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Alexander W. Lyon
(York)
: Clause 20 is simply a revision of the statutory arrangements whereby the local authority funds intermediate treatment. I hope that the Chair will not regard it as being out of order if we use the opportunity to urge a rather wider use of intermediate treatment to encourage local authorities to be more generous and the Government to be more generous to the local authorities in relation to intermediate treatment.

On the latest figures that I have—they may be corrected by the Minister—in 1978, when the last count of intermediate treatment was carried out, about 20,600 young people were the subject of supervision orders and about 12,500 of that total were undergoing intermediate treatment. As the Minister indicated, for last year for the whole of England and Wales about £6¼ million was spent on roughly 12,000 children. The number of children in borstal is about 6,500 and that cost the taxpayer something approaching £50 million.

That is a fair estimate of the balance between dealing with youthful offending in a closed security and dealing with youthful offending in circumstances where the child can live at home and can still maintain his links with his local background but might be encouraged to adopt a different attitude towards his life and crime.

Although I do not know the recidivist figures for intermediate treatment, I suspect that they are a good deal better than for borstals. It therefore raises in a very simple way the issue that is now becoming live in our society. It is this on which I want to dwell for a few minutes.

In order to illustrate it, let me read from a letter of some length that came to me last week from a teacher in borstal who gives his name and address —which I do not intend to repeat but which I intend to send to the Minister. It states:

" I have been a teacher in a closed borstal for almost four years. Previous to this I was a voluntary youth leader for some seven years working with delinquent and often disturbed young persons. It would be hard for anyone to accuse me of being an idealist with that type of background. In the borstal that I teach in I am aware that many of the trainees of the establishment, together 310 with others in similar institutions, will form the bulk of the prison population of the future. Yet the majority of them are a long way from being bad or wicked. Of the youngsters that I come into contact with, within the borstal, some 75% should not be in custody at all. The other 25%, I submit, are nasty vicious thugs who must be detained for the good and protection of society. I will deal with this group first.


"These thugs will never learn no matter what punishment is meted out to them and so calls for corporal punishment are of little use. Imprisonment certainly means nothing to them. Yet it is from this 25% that individuals emerge who do, in no small measure, control the lives of all those who are confined in, or work in, penal institutions. Most governors will confirm that. These are the bully boys who offer protection in return for goods or favours and the tobacco barons … My concern is not with these but with that vast majority of youngsters who can so easily be led or misused by these thugs and learn from them that it pays to be tough or ' hard' as they would have it."

The letter goes on to instance a number of these children. It says:

"Paul was a youngster of 15 when he first came on to one of my classes. He had been in care from a very early age. He had passed from one custodial sentence which in many cases ' care' is, to another. Along the way he had been to what he called approved school, detention centre, remand centre, prisons. There can be little doubt that Paul is going to spend most of his life in penal institutions. I have watched him change from a carefree young person into someone who blindly imitates the mindless thugs. He has made himself become hard. I did all I could to help Paul, for I could plainly see that he was allowing himself to be trapped, but what I could do was not enough …


"Gary is a young person who is on my classes at the moment. He is full of zest and fun and any normal parents would be proud to call him their son. Yet Gary's parents have not visited him for the last three years. They do not want to see him nor do they want him when he leaves the borstal in a few weeks time. They will not even write him a letter. From what he has told me Gary's father simply wanted his son as an unpaid servant, and when Gary rebelled he was thrown out. Gary has nowhere to go when he leaves the borstal. He will be offered hostel accommodation. But most young offenders see an hostel merely as an extension of their sentence. However, Gary may have no choice. He is homeless. He has no means of supporting himself. He will, probably, be friendless in a strange area and most borstal boys are very ethnocentric. He will, almost certainly, offend again within a short time of being released. I have told him that I will meet him when he is released and I will go with him to look for employment. He has all but told me that it would be a waste of time.


"Jimmy was a youngster of brilliant intellect. His I.Q. was certainly much, much higher than my own. Yet life in an institution is, for him, more preferable to life outside of one. How, in the name of all that is decent and civilised, can a society justify this kind of situation. Jimmy had been sold short all his life and he knew it. I was able to form a very good relationship with him during his time on my class. However, when he left the borstal, because of distance, I lost touch with him. I learned that within weeks of leaving the borstal he was arrested and sentenced to three years imprisonment Yet Jimmy would have been far more suited to a university campus than to a prison. He had rejected, so he said, society completely. Several times I asked him why and his answer was always the same: ' No one cares about me or the way that I feel; therefore I have no concern for anyone else.' I was never able to accept that answer when he gave it. I do now."

This letter is typical of the wide experience of anyone who has had to deal with juvenile offenders. It is deplorable to go on arguing that we are JPEG 311 dealing with a rising crime wave by serious offenders who have to be dealt with by custodial sentencing, and that that is the only way to be tough about crime in our society.

I make this appeal partly in relation to the new clause, because I think it would be better if we spent more of that £50 million on widening intermediate treatment in order to keep more children out of custody, even if we are going over from borstal to youth custody. I also make it partly— and this debate is particularly opportune—because a real movement is taking place in the higher realms of the police force to counter the trend that has developed over the last few months to try to establish shorter sentencing and more effective means of policing that did not call for the heavy hand which has been demonstrated in the past. It is perfectly clear from the recent statement of the Chief Constable of Kent, as the chairman of the Association of Chief Police Officers, and from a number of other leaks that have been made recently, that the police are fighting back of their own volition against the policy which has been adumbrated by the Home Secretary arguing for shorter sentencing and for a different kind of policing operation.

If that is allowed to continue—and I ought to say that they are financed by public money in their public relations exercise—an alternative political philosophy will be put across by police officers who are accountable to no one. The real problem at the moment in relation to policing is that police have become divorced from the community. We desperately need to get them back under the control of the community and to reassert the power of police authority.

In so far as there is any control at the moment, it comes from the Home Secretary. If the Home Secretary is being attacked at a moment when he is vulnerable from his own Back Benches, by police officers who are only paid officials and who are not representatives of penal policy, that would be a serious setback to the campaign behind which the Home Secretary has put his weight, and to which the Minister has referred on a number of occasions in the Committee.

The reason I make this point is that I reject completely the police suggestion that shorter sentencing, or more community policing, are soft options which are taken simply in order to save money at a time of reduced national resources. That is totally untrue. Shorter sentencing and more effective community policing are better ways of doing the job that the community wants—of dealing with crime effectively in order to reduce the incidence of crime.

If it were possible to take the money from borstal, or now from youth custody, and to put a greater amount into intermediate treatment, children could be prevented from committing crimes in a more effective way than they will be by simply enlarging the scope of youth custody. If there was more community policing of the kind that took place in Handsworth, that would not mean that serious crime would increase. What happened in Handsworth was 312 that mugging was reduced by half. Where serious community policing has taken place that has always been the experience. What the police—and the community at large, particularly the Daily Express and the Sunday Express—must get into their heads is that we are urging not softer but more effective, treatment of criminals so as to reduce the incidence of crime. That must be said perfectly clearly and loudly.

I hope that the Minister will say it perfectly clearly and loudly, because at the moment I detect that the police are coming back against Scarman, against the incidents of last year and against the appeals for shorter sentencing, in order to dictate the policy of treatment of the offender which has been used in the past and which has proved ineffective. We are now arguing for a more effective way of dealing with crime than we have had in the past.

I do not think that is simply a matter of saving money, but I think we could actually save some money if we were to operate more effectively. Therefore, I appeal to the Minister, not only that he should reject the suggestions that have come from Mr. Pain but also that he should look at the amount of money allocated to intermediate treatment as against borstal training. If we could make intermediate treatment a real option for the courts, to be used much more widely, and if we could give local authorities enough money to provide effective methods of intermediate treatment, I am sure that this would prove to be a real advance in dealing with juvenile delinquency.

(Ormskirk)

Indeed, in 1981 the total expenditure on intermediate treatment by local authorities was no more than £7 million, and yet, at the same time, approximately £85 million was spent on community homes with education and on local authority observation and assessment centres. That clearly demonstrates the present lopsided priority we have in this country and that we have had ever since and before the 1969 Children and Young Persons Act. Despite all the rhetoric of a variety of organisations, institutions, individuals and Governments that we should shift the emphasis away from custodial care and custodial sentences to treatment in the community, here we are JPEG 313 with £7 million spent on intermediate treatment compared to £85 million on local authority CHEs and observation and assessment centres.

There is considerable cause for concern, first of all, about the inconsistency of intermediate treatment provision from one local authority area to another, the different types, the different quality, the different intensities of intermediate treatment schemes and, more particularly, the actual lack of intermediate treatment schemes at all in some local authority areas. Indeed, very few local authority areas have a complete intermediate treatment facility available. Ten per cent, of local authorities last year spent 38 per cent, of the total amount expended on intermediate treatment. A further 10 per cent, of local authorities spent less than £5,000 in the entire year on intermediate treatment, that is less than it cost per place per year per child to hold them in a residential institution. Again, that demonstrates our lopsided sense of priorities that 10 per cent, of local authorities can spend less on a highly successful venture such as intermediate treatment than they actually spend on keeping one child in a residential institution during that year.

For that reason, I support the good intentions that lie behind clauses 19 and 20. As I pointed out to the Minister on a previous occasion, the good intentions that he and the Government apparently have, which are demonstrated by some of the provisions in the Bill, will not be realised in practice unless he is both willing and able to give more substantial amounts of money to local authorities and to voluntary organisations to provide intermediate treatment schemes than he has been prepared to provide in the past.

I know that the Government will now enable the probation service to finance their own schemes. That is welcome. As we know, some local authority areas have been reluctant to provide, or have not provided, intermediate treatment. Now that the probation service can provide it itself, perhaps the Government ought to pick up the Bill in that particular context.

I know also that the Government have increased the amount of money that they have given to the Rayner foundation, intermediate treatment funds and, again, that they are giving local authorities additional finance. We have been told—this reinforces the point that my hon. Friend the Member for York (Mr. Lyon) was just making—that the total cost of this Bill will be about £8 million. The Minister said that in a parliamentary answer. We also know that the cost of one single measure— that is, the residential care order—is £5 million. That, too, the Minister has acknowledged in an answer to a parliamentary question.

We are, then, left with £3 million to cover all the additional expenses that will be incurred as a result of implementing the various provisions of the Bill. How much of that will actually go to local authorities for intermediate treatment? The Minister is making a big song and dance about its importance. So has the Home Secretary, the Secretary of State for Social Services and their various underlings throughout the two years that they have been in office. 314 They have been saying the right things and I congratulate them on saying it. Here, however, they have their first opportunity to put their money where their mouths have been, yet we know that at the very maximum no more than £3 million will be made available in the Bill for all the services.

As you know, Mr. Dean, amendment No. 46 was not selected. I understand the very good reasons why it was not, and in no way dispute that decision. The amendment would have enabled the Government to have made grants to local authorities specifically for, and tied to, the provision of intermediate treatment. I know that all Governments are reluctant to accept that principle, but the Government have made it clear that law and order services are paramount, and have gone further still and said that the children's services are regarded as an integral part of law and order services. The then Secretary of State for Social Services, the right hon. Member for Wanstead and Woodford (Mr. Patrick Jenkin), speaking in Sheffield on 9th July 1979 said:

"The Government is prepared to regard child care services as an integral part of the national pattern of law and order services, and to have the priority which that accords it."

Fine words. There we have it. Child care services, of which intermediate treatment is presumably an integral part, have a high priority. They are regarded as

"part of the national pattern of law and order services, and … have the priority which that accords it."

The only trouble is that

"to have the priority which that accords it"

suggests a very low priority, on the evidence of the money available here.

There are precedents for grants being made for specific services. We have it with the secure units in the community home system under the Children's Act 1975, where substantial amounts of money have been given to local authorities, tied to the provision of secure units. Again, section 2 of the Local Government Act 1966, provides for specific grants for the programme for ethnic minorities. There are also many other precedents where the Government have regarded a particular service as having a high national priority; where there is a clearly determined and articulated national policy; where it is essential that local authorities and other organisations be enabled to comply with that policy and that priority; and are enabled to do so by grants tied to the provisions of that specific service.

The Government should now do the same for intermediate treatment. If they do not provide sufficient finance, local authorities will not provide the intermediate treatment, and if they do not tie it to intermediate treatment, they will certainly not provide the schemes that the Government accept are both advisable and necessary. By the way, there will still certainly be an enormous variation both in the provision and quality of the schemes provided. The result will be what we all, apparently, wish to avoid. Unless more, better and more effective intermediate treatment schemes are provided by JPEG 315 local authorities, in which the courts can have confidence—the Minister keeps saying that the courts must have confidence in community-based schemes, supervised activities, and the intermediate treatment schemes—we shall need more of them, of higher quality and better funded than they are at present.

If that does not happen, more young people will be shovelled into penal institutions. We all, presumably, want to avoid that. Perhaps the Minister will now indicate that he will make a reality of some of the fine words that he has uttered, and which exist in the Bill.

In criminal proceedings, however, which is our concern at the moment, the intention of the court making the care order is normally that the child should be removed from its home and unless that order is discharged by the court it will continue until the child reaches the age of 18. Indeed, the average length of time for which care orders are made in criminal proceedings lasts for something like three years. In recent years, there has been a fall in the number of care orders made for juvenile offenders and a resultant drop in the number of care orders. On 31 March 1979 6,700 young offenders were resident in local authority community homes while, in relative terms, only 1,500 juveniles were in borstals and detention centres. That goes against the growing volume of research evidence, provided not least by some of the major reports produced by the DHSS, that most young offenders who are remitted to residential establishments under care orders are inappropriate subjects for such orders. Much of the evidence has been collected by the Centre of Youth Crime and Community at Lancaster university. In a series of recent studies in different local authority areas, the centre has analysed care orders made on young offenders and measured them against a set of criteria designed to establish whether there was a genuine need for the orders.

The criteria were reasonable. Research carried out by the DHSS shows also that the majority of care orders made in criminal proceedings are for young people who have been convicted of petty property offences, few of them with previous convictions.
