Mr. Woolmer
: The Committee will readily appreciate that the Opposition amendments are essentially probing ones to provide for a discussion on a number of matters. As the Opposition made clear on Second Reading, we have no objection to the Bill in principle. The purpose of our amendments is to examine a number of aspects of the proposals and what lies behind them. The Under-Secretary of State, replying to the Second Reading debate, shared 4 in the frankness for which his right hon. Friend the Secretary of State is so well known in the House when he said:

"The main purpose of the Bill is simply and straightforwardly to increase the borrowing limits of British Airways and the British Airports Authority".— [Official Report, 16 November 1981; vol. 12, c. 83.]

I look forward to that frankness and simplicity being shared by many of his right hon. and hon. Friends when they introduce Bills to increase the borrowing limits of public corporations and nationalised industries. The Bill shows that on occasions even the present Government are able to recognise that increased borrowing limits of public corporations and nationalised industries are not to be sneered at or derided but are firmly based on reasonable needs. It contrasts with the need of his right hon. Friend to chastise one of his hon. Friends who saw fit to be more critical of these public corporations.

My first inquiry is to ask why the increase is required. Hon. Members will appreciate that at the time of the Second Reading I was not aware of the close interest that I should be taking in these matters. My knowledge is therefore gleaned from reading the Second Reading debate. It did not become so clear, as I think might have been the case, why the borrowing limits were being increased. The only detail mentioned was the expenditure of £10 million at a Scottish airport. Other large sums of money appeared to have slipped into broad headings.

The amount of the investment programme over five or six years is about £700 million, a certain amount of which is to be financed by the increased borrowing limits. It is remarkable, reading the annual report of the British Airports Authority, to discover how much of that sum has been taken up by a limited number of major proposals and how some of that spending depends on decisions yet to be firmly taken.

I note that Ministers were at pains to explain to the House that the borrowing limits for which approval was sought did not themselves imply a firm decision to do certain things. One decision that will have to be taken in due course is that relating to Stanstead. The increased borrowing limit that we are asked to approve amounts to about £175 million in total, although I appreciate that the second tranche will depend upon an affirmative order of the House. That seems to amount to about 25 per cent, of the investment programme. I should like to know if the 25 per cent.: 75 per cent, ratio of borrowing limit expansion in aggregate and the size of the investment programme is coincidental. A substantial argument is taking place in various places about the implications of the need for a very large amount of self-financing. I shall touch upon those matters during my remarks.

The only other reference I have seen relating to this issue is a statement by Mr. Payne that he, as a matter of practice, was aiming at a 70 to 75 per cent, internally generated financing programme. I wonder why that sort of proportion is the aim. The implication of the need to find 70 to 75 per cent.

JPEG 5 internally is that charges have to be raised to provide finance. I suspect that many private industrial companies would not aim at 70 to 75 per cent, minimum self-financing. Those are the kinds of figures that the British Airports Authority has been practising so far.

A number of issues arise. My right hon. Friend the Member for Lanarkshire, North (Mr. Smith) pointed out on Second Reading that yet again the present generation of consumers was paying for use, by future generations. An even wider issue that arises is the reason why current profits of the Brtsh Airports Authority are not used to pay some kind of dividend to the tax-paying shareholders. As the Under-Secretary of State will recall this was developed at some length in paragraphs 10 to 18 of the Public Accounts Committee's report last year.

The Public Accounts Committee suggested to the Department that the financial structure and the way in which the business conducted itself should take into account the need to pay the taxpayer or the shareholders—that is, us—some of the money back from time to time. That argument was resisted by the Department and to some extent, remarkably, by the Treasury. I often wonder whether Governments, always view nationalised industries and public corporations with that in mind. If the intention is to do anything more than break even, in line with the practice of private commercial companies, part of the present profits should come back to the taxpayer. It would mean that a larger part of the current financing of the investment programme should justifiably be financed by borrowing. We know that that argument does not always make sense, but it is the kind of practice that I suspect the British Airports Authority would have to follow if it were privatised.

One of the problems that we face in discussing the pricing policy of the British Airports Authority is the existence at present of the litigation that is proceeding. That will make the analysis of arguments difficult. However, it would be most unfortunate if we were unable to explore the matter considerably further than we did on Second Reading. At the start of that debate, the Secretary of State appeared to offer more hope in that respect than did the Under-Secretary when he replied. It would be unfortunate if the House and the Com-mitttee were asked to approve a substantial increase in borrowing limits, yet be unable to ask the questions and get the answer on issues about which law suits are pending. The Opposition have no wish to hold up the Bill or prevent the necessary financing, but at the same time we insist on asking the questions that we feel need to be asked, and we hope that the Under-Secretary will be more forthcoming.

I refer, first, to the 6 per cent, rate of return on a current cost accounting basis. In the circumstances of the present recession, that is a significant and substantial rate of return. Indeed, the vast bulk of British industry would be delighted to have that rate of return. Does the Under-Secretary feel that there has been some inflexibility in the way that the British Airports Authority has been asked to pursue that target? Why has that target of 6 per cent, in current cost accounting been adhered to in 6 the current climate of the civil aviation industry, and how does it compare with the returns that are being earned in the private sector? My impression is that in the private sector a rate of return of 2 per cent, is more the order of the day. Yet the British Airports Authority is trying to achieve a rate much higher than that, and as a consequence, it has had to raise its landing charges substantially.

Landing charges have aroused much feeling among airport users, not only because of the impact on airlines and their customers, but also because of the possibility of an impact on the location of flights and so on. I shall only say, in parentheses, that it is another example of the situation into which the Government have got themselves with nationalised industries and public corporations. The Government want to contain inflation, and to do so they control the money supply. That was the story two years ago, but it was not mentioned much in the statement of the Chancellor of the Exchequer yesterday. The Chancellor started by asking all bis Departments to ensure that the nationalised industries and the public corporations did not make losses, and, if they were making profits, that they made them bigger. For the British Airports Authority, the best way to fight inflation has been to raise prices, and people, faced by depression and a serious recession, have a double reason for resenting these rising prices.

There is an analogy here with the gas industry which, like the British Airports Authority, has grounds for feeling resentment for being blamed for having to increase prices. It has got all the stick, and is even being taken through the courts for policies which I suspect it does not completely endorse. So it appears that the British Airports Authority, as a consequence of having to finance a certain proportion of its investment through internally generated funds, will have to raise prices substantially.

Will the Under-Secretary tell us what is the current intention in the next round of charges It would be unfortunate—I make no comment on the current litigation—if next year's fight against inflation were accompanied by a further big increase in charges by the public corporations. The figure that has been mentioned is 12 per cent., which is well above the optimistic view of inflation that was given by the Chancellor of the Exchequer yesterday. It would mark a significant increase in the real prices charged by yet another public corporation.

I understand that if the British Airports Authority has to confine its borrowing within the limits that are being set in the clause, which we seek to amend, it will have to raise charges considerably. It is reasonable to ask what are the implications of the limits being set here for those charges. It is not ironic that just over the river a Labour-controlled Greater London Council is being taken to court for having bus fares that are too low, yet here a public JPEG 7 corporation airline is being taken to court because a Tory Government are making it charge prices that are alleged to be too high? The purpose behind my amendment is to carry further in some detail the argument which I advanced on Second Reading and to which the hon. Member for Batley and Morley referred.

First, it is not right that nationalised industries should come to the House of Commons to raise loan capital and extend their borrowing facilities by means of statutory instruments. The significance of my amendment would be to extend the borrowing facility of the British Airports Authority to the £200 million stipulated in clause 1(1) and not carry it further. Secondly, the £200 million limit, in my judgment, provides ample borrowing facilities for the authority to carry forward those of its current capital expenditure plans that need to be financed by borrowing while allowing it time to divest itself of either part of its capital by means of an infusion of equity through the market, or even to go further and seek to dispose of assets—probably whole airports—and to begin the process of denationalisation.

I am more interested in denationalising the British Airports Authority because, in that way, we would get a competitive structure within United Kingdom airports which would be wholly beneficial to civil air transport. My amendment genuinely gives the British Airports Authority ample financial leeway to prepare itself for the issuing of a prospectus for the assets which it ought to divest. I am certain that there would be investors very glad to acquire certain airports in the United Kingdom which are now owned by the British Airports Authority. The authority has done well in recent years, and its latest annual report for the financial year 1980-81 gives eloquent testimony to its good management and efficiency.

I regret the policy which has led the authority to finance so large a proportion of its capital programme from current revenue and that it has had to raise exorbitant landing and other charges to the civil airline operators. That is a mistaken policy. However, by and large, when the individual results are considered as a whole, the authority, according to its own lights, has not done too badly. That is because the airports have generally been well managed. Therefore they provide good opportunities for acquisition by the private sector.

First, I take Heathrow airport as an example because I know that best. That airport's profit almost doubled in the financial year 1980-81 against the profit achieved in the financial year 1979-80. The profit in 1980-81 doubled to £46-4 million. Even Gatwick airport has improved its position substan- 10 tially; the loss in the financial year 1980-81 decreased to £1-6 million as against £4-2 million in the previous year.

Many regard the Scottish airports as perpetual loss makers, but I do not believe them to be so. Aberdeen airport has been singularly successful; the profit is up by almost £| million against a loss in the previous financial year of £1-4 million. From an aviation aspect, Prestwick airport has almost surpassed aviation facilities in Scotland, and the loss there is down to £800,000 as against £2-7 million the year before. Edinburgh airport has had a slight reduction of loss to £1-8 million as against £1-9 million, and the loss at Glasgow airport, most creditably, is down to £700,000 as against £2-6 million. Therefore, the trend in all the Scottish airports owned by the British Airports Authority is favourable and, in the case of Aberdeen airport, is so favourable as to lend itself to becoming a good prospect as an investment for a would-be acquirer.

I have said before that we need a competitive environment. We need Gatwick competing against Heathrow for air traffic. We would obviously like to see Abbotsinch competing against Prestwick and Edinburgh against Glasgow. If everything is done to maximise the commercial incentive of airport owners, the operators will get the best deal. So long as Glasgow airport remains in the same ownership as Prestwick and Abbotsinch in the same ownership as Turnhouse, that competitive incentive will not exist.

It is notable that in the United States the capital programmes for improvements and expansions of airports are financed usually by raising bond issues or even by the infusion of equity capital. That should be the process in the United Kingdom. The commencing capital and existing debt of the British Airways Authority is not over-large: it is some £52.9 million, subsequent loans amount to £8.58 million, and that is a total of £61 million.

The works envisaged and the future capital commitment of the British Airways Authority amounts to about £136.9 million, of which £45.8 million is not itemised in the annual report because it represents contracts already placed but not dealt with in the accounts. The gearing of the British Airways Authority is not unreasonable. Therefore, it lends itself to privatisation. However, the view that I express to my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary of State, with all seriousness, is that it is not a case so much of privatisation as of breaking up the authority into individually manageable units—preferably under different ownership—so that there is a genuinely competitive environment.

My amendment is modest and reasonable from a parliamentary aspect, and it is responsible. I do not believe in nationalised industries coming to the House for more and more money for extra borrowing by statutory instrument. The amendment would give us time to break up the British Airports Authority which, on the whole, has been well managed apart from its policy of financing capital development to such a large extent on current revenue. We should then see landing charges coming down, a JPEG 11 better deal for civil airline operators, more jobs and more importantly a better deal for the travelling public.
