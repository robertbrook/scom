Administration of Justice Bill [Lords]

New Clause 4
APPOINTMENT OF SPECIAL CORONER

"(1) In any case where an event has occurred which but for this section would entitle or require a coroner to exercise his functions, the Lord Chancellor may appoint a special coroner.

(2) No person shall be appointed a special coroner under this section who is not a judge of the High Court or a circuit judge.

(3) When a special coroner has been appointed under this section the Secretary of State shall specify the event in respect of which he has been appointed and the special coroner shall in relation to that event possess and exercise all the rights and duties of a coroner and the same administration and financial arrangements shall apply as to activities of other coroners.".—[ Mr. George Cunningham.]

Brought up and read the First time.

10.30 am

Mr. George Cunningham
(Islington, South and Finsbury)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The clause relates to the manner in which inquests are conducted. I am proposing that the Lord Chancellor should have power to appoint a special coroner, so that some inquests may be taken by the special coroner rather than by the normal coroner for the area in which the body is found.

At present, coroners have jurisdiction on a geographical basis. They are appointed by the local authorities. Many have some medical training and some legal training, and all have one or the other. But there are some cases where, because of great public interest or the sensitive nature of the case or for other reasons—perhaps a difficulty expected during the hearing—where it would be more appropriate for the inquest to be conducted by a person of much higher standing. In the new clause, I have suggested that that person might be either a Higher Court judge or a circuit judge.

High Court judges at present have the general jurisdiction of coroners, but they have no geographical jurisdiction. The legislation on coroners during the past 100 years or so has ensured that any particular case is considered by one of the coroners for the area in question, so I do not know how a High Court judge could come into the act except in an unusual circumstance which I shall mention. It is not enough to say that High Court judges are at present coroners. They may be coroners but they are not coroners for any particular case. There seems to be no way by which anyone else, or a High Court judge himself, can take a particular case 46 to himself, to be heard by him and not by the normal coroner.

The new clause does not reflect on the quality and the work of normal coroners. They do an excellent job. They are, I believe, the right people to conduct 99-9 per cent, of the cases. I suggest only that there is an occasional case, perhaps one in 10 years, where, for one reason or other, a need is felt for a higher level of person to be in charge of an inquest.

This is not a new idea. It has been raised three or four times in the House. I have raised it at Question Time and in the course of debates, and other hon. Members have done the same. I have found, in talking principally to lawyers in the House—it is perhaps significant that lawyers have this reaction—that they have a sympathetic attitude to the proposal that some cases should be tried by a High Court or other judge.

The Brodrick report on coroners' work, so far as I recollect, rather stressed the importance in many cases of the coroner having not only some medical knowledge but the skills required for conducting a judicial hearing. In the past year or two, two particular cases have given rise to some concern about the lack of such facilities—the Blair Peach case and the Deptford fire case. The Deptford fire case is the subject of court proceedings at the moment, and I should not want in any case to go into detail about it. But I do not think that it is sub judice in the course of our considering this proposal for legislation.

I do not wish to base my case on those two incidents because one was—perhaps both of them were—conducted properly, whatever people felt about the outcome. Even if the Deptford fire case inquest turns out to have been conducted wrongly in some respects—we have yet to find that out—the remedy may be the one that has been taken, which is to have the verdict of the inquest quashed in the High Court. Some people may suggest that as that facility exists for quashing a verdict in the High Court, there is no need to do anything else. I do not think so. I am suggesting that we should anticipate difficulties which might occur by having a particular case heard in the normal way, and avoid those difficulties before they occur.

It is particularly important to do that, because even if an inquest verdict is quashed by the High Court, the practice—I am not sure whether this has to be the practice—is for the High Court to quash the verdict and require a new inquest to take place by a named coroner. But I understand that the named coroner is, as a matter of practice, one of the normal named coroners for that county. I do not think that there has been a precedent for the High Court, in quashing an inquest verdict, to appoint a High Court judge to take the substitute inquest.

From conversations with the Law Officers, I understand that there is doubt whether, at least in those circumstances, the High Court could name a High Court judge to take the second inquest. But even if it could do so, where a motion had been JPEG 47 made in the High Court to quash a verdict, it would still not deal with the problem that I am getting at, which is how to avoid an unsatisfactory inquest hearing. Therefore, it would be taken to a higher level without taking it first at a lower level and going through the business of quashing it.

It is always worth referring, in matters of English law, to Scottish practice. In Scotland, what we call inquests are not conducted by a coroner. I do not think that there is such a person as a coroner north of the border. Inquests are conducted in the sheriff court. Therefore, it is possible to conduct them either in a lowish level way or in a more elevated way.

Recently, there was a case in Scotland, in either Dunfermline or Kirkcaldy where a large number of deaths occurred in a nurses' home after a fire. Instead of the case being heard in the sheriff court with simply the local procurator fiscal presenting the case, the Solicitor-General for Scotland presented the case. That is not an exact parallel, because in that case we are talking of bumping up the quality of the person who presents the case. The person conducting the inquiry remains the same, namely the sheriff principal or sheriff. There is a choice north of the border between handling the matter in a regular and routine fashion, or in a more highfalutin fashion for important or sensitive cases. That is what I seek south of the border in a different form.

The new clause says:

" In any case where an event has occurred which but for this section would entitle or require a coroner to exercise his functions, the Lord Chancellor may appoint a special coroner."

 I have not sought to define the circumstances where it would be appropriate for the Lord Chancellor to use that power. I do not think that that can be done. The power should be left entirely to the discretion of the Lord Chancellor, after whatever representations are made by the Law Officers or other persons.

We may find precise words to limit the Lord Chancellor's power. We could say that it should be used in a case of great public importance or of general significance, using the sort of concept now used to distinguish cases taken by a coroner alone or with a jury. But there could be all sorts of relevant considerations about whether it was desirable to have a High Court judge on a particular case. It might be a matter of great public importance or political sensivity. There might be general circumstances to consider—the people involved, and the anticipated difficulty arising from the people involved. It would be impossible to find words in a statute to define the circumstances precisely, yet not be too narrow. On the other hand, if we put in limiting words which were so vague that they were elastic, there would be no point in putting them in at all. It would be better to leave the matter to the Lord Chancellor's discretion.

I have not suggested that the decision should be taken at the request of persons with merely an interest in the hearing. They are not legimitate requesters for this purpose. It would often be the Law 48 Officers, conceivably the Home Office and possibly the police who perceived a particular need in a case. I would leave it entirely to the discretion of the Lord Chancellor, recognising, of course, that the Attorney-General and the Solicitor-General would often have a role to play in making representations to the Lord Chancellor that it was appropriate for him to use his power in a particular case.

I tabled an amendment to the Criminal Justice Bill similar to this new clause. It was held to be outside the scope of the Bill. In it, I said that it should be the Home Secretary rather than the Lord Chancellor making the appointment. The Home Secretary is responsible for legislation on coroners, not the law officers or the Lord Chancellor. The appointment of a judicial person to hold an inquiry or trial is a function which really belongs more happily to the Lord Chancellor than to the Home Secretary or the Home Office.

I am pretty sure about those aspects of new clause 4, but in other cases we may be able to achieve the objectives in a slightly different way. For instance, should the power of appointment be limited to a High Court judge, or go down as low as a circuit judge or county court judge?

It may be necessary to provide in the drafting for the new coroner to have jurisdiction over the particular incident—I think that I have provided for that in the new clause—and also to call off the normal coroner, which has not been done here. That will probably be necessary. Although I do not agree that parliamentary draftsmen are essential to the drafting of all legislation, this is not the sort of matter on which I should expect to get the drafting exactly right myself. But that can be taken care of later.

I hope that the Solicitor-General is therefore able to tell the Committee that, in principle, he agrees that in some cases it should be possible for a High Court judge, a circuit judge or a county court judge to take an inquest under the normal rules of inquests—not conducting a special type of inquiry. I hope, too, that he will throw light on whether that could happen without any change in the law and say that, if it turns out that it cannot happen under the law, he agrees that the law ought to be changed to allow it to happen and that it should be done in the Bill, although not in this Standing Committee, at a later stage on the Floor of the House.

New clause 5
AMENDMENT TO SECTION 22 OF THE JUSTICES OF THE PEACE ACT 1979

" After section 22(2) of the Justices of the Peace Act 1979 there shall be inserted a new subsection (2A) as follows: —

' Where there is a separate magistrates' courts committee for an area to which section 19(2)(a) applies which is divided into petty sessional divisions, the clerk to the justices for any of the petty sessional divisions as the committee might select shall by virtue of his office be the clerk to the committee.'." — [Mr. Archer.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Archer
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

This clause stands in the name of the hon. Member for Plymouth, Drake (Miss Fookes). Unhappily, she is absent from our deliberations, so what was originally intended by my hon. and learned Friend the Member for Accrington (Mr. Davidson) and me as a little demonstration of support has turned to have rather more importance.

The new clause deals with magistrates' courts committees. A magistrates' courts committee is a committee of magistrates which decides what is required by way of court accommodation, court staff, training for justices and staff, and generally the servicing of magistrates' courts in its area.

When a magistrates' courts committee decides what is required, the local authority has to provide the finance. So the local authority is landed with the bill for decisions taken by the magistrates' courts committee. The local authority is consulted before the decision is taken, and there is provision for formal consultation, and if, subsequently, the local authority objects to certain expenditure which has been called for by the magistrates' courts committee, it may appeal to the Secretary of State.

The clerk to the committee may be either the justices' clerk for the area or the clerk to the local authority, or it may be someone else. It is usually either the justices' clerk or the clerk to the local authority. I believe that there are three arguments why it should be the clerk to the justices.

54 First, the clerk to the justices is a trained lawyer —normally, of course, so is the clerk to the local authority, but his expertise will normally lie in a different area. The clerk to the justices has experience in the law which is administered in the magistrates' courts. He is, therefore, familiar with the issues and the difficulties which are likely to arise from them.

Secondly, the clerk to the justices is the man who has to administer the magistrates' courts. He knows their needs because he is dealing with them every day. He is the clerk to other committees of magistrates, and so no one is more familiar with what is required than the clerk to the justices.

Thirdly, if the clerk to the local authority is clerk to the committee, he may have divided loyalties. The magistrates' courts committee may wish to invite certain expenditure. The local authority, quite understandably, may well wish to resist it. The magistrates may then feel that the clerk's primary loyalty is to the local authority.

I shall give one example. I do not even know anything which would identify the individual concerned, so fortunately I do not have to decide whether to give anything away. I have no intention of identifying anyone. A magistrates' courts committee decided to change a number of salaries of the staff in a magistrates' court. Under section 56 of the Justices of the Peace Act 1979, the committee had to consult the local authority before taking a final decision about that expenditure. It therefore instructed its clerk to communicate that fact to the clerk to the local authority, who was, of course, himself. So he took it to his own local authority and the local authority refused to agree to that expenditure. So the clerk to the local authority communicated that to the clerk to the magistrates' courts committee—again himself—and he went back with the information to the magistrates' courts committee.

The magistrates' courts committee decided that it was not persuaded by the objections of the local authority, and it made a formal determination, which it instructed its clerk to pass on to the clerk to the local authority. He passed it on to the local authority, which decided to exercise its right of appeal to the Home Secretary. The clerk to the local authority was placed in the position of being an official both to the local authority and to the magistrates' courts committee. In one capacity he was arguing that the requirement for the expenditure on salaries was right and proper, and in his other capacity he was having to argue that it was totally wrong and unjustifiable. It was an impossible position in which to place an official.

It is fair to say that there are some arguments for the other view. Following the Local Government Act 1972, which precipitated the problem in relation to counties, the Home Office published circular 130/ 73. As it was expressed with a succinctness which does not always categorise Home Office circulars, I cannot do better than read to the Committee what it said on this subject. It said that the Secretary of JPEG 55 State wished to remind magistrates' courts that they had this discretion and then said:

" The Magistrates' Association have expressed the view that a justices' clerk should hold this post on the grounds (a) that the committee should be entirely independent of the local authority and that this is not possible if the clerk of the committee is also clerk of the local authority, (b) that difficulties can arise, in the event of a disagreement between the committee and the local authority, if the same person holds both posts and (c) that with the abolition of the office of clerk of the peace by the Courts Act 1971, clerks of local authorities will increasingly have less direct knowledge of the courts ".

 The circular then added, quite fairly:

" It is understood that while members of the Central Council of Magistrates' Courts Committees support this view, the council itself considers that the selection of the clerk should be at the discretion of the committee ".

 It then proceeds:

" On the other hand, the County Councils Association has expressed the view that the clerk of the local authority is, in view of his other responsibilities, in the better position to ensure the fullest possible co-operation between the committee and the local authority which is responsible for meeting the committee's expenditure. Committees will no doubt wish to bear these considerations in mind in deciding whom to appoint."

 I take the point made by the circular, which seems to be the only point in favour of appointing the clerk to the local authority, that that might ensure a smoother relationship with the local authority. But that is open to the comment that it might result in too good a relationship with the local authority. If it has a clerk who feels that he is bound always to advise the committee to minimise expenditure, that may not be the best way to ensure the fullest possible administration of justice in the magistrates' court. There is very little evidence that there are difficult relations with the local authority, even where the clerk is clerk to the justices, because he has every interest in having the smoothest possible relations with the local authority.

Following that circular, of 89 magistrates' courts' committees in England and Wales, 63 have appointed the justices' clerk, 22 have appointed a local authority official and four have appointed someone else.

I should like to add to one pragmatic argument. The clerk to the local authority is a very busy man who has many other commitments. It is less likely that he would give a great deal of his time and attention to this function than if the appointment were the clerk to the justices, whose whole life is filled by the matters deliberated upon by the committee.

I believe that the weight of argument is in favour of this function being discharged by the justices' clerk. Clearly the hon. This is a somewhat narrower and more technical matter which has been ventilated in another place. It is also a very important matter which will be much debated from time to time.

The basis of compensation for accident in this country is that if a person causes loss or damage to another, either deliberately or negligently, he is under an obligation to compensate the victim. But in the absence of any fault, the loss lies where it falls and the victim must bear his misfortune with as much fortitude as he can command.

That has been the general rule, but over the past century the law has made inroads into what was originally a laissez-faire position. Now we can insure against accidental damage and injury. We can protect ourselves by getting the strength of the insurance companies around us. In some cases, the community will help us. We have a national insurance scheme which provides benefit for those who are off work sick, however the sickness or disability came about. There are benefits and help for the handicapped, and although they are not sufficient, they are growing in number.

We do not cling as a matter of principle to the philosophy that those who suffer injury or loss are on their own and should be left to cope unaided. In certain areas we have embraced the principle that those who engage in hazardous occupations or undertakings should be strictly liable to compensate those who are injured in consequence, irrespective of the fault. If they benefit from conducting the undertaking, they should insure the public.

The occupier of a factory has a statutory obligation to ensure the safety of his employees in certain respects, and visitors to the premises. If he fails and they are injured, he must compensate them, even if they cannot establish that he was negligent.

Therefore, our proposal is not novel in principle. Those who believe that leaving the victim of an accident to establish, if he can, that someone was at fault, or else to bear his misfortune alone, are adopting an outdated approach. Let us say, for example, that two men each lose an eye. In one 76 case it is clearly the fault of someone else. The man has the good fortune to have a witness who is prepared to give evidence. The wrongdoer has assets to satisfy any judgment which may be given. So the man is fully compensated, in so far as money can compensate for the loss of his eye.

The other man suffers identical injury. His loss is precisely the same and the consequences for his future the same, but it was no one's fault. It was just one of those things, there was no witness looking in the right direction at the right time, or the wrongdoer has no assets so cannot satisfy any judgment given. That person is left to cope without compensation.

Perhaps we should consider, not how or why a person came by an injury, but what are his needs. That was the subject of a recent letter in The Times by my right hon Friend the Member for Manchester, Wythenshawe (Mr. Morris). It was also the theme of the Pearson report.

It is certainly possible to argue that the community as a whole should look after those who are injured in this way.
