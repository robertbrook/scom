Criminal Justice Bill

New Clause 24
LEGAL AID FOR BAIL APPLICATIONS TO CROWN COURTS

"(1) In section 28 of the Legal Aid Act 1974, after subsection (5) there shall be inserted the following subsection—

"(5A) Where a person who is in the custody of a magistrates' court and who has been refused bail by that court desires to apply to the Crown Court for bail either of those courts may order that he shall be given legal aid for the purpose of that application"

(2) In section 81(1) of the Supreme Court Act 1981, after paragraph (e) there shall be inserted the following paragraph—

"if who has been refused bail by a magistrates' court and remanded in custody".".—[ Mr. Kilroy-Silk.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

4.30 pm

Mr. Robert Kilroy-Silk
(Ormskirk)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

This new clause would provide defendants remanded in custody by a magistrates' court with a legally-aided right of application to the Crown court for bail. Given that the Minister has already intimated in our last but one sitting that he will accept the new clause, I shall not detain the Committee.

I wish only to say that at the moment a defendant who has been committed in custody to the Crown court for trial or sentence may apply for bail to the Crown court, and the cost of such a bail application is covered by the Crown court legal aid order. However, defendants who are still on remand before a magistrates' court have no right to apply for bail to the Crown court. This new clause would give them such a right.

The advantages of a Crown court application over the High Court procedure are clear. It is more accessible and therefore speedier and, because of the number of Crown court centres in England and Wales, often in the same building as the magistrates' court, means that a Crown court judge is more likely to be near at hand.

In 1979, the Royal Commission on Legal Services in paragraph 14.21 of its final report stated:

"It has been proposed by the Law Society that, when a defendant has been remanded in custody by a magistrates' court, his solicitor should be able to make a single oral application to a Crown Court judge in chambers, recovering the fee from criminal legal aid funds "

 It went on to say:

"We recommend that this procedure be adopted as an interim measure, and that the system as a whole should 668 be reviewed when the effects of the Bail Act 1976 can be fully assessed."

 I know that Government supporters do not like this reason being put forward, but at a time of unprecedented overcrowding in local prisons and remand centres, it is good to know that the Minister is adopting a positive and constructive attitude. It is true that this proposal will have financial implications, but against the relatively modest cost must be offset the actual cost of keeping defendants in prison and having to produce them in court at regular intervals. That cost must be set against the increase in resources to implement the new clause.

It is another significant, albeit relatively modest improvement in our criminal justice system. I am grateful to the Minister for having intimated that he is prepared to accept the new clause.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Patrick Mayhew)

He will recall that I drew attention to the fact that matters relating to legal aid were the province of my right hon. and noble Friend the Lord Chancellor, and therefore I am not able to go further than I intimated at our previous sitting, which is highly encouraging but not the whole works.

Persons in the custody of magistrates' courts can apply for bail to the High Court either via the Official Solicitor or through the Crown Office procedure. The Official Solicitor acts without any means or merits test being applied, but applications are normally dealt with on the papers alone. For one reason or another, there is a low success rate.

The Crown Office procedure involves an oral hearing, and civil legal aid is available for it. But that means that one has to satisfy the local committee that there is a reasonable prospect of success and that one's means are below the statutory maxima.

It is subject to those normal merit tests, and it has rarely been granted in the past, although it appears likely that that will change if no alternative avenue of application is provided. The Crown Office procedure has a much higher success rate, although that is probably attributable more to the fact that those using it must either pay privately or satisfy the merits test for civil legal aid than it is to the superiority of the procedure. In other words, it is probably a better case that is taken to the Crown Office than that taken to the Official Solicitor. It has been made clear that both the Lord Chancellor and my right hon. Friend the Home Secretary recognise that the present arrangements are not fully satisfactory.

The new clause would provide an avenue of application for bail to the Crown court for persons in the custody of a magistrate's court, and it would enable either the Crown court or the magistrates* court itself to grant legal aid. The Government accept that these arrangements at present are not: satisfactory, and we have already discussed the impact of the divisional court's decision in the JPEG 669 Nottingham justices' case. It is true that that decision had added an extra complication, because effectively it has restricted defendants to one fully-argued application for bail unless there is a change of circumstances. That in itself, with the effluxion of time, can constitute a significant new circumstance.

For a while, however, a defendant is limited to one fully-argued application for bail, although, as I emphasised the other day, the magistrates are under an obligation to consider bail every eight days whether the defendant is there or not. In our view, the solution proposed by the new clause, namely, to provide a legally-aided avenue of application to the Crown court, is the right one. The problem has been a lack of the necessary resources to introduce the new means of applying for bail.

It would clearly be necessary to withdraw legal aid from the Crown Office procedure to help offset the cost, particularly as those applications are, on average, considerably more expensive than applications to the Crown court would be. It would also be necessary to ensure that solicitors had a right of audience for the Crown court application, because that would also help to reduce the cost. A further point that will need to be considered is the form in which legal aid should be provided. The new clause proposes that a separate legal aid order should be granted. We think that it may be preferable to extend assistance by way of representation, which is a more flexible scheme, instead, but we have not yet come to a firm decision about that.

I am therefore able to give an undertaking that we shall give the new clause serious and, as I indicated, sympathetic consideration. We must decide the form in which legal aid should be provided, and we must ensure that the necessary resources are available. The other day, I gave an indication of the measure of resources that would be needed. But if the hon. Gentleman will accept those assurances, I shall undertake to consider the possibility of putting down a Government new clause on Report to give effect to the principle of his new clause.

New Clause 27
LIMIT ON CUSTODIAL REMANDS IN ENGLAND AND WALES

"(1) Subject to subsections (2) and (3) below, an accused who is committed for any offence until liberated in due course of law shall not be detained by virtue of that committal for a total period of more than 110 days, unless the trial of the case is commenced within that period, which failing he shall be liberated forthwith and thereafter he shall be for ever free from all question or process for that offence.

  1. A single judge of the High Court may, on application made to him for the purpose, extend the period mentioned in subsection (1) above where he is satisried that delay in the commencement of the trial is due to—

    2. the absence or illness of any necessary witness; or
    3. any other sufficient cause which is not attributabie to any fault on the part of the prosecutor.
  2. The grant or refusal of any application to extend the period mentioned in subsection (1) above may be appealed against by note of appeal presented to the High Court; and that Court may affirm, reverse or amend the determination made on such application.

The new clause would require that defendants committed in custody should be brought to trial within a period of 110 days of their committal. As with many of the other new clauses debated today and on previous occasions, this embodies a recommendation of the parliamentary all-party Penal Affairs Group in its report " Too Many Prisoners " published in 1980. It also embodies a recommendation of the Select Committee on Home Affairs in its report of last year entitled " The Prison Service."

The Committee may recall that the May committee, in its inquiry into the United Kingdom prison service in 1979, reported on what it referred to as the scandal of the numbers of people held on remand and the length of time that they were held on remand. It observed particularly that remand periods in England and Wales were "excessively long " and had been increasing. It went on to assert that there was a need to do everything possible to bring defendants in custody to trial as soon as possible.

Since the report of the May committee, which yet again drew attention to the deplorable state of affairs of remand prisoners, certain events have transpired. The Lord Chancellor and the Lord Chief Justice have both, I acknowledge, made great efforts to reduce waiting times for trial, and to some extent they have had limited success. They deserve credit for the efforts that they have been making in the last year or so. Yet, at the same time, in spite of their worthy attempts, in spite of their acknowledgment of the seriousness of the problem and, in spite of their attempts to bring down the waiting time, waiting times are much longer today than they were in the mid-1970s.

"would be intolerable enough when inflicted on persons found guilty of an offence, but for the many persons awaiting trial and innocent before the law, these experiences are completely insupportable."

 A written parliamentary answer on 23 November 1981 revealed that on 30 September 1981 there were 700 prisoners who had been awaiting trial for between three and six months; 300 had been awaiting trial for between six and 12 months; and 100 had been awaiting trial for over 12 months. A further parliamentary answer also estimated that about 10 people awaiting trial in custody on that date had been first remanded in custody more than two years earlier, although some of them had spent time out on bail.

In 1980, I made a series of detailed inquiries into the specific cases of prisoners who had been awaiting trial in custody for long periods. Those investigations disclosed that in June 1980 one prisoner had spent 600 days in prison on remand awaiting trial, and was then subsequently found not guilty. The following month, a man who had been held in custody for two and a half years was formally found not guilty when the prosecution offered no evidence against him. Those clearly are indefensible cases. It is indefensible, in any event, that somebody should have been held in prison unnecessarily and subsequently found innocent—not guilty, or even guilty but given a non-custodial sentence. It is a disgraceful scandal that they should be held in prison for such long periods of time and then subsequently found not guilty, or have no evidence offered.

Apart from that matter of principle, there is also the fact that very long delays in bringing a person to trial can reduce seriously the chances of justice actually being done in a particular case, not only because the evidence of the prosecution and of the defence is less fresh—their memories are gone—but for a whole variety of reasons to do with the individual and his family, the longer the delay the less likely it is that justice will be done or be seen to be done.

On any one day, on Home Office estimates, there are between 800 and 1,000 prisoners in England and Wales who will have been awaiting trial for periods in excess of 110 days. In Scotland, on the other hand, the law provides that, once a defendant has been fully committed for trial, unless he is released from custody he must be brought to trial within the 110 days. The 110-day period can be extended only at the request of the defence, or if the delay is due to such factors as the illness of the accused, or of a necessary witness, or the judge or a juryman. Both the parliamentary all-party Penal Affairs Group and 674 the Select Committee on Home Affairs have now recommended in separate reports that a similar rule should be introduced in England and Wales.

It is true that the introduction of such a 110-day rule here would not result in a large reduction in the prison population. For one thing, as the Minister has said on previous occasions, in some cases the delay is due to the defence rather than to the prosecution, and the 110-day rule can in any case be waived at the request of the defence. Again, more than 50 per cent, of those remanded in custody eventually receive custodial sentences. I accept that the time spent in custody awaiting (trial will count towards their prison sentence, and therefore that will not reduce the numbers or the time spent in custody. Bringing the trial forward would not affect the total length of time which those people spend in prison.

The Home Office nevertheless, in a memorandum to the Select Committee on Home Affairs, estimated that even when those and other factors were taken into account the 110-day rule would reduce the daily average prison population by about 200 —not a negligible or insignificant number.
