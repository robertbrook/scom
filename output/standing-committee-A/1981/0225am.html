Criminal Justice Bill

Clause 9
PROVISION OF PREMISES FOR YOUNG OFFENDERS

Question again proposed.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Patrick Mayhew)
: I begin, Mr. Watkins, with a warm welcome to you to our Committee proceedings. Although we are sad not to have Mr. Dean with us, we know that we shall benefit from your guidance of our affairs.

At the end of our last sitting I was asked a number of questions in the course of some excellent speeches on the debate that clause 9 stand part of the Bill, and I hope that I may take a little time to reply to those questions.

The hon. Member for Barking (Miss Richardson) asked what establishments would serve as remand centres. There was some interest in that general point so perhaps I could begin by saying a little on that. New section 43 (1) of the Prison Act 1952 provides the definition for remand centres. They are described as:

"places for the detention of persons not less than fourteen but under twenty-one years of age who are remanded or committed in custody for trial or sentence.

Section 43 (2) goes further by saying that although statutory provision for remand centres was first made in 1948, none of the establishments now called remand centres has been legally designated as such. In law they are still prisons. The main reason why no remand centres have been designated is that the present law imposes certain inflexible requirements that cannot be satisfied in practice. Once the Bill is in force, including clause 9, the modifications to the law that are necessary to remove these difficulties will have been made and the existing remand centres will be legally designated as such.

I shall outline briefly what the inflexible requirements of the present law are. First, the Secretary of State is obliged to provide facilities in remand centres for the observation of any person detained there on whose physical or mental condition the court wants a medical report. There is no similar obligation in respect of any other type of establishment. It is not considered feasible to provide permanent facilities for the medical observation of offenders in all remand centres. For example, a 252 remand centre is quite often attached to, or is close to a local prison where there are facilities for medical examination, and therefore it is not practicable to comply with this requirement.

A further necessary modification in the use of remand centres is made in the new section 43 (2), which provides that a woman aged 21 years or over who is serving a sentence of imprisonment, or who has been committed to prison for default may, as the Secretary of State may from time to time direct, be detained in a remand centre or a youth custody centre instead of a prison. The reason for this is set out in paragraph 25 of the White Paper, which reads:

"Because of the relatively small number of women in custody, it has not in recent years been considered necessary or desirable to enforce a strict segregation between the various age groups; indeed, it is generally considered to be positively beneficial for younger women to share facilities with suitable adults. This practice will be continued where approprate."

The third requirement of the existing law, which is impracticably inflexible, is met by the new section 43(2) of the Prison Act which will be created by the Bill. A woman aged 21 or over who is remanded in custody or committed in custody for trial can be held in a remand centre instead of prison. The need for this arises because there are few womens' prisons and many parts of the country are a long way from an establishment that can hold adult women.

Three of the places that are now called remand centres—Pucklechurch, Low Newton and Risley—do have facilities for women. They are in law still prisons, and if under the present law they become remand centres, it will no longer be possible to hold adult women in them. Without these centres, the women's system will not be able to cope with the reception and production in court of women remanded in custody. It is therefore necessary to take statutory power to hold them in remand centres.

Mr. Mayhew
: We have a great deal of sympathy with the thinking that lies behind the hon. Gentleman's two amendments. He is right in saying that the Bill forbids a sentence of imprisonment on a juvenile. There are, however, circumstances that will rebound to the advantage of a juvenile, exceptionally, in permitting the Secretary of State to direct that a juvenile may be detained—not sentenced—in certain circumstances in a prison.

The point is threefold. To enable the efficient allocation of offenders sentenced to youth custody, just like borstal trainees and young prisoners now, young offenders will after sentence be received direct from court into local prisons, which are the establishments that serve the courts. Incidentally, they are normally closest to families, probation officers, and legal advisers, which can be particularly important just after a person has received a custodial sentence.

The period is normally no more than a week for allocation. It is important that allocation should take place at the establishment which serves the court, and which normally will be near to the officers and agencies, and certainly to the families, as I have described. The process of allocation—interviewing, gathering of information, assessment, decision about where the person should go, and the actual transfer—should normally take about a week. We are anxious to make that process as quick as is consistent with the need to take proper decisions, so that the person can reach a training establishment without unnecessary delay. The local prison is the best place to do that.

Direct committal from courts to training youth custody centres is not desirable because the establishments could not readily be geared to receiving direct from court. If they tried to gear themselves, they would take staff away from training activities.

The hon. Gentleman will know that proper selection of individuals for open or closed conditions, and medical, psychiatric or educational facilities, would still require a form of allocation process, resources devoted to assessment units, and further transfers between establishments. Combined remand and allocation centres normally on urban sites, therefore local sites, such as urban prisons, for persons under 21, are, however, a possibility for the future. They are plainly desirable. That is currently under consideration in the context of the developing remand structure.
