Criminal Justice Bill

New clause 61
POWER TO REVIEW THE SENTENCE OF A FINE DEFAULTER INSTEAD OF COMMITTING HIM TO PRISON

"Where, after the occasion of an offender's conviction, a court has the power to issue a warrant committing him to prison for a default in paying a fine, the court may deal with him for the offence in respect of which the fine was imposed in any manner in which it it could deal with him if it had just convicted him of that offence."—[ Mr. Dubs.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

4.30 pm

Mr. Alfred Dubs
(Battersea, South)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

This is a fairly modest proposals and it is intended to give the courts the power to review the sentence in those cases where someone on whom a fine was originally imposed has not paid that fine and would, under present circumstances, be committed to prison. The intention is that the courts would have the power to review the sentence in the hope of avoiding a prison sentence for all the individuals in that category.

I should like to give a somewhat dramatic example of something that happened earlier this year when a magistrates' court in Dewsbury sentenced a mother of five to prison because she was not able to pay £55 in penalties as a consequence of not having a licence for her television set. Mercifully, the local church collected the money so that the woman in question spent only a short period in gaol. Nevertheless, she was sent there basically because of what seems to us to be a trivial matter and certainly not one demanding a custodial sentence.

Although I do not have any figures on how often this happens, I am assured that that instance was not an isolated one. According to the local paper for the area, one of the magistrates said that he would send pregnant women to prison if that was the only way they could get the money from them. When the lady who was sentenced to custody had originally been fined for not having a television licence, she had been in hospital expecting a baby. It appears that she had been in hospital for about three months and her husband had not wanted to tell her about the fine because he did not wish to upset her. Shortly after she came out of hospital she found herself back in court and sentenced to gaol.

Clearly, the majority of fine defaulters are not of that kind—or I certainly hope that they are not of that kind. However, there are many of them. In 1979, more that 17,000 fine defaulters were received into prison. That comprises nearly a quarter of all the people who were received into custody as part of their sentence. The figures for 1980 are rather more difficult to pin down because of the prison officers' dispute, but until the autumn of that year when the dispute began there was an increase in fine defaulters sent to prison of 14 per cent, over the year before.

850 I am aware that the sentences are usually not all that long. Indeed, in 1979 nearly a quarter of all those fine defaulters sent to prison were sent for a period of up to one week. Just under a third were sent to prison for between two weeks and one month, but almost one in 10 was sent to prison for between two and six months. That is quite a large number sent for what is a pretty long period of time when the courts had originally not intended a custodial sentence at all. But, because the average sentence of a fine defaulter sent to prison is relatively short, they accounted for 2.5 per cent, of the sentenced population or just under 1,000 out of nearly 37,000. The prison population is larger than that because people are also remanded in custody, which brings the numbers up.

Most of the fine defaulters sentenced to prison are males—in 1979 over 16,000 compared with just under 1,000 who were females. A fair proportion are in the 17 to 20-year-old category—just over 2,000 of them are between these ages. What have they originally done to attract the fine which in the end they have not paid? Looking at the males only in 1979, the largest category to be given fines leading to a prison sentence for a fine default because of theft, handling, fraud and forgery was some 4,500. Motoring offences was the second largest category, 3,000, and drunkenness, 2,400.

Section 59 of the Criminal Law Act 1977 reduced the maximum period in prison that the court could set in default of a given amount of fine. That had the result that the group which was given the longest prison sentences as a consequence of fine default—that is, a two to six-month sentence—was reduced from 30 per cent, of the total to 9 per cent, after the Act came into force. However, there is still quite a large number who are sentenced to fairly long periods of imprisonment for a fine default.

It is my purpose in moving this new clause to encourage the courts to look for alternatives to prison in the case of fine defaulters. At the moment only one alternative sentence can be imposed. That is an attendance centre order for those under 21, but this is seldom used or considered by the courts.

The Criminal Justice Act 1972 provided for community service orders for fine defaulters, but this section of that Act has not been implemented and, therefore, cannot be used.

The evidence is that in quite a number of instances, after a fine has been imposed, there has been a change of circumstances affecting the person fined which makes it more difficult for him or her to pay the fine. A Home Office research study called "Fines in Magistrates Courts" suggested, on the basis of a sample survey, that 29 per cent, of defaulters had had changes in their circumstances after the fine had been imposed which made it more difficult for them to pay the fine. That is one of the main reasons why there are so many fine defaulters. This proposal, which is a modest one, might go some way towards reducing the prison population, although not by very large numbers. The people who are in prison through fine defauls are there not through the direct wish of the courts in the first instance, otherwise a custodial sentence would have been imposed at the outset, but because they have either not been willing to pay the fine or, in many cases, not been able to pay the fine or found great difficulty in doing so.

JPEG 851 This modest proposal is one which should commend itself to the Committee, and I hope that the Minister will see his way to accepting it.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Patrick Mayhew)
:I warmly agree with what the hon. Gentleman has just said. Fine defaulters who end up in prison, albeit for only a short space of time in the majority of cases, find themselves in a place where it was not the original intention of the court that they should go. We all agree and have expressed our agreement in the past in this Committee that it must only be a matter of last resort that a fine defaulter should go to prison.

We have observed in the course of our earlier proceedings that Parliament has already written into statute law some very considerable safeguards with the purpose of ensuring that it is only as a last resort, and as an ultimate sanction, that the power to imprison shall be used. Therefore, we must bear this in mind when examining the new clause that the hon. Gentleman has very helpfully tabled and extremely fairly and lucidly explained this afternoon.

We sympathise with his purpose of encouraging courts to find an alternative to custody for defaulters, but listening to his account of the matter I do not think he gives sufficient weight to the fact that the courts have to be satisfied that there was wilful default or culpable neglect to pay the fine before they can send someone to prison in default.

Nor, I thought, did he give sufficient weight to the procedures which oblige them to consider whether there ought to be a review of means, whether the amount of the fine or the instalment order should be considered, and so on. The effect of the new clause would be to enable courts to carry out a review of the sentence instead of committing a fine defaulter to prison.

It is, I think, common ground that not very many fine defaulters actually go to prison once the warrant of committal has been issued, but this new clause carries the danger of the reverse effect to that intended by the hon. Gentleman, namely, increasing the length of time spent in prison by fine defaulters. It could come about in this way. An offender convicted of theft in a magistrates' court, for example, would be liable to a maximum penalty of six months' imprisonment or a fine of £1,000 or both. He may have been fined £200, in the event, and if he refused to pay until the point where a court had no alternative but to consider a warrant of committal, the maximum number of days he could spend in prison is only 30. That is the maximum permitted in relation to a fine of £200 in the table of imprisonment in default in schedule 4 to the Magistrates' Courts Act 1980. If, as this new clause would provide, a court could sentence the offender for his original offence, what is there to prevent it from imposing the original option of six months' imprisonment, or in any case for a term longer than 30 days?

One could devise a solution. The new clause could be extended to exclude custodial sentences from any review of sentence. The court would still have the option of sending the defaulter to prison for the usual default term, but this would not, I think, be satisfactory for a number of reasons. We must consider what such a limited review of sentence would enable a court to do. It could give the fine defaulter an abosute or conditional 852 discharge. I doubt very much whether it would so so. It is likely to be regarded as far too soft an option for an offender who has defaulted through what would have been found to be culpable neglect or wilful refusal to pay.

I should add that an offender who has defaulted through no fault of his own can also have his fine wholly or partly remitted, and is not liable to imprisonment. There are also probation and community service orders available, but such orders are hardly likely to be suitable for offenders who have already proved their unwillingness to follow the court's direction, notwithstanding that they were able to do so. The circumstances of the offence would once again be considered. Probation officers would have to report on the candidate's suitability. Even the potentially-suitable offenders might not agree to an order, and a small but troublesome proportion of deliberate fine defaulters seem to be determined to go to prison to make a point.

The right hon. and learned Member for Dulwich (Mr. Silkin) the other day gave an example of that from his own experience as a Member of Parliament and an advocate. A few might succeed in fulfilling their obligations to the court and to society in this way but the rest, I fear, would be back full circle, having their sentence reviewed once again as a consequence of the probation order or community service order. Imprisonment would be the final resort, as it is intended to be at the moment, but after a delay incurring much more work for the courts, and possibly also for agencies such as the probation service, and much greater cost to the taxpayer.

Also, it is not clear how sentence reviews would take account of offenders who had partly paid off their fines. Should Crown courts spend their time reviewing the sentences of fine defaulters whom they had sentenced, or should magistrates' courts be given the power to alter as well as to enforce sentences imposed by the Crown court? One should also bear in mind that there has yet to be found a more effective way of securing payment of a fine than an immediate or a suspended warrant of committal. We should be wary of further diluting the deterrent powers of the fine enforcement procedures.

4.45 pm

The hon. Member for York (Mr. Lyon), who is not here today, spoke convincingly at our last sitting, with the experience of a former Home Office Minister, of the fact that the power to imprison as a last resort secures the payment of a large proportion of maintenance orders which have been in default, and the experience in relation to fines is not dissimilar. Therefore, while I wholly understand and sympathise with the object behind the new clause proposed by the hon. Gentleman, in the vast majority of cases it would serve only to postpone the time but not to avoid it when a warrant of committal would be issued.

That being the case, we cannot neglect the substantial extra work both for the court and the probation service that would ensue, the time that would elapse and the expense to the taxpayer. These considerations oblige the Government to take the view that this new clause should not be included in the Bill.

Mr. Dubs
: I am sorry that the Minister has taken that JPEG 853 rather hostile attitude to the new clause. However, I do not want to press it to a vote at this stage. I therefore beg to ask leave to withdraw the motion.

Motion and clause, by leave, withdrawn.

New clause 62
REDUCTION IN THE MAXIMUM LENGTH OF PROBATION ORDERS

"In section 2(1) of the Powers of Criminal Courts Act 1973, the words 'three years' shall be omitted and the words 'two years' shall be inserted."—[ Mr. Parris.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Matthew Parris
(Derbyshire, West)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The purpose of the new clause is to reduce the maximum length of probation orders. I put it forward in a tentative spirit, awaiting my hon. and learned Friend's reply before I decide whether to persist with it.

The Criminal Law Act 1977 reduced the minimum length of a probation order to six months, and section 57 gave the Home Secretary the power to change the minimum and maximum periods. This is a power which he has never yet used and I invite the Committee to consider whether it is now time for him to exercise his power to reduce the maximum period of probation from three years to two years.

Current wisdom advocates the use of shorter periods of supervision. The figures indicate that the courts have been prepared already to move a good way in that direction. The probation statistics for 1980 show that only 2,137 offenders were placed on probation orders for periods longer than two years. That represents 6 per cent, of the total orders made during that year.

The decreasing use of longer orders is shown by the fact that in 1971, nine years previously, 8,681 such orders were made—27 per cent, of the orders made in that year. There has been a corresponding increase in the use of short orders. In 1980, 30 per cent, of orders were for one year or less.

There is a consensus in the probation service that supervision should be purposeful and aim at the achievement of identifiable goals within a reasonable time span. Commonly, the first few months of a probation order is the period in which most work is done with probationers, and in most cases any beneficial effect will be achieved within that period. A shorter order may help to preserve some momentum and more successfully enlist the active commitment of the probationer to tackle problems and to achieve change. To put it at its lowest, if nothing has been achieved within two years there is probably not much point in carrying on.

It is true that probation officers can apply to the courts for discharge of orders of any length before they have run their full course. However, this would not be normal before the order was between a half and two-thirds complete. Even in that case, discharge can normally be sought only on grounds of positive progress. The National Association of Probation Officers believes that some orders are left to run their full course for a variety of inadequate reasons, when little more can be achieved, and that this is not a very effective use of scarce probation resources.

854 Finally, Mr. Dean, I remind the Committee that when offenders are placed on probation they remain liable for the duration of the order to be sentenced for the original offence should they re-offend. At present, though, a suspended sentence can be imposed for up to two years where as a probation order can be for up to three years so the period of jeopardy of being re-sentenced, so to speak, extends for three years if one is on a probation order and two years if one is given an ordinary suspended sentence. The Committee may think this rather anomalous.

I suggest that this change would not materially affect the courts' sentencing powers but would assist good practice in the probation service and simply give legislative effect to changes which are already taking place in sensible practice.

I have had evidence given me by the National Association of Probation Officers. I have heard their side of the story, I have not heard my hon. and learned Friends' view and I shall await that view before I make up my own mind on whether to persist with this new clause.

Dr. Shirley Summerskill
(Halifax)
:I support what the hon. Gentleman has said, that we have a situation where the minimum length of orders has been reduced but the maximum length has remained three years. In practice, I understand that courts make relatively few orders that are longer than two years. Therefore, is this not the time to reduce it to a maximum of two years?

As has been said, we are always told that any good which will be achieved from supervision will have been achieved or not within the period of two years. Increasing the time only puts more work upon the probation officers with apparently less effect resulting. Lastly, if this change was incorporated in the Bill, it would have the advantage of making the maximum period for which a person based on probation is liable to be dealt with for the original offence the same as the maximum one for which a sentence of imprisonment can be suspended, that is, two years. For all these reason, I support what the hon. Gentleman has said.

Mr. Mayhew
: I am grateful to my hon. Friend for giving us the opportunity to discuss the present maximum duration of a probation order and for the way in which he has moved his new clause.

The amendment that is tabled would reduce the statutory maximum from three years to two. I am sure it is generally agreed that probation orders should run no longer than is necessary for the offender to gain any benefits that supervision may yield. Therefore, one naturally sympathises with many of the things that my hon. Friend has said.

But on the other hand, I feel it is very important that we should not do anything to diminish the attraction to courts of probation orders as a means of disposing of a criiminal case. If it were the case that the period between two years and three years was virtually never used by courts today when making a probation order, I would certainly feel able to take a different view towards this new clause than I am at the moment. But quite a significant number of cases still appear in which the courts make an order more than two years, and I think it must follow from that that the probation order as a disposal would appear, in a significant number of JPEG 855 cases, to be less attractive to the courts and the courts would therefore be less likely to use it and more likely to use, for example, a custodial disposal if we were to accede to my hon. Friend's suggestion.

Dr. Summerskill
: The hon. and learned Gentleman says quite a significant number of orders are made for a period of more than two years, but probation officers say that relatively few orders are. May we perhaps have some numbers?

Mr. Mayhew
: Yes, the number was 2,100 in 1980. That is relatively fewer than nine years earlier in 1971 when 8,700 persons were given orders of longer than two years. In 1980, the number was down to 2,100. It is therefore a diminishing number, and I think that we may see this trend continue.

At the moment I do not believe that any hardship or wrong exists in maintaining the present maximum. There has been a decline, too, in the proportionate use of longer orders. In 1971, nearly 27 per cent, of persons given orders were placed on probation for more than two years, compared with just over 6 per cent, in 1980.1 suggest therefore that the figures demonstrate a growing awareness on the part of the courts and the probation service that a short or medium-length order can be just as effective as a long one. It has been one of the recurring themes of our deliberations that maximum flexibility should be retained for the courts in deciding how to dispose of offenders, within certain parameters which we have thought it right to lay down.

We have, for example, thought it right to say that there must be tight restrictions on the power of the courts to send somebody to prison or into custody of any sort, if he or she is under 21. Here, however, we are talking only about whether a court may make a probation order for as long as three years, or whether the maximum should be limited to two years.

I think that there is a fairly important distinction in principle between the sentence of imprisonment, which is what a suspended sentence is, and an order that somebody shall submit to the supervision of a probation officer for a given time. I do not believe that because we say that the maximum period over which a sentence of imprisonment may be suspended is two years, that we are therefore logically led to say that the same should be the maximum period for a probation order.

I suggest that it is a question of judgment in each case, and courts in more than 2,000 cases in 1980, evidently still thought it right, at any rate at the time of making the order, to say that it should persist for three years. We must assume that the courts clearly took into account the power to make an order of shorter duration but felt that that the circumstances of the offence, if not the offender, rendered it desirable that the order should so as far as three years.

Despite the trend to which I referred, the courts still find a three-year order a useful option in certain cases. Therefore, as I said, it would be contrary to our entire approach to the Bill to limit the flexibility of the courts by depriving them of a disposal which they may find helpful in dealing with difficult cases.

We must consider as well how the 2,000 or so offenders who in 1980 were put on probation for more than two years would have been dealt with if the courts 856 had been restricted to making a two-year order. I do not think that we can confidently assume that all would have been given short-term orders. We cannot rule out the possibility that some of them would have received custodial sentences.

If we give our minds to the type of circumstances that must have accounted for a fair number of those 2,100 cases in 1980, there must have been a reasonable proportion in which the court said "It is borderline whether you go into a form of custody. We feel that we can take a lenient course, but we must ensure that you are subject to supervision for a longer rather than a shorter time". My guess is that a fair proportion of those cases fell into that category.

The statutory framework of the probation order ought to remain as wide and flexible asjpossible. Within the framework there are arrangements designed to ensure that probation orders are not continued for longer than they need be. The majority are made following a favourable assessment in a social inquiry report prepared by a probation officer.

In 1974, the Home Office, with the support of the then Lord Chief Justice, encouraged probation officers to suggest a specific term for an order when offering advice to the courts on the suitability of an offender for probation. This advice still stands, and so, if a probation officer considers that a short period of probation will suffice in a particular case, he is quite free to say so, and he will help the court if he does.

We are here in much the same position as we shall be when we discuss maximum sentences later on, if we reach that new clause—namely, whether we ought to cut back the maximum in order to ensure that a shorter average length of order or sentence should be made.

I therefore hope that in the light of this explanation of how the power is operated at present, my hon. Friend will feel it right to withdraw his new clause.

Mr. Parris
: I am grateful to my hon. and learned Friend for his careful reply to the case that I put forward. I was encouraged by what he said about the trend towards shorter orders, and I note that he does not rule out the possibility that when that trend is even more firmly established, and even fewer of the longer orders are being given, the Home Secretary may invoke the powers that he already has to reduce the maximum length of probation orders.

In the light of what he has said, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the motion.

New clause 63
TO INCREASE THE MAXIMUM FINE FOR IDENTIFYING A JUVENILE IN REPORTS OF COURT PROCEEDINGS

(2) Any person who publishes any matter in contravention of any direction made under section 39(1) of the Children and Young Persons Act 1933 shall on summary conviction be liable in respect of each offence to a fine not exceeding £5,000.

(3) Any person who publishes any matter in contravention of section 49 of the Children and Young Persons Act 1933 shall on summary conviction be liable in respect of each offence to a fine not exceeded £5,000."—[ Mr Kilroy-Silk.)
