Criminal Justice Bill

Clause 2
ORDERS FOR DETENTION OF MALE OFFENDERS AGED 14 TO 20

Amendment proposed [16 February], No. 14, in page 3, line 39, at end insert—

" (5A) For the purposes of determining whether the detention of an offender in a detention centre would be unsuitable because of his mental or physical condition the court shall obtain and consider a report into his mental and physical condition "—[ Mr. Kilroy-Silk]

10.31 am

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Patrick Mayhew)
: I had virtually completed what I wanted to say and perhaps I can round it off. It is a general rule that all boys are examined by the detention centre's medical officer on arrival at a detention centre. This ensures that, where necessary, they may be excused from some or all of the physically demanding activities that constitute a normal part of the regime. Alternatively, activities such as physical education can be graduated to meet their fitness. Few of the boys sent to the pilot project dention centres have had to be referred to the detention centre hospital. The hospital facilities do not exist only to deal with persistent medical conditions—they are there to cater for the ordinary run of illness to which detention centre boys are no more immune that the rest of their age group.

The detention centre system, like the rest of the prison system, must and does cope flexibly with a wide range of individuals. It is possible within the centre regimes to devise individual programmes involving light work and light physical education to cope with the less fit. So, while it is important that the courts should take into account the physical and mental condition of offenders in deciding whether a detention centre order is appropriate, it is important not to regard any mental assessment made before sentence as being the irrevocable last occasion upon which all depends.

This is not a question of coping with mistakes. After all, a boy diagnosed on sentence as fully capable of meeting the full demands of a detention centre at a medical examination which took place before his sentence may quite well subsequently develop a condition that renders him unsuitable. The system must be sufficiently flexible to cope with such changes without damage to the individual, and it is designed with this need firmly in mind.

The hon. Member for Ormskirk (Mr- Kilroy-Silk) gave figures on Tuesday of boys transferred from 148 detention centres on physical grounds and I am told that they amount to less than 7 per cent, of those received under the pilot project, to which he referred.

On the point that I have already made of the undesirability of remanding more young people than is necessary to either remand centres or adult prisons for the purpose of medical examinations, it may help the Committee to know that in 1980 1,863 persons aged under 21 were remanded in custody for medical reports. Of that number, 1,524 were held in remand centres and 339 in local prisons.

It is important that the numbers of young people remanded for that purpose be kept to the minimum. In particular, of course, the court may be minded to impose a very short period of detention centre custody of three weeks, and it could well be that the entire custodial period would be taken up, having regard to remission, in the time spent on remand for medical examination. I must therefore advise the Committee and my hon. Friends to resist the amendment.
