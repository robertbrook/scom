Mr. Kilroy-Silk
: On Thursday my hon. Friend the Member for Halifax (Dr. Summerskill) chided the Minister for having announced that there would be an increase in the prison building programme but that the result would not be an increase in the total number of places. I and many of my hon. Friends do not want an increase in the total number of prison places. We are at one with the Minister in that particular objective and are in slight disagreement with my hon. Friend.

What we want is certainly an increase in prison building to replace the outdated and obsolescent conditions in many of our urban prisons, but we do not want to see an overall increase in places. We want to see a reduction in the prison population. These amendments, though not designed specifically to achieve that, will certainly—if they are accepted and implemented—lead to a reduction in the numbers of those sent to prison. The amendments stipulate that a court should impose a custodial sentence on a young offender only if he has shown himself unable or unwilling to respond to non-custodial penalties, or if a custodial sentence is necessary for the protection of the public. I move these amendments on behalf of the parliamentary penal affairs group, whose recommendations appear in its report "Young Offenders: A Strategy for the Future", published in July 1981, which these amendments closely follow.

There is now general agreement among all those working with young offenders that there must be a major shift of emphasis and resources away from treating them in custodial conditions towards more emphasis upon treatment and care in the community. 40 Indeed, the Advisory Council on the Penal System's major report, entitled "Young Adult Offenders", published in 1974, called for a major change from custody for those aged 17 to 20 to supervision within the community. Following that, the report of the Expenditure Committee, entitled "Children and Young Persons Act 1969", published in 1975, also called for a major shift of emphasis and resources away from custodial and punitive techniques towards intermediate treatment, supervision and nonresidential care in the community. Indeed, the whole weight of research evidence, both from this country and from abroad, from within the Home Office and from outside, suggests very strongly that we are likely to be far more successful in dealing with the real—sometimes impractical problems—of young people if we deal with them in the community. We are more likely to reach our objective of ensuring that they are law-abiding and constructive members of the community, not likely to commit further offences, if again we deal with them not in institutions but try to solve their problems where they have their origin, where eventually they have to be resolved, in the community in which they live. As the Advisory Council on the Penal System's report on "Young Adult Offenders" said in reviewing all the evidence, both from this country and abroad, the time spent in custody whether it was longer or shorter, did not produce any significantly different results in terms of re-conviction rates for the offender. Indeed, it said that the length of prison sentences or custodial sentences was very largely irrelevenat in terms of the effect upon the behaviour of the young person, once released.

Since that report was published in 1974, reconviction rates have worsened quite significantly. In the last five years, for example, the proportion of young adults leaving prison who have committed further offences within two years of leaving has risen from 64 per cent, to 68 per cent. Sixty-eight per cent, of young adults sentenced to a term of imprisonment now commit further offences soon after their release. The proportion of those sentenced to borstal training committing further offences has risen in the last five years from 63 per cent, to 69 per cent., but most striking and most alarming of all, is the increase in the numbers of boys sent to detention centres, those who are now being subjected to the short, sharp shock and the drill parades and inspections. In the last five years the recidivist rate of those boys sentenced to detention centre has gone up from 54 per cent, to 68 per cent.

What the Advisory Council on the Penal System was telling us in 1974, what the Expenditure Committee was reiterating and reaffirming in 1975, seems to have been borne out by the evidence we have subsequently of the reconviction rates of those juveniles and young people sentenced to some form of custodial treatment.

The figures for juveniles which were quoted and used quite considerably last week are even worse. Seventy-six per cent, of the under-17s leaving detention centres commit offences again within two years of release. That says very little for the efficacy of the present methods of dealing with young offenders. The whole point and purpose of the short, sharp shock is to point out to them, supposedly, the error of their ways and to put them on to a more con- JPEG 41 structive and useful path. Yet on the evidence of the sole criterion of whether or not they are reformed or rehabilitated, whether or not upon leaving they commit further offences, those institutions have clearly been demonstrated to have failed to an amazing and alarming extent. That should cause everyone to want to review in great detail, and with great speed, what has gone wrong with our penal system that puts so many young people into institutions for such relatively long periods of time and seemingly to so little effect.

Eighty-three per cent, of those leaving borstal are reconvicted within two years. These horrible, horrendous—some would say brutal and dehumanising— institutions seem to hold such little fear for those who are incarcerated in them that they are re-offending again within a short time of leaving the institutions. That perhaps ought to suggest that we are not really dealing with the problems of these juveniles and young people in the most appropriate and efficient manner. To some extent it must surely raise at least a question mark that we are not doing the right thing if we are trying to achieve the objective that we all allegedly share, that we are not using the most appropriate methods and that perhaps other means would be more efficient, appropriate and successful.

Despite the fact that all informed opinion seems to be on the side of non custodial measures, and that all the evidence shows that that is likely to be far more successful than incarceration in institutions, the trend in the last decade has been precisely the opposite. It is a myth to suggest that following the Children and Young Persons Act 1969 fewer juveniles have been put in institutions. It is a myth to suggest that they have been taken over by social workers. In fact, precisely the opposite has happened. There has been a sharp increase in the numbers and proportions of juveniles sent to detention centres and borstals. The Government in their White Paper " Young Offenders,'" in paragraph 39, referred to this disturbing trend.

Let me give some figures. The number of juveniles sentenced to borstals and detention centres was 3,000 in 1970, but rose to 7,500 in 1980. In the same decade, the number of juveniles given supervision orders in 1980 was 4,000 fewer than those given probation orders in 1970. That clearly shows that in that decade we have been less ready to supervise young offenders in the community, less ready to deal with them by more imaginative and potentially successful schemes in society, and far more ready to lock them up and send them away, out of sight and mind, into institutions.

I acknowledge that there has been an increase in juvenile crime during that period, but it is relevant to point out that the rise in the number sentenced to penal institutions has increased faster than the rise in the number of juveniles coming before the courts in that period. The Government may want figures. I have no doubt that the hon. and learned Member for Solihull (Mr. Grieve) will want to challenge them, given the slightly irrelevant figures that he quoted last week that made the argument more for prevention than for an increase in sentencing sentences.

Although juvenile crime has increased, it has increased at a slower rate than those sentenced to penal institutions, and a larger number of juveniles convicted of indictable offences are now sentenced to custodial sentences than in 1970. The number has doubled. It has increased from 6 per cent, of those convicted of indictable offences sentenced to custodial care in 1970 to 12 per cent, in 1980. In the same period, there was a drop in supervision orders from 22 per cent, to 16 per cent.

Let us look at young adults. We do not find the same dramatic increase in the use of custody for young adults, but it is also fair to point out that there has been little progress towards dealing with young adults in the community as called for by the Advisory Council on the Penal System in 1974. In all the years that have elapsed since that major and important report was published, we have done little in relation to young adults to implement its sentiments and arguments. We have done quite the reverse in relation to juveniles. Far more young adults now receive immediate prison sentences and there has been a decline in the use of probation orders.

I accept that it is necessary to lock up certain juveniles and young offenders. Of course, the public must be protected from those who are more violent and aggressive and there will always be a continuing need for some kind of custodial facility to cater for 44 the small minority of violent and dangerous juvenile and young offenders. I accept that, but I do not in any way allow it to detract from my main point which is demonstrated overwhelmingly by the evidence. In this country we have an enormous propensity for the over-use of custody, not just for juveniles or young people, but for adults and children too, in all spheres not only in the penal system but also in respect of mental health and mental illness.

The Powers of Criminal Courts Act 1973 laid down that an offender under 21 cannot have a prison sentence passed upon him unless other methods can be shown not to be appropriate. If he is given a prison sentence, the court must state why it has decided on that particular option. This stipulation applies only to those under 21 who are to be subjected to a sentence of imprisonment. Clause 1 now extends that same principle to all custodial sentences for young people. This in itself is to be welcomed and applauded. However, like many other things in the Bill, the Government have gone only part of the way. For some reason or other they have stopped short of the proper full-blooded implementation of a proposal that would have the effect that not only I and the all-party penal affairs group but the Government want as well. A recent editorial in the Criminal Law Review said on page 753:

"The Government proposes to use a formula which even its own advisers recoginse to be ineffectual."

 The reference was to a comment by Mr. Michael Moriarty of the Home Office on the formula in the Bill, which is in the Powers of the Criminal Courts Act 1973. In evidence to the Expenditure Committee on 19 June 1978 he said:

"There is not very much evidence that it has greatly affected actual sentencing practice."

 That view of the Home Office official responsible for these measures, before a Select Committee of this House has also been supported by an examination of the whole question of giving reasons in magistrates' courts carried out by Brian Harris, who is the president if the Justices' Clerks' Society. His examination appeared recently in the journal Justice of the Peace.

This amendment is an attempt to provide a formula that will affect the actual sentencing practice. Indeed, Mr. Dean, it will come as no surpirse to you or to the Minister that the words of the amendment reflect almost exactly the words used in paragraph 3 of the Government's White Paper "Young Offenders". This contains what I believe to be an important statement of the Government's view of circumstances in which custodial sentences might most appropriately be imposed on young offenders. It says:

"The Government is doing as much as possible to encourage the development of non-custodial facilities so that the court may continue to have a range of alternatives sufficient to ensure that an offender is given a custodial or residential sentence only when he is a real danger to society or has shown himself unwilling or unable to respond to non-custodial penalties, or, in the case of a juvenile, is in need of care and control that he is unlikely to receive at home."

 The last of those three criteria is relevant to care orders rather than to custodial sentences, so it does not feature in this amendment. With that qualification, the amendment is a faithful reflection of the Government's own stated view of the circumstances in which custodial sentences should be imposed on young people.

JPEG 45 All too often, unfortunately, sentencing practice at present does not correspond either with the sentiments expressed in the White Paper, laid down in the amendment or, indeed, with what is enjoined by the Powers of Criminal Courts Act 1973. Again a myth seems to have got around that courts pass a custodial sentence only as a last resort, whereas if one examines the evidence they seem all too ready to use a custodial sentence inappropriately when other non-custodial measures might have been more sensible and beneficial, both to the offender and to the community.

For example, a Home Office study of young persons received into borstal between 1 July and 31 December 1977 showed that 35 per cent, of them had never previously received a supervision order or a probation order and that 35 per cent, of them went straight to borstal without there being any previous attempt to deal with them effectively in the community. Again, it was true of 78 per cent, of a sample taken by the Home Office of boys entering junior detention centre in that same year. Seventy-eight per cent, of them had not previously been dealt with in the community by supervision or by probation.

Again, the overready use by which the courts use custodial sentences at a very early stage in a young person's criminal career is demonstrated by the fact that 17 per cent, of juveniles in detention centres in 1980 were first offenders, 46 per cent, of juveniles entering detention centres in 1980 were only second or third time offenders, and 50 per cent, of those entering borstal in 1980 had two or fewer previous convictions. There does not seem, from that evidence, to be any real reluctance on the part of the courts to pass custodial sentences. There does not seem to be, on that evidence, any suggestion that the courts are passing custodial sentences only when they have explored and exhausted all other alternatives within the community.

The Bill itself provides two further and extremely powerful arguments in support of the amendment, one of which is relevant to the youth custody sentence and the other to detention centres. I should like first of all to deal with youth custody.

Clause 10 guarantees that a training place will be available only to those who receive sentences of between four and 18 months. Those who are given youth custody sentences of under four months or over 18 months will not necessarily or, indeed, are unlikely, to achieve any sense of training. They will not get a place where a training regime is in existence. Yet, as everyone will acknowledge, those who are given the longer sentences, over 18 months, are probably in greatest need of a training regime, because they are more likely to be subject to institutionalisation by being in there for longer periods of time.

They will also be cut off from whatever family and friends they have for a longer period than those sentenced for a shorter period of time. Therefore, it would seem to be far more important to give them the element of training than those sentenced to shorter sentences. Indeed the parliamentary all-party penal affairs group in its report "Young Offenders: A Strategy for the Future" recommended that training should be available for all those sentenced to youth custody—education, vocational training, training in acquiring social skills and so on.

We recognised, however, that the element of training that could be provided would be dependent upon the actual numbers involved. Here we have an opportunity in the amendment to reduce the numbers and, therefore, to try to ensure that the training regimes will be available to all those sentenced to youth custody, not just to those sentenced for periods of between four and 18 months. This amendment has, therefore, another powerful advocate in the Bill itself.
