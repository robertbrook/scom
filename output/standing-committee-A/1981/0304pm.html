clause Clause 26

4.30 pm

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Minister of State Home Office (Mr. Patrick Mayhew): We had a thorough and serious discussion of an important and sombre question and I was giving the Government's reflections upon it when we adjourned this morning.

Before proceeding, perhaps I should give a fuller account of the nature of clause 26 than I was able to give this morning. Then I shall come to my response to the amendment of my hon. Friend the Member for Ilford, North (Mr. Bendall).

Clause 26 seeks to provide the Government with emergency powers to be used in the event of a breakdown in our present system. The emergency nature of these powers has not been fully brought out in the course of our short discussion today. We know, all too well, from the overcrowding which exists, and from the neglected state of prison buildings, that it is a prospect for which we must make contingency plans. I stressed that this morning and it is common ground in this Committee.

My hon. Friends are worried that use may be made of clause 26 powers that goes beyond emergency use. I understand on the face of the clause as it is drafted that that could take place and that it could include the release of those who have been convicted of offences of violence against a person, provided that they had only six months or less to serve of their sentence.

Clause 26 requires the Government to set out the categories of prisoners who may be released up to six months before their normal release date. The primary test of the seriousness of an offence—the measure applied by the courts—is the length of the sentence that they think it right to give.

I have to acknowledge, however, that the clause is so drafted that it is the remainder of a sentence that is the criteria.

The Government have had an opportunity to reflect on what had been said by my hon. Friend and I am grateful to him. Earlier I said that the power Col. 422 conferred by clause 26 would not be used to secure the release of what are loosely known as violent offenders, as the Home Secretary had previously affirmed.

I also see the force of what he said about the fact that an assurance given by one Government does not bind another. Friend can find form in legislative language.

I am grateful, anyway, for the Government's decision to accede to our request and to postpone proceedings.
