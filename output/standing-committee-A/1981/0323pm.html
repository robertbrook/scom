MR. PAUL DEAN in the Chair

Criminal Justice Bill

New clause 44
CORPORAL PUNISHMENT

"(1) Any person who shall—

  1. unlawfully assault or beat any other person;
  2. make use of provoking language or behaviour tending to a breach of the peace; or
  3. commit an act of criminal damage

    shall be liable on summary conviction in addition to, or instead of, current penalties, if the offender is a male child or male young person, to be whipped.
  1. The expressions "child" and "young person" mean respectively an individual of or over the age of 10 and under 14 and an individual of or over the age of 14 and under 18
  2. The instrument used shall, in the case of a child, be a cane, and in any other case shall be a birch rod.
  3. The court in its sentence shall specify the number of strokes to be inflicted, being in the case of a child not more than six strokes, and in the case of any other person not more than 12 strokes.
  4. The whipping shall be inflicted privately as soon as practicable after sentence.
  5. The whipping shall be inflicted by a constable in the presence of an inspector or other officer of police of higher rank than a constable or by some other person appointed by the Court, and, in the case of a child or young person also in the presence if he desires to be present of the parent or guardian of the child or in the case of a child or young person also in the presence if he desires to be present of the parent or guardian of the child or young person.
  6. The instruments to be used shall be—
    1. in the case of a male child who is under the age of 14 years a light cane not exceeding four feet in length and not exceeding half an inch in diameter; and
    2. in the case of a male person who is over the age of 14 a birch rod of the following dimensions:
    Weight not exceeding 9 ounces
    Length from end of handle to tip of spray 40 inches
    Length of handle 15 inches
    Circumference of spray at centre 6 inches
    Circumference of handle at top of binding 3½ inches
    Circumference of handle 6 inches from end 3¼ inches
  7. In all cases where a court is empowered to impose a sentence of whipping a medical report as to whether the offender is fit to receive the punishment will be made available to the Magistrates before they consider sentence.
  8. The whipping shall be inflicted on the posterior over the child's ordinary cloth trousers.
  9. A medical practitioner shall be present during a birching and may at his discretion order the stopping of the punishment at any time.
  10. Where a birching has been stopped on medical grounds a report of the facts shall be forwarded immediately to the Home Secretary.".—[ Mr. Hawksley.]

The important factor about that admission is that it tends to animate our discussion about corporal punishment in this country. Corporal punishment has never been a punishment in other countries in Western Europe. In Western Europe they regard it as the English vice that one should beat people, particularly children. The reason why it has got into our code of law is that it became an institutional part of the public school and got into our penal system as a result. I think it has got into the public mind that somehow one can beat criminals into being better, because it has been handed down to them from that tradition. There is no evidence whatsoever for that proposition.

In the period since 1948, serious crime, in particular violent crime, has risen. It is also true that since the abolition of the death penalty in 1965 it has risen. It is not a logical connection that it has risen because of that abolition. In the Isle of Man, in the period when birching was taking place between 1950 and 1972, indictable, serious crime rose by 300 per cent, from 743 offences in 1950 to 2,262 in 1972. Crime did not rise by any more than 300 per cent, in this country in that period.

It is important that the public should recognise, not only Conservative Members—it is perhaps not so important that they should recognise it—that one cannot solve serious criminal activity and a serious in 742 crease in criminal activity by simple expedients of changing the nature of punishment. It is a vast, complex problem about how we bring up our children, educate them and raise them in standards of morality, but also how we police our society. There is a good deal of evidence to indicate that the best deterrent is the certainty of being apprehended, not the kind of punishment that is handed out on conviction. The policing element is crucial to how this is done.

Over recent years our detection rate has fallen very considerably, not least in the last two years when more resources have been put into the police and more policemen available in a city like London. Therefore, we have to find better ways of policing immediately— that is the best deterrent—and also look at the way we treat our children.
