New Clause 9
ACTUARIAL EVIDENCE IN CERTAIN ACTIONS FOR DAMAGES

" 13.—(1) Where—

  1. in an action under the Fatal Accidents Acts damages are claimed in respect of future pecuniary loss; or
  2. in an action for damages for personal injuries damages are claimed in respect of future pecuniary loss (including future pecuniary loss consisting of the reasonable value of any services to which section 5(1) of this Act applies, being services that would probably have been rendered after judgment),
then, subject to any relevant rules of court and without prejudice to any power or discretion of the court as to the costs of or incidental to any proceedings, subsection (2) below shall apply in relation to that claim.

(2) For the purpose of establishing the capital value, as at the date of judgment, of any future pecuniary loss to which the claim relates, or the capital sum which at that date represents the reasonable value of any services to which the claim relates, any party to the action shall be entitled to adduce and rely on any admissible actuarial evidence; and where any such evidence is relied on for that purpose, the court shall have due regard to it in assessing the damages claimed.

(3) The Lord Chancellor may, after consultation with such persons or bodies of persons as appear to him requisite, by order approve for the purposes of this section any actuarial table or set of actuarial tables which in his opinion merit such approval; and any such table or set of such tables that is for the time being so approved shall, as regards any claim in relation to which subsection (2) applies, be admissible in evidence for the purpose mentioned in that subsection in so far as it is relevant for that purpose.

For the purposes of this subsection any notes or other explanatory material issued in conjunction with any actuarial table or set of actuarial tables shall be treated as part of that table or set.

(4) The power of the Lord Chancellor to make orders under subsection (3) above shall include power to revoke a previous order and shall be exercisable by statutory instrument.".— [Mr. Archer.]

4.30 pm

Mr. Peter Archer
(Warley, West)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The new clause deals with a problem that has troubled the legal profession for many years. The courts normally welcome the evidence of experts and have no difficulty in understanding or handling it. Part of the expertise of judges and lawyers is to understand the expertise of others. However, when the need arises to arrive at a view about the future—for example, in order to assess damages for loss of expectation of life or for the future effects of a 84 personal injury—they need to know such matters as the average expectation of life of a person having characteristics of the plaintiff or how long he might have been likely to have remained in work had he not sustained the accident, what his prospects were of remaining in good health, what income tax he might have been likely to pay and all the eventualities that affect individuals in this world of ours. In particular, if they wish adequately to reflect what they wish to reflect, they need to assess future inflation.

Those matters fall within the expertise of actuaries. Of course, it is true that by the standards of older professions that profession is a relatively new arrival, but its members possess a real expertise which is much in use in the age in which we live and is indispensable to such commercial activities as insurance. Yet the courts have been most reluctant to hear actuarial evidence. In the case of Mitchell v. Mul-holland in 1971, it was held

"that any element of certainty obtained by use of actuarial evidence, when applied to future contingencies, resulted in such an imprecise mode of assessing damages for future earnings as to present no advantages over the conventional method which was the best primary basis of assessment".

More recently, in the case of Auty Mills Rogers v. National Coal Board, reported in The Times of 1 July 1981, Mr. Justice Tudor Evans, a judge very experienced in matters of assessing damages, for example, of personal injury, held that he could not admit actuarial evidence of likely future inflation in a claim for the loss in value of a pension.

What is the alternative to that method of assessment? The answer is that it is an informed guess. It is usually said to consist of the multiplier and, basically, that involves the judge making an informed guess about the likely length of the working life of the plaintiff and then discounting something for the vicissitudes of life. He will consider the likelihood that the plaintiff will die or be killed in an accident, be incapacitated or contract a disease. That is what actuaries do. That is not adopting an alternative method of assessing damages but retaining the same method and doing it less scientifically and accurately. It is simply rejecting the expertise of actuaries.

An actuary attempts to predict the likelihood of those things happening in the future. Of cuorse, they do not guarantee accurately to predict what will happen. They look at what has happened in the past in relation to specific factors and consequences and then do what scientists have always done. They assume that unless something happens to the contrary, past trends will continue into the future. Unless there is some reason why the trend should be altered, it is likely to continue. The sun rose yesterday morning, it rose this morning, and the probabilities are that it will rise tomorrow morning.

The Chairman
: The moon was eclipsed yesterday.

Mr. Archer
: I am sure that the Institute of Actuaries is aware of that. I confess that I was not, but I am not an actuary.

Actuaries avail themselves of human experience and use the past as a guide to the future, but they do it precisely and with expertise. If I may adopt JPEG 85 the words of Aneurin Bevan, " Why look in a crystal ball when you can read the book?"

In fact, the courts' arguments frequently seem to be that because the future is uncertain and because we cannot be 100 per cent, accurate in our predictions, we should abdicate any attempt to predict. Of course, it does not simply rest there. We know that the courts' informed guesses are usually extreme ely moderate. If we cannot be sure, it seems always better to err on the side of moderation. The effect in practice is that damages for personal injuries compare most unfavourably, for the victims, with damages, for example, in the United States.

There is an economic argument for keeping damages low. Damages paid for industrial accidents, for example, are one of the costs of industrial production. One way to keep down production costs is to decide that the damages for industrial accidents should be low. But that should be a conscious decision of the House after proper debate. It should not be done by suppressing the evidence when pretending to be deciding something else.

The Pearson Commission, as in so many cases, turned its attention to this problem but decided to leave the matter, because the Law Commission was considering it. But the Pearson Commission ventured in paragraph 655 to make this comment:

"The majority recognise that no method of lump-sum calculation can achieve mathematical perfection. But they consider that use should be made of such practical assistance as mathematics can provide."

The Law Commission considered the question at much greater length and reported in 1973—report No. 56. It recommended the use of actuarial evidence when appropriate. It pointed out that such use is not a novelty suddenly foisted on us in the latter half of the twentieth century. It quoted Lord Blackburn in the case of McDonald v McDonald in 1880. Since I would not presume to paraphrase or improve on Lord Blackburn's language, perhaps I can weary the Committee for a moment by quoting it. He said:

"But I think the Legislature knew that the value of an expectancy must, in a great degree at least, depend on the probabilities of the duration of life, the chances of marriage, and the chances of such marriages proving fruitful. They must, I think, have known that actuaries had tables, founded on extensive experience, on which they acted, which enabled them to value with considerable accuracy the probabilities of life: and that though the experience on which the calculations as to the probabilities of marriage and issue were based was much narrower, and the results more subject to uncertainty, yet that some calculation could be made in that way and none could be made in any other. I think therefore that the Legislature must have contemplated that the Court would call in the assistance of an actuary to report to them on all those matters which properly come within the province of an actuary."

We are not asking more than that.

The Law Commission went further. It proceeded to draft a clause which it thought would be appropriate in some future Bill to effect what it had in mind. This is that clause.

The Solicitor-General (Sir Ian Percival)
: I hope that the right hon. and learned Gentleman will not think me discourteous if I do not adopt the argu- 86 ments against the new clause which he put forward for the purpose of knocking it down. Neither singly nor collectively do they represent my grounds for resisting it.

In the early 1970s, a sub-committee of the Law Reform Committee and the Law Commission both said that they thought that more use should be made of actuaries. The Bar was active on the Law Reform Committee's sub-committee. However, time has passed since then and views have changed. The Pearson Commission, as the right hon, and learned Gentleman said, did not endorse the Law Commission's recommendation. It gave a cautious and tentative support to the possibility that further use be made of actuaries. One suggestion was, I believe, that the court might call an actuary who would then assist the court as far as an actuary could.

Much thought has been given by those who would have to operate the provisions since the Law Commission made its suggestion. The result of that thinking is that, far from leading to accuracy or unanimity, busy practitioners at the Bar feel strongly that such use of an actuary might be misleading, because the actuary has to work on factors which have to be estimated by the judge. The damages will depend, in many cases, on the judge's estimate of the number of years of life that the plaintiff has lost. No one can know that until the judge has decided it.

The value of actuaries in the run up to the trail, therefore, is much lessened where that is one of the factors, because they will have to guess what the judge will say. I have heard it described as giving an impression of accuracy, just as it gives an impression of accuracy to calculate the area of a circle by multiplying the radius by IT to four decimal points, but that if there is an error of 25 per cent, in measuring the radius the calculation will not be half as accurate as it appears. Here, one must start by estimating the loss of expectation of life. If that is wrong—how one gets it right I do not know—the supposed accuracy goes for a Burton.

There is a case before the court on appeal—about which, therefore, I must not say too much—in which one side called an actuary whose evidence took five days. I doubt whether that added much to the simplification of the matter.

The practitioners feel that, if it becomes the practice to call an actuary, as soon as it is know that one side is to have an actuary the other side will feel that it must have one, too. That is understandable. Then there will be two lots of experts, both supposedly doing a precise mathematical calculation but based on figures which have, in the first place, to be estimated. The view is strongly held that that would make settlements much more difficult.

That does not mean that there is not scope for better use of actuarial tables. They are used so that, once the judge has made his findings on the basic facts—which must be known before the tables are used—the court can in some cases turn to the tables and make good use of them. There is every JPEG 87 reason for the practitioners and the actuaries to get together to see in what respects, if any, those tables could be improved so as to be of greater assistance to the parties and to the court in determining difficult questions.

I do not think that I am wrong in thinking that this is a probing new clause. I am sure that the right hon. and learned Gentleman believes that, before legislating on the matter, we should need lengthier debates than are possible on the Bill. Therefore, I have limited my remarks, for the moment, to objections in principle and the current views of the legal profession. There is sufficient reason, even based on the little I have said, to say that the matter is unsuitable for the Bill. We shall want to return to the issue later, and I should like to see members of the legal profession in close consultation with the actuaries, to see how much the tables can be improved. Despite that, I suggest that the matter is not suitable for inclusion in the Bill, and I invite the right hon. and learned Gentleman to withdraw the new clause.

Mr. Archer
: I agree with the Solicitor-General that we have been debating today basically two categories of new clause—those which have been called preliminary canters round the course, and those which we might call gallops round well-worn trenches. I accept at once that this measure is in the former category. I am grateful for the invitation of the Solicitor-General, and I accept that there should be further discussion before I attempt to' persuade the Committee that the clause should be accepted.

I feel bound to say that I was not wholly persuaded by what I understood to be the difficulty expressed by the Solicitor-General. Of course, an actuary cannot proceed until he knows the assumptions on which he is required to proceed. But there are two answers to that objection. First, having made the presumptions, we proceed unscientifically. Even having measured the radius accurately, we multiply it by two and a half times π and so we do not get the area correct.

The second answer is that it is part of the expertise of an actuary to assist us with our assumptions. When a judge takes a figure for loss of expectation of life, he does not pluck it out of air. He wants some assistance from past exprience, and that is where actuaries can help him. But I accept that the precise use which can be most profitably made of actuarial evidence must depend upon discussions between the Institute of Actuaries and representatives of the profession. I gathered from what the Solicitor-General said that the profession would be happy to meet the institute for such discussions. With that preliminary canter, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the motion.

Motion and clause, by leave, withdrawn.

'2A. Mr. Archer
: I beg to move, That the Clause be read a Second time.

This is another new clause about magistrates' courts committees. For the benefit of any member of the Committee who was not present this morning, I ought perhaps to explain briefly that a magistrates' courts committee is a committee of magistrates which decides what is required by way of court accommodation, office facilities, staff, training, training for justices, and which generally services the courts in a particular location. The Committee will recollect that that was the subject of new clause 5.

The magistrates who serve on those committees are elected by their own benches. Before 1974, each city and county borough had its own magistrates' courts committee. County benches had a separate committee, usually consisting of representatives of the rural courts in the county. East magistrates' courts committee dealt with one local authority. It emerged from discussions this morning that there are already sufficient problems in the relationship between the magistrates' courts committee—whose function it is to call for expenditure—and the local authority which has to bear that expenditure if one magistrates' courts committee is dealing with one local authority.

Some cities and towns found, in the Local Government Act 1972 which amended local government boundaries and which some of us remember to our intense displeasure, that their magistrates' courts committees had been abolished. The city bench was represented by the county magistrates' courts committee. So the county magistrates' courts committee found itself dealing with the diverse problems of city benches and rural benches, and a number of local authorities. There has been some feeling, too, subsequently among city benches that representation of the cities on magistrates' courts committees was disproportionate to the size of the population that they served or the amount of work that they did.

I am not voicing any local complaint from my constituency, which is a metropolitan district. Metropolitan districts have their own magistrates' courts committees. That is one of the anomalies and one of the things that surprised the non-metropolitan districts, because they could not see the distinction between them and the metropolitan districts for this purpose. The proposal was not made by the Lord Chancellor's advisory committee. Certainly, it does not meet with the approval of the Justices' Clerks Society, which would like to see things done differently.

I make this proposal to deal with a specific case which came to my hon. Friend the Member for Crewe (Mrs. Dunwoody), on which she then asked my advice. May I first simply recount the facts? The new clause would replace section 2 of the Law Reform (Personal Injuries) Act 1948. At present, a person who claims loss of earnings in a personal injuries claim must deduct half the amount that he has received from social security for his claim for loss of earnings. The reason is that he must not be over-compensated and receive benefit from the State and from the tortfeasor for the same accident.

There has been a growing voice over a number of years for the whole of the benefits to be deducted in a claim for loss of earnings. I quote the much-mentioned Pearson report of 1978, the Government's 1981 White Paper and the Solicitor-General himself, who said on Second Reading that the Government 98 had not yet decided whether to offset the full amount, but that clause 5 of the Bill sought to reduce the claim by the amount paid to or for the benefit of, an injured person whilst being treated in an institution.

New clause 12 seeks to offset the full amount of social security benefits against the loss of earnings claim. It also ensures that the claimant receives the amount that he contributed towards that benefit. These figures can be worked out easily on a day-to-day basis because the assessment is made on the fiscal year.

Paragraph 341 of the Pearson report's recommendation relating to subsection (4) states in part:

"If a plaintiff decides to seek private treatment for his injuries when the same treatment would have been equally available under the National Health Service, we do not think that the defendant can be reasonably expected to meet the cost."

We include subsection (4) in the new clause to ensure that the treatment that the patient receives is treatment that is within reasonable limits. We have a Welfare State and a National Health Service. I submit that the Health Service should be used on that basis.

New clause 13 deals with a principle, namely, the right of recovery of public funds. It is, in effect, a declaratory clause. The National Health Service should be just that. It is a national service and should provide free medical treatment to all.
