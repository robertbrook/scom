Mr. Kilroy-Silk
: Since the introduction of community service orders in six experimental areas in 1973, I think that they have proved to everybody concerned to have been a viable and constructive alternative to custody, and a sensible non-custodial penalty that has been available to the courts. However, although in passing the legislation, the Home Secretary and various Ministers gave a clear impression that they assumed that community service orders would be used as an alternative, where otherwise a custodial sentence would have been imposed, research carried out by the Home Office research unit suggests that perhaps only 40 or 50 per cent, of community service orders have been imposed where otherwise a custodial sentence would have been the case. The figures demonstrate this fairly convincingly. In the last decade, there has been an increase in community service orders imposed on those in the 17 to 20-year-old age group. At the same time, there has been a reduction in the number of probation orders passed on that age group.

Amendment No. 82 stipulates that a community service order may only be imposed if otherwise the court would have imposed a custodial sentence. As I said earlier, this was indeed the notion that was in the Home Secretary's mind in instituting the Criminal Justice Bill in 1971, which had within it the provision to make community service orders. I understand that the courts like to feel free to make their own decisions as to the appropriate penalty in a specific case with a particular offender. As we have heard reiterated several times in these debates, we must give as much discretion, flexibility and freedom of manoeuvre to the courts as possible; but at the 484 same time it is both fair and reasonable to suggest that this measure, which was conceived as an alternative to custody, is currently being misused, as evidence from both the probation service and the British Association of Social Workers demonstrates, and that therefore every effort should be made to bring it back to what it was originally conceived to be. That is, an alternative which will be imposed when otherwise the court would impose a custodial sentence.

One could argue, at one level, that if unlimited resources were available, there would not necessarily be a great deal to complain about in community services orders being given to offenders who would not attract a custodial sentence. I do not necessarily accept that argument, because that then tends to lower the esteem of the community service order in the eyes of the courts and those outside. It brings it down the tariff.

That is the fear of the probation service and the social workers, in relation to the extension of community service orders to 16-year-olds. However, it is not the case that with unlimited resources we could use community service orders for those who did not attract a custodial sentence. We know that the probation service in many areas is having to tell the court that it cannot supervise any further community service orders because of lack of resources in those areas. That in itself is regrettable. Perhaps the Minister will indicate to the Committee today what action is taken to ensure that in such areas there is no inhibition on the freedom of discretion of the courts to pass community service orders.

Given that there are limited resources, it seems sensible to ensure that those resources are concentrated where they are most needed—and where they are most needed is in offering an alternative to custody and therefore ensuring that community service orders are applied so far as is possible only to those offenders who would otherwise have been sent to prison.

This is a point made by the British Association of Social Workers in its comments made available to every Member of the Committee. Equally important, it points to the different interpretation that courts place on community service orders. In some areas they see them as an alternative to custody, whereas in others they see them as another noncustodial alternative. The association points out that the different interpretations reflect badly on sentencing practice and can lead to injustices, and adds:

" We are aware of one area where every effort is made by courts and the probation service to use the sentence as an alternative to custody and 120 hours of community service is regarded as an acceptable alternative to a ' short custodial sentence '."

We take this to mean three months imprisonment. It continues—

" However, in another part of the country there are suggestions that people have been sentenced to 240 hours who would otherwise have been fined."

We all know that is the case. That is the price we have to pay for giving the courts the discretion JPEG 485 that Conservative Members seem to think is so wonderful and sensible. Giving them that kind of discretion leads, as BASW has pointed out, to injustices and anomalies where in one area a community service order is only given if otherwise a sentence of imprisonment would have been imposed yet in another area it would be used as an alternative for other non-custodial alternatives. It is unfair and unreasonable and it goes against the whole principle and intentions behind the introduction of community service orders which was to try to divert people from penal establishments and instead make them provide a constructive contribution to the community by being made to work in the community.

I also support amendment No. 52 tabled by my hon. Friends on the Opposition Front Bench which makes a similar stipulation to my amendment No. 82 in relation to 16-year-olds. As I have already indicated, like NAPO and BASW, I support an extension of community service orders to 16-year-olds that is provided for in the Bill and that was a recommendation of the all-party Penal Affairs Report "Young Offenders—A Strategy for the Future ".

I am aware of the fears of the probation service, or at least some members of the probation service— not all and certainly not the chief conference of probation officers. I am aware, too, of the fears expressed by BASW. It is slightly unfortunate that both those organisations, which are constantly quite rightly clambering for a more humane and senstive penal policy and always suggesting a greater use of non-custodial measures should, every time that one is proposed—whether it be, as in this case, a community service order extending to 16-year-olds or in the case of automatic release on parole—suddenly throw up a whole series of objections from their particular vantage point, their vested interest.

It is unfortunate and regrettable that those two organisations should always seek on these occasions to take a narrow perspective of what is regarded by everyone else to be a radical and sensible proposal aimed at reducing either the prison population or the number of people incarcerated in institutions. It is suddenly not so attractive if it happens to affect their particular vested interests. I do not accept the arguments advanced by the probation service that the extension of community service orders to 16-year-olds will bring it down the tariff and, therefore, to some extent undermine the viability and credibility of the community service orders and make them less attractive to the courts.

The remedy is in the hands of the probation service and individual probation officers. They, as I have indicated before, are responsible for making the recommendation to the courts, and the courts are required to take their recommendation into account when considering whether or not to impose a CSO.

However, the amendments would obviate those fears. If amendment No. 52 were accepted, so that a community service order could only be imposed on a 16-year-old as a direct alternative to a custodial sentence, it would clearly overcome all the fears that 486 have been expressed by both BASW and certain probation officers, stop the community service order slipping down the tariff, prevent the undermining of its credibility and validity in the eyes of the court and make a sensible contribution to penal policy. I hope, therefore, that the Minister will look favourably on both the amendments.

I agree with what the hon. Gentleman said at the outset—that the success of the community service order has proved most encouraging. I also agree that it has as its primary and general purpose the provision of an alternative to custodial sentences, and that it is important that it should not, as it were, slip down the tariff. But I part company with what was implicit in his speech—that there are no circumstances in which it is proper for a community service order to be made as an alternative to another non-custodial sentence.

I think that I have fairly indicated the intention of the hon. Gentleman's proposal. We have some sympathy with its objective, but there are difficulties in legislating in this way. The CSO was introduced in the 1972 Act to provide the courts with an alternative to a short custodial sentence. Since then, the scheme has grown rapidly. National coverage was achieved early in 1979. In 1980, more than 22,000 offenders were given orders—a figure which represents 4 per cent, of all those sentenced for indictable offences. These offenders all committed imprisonable offences, and it is believed that many of them would otherwise have gone into custody. We therefore have the community service order to thank for the fact that a considerable number of offenders have not received custodial sentences but have instead remained in the community and made constructive and practical reparation for their offences.

Speaking from memory, I think that in the last full year there was an increase of 40 per cent, in the number that the courts made. That indicates the confidence of the courts in the way in which the community service orders are working. I pay tribute to the part played in that by the probation service.

That community service has achieved these results owes a great deal to the efforts of successive Governments to encourage the courts to regard it as an alternative to custody. Most hon. Members will be 488 aware of the booklet, " The Sentence of the Court" and the fact that it was produced by the Home Office and is widely consulted by sentencers. The section on community service orders says:

"The community service order was introduced with the primary purpose of providing a constructive alternative for those offenders who would otherwise have received a short custodial sentence … It was the emphasis both on the Advisory Council on the Penal System in its report on Non-Custodial and Semi-Custodial Penalties … in proposing the introduction of community service, and of Parliament in giving effect to the recommendations of the Council, that community service should be seen as a penal sanction that made serious demands on the offender and could thus be regarded as a viable alternative to a custodial sentence."

Like our predecessors, we have taken every opportunity to stress the possibility of using CSOs as an alternative to custody, but to go further and to impose a statutory requirement that courts should not use CSOs other than as an alternative to cutsody would, I think, be to overstep the mark that is desirable. We are faced by the issue which has already arisen several times in the Committee— the hon. Gentleman has referred to it—of the extent to which we should confer or restrict a discretion exercisable by the courts. This is always a matter for careful judgment, but I believe that the amendment, quite apart from its uncertain practical effects, would err on the side of undue restriction.

We also ought to consider the effect that the amendment would have on offenders who, having breached the requirements of a CSO, are dealt with afresh for their original offence. Of course, it is to be hoped that as many orders as possible will be successfully completed, but we must accept that there will always be some failures, and at present these offenders do not always automatically receive custodial sentences when they are brought back to court. But if the amendment were passed, the courts might feel obliged to pass a custodial sentence whatever the special or extenuating circumstances, and I think that is a consequence that all members of the Committee would regret.

That is not to say that there must be any slackening of what is said—the warning that is given to an offender, when sentenced to community service, that if he breaches it he will be dealt with for the original offence, which is, of course, an imprisonable offence. However, there should not be thought to be an automatic requirement that custody shall be the result in those circumstances. The same argument might also be applied to a subsequent offence.
