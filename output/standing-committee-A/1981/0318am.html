PROHIBITION OF REMANDING OF JUVENILES TO ADULT PRISONS

"(1) Subsection 5 of section 22 of the Children and Young Persons Act 1969 is hereby repealed.

  1. In section 23(1) of the Children and Young Persons Act 1969, the words ' subject to the following provisions of this section ' shall be omitted.
  2. Subsections (2) and (3) of section 23 of the Children and Young Persons Act 1968 are here repealed.".— [Mr. Kilroy-Silk.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Robert Kilroy-Silk
(Ormskirk)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time. This new clause would prohibit the remanding of school children to prisons and remand centres to await trial or sentence. This is a subject with which I have been involved since I first came to the House. Indeed, it was the discovery of the existence of several thousand school childen who were remanded to remand centres and adult prisons in 1974-75 that first brought me into the whole area of penal affairs and reform. I was very untutored and ignorant then, as I no doubt am now, in legal and penal matters and obviously extremely naive. I was horrified when in pursuing a constituency case of a burglar who was alleged to have been held on remand for nine months without trial—which I thought at the time would have been inconceivable—I discovered that it was not only true of that particular burglar but that he was one of many thousands.

I also discovered what was obviously not new to other people, that there were several thousand school children remanded every year into remand centres and adult prisons. It was that discovery which involved me in the path which leads me to being on this Committee today.

At the moment, courts are empowered to remand juveniles to Prison Department establishments by virtue of section 23 of the Children and Young Persons Act 1969. That particular section stipulates that when a court remands or commits a juvenile, if it is satisfied that he is of so unruly a character that he cannot be safely committed to the care of a local authority, then it shall commit him to a remand centre or, if no remand centre place is available, to a local prison.

In 1980, the last year for which figures are available, 3,372 juveniles—school children between the 628 ages of 14 and 16—were remanded or committed to Prison Department establishments. Well over one quarter of these were subsequently found either not guilty or received a non-custodial sentence.

That is an important point to note. It is not within the power of our courts to actually sentence a school child of 14 or 16-years-old to prison on conviction, but it is possible to remand them in custody in adult prisons while they are awaiting sentence or trial. It is an extraordinary state of affairs. Given the fact that something like a quarter of them are eventually found not guilty, or to have committed an offence that does not warrant a noncustodial sentence, it is highly offensive that they should have spent time, in effect, serving a prison sentence.

The latest figures which are available relate to 31 December 1981, given in answer to a parliamentary question on Monday, and show that on 31 December there were 178 untried and unsentenced juveniles in prisons and remand centres, of whom 21 had first been remanded in custody between two and four months previously, a further nine more than four months earlier, one juvenile in a prison had been remanded in custody 233 days earlier and one juvenile in a remand centre had first been remanded in custody 189 days earlier. Those figures are not just a criticism of the fact that juveniles are actually spending time in custody in a remand centre or an adult prison, but also that on many occasions they spend very long periods of time in those adult prisons or remand centres.

It is not as if it is a matter of one or two days. The latest figures demonstrate that on many occasions it can be several months that children are being held in the highly inappropriate locations of remand centres and adult prisons.

In 1975, the House of Commons Expenditure Committee chaired by the hon. Member for Plymouth, Drake (Miss Fookes) in its report on the Children and Young Persons Act said in paragraph 23:

"We condemn in the stongest possible terms the use of certificates of unruliness as a means of achieving secure accommodation. We recommend that the practice of remanding young persons to adult prisons should cease forthwith; alternative arrangements must be made"

That was in 1975, when an all-party Committee of this House condemned in the strongest possible terms the use of certificates of unruliness, saying that the practice of remanding such children to adult prisons should cease worthwith and that alternative arrangements should be made.

In expressing that view at that time the Expenditure Committee was reflecting the views of most of those involved in working with juvenile offenders. Indeed, it is now universally accepted that those custodial remands are an indefensible scandal and that there is no necessity to remand a juvenile to an adult prison or to a remand centre. If secure conditions are regarded as necessary and desirable, they should be found in a secure place in a local authority community home, staffed by those specialising in work with children, rather than in a Prison Department establishment.

JPEG 629 Not only are these children—and that is what they are called in the Act—being held in inappropriate locations where they should not be held, and it would be more sensibe to put them into local authority homes with specialist staff, but they are also being held in the worst of our Prison Department establishments

It is a fact that remand centres and local prisons, which invariably will be the institutions that will house the juveniles, also happen to be the oldest of our prison service establishments, the ones where there is the most overcrowding and the most appalling physical conditions. To put children into those conditions is both a disgrace and a scandal Very often, because of the small numbers of juveniles detained, there will be only one or two in the local prisons. For example, on 31 December 1981, there was one 16-year-old boy alone in Canterbury prison, and one alone in Lincoln prison. As these juveniles are not allowed to mix, for very good reasons, with adult prisoners, it means that they are being contained alone without proper access to other peope, and without access to educational or training facilities because they do not exist for 16-year-olds in local adult prisons or remand centres. They are also, like the adult prisoners, being locked up for 23 hours out of every 24. That is an additional dimension that we must take into account. It is not just the fact that they should never have entered a remand centre or adult prison in the first place, it is also the compounding of the offence by the fact that the conditions in the particular institutions happen to be as deplorable as they are.

Juveniles have the same general right to bail as adults. The 15 and 16-year-old boys upon whom this debate focuses will have been refused bail because the courts have substantial grounds for believing that they would fail to surrender to custody, commit an offence while on bail or interfere with witnesses. There must be good reasons for the court to have refused them bail in the first place.

In such cases, the provisions of section 23 of the Children and Young Persons Act 1969 is that they should be remanded to the care of the local authority, but in the case of the boys we are talking about, the courts will have certified as to their character that they cannot safely be committed to the care of a local authority.

The issue of certificates of unruliness has since 1977 been subject to very strict conditions. The boy must be charged with an offence punishable in the case of an adult with imprisonment for 14 years or more, or with an offence of violence, or have in his history a previous conviction for an offence of violence. In addition, a court must be satisfied on the basis of a written report from the local authority that no suitable accommodation is available for him in a community home where he could be accommodated without substantial risk, either to himself, or to others, or the court has to be persuaded that he has persistently absconded from, or has previously disrupted the running of, a community home. I think that those are fine gauge filters.

These are, therefore, boys charged with very serious offences for whom both the court and the local authority agree that a remand in care is not suitable. I do not think that we ought to approach this without taking into account the practical problems that confront the local authorities dealing, as Parliament has said they normally should, with boys of this kind of character.

Since April 1981, about five-sixths of juveniles remanded to Prison Department custody have been held in remand centres, not in prisons. We cannot solve the problem of dealing with them by simply depriving the courts of their powers to remand in custody and depriving the local authorities of their power to apply to have a difficult boy remanded in custody. Each of them has a duty to ensure that the public is, where necessary, given some degree of protection and to ensure that the juvenile attends court for the trial or sentence in his case. We must work towards a solution which makes allowance for these duties.

Our view has been made clear that there will always be a minority of juveniles for whom a custodial sentence is inevitable. In spite of that, we remain committed to a policy of trying to make sure that unconvicted or unsentenced juveniles are not 636 held in custody. We took one step last March, which has been referred to today, ending the remand in custody of 14-year-old boys. Our next step will also have to be cautious, provided we can take it only when we are satisfied that proper alternative provision can be made and that these boys can safely be committed to the care of a local authority. We want to work towards that.

It has been argued that remand in custody cannot be justified because many juveniles are eventually found not guilty or receive non-custodial sentences. It is easy enough to say that after the event, but the courts are here dealing with an essentially predictive decision. They do not know at the time of the remand that the ultimate result of the case will be a predictive decision about whether the offender will turn up for trial or sentence, or will get up to further trouble to himself or other people while on bail. The courts have to base their decisions on substantial grounds but they cannot be clairvoyant. They have to do the best they can on the facts available to them at the time, and it is no help to that process to point out that in the event things sometimes turn out otherwise.

I hope therefore that I have been able to show that our approach to this new clause is not different from, but is wholly consistent with, the approach which led us to release boys of 14 from liability to be remanded to prison, which we were able to do last March. Our approach is consistent but it has to take account of the practical considerations facing the courts, the most important of which is the consideration of safety. I therefore hope that the clause may be withdrawn.

Mr. Kilroy-Silk
: The new clause would make the offences of sleeping rough and begging non-imprison-able. This, as hon. Members of the Committee will know, was a recommendation of the Parliamentary all-party penal affairs group in its report " Too Many Prisoners " published in 1980. It was also a recommendation of the all-party Home Affairs Select Committee in its report "Vagrancy Offences " published last year which stated in paragraph 26:

"We are convinced that imprisonment is neither a necessary nor an appropriate penalty for minor offences of this type. Though we accept that the criminal law must continue to play a limited role in controlling the activities of beggars and vagrants, we consider that it is quite wrong that they should be faced with even the theoretical possibility of imprisonment on these grounds alone, and we therefore recommend that both sleeping rough and begging should cease to be imprisonable offences "

A minority of the Select Committee members, myself included, favoured going much further than that and repealing what were regarded by a minority of the Select Committee as Dickensian offences which punish and label as criminals people with fundamental problems of poverty and homelessness. It seemed to us and to those who signed the minority report that the real solution for these people, who are not criminals, have no criminal intent or propensity, and are very often extremely vulnerable, inarticulate and disadvantaged members of the community was to try to deal with their needs within the community rather than to publish their inadequacies by a period of imprisonment. That was certainly the view of the majority, but it was not 642 the view of the majority that all penalties for begging and sleeping rough should be abolished.

There are four main reasons why these offences, for which in 1980 150 people were sentenced to immediate terms of imprisonment, should be made non-imprisonable. The first is the effect that it may have on the prison population. Not a significant number are involved, though some of them with their difficulties pose disproportionate problems for prison staff. Any reduction in the prison population, however marginal, is to be welcomed. I have the Home Secretary's support for that. In a speech on 21 March 1980 to, of all people, the Conservative Central Council, he said:

"We shall need … to see that the prisons are not allowed to remain cluttered up with trivial and inadequate offenders who are no real threat to anyone, except possibly themselves, but whose presence helps to create the extremely acute pressures which afflict our prisons today and all who work in them."

I endorse what the Home Secretary said then and he will, no doubt, endorse the desire to end the imprisonment of beggars and vagrants.

Secondly, the minor degree of annoyance that is caused to the public by sleeping rough and by begging is not sufficiently serious and certainly not dangerous enough to warrant a draconian punishment. The offences in the Vagrancy Act are designed to protect the public from minor forms of nuisance, embarrassment or annoyance, rather than to protect the public from substantial harm, damage or danger. If members of the public are annoyed or embarrassed it should be incumbent upon them to be prepared, as in the case of soliciting by prostitutes, to appear in court and testify that they were personally annoyed, offended, disturbed, or whatever by the defendant's behaviour. I am saying that such offenders should be caught by the ordinary law that covers the rest of us if we annoy or offend our fellow citizens.

Thirdly, I believe that imprisonment is not, and cannot be, a deterrent, and cannot have any real deterrent value for vagrants. Anyone who is not already deterred by the extreme discomfort of the way of life of a vagrant will not be deterred by the threat of a period in prison. Indeed it is well known—and sometimes this is advanced in support of the retention of the penalty of imprisonment— that many vagrants actually welcome a period in prison as a short respite from the rigours and hardship of life outside.

I accept that that is the case, but I do not accept that as a reasonable argument for continuing the penalty of imprisonment. It is, I think, a sorry commentary on an allegedly compassionate society that the only way in which we can respond to the homelessness and the social and personal inadequacy of many of our fellow citizens is to resort to imprisoning them in the overcrowded and deplorable conditions of our local prisons.

The fourth and most important reason why imprisonment should be ended is that vagrancy is a social problem and it requires social solutions, which imprisonment does nothing to provide.
