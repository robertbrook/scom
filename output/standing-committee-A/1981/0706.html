Clause 3
AMENDMENTS OF FATAL ACCIDENTS ACT 1976

Mr. Peter Archer
(Warley, West)
: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 4, line 11, after "husband", insert "or unmarried minor child".

The first two groups of amendments relate to the proposal to replace the claim for damages for loss of expectation of life by an action for bereavement. We understand the reasons for the proposal and we approve of it and support it. But we have two criticisms. If the damages are limited to a conventional figure, and that is the subject of the next two amendments, it follows that the more relatives there are to share that sum the less there will be for any one person—the less cake there will be to go round. The two groups of amendments go together.

Our colleagues from Scotland are not troubled by this. I asked my hon. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Garscadden (Mr. Dewar) what was the view of both the Law Society of Scotland and those who have considered these matters on behalf of Scotland. He replied that they do not have a view.

In any event the figure will appear derisory if one takes a small conventional sum and then divides it among the family. If the family is extremely large the division of the sum may well border on the ridiculous. However, that is the only reason that I understand for excluding children from the range of beneficiaries. Will the Committee bear in mind that amendments Nos. 2 and 3 would remove that objection? We propose that damages be enlarged so that the number of claimants will not prejudice the amount awarded to any of them.

Why should no claim be made in cases of a child's bereavement? Does not a child feel bereavement? A very young baby may not be capable of feeling the loss, but a slightly older child may feel it more than anyone else because his parents may be his whole world.

4 That is the case in a nutshell. It has been fully deployed in correspondence and in debates in another place and, I do not believe that it will be improved by repetition. I hope that I have explained it clearly. The onus is now upon those who maintain that there is a reason for excluding children from the range of beneficiaries. I do not believe that any process of logic will reinforce the case for the amendment.

The Solicitor-General (Sir Ian Percival)
: I agree with the right hon. and learned Member for Warley, West (Mr. Archer) that the problem cannot be dealt with as a matter of logic alone. Choices must be made.

Assuming that we were to abolish damages for loss of expectation of life and that a new head of damages should be introduced—I do not believe that anyone would quarrel with those assumptions—the Government had to choose from three courses of action. They could follow the Scottish system, the recommendations of the Law Commission or those of the Pearson Committee, which sought a middle way. Deciding which was preferable was a matter not of logic but of judgment.

The right hon. and learned Gentleman asked why damages should not be awarded to a child and why should they not be awarded at large. Such damages are intended to assuage grief. The provision is an experiment, because some people say—I see the force of the argument—that grief cannot be assuaged by money. They argue that to try so to assuage grief is immediately to enter difficult country. However, the Government decided to try to arrive at a satisfactory head of damages.

The awarding of such damages to children would not make sense. There may be something in the argument put forward by the commissions that the grief of a person's wife, husband or parent may be assuaged in part if the party responsible for his or her death is ordered to pay damages. However, the argument does not run with children. One cannot measure even hypothetically, a child's grief. Does the likelihood of grief vary with a child's age? Furthermore, unless we introduced another wholly new principle, we should not assuage a child's grief by awarding damages, because the sum awarded would be paid into court and managed until he became an adult. For those reasons, we do not believe that it would be appropriate in such cases to award damages to children.

On the question of whether one should have damages at large or the conventional sum, the argument is short but none the worse for that. If one had damages at large, each case would have to be dealt with on a case-by-case basis. That would necessarily involve examining how much grief there was in each instance. That is an unattractive proposition.

The view is that—I accept that it is a matter of judgment rather than logic—it is better to have a conventional sum of a reasonable figure, not a derisory one, which will be paid regardless of the particular circumstances of a family. That will avoid examining the family's circumstances. The chosen sum is that recommended by the Law Commission of £1,000 in 1973, uprated to take account of changes. That is around the figure that the Pearson Committee recommended.

Those are the Government's reasons for choosing that course. I do not claim that they are conclusive in logic or on any other basis. That is the Government's JPEG 5 judgment on those two points and it has the full support of the Law Commission.

Mr. Archer
: Mr. Solicitor-General appears to have deployed two arguments. First, if damages for bereavement are intended to assuage grief, a child's grief will not be assuaged because he does not understand the concept of money. As I understand it, the purpose is not to assuage grief but to compensate for it. I believe that there is a difference. We could not assuage grief by an award of damages. Of course we cannot approach the problem by asking a hypothetical question "What sum would a reasonable person agree to accept in exchange for agreeing in advance to his loss?". That cannot be a possible approach over a whole range of subjects with which the courts are confronted daily. One cannot ask what sum would be accepted by a reasonable person for the loss of an eye in a factory or road accident. I accept that the matter cannot be approached in that way. However, it should not follow that one cannot ask any questions at all.

Every day the courts assess damages for injuries which cannot be measured financially and which clearly have no market value as one cannot do a deal about them. They give damages for pain and suffering and for loss of amenities of life. It is also true that no damages could ever adequately compensate for the loss of a member of one's family. How would we begin to explain to a non-lawyer that, because the loss that has been suffered is beyond all compensation, the compensation that is awarded should be substantially less than would be awarded for a much less serious injury? Those who have suffered the loss will not be thinking first of the damages which they may recover. But it does not follow that it is wrong or undignified to turn grief into money. If we recognise that there is to be compensation for bereavement, it ought at least to be commensurate with the bereavement. I would find it easier to swallow if the conventional figure were substantially higher—if it bore some relation to the scale of damages for all the other injuries which one individual can inflict upon another or to the scale for the loss of an eye or an arm.

I would prefer the courts to approach each case on its merits. There are some questions that one cannot ask. There cannot be a scale of how much grief has been felt. However, certain questions can be asked: what are the circumstances, what other relatives are there to offer consolation, how far will the person involved be left completely alone and to what extent will he be deprived not only of the society and the companionship of the victim but be reliant on a total stranger for ordinary personal services? I fully agree with Mr. Solicitor-General that we probably are not going to convince each other by any process of logic, but I felt it right to deploy that case for the amendment.

The Solicitor-General
: I deliberately linked the main point of the Government's argument on this amendment with what I was saying on amendment No. 1. They go together as packages. The right hon. and learned Gentleman puts forward one package and I am putting forward another.

I shall not repeat what I said then; I just add this. The right hon. and learned Gentleman rightly said that the courts already have the difficult task of putting a price on something that has no price. That is bad enough when one is dealing with personal injuries where there is often an inquiry into whether the alleged pain and suffering is genuine. Can we really contemplate extending that into the field of grief? Can we really contemplate having to say as the representative of the defendant that the grief is not genuine—to say "They were at each other like cats and dogs for years". The Government believe that we should not introduce any new head of damages that would lead to that kind of inquiry—one which might result in awful squabbles over grief.

We are discussing damages for grief. I see an argument that it is a head of damages that should not exist, but I also see the argument that it is one that we should try. However, I simply could not stomach extending it to a head of damages under which the parties will be open JPEG 7 to inquiries as to whether the grief is genuine. The Government's view, in line with that of the Law Commission and with the majority of the Pearson Committee, is that if one is going to compensate one must take a reasonable round figure and then forget that in some cases it might be compensation for grief that was not actually felt. That risk has to be taken. However, one cannot say that grief is beyond price and that a high figure must therefore be put upon it, because some people may, indeed receive money to which they are not entitled. We are assisted in trying to strike a balance by the views on appropriate sums expressed by the Law Commission and in the Pearson report.

Do not let us enter into the inquiries to which the amendment would lead. Let us give the provision a fair run and see how it works, in the hope that it will be of some comfort to people who lose a husband, wife or child.

I had looked forward to hearing the Solicitor-General on the subject of the civil law of Scotland but that pleasure will clearly be denied to us. We shall have to make do with his Scottish counterpart.

The Solicitor-General for Scotland has become an itinerant among Scottish Ministers, a hermit crab of politics cropping up in all sorts of unexpected places. He is usually added to Committees suddenly and not as a bona fide member. However, I am glad to see that he is here in his own right as a member of the Committee. He, like me, will no doubt have to sit through all the debates on new clauses relating to England and Wales.

It is appropriate that this will be something of a Scottish debate, because the Bill is a hybrid. Although it deals largely with law reform in England and Wales, some of its provisions relate peculiarly to Scotland and are of real importance and interest for Scottish law. Proper consideration of such hybrid Bills is extremely difficult.

I do not criticise, but it is worth noting that no Scottish Member, and therefore no one with a special incentive to deal with Scottish matters, sat on the Second Reading Committee. The reference by the Solicitor-General to the Scottish part of the Bill occupies 12 lines in c. 6 of the Official Report. It was a mere nod to the existence of provisions for Scotland. No attempt was made to argue the case for the drafting of the provisions.

I have made those preliminary remarks because some amendments, of which No. 47 is a good example, raise general points of principle concerning part II of the Bill which could usefully have been aired on Second Reading. I have outlined the reasons why they were not. I hope that the Committee will bear with the fact that we have not hitherto had the opportunity of tabling these broad, probing amendments.

The amendments are to clause 8, which deals with "Services rendered to injured person". No one suggests that the provision will ultimately be drafted as suggested in the amendments. However, the intention is to substitute "third party" for the key limiting word "relative". There has been much debate by correspondence—a theme running through many of the amendments— between the Law Society of Scotland and the Solicitor General's Department about changes that the society wishes to see. This amendment, like many others, is based upon the views of the Law Society, which I share in this particular at least.

Let us start not with Pearson, for once, but with the Scottish Law Commission's 1978 report entitled "Damages for Personal Injuries Report on (1) Admissibility of Claims for Services (2) Admissible Deductions". We shall come to the admissible deductions later. Paragraph 20 on page 9 of that report sets out the basic argument behind the limitation of the provision to relatives. Although it may seem presumptuous to criticise the reasoning of the Law Commission, on some occasions one must screw up one's courage and be presumptuous. I find the argument in paragraph 20 extremely unsatisfactory.

I suppose that we must all try to be pragmatic and it seems that the Committee started with a prejudice. It had decided that it wished to limit to relatives the head of claim relating to services rendered to injured persons. Paragraph 20gave me the impressionthat the Committee JPEG 9 had set out to find arguments to justify the conclusion that it had to reach for reasons of expediency. I do not necessarily mean that pejoratively—perhaps "convenience" is the better word.

The admission in the paragraph is most helpful to my argument:

"While such a restriction"—

that is to relatives—

"might lead to anomalies between the treatment of persons rendering identical services, one of whom is within the family group and the other without it, in the absence of such a restriction the class of persons whose services must be taken into account might include, according to the circumstances, such bodies as hospitals, ambulance services, the police and fire service."

The report goes on to spoil that point:

"It would, no doubt, be possible in the relevant legislation to allow only private individuals to present claims."

The fact that that would, indeed, be possible, seems largely to take out the earlier argument. A contorted argument follows, in which the Commission seeks a moral basis for limiting the head of claim to services offered by a relative as distinct from someone else:

"Services rendered by persons within the family group are often motivated by a high sense of duty, and in order to render them members of the family may be prepared to make considerable sacrifices, including leaving their employment."

I have no doubt that that is true. Often members of the family are

"motivated by a high sense of duty".

The report then goes on to belittle that "high sense of duty" by saying:

"But they may expect, in the long run, to receive some benefit as a counterpart, though not necessarily a benefit of a tangible nature."

I am not clear what the benefit would be if it is not "of a tangible nature." I suppose that it may be the love and affection of the husband. However, that is taking the argument into a very difficult area in which to weigh the substance of the point. It is knocking copy to say that the unfortunate relative is

"often motivated by a high sense of duty"

but is really doing it because he expects something in return—even if that something is not "tangible."

The report continues and states that the unfortunate person who has rendered the service which may be the subject of the head of claim but who is outside the family is doing that service:

"in a spirit of disinterested philanthropy without any prospect or even thought of benefits in counterpart."

There is the unlikely distinction that a wife is seen as some sort of cunning vixen and when she gives up her job to nurse her husband it is because she expects something in return; when a non-relative does that, it is because of some good Samaritan act without any expectation of return, and the law should therefore see that they do not get anything in return. That is an immoral argument that smacks of remarkable experiences. It runs counter to arguments used by the Law Commission in respect of clause 9, and I find the argument remarkably unimpressive.

We know that that does not square with what Pearson said. I do not want to weary the Committee and outstay my welcome. We remember paragraph 344 of the 10 Pearson report with its quotation from the case of Donnelly v Joyce. I shall quote briefly from paragraph 346, which concluded:

"We would not ourselves wish to exclude the recovery of damages in respect of services rendered gratuitously by someone outside the victim's family group. On the approach in Donnelly v. Joyce the damages are awarded for the plaintiff's need to have services rendered to him. It seems to us that this need is not different in character if it is met by a friend rather than a member of the family."

That is absolutely right. There can be no doubt that all the attempts to meet that moral distinction are based on shifting sands and I do not believe that they can be seriously maintained. The Minister will know that the Law Society of Scotland has been hammering at his door and has sent a memorandum with regard to part II of the Bill which has been fairly widely circulated. That is a quotation from paragraph 346 in the Pearson report, as mentioned previously.

The Law Society continues—this is its own view—

"The committee strongly supports this proposal and can see no good reason for the limitation of the award to the cost of a relative's services."

Why was what appears to be the evident equity of a broader definition of a person giving services in regard to clause 8 not adapted when the clause was drafted in the inner recesses of the Scottish Office?

That sets out briefly the arguments as I see them. Will the Solicitor-General say a word or two? We can then take the matter from there.

    New Clause 1
    ASSISTANCE BY RECORDERS FOR TRANSACTION OF BUSINESS OF HIGH COURT

    At the end of the Table in section 9(1) of the Supreme Court Act 1981 (under which certain persons may be requested to act as judges in specified courts) there shall be added—

    "6 A Recorder. The High Court"— [The Solicitor-General.]

    Brought up, and read the First time.

    The Solicitor-General
    : I beg to move that the clause be read a Second time.

    The purpose of the new clause is to restore to recorders the eligibility to sit as High Court judges if so requested by the Lord Chancellor, which they had under section 23 of the Courts Act 1971. That eligibility was removed by section 9 of the Supreme Court Act 1981 and the repeal by the same Act of section 23 of the Courts Act.

    The reason for making the change in 1981 was because it seemed inconsistent with the terms of his appointment to require a recorder to sit for longer than the terms of his appointment allowed. The scheme of work devolution provided by section 9 of the Supreme Court Act 1981 is for the work of one permanently appointed judge to discharge in certain circumstances the functions and business of another on a different rung of the judicial ladder. It does not provide for the discharge of work by recorders who are not permanent judges, but practitioners who have agreed to sit as recorders on a certain number of days a year.

    The result of the change has been to prevent recorders from sitting as High Court judges. Barristers who happen to be recorders may continue to be appointed as deputy high court judges, but that possibility lias never been open to solicitors, nor has it ever been suggested that it should be.

    The change has had little effect in practice because 40 solicitor-recorders have never been requested to try High Court cases. It appears, however, that solicitor-recorders have in the past been known to take interlocutory applications in defended cases or wardship cases. This very exceptional practice was no doubt of value to the parties in the cases where it occurred and this is one reason why the Government have changed their mind and are now moving to restore recorders to where they were before.

    The other reason is that the changes made by the Supreme Court Act were not specifically drawn to the attention of the Law Society either before or during the passage of the Bill, and it is said by the Law Society, and accepted by the Government, that they would have wished to object to the change on the ground just mentioned, that solicitor recorders have in the past been able to help out with matrimonial applications in High Court cases.

    The Government are grateful to the Law Society for taking the matter up and the amendment is the result of the combined effect of all those considerations.

    Mr. Barry Porter
    (Bebington and Ellesmere Port)
    : The gratitude of the solicitors' side of the profession should be placed on record for the sensible way in which the Government have approached this and put right a matter which had apparently slipped through unnoticed.

    Question put and agreed to.

    Clause 3 read a Second time and added to the Bill.
