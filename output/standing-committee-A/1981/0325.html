Criminal Justice Bill

New Clause 47
POWER OF COURTS TO COMMIT OFFENDERS TO OTHER COURTS FOLLOWING DEFERMENT OF SENTENCE

'Where a magistrates' court has deferred passing sentence on an offender under section 1 of the Powers of Criminal Courts Act 1973 for the purpose mentioned in subsection (1) of that section, the court may, instead of passing sentence itself, commit the offender to the Crown Court for Sentence in accordance with Section 56 of the Criminal Justice Act 1967 or any of the enactments to which that section applies.

(2) Notwithstanding anything in subsection (2) of section 1 of the Powers of Criminal Courts Act 1973, the Crown Court may defer passing sentence in accordance with that section on an offender who has been committed to the Crown Court for sentence in accordance with section 56 of the Criminal Justice Act 1967 or any of the enactments to which that section applies following a deferment of sentence by the magistrates' court.

(3) In section 1(8) of the Powers of Criminal Courts Act 1973, the words "does not extend to committing him to another court for sentence but, subject to that" shall be omitted.'.— [Mr. Kilroy-Silk.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

10.30 am

Mr. Robert Kilroy-Silk
(Ormskirk)
: I beg to move, that the clause be read a Second time.

This new clause would enable a magistrates' court which has deferred passing sentence on an offender to commit him to the Crown court for sentence at the end of the period of deferment. Like many other new clauses that I have moved in the Committee, it embodies a recommendation of the all-party Penal Affairs Group in its report "Too Many Prisoners". It has the support of the Justices' Clerks' Society and of the Magistrates' Association.

Section 1 of the Powers of Criminal Courts Act 1973 gives the courts powers to defer the passing of a sentence on an offender for up to six months, to enable the sentence to be deferred for up to six months, in order to enable the court to have regard to the conduct of an offender after he is convicted and to take into account whether he has made any reparation for his offence or whether there has been any change in his circumstances.

Clearly, the decision to defer the passing of a sentence contains the clear implication that if at the end of the period of deferment the offender can show that there has been a change in circumstances or that he has made some form of reparation, or indeed, that a favourable report on the offender can be made back to the court, the court will, as a consequence, impose a more lenient sentence than it would otherwise have done had it been compelled to impose a sentence immediately rather than deferring it. Therefore, the power to defer sentence enables the court to have the option to impose less severe sentences than perhaps it might have felt obliged to impose if it had no alternative but to sentence the offender immediately.

778 In 1975, in the Gilby case, an 18-year-old youth was convicted of burglary. The magistrates deferred sentence, but when the offender came back to the magistrates for sentence to be imposed they had received a very adverse report from his probation officer. The magistrates therefore committed the offender to the Crown court for sentence with a recommendation that he be sentenced to borstal training, and indeed a borstal training sentence was imposed by the Crown court. On appeal, however, the Court of Appeal held that once the magistrates' court had deferred sentence it could not thereafter commit an offender to the Crown court for sentence. In 1977, the Criminal Law Act amended section 1 of the Powers of Criminal Courts Act 1973 in order to put this decision in a statutory form. New clause 47 would reverse that decision.

In some cases, as I am sure the Minister is well aware, the choice between the imposition of a substantial custodial sentence and perhaps the bold step of imposing a probation order is relatively narrow, or so I am advised, not having had experience in the courts in any form. I am told by the Magistrates' Association that despite appearances to the contrary, there can be quite a narrow dividing line between, on the one hand, the imposition of a fairly severe, certainly a heavy, custodial sentence and the imposition of a probation order.

Therefore, in such borderline cases, the magistrates say that they often wish to defer sentence where they would otherwise commit the offender to the Crown court with a view to the imposition of a substantial custodial sentence. They prefer to defer it to see whether they can actually avoid a custodial sentence because of some potential change in the offender or his circumstances or the implementation of some promise of reparation or of another kind made by the offender at the time of his trial. However, the Crown court cannot ignore the possibility that the offender will not live up to his promises, in which case it may consider that a substantial custodial sentence is justified.

The result of the Gilby case is that in those circumstances the courts cannot defer sentence without losing the option of committing the offender to the Crown court at the end of the period of deferment. As the Justices' Clerks' Society pointed out in its statement "Sentencing in the 1980s":

"The effect of the present law is to discourage magistrates from deferring sentence in situations when it might have been most desirable to do so and to encourage magistrates to commit in custody for sentence forthwith when they might otherwise have wished to take a more lenient course."

It is, of course, possible that the Crown court would decide to defer sentence but, if it did, it has been held that committals to the Crown court should normally be in custody, and this period in custody may effectively kill the chances of a successful deferment because it increases the chances of the offender losing his job or whatever hopeful future development has induced the magistrate to defer passing sentence in the first place.

The only solution to all that, therefore, is to reverse the effects of the Gilby judgment, as this new clause would do, so as to allow magistrates to commit to the Crown court following a deferment. This would encourage magistrates to offer a chance of redemption more frequently than they perhaps are now able to do, and particularly to those offenders who might be on the brink of a substantial custodial sentence.

This is a matter of some technicality and fairly fine judgment. I am glad that the new clause has given us an opportunity to discuss the courts' power to defer sentence. Deferment is a useful power, the availability of which is not perhaps as widely appreciated as it might be outside the immediate circle of practitioners in the law.

Under section 1 of the Act, the Crown court or a magistrates' court may defer passing sentence on an offender with his consent for a period of up to six months from the date of conviction, but they may only defer passing sentence once. The aim is to help the court to select the appropriate sentence by taking into account the conduct of the offender after conviction, usually after some expected change in his circumstances of which evidence has been given such as, for example, obtaining employment, arranging accommodation, getting married, or something like that, or where the offender has promised to make voluntary reparation. In other words, the courts say, "All right. We have heard what you say and what is said about you. We think that for the moment we shall defer making up our minds as to what is the appropriate sentence until we see how you get on in the next two months."

But the section provides also that the offender may be sentenced by the same court or by a different court before the period of expiration of deferment if he is convicted of another offence during that period. It also prevents the court from committing an offender for sentence to another court once it has made the decision to defer passing sentence, and that is what the new clause is really about.

The new clause is designed to remove the effect of a ruling that was made in the case the Crown against Gilby, which prevented a magistrates' court which has deferred passing sentence from thereafter committing the offender to the Crown court for sentence at the end of deferment. That was a judgment the effect of which was subsequently incorporated in the Powers of Criminal Courts Act by an amendment made during the passage of the Criminal Law Act 1977.

I know that both the Magistrates' Association and the Justices' Clerks' Society continually feel that the effect of the Gilby judgment, now given statutory force, is to impose an unjustified restriction on the use by magistrates' courts of the deferment power. This is that this may lead to courts committing offenders to custody where they might otherwise defer sentence. The possible implications 780 of this for the prison population have also been noted by the all-party Penal Affairs Group, as has been mentioned, and have led it to recommend an amendment on the lines that we are discussing.

It is really a question of whether, having deferred and having taken into account how the offender has got on during the period of deferment, the courts are to be given back the power, which they normally have, to commit to the Crown court someone whom they believe needs more than the magistrates' courts are entitled to award.

In the case of Gilby, the Court of Appeal said that the decision to commit for sentence is based on the magistrates' consideration of the nature of the offence and the character and previous history of the offender. All the relevant data are available at the moment of conviction and if, in all the circumstances, the courts judge the case to be appropriate for sentence by the Crown court, it should commit for sentence and leave to the Crown court the decision whether to defer passing sentence, because the Crown court also has that jurisdiction.

New Clause 54
CAUTIONING OF OFFENDERS


'In every case where a person has not committed any similar offence before, he should not be charged with an offence which he admits until the police have considered cautioning him for the offence.'.— [Mr. Alexander W. Lyon.]

Mr. Alexander W. Lyon
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The system of cautioning is not sanctioned by any statutory backing, but is used widely throughout the police force to deal with people who have admitted their offences and either have not been in trouble before or are such unusual offenders that the police think it appropriate to administer a formal caution. The procedure has been built up to allow that caution to take place.

What has always concerned me is that cautioning is widely used in the case of children, but nothing like so widely in the case of adults. Yet it has always seemed to me appropriate to divert from the criminal prosecution system those who are not grave or serious offenders but have simply stumbled into crime on one or two occasions.

I tabled the clause as a basis of trying to press for a wider application of cautioning for adult offenders, or at least offenders over the age of 17. The figures for 1980 show that of 2V2 million offences, the number of offenders dealt with in that way was 107,000 males and 38,000 females. That represented 12 per cent, of all ages of male offenders and 23 per cent, of all ages of women offenders. Only four per cent, of male offenders and 12 per cent, of female offenders, however, were over 17. It is thus quite clear that the vast majority of police forces regard cautioning as a sanction that they can use for children but not adults—other than in motoring offences, where it is done on a much more informal basis and is not counted within the statistics.

There are a number of cases where a formal caution is all that is required. In the case of middle-aged respectable shoplifters, it would be perfectly possible, if the person were to admit the offence, to administer a formal caution by a superintendant. That person would probably never 796 come before a court. There is no reason why they should be dragged before the courts and humiliated, given the possibility of recurrence of that kind of offence.

I found it fascinating to look through the areas where cautioning is given in existing circumstances, which, as I said, is heavily weighted on younger offenders. A considerable proportion of those offences involve violence. It is clear that even on occasions of violence with children under the age of 17, the police often feel that it is not necessary to proceed further than a formal caution. If that is true of young offenders, it may be true also of older offenders.

One of the absurdities of the present row about criminal statistics, and in particular the row about statistics on violence, is that violence, just like rape, is capable of an infinite variety of circumstances—with rape perhaps less so than assault. If somebody pushes against a man in a pub deliberately, it constitutes an assault, but nobody could conceive that that was a very serious offence. In most cases it is not even reported. If it amounted to a little more than that it might be reported, but it should go to court if it is committed by a man who is of good character, has never been in trouble before, and does not look as though he will get into trouble again. In those circumstances, even in the case of violence, it should be possible to caution. It would certainly make a vast difference to the criminal court system if we could use cautioning much more extensively with adults.

Although on the odd occasion a curfew order may be breached with impunity and will not come to the notice of the police, if it happens with any kind of regularity during the period of the order, it is extremely likely—this seems to be the view of those in the probation and social services with whom I have had the benefit of discussing this, as well as with magistrates—that the young people concerned will bring themselves rapidly back to the notice of the police.

There is a major distinction to be drawn between somebody who deliberately breaches the order and goes back to his old ways, and the occasion where, perhaps, one slips down to the corner shop for a bag of crisps and then back home. That, of course, is a technical breach of the order, which should not happen, but is not the principal mischief at which the order would be directed.

Mr. Lyell

Dr. Summerskill

The responsibility for keeping the curfew as a condition of bail lies squarely with the defendant, whereas the responsibility for keeping the curfew that the hon. and learned Gentleman is suggesting will lie with a third party.

Mr. Lyell
: I can compare those two aspects. It is helpful of the hon. Lady to give that comparison, which reinforces the arguments in favour of the new clause. She is right in saying that in the case of bail the responsibility falls fairly and squarely upon the person who has been placed on bail. The responsibility for obeying a curfew order such as I suggest remains fairly and squarely with the person against whom the order has been made, but it is reinforced by the fact that the parent, by granting consent, also takes on a responsibility. There is, therefore, a dual responsibility.

In the case of bail, the person knows that if it is breached he may well find himself taken into custody, and to some extent there is perhaps an even more formidable penalty hanging over him for breach than in the case of the curfew order. I shall discuss enforceability in greater detail later. We do not wish to see those who would not otherwise have been sent to prison finding themselves sent there because they have breached the curfew order. Nevertheless, appropriate penalties will be available to the courts in the case of breach, to which I now conveniently turn.

Let us assume that offenders breach the order and are brought back before the courts. I will deal in a moment with the mechanism by which they may be brought back and with the question of police involvement. The clause provides that the courts shall have the power to resentence them for the original offence.

What would be an appropriate penalty in those cases? As a result of this wise and sensible Bill, a wide range of measures will be available. The child or young person would know that it would be possible for the courts to impose a custodial sentence, and I think that would have some deterrent effect—rather similar to going into custody in the case of bail. I think, and those with whom I have discussed the new clause and who support it think, that the kind of penalty that would be most appropriate would be either a fine or a sentence to a period at an attendance centre. Those would be highly appropriate penalties in such cases.

In the context of the fine—and I make this point against myself on one of the detailed aspects of the drafting of the Bill, which I thank the Home Office for drawing to my attention—under the general structure of the Bill the parent would have a responsibility for ensuring that the fine was paid. That is something that the court would consider carefully in the case of a child or young person who was brought back before it. The court would consider carefully JPEG 811 whether the parent, whose consent was a pre-condition of the making of the order, had sought to exercise his or her responsibilities, and whether it was in any way the parents fault that the child had kicked over the traces and breached the curfew order.

In cases where the parent had been lax in his duties or responsibilities, the fact that the fine had to be paid by him might well be a thoroughly appropriate reinforcement. In cases where the parent had done his or her best, it would be quite inappropriate, and the court would have all the power and flexibility necessary to deal with that. In that 812 context subsection (11) of the new clause as drafted would have to be deleted. It would not be wholly appropriate, since section 55 of the Children and Young Persons Act 1933 deals with that aspect. I understand that the drafting is not quite correct in the context of the new clause. However, it would be desirable that the parent, as well as the child, takes that consideration into account.
