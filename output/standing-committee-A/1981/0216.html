Mr. Alfred Dubs (Battersea, South): I beg to move amendment No. 57, in page 2, line 35, at end insert—

"(7A) Where a person is convicted or found guilty of an offence and he has not been legally represented, the court may not—

  1. make a detention centre order in respect of him; or
  2. pass a youth custody sentence on him."

Although, strictly speaking, the wording of theamendment is intended merely to ensure that no custodial sentence will be imposed on a young person unless that young person has been legally represented in court. I hope that the effect of the amendment will be to ensure that all young people appearing in court will have such legal representation. I have never met a member of the legal profession with whom I have discussed this problem who is not positive that legal representation should be available for all people— not just for young people—but, of course, I am concerned only with people under the age of 21.

The first experience of appearing in a court by a young person can be frightening and intimidating. Often young people are on their own, as it were, without any help or advice as to how they should conduct themselves or how they should plead. This may well have been preceded by experiences with the police which, on the basis of conversations I have had with people who have been in trouble, make one rather concerned about what the police do and say to young people who have been arrested and are due to appear in court.

Let me quote one or two instances that give full support to the belief that young people are desperately in need of legal representation when they get themselves into trouble of this sort. I have known instances where the police have come upon four young people doing something and have said " We'll have two of you " regardless of which of the four were actually doing any particular thing. Alternatively, the police have said, either in the street or in the police station, "You haven't got a previous record; you will get off lightly; you might as well plead guilty ". They have even said " Your mates have got a record; they will get into big trouble; you had better plead guilty to save them getting into more trouble ". A lot of evidence suggests that frequently when young people get into trouble with the police they are coerced into pleading guilty when they come into court. As they have no legal repre- Col. 110 sentation, and are not given the sort of advice that they ought to have, they inevitably get into trouble. They are convicted. They serve a sentence; it may not be a custodial sentence—it may be a fine—but they serve a sentence. They are then on a slippery slope to getting into further trouble thereafter.

This is all compounded by the fact that if the police know that the young people are going to plead guilty, the evidence frankly is sometimes rather sloppy. Any member of the Committee who has been in some of these courts will have seen that the evidence as given by the police would not stand the test of scrutiny by a solicitor if he were representing the young person concerned. Added to it, sadly, there is sometimes the sense of bravado that young people may feel about being arrested and appearing in a court. They may indeed boast of the offence that they have committed and give the worst possible impression of what they have done, rather than present themselves in a more favourable light.

The case is, therefore, fairly clear, but it is necessary to turn a little bit to what actually happens in terms of how many people have legal representation. As far as I have been able to ascertain, there are no adequate statistics on how many people do have legal representation, especially young people. One is advised to look at the figures that are given for legal aid. Of course, it does not mean that because a person has been refused legal aid he many not be legally represented. Such people can pay for legal representation themselves or their parents may. However, in practice, there is a close relationship between whether legal aid is granted and whether an individual has legal representation although, as I shall point out in a moment, it depends to some extent on the type of court at which the hearing is conducted.

Legal aid is granted usually depending upon the financial circumstances of the applicant. If the applicant is poor enough—and this would apply in the case of many of the young people we are talking about—then on those grounds legal aid should be granted. There are also, however, other grounds, for example, whether the offence is serious enough to merit legal representation and there are instances where legal aid has been refused because the offence is regarded by the court as fairly trivial. Even if it is a trivial offence in the eyes of the court, or the magistrate, in deciding whether to give legal aid, for a young person it may well be the first step on the path to becoming a habitual offender. I think the first experience of appearing in court, of being prosecuted and of being punished sets many people on the path to doing the same sort of thing again. One finds that legal aid may be refused on such grounds.

Another criterion for granting legal aid is to ensure that justice is done, and the court has to decide that it is desirable to do so in the interests of justice. It goes on to talk about the means of the individual.

What happens in practice as regards legal aid? Some statistics have been published. It is clear that the majority of people appearing for trial in front of a Crown court will have legal aid and, therefore Image Col. 111 legal representation. The figures for 1980 suggest that in trials in the Crown courts, 97 per cent, of the people applied for legal aid and legal aid was granted to 99 per cent. This means that the percentage of people who were granted legal aid was 96 per cent. That covers virtually all before the Crown court on trial. Where hearings deal with appeals against sentence following a magistrates' court conviction or appeals against a magistrates' court conviction, particularly in the latter case, the level of legal representation may be lower.

We turn to magistrates' courts proceedings where we have both adult and juvenile courts. Young people under the age of 17 will be appearing before juvenile courts. The figures for 1980 for England and Wales were as follows: 30 per cent, of those who were due to appear at a juvenile court applied for legal aid; 91 per cent, of such applications were granted, which in effect means that only 27 per cent, of defendants were legally represented except for those who may have paid for it themselves. Even if one makes an allowance for those who paid for it themselves, the large proportion of people appearing before juvenile courts would not have been legally represented.

One may argue that probation officers or social workers would have been present and there would have been social inquiry reports and therefore to an extent, some statements might have appeared in favour of the defendant. Nevertheless, no clear advice would have been given to the defendant as to how to conduct himself or herself. There would have been nobody there speaking clearly on behalf of the accused.

Perhaps the greater problem concerns young people between the ages of 17 and 21. If they appear before magisrates courts charged with an indictable offence, the figures for 1980 suggest that 70 per cent, applied for legal aid and 89 per cent, of the applications were granted, resulting in 62 per cent, of people having legal aid.

It is much more significant, however, in the case of summary offences. Although summary offences are sometimes regarded as being less serious than indictable offences, quite a number of summary offences carry imprisonment as a possible punishment or penalty. In 1980, only 3 per cent, of those charged with summary offences actually applied for legal aid. Sixty-three per cent, of those were granted with the result that only 2 per cent, of defendants were legally represented, unless they had private means to have legal representation.

It may seem that a large number of people are able to afford this commodity but, frankly, I doubt that they would have made a significant difference to the figures. The fact is that for those people charged with summary offences, the vast majority would not have had legal representation. As far as 1 can establish, statistics are not available to show separations between people over 21 and people under 21, but I can only assume that these statistics hold good for those people under the age of 21.

From the overall figures, therefore, it is seen that the vast majority of young people charged with Col. 112 summary offences in magistrates' courts will not have had legal representation. There also appears to be a random pattern of refusal rates of legal aid, as published recently in the LAG Bulletin which produced some figures for courts in the London area. It showed that the average refusal rate in proceedings in Inner London magistrates' courts in 1977 to 1980 was 3 per cent, before the Inner London juvenile court and 4 per cent, in Hampstead. At Highbury Corner the figure for refusal was 26 per cent., with an average for the Metropolitan area of 16 per cent. The figures for legal aid refusal rates in magistrates' courts in Outer London 1980 vary from 6 per cent, in Watford, which was the best, and 36 per cent, in Uxbridge, which was the worst.

Not only do we have a low proportion of people charged with summary offences who have legal representation as a result of refusal of legal aid, but the legal aid refusal rates themselves are more eratic than the merit of the individual applications would suggest. There is a large element of the lottery in this. It is quite wrong that this should be the case, and it is particularly wrong with regard to young people.

If we wish to see justice done to young people, if we wish to see that some of their bad experiences with the police can, at least, be redressed by legal representation in the courts; and if we wish to stop young people being found guilty because they have been coerced into it by the police or because they do not know how to put the best case forward for themselves in court, it is essential for legal aid to be granted. I hope, therefore, that the Committee will support the amendment.
