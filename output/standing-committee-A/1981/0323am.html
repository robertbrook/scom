Criminal Justice Bill

New Clause 44
CORPORAL PUNISHMENT

"(1) Any person who shall—

  1. unlawfully assault or beat any other person;
  2. make use of provoking language or behaviour tending to a breach of the peace; or
  3. commit an act of criminal damage
shall be liable on summary conviction in addition to, or instead of, current penalties, if the offender is a male child or male young person, to be whipped.
  1. The expressions "child" and "young person" mean respectively an individual of or over the age of 10 and under 14 and an individual of or over the age of 14 and under 18.
  2. The instrument used shall, in the case of a child, be a cane, and in any other case shall be a birch rod.
  3. The court in its sentence shall specify the number of strokes to be inflicted, being in the case of a child not more than six strokes, and in the case of any other person not more than 12 strokes.
  4. The whipping shall be inflicted privately as soon as practicable after sentence.
  5. The whipping shall be inflicted by a constable in the presence of an inspector or other officer of police of higher rank than a constable or by some other person appointed by the Court, and, in the case of a child or young person also in the presence if he desires to be present of the parent or guardian of the child or young person.
  6. The instruments to be used shall be—
    1. in the case of a male child who is under the age of 14 years a light cane not exceeding four feet in length and not exceeding half an inch in diameter; and
    2. in the case of a male person who is over the age of 14 a birch rod of the following dimensions:
      3. Weight not exceeding 9 ounces
      Length from end of handle to tip of spray 40 inches
      Length of handle 15 inches
      Circumference of spray at centre 6inches
      Circumference of handle at top of binding 3½ inches
      Circumference of handle 6 inches from end 3¼ inches
  7. In all cases where a court is empowered to impose a sentence of whipping a medical report as to whether the offender is fit to receive the punishment will be made available to the Magistrates before they consider sentence.
  8. The whipping shall be inflicted on the posterior over the child's ordinary cloth trousers.
  9. A medical practitioner shall be present during a birching and may at his discretion order the stopping of the punishment at any time.
  10. Where a birching has been stopped on medical grounds a report of the facts shall be forwarded immediately to the Home Secretary.".

This new clause, which I believe is one of the most important that we shall debate, has the support of 23 hon. Members covering the whole spectrum of the Conservative Party. I said on Second Reading that I believed the Bill did not go far enough, that it was not a strong enough deterrent and that it did not give enough encouragement to young offenders to reform. In particular, I believe that it does not give enough 702 protection to the victims of crime.

I move the new clause as a deterrent to offenders and as a protection for the victims of those offenders. I suggest that we have for too long in debates on new clauses and amendments, discussed the rights of the criminal. Let us today try to remember the victims.

Since the new clause was put down, I believe that the urgency for it has increased, following the publication of the latest statistics showing that violent crime has increased even more than we realised when we began our discussions. We have seen statistics which show that robberies and violence in London have risen during the past year by about 34 per cent., and public concern about violence has been expressed more strongly during the past few weeks than at any other time.

The Government are right to praise the police and the Home Secretary for the increase in the number of policemen on the beat, and they are right to improve the pay and conditions of police officers, but I believe that the outcry which we are all receiving in letters, when we talk to people, or when we have our advice clinics, shows how concerned the public are about the situation.

Let us consider the national figures for crimes of violence against the person. I have chosen three years in particular, starting in 1948, which was the year that corporal punishment was abolished. In that year crimes of violence against the person totalled 5,183. The next year I have taken is 1968, the year that the Barry report on corporal punishment was published. We find that the total in that year had increased to 15,759. If we look at 1981, we find that the figure has gone up to 100,200—a 20-fold increase from the time when corporal punishment was abolished.
