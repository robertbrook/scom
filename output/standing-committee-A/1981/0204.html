Mr. S.C. Silkin (Dulwich): I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 2, line, 1, leave out subsection (2).

I think that the Government themselves would very much like to be able to leave out subsection (2) because it enables persons under 21 years of age, who are either remanded in custody or committed in custody for trial or sentence, to be committed to prison. As I understand it, the reason it is in the Bill is that the proper places for them to be remanded to— remand centres—are not available in sufficient numbers to cope with the number of persons under 21 who fall within this category. I am sure that the Government would not willingly wish any young person under the age of 21, who is being remanded for one reason or another, to have to spend his time in a prison with either adult persons on remand or adult persons who have been convicted.

It must have been with considerable reluctance that the Government inserted this provision into the Bill. I doubt whether they would have done so but for the necessity arising out of the absence of remand centre places. The absence of adequate remand centre places is something that should be remedied as rapidly as possible.

The purpose of the amendment is to ascertain from the Government their intentions in the building of remand centres so that the provisions of this subsection will, one hopes before long, cease to be necessary and, indeed, perhaps in some future piece of legislation be removed altogether because it is clearly a subsection that one would prefer not to have in the law.

I do not intend to speak long on this amendment Col. 4 because I have little doubt there will be at least a vast majority, if not unanimity, in support of the view that I have just expressed.

It is necessary that the Government should explain their intentions. We have heard of proposals for the building of further prisons. I personally have not heard so much about proposals for the building of further remand centres, and I cannot help feeling that in many cases the sort of objection that arises to the building of new prisons would certainly not arise with the same force if one were considering the building of remand centres, the purpose of which would be to take young people who ought not to be there at all out of prisons.

I have in mind, for example, the considerable controversy over the proposal to build another prison in Woolwich. I can well imagine that many who dislike that proposal—I would not suggest that they would all welcome with great enthusiasm the idea of having a remand centre there instead—would feel that if such a complex were erected, it could help to relieve the prison population of those who should not be there. There would be much less offence to the principle, which I hope all learned Members in this Committee will seek to uphold, that people who ought not to be there should be kept out of prison. There would be much greater sympathy for such a proposal than the proposal to erect a prison, which many people believe—whatever the best intentions of the Home Office—will, in practice, simply supplement the number of prisons rather than being used to remove the obsolete prisons that ought to be destroyed and demolished.

In that category I would certainly count Brixton, to which Woolwich could well be a natural successor from the point of view of remand. I accept, of course, that Brixton is used for adult offenders, and I see no reason why remand centres should not be built as long as there is an adequate separation between the adults and young persons housed there.

I hope that the Minister will be able to give some encouraging news on this point. It is most important that one should concentrate on the provision of this kind of establishment rather than on the provision of more prisons. Equally, in their building programme, the Government should be concentrating more and more on the type of building that is not a prison, or necessarily even custodial in character, rather than in simply building more and more of prisons to house a greater and greater proportion of the population. It is in that spirit, and not in the desire to create a hole in the Bill in the place of something which, I accept, unhappily needs to be there at the moment, that I move the amendment.

He does not want to come back; we do not want him to come back. That is what it is all about.
