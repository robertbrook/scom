CONSECUTIVE TERMS AND AGGREGATE PERIODS OF DETENTION

Mr. Silkin
: I beg to move amendment No. 16, in clause 3, page 4, line 13, leave out from " direction " to " so " in line 14.

The Chairman
: With this it will be convenient to take amendment No. 17, in page 4, line 16, leave out subsection (4).

Mr. Silkin
: The provisions of clause 3 to which I am directing attention are, for me at any rate, somewhat unusual. They deal with what is to happen when a court makes a detention centre order which, if it were given effect, would involve the person concerned remaining in a detention centre for one period longer than four months. Subsection (2) provides:

" No court shall give a direction under subsection (1)... whose effect would be that the offender would be ordered to be detained in a detention centre for more than 4 months at a time."

Normally, if my recollection of the provisions of the criminal law is accurate, it would be sufficient to stop there, and anything over four months would be unlawful and would not be served. However, for reasons about which I am not clear, the clause goes further and splits off in two directions. In subsection (3) it provides for those offenders who are aged less than 15 years and sets out what would be the normal rule. If a court does that which it is prohibited from doing by subsection (2), the excess over four months 186 which would be affected by the prohibition is to be "treated as remitted". I am not altogether happy about the use of the word "remitted" because that can be used in more than one sense, but I take it that it should be treated as if a sentence of four months only had been passed, so that any remission earned by the offender during the four months would be received by him. If it does not mean that, there is a serious fault in the subsection.

For example, if the effect of consecutive sentences, which will amount to eight months at a detention centre, is that four months will be remitted to cover the normal one-third remission, that will be a grave defect. I am sure that that is not intended, but the Minister may wish to give his mind to the word "remitted" in that context. However, that is not the purpose of the amendment.

When we come to the other branch of the provisions, we turn to offenders aged 15 years or more. We have what seems to be the unusual and peculiar position that, in the case of those aged 15 and over, if the court has made a direction which is prohibited by subsection (2), the effect of which will be that the offender will be detained for more than four months at one time, it will have partial effect for those over 15 by virtue of subsection (4). In those circumstances the youth custody order will operate for the full period, including the period absolutely prohibited by subsection (2).

That seems an extraordinary provision. I do not know whether it is unique but I have not been able to find any other such provision. The Minister will be able to tell us whether a similar provision exists elsewhere. The rationale behind it is extremely difficult to follow. We are telling a court not to do something and we expect it to obey the law. We then say that if it does not obey the law some effect will be given to its sentence and that will have the effect of depriving a person of liberty for a period which, because it will be translated into youth custody rather than attending a detention centre, will become a lawful sentence. If the courts do something unlawful, the operation of the Act will make it lawful.

That appears to be almost an incentive for courts in such circumstances not deliberately to pass longer detention centre orders than subsection (2) permits but to pass youth custody sentences for the periods that they wish. These provisions would certainly give a licence to courts to be slapdash in their methods for reasons that I cannot understand.

The situation that is being envisaged is one in which the sentencing court break the law by making an order for more than four months at detention centre ab initio, which it should know it cannot do. It should surely be perfectly clear to the court that four months is the maximum. Alternatively, the court can produce the same result by the addition of a new sentence to one which is already being served, or is about to be served. In either instance there could not possibly be any excuse for a court not knowing what had happened hitherto. If the young person is sent to a detention centre, the court will be told that he is serving such a sentence. If such JPEG 187 a sentence has been passed by another court, or by the same court in an immediately preceding case, the court will know. It should be the duty of the court authorities to ascertain the current situation before sentence is passed. There can be no excuse for not doing so. The Bill provides that if the court, in defiance of the provisions of subsection (2), and acting in what seems to be a slapdash, careless way, passes a sentence that it is prohibited from imposing, the offender is nonetheless required to serve the sentence within youth custody rather than in a detention centre if he is over 15.

At an earlier stage of our discussions the Minister explained to us the importance that he attributes to the difference of regimes, and that lends force to what I am saying. The Minister will recall that I asked him which he regarded as the heavier sentence, youth custody or detention centre. He said that he regarded them not in that way but on the basis of different regimes which are appropriate to different offenders.

If that is so, surely the provisions of subsection (4), in addition to the defect that I have described of penalising a person because of an unlawful order, have the additional defect that the offender will serve a sentence in a regime different from the one that the court intended, the court having presumably considered whether it was appropriate to send him on to a detention centre. Having decided that he should be sent to a detention centre, the court will find that because a mistake has been made he will serve a youth custody order instead, which may not be appropriate.

Mr. Mayhew
: It might be for the convenience of the Committee if I briefly rehearse the effects of clause 2(3), to which the amendment applies. The subsection guards against the imposition of a custodial sentence of more than four months on a 14-year old offender because the maximum custodial sentence for 14-year olds is set at four months. A court can order that two or more detention centre orders should run consecutively, but it should not do so if the effect is that the offender is ordered to 188 be detained in a detention centre for more than four months at a time.

Subsection (3) provides that if a court does give such a direction in respect of an offender aged less than 15 years, so much of the term as exceeds four months shall be treated as remitted. The provision is automatic and it means that the excess over four months has no effect at all in the case of a 14-year-old offender for whom the maximum sentence is, as I have said, four months.

The effect of the amendment of the right hon. and learned Member for Dulwich (Mr. Silkin) to extend that provision to all offenders aged under 21, so that where a court mistakenly passes two or more detention centre orders which result in a term which is longer than four months, the excess over four months is treated as remitted.

The use of the word "remission" is not susceptible to any ambiguity or confusion. It certainly does not mean what it means in its normal connotation of remission for good conduct, which is that if one loses one's good conduct one may serve the full nominal amount of the sentence in custody. It is used in the subsection because the offender would be released after four months with the remainder treated as if it had no effect at all. That is the distinction between remission used in this sense and remission used in the normal good conduct sense under the prison rules.

That is the same as the position of a prisoner who is released after two-thirds has been served under normal remission arrangements, but subject to there being no parallel provision that if the condition for remission, good conduct, is not fulfilled, the full nominal amount of the sentence is served. The point, which I think is plain from the wording of subsection (3), is that for all purposes that part of the sentence that is surplus to four months shall be of no effect at all. The use of the word " remit", therefore, is appropriate and we need have no fears on that score. I shall look at it again in the light of what the right hon. Gentleman has said, but I do not anticipate the need to make any change.

The provisions in subsections (3) and (4) are there because the calculation of consecutive and aggregate sentences is notoriously difficult, particularly when an offender is already serving one sentence and the next is imposed. No doubt that would be logical, but it is not very practical when one is dealing with short sentences. I do not believe that that would be right, even though the courts would no doubt do their best to expedite the hearing of the appeal. It is proper and wise for Parliament to face this possibility at the outset and to say in the legislation that if the court makes a mistake we wish certain consequences to follow.

Mr. Lyon
: This series of amendments attempt to repeal section 53 of the Children and Young Persons Act 1933. I had hoped that the Minister would say that he would accept that, since it follows the logic of his own Bill. However, since I have had no such sign, may I be allowed, even in these strained times, to explain why I take that view?

Section 53(1) of the Children and Young Persons Act permits a court to sentence a person under the age of 18 to be detained at Her Majesty's pleasure for a limit of life, and refers specifically to the former capital offences, including murder. It is a curious provision, in that it refers to the break-off age of 18, and not the the break-off age for children and young persons, which is normally 17. The provision has been used, however, since 1933 in all cases of murder.

I know that the Minister, along with most of the legal establishment, has always been opposed to section 3 of the 1961 Act even though it was passed by a Conservative Government. Until this Bill is passed the judges have a manifest custodial alternative. They can send young people to borstal or to a detention centre. In future they will have the capacity to send them to a detention centre but not to borstal, and therefore section 3 becomes otiose; But what is not otiose is the capacity of the courts to send people for longer and longer periods of custodial sentence, even if it is called youth custody. Under the provisions of the Bill youth custody can become imprisonment if the sentence goes on until the offender is over 21, because it is up to the Home Secretary to decide where the sentence is served, and it is possible to serve it in an adult prison, and even to serve a substantial period in an adult prison.

What concerns me about the text of the Bill as it stands is this. Under the tariff system so far, what would happen to a young person who came before the court? It is likely that he would get a noncustodial sentence initially appropriate to the offence. He might then go to a detention centre and after that he might go to borstal. If he came back again after serving a period in borstal he might be sent back to borstal, either by recall or on a new sentence. However, by the time he reached the age of 21 the likelihood is that he would not have served any period of imprisonment. When he came before the court at 21 it would decide that in view of his bad record it could not overlook the offence and that it must send him to prison. He had never been to prison before and prison would be a great culture shock for him, therefore six months, or three months, or two months might be an appropriate sentence because he would be tasting a form of custodial treatment that he had never had before, even though he had been in and out of custody since he was 13 or 14.

Under the provisions of the Bill, however, the courts will not be thinking in those terms at all. The offender will be sent to a detention centre on his first custodial sentence, and then the courts will begin the whole process of differing terms of youth custody. No doubt he will start with two or three months of youth custody. Then he will come back again after having done two months or three months, and he will be given six months. He will serve four months of that and will come out again. Then he will go back again and this time he will get a year. By the time he gets to 21 he might very well already have served sentences of up to 18 months—or maybe even two years—of youth custody, because it would be posible within the time span to have done that. When he gets to 21 the courts will have to decide what should then be done with an offender who has already been through youth custody for periods of 12 months, 18 months or two years. Are they to decide that he should go to prison for three months?

Can hon. Members imagine what the court would say in such a situation? He has already served 21 months, so what is the point of sending him to prison for three months? The courts will decide that, since he has already served 21 months' youth JPEG 213 custody, they should now sentence him to three years in pirson. That seems to me to be the trap which is laid bare by this provision in the Bill, and I am certain that it is not what the Minister or the Home Office ever intended. But I am absolutely certain that that is the way that the tariff mentality of the judiciary will apply it.

We shall find that instead of reducing the number of people in prison, which is supposed to be one of the aims of the Government, there will be a whole new operating of the tariff so that there will be more people in prison. We all know that the overcrowding in prison at the moment relates to local prisons, in relation to sentences of four years or less. Although it is sometimes argued that the problem is caused by people who go to prison for long periods and by those who have been sentenced to life imprisonment, the fact is that the real overcrowding occurs amongst prisoners who are serving shorter sentences. If the sentences of all those who are sent to prison at the moment for four years or less were halved, the prison population would fall dramatically from about 45,000 to about 34,000.
