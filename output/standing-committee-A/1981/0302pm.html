Criminal Justice Bill

Clause 22
PAYMENT OF FINES BY PARENTS AND GUARDIANS

The Chairman
: We come now to amendment No. 61. The right hon. and learned Member for Dul-wich (Mr. Silkin), who tabled the amendment is not present, but it is open to any hon. Member to move it.

Dr. Shirley Summerskill
(Halifax)
: My hon. Friend the Member for Ormskirk (Mr. Kilroy-Silk) has offered to move the amendment formally, and I am pleased to speak on behalf of my right hon. and learned Friend the Member for Dulwich (Mr. Silkin), who cannot be here. His contributions have been extremely intelligent and sensible. I am sure that he would not want the amendment to fall because of his unavoidable absence from the sitting.

The clause deals with payment of fines by parentsand guardians. It will be

"the duty of the court to order"

that such fines be paid by the parent or guardianunless it is satisfied in two ways:

  1. "that the parent or guardian cannot be found; or
  2. that it would be unreasonable to make an order forpayment, having regard to the circumstances of the case."

As I understand it, the court shall order a parent or guardian to pay instead of the child or young person, but it can order him to pay only the whole fine, the whole compensation, or the whole costs. Either the entire sum is paid by the parent or, in certain circumstances, the entire sum is paid by the child. There seems to be no provision for partial payment by a parent and partial payment by a child. The amendment seeks to insert

"or a specified part thereof."

Therefore, it would be possible, as the court sees fit, for the parent to pay a part, and the child to pay a part. That seems to be a sensible provision.

There could be circumstances where, from the financial point of view, the parent might be able to pay some, but not all, of the fine. Similarly, the child might be able to pay some, but not all of the fine.

Apart from the question of who can afford to pay, there is the question of criminal responsibility. Here again, there may be cases where the court feels that the criminal responsibility does not he entirely with the parent, having failed to exercise due care and control, or with the child, with the parents being blameless. There may be occasions when the 348 court feels that the criminal responsibility for the offence could be due partly to the parent, who had not exercised due care and control, and partly to the child. Therefore, the court might feel that it would like to impose a fine on both.

The second reason why I believe that the amendment would improve the clause is that the clause itself has been put in by the Government with a great fanfare of trumpets in their paper on young offenders. They say that at the moment there is no clear judicial interpretation of the scope of the provisions and that the courts have been reluctant to use the ability to fine parents because of the uncertainty about their ambit.

Courts have been reluctant; either they did not know that the provisions existed, or they were not clear about the powers. We are led to believe that the purpose of the clause is to strengthen and clarify the existing provisions. If my right hon. and learned Friend's amendment were inserted to widen the provisions to permit part of the fine to be paid by the parent and part by the child, the provisions in the clause would be clarified and strengthened and made even more sensible.

A good case has been and can be made for not fining parents on principle on the grounds that it would impose an unreasonable burden on people of low income and the unemployed, or, in the case of a strained relationship between a young offender and his parents, it would result in resentment that could only damage that relationship further. In other cases, parents may have done everything possible to discipline and control the young person, and the imposition of a fine would be unjustified.

Those points may be raised on other amendments. However, the main argument for this amendment rests on the fact that it broadens the options open to a court and accepts that in many cases the blame is not entirely on one side or the other.

It must be difficult—as it is in a marriage breakdown—for a court to lay all the blame on one side or the other. The court should be able to say in the case of a young offender that because neither the parents nor the young offender were blameless, both should be fined or made to pay the costs. Obviously, the court takes into consideration both the financial aspect and the question of criminal responsibility.

352 We proceed from the basis that it is desirable that this power should exist. The next sensible step is to see that the power is expressed as plainly as possible, provided that it does not result in injustice for the parents.

The hon. Member for Battersea, South (Mr. Dubs) asked how one could find out whether the power was reasonable. New clause 22 reverses the presumption and says that the court shall do it in all cases. The clause abolishes the distinction at present in operation between the 10 to 14-year-old and the 14 to 17-year-old and says for juveniles as a whole that

"it shall be the the duty of the court to order…unless…it would be unreasonable in the circumstances."

Therefore, the court wants to know about the circumstances. Subsection (2) states:

"An order … may be made against a parent oi guardian who, having been required to attend, has failed to do so."

It follows that the court in all circumstances would be obliged, before making such an order, to require the attendance of the parent and to make such inquiries as the circumstances of the case necessitate.

New section 55(2) of the Children and Young Persons Act ensures that a parent has an opportunity to be heard but does not oblige the parent to submit to examination.

I followed with interest the remarks of the hon. Member for Battersea, South (Mr. Dubs). I do not think that we should be too upset by the prospect of a parent submitting to questions about what has gone wrong. It is part of our purpose to bring home the fact that there is a family responsibility for what youngsters do when they go wrong, in the same way as a family is entitled to take credit when things go right and a child behaves in a creditable manner.

It is part of our purpose that family responsibility as a whole should be engaged and emphasised. I am not deterred from arguing the merits of this case by the fact that parents might be embarrassed or humiliated, as the hon. Gentleman said, by having to admit to failures with their children. If one has failed with a child and the child has gone out and hurt someone, it is not necessarily bad that one should be made to face the fact.

I see some force in that line of argument. Therefore, when I am asked not to have a closed mind about the matter, I can say that my mind is not closed. However, there are substantial difficulties.

Since 1977, section 36 of the Criminal Law Act has contained the provision, where a juvenile has defaulted on the payment of a fine, and has been found to have had the means to pay but has wilfully refused to do so—so there is no question of his not being able to—for a court to order the parents to pay the unpaid amount where it would be reasonable. I emphasise those words, because they form the substance of another amendment proposed to this clause. That provision was introduced by the JPEG 353 previous Administration. The hon. Member for Pontypridd (Mr. John) held my present post in the Home Office at that time. He was content, as was the entire Committee, so far as I can ascertain, to accept that formula. That provision is now consolidated in section 81 of the Magistrates' Courts Act 1980.

Suppose the court were to say "All right, Mr. Mayhew. We think that you have failed with your children. You are not wholly to blame "—at least I hope that they would say that because, as I have four children, it would likely be expensive. " We think, therefore that justice will be done if we order you to pay 60 per cent, of the fine that we have imposed upon your child." Suppose my child does not pay his part of the fine. Immediately, section 81 of the Magistrates' Courts Act would be engaged and the court could, if it felt reasonable to do so. make me pay the remainder.

Great difficulties would be caused, involving the amendment of that provision. We should end up with a tremendous meal being made of a procedure intended to be ancillary to the court's power to fine a juvenile. It is ancillary, because the principal purpose of the procedure is to punish the juvenile. The ancillary purpose is to bring home to the parent that they also have a responsibility.

I understand what lies behind the amendment, and I give great weight to the idea that apportionment according to perceived degrees of responsibility is generally thought to be fair. However, the difficulties that we shall run into are considerable. There is also the question of compensation. A child and his parents may be held equally responsible for an offence and an order for compensation be divided between them, but the parent will have ample means and the child none. If compensation of £600 were awarded, and payment was ordered to be shared equally, assuming that the child had no means, the victim might get only £300 at most. The courts might feel bound, in the interests of the victim, but ignoring the painstaking care that it had taken to apportion responsibility, to make the parent pay the whole £600.

I have an open mind, and I shall consider the matter further, but I felt it my duty to make it clear that there are substantial obstacles. If that will assist the hon. Lady, I shall feel that I have not detained the Committee in vain.
