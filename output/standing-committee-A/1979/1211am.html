New Clause 49
SHADOW DIRECTORS

"(1) Subject to subsection (2) below, a person in accordance with those directions or instructions the directors of a company are accustomed to act ("a shadow director") shall be treated for the purposes of this Part of this Act as a director of the company unless the directors are accustomed so to act by reason only that they do so on advice given by him in a professional capacity.

"(2) A shadow director shall not be guilty of an offence under section (Other provisions relating to recognised banks) (4) above by virtue only of subsection (1) above.

"(3) Section 199 of the 1948 Act (disclosure by a director of a company of his interest in a contract, transaction or arrangement with the company) shall apply in relation to a shadow director of a company as it applies in relation to a director of a company, except that the shadow director shall declare his interest, not at a meeting of the directors, but by a notice in writing to the directors which is either—

  1. a specific notice given before the date of the meeting at which, if he had been a director, the declaration would be required by subsection (2) of that section to be made; or
  2. a notice which under subsection (3) of that section (general notices) falls to be treated as a sufficient declaration of that interest or would fall to be so treated apart from the proviso;
and section 145 of that Act (minutes of proceedings of meetings) shall have effect as if the declaration had been made at the meeting in question and had accordingly formed part of the proceedings at that meeting.

"(4) A shadow director of a company shall be treated for the purposes of section 26 of the 1967 Act (director's service contracts, etc., to be open to inspection by a company's members) as a director of the company."—[ Mr. Eyre.].

The Under-Secretary of State for Trade (Mr. Reginald Eyre)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

Members of the Committee will recall that, in introducing the first clauses on directors' conflicts of interests, we gave notice of our intention to bring forward a provision to extend them to cover persons on whose instructions the directors of a company are accustomed to act. The reference in new clause 49 to

"this Part of this Act"

636 therefore means new clauses 21 to 37.

The circumstances in which shadow directors operate are such that they can procure the board to act otherwise than in the best interests of the company and yet might escape responsibility for such actions. The Companies Acts already recognise the possibility of shadow directors, and only now have we coined a term for a long descriptive phrase which has been with us since the 1948 Act. For example, the 1948 Act provides for the offences created in relation to fraud in the course of winding up to be equally applicable to shadow directors, and in the 1967 Act applies the provisions on directors' dealing in a company's own shares to what we now call shadow directors.

Since it is clear that a substantial shareholder, by pulling the strings from behind the scenes, would be able to evade the purpose of the new clauses which tighten up the extent to which directors can take undue advantage of the company's resources, we think it necessary to treat such a person as if he were a director. An example of such behaviour is to be found in the inspectors' report on Larkfold Holdings.

I hope that the Committee will find the clause commendably short and self-explanatory. It seemed much better to have a separate clause rather to insert references to shadow directors into all the other clauses regulating loans and so on to directors. I shall therefore confine myself to a few points.

Subsection (2) simply clarifies that the offence created in new clause 31 which the directors of a recognised bank commit if they fail to maintain the register or produce the annual statements of loans and so on shall not apply to shadow directors since they are not duly appointed directors on whom the statutory responsibility will lie.

Subsection (3) applies the disclosure rules of section 199 of the 1948 Act, as amended by new clause 33, to shadow directors so that they are required to inform the board of their interest in a transaction and so forth which the company has entered or is about to enter into. The penalty in section 145 of the 1948 Act for failure to keep proper minutes—under which the company and every officer who is in default shall pay a default fine—is applied to this information. This therefore obliges the board to take notice of the shadow director's interest.

JPEG 637 Finally, subsection (4) applies section 26 of the 1967 Act, which requires the disclosure of directors' contracts of service, to shadow directors.

Thus, shadow directors and their connected persons shall be just as much prohibited and made liable in the event of the contravention of such prohibitions, and shall have their loans and so on disclosed, as duly appointed directors.

Members of the Committee may remember that, while loans and so forth between holding companies and subsidiaries are not prohibited by virtue of the director of one being connected with the other, loans made where such connection exists are to be disclosed in the annual accounts.

New clause 11
GENERAL FIDUCIARY DUTY OF DIRECTORS

"(1) The directors of a company (whether performing their functions or acting individually) shall act honestly in relation to the company and observe the utmost good faith towards it and accordingly shall perform their functions in what they consider to be the best interests of the company as a whole.

"(2) The directors of a company shall not perform any of their functions for a purpose not contemplated by the instrument or resolution conferring or imposing that function.

"(3) A director of a company shall not—

  1. allow a conflict to arise between the duties of his office and his private interests or any duties he owes to any other person; or
  2. do anything which, at the time it is done, could reasonably be expected to involve his contravening paragraph ( a)above.

"(4) (i) Without prejudice to subsections (1) to (3) above, a director of a company or a person who has been a director of a company shall not, for the JPEG 641 purpose of gaining, whether directly or indirectly, an advantage for himself—

  1. make use of any money or other property of the company; or
  2. make use of any relevant information or of a relevant opportunity—
    1. if he does so while a director of the company in circumstances which give rise or could at the time he does so reasonably be expected to give rise to such a conflict; or
    2. if while a director of the company he had that use in contemplation in circumstances which gave rise or could at the time he had that use in contemplation reasonably have been expected to give rise to such a conflict.

"(5) In this section—

"relevant information", in relation to a director of a company, means any information which he obtained while a director or other officer of the company and which it was reasonable to expect him to disclose to the company or not to disclose to persons unconnected with the company;


"relevant opportunity", in relation to a director of a company, means an opportunity which he had while a director or other officer of the company and which he had—

  1. by virtue of his position as a director or other officer of the company; or
  2. in circumstances in which it was reasonable to expect him to disclose the fact that he had that opportunity to the company.

"(6) If any person contravenes subsection (4) above, he shall be liable to account to the company for any gain which he has made directly or indirectly from the contravention and shall be liable to compensate the company for any loss or damage suffered directly or indirectly by the company in consequence of the contravention.

"(7) A person shall not be regarded as contravening any of the foregoing provisions of this section by reason of any act or omission which is duly authorised or ratified; but the foregoing provision shall not be construed as permitting the authorisation or ratification of any act or omission which cannot be authorised or, as the case may be, ratified apart from this section.

"(8) This section has effect instead of any rule of law stating the fiduciary duties of directors of companies, but is without prejudice—

  1. to any remedies which may be available apart from this section for a breach of any such duty; and
  2. to any other provision of the Companies Acts imposing duties or liabilities on such directors or defining their duties or liabilities;
and compliance with any requirement of those Acts shall not of itself be taken as relieving a director of a company of any liability imposed by this section …"—[ Mr. Archer.].

New clause 11 will not occasion the Government or the Committee any astonishment. The principle was referred to on Second Reading by my hon, Friend the Member for Hackney, Central. Moreover, this is one of the debates when some members of the Committee will experience a feeling of déjà vu. It was debated in a long and interesting sitting of the Standing Committee on the 1978 Bill, and I feel that it is almost an impertinence for a newly-arrived upstart like me to venture to repeat all the arguments which were then so fully rehearsed.

The basic issue was how far the law on the duties and responsibilities of directors should be left where it lies among the varied judicial decisions or whether it should be embodied in a code. Those who argue that practitioners and those who have to operate this branch of the law should be left to cull the principles from the various crevices where they lie were, no doubt, left hoping that their textbooks would be up to date, and employing such latter-day aids to construction as the index to the Law Reports and Current Law.

It is fair to say that those who took that view did agree that there should be some statutory provisions to supplement the case law. I shall, if I may, state tendentiously what they were proposing. They were proposing that Parliament should add yet another crevice where practitioners and JPEG 643 company secretaries would have to seek for their law.

The argument for that course of action, as I understand it, is that codification cannot provide for every eventuality, so any code is necessarily incomplete. I am bound to say that I have some difficulty in understanding why that should be said to be a disadvantage as against leaving the law in the existing case law, since that must be even more partial and incomplete than a code would be. I would not seek to argue with St Paul, that

"when that which is perfect is come, that which is in part shall be done away".

I would only submit to the Committee that the more complete should be preferred to the less complete. There it is, Sir Stephen—there are those two views, and each may claim a respectable measure of support.

Against codification stands the Jenkins committee; for codification there is the Bullock committee. Against codification, at least during the previous debates, was the Law Society. For codification we may cite the Consultative Committee of Accountancy bodies, the CBI, the Bar Association for Commerce, Finance and Industry, and the Association of British Chambers of Commerce. Since those debates took place, no doubt other distinguished bodies will have had an opportunity to range themselves on one side or the other.

The argument for codification or for leaving it all where the judges have left it extends well beyond the scope of company law. I favour taking steps to bring the law together in one place, even though I accept that situations arise for which the code has not provided and therefore the code would need to be looked at from time to time in order to bring it up to date. I am not attracted by the argument that because we cannot provide for every situation we should not attempt to offer some comprehensive guidelines.

Subsection (1) of our new clause relates to the duty to act "honestly" and to

"observe the utmost good faith.

One criticism levelled at that in earlier debates was that the expression "utmost good faith" is taken from insurance law and entails an obligation to disclose completely any information which might be relevant in the course of negotiations for a deal. It was said that that would not be appropriate for company law because it might entail disclosing company secrets which might be helpful to competitors.

The duty to disclose is owed to the company. If there is some transaction between a director and the company, it is not easy to see why there should not be the fullest possible disclosure, either to fellow directors or to the appropriate officer of the company, and in most cases, with some possible exceptions, to the whole body of shareholders. I think that new clause 12 is much less likely to be controversial. It has certainly been less controversial in the past. At one of the recent sittings of this Committee, there was some discussion of the duties of tea-ladies.
