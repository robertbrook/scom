Motion made, and Question proposed,

That during the proceedings on the Companies Bill [Lords] the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock.—[Mr. Parkinson.]

The Chairman: Before I call the hon. Member for Hackney, Central (Mr. Davis), may I say that there are several hon. Members in the Committee, and gentlemen among the Minister's advisers, who have been round this course before. I hope, therefore, that we shall be spared too much repetition of what took place on the previous Bill.

Mr. Clinton Davis: I sympathise with your predicament in that regard, Sir Stephen. It was a predicament that I was spared, as I was dealing with another Bill at the time, when a number of hon. Members who are present today were rehearsing a series of arguments with which you are now wholly familiar.

It is not the Opposition's intention to oppose the sittings motion or to hold up the Bill. We do, however, have a number of anxieties about the Bill, and the sittings motion provides me with an opportunity to rehearse those problems and to seek some particularisation from the Government on how they intend to deal with these issues.

We face an extraordinary situation, embarking on the Committee stage of a Bill that is far removed, evidently—certainly as regards many important issues—from the Government's intentions. We have been promised that the Government propose to introduce a substantial number of amendments dealing with the duties of directors, conflicts of interest and so on, and, most importantly, the issue of insider dealing. However, none of those proposals is yet before the Committee. I can hardly recall a situation during the nine years I have been in the House when a Committee has been presented with a Bill in this shape.

The truth is that the Government have got themselves into a mess. Originally, Col. 4 they were contemplating a truncated form of the Labour Government's Companies Bill, which would incorporate parts I, II and III of that Bill, and they were adamant on not shifting from that position. In another place the Government then resolved that they would make some amendments which met points raised by the Opposition in another place but which clearly did not go far enough. However, there they stood at that stage, determined not to move an inch from that point.

We then had the debate on Second Reading. The Minister of State and the Under-Secretary made certain important additional observations about the Government's intentions at that stage. It was dressed up with the excuse that they had intended to have a Bill later in the Session in 1980 which would have incorporated many of the issues that both sides of the House regard as important.

That did not square with the statements made in another place by Government spokesmen, because they were then talking of a Bill in the 1980–81 Session, but it is for the Government to address themselves to the excuses that they want to proffer to Members of the House and of the Committee. It is not for me to embark on a major debate on that aspect at this stage, because I welcome the Government's change of heart.

I pay tribute to the Minister of State for the way in which he has caused his Department to rethink its original stance. I have already made certain comments on that on Second Reading, and I believe that the Bill as we eventually see it should be a substantial improvement on the present text.

Therefore, I say at once, for the reasons that you have already anticipated, Sir Stephen, that the Opposition see little cause for debating at any length the Bill as presented to us now because it substantially incorporates parts I, II and III of our own Bill. But there will be some interesting debates. I am delighted to see that the hon. Member for Kensington (Sir B. Rhys Williams) has successfully tabled amendments and new clauses to be debated shortly on the question of audit committees and non-executive directors. We welcome that because it will provide the Government with an opportunity to Image Col. 5 say something more about the state of their consultations on the issue—a matter to which I alluded on Second Reading.

I should like to know at what stage the Government propose to put before the Committee the amendments on the vital issues of insider dealing, duties of directors and so on, because it is right that the Committee should have an opportunity to consult about these matters, just as the Government have taken that opportunity. This is not just a one-sided transaction. We therefore demand the maximum possible time within which to carry out those consultations.

If it is clear that we are likely to have a fairly short Committee on the Bill as it now stands, do the Government propose to introduce the amendments on Report? If so, it is essential—I am not making a party-political point because we all want to see the right company law reforms enacted—that the Government should permit hon. Members on both sides of the Committee to have a sufficient gap between Committee and Report to undertake further research and take advice on the propositions which the Government are advancing.

At the moment the Opposition are handicapped in tabling amendments. I do not want to put down needless amendments if I achieve only a duplication of what the Government intend. Therefore, I shall wait until the Government put forward their proposals in the main, save that, as the hon. Gentleman indicated on Second Reading—this is at col. 61 of Hansard—thathe did not propose to bring forward clauses dealing with the codification of duties of directors, we shall do so and provoke a debate on that interesting topic.

I wish to refer to insider dealing at this stage, but without going into the merits of the Government's consultative document. How long do the Government now envisage that the consultation is likely to take? What prospects are there of bringing forward amendments within the period of our sittings in Committee? The Minister of State indicated on Second Reading that he hoped to be able to bring forward proposals during Committee. I am doubtful that he will succeed in that, particularly if the Committee stage is not of long duration.

Col. 6 It would be helpful also if the Government were to spell out now their intentions on other matters of significance so that we can consider separately, or with them, further ways of improving the Bill. I was interested to note from the Under-Secretary's speech that the Government have abandoned the idea of a standing advisory committee to undertake a continuing review of company law and to provide the Secretary of State with both general and specific advice. I am sad that it was abandoned because it was a proposal widely welcomed in financial and professional circles. The proposal that the Government are now putting forward is infinitely less satisfactory, that is, for a departmental panel of advisers. While I applaud the choice of Professor Gower to advise the Government—I can think of nobody better—my doubt about the mechanism chosen by the Government is that our proposal would have enabled a public debate to take place following the proposals for reform that the standing advisory committee would have produced. If they do not go so far as that, we shall have to consider putting down amendments.

"the law relating to companies"

"the law relating to the fees chargeable in respect of the registration of business names."

After all, as I said on Second Reading, it is absurd that the business names registry should be involved in continuing loss simply because the fees which were established—in 1916, I believe—have not since been amended. It costs 25p to register a business name and 5p to carry out a search. For a Government who believe in saving money to accept that continuing loss for no good reason is palpably absurd.

Another matter that I hope the Government will be able to comment upon is the practice of window dressing. Do they propose to put forward any proposals here in Committee to deal with something that has become an appalling abuse? It is in the main a practice designed to subvert the purposes of the Companies Act in ensuring a true and fair view of balance sheets and profit and loss accounts, and it is a practice which has been highlighted in a number of reports as being thoroughly disreputable. Are matters to be left solely to the accountants' profession to devise a better and higher standard? If so, we shall want further particularisation of the grounds for resisting legislative action.
