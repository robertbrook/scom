Mr. Eyre: The purpose of these amendments is to remedy two weaknesses in the clause. The first point turns on the phrase presently in the Bill,

"nominee for the company alone".

What lies behind this phrase is the directive. In articles 18 to 24, which deal with various aspects of the acquisition by a company of its own shares, the directive assimilates acquisitions by the company itself with acquisitions by a person

"acting in his own name but on the company's behalf".

Image Col. 245 The phrase in the Bill

"nominee for the company alone"

is an attempt to express this idea, but it is clear that it is an unsuccessful attempt. It has been pointed out that a person may acquire shares and hold them on behalf of the company without the company having any knowledge of his actions.

In such a case, it is possible to describe the acquirer as a

"nominee for the company alone".

The consequences of this under the present clause could be grave. If the shares were taken on subscription or were acquired partly paid up, the directors or the subscribers to the memorandum could, without any knowledge of the transaction on the part of the company, become liable to pay any sums due if the nominee for the company did not. That is clearly not sensible and not what the directive is after.

I do not overlook the fact that, as drafted, the clause allows directors and subscribers to apply to the court for relief, but that is not a satisfactory answer. We should not force perfectly innocent people to apply to the court for relief from arbitrarily imposed liabilities.

Where the Bill has gone wrong is that the nominees who should be covered are those nominated by or with the consent of the company to hold shares in the company on its behalf. When one concentrates on such a concept, the problem mentioned earlier, of a person acting as nominee for the company without the company being aware of it, is solved. Such clandestine behaviour does not bring the provisions of clause 36 into play.

Furthermore, under the new approach, knowledge and consent of the company is the cornerstone. In such a case, there is no need to limit the Bill as it is presently limited to cases where the person is acting as nominee for the company alone. That limitation is an open invitation to avoidance, and if the company knows what is happening then it is not clear why directors or subscribers should avoid liability merely because someone else has a beneficial interest in those shares.

The amendments proposed seek to define the nominee in the sense that I have described, and to do this we propose Col. 246 an apparently slight change but one which has the effect that we wish for. Instead of referring to a

"nominee for the company alone",

the amendments speak of a "nominee of the company".

Thus, the latter phrase restricts the nominee to those cases where he can properly be described as a nominee of the company, with the necessary requirement that the company should be aware of what is going on.

I now turn to the second weakness in the clause as it stands. Paragraph 2 of article 18 of the directive says that where a person acting on behalf of the company subscribes for shares, he shall be deemed to have subscribed for them on his own account—that is, the company is to have no beneficial interest in the shares. Whatever the answer is to that difficult question, we face a Morton's Fork, where the bill as drafted fails to comply with the directive.

A recent case decided by the Court of Appeal, Belmont Finance v Williams, reported in 1979 1 All England Law Reports at page 118, explained that section 54 was intended to protect creditors and therefore while the directors may be penalised for breach of the criminal provisions, the civil sanctions which the courts have developed will not be employed to the prejudice of the creditors. Therefore, the company may still have a beneficial interest in shares acquired with its funds by third parties. In the light of that recent decision, there must be a real possibility that the company could have a beneficial interest in such a case.

In such a case, the directive requires that unless the shares are disposed of within one year, they must be cancelled. That is the effect of article 21.
