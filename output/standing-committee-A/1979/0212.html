Clause 2
ISSUE BY SECRETARY OF STATE OF CODES OF PRACTICE

Mr. Harold Walker
: I beg to move amendment No. 12, in page 3, line 39, at end insert

'except that in the case of a code issued by the Service under section 6 of the 1975 Act or having effect by virtue of paragraph 4 of Schedule 17 to that Act the Secretary of State shall not proceed unless he has obtained the consent of the Service'.

 May I extend to you, Mr. Leadbitter, a warm welcome to our exciting proceedings on this important Bill. I hope that your presence does not mean that Mr. Goodhew is indisposed.

We have repeatedly expressed our belief that the decision in 1974 to establish an Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service and that the Government—and the Secretary of State in particular—should not be over-involved in the day-to-day management of industrial relations is something to which we attach great importance, and that is reflected in this amendment.

The 1975 Act gave the Service the power to make codes of practice, subject to the approval of both Houses of Parliament. This clause would give the Secretary of State additionally a similar power. We believe, for all the reasons that we have advanced previously, that the Service is still the right body to produce codes of practice. We have reaffirmed that, and we shall no doubt do so again.

By subsection (7), to which the amendment applies, not only is the Secretary of State given the power together with ACAS to produce codes of practice but also, where there is an existing code of practice on which there may be conflict, where the Secretary of State may be in conflict with the Service, the Secretary of State's code shall supersede or have priority over that produced by the Service.

266 We regard that as a daft state of affairs. When the power is exercised—presumably, it is intended that it should be exercised, since otherwise there is no point in having it there—it will bring the Secretary of State into conflict with the Service. That will not only be detrimental to the standing and morale of the Service but be bad for industrial relations, too.

Incidentally, perhaps the Under-Secretary will say something about the resources of experience and expertise in this field that may or may not be available to the Secretary of State to guide him when producing his code or codes of practice. My recollection is that most of the people in the Department of Employment who had a great deal of ability and expertise in this work were transferred, rightly and understandably, to the staff of ACAS. Although there are one or two officials still in the Department with a role in the industrial relations field, I should have thought that their number was limited and that the majority were on the staff of ACAS and not available to the Secretary of State, by virtue of the statute that established the independence of ACAS and notwithstanding that they continue to be civil servants.

I come back to the main point, the conflict that may well arise between the Secretary of State and the Service and the effect of that on the morale of the Service. In the circumstances hypothesised in this subsection, for the Secretary of State to produce a code that overrides or sets aside a code produced by ACAS is, in effect, a vote of no confidence. He is saying that he thinks better than the Service, that the Service somehow has misjudged or misunderstood or, for political or whatever reasons has produced a code in which he has no confidence.

One is bound to ask also about the effect on industrial relations of what would be two conflicting codes of practice, one produced by ACAS and presumably urged on both sides of industry by the Service as showing the right course and right practices to follow, and the other produced by the Secretary of State saying, in effect, that the earlier one is wrong.

People in industry who look to ACAS may well find themselves in considerable difficulty, as may the conciliation staff of ACAS, one of whose tasks is to encourage JPEG 267 the use of the procedures, principles and measures set out in a particular code. For them to have to change course may result not only in confusion and lack of confidence in the Service but in difficulty for those involved in the day-to-day conduct and management of industrial relations.

The main question is that of the conflict, which we seek to eliminate. No doubt, my hon. Friend the Member for Bethnal Green and Bow (Mr. Mikardo) will refer to amendment No. 13, which Mr. Goodhew chose not to select for debate. My hon. Friend was convinced that the weakness of my amendment was that it was a direct negative. I am sure that if it had been it would not have been called for debate. It is not a direct negative. The words that we do not like give the Secretary of State power to produce codes of practice. Codes of practice are a useful tool in industrial relations, but such codes should be produced by those with a much closer involvement and more day-to-day experience than the Secretary of State is likely to have had.

More important is that if the council of ACAS is made up of representatives of both sides of industry who feel an obligation to ensure the effective implementation of whatever they are recommending, there is much greater likelihood of acceptance and implementation of a code of practice—clearly they will urge it on those whom they represent—than if such a code were seen to be imposed by the Secretary of State.

Notwithstanding our reservations about the clause as a whole and the powers which it gives to the Secretary of State, and our equally strong reservations about the specific power under subsection (7), if this is subsequently to become part of an Act of Parliament, in order to avoid conflict and all the consequences that I have described the very least that should happen is that the Secretary of State should go along to ACAS. He may be able to persuade it that it had the thing wrong and he has it right. ACAS may well say "Yes, the Secretary of State has something. Perhaps we did not understand or give it sufficient consideration." He may persuade ACAS that he has a case. We say that he should be required to go along and persuade it of his case and not proceed further himself unless ACAS thinks that it is a sensible thing to do and that 268 it will be beneficial rather than detrimental to industrial relations generally. It makes good sense to require the Secretary of State to obtain the consent of the Service before proceeding.

Member for Doncaster (Mr. Walker) has raised various anxieties and doubts about the provisions of clause 2, and he is seeking to exclude the Secretary of State from exercising his power to issue a code under the clause in respect of any matter which is already the subject of an ACAS code or in respect of which ACAS has a statutory duty to issue a code. It is, perhaps, helpful if we remind ourselves of the sources of the powers and the duties conferred upon ACAS to issue codes of practice itself. They are to be found in section 6 of the Employment Protection Act 1975.

ACAS has power to give such practical guidance as it thinks fit by way of a code for the purposes of promoting the improvement of industrial relations. As to its duties, there is imposed upon ACAS the particular duty to give practical guidance by code of practice in relation to disclosure of information and, second, time off to be permitted by an employer.

An important point for the Comittee to note is that an ACAS code cannot be laid before Parliament without the approval of the Secretary of State. Even with Parliament's approval, ACAS may only issue a code as a draft. It does not come into effect until such day as the Secretary of State may appoint. It is to the Secretary of State, therefore, that the 1975 Act expressly looks for the approval of even a code drawn up by ACAS in the first place, thereafter to Parliament for approval, and thereafter once again to the Secretary of State for bringing it into effect and investing it with the influence which the Act stipulates.

The implementation of an ACAS code, therefore, made in pursuance of its powers is effectively subordinated to the Secretary JPEG 269 of State in the way that I have indicated. The amendment moved by the right hon. Gentleman can thus be seen to conflict with that distribution of responsibility. I wish to make clear that the Government set great store by the jurisdiction given to ACAS to issue practical guidance. They attach great value to that and to the code-making powers of ACAS, and they envisage that by far the majority of any codes to be produced for industrial relations purposes in future will be produced by ACAS. The reason for that value is primarily that the representative structure of ACAS affords it wide experience of industrial relations so that the guidance that it can offer in a code of practice can be expected to be practical, as the Act itself requires that it shall be.

The Government would not intend to produce, through the Secretary of State, a new or a revised code unless they were convinced that this was clearly necessary and that it could not be done by ACAS. Of course, the right hon. Gentleman raises the question of the circumstances in which this might arise. We believe that it might well arise where an issue of contested policy flavours a straight issue of industrial relations—for example, the application of the closed shop to the freedom of the press. Here, the representative structure of ACAS may result in a sharp difference of opinion within the council between those nominated by the TUC perhaps and those nominated by the CBI, which may prevent it from agreeing to produce or revise a code. The same may be said of the closed shop in other circumstances and, as the Government have already made plain, it is their intention to introduce a code to deal with the institution and the implementation of a closed shop agreement, drawing upon the best practice of the time.

We believe, therefore, that it must be right that this power should lie with the Secretary of State. But there is no ground for fear that ACAS will be disregarded, or that its code, if any is already in existence, will be automatically superseded, because under subsection (2) and (6) the Secretary of State has to consult and any inadvertent conflict can be avoided. He has to consult with ACAS. He is required to do that before he can even prepare a draft. The right hon. Gentleman said that a conflict here would be bad for the morale of ACAS. I do not think that that is established, because the circumstances in which it is envisaged that the Secretary of State will issue a code are likely to be those where ACAS is unwilling itself to introduce a code of its own. The provisions for consultation that I have referred to will, in all foreseeable circumstances, be sufficient to ensure that there is no adverse effect upon the moral of ACAS, which is 272 the last thing that this Government would want.

The right hon. Gentleman asked me what were the resources available to the Secretary of State for drawing up a code of practice. He said that many experienced staff—this is indeed the case—had been transferred from the Department of Employment to ACAS when ACAS was set up. There is no intention to draw up numerous codes. ACAS is largely staffed by Department of Employment civil servants and mobility between ACAS and the Department of Employment will continue. The consultation that I have already referred to, which is the duty of the Secretary of State, will ensure that sufficient expertise in the matters which I have referred to will be available to the Secretary of State for this purpose.

This is certainly, I hope this is clear from what I have already said—not capable of being seen as a vote of no confidence in ACAS. The Government have the greatest confidence in ACAS.
