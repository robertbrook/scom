I remind the Committee that the effect of clause 9 is to enable the tribunal at one fell swoop to hear all the relevant issues. Under clause 9, we are concerned with whether the employer will be able to bring in the union and say to the tribunal "I admit I dismissed this chap. I only did it because my arm was twisted by the trade union, and justice requires that the tribunal should hear what I have to say about that". Then, after the tribunal has heard what the union has to say, it should make up its mind, first, whether there was an unfair dismissal, and, if so, whether it was brought about because the trade union in some respects twisted the employer's arm. If that is established, the JPEG 1057 tribunal ought to be able to decide the proportion of responsibility and award compensation accordingly. That is the theme and purpose of the clause.

First, there has to be a claim for unfair dismissal. Second, the employer admits the dismissal but considers that he was forced to effect it. Third, the employer claims that he was induced by the trade union to do it because the employee concerned was a non-union member. Fourth, the tribunal joins, if so advised. Fifth, the tribunal determined the unfair dismissal issue, with the assistance of the trade union—which can contest that if it wants to—and then, if, but only if, it finds unfair dismissal does it award compensation.

The tribunal next considers and determines whether the complaint of inducement is well founded, and lastly, it considers and determines whether to make a contribution order, the criterion being whether it would be just and equitable to do so.

In all those stages, once joinder has taken place, which is early on, it is open to the trade union to contest any evidence, to challenge it, to cross-examine it, to adduce evidence of its own and to make representations on its own behalf.

We shall put pressure upon you through industrial action. We shall black you or call our members out. We shall exert against you, the employer, exactly the same kind of pressure that we exerted against you to get rid of Mr. X. Notwithstanding that we have not managed to get a closed shop agreement out of you, we shall twist your arm just as hard to get the money back as we twisted it in the first place to make you get rid of Mr. X, with the consequence that we have been held liable by the tribunal to contribute to his unfair dismissal compensation."

That is the reality of the matter. I have too great a respect for the hon. and learned Gentleman to believe that he can seriously suppose that to forestall that sort of action JPEG 1095 would be to strike at the rights of trade unions to take industrial action. It cannot be so. What it would do is to forestall the ability of a trade union immune from civil action to twist somebody's arm in order to override or circumvent the order of the tribunal. It therefore puts the matter in a totally different light. I think that it must be common ground, because that is the motive of the trade union's action.

The hon. and learned Gentleman asked me, and he is entitled to an answer, whether the Government wished to see such activity as he posits protected by immunity. The Government would be absurd to wish to see that sort of immunity existing when they had gone to the trouble of conferring upon an employer in these circumstances the right to claim and to receive a contribution. They would be mad to do so. Therefore, my answer must be that it is the sincere hope and intention of the Government that such conduct shall not be cloaked—protected—by immunity.
