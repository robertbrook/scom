The Minister for Trade (Mr. Cecil Parkinson)
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

It might be helpful to the Committee if I set out the general position on the insider dealing clauses as a whole, since certain general themes and issues run 554 through all these new clauses from 42 to 46.

I express the hope that in this instance it will be third time lucky, and that we shall finally succeed in having on the statute book an acceptable set of clauses dealing with the problem of insider dealing.

In 1973 the Stock Exchange and the Take-Over Panel issued a joint statement calling for criminal sanctions against insiders. In the 1973 Companies Bill the then Conservative Government proposed criminal sanctions and some civil remedies. In that Bill much of the structure of subsequent proposals was established, and many of the definitions, although subsequently refined and, we believe, improved, were first set out.

The main criticism of that Bill was that there was no requirement for the prosecution to prove mens rea, or guilty mind, and that criticism was regularly repeated. The Bill, of course, fell with the general election of February 1974.

In 1978 the hon. Member for Hackney, Central (Mr. Davis), who played a large and important role in our discussions of company law in the years 1974–79 and who earned himself a considerable reputation for doing so, came forward with his own Bill. In that Bill a clear distinction was drawn between Stock Exchange deals and others. There were criminal sanctions for Stock Exchange deals and civil sanctions for the latter.

I found it puzzling at the time, and have not yet found anyone who could explain to me, why there were those two different approaches. I think that the explanation is that there was much more likely to be a face-to-face transaction off the Stock Exchange so that it might be possible, for instance, for the person who had sold the shares at below price to follow the insider and to tackle him for his profit. I think that was the reason. When I read these notes recently, it reminded me of my puzzlement at the time.

There was some refinement of the definitions in the clauses, both before introduction of the Bill at that time, and in Committee, and we introduced the concept of mens rea. But there was widespread concern about the width of the prohibition and definitions. There was much debate on the subject during that Committee stage, and widespread JPEG 555 dissatisfaction was expressed that the clauses were too widely drawn and that the definitions were not sufficiently accurate.

Earlier this year, after lengthy consultation and consideration, the Stock Exchange adopted a model code for directors' share dealings, with certain defined periods when dealings were not to take place, and other times when dealings could take place, but there was a procedure for prior notification and acknowledgment.

Finally, we have our own set of proposals, published last month, but already widely discussed. A number of substantial representations have been received about our proposals, and we believe that after a six-year period of more or less continuous debate on the subject, during which proposals have been put forward, refined and—in our opinion—improved, we now have a set of proposals in which, although they are obviously capable of improvement, we believe we have dealt with most of the flaws in previous proposals. The proposals contained in new clause 42 and the succeeding clauses should do the job that successive Governments—and not only Governments but influential bodies in the City and people involved in these issues—have felt should be done.

I come now to the central issue, the imposition of criminal sanctions. The Government's view, which I stated quite clearly on Second Reading, is that self-regulation is not enough. That view is widely held in the City, in industry and commerce and, I believe, in the House. It is notable that many of the critics of our proposals say that they believe that insider dealing should be a criminal offence and that there should be criminal sanctions. They may object to some aspect of our proposals, but they believe that criminal sanctions are needed if we are to outlaw insider dealing and make it a much less attractive proposition.

In saying that, I in no way denigrate self-regulation. It has played an important role in filling the gap caused by successive failures by Governments to get proposals such as these on to the statute book and in promoting a basic change in attitude towards insider dealing. But to make the Government's position clear, I make the clear and unequivocal statement that we believe that insider dealing 556 is wrong and should be subject to criminal sanctions.

I shall give the Committee examples, one of which I gave during our Committee proceedings on the previous Bill, to demonstrate why I personally feel so strongly about this crime. I say "crime" because I believe it to be a crime.

Some years ago, when I was in practice as an accountant, my firm acted as registrars of a company. A bid was mounted for the company, which was in great difficulties and likely to collapse. After discussions, a person decided to mount a bid for the company. Between the taking of the decision and the date of the formal announcement, a parcel of over 100,000 shares was acquired continuously in the market. The market in those shares was very narrow. This activity in the market pushed the price of the shares to very near the bid price, and almost torpedoed the bid. Had the bid not gone ahead the company would, I believe, have collapsed. Employment would have been lost. Indeed, a business would have been lost, which was subsequently turned round and built into a very successful business by the person who took it over.

JPEG 557 Several years later, we discovered, through a coincidence with which I shall not bore the Committe, that the person who had been dealing in the market was one of the directors of the company and that he had made a very substantial profit by his activities.

It seemed to me that that person would not have been deterred by the prospect of civil action against him, but that he would have thought twice if one of the possibilities had been that he would have had to appear in the dock at the Old Bailey, charged with a criminal offence. The very fact that that might have been a possible result of his activities would, in my opinion, have been a substantial deterrent. And if that prospect had not prevented him from doing what he did—as in my view it would have done—it would quite properly have opened him up to severe punishment for what I believe was a crime.

That was my first contact with a real insider. Ever since those days, I have had very strong views, especially since I subsequently came across another case which, similarly, almost had disastrous results.

I am convinced, and I know that the hon. Member for Hackney, Central is convinced, that we must press ahead with this legislation. But I realise that the introduction of criminal law into a new area causes feelings of apprehension, and it is essential that the definitions should be as precise as possible, so that people should know clearly what their obligations are and at what stage they are committing a crime.

The Government believe that our proposals achieve the desired result, but we are open to suggestions about improving them. We look forward to what members of the Committee have to say, but we believe that the proposals at present before the Committee constitute a viable set of measures.

I turn now to those proposals in detail. First, I shall set out the main changes from the earlier legislation. We believe that new clause 42 includes a better definition of unpublished price-sensitive information. Our definition includes a restriction relating to information about specific matters. It excludes general overall impressions. It is more specific than the earlier definitions. We 558 believe that by the new wording we have removed some of the difficulties, debated strongly in the Committee on the last Bill, about what information is generally available and about the problems of information that might be available on request. On whose request? That was the question. We believe that we have removed those difficulties.

We think that the introduction of a restriction on information obtained by an insider in his capacity as such, and on information which it is reasonable for such a person not to disclose, except for the proper performance of his functions, will be welcomed by the Committee.

We believe that that encapsulates the element of confidentiality within a company in a way that avoids problems and uncertainties, some of which were pointed out to us on the previous Bill in a rather negative way. We think that the proposals before the Committee help towards achieving a better definition of activities that the previous Committee considered to be a problem.

We have moved away from the requirement in face-to-face transactions to disclose a connection with a company. It would not have been satisfactory and would have meant very little to many people. It would not have applied to tippees, it could have been avoided by the use of nominee companies, and could have trapped the innocent very easily. We have proposed instead an extension of the approach that we take to Stock Exchange deals and to other deals in similar off-Stock Exchange markets.

We have dropped the special power to appoint inspectors to investigate suspected cases of insider dealing. We do not think that it is right to provide such far-reaching powers that can threaten individual rights. If such powers are not generally needed for the investigation of crime, they should not be needed in this area.

Those are the key features of our proposals.

Although, some years ago, the series on television—"The Power Game"—gave the impression that big business is a permanent, 100 per cent. full-time drama, I do not believe it. I do not believe that the directors of businesses, on a daily basis, are in possession of information which, if known, would move the price mountain. Therefore, although there will be occasions when directors know things which are not published, which are price-sensitive and which, if revealed, might have an impact on the shares—or, when revealed, will do so—such directors will know the distinction between their day-to-day knowledge about their business and their knowledge of important factors which, when revealed to the market, will shift the price. It is in that set of circumstances that we wish to protect the shareholder.
