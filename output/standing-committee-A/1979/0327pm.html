I do not propose to read long extracts from the Donovan report. No doubt that will be to your relief, Mr. Goodhew, and that of the Committee. But I ought to make one or two references to the Report. The first reference to the Terms and Conditions of Employment Act 1959 occurs very early in the report—in paragraph 41 on page 10. In paragraph 40 it is stated that:

"Parliament has long been committed to the view that the best means of settling such questions"—

"is voluntary collective bargaining"—

Paragraph 41 states:

"One example of support for collective agreements is the Fair Wages Resolution of the House of Commons. The first was passed in 1891".

That shows that we are not talking about anything new. After a brief reference to the Fair Wages Resolution, paragraph 41 continues:

"Other examples are the Terms and Conditions of Employment Act 1959, under which an employer may be required under certain conditions to observe terms and conditions not less favourable than those established for his trade or industry by collective bargaining; and the statutory obligation placed on the boards of nationalised industries to seek agreement with trade unions over the establishment of negotiating machinery for settling terms and conditions of work for their employees."

The authors of the Donovan report refer to the Act many times, and always in Image Col. 1655 approving terms. Clearly they felt it to be an important piece of legislative machinery. There are many references to that Act in the report. But at no stage did Donovan suggest that we could do without the basis the underpinning, of collective bargaining provided by such legislation.
