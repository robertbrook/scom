The Secretary of State for Employment (Mr. James Prior): I have been asked a number of questions by the right hon. Member for Doncaster (Mr. Walker), and I shall try to answer them.

The right hon. Gentleman made great play about the fact that if there was a conflict on the shop floor, it would have to be determined by the courts. He said that this was a fertile ground for litigants. In my view, there is no need for concern here. Section 3(4) of the 1976 Act says:

"For the purposes of this Act employees are to be treated, in relation to a union membership agreement, as belonging to the same elass if they have been identified as such by the parties to the agreement, and employees may be so identified by reference to any characteristics or circumstances whatsoever."

That means, in my opinion, that it is a matter on the shop floor for the employer and the union—or, if more than one union is concerned, the unions—to agree among themselves who should be identified as belonging to the same class by the parties to the agreement, which must be the unions involved and the employer, and the employees may be so identified by reference to any characteristics or circumstances whatsoever. That is the situation that will persist. In my view, Col. 876 therefore, this can in no circumstances be the subject of litigation.

Mr. Ted Fletcher: What would happen in the event of failure to agree?

Mr. Prior: There is nothing at the moment which can force one party to a union membership agreement to agree. If the employer is so determined, he cannot be forced to agree to a union membership agreement. A lot of pressure may be put on him by a union. The union may go on strike or take certain action in support of its desire. But there is nothing in law which can actually force an employer to make a union membership agreement. In certain circumstances he might be unwise not to make a union membership agreement, but there is nothing that can force him to do so.

Another question was whether the employer would be able to veto a ballot which excluded some and not others. Really the answer is the same. Yes, of course. A ballot will take place on a union membership agreement, and if the employer does not agree, there is no union membership agreement. It is as straightforward as that. It is a risk that the employer has to take.

Another question asked by the right hon. Gentleman was whether it was right that those who voted "No" nevertheless had absolute protection? In the circumstances that we are laying down, if the people who vote "No" are existing employees—in other words, if they are not members at the relevant time—they do not have to join a union. That is the rule that we are laying down for the existing work force.

The right hon. Gentleman then asked "What will the questions be?" The Bill leaves it to the parties to decide the actual questions. The ballot must be held on the principle of whether there is to be a closed shop—that is to say, that employees be required, subject to the statutory provisions of any additional protection given in the union membership agreement, to join the specified union or unions.

Again, the right hon. Gentleman asked "Who will agree who the employees affected are? Will we reach the stage of some employees in the shop being members of the AU EW and some being members of the sheet metal workers' union or some other union?" That is entirely a matter Image Col. 877 for the union membership agreement to define. The portion of the I976 amendment Act that I quoted would cover the operation of the union membership agreement in that respect.

That matters that the right hon. Gentleman raised have proved useful subjects of discussion. Again, I stress the importance that we attach to the need for a ballot of all those involved in a group of workers before a closed shop comes into operation. It makes such a change in the circumstances of those involved at their place of work that there ought to be a secret ballot and an overwhelming majority, which we have stated as being 80 per cent. voting in favour of those affected, before a closed shop can be brought in.

I think that I have answered the right hon. Gentleman's questions. I hope that in view of the assurance which I have been able to give him, he will feel able to withdraw the amendment.
