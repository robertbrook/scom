I hoped that when the Attorney-General was with us he would give us some background information as to the evolution of the law on picketing. It was first referred to in the 1875 Act which set out various proposals for containing violence. This was the Conspiracy and Protection of Property Act 1875. There are many sections of that Act still in force today. The Act specifies as illegal five specific things if they are done to compel someone to do, or to abstain from doing, anything lawful—that is, if he (i) uses violence to, or intimidates such other person, his wife or children, or injures his property; (ii) persisently follows him; (iii) hides his tools or other property, or deprives him of them, or hinders their use; (iv) watches or besets any house or place where he is or any approach to it; (v) follows him in the street with two or more others in a disorderly manner.

Those crimes are applicable to all picketing, irrespective of whether there is a trade dispute. The Act, however, sought to legalise peaceful picketing and to make a distinction between the criminal law and picketing. There was a proviso in that Act which deemed that attending at or near a place merely in order to obtain or communicate information was legal.

Those were the first rights that the trade unions had to conduct peaceful picketing. After that, the judges got their teeth into it and began to emasculate the Act. As a result, in 1906 we had the notorious Taff Vale judgment in which the Associated Society of Railway Servants was heavily fined as a consequence of an industrial dispute. The situation created by the 1906 judgment caused such a stir in the country—the hon. Member for Rochdale (Mr. Smith) may be interested in this—that it brought about a landslide for the Liberal Party. It gave birth to the Labour Party, which, for the first time, got a substantial number of Members of Parliament. There was tremendous outrage at that method of crippling the trade union movement, and as a consequence the 1906 Act was passed.

Sometimes that frightening is carried to the extent that the criminal offence of intimidation is perpetrated. Of course I agree with the Attorney-General that the criminal law is adequate in the sense that a new offence is not needed. It is no good putting a new offence on the statute book—that it shall be an offence to commit a criminal offence. There are enough criminal offences on the statute book to deal under the law with all the offences against public order that can be committed on the picket line.

The problem is enforcement. It is not enough to say "If a policeman hears the offence of intimidation being committed, he can arrest the offender. End of problem." The policeman must be present at the time. He must hear the words. There must be enough policemen to cope in a private situation. It is for that reason that it is not enough to rely on the existence of the criminal law and the ability of the police to arrest someone who is guilty of the criminal offence of intimidation. That is not enough.

But, more often than not, the frightening conduct does not extend to the scale of the criminal offence of intimidation. It extends Col. 1412 only to what the previous Attorney-General, in a memorable phrase, described in the House of Commons as lawful intimidation. It is for that reason that public opinion is so much against secondary picketing.

To that extent that it is criminal, it is not enough to say, "Let the police get on and deal with it", for the reasons that I have ventured to suggest, especially in relation to the points made by the hon. Member for Chester-le-Street (Mr. Radice) last Tuesday. If one acknowledges that there is a problem and that rights are being interfered with which should not be interfered with, one must look to see if there are any means, short of adding to the criminal law, to remedy that situation. We believe that there are.

We believe that, not by using the criminal law but by amending the civil law, it is possible to diminish the number of occasions on which people are deflected from doing what they want by frightening conduct, such as "If you come in here you will never work here again", or "You will lose your card and you will be blacked". The way in which we believe it is possible to do that is reflected in the Bill.
