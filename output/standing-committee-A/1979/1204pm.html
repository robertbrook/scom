Mr. Parkinson
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The purpose of the clause is to specify the particulars which are to be given for each transaction, attangement or agreement for which the previous clause requires disclosure. Basically, the requirement is the very simple one stated in the opening to subsection (1), that the principal terms of such deals shall be entered into the accounts for each financial year, except for recognised banks, to which, as we know, separate procedures will apply.

The "principal terms" include those relating to the advancement of the cash or non-cash asset, and the arrangements for the repayment of the value of such an asset, including any interest component, together with any securities or guarantees thereto.

The clause goes on to state two particulars which are required of any transaction JPEG 521 and so forth, and then specifies certain details, depending on the sort of transaction and so forth in question. Thus, paragraphs ( a) and ( b) require the statement of the obvious, namely, that the transaction and so forth was made or subsisted during the financial year in question and that the director involved be named.

Next, paragraph ( c) requires that, where the material interest test applies, that is, for transactions and so forth other than those defined in new clause 23, the director be named and his interest disclosed.

Paragraph ( d) provides that certain details must be provided in the case of loans and agreements to enter into loans, and also loans subject to arrangements for assignment or back-to-back deals.

Paragraph ( e) covers guarantees and securities, and arrangements for the assignment of guarantees and securities.

Paragraph ( f) requires that in the case of any other transactions and so forth other than those covered above in paragraphs ( d) and ( e), the financial quantum to be disclosed is the value of such transaction or arrangement.

Paragraph ( f) is therefore a very concise way of requiring the following particulars. For quasi-loans, the sum to be disclosed is the amount to be reimbursed, in the case of the company buying goods, for example, on behalf of a director, as happened in the Peachey case, or the maximum amount to be reimbursed, in the case of company credit cards. For credit transactions the sum to be disclosed is the arm's length value of the goods and services concerned.

Arrangements for assignments are to be disclosed by reference to the value of the basic transactions to which they relate, except where they relate to loans, guarantees and securities, in which case the sums prescribed earlier, in paragraphs ( d) and ( e), are applicable.

Finally, arrangements for back-to-back deals are to be disclosed by reference to the value of the basic transactions and so forth to which they relate, except in the case of loans, which are covered by paragraph ( d).

The Committee will recall that when we looked at new clause 24 and discussed the exemption for recognised banks from the prohibitions on lending and so on we noted that a separate disclosure regime was to apply for recognised banks as regards the transactions and arrangements specified in new clause 23. Mr. Parkinson
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

This clause provides threshold or de minimis limits for various purposes in relation to the disclosure provisions in new clauses 28 and 31.

The effect of subsections (1) and (2) is to provide a de minimis limit for the disclosure of credit transactions. If the aggregate of the outstanding liabilities under credit transactions, or any arrangements relating to such transactions, of a director, together with his connected persons, do not exceed £5,000 at any time during the financial year, then none of these transactions need to be disclosed in the accounts.

This threshold limit is necessary in order to avoid an excessive number of entries in the company accounts. Petty transactions involving deferred payment by directors are very common and are not considered to be the subject of an abuse which we wish to control; it is only when such transactions involve larger sums that disclosure is necessary in order to prevent abuse.

Subsection (3) provides a threshold limit for the disclosure of the residual category of transactions described in subsections (1)( c) and (2)( c) of new clause 28, namely, transactions which do not fall within the loan, quasi-loan, or credit transaction categories but in which a director has a "material interest"; these are mainly property transactions of the type described in new clause 22 and contracts for service covered in new clause 21.

The de minimis figure, below which such transactions do not require to be disclosed, is flexible to take account of the needs of different sizes of company. If the aggregate of all such transactions involving a director and his connected person does not exceed £1,000, then, for all sizes of company, they will not have to be disclosed. For large companies the relevant figure is £5,000, and for companies whose net assets are worth between £1,000 and 528 £5,000, disclosure is required only if the aggregate figure exceeds 1 per cent. of the company's net assets.

The effect of subsection (4) is to exempt disclosure by recognised banks, in the manner described in new clause 31, of any transaction and so forth if the aggregate of the values of each transaction made for that director or for any person connected with him does not exceed £1,000 at any time during the financial year. The purpose of this is to avoid cluttering the statement available to shareholders with unnecessary detail of transactions with directors.

Hon. Members may wonder why this threshold is considered necessary for banks but not for other companies. The reasoning behind this is that the volume of loans and quasi-loans made by a recognised bank to its directors and connected persons—which includes associated companies—in the ordinary course of business is likely to be considerable. For other companies such transactions and 'so forth are not so common and, in the case of loans and quasi-loans, are subject to varying degrees of prohibition.

The purpose behind all the threshold limits detailed in this clause is to eliminate the disclosure of unnecessary information, and I trust that hon. Members will find them acceptable.

Mr. Eyre
: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

Subsection (1) lists the persons to be included in the definition of connected persons. First of all, there are a director's immediate family, taken to include a spouse—including a separated, but not divorced, spouse—and children under the age of 18, and the definition of children is to include any step-children and illegitimate children.

Next, any company with which a director is "associated" is also deemed to be a connected person of that director for the purpose of most of our provisions. The definition of association with a body corporate is given in subsection (3)( a); only if the director, together with his connected persons, is interested in at least 20 per cent. of the share capital of the company or is able to control at least 20 per cent. of the voting power at any general meeting is he deemed to be "associated" with that company. An exception to this rule of connection is given in new clause 24(5), which exempts loans made by money-lending companies to directors' associated companies from being included in the calculation of relevant amounts for the purpose of the maximum permitted limit; in other words, money-lending companies are allowed to lend to such associated companies provided they are not controlled by the director without limit—though, as hon. Members will recall, we shall bring forth an amendment to ensure the application of the rules of ordinary course of business and normal commercial terms to loans, etc., to associated companies. A similar exemption will also be granted, as I have already explained, for the disclosure of loans, etc., by those money-lending companies which are recognised banks. Neither of these exemptions extends to cover companies in which directors have a controlling interest, and the definition of control of a body corporate is given in subsection (3)( b), namely, that directors—together with their connected persons—are either interested in more than 50 per cent. of the share 536 capital or can control over 50 per cent. of the voting power at any general meeting of the company.

The tailpiece to subsection (3) excludes associated companies—and trustees of trusts whose beneficiaries include such companies—from the circle of the directors' connected persons for the purpose of calculating if a company is to be associated with or controlled by a director by virtue of his, and his connected persons', interests in such a company. This exclusion is to avoid the problem of circularity in definition, and to prevent the necessity of long and complex calculations of relatively remote interests, involving the collection of information about companies' holdings in other companies, which may be dfficult to establish. We have, however, been careful to ensure that this exclusion does not provide a loophole through which a director may benefit from transactions with a company controlled indirectly by himself, through the intermediary of another company which he controls and which in turn controls the first company. We have provided, in subsection 4( b), that a director's "control of the exercise of voting power" is to include control through a company controlled by the director.

The last category of persons included in the "connected person" definition in subsection (1) consists of the trustees of trusts whose beneficiaries may include a director or any of his other connected persons. Trustees under a pension scheme or an employees' share scheme, as defined in clause 56, are excluded, so as to allow executive directors to participate in share incentive schemes.

Any person included under any of these categories is deemed to be excluded from the definition of connected persons if he or she also happens to be a director of the company. In other words, if, say, husband and wife are both directors of the same company, they are to be treated separately for the purpose of calculating relevant amounts; thus, under the money-lending exemption, for example, husband and wife will each be allowed to receive up to £50,000 in loans on ordinary commercial terms. Thus, all directors are on an equal footing, notwithstanding any connections between them.

Finally, subsection (4)( a) defines "equity share capital" for the purposes of this clause, by reference to section JPEG 537 154 of the 1948 Act, and means the issued share capital—but excluding non-participating preference shares, which are generally regarded, with debentures, as prior charges.

New clause 37 and the Government amendments supplementing it are purely consequential and do not merit much discussion. However, I should say a short word about subsection (2) of new clause 37, which has the effect of repealing the relevant disclosure provisions in the 1948 and 1967 Acts, which are superceded by the disclosure provisions in our Bill. In formulating the disclosure provisions, we did not want to introduce any element of retrospection which would require companies to disclose in their accounts any transaction or arrangement that was entered into and was completed—that is, under which no liabilities remained—before the appointed day. All such transactions were, therefore, excluded from our new disclosure provisions, but for the purpose of the accounts prepared in relation to the first financial year ending after the appointed day, it is important that any such transactions that are thus exempted from the new disclosure regime are still covered by the existing disclosure rules. Thus, the accounts for this particular financial year will be covered by two different sets Of rules. We recognise that this may be slightly burdensome for some companies for the first year of the operation of our provisions, but this is unavoidable if we wish—and this is our prime wish—the disclosure provisions to be effective from the appointed day while ruling out any element of retrospection.

Section 16(1)( c) of the 1967 Act, which requires disclosure in the direc JPEG 547 tors' report of contracts in which a director has an interest, is repealed because these contracts are now required to be disclosed in the accounts. It is appropriate that all the details of transactions involving directors should appear in one place—namely in the accounts—and disclosure in the accounts of these particular contracts renders disclosure in the directors' report superfluous.
