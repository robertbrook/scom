Mr. Walker
: I must tell you at the outset, Mr. Goodhew, that I drafted these amendments on certain assumptions that seem to have been knocked somewhat askew by the statement of the Chancellor of the Exchequer in opening his Budget yesterday. I felt that whilst obviously there were many matters which deeply divided, the two sides of the Committee, none the less there was some common ground. I say that I had assumed that. I felt—to take a step-by-step approach, which is fashionable these days—first, that we all believed that the best way to conduct our industrial relations system was by collective bargaining and that that was a belief and approach to industrial relations that was shared not only by members of the Committee but by members of the Royal Commission on trade unions and, indeed, by the Chancellor himself. I remember that when the Industrial Relations Bill was presented to the House in 1970, those who were then responsible for that legislation declared their belief in the idea that our industrial relations system should be based on collective bargaining conducted through strong trade unions. So I assumed that we shared a view that collective bargaining was the best way of conducting our industrial relations system.

It follows that, if we believe in collective bargaining, we believe in bargaining through collective organisations—workers' organisations—and that we then believe that those organisations should conduct their collective bargaining with employers 1590 or employers' associations on the basis of a mutual recognition of one party by the other. We know that in many cases all our assumptions are not shared by employers; that many employers resist trade union organisation of their work force or at least refuse to enter into negotiations and discussions with the relevant trade unions. I thought that we shared that assumption and that therefore we all accepted that trade unions ought to have the right to seek to be recognised for purposes of collective bargaining by employers, and that all that divided us on this was what were the right means of doing it or the extent to which trade unions should have recourse to machinery of one kind or another when faced with a recalcitrant, stubborn and pig-headed employer who refused to recognise them.

Unhappily, however, these assumptions were somewhat knocked askew by the Chancellor of the Exchequer in his Budget statement yesterday. I do not want to trespass on amendments that we shall be debating later in the day, Mr. Goodhew. Doubtless there are other parts of the right hon. Gentleman's statement yesterday which my hon. My assumption was that we all shared the view that trade unions were essential for collective bargaining and that collective bargaining should be the basis of our industrial relations system, and that it followed from that that trade unions should have the right to seek to be recognised for collective bargaining purposes. I turn to the new clause which seeks to JPEG 1593 exclude the courts or any other judicial bodies from involvement in this process. We have from time to time talked about the conflict that arises between the courts and those engaged in industrial relations. The courts are bound—it is in their very nature—to have regard to the letter of the law, whereas those engaged in managing industrial relations—full-time trade union officials, shop stewards, and managements at all levels—are concerned not so much with the law as with keeping good relations, keeping production going and avoiding disputes. Often the letter of the law and the conduct of industrial relations are incompatible.

That is not to say that we should not have provisions laid down in an Act of Parliament. But to those who say that an Act of Parliament ought to be enforceable or treatable by some judicial body, I reply that that is generally but not always true.

What I have suggested in the new clause is by no means unprecedented. It is not unique. The Trade Union Act 1913 excludes from certain of its provisions the intervention of the courts. Indeed, the words that I originally drafted were culled direct from that statute.

I give an example. The hon and learned Gentleman will recall that when the Employment Protection Bill was in Committee, we debated certain parts of the 1975 Act and I think that there was an amendment about part of section 14. This dealt with an obligation on the Service in the course of attempting to resolve a recognition reference to it, to consult the workers. We provided that if a trade union had made a reference to the Service complaining that an employer refused recognition and the trade union thought that recognition should be accorded, first, if the Service thought that it was a valid reference, it would by conciliation seek to resolve the difficulties and make inquiries.

We provided in section 14 of the 1975 Act:

"in the course of its inquiries into a recognition issue under section 12 or 13 above the Service shall ascertain the opinions of workers to whom the issue relates by any means it thinks fit".

 I argued in the Committee then, and, as I said, I think that there was an amendment, the case for changing the deliberate vagueness of the obligation on the Service to ascertain the opinions of workers. The then Opposition asked "What does 'ascertain the opinions of workers' mean? Surely it should mean 'the workers' or `all the workers', otherwise it gives the Service the discretion to consult a proportion as it thinks fit." We replied "Yes, that is our intention." We acted on the premise that the Service was staffed by sensible people who would have a great deal of experience in industrial relations, and that such people would make judgments about the right proportion, and about how practical or impractical it might be to consult all the workers. To make the wording other than that which we had JPEG 1595 chosen, to make it "the opinions of the workers" by the insertion of the definite article, would be interpreted—indeed, it could have no other meaning than this—as an obligation to consult all the workers. We might as well have put in the phrase "all the workers".

I pointed out that if we adopted that course, the absence of just one worker in any situation anticipated by the section would automatically invalidate in the court any recommendation made by the Service. The person could be working abroad, or off sick, and so on. The Committee divided, and the vote confirmed the words that the then Government had drafted.

Then there arose an actual case, the Grunwick case, where, for reasons that I shall not go into now, the Service had not consulted all the workers. The case was referred to the court and eventually finished up before Lord Denning, who said, in effect "Ah, how odd of Parliament to put in such a phrase as 'the opinions of workers'. Surely it must mean 'the opinions of the workers', which in turn means all workers. "Lord Denning stated that the Service had not consulted all the workers, therefore the recommendation of the Service became invalid, and his Lordship decided against the Service.

Here was a judge not merely perversely interpreting the statute but actually making law that Parliament had specifically rejected. We had turned down the word which he put in. I maintain that that is a monstrous state of affairs. We must respect the courts, of course, but in turn the courts have a duty to recognise what we put into a statute. When we uphold the courts, we do so in respect of their judicial function, not in the sense that they can override Parliament, or become a one-man parliament to make the law as they wish. That is just one illustration where the judges and the courts have misinterpreted or sought to create a law that Parliament never intended.

For that reason, amongst others, I think that it would be right, if we can devise a satisfactory statutory procedure on the basis of seeking to understand how our industrial relations system works and to strengthen and reinforce that industrial relations system in the interests of good and improved industrial relations to help industry and commerce to get on with its job—and I hope that that is what the Committee is all about—to do that without the 1596 risk of it being interfered with and upset by judicial figures who perhaps have scant knowledge of and little regard for those matters.

Some may say that, desirable as that is, it is no part of the function of our judges to be concerned primarily with ensuring that improved industrial relations prevail. It is their task to interpret, as they feel right, the statutes passed by Parliament—to interpret those statutes not in the light of experience in industrial relations, because they do not have that, but in the light of their accumulated experience of the law and the letter of the law, conditioned by their own class origins, background and social milieu.
