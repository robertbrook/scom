Mr. Arthur Davidson
: Perhaps I may 1306 now sum up. I was explaining that the phrase "place of work" assumes enormous importance in the Bill. In fact, "place of work" is the phrase on which this whole clause turns. The notes on clause 14 which the Government have kindly provided and which are of great assistance make it quite clear:

"The new description of picketing declared to be lawful when in contemplation or furtherance of a trade dispute covers:

  • "(i) a person picketing at his own place of work;
  • "(ii) a trade union official accompanying a member of his union when the latter is picketing at his own place of work;
  • "(iii) a person who does not normally have one particular place of work, or for whom it is not practicable to picket at his actual place of work, who may then picket at the premises of his employer from which he works, or from which his work is administered; and
  • "(iv) an unemployed person picketing at his former place of work in contemplation or furtherance of a trade dispute connected with the termination of his employment."

    • The purpose of the clause is to limit the rights of lawful picketing to places of work and subsidiary places of work. Yet the notes on clauses contain one of the most throwaway lines in the history of legislation:

    "'Place of work' is not defined in the clause but for most workers the term has an obvious meaning."

    It may have an obvious meaning for some workers, but I shall be interested to hear whether the Government are suggesting that the Court of Appeal, or the courts in general, would necessarily find the place of work to be quite as obvious as the workers are supposed to find it. There will be different interpretations and meanings. A plethora of legislation, different decisions and a general aura of uncertainty and complexity will be added to what is already a difficult field.

    Let me give two examples of what, to workers, would appear to be obvious places of work. As the clause is drafted, there appears to be no entitlement for workers in a factory to picket a corporate head office even if the personnel policy intimately affecting the workers' wage levels, rights and conditions of work is decided only at head office. The clause as drafted seems to cast great doubt on whether a worker is entitled to picket a head office even if it is only at the head office that industrial policy and the workers' rights and conditions have been and are being decided, those matters being JPEG 1307 the very cause of the dispute—the reason why he and his fellow workers are picketing.

    Do the Government seriously suggest that that obvious place of work—obvious to the workers—is not an obvious place of work to the Government? In other words, will the workers picketing that head office lay themselves open to an action for damages, which is the remedy that it is contemplated that the employer will have under the Bill in those circumstances?
