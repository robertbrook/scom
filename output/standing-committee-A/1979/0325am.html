The first example was the enormous 1482 damage done to ICI during the 1979 strike. It is important to emphasise that not one employee of ICI was actually involved in the dispute, yet 38 of its factories were shut down and £100 million worth of sales were lost, of which £30 million was for export. The same story was repeated throughout industry, and this has happened again in the steel strike.

My point is that, translated on to a national scale, secondary picketing has meant that these disputes, instead of being contained at their place of origin—as they should be, according to good industrial relations practice—are now taking a fearful toll throughout the whole of industry. If, as I believe to be the case, secondary picketing has actually destroyed jobs on a significant scale, I cannot see how the Opposition can be content to allow it to remain a regular feature of British industrial life.

My second example is the events which took place outside the factory where I was working during that strike in 1979. One morning, right out of the blue, flying pickets arrived. They failed to dissaude the workers in our factory from going in, which was not surprising as they had no wish to be involved in the dispute. They could only lose by it. But the pickets then turned their attention to lorries, and these they had more effect in stopping. The reason that they were effective is that many lorry drivers will simply not go through any picket line. Some companies will not allow their own drivers to go through a picket line for fear of being blacked. In other cases it is enough—I have actually stood on the picket line and watched this—if pickets ask a few questions such as what union a lorry driver belongs to or whether they can see his union card, the kind of so-called lawful intimidation that was referred to last week.

There is no need for overt intimidation. The effect is the same, which is to cut off essential materials to a plant and to cause lay-offs among thousands of workers who have no involvement and want no involvement in the dispute. Only a small number of people are needed to bring a complex process plant to a halt very quickly. In the case that I witnessed, the anger of employees in the factory was such that a large phalanx of women production workers came out in the lunch hour and virtually drove them away, in a less than peaceful manner.

We have really had a stand part debate, which has been extremely helpful. It enables me, without seeming to be discourteous in any way to those who have moved or spoken to these amendments from the Opposition Benches, to deal briefly with the first amendment, No. 171.

The effect of that amendment would be to delete the whole of subsection (2) and with it the heart and purpose of clause 14. The subsection provides that section 13 immunity shall not apply to anything done while picketing elsewhere than at the picket's own place of work, which would be actionable at common law. In other words, if one interferes as a picket with a business elsewhere than where one works oneself, one risks an action—in practice, as we know, for an injunction, but nominally for damages.

Last Thursday, I once again detained the Committee for too long in explaining the Government's commitment to the principle of the clause, but I hope that it is beyond dispute that I did explain it. I therefore pass at once to amendment No. 170. The effect would be that those picketing outside the new section 15 as provided by clause 14 would lose the immunity provided by section 13(1) of the Trade Union and Labour Relations Act 1974 but would, apparently, keep the immunity provided by the rest of section 13.

That would create the opposite of certainty. It would convey to the courts that Parliament intended some immunity to remain under section 13 for all picketing, but it would not say clearly what. It could have the effect of retaining the immunity for indirect inducement of a breach of contract of employment and, if so, it would seriously weaken the effectiveness of the Government's proposal because retained would be immunity under subsections (2), (3) and (4) of section 13.

Subsection (2) of section 13, which would be retained, relates to immunity when interfering with a trade, business or employment, and in so far as the common law would provide a right of action to an employer or to an employee suffering such interference, we should not wish to see immunity from such civil action conferred on the responsible pickets unless they picketed at their own place of work. There is no difference in principle, we believe, between the interference to which section 13(1) relates, the immunity for which would be left untouched by amendment No. 170, and the interference which is governed by section 13(2).

Section 13(3) also confers a fairly important immunity, and we do not believe that it would be right that picketing other than at one's own place of work should be protected in those circumstances. We think that the same principle applies.

Section 13(4) relates to liability for conspiracy. Therefore, we do not think that it would be right to accede to amendment No. 170.
