I support the hon. Gentleman's amendments rather than my own in some ways, because they are more comprehensive. Not only do they establish the right of a person not to belong to a trade union but they also achieve what mine do not. They protect those rights from being circumvented by contractual arrangements. That is highly desirable.

My amendment confines itself to establishing the right of an individual not to belong to a union "on any grounds whatsoever". That principle is enshrined in amendment No. 30. Amendment No. 31 is consequential in that it seeks to omit the new subsections (3B), (3C) and (3D). These are not necessary if an employee cannot be dismissed fairly for not belonging to a union. Subsection (3) should e taken out because that is unnecessary for the same reason. If an employee is not covered by a union membership agreement, he will not take part in the ballot because he will not be entitled to vote, he will not be a member, and therefore that subsection is not necessary. The remaining three amendments are consequential.

The aim of these amendments, whether mine or those put down by the hon. Member for Rochdale, is the same. All seek to undermine and eventually to abolish the closed shop. There is no doubt about that. The justification for seeking to achieve that aim has been aired so often that it is unnecessary to go further than to summarise the arguments that arise.

Foremost is the opportunity that exists for abuse in closed shops. Closed shops constitute a coercive power in the hands of trade union officials. That coercive power enables officials to threaten an individual with the loss of his job and therefore his livelihood; and it is also open to misuse. The power is considerable yet it is not essential—if it is argued that it is—for the maintenance of control and discipline over members. It is not a necessary power; nor is it necessary for furtherance of the commercial prospects of an undertaking. On the other hand, in the wrong hands, the temptation exists JPEG 701 to use that power for political, racial or even corrupt purposes.

A second justification for abolishing the closed shop is that it forces men to join what has hitherto been a voluntary association. It is a proud claim of the trade union movement that it has always been a voluntary association. I understand that, as the hon. Member for Rochdale said, there is an argument that one should contribute to the costs of a body that provides benefits that one enjoys. But some of those benefits are questionable in the minds of many people who belong to trade unions.

Specifically, there are at least 16 trade unions, covering approximately half of all trade union members, which have rules that pledge them to some form of Socialism.

Mr. Gorst

I wish to sidetrack for a moment to ram the point home. It is interesting to see the extent and speed with which union membership agreements have grown in recent years. In the mid-1960s 3/4; million people were in such arrangements. At present, according to the Employment Gazette that came out in January, the number has grown to 5.2 million. Those who have recently conducted research on the subject say that that figure is probably considerably underestimated because, if one adds unwritten agreements, the figure is more likely to be between 7 million and 9 million. Therefore, some 30 per cent. to 40 per cent. of the 22 million who form the work force of this country are now forced to belong to closed shops.

Returning to the argument against closed shops, I add that they deny employees certain basic rights—not least, the right to belong to a union of their own choice. But there are other arguments. Closed shops create a monopoly in the supply of labour. They deny a trade union member the opportunity to leave if he is dissatisfied with the union of which he has been forced to become 702 member or if he disagrees with the way that the union is run.

Let us suppose, for example, that the union increases subscriptions. That is not fanciful. In February 1979, when a closed shop arrangement was made by the Strathclyde region council, membership subscriptions increased to about £70,000 in that year. I can understand that a member of NALGO in that situation might say "This is a high price for me to have to pay for the alleged benefits that I am offered. Why should I not resign and get out?" But he cannot if he wishes to continue to work in that particular job.

Why should not a trade unionist resign if he feels that the union is inefficient or is inadequately run? It can be argued that he has his democratic right to remove the leadership. But perhaps he cannot do that. Why should he not have the option of resigning? Perhaps he feels that the leadership has been arbitrary or misguided. There can be any one of a number of reasons. But he cannot work elsewhere without his union card, and he therefore loses his way of earning his living.

Those are some of the political, libertarian and economic objections which give cause for disposing of the closed shop.
