EMPLOYMENT BILL

Clause 3
UNREASONABLE EXCLUSION OR EXPULSION FROM TRADE UNION

10.30 a.m.

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Harold Walker
: The clause is intended to supersede the procedures that were established by the Trade Union and Labour Relations (Amendment) Act 1976, which recognised that people who were unfairly denied admission to a union or unfairly excluded from a union should have a means of seeking redress. But the view of the then Government and Parliament, reflecting the views of the Royal Commission, was that these were matters best dealt with by the trade union movement itself, either by the individual union the membership of which was at issue, in accordance with its full rules and procedures, or, failing redress there—which should be the first avenue of redress—within the wider trade union movement. Not least because of the sensitivities, the long-standing traditions and the recognised democratic procedures of trade unions, it was recognised that these matters could be better dealt with by those who understood them, thus more likely avoiding problems which could otherwise arise of difficult inter-union relationships, conflicts between trade unions and so on.

None of that was intended to deny the right of the aggrieved individual to seek redress in the courts. So we had a system which, first, sought to provide an opportunity for the aggrieved individual to seek his rights as he saw them within his own individual trade union, second within the broader trade union movement through the machinery of the independent review committee of the TUC, and finally in the courts.

The Government propose in the clause to remove the role of the trade union 530 movement and to give the aggrieved individual immediate access to the industrial tribunal. In the course of our debates, the Government have not at any time established a need for this change; nor have they shown that the existing procedures are inadequate and not working properly. The Committee rightly twitted the Under-Secretary on his inadequate or abortive attempts to provide arguments and examples to sustain his case. He could not do so.

It seems to us extraordinary that the Government should seek to add to the Bill these needless bits which cannot be beneficial in their outcome and which can only be seen as provocative to the trade union movement. It is for that reason that I shall ask my hon. Friends to vote against the clause.

One matter that we have not so far adequately recognised is the way in which this clause hangs together with the later provisions which further extend the opportunities for individuals to opt out of the obligation to belong to a trade union. It may be difficult for us to continue to avoid that link when we come to discuss the next clause, but I shall avoid testing your patience by exploring that ground now, Mr. Leadbitter. I rest on what I have said and urge my hon. Friends to resist the clause.

I am afraid that the clause will not contribute towards the objective of making the law the longstop rather than the wicket—keeper. The Government will carry the clause because they have the majority to do it, but I think that they will live to rue the day. If they live long enough, they may even seek to change it altogether in the light of the results which will flow from it.

The Government have not cited one instance where that procedure has fallen down and it has been necessary to bring in the industrial court. So we have to consider the basis on which this clause is introduced. Why are people excluded from union membership? In the overwhelming majority of cases, it is because members have dropped out of the union voluntarily by not paying their contributions.

There are many different rules in different unions. Some unions have a rule that a person is out of compliance when his contributions are eight weeks in arrears. Some unions specify 10 weeks or three months. But after a stated period a member becomes oat of compliance if he does not pay his contributions. This does not mean that he is excluded from the union. It means that he is not entitled to the benefits of the union; he cannot receive strike pay, lie cannot 534 receive sickness pay—which is one of the assets of being a member of the union—and he is excluded from taking part in union activities. Some unions exclude members only at the end of the financial year, at 31 December. So a member could become out of compliance in January or February and not until the end of the year would he actually be excluded from union membership.

Many members who have a grievance against the union, or against the local officials, protest by withholding their contributions. It could happen that people who would normally, under the rules of the union, be out of compliance or excluded would say "True, I broke the union rules. I am out of compliance; I have not paid my contribution." But that is not the real reason. They would say "I had a dispute with the union. They have not taken up my case properly "—"I have disagreed with the attitude of the union "—" I think that the local branch is run by Trotskyists "—all sorts of excuses can be brought forward for being out of compliance, and these would have to be considered seriously by an industrial tribunal.

We must therefore consider whether there is any necessity for people to have a right to go to the industrial tribunal if they have been excluded from a union through non-payment of their contributions. In many unions there is no dubiety about the rules. I can remember what happened in my time, although it is 40 years since I joined the Amalgamated Union of Engineering Workers. My hon. Friend the Member for Newton (Mr. Evans) is more up to date on the rules now, but in my time, when I finished my apprenticeship, every new member had to appear at a full branch meeting, before the chairman.

The chairman read out a catechism which started "Have you read the rules of the union?", to which the young chap had to reply "Yes, I have." "Do you understand that the rules of the union are—" so-and-so, going into a long explanation. The chair man then made a solemn declaration about the principles of the union, how members had to stand together, how they must help each other, how they were trying to enhance the position of working people, and he would say that, in addition to the industrial activities of the union, the members were endeavouring to build a new society—this was made quite clear—on a Socialist JPEG 535 basis. That declaration was read to every new member entering the union. When this little ceremony was finished, the chairman shook the chap's hand and he realised that at last he had left his apprenticeship. He had become a man and had joined the union. It was and is still a solemn occasion.

There is no dubiety about the need to observe union rules. In the light of the deep regard for union rules—rules that have been applicable in many unions for 150 years—we should not set up another authority, an industrial tribunal, to question the motives of union rules. My hon. Friend the Member for Bethnal Green and Bow (Mr. Mikardo) gave the example of a Tower Hamlets temperance society which discovered that it had a drunkard as a member. He had disobeyed the rules. I do not for one moment suggest that such a person should be accepted back into membership.

It seems to me that clause 3 is setting up an apparatus that will probably lead to further employment for lawyers, calling in the law in odd cases that could very well be decided on a voluntary basis by the TUC. I warn the Minister that he will step into a quagmire if he goes into this area. There is no necessity to set up a different apparatus to consider questions of admission to or exclusion from a union. The present system seems to work very well. This is one more encumbrance that the Government intend to put in the path of trade unions to make it more difficult for them to operate. The clause should be rejected.
