EMPLOYMENT BILL

Clause 15
ACTS TO COMPEL TRADE UNION MEMBERSHIP

Amendment proposed [this day], No. 176, in page 14, line 36, at end insert

"and in this subsection 'contract' means a contract existing at the time of the act done in respect of which liability is alleged".—[ Mr. Arthur Davidson.]

4.30 p.m.

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Solicitor-General (Sir Ian Percival)
: I am not exactly certain which part of the sentence I had reached at the conclusion of this morning's sitting, but I remember that the point I was making was that, so far as I knew, there was no relevant decision of a court on the meaning of the word "compel". I was suggesting that one inference which may be drawn from that is that there has never been any difficulty over its application. I quite accept that that is not conclusive. I do not put it forward as such, but I think it probably is the reason here. Looking at these matters from a practical point of view—and I think that most right hon. and hon. Members know that despite all that is said about the courts, on the whole their approach is a practical one because they are dealing with practical matters—the advice I give the Committee is that the courts would adopt a practical approach to the word " compel". In so far as they had to put a meaning on it, would put on it the meaning I have advanced, i.e. that it means making someone do something he does not want to do by leaving him no practical alternative but to do it.

An even more practical way of testing what I am saying is by reference to these provisions. It is to be remembered that they come into operation by an employer saying "I have a right of action. I accept that that right of action might be barred by clause 13, unless I can bring myself within the provisions of this clause, but I can bring myself within the provisions of this 1534 clause." In order to do that, he has to satisfy each of the tests about which I have advised the Committee.

Let us translate that into practical terms. Take an employer who is saying in a SLADE situation that this clause applies. Let us assume that on everything down to subsection (2) the court is already satisfied and the employer has next to deal with the requirement of the first part of subsection (2). He has to satisfy the court that what was being done was

"for the purpose of compelling workers to become members of a particular trade union".

In the SLADE situation one could start with a proposition about which there would be no argument, namely that the whole purpose of the action being taken was to get the artworkers to join SLADE. That would still however leave the question: was the purpose to "compel" them to join? I think this is the way that would be approached. One would ask the question: how were they trying to make them join or get them to join? Was it by persuading them or encouraging them, or inducing them? Of course not. It was by compelling them to join by leaving them with no practical alternative but to do so. That is how I think the court would approach the application of the first part of subsection (2).

If I am wrong on this point and the hon. Member for Rotherham (Mr. Crowther) is right, far from widening the application of the subsection that would narrow it. The way this subsection operates, as I have said, is that it will be the employer who wants to bring himself within the provisions of the clause. It will be he who has to satisfy the court that the purpose of the action of which he is complaining was to "compel" workers to join a particular union. If the meaning put on the word "compel" by the court is the meaning attributed to it by the hon. Member for Rotherham, the burden placed upon the employer would be that much higher.

I think that those who are concerned about the operation of this clause from the industrial action side have nothing to fear from this difference of opinion about the meaning of the word. If the meaning of the word or the meaning to be given to it in proceedings is as I say, it still imposes a very substantial task upon the employer, because it will not be enough for him to show that the union in question JPEG 1535 was trying to get people to join by persuading, encouraging or inducing them. The burden will be on him to prove more than that. I have said what I think that burden would be. If I am wrong, and the meaning to be attributed to the word is that given by the hon. Member for Rotherham, the burden on the employer is that much higher and the clause is that much narrower.

There is one other matter that I should like to mention while I am on my feet. I think I did less than justice to the point raised by the hon. and learned Member for Accrington (Mr. Davidson). I should have said a little more than I did. I repeat what I said, that my advice to the Committee at this stage is that I do not think the danger which he foresaw and to which his amendment was directed would in practice arise. That remains my advice, but I should have added that the Department's lawyers will of course be studying what is said by everyone in this debate and particularly such matters of law as are put forward. If they advise me that there is more to it, the point made by the hon. and learned Member than I have said. or if on reconsideration I form that view, I shall speak to the hon. and learned Gentleman. At present my advice to the Committee is that the danger to which the amendment is directed would not arise in practice and therefore there would be no practical purpose in making the amendment.

I was then asked about the self-employed. There are two comments I must make on that. I think I have an accurate note of what the hon. Gentleman said. Before one comes to consider the effect of this clause and whether it has any relevance to the situation, one has first to establish that a particular person has a cause of action at common law. The clause makes no difference whatever to whether anybody has a cause of action at common law.
