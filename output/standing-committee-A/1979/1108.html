Clause 1
CLASSIFICATION OF COMPANIES

Amendment No. 94 proposed[ 6 November] in page 1, line 20, at end insert—

"'proprietary company ' means a company limited by shares in which each of the directors is a shareholder, each of the shareholders is a director; and none of the shareholders is a company which is not a proprietary company; and in which two of the following criteria are applicable:

  1. the amount of its net assets, as shown in the latest balance sheet at the conclusion of the annual general meeting (taking account of any distribution to shareholders or employees approved by that annual general meeting) together with the net assets of any other proprietary company or companies of which any of its directors is a director, is not greater than £650,000;
  2. the number of its employees other than directors does not exceed 50;
  3. the annual turnover in any of the last three accounting reference periods of the company, did not exceed £1,300,000.".—[ Sir B. Rhys Williams]

Sir Brandon Rhys Williams
: I begin my remarks, Sir Stephen, by thanking you for your indulgence in allowing the Committee this opportunity to debate the clause. I was taken by surprise at the speed with which you conduct the business, although having served under your Chairmanship during many happy mornings I certainly ought not to be.

The point I make on this clause is that it appears, for the time being at any rate, that the Department is still content that there should just be two classes of company—public companies and JPEG 63 private companies. Although Ministers have given sincere acknowledgment of the need to introduce a new category, namely, proprietary companies, is that quite sufficient? Are the definitions of a proprietary company which we have been given strictly in accordance with reality and the needs of the moment?

A proprietary company is reasonably well described in amendment No. 94, which I have just withdrawn—in other words, a company where the supervisors are the managers and are also the work force. That is a very small undertaking. However, in the Government's Green Paper the bottom tier proposed is a much larger body and so different in character that it appears to be a different animal, namely, a company with an annual turnover of £1,300,000, an average number of 50 employees and a balance sheet total of £650,000.

I said on Second Reading that that appears to be different from a proprietary company. We are, therefore, entitled to ask the Minister to enlighten us whether the legislation which is in preparation to assist proprietary companies is the same as that contemplated for this bottom tier, or whether another category of companies is envisaged for the next Companies Bill. I should like that, although I realise that it is a complicated matter to introduce too many classes of companies for tax purposes, audit purposes, registration, supervision and the rest.

I believe that the very small family business where, as I have said, the supervisors are the managers and also the work force, is different from even a private company with 20, 30 or 50 employees. In that regard, I hope that there may be opportunities later to propose a new departure in company law to make possible what it might be appropriate to call a corporate partnership.

It seems to me that when companies begin to develop and grow, to take on more staff and increase their assets and responsibilities, company law as it is now defines for them a single path from a one-man business to a small private company and, eventually, to a public company, quoted public company and the rest. That is the nineteenth-century idea of the structure appropriate for the creation of wealth in the private sector, but with inflation, modern social con- 64 ditions and the many changes that have taken place in attitudes to work and employment in the twentieth century, we have to think of other forms of cooperation for the creation of wealth.

It is a pity that ideas of the partnership have more or less atrophied where they stood 80 or 100 years ago. Partnerships are still very small bodies, and if an entrepreneur wishes to expand his business it will not normally occur to him, or even be possible for him, to do it by way of making it a corporate partnership. But for a number of reasons we need to pave the way for a certain option to be opened.

One of the most distressing aspects of small businesses is their unwillingness to expand because the proprietors are in danger of losing control if they do expand. We all know, unfortunately, that most British businesses are working unprofitably, or at a loss if one takes inflation accounting into the picture. A business is therefore not able normally to generate from its own cash flow the funds on which significant expansion can be based. Therefore, the proprietor or entrepreneur is obliged to go, first, to the bank, and eventually to other sources of capital, in order to obtain the wherewithal for the business to grow. In so doing, he risks the loss of control of the organisation.

I believe that we should help small businesses to find a way round that dilemma, which we shall not do if we simply stick to nineteenth-century forms as we do in the clause. We must consider new ways of working in which the proprietor can retain control. I envisage a kind of corporate partnership where there is no equity but possibly a reversion to the old idea of a debenture or preference share, dynamised to take account of inflation.

One cannot expect sources of finance to invest in debentures where there is no system of indexation. But I think that the growing acknowledgment of the value added as one of the measures of the success of a business is something that we can build on. It would be worth permitting companies to experiment with a new form of organisation in which they have no equity and no dividend in the traditional sense but where the capital is remunerated by a fixed proportion of the value added, the remains JPEG 65 of the surplus from each year's trading remaining with the business or being taken out in the form of salaries and wages.

It is so important to encourage businesses to finance their own expansion that we ought to pamper them with privileges if they adopt that formula. We ought, for instance, to make them exempt from corporation tax. We ought to give them the same sort of benefit for funds which they set aside for particular purposes within the business, such as retraining and research, as we do for the provision of pensions. Over the past 50 years there has been a tremendous new source of saving developed as a result of the tax concessions given to companies to build up pension funds with useful exemptions from tax until such time as the moneys are disbursed.

I believe that we should find every way we can of encouraging business to put money out of the current account into the capital account, particularly for retraining and the development of the sense of corporate activity within the business.

I do not suggest that in limiting the distribution one should put companies in a position to make very large bonuses. Instead, one should encourage business to build up very large reserves which can be dedicated to the survival of the business and the interests of all concerned.

These were the thoughts passing through my head as I considered whether we ought at once to move on from clause 1 on the classification of companies. It seems rather sad that we are preparing to pass into law something which the Government in their Green Paper have acknowledged to be out of date and upon which the Green Paper seems to give us inadequate leadership.

That is the comment I make to my hon. Friend. If he is willing to give us some of his thoughts at this stage, the Committee will, I believe, be grateful. I certainly should.

Sir Brandon Rhys Williams

I fully recognise that we must not make our company law too complex; if anything, it needs to be simplified. However, we should at the same time provide small businesses with a range of options. Even if we do not go into a proliferation of possibilities in company law, I hope that there will soon be an opportunity for developing the concept of a corporate partnership in which all concerned recognise that they are on the same side and have a stake in the business.
