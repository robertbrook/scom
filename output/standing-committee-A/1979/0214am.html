The Under-Secretary of State for Employment (Mr. Patrick Mayhew): I beg to move,

That during the further proceedings on the Employment Bill the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock and half-past Four o'clock.

I can advance the argument for this very shortly. On the basis of morning sittings only, we are not getting on fast enough. After six sittings, we are still not at the end of clause 2. I understand that the first two clauses are not regarded by the Opposition as the most controversial of the Bill. Another 16 remain, plus the schedules, and, at the current rate of progress, I calculate that that would mean at least 57 more mornings spent upon the Bill, or, say, 28 weeks.

I make no allegation of filibustering, but the country attaches great importance to this Bill and it wants the Bill upon the statute book as soon as reasonably possible. The Bill is needed on the statute book. If it were there already, picketing carried out at places where the pickets do not work could be restrained by the Courts on an application for an injunction, and daily the country is seeing the need for that provision.

Therefore, if we are to meet our responsibilities, the Government must ask the Committee to allow itself more time to consider the Bill in the next few weeks.

Mr. Davidson: It does not have regard to a code of practice in as sensitive an area as industrial relations. It is all very well for the civil courts to have regard to wide interpretation of codes of practice. Someone's liberty is not at stake. In the case of an incident in a picket line, where the hearing in the magistrates' court is taking place against a background of highly charged emotion, the court's interpreting a code of practice in a way which turns out to be detrimental to the person charged adds to that emotionally charged atmosphere. The man will not feel that he has been treated fairly by the court.

A result which the person charged believes has been brought about by the court placing undue weight on a particular aspect of a code, which is not written into the law but which is a code of conduct, can heighten the temperature and do the very thing which the Government, if they have any regard for industrial relations, would not want to do, that is, prolong the unpleasantness. That is the danger.

The hon. and learned Gentleman, who has great experience in the courts—if I say that, people will think that I am flattering him unnecessarily, but he has—must understand that the feeling that undue weight has been put on things in that kind of atmosphere is doubly dangerous. First, it goes to the Court of Criminal Appeal, which may take the view that undue weight has been put upon a particular section of the code. Uncertainty is added to uncertainty. The pickets do not know where they stand, there is more emotional talk, the matter may even go to the House of Lords.

The constant interpretation by courts of wage concepts will, in my view, lead to a person being put in a very difficult position. Whether he has brought it about himself is irrelevant. If one is charged with a criminal offence it is a very serious matter. One does not feel happy about having one's liberty, or a stain on one's record being decided by some vague concept which one does not fully understand. The hon. and learned Gentleman ought to think very carefully about the minefield that he is laying down. I have no great objection to a code of practice, but codes can become very detailed, and the worse is the result.
