Mr. Walker: I fear that I may have to inflict a rather tedious and technical—and, I regret, rather lengthy—speech on the Committee, in the course of which I hope to encompass the arguments or both the amendments and the new clauses. I shall try to be as brief as possible, although I cannot promise to be entertaining.

By way of background I shall be frank with the Committee and explain that the amendments and new clauses have been tabled after Opposition consultation with the National Union of Teachers, which has pointed out to us the anomalous position in which teachers at new small aided Image Col. 1001 schools may find themselves as a consequence of the Bill.

Teachers at a small aided school are appointed by and are in the employ of the managers or governors of the school yet their salaries are paid by the local education authority. The managers or governors have the exclusive right to dismiss a teacher appointed by them to give denominational religious instruction if he is not giving it efficiently and suitably. On the other hand, the local education authority is empowered to prohibit the dismissal of a teacher for any other reason without its consent and also has the power to require the dismissal of any teacher.

One can contrast the position of teachers in small aided schools with that of other staff such as those employed to provide school meals. They will be appointed to the staff of the local education authority's school meals service and are not therefore the employees of the managers or governors.

The special position of teachers in aided schools is already recognised, for example, in certain sections of the Employment Protection (Consolidation) Act 1978. Section 80 enables an employee who has been dismissed by the governors at the request of the local education authority under its statutory powers to treat the local education authority as the employer for the purposes of unfair dismissal. Section 117 also enables the local education authority to be placed in the position of the employer for the purposes of redundancy.

Perhaps that is not unique, and there may be areas of employment where the same problem could arise, but the National Union of Teachers tells me that teachers are in this respect in a unique position, which inevitably gives rise to anomalies because statutory provisions will apply in the case of the direct employees of the managers or governors different from those relating to the employees of the local education authority working in the same establishment. The amendment is designed to remove that anomaly.

It might save a bit of the Committee's time if I explain that exactly the same arguments apply in respect of amendment No. 93. I shall put matters simply and succinctly. A teacher could be dismissed from a new small aided school, perhaps for being pregnant, and she would have no protection under the provisions, whereas the caretaker at the same school who also Col. 1002 might be pregnant would have protection under the Act. Clearly, that is anomalous and wrong, and the amendment has been designed to avoid it.

I turn to new clause 3. At present section 117 provides that an employee who is paid by a person other than his employer may make a claim for redundancy payment against that person who is responsible for payment of salary or wages. When, however, the employee has transferred from the employment of one person to another, continuity of employment is broken despite the fact that salary or wages remain payable by the same person.

Again, there is an example in teaching when the teacher is first employed by the local education authority and becomes employed by the managers or governors of an aided school within the area of the same authority. If subsequently he is made redundant from his aided school employment, he has only that redundancy payment calculated on the basis of his service in the aided school. The redundancy payment is similarly limited in the event of a move out of aided school employment into employment with the local education authority, and, indeed, in the case of a move from one aided school to another. In all those circumstances the teacher's salary derives from the same source.

The amendment may have wider relevance. When one person remains the person responsible for the payment of salary of an employee despite technical changes of employer, surely there should be deemed to be continuity of employment throughout the period in which pay derives from the same source.

I come next to new clause 4. Paragraph 9 of schedule 13 to the I978 Act at present provides that the continuity of employment of an employee for the purposes of qualifying the statutory rights which depend upon continuity is not broken by certain periods in which no contract of employment subsists. Those include temporary cessations of work and periods in which by custom and practice the employee is deemed still to be in his employer's employment. However, the employment appeal tribunal decided in a precedent case that where there are two separate and distinct contracts broken by more than a week continuity is broken.

An illustration of this type of case is when a teacher is employed as a supply Image Col. 1003 teacher for a full term and is then again employed on the same basis in subsequent terms. This was the case at issue before the employment appeal tribunal, when the supply teacher concerned had been employed on a daily paid supply basis for eight years yet was held not to have continuous employment.

Such employees, whether part-time or full-time, regard their employment as effectively a permanent employment. However, when their contracts are not renewed they have at present no entitlement to a redundancy payment and, in the main, also no entitlement to protection against unfair dismissal now that the qualifying period of continuous employment has been increased. The employment tribunal's decision has been criticised as incorrect, but it stands as a precedent and there is a need for the law to be clarified on this question.

The new clauses following new clause No. 7 are consequential, so I shall speak only to that clause. As a result of a recent Court of Appeal decision, a contract which at its outset does not set the date on which it will expire is not a fixed-term contract and, therefore, in the event that it expires without renewal, the non-renewal will not amount to dismissal. A number of employment protection provisions depend on the fact of dismissal.

The case involved a lecturer who was appointed under a contract about which it could have been said that the date of its expiry was uncertain at the time when the contract was made—for example, the date on which the lecturer's classes might finish. That date was not known by the employer until students' examination dates were fixed halfway through the academic year. The case of the lecturer was won on other grounds, but it would have been lost if the court had decided that her contract was made to expire on a date which was uncertain at the time it was made.

That decision, if not altered by statute, could result in a large number of employees in a variety of jobs being employed for a duration that was uncertain at the time when the contract was made but later made certain by a unilateral decision of the employer. They might thereby lose protection.
