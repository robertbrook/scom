COMPANIES BILL [ LORDS]

Clause 47

Power of court to grant relief against company where members unfairly prejudiced.

Amendment No. 221 proposed [ 20 November], in page 57, leave out lines 11 to 33 and insert:—

  • "(1) Subject to the remaining provisions of this section any member of a company may in a representational capacity for himself and all other members of the company petition for or on behalf of the company to enforce any rights, duties or obligations owed by the company which could be enforced by the company itself or to obtain changes for any breach thereof.
  • (2) No such petition shall be filed without leave of the court which may be obtained on application to a judge of the High Court.
  • (3) Such petitioner must establish to the satisfaction of the court that he is acting in good faith and prima facie in the interests of the company in filing such petition.
  • (4) If in the case of any company—
    1. the Secretary of State has received a report under section 168 of the 1948 Act (inspectors' reports) or exercised his powers under Part of the 1967 Act or section 36(2) or (6) of the Insurance Companies Act 1974 (inspection of company's books and papers); and
    2. it appears to him that any ground exists by virtue of which a member could apply to the court under subsection (1) above,
    he may himself (in addition to or instead of presenting a petition for the winding up of the company under section 35(1) of the 1967 Act) apply to the court by petition for an order under this section.
  • (5) the court may if it thinks fit:
    1. make such order for security for costs as it thinks fit;
    2. make an interim order for costs in favour of such petitioner who shall be accountable to the company therefor in the event that the action is dismissed with costs;
    3. order costs to be payable to such petitioner as between a solicitor and his own client;
  • (6) Any petition filed by such petitioner shall not be discontinued, compromised or settled without the approval of the court.
  • (7) If the court is satisfied that a petition under this section is well founded it may make such order as it thinks fit for giving relief in respect of the matters complained of".—[ Mr. Archer.]

    The main object of the amendments is to provide for the setting aside of the rule in Foss v. Harbottle. It is worth remembering that this rule is based on the fundamental principle of our law that the company is distinct from its members, that the business of the company shall be managed by its directors, and that the directors are answerable to the company, not to each individual member.

    There are also practical benefits which derive from that general rule, namely, that it prevents a multiplicity of suits by individual shareholders, and, more important, that where the proposed action concerns a matter which can be ratified by the general meeting, it is futile to embark on litigation about it except with the consent of the general meeting.

    The general rule in Foss v. Harbottle has, however, been set aside by the courts in strictly limited circumstances, mainly relating to cases involving fraud. There has been criticism that these exceptions are too narrow, and one of the provisions of clause 47, namely, subsection (4), deals with such criticisms by giving the court the power, where it thinks fit, to authorise the bringing of proceedings in the name of the company against a third party.

    The Opposition amendments go very much wider than the provisions of Clause 47(4) ( c) in the Bill as printed. They would enable an action to be taken by a shareholder not merely when the conduct complained of was "unfairly prejudicial" to some of the members but in any case where he could convince the court that he was acting in good faith and in the interests of the company. This makes it much more likely that such an action could be brought to challenge the judgment of the directors of a company, who also were acting in good faith.
