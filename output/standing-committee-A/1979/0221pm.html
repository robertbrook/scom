The Under-Secretary of State for Employment (Mr. Patrick Mayhew)
: I am grateful to the hon. Member for Bethnal Green and Bow (Mr. Mikardo) for describing amendment No. 65 as a probing amendment. I entirely agree that the language here is formidable, not to say obscure, but the formulation for the calculation of compensation is exactly the same as that which is found in the Employment Protection Act. The purpose is to ensure that the maximum scales of compensation under clause 4 shall be the same as those for unfair dismissal under the Employment Protection Act.

The hon. Member asked why 30? What is magic about that? There is nothing magic about it, but it so happens that the way in which compensation is calculated for unfair dismissal embodies this formula for the basic award. Section 73 of the Employment Protection (Consolidation) Act 1978, gives one and a half weeks' pay 582 for years of service for the whole of which the employee was not below the age of 41, one week's pay for each complete year below 41 and not below 22, and half a week's pay for complete years below 22 and not below 18.

The Act provides that no more than 20 years' service can be taken into account in calculating the basic award. So the answer to the question "Why 30?" is that 30 is one-and-a-half times 20.

The next question is, why one and a half? The answer to that is that this is the maximum amount of a week's pay allowable as a basic award for compensating unfair dismissal provided under section 73 of the 1978 Act. Then, why 20 years?—because that is the maximum number of years that can be taken into account in calculating a basic award.

I do not think that I need pursue further the intricacies of this calculation. We are following the pattern for unfair dismissal under the Labour Government's legislation and, if further inquiries are thought necessary, I respectfully suggest that they be directed to the right hon. Member for Doncaster (Mr. Walker), who was one of the architects of that Bill. That is the reason. We do not want there to be a different formulation. We consider it appropriate that the formulation and the basis for calculation should be the same.
