Mr. Eyre
: All the amendments are concerned with what is likely to be a most unusual case. To put the matter very briefly, clause 26 controls a limited class of agreements entered into within a specified period by public companies with certain of their members, under which the company acquires assets from the member. The consideration which the company provides is not specified; it may be anything—cash, other assets, or even shares in the company.

It is in this last case that problems arise. The sanctions set out in clause 26 when the clause is not complied with involve the company retrieving whatever consideration it has provided, and the agreement becomes void. If these rules applied where the consideration provided by the company was the allotment of shares in the company, we should be involved in the company retrieving its own shares and the promises to pay up the shares being unenforceable.

That is in complete conflict with the policy of the Bill where the rules in clauses 20 to 24 for the payment up of JPEG 181 shares are not complied with. In those cases, the shares are to be validly allotted, the undertakings to pay them up are enforceable; there is frequently an additional statutory liability in allottee and holders but the court is given wide power to order relief.

The Bill seeks to introduce consistency by providing that where the company breaches clause 26, where the consideration it provides is the allotment of shares the sanctions in clause 27(7) will not apply, but the sanctions provided for clauses 20 to 24 will apply. This is not in question in the present amendments. What they seek to do is to iron out defects in the provisions, achieving consistency and bringing them into line with the new terminology elsewhere.

Amendment agreed to.
