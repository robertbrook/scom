There is a shop, a department, a factory, or whatever it is, in which 60 per cent. of the employees belong to a trade union which I shall call trade union A. There is some recruitment in the department, perhaps because of expansion, perhaps to replace people who have left, and there are recruited into the same department, the same area of work, the same job area, a number of people who are members of union B. These chaps come in and discover that there are a number of people in the department who are not members of a trade union and, being themselves trade unionists, and good trade unionists, they think that it would be a good idea to go about recruiting some members there.

Here we have a situation in which there is one union with a majority of the employees in its membership—indeed, having in its membership all the employees who are members of any trade union at all—which is recognised—this is the important thing—by the employer as being the instrument with which he bargains, with which he negotiates, with which, he reaches agreement. Now we have a second union coming into this area and starting to recruit, and a dispute arises between the unions and off they go to the TUC.

As the Under-Secretary will know, before the formal Bridlington procedures are set in train, there is always an attempt to deal with the matter by informal means, by conciliation, by getting everybody round a table and chatting, and in a remarkably high proportion of the cases that succeeds in disposing of the matter without the need to go through the formalities of the Bridlington procedures. But, in the final issue, there are the Bridlington procedures.

There, the matter is heard by a quasi-judicial body provided by the TUC, or under the auspices of the TUC, and a finding is made. That finding is binding upon the parties. The finding might well be, in the case which I have just been describing, that union A should continue to be recognised as the bargaining agent, that union B should not seek to become a bargaining agent in that area, that it should not seek to recruit, and sometimes even that it should shed that small number of its members who are there in that department and transfer them to the other union.

The hon. Gentleman asks if we are satisfied that this clause will provide safeguards of that sort, that it will not undermine the Bridlington procedures. The answer is: yes, we are. But I should like to explain why we take that view.

The key to the clause is the concept of what is reasonable and unreasonable. The Government take the view, and are widely supported in this, not least among trade union members, that it is not sufficient to enable a union member who believes that he is unjustly or unreasonably excluded from his union in a closed shop simply to get a declaration from some independent body that that is indeed the case, or even a recommendation that he should be reinstated.

The Government take the view that he ought to have an enforceable remedy. That is why we have drafted clause 3 and incorporated it in the Bill in the way that we have. It provides a new remedy against a union in those circumstances, and one which is enforceable by a claim for compensation against the union concerned if the complaint is made out. The jurisdiction lies with the industrial tribunal in the first instance for the declaration. If that declaration is granted, the union then has an opportunity to act upon it. If it does not act upon it within the period set out in the clause, the complainant goes, if he wishes, to the employment appeal tribunal and says, "Compensate me, please. Grant compensation because the claim has been made out, a declaration has been granted, but the union has not acted upon it."
