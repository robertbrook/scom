EMPLOYMENT BILL

Clause 6
DISMISSAL RELATING TO TRADE UNION MEMBERSHIP

Amendment proposed [28 February], No. 116, in page 7, line 25, at end insert—

"but provided that an employee holding any such objection pays or is prepared to pay to a registered charity the equivalent to contributions he would otherwise make to the appropriate union under a union membership agreement".—[ Mr. Needham.]

10.30 a.m.

Question again proposed,That the amendment be made.

Mr. Richard Needham
: If I may remind the Committee of what happened last Thursday, my point in proposing this amendment was to make sure that a balance was established and maintained in the whole area of collective bargaining, particularly relating to the question of union membership agreements. As I said, many of us on the Government Benches feel that the balance has gone too far out in the last few years and it is time that it was redressed somewhat.

Equally, it seems to me that, when the Employment Bill becomes law, there is little point in giving hostages to fortune. We must try to ensure that the Bill works effectively, bearing in mind the point made about free riders. I mentioned what Donovan said on this matter, and we must all remember that what Donovan said has usually proved in the past to be right.

I also said that I welcomed and accepted the views of my hon. and learned Friend on the question of the psychology that is required behind industrial relations legislation and the need to make sure that the atmosphere and temperature are kept low. I said that opponents of my amendment might object that it offered a method of requiring Danegeld, but that argument falls if the employee concerned accepts the benefits that come from bargaining by the 824 unions with the management. I pointed out, referring to Donovan, that that was accepted by those who, because of a religious conviction, said that they did not wish to be members of a union.

The second point was whether such a provision could form part of a code of practice, particularly as there are many agreements which allow for conscience clauses and for opting out by those with conscientious objections. Could that now form part of a code of practice? As the hon. Member for Rotherham (Mr. Crowther) said, an individual who had become an employee of a company might refuse to abide by the union membership agreement which stated that a conscientious objection was OK, but that the money should be paid to charity in lieu of the subscription. In those circumstances, there could be a conscientious objector failing to pay to a charity. This would make it difficult for the employer, who might well be abiding by the code of practice while the employee concerned would then rely on the primary legislation. One imagines that in those circumstances the primary legislation would prevail rather than the employer's reliance on the code of practice.

Third, it may, I suppose, be argued that very few people would be involved in my proposed arrangement. Equally, that could apply to those who have been unreasonably excluded or expelled. It is not a question of numbers here. Once again, it is a question of atmosphere. If the atmosphere is allowed to become soured because of somebody wanting to have the advantages of trade union membership without being prepared to pay for them, my feeling is that that will undermine much of what we are trying to do.

There is also a persuasive argument here purely in industrial relations terms. Surely, it is better if someone who refuses to join a union pays a contribution to charity rather than going off and joining another or setting up one of his own with his colleagues. If somebody dislikes what a union has done, for conscientious reasons or reasons of deeply held personal conviction, it would be better for him to pay the equivalent subscription to a charity than to go off and try to get another union involved. This goes back to the arguments put by the right hon. Member for Doncaster (Mr. Walker) about the number of unions that we have already.

The corollary to that, I suppose, may be that the unions themselves may dislike JPEG 825 the idea of allowing people to pay subscriptions to charity, on the basis that it might encourage many more people, who might not have consciences, to decide suddenly that they had. I am not convinced of that argument. I believe that in any event it is up to the union to show that it properly represents the views of its members. If it is not representing the views of its members and not doing a proper job, it can hardly be surprised if people decide to get out, for genuine reasons, and take their money elsewhere.

Again, therefore, I think that it will benefit industrial relations to ensure that the trade union takes care in looking after its members' interests and has behind it the rather blunted threat—and rightly the lather blunted threat—of what will happen if it does not do so.

Therefore I feel that the amendment has much to commend it. It strengthens the Bill rather than detracts from it, and I think that it is in line with good industrial relations practice.

The Industrial Society, which has great expertise and experience in these affairs and has a large number of records of closed shop agreements—where they have taken place and how they operate—welcomes what I am suggesting and has said that it has the merit of making lawful what is known to be good practice and to work, and that this has been the thrust of the society's suggested legislation on such matters as health and safety, contracts of employment and so on.

I believe that my amendment falls within the broad grounds of industrial relations improvement, and I commend it to the Committee.

Friend the Member for Chippenham (Mr. Needham) has a degree of interest in matters relating to industrial relations and of sympathetic experience in their practice that is the envy of many people in the House. All these provide interlocking and overlapping conflicts of interest. The art or judgment—whatever one likes to call it—lies in knowing where and how to strike the balance.

I sympathise, too, with the argument about free riders. There is a great deal of resentment on the shop floor among those who subscribe to their trade unions and benefit from the negotiations that they carry out on their behalf. It is only human nature that there should be resentment that those advantages should be gained by people working alongside who do not contribute. That is rather trite and one need not take time over it, but it is at the root of what lies behind the amendment.

It was also at the root of the proposal in the Industrial Relations Act 1971 which provided that in the case of an agency shop a contribution equivalent to the cost of the subscription to the trade union could be made to some other body. Interestingly, that did not meet with the approval of the TUC. I say "to some other body", the payment was there made on an agency basis to the unions.

The TUC said that to unions in Britain money is less important than commitment. That reaction must surely be reinforced if it is suggested that the money should be paid not even to them but to some charity. It said that the agency shop would create two classes of worker in a particular place—one party to and bound by the union's rules, and the other not party to and not bound by them. The proposal, it said, was therefore meant to weaken unions' authority. That comes from a TUC pamphlet called "Reason", on the I971 Act.

My hon. Friend concluded his attractive argument by saying that if we did not go down his road we should be contributing to a worsening of industrial relations. Any adverse effect is something for which they cannot seek compensation from the union because it is for the employer to decide what to offer to each of his individual employees, and it is for each individual employee to decide what to accept by way of pay. That is the principle. Exactly the same is true where a union brings about a benefit.

That may be thought to be altogether too legalistic, which is a charge to which I am very sensitive, as the Committee knows. I am very sensitive to the flattery of being thought to be a good lawyer. I always try to disclaim it, and I generally do not have much difficulty.

If we do not all have to work for the same employer, let us take the hypothesis that we are all residents of a district served by the same local authority. Gentleman has said about these matters being best left to be determined between the employer and the trade union—that because the matters they are seeking agreement about are statutory provisions, there is really no point in making statutory provisions, which are part of the law, and then saying that it is for those who are involved to agree about what the statutory provisions mean. If that was the case there would be no point in making statutory provisions.

When questions arise, if those questions are to be determined by voluntary agreement, they should be based on a voluntary agreement in the first place. I have often been involved in the making of industrial agreements. We who had made the agreement—the employer, myself and my colleagues, the shop stewards' committee—would agree that if a dispute arose it was for us to interpret it. If we could not agree, there was a voluntary procedure laid down by means of which we could go through the engineering disputes procedure. There were different stages, but at every stage it was a voluntary procedure. It was only voluntary because it started off on questions of interpretation as a voluntary agreement.

872 Here, we are talking about the interpretation in the future not of a voluntary agreement but of something put into an Act of Parliament by this Committee and by the House of Commons and the House of Lords. There is no point in putting it in unless it is assumed that it will be determined by some kind of judicial body in the event of conflict or dispute. Because there is no provision in the Bill for going to an industrial tribunal when there is a dispute, one assumes that it will go to the courts. There is so much fertile ground here for the litigious-minded, as my hon. Friend the Member for Bethnal Green and Bow described them, to nip off to the High Court to get a judgment about these matters.

Is this a sensible way to determine these difficult and sensitive matters? I very much doubt it. I think the most sensible thing that the Secretary of State has said since he launched the Bill is his intervention in my remarks this morning when he was saying that these were matters best determined by discussion and voluntary agreement between the parties. That is absolutely right, and the right hon. Gentleman should start off by basing the whole thing on that.

Much as we disagree with the right hon. We do not need all this nonsense and morass and quagmire that he will create with all the roads leading down a mass of avenues joining together at the High Court. I suggest that he should think again.
