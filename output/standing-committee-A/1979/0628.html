Mr. Christopher Price
: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 11, after "are", insert

"as far as the duties of local education authorities relating to secondary education are concerned".

 This amendment is very important, in my view, but not one on which I shall make a party political speech. The remarks I shall make in support of the amendment are important for all local education authorities, whatever form of secondary education they intend to adopt in the future.

The amendment proposes that the procedures which were outlined in the the 1976 Education Act should become permissive, although they cease to be JPEG 5 mandatory, and not be abolished altogether. Though it will no longer be a duty for local education authorities to follow those procedures laid down in the 1976 Act, if any authority wished to follow those procedures—they have great advantage in terms of the linkage of plans to section 13 proposals—it should he able to do so. It is for that reason that the amendment seeks to insert the words

"as far as the duties of local education authorities relating to secondary education are concerned".

 I refer the Committee to the wording of clause I of the 1976 Act—a clause which this Bill proposes to repeal. It says:

"Subject to subsection (2) below, local education authorities shall, in the exercise and performance of their powers and duties relating to secondary education, have regard to the general principle that such education is to be provided only in schools where the arrangements for the admission of pupils are not based (wholly or partly) on selection by reference to ability or aptitude."

 The amendment Seeks to enSure—the Minister will tell us whether he feels that it will do so—that, whatever might happen to the duties of local education authorities under the 1976 Act, their general powers—referred to in the 1976 Act, as set out in the 1944 Education Act and subsequent ActS—should be preserved, and that they should have the power to ask to go along the 1976 road, if they wish, and use their powers in that way even if they no longer have the duty to do so.

I suspect that the fatal flaw in this short Bill is that it says what Government policy is not, but it does not say what Government policy is. It leaves an enormous vacuum where the Government have a duty to fulfil, under the law. I say that they have a duty under the law, because that is what the law says. The law—that is, section 1 of the 1944 Education Act—says quite clearly that it shall he the duty of the Secretary of State for Education and Science

"to promote the education of the people of England and Wales and the progressive development of institutions devoted to that purpose, and to secure the effective execution by local authorities, under his control and direction, of the national policy for providing a varied and comprehensive "—

 —there "comprehensive" means what they thought was comprehensive in 1944, 6 not what we mean by the word today—

"educational service in every area."

 The law there lays a duty on the Secretary of State to control and direct the planning of the development of primary and secondary schools.

Now the Government are saying that they no longer intend to fulfil that duty, as laid upon them by law in the 1944 Education Act. In my view, they propose to act completely illegally.

I want to go into that subject at length, but first I want to dispose of one of the arguments. The Government say that they want to release education authorities from that duty in the 1976 Act, saying that their intention is to give local freedom. It is implausible to believe in the idea that this Government want local freedom, when the headlines day by day produce instances showing that they clearly do not want local freedom.

I could refer to the Government's policy over council house Sales, their policy on local government revaluation, the new powers it is said they are to take to prevent local authorities from raising the rates, or to penalise them for doing so. But if I did go on like that, Miss Fookes, I am sure you would pull me up as I would be outwith the scope of the Bill.

It is probably just as well for the Government that I am not allowed to refer to house Sales, revaluation, and the rates, because to do So would make it clear that the Government do not support the principle of local authority freedom. They are just talking about it in the Bill because they dislike comprehensive education.

Historically, section 1 of the Education Act 1944 imposed upon the Minister the duty of controlling and directing the development of secondary schools, and clause 11 gave local education authorities the duty of producing development plans. The 1944 Act would have been better if that had been made a continuous duty, and not a once-for-all duty. But it was made Such a duty, and that is how the Education Act 1944 worked for the first 15 to 20 years. The Minister controlled and directed, the local authorities created development plans, and the Act worked according to the law. The Minister always had section 13 as a sort of long-Stop ministerial approval.

JPEG 7 Section 13 is relevant to the present position. Section 13 was not introduced into the 1944 Act with any idea of being concerned with comprehensive education. It was introduced at the behest of nonconformist interests which were terrified, particularly in Wales, that a new Government might alter the balance between church schools and county schools. Those interests wanted the Minister to hold overall control. It is only more recently that section 13 has been used for comprehensive education. But in those days the planning of local education authorities developed in this way.

A development plan was drawn up. When one neared the point of applying for Section 13 approval for a change in use of a school, or for a new school—usually it was for a new school, because the population was increaSing—one went to the Department, discussed it, got fairly big nods and winks about the way one would probably get section 13 approval if one stuck to one's own development plan. one continued planning and, almost invariably, in the end, received that section 13 approval.

The process was first shattered by the first Conservative Minister in 1951, who rapidly had to be replaced by Sir David, now Lord, Eccles, simply because she was, through her policy, causing such chaos in local education authorities. She did not last very long.

The next time that consensus was shattered was when, very foolishly, the right hon. Member for Sidcup (Mr. Heath) appointed the present Prime Minister as his Oppositon spokesman on education. When she became Secretary of State for Education and Science in 1970, she did much of what this Bill purports to seek to do. She just tore up circular 10/65, without putting anything in its place. That is the negativity of Toryism: "We do not believe in comprehensive education, but we do not know what we do believe in, so we are not putting any policy at all in its place".

For four years, local education authorities—particularly Conservative authorities—were thrown into absolute chaos. They could not indulge in any planning at all for the future development of their systems because they were given no guarantees, such as were given under circular 10/65, of what they might expect to happen to their section 13 applications.

The present Prime Minister, who was then Secretary of State for Education and Science, was so keen to interfere with planning and development at local level that she actually issued a section 68 order saying that the local authority of Surrey—one of her own Conservative local authorities—was behaving unreasonably in wanting to go comprehensive.

I was temporarily away from this place during those years. I was trying to turn an honest penny as an education journalist, which is extremely difficult. I used to go to press conferences at which, time and time again, the then Secretary of State was begged to give 10 some certainty in planning, and, time and time again, she put on her legalistic hat and said that the Education Act 1944 told her to consider every section 13 on its merits and that that was what she would do. She would not provide any clues at all to the possibility of a section 13 application being approved. Education authorities up and down the country were dogged, day in and day out, by complete uncertainty.

Right from 1970 the policy was to go back to grammar schools, but, once again, the Tory Party dared not say so. The unsung heroes of comprehensive education in those days were some of the Conservative local education authority chairmen, who told the then Secretary of State exactly what they thought of her policies on comprehensive education. I am thinking of people such as Patrick Crotty of Leeds and others who insisted that their Tory education authorities went comprehensive and prevented the worst effects of the Tory policies in those years from 1970 to 1974.

Exactly the same policy is now being introduced again. The Government have come in and promptly introduced legislation in exactly the same way as the Government tore up circular 10/65 in 1970. They are saying: "No guidance. We will not tell you anything. We will say what we do not believe in—comprehensive education—but we will not say what we believe in. Just see how you go and when—right at the last minute—you are ready to develop the next stage of your system, put in your section 13 application. We may chop it, wasting millions of pounds of ratepayers' money, or we may let it go through, according to our whim and caprice. There is no guarantee that we shall allow anything, because we are going to smash carefully-built-up procedures for consultation between local education authorities and the central Government."

The 1976 Act was an attempt to bring back some order where chaos had resulted from the policies followed by the present Prime Minister between 1970 and 1974. Certainly we wanted to encourage comprehensive schools, but we did not feel that changes should be made at a tremendous rate. It was quite understood that it might take 10, 15 or even 20 years in some areas.

It filled a gap in the 1944 Act in a way which I believe every local education authority welcomed.

The Act produced some linkage, which is now being destroyed and which we are trying to put back in a little way, between the presentation of development plans for Secondary education and some sort of guarantee that section 13 notices would be approved when they were put in. Of course no absolute guarantee can be given on that, because the law lays it down that the Minister must consider every case on its merits, but one has to have some certainty in development and planning.

That certainty was smashed in 1970 by the present Prime Minister and it is now being smashed by a very reactionary Conservative Government, with the hon. Member for Brent, North (Dr. Boyson) in charge of schools. We are right back to Florence Horsbrugh, right back to the present Prime Minister's regime, right hack to the anarchy of that regime, and hack, I would say—I am not in a position to challenge the Department of Education and Science—to a complete reneging on the duty laid on the Minister by section I of the 1944 Education Act, which talks about
