The Under-Secretary of State for Employment (Mr. Patrick Mayhew)
: For my part, I can fulfil my side of that exchange. I immediately appreciate the motive that lies behind the amendment moved by the hon. Member for Bethnal Green and Bow (Mr. Mikardo), and I appreciate the clarity and precision with which he has moved it. I am able to tell him that it is not necessary because the clause is not expressed in a way which is on all fours with the approximately parallel provision in the redundancy provisions of the 1978 Act.

I can explain why that is so, but at the outset I assure the hon. Gentleman that the woman coming back at the end of her maternity leave who is offered a job which the employer regards as a suitable alternative is no worse off than the woman who is an applicant under the redundancy provision. There is an equal, indeed better, opportunity for a trial period.

The section of the 1978 Act which deals with the redundancy situation provides that where a job given to an employee by his employer as an alternative when he has been made redundant involves a change in the terms and conditions of his employment, and the employee takes that job, he must be given a trial period of four weeks in which to decide finally whether to accept that job. The trial period is given to him because section 84 does not require that the job offered be suitable or appropriate. It has been thought desirable in that event that the employee should have a chance to experience the job and give it a try before committing himself to accepting it. As a result, once he has accepted it, he forgoes his right to a redundancy payment.

On the other hand, section 60, which provides for the offer of an alternative job when an employee cannot have her original job back after a confinement, does not require a trial period. Like the clause with which we are dealing, it provides that the job offered must be suitable and appropriate to the employee and contain terms not substantially less favourable than the corresponding provisions of the previous contract. Where the previous employment is terminated, should the alternative job not meet those criteria, the employee cannot, even by accepting it, debar herself from making a claim for unfair dismissal in respect to that termination. If she accepts the job and then finds, JPEG 1257 within the three-month period, that it is not suitable, and can establish that it is not suitable—indeed, I do not think that the burden would be on her to establish that it was not suitable—she is not debarred from her claim of unfair dismissal in respect of that termination.

The same is true of new section 56A. Unless the job complies with the requirements of new section 56A(3), section 56 will not be disapplied, regardless of whether the woman has accepted the job or refused it, reasonably or unreasonably. She will be treated in accordance with section 56 as having been dismissed for the reason that she was not allowed to return to her former job. Accordingly, the employee will be better able to judge whether the job is acceptable after she has tried it out. The new provision does not prevent her from doing so.

If I am not to depart from the example of brevity set by the hon. Gentleman, I think that that is enough to assure him that his amendments are unnecessary. I shall gladly expand on the matter if I have to.

Mr. John Grant

Mr. Mayhew
: Quite right. I am grateful for the correction. The woman's job should be exactly as good, or better. That would be to impose an unfair burden upon an employer. How much better? We would then have to say: is it to be substantially better or would any degree better suffice? Therefore, because it would impose an unfair burden on tribunals, as well as upon employers, we think it important to have some sort of adjective and guidance.

If we simply said "exactly as good or better", an argument could be mounted that the job was marginally, superficially or trivially worse and therefore she had been unfairly dismissed. That would not be the intention of Parliament, because it would take up the time of tribunals in determining how many angels can stand upon the head of that pin.

So "substantially" is used. It is not used here for the first time. Section 45 of the 1978 Act reproduces the working of the 1975 Act in this regard. Section 45 confers a right to return to work by subsection (3), and by subsection (3) it requires that the offer of an alternative job should be made and says that if it is not practicable for the employer to permit her to return to her job by reason of redundancy, then, provided that there is a suitable vacancy, an offer of an alternative job must be made.

Subsection (4) of that Act goes on to describe what shall be a suitable altern- JPEG 1261 ative job. That wording of subsection (4) is reproduced in the new provision for section 56 that we are discussing.

That is what is done in the existing statute by Parliament. "Substantially" is the word used. I shall read it so that we have it absolutely in perspective:

"(4) The new contract of employment must be such that—

  1. the work to be done under the contract is of a kind which is both suitable in relation to the employee and appropriate for her to do in the circumstances; and
  2. the provisions of the new contract as to the capacity and place in which she is to be employed and as to the other terms and conditions of her employment are not substantially less favourable to her than if she had returned to work in accordance with subsection (1).".

That is the wording used in 1978 and 1975 and to which the tribunals are well accustomed.

British workers will have to allow themselves to be party to such exploitation, to do otherwise might land them in the courts. But the Bill proposes additionally to expose to damages anyone who seeks to communicate with that Asian crew, or any other, to persuade them that what they are doing is wrong, or peacefully to persuade them to press for decent pay and conditions of employment.

On pain of legal penalties the dockers are compelled, however abhorrent it may be to them, to offload such a vessel and—what is equally abhorrent—under this measure the workers' organisations are to be refused access to the sailors who are being exploited. By this measure and by his other proposals the Secretary of State is not just condoning the kind of sweated labour that we prohibit by our domestic law but is actually conniving at its continuation and extension by providing savage penalties for those who, in the most modest way, try to do something about it.
