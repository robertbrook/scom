I expressed the view on Second Reading, and shall return to it later, that although the recognition provisions as conceived in the 1975 Act have subsequently been shown to be defective in some respects—and if they were not, they were made defective by court judgments—none the less it would be wholly deplorable for applications by trade unions for recognition by employers not to be conducted through sensible, reasonable machinery. "in particular of encouraging the extension of collective bargaining".

To illustrate that, I think of the application by the United Kingdom Association of Professional Engineers in the employ of the firm W. H. Allen of Bedford for recognition by that employer as an independent trade union representing a description of workers in the establishment.

The employer, not wishing to encourage a proliferation of trade unions in his establishment, not wishing to do anything which might disrupt the existing good industrial relations at that establishment, turned down the application by the United Kingdom Association of Professional Engineers, which then very properly followed the statutory procedure and went to the Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service.

ACAS did what was appropriate. It balloted the group of employees of the appropriate description, and the ballot undoubtedly revealed that a majority of the employees of that description wanted to be represented by the United Kingdom Association of Professional Engineers. I am speaking from memory as I do not have the report with me.

In that case, one could have invoked, and this is probably what was done, those provisions in the terms of reference of ACAS which laid upon the Service the obligation of encouraging the extension of collective bargaining, and it could be reasonably argued that here was an extension of collective bargaining, namely, the recognition of a trade union to represent a particular group or description of people in that establishment.

Friend the Member for Bethnal Green and Bow (Mr. Mikardo), ASTMS, and the draughtsmen's section of the Amalgamated Union of Engineering Workers, known as TASS.

That was challenged in the courts by the United Kingdom Association of Professional Engineers. Mr. Justice May then ruled, in looking at the two limbs of the terms of reference of ACAS, that the general duty of promoting the improvement of industrial relations must be subordinated to the particular duty of encouraging the extension of collective bargaining. He accordingly ruled in favour of the United Kingdom Association of Professional Engineers and said that the employer ought to grant recognition for bargaining purposes. At this stage, or even earlier, not only the employer, not only the trade unions which already enjoyed recognition by that employer, but also the Engineering Employers' Federation pointed out that to do so would be harmful to industrial relations.

I mention that only to illustrate that we have recognised one of the areas of difficulty that have arisen.
