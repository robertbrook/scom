Also, I call attention to the fact that adequate notice should be given of amendments. As a general rule, I do not intend to call manuscript amendments.

Clause 1
PAYMENTS IN RESPECT OF SECRET BALLOTS

Mr. Harold Walker
: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 6, after 'Secretary of State', insert

'after consultation with the Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service'.

The Chairman
: With this we are to discuss amendment No. 6, in page 2, line I5, after 'Secretary of State', insert

'after consultation with the Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service'.

Mr. Harold Walker
: We are quite happy to debate with this, as you suggest Mr. Goodhew, amendment No. 6 because they cover the same theme.

4 Looking around the Committee, I am glad to see at least one or two faces familiar from previous occasions when we have been round this course. For my part, I feel that I have been going backwards and forwards over some of these issues half my life, and I am bound to say that I may have to take some time to readjust to having the sunlight in my eyes in this Committee rather than at my back.

This is not the first time both in Committee and in the House that we have debated ballots, which are the subject of clause 1. It happened in 1975, and again much more recently. Nor is it the first time that we have debated the role of ACAS relating to any proposed ballots in connection with industrial disputes, the election of trade union officials and the other matters covered by the clause, although I must say that when, in the debates on the Employment Protection Bill, as it was, in 1975 we debated an amendment from the then Opposition to give ACAS a role in these matters, it was a very different role from that which we are envisaging now.

If I recall, the right hon. Member for Lowestoft (Mr. Prior) was suggesting then, seeking to write into the Bill, an obligation on ACAS to produce codes and also to regulate the implementation of codes on balloting. We are proposing something very different. However, I am encouraged by the fact that as long ago as that the right hon. Member for Lowestoft and his hon. Friend's were prepared to consider that ACAS had a role in these matters.

It is important that I say something about how we envisaged the role of' ACAS and the raison d'etre of its establishment when it was conceived back in I974, for. indeed, it was set up by administrative action, if I recall correctly, before it was given the statutory base that it now has under the Employment Protection Act.

The Committee will recall that in those days there was a widespread feeling—whether it was well founded is another matter—that there was an excessive involvement by Government and Ministers in the day-to-day management of industrial relations, and there was a widespread feeling that it would be better if the Government withdrew from that excessive involvement and left the day-to-day management of industrial relations, the regulation of disputes, the development of policy and the formulation of initiatives in these matters JPEG 5 to those involved in them from day to day, namely, management, trade union officials and others.

This was the underlying idea of the establishment of the Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service, partly so that those who would be beneficiaries, I presume to say, of the work of the Service would have confidence that it was an independent Service, that it was not the tool of Ministers and the Government, that it was not perhaps being used to implement whatever pay policy of the day there might be.

That was very much the theme on which ACAS was established. It was never intended to mean that the Government had no involvement or responsibility in these matters. Obviously, the Government cannot stand aloof from these matters when the public interest is affected by for, example, industrial disputes. One thinks of the steel industry. It is not a private matter between the workers in the steel industry and the British Steel Corporation. The current dispute is one which has a serious effect on the public interest—indeed, on the national interest. The Government have a responsibility for the public interest, the national interest, We envisaged then that in those circumstances, where the public interest was jeopardised, Ministers not only had a right but had a responsibility and duty to involve themselves to safeguard the public.

Obviously, I should be out of order if I were to talk about the steel dispute, but I think that I might fairly make the point that we believe that the Government are right to have assumed a role now in this dispute. We Very much regret that they did not do it earlier.

It would be wrong to assume that we intended the Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service that was the only agency, the only instrument of outside involvement in such very serious disputes.

Having said that, I now turn to the question of the ballots and why we think ACAS should be involved. The first point to note is that here the Secretary of State is taking unto himself a power which will involve him in these matters without putting an obligation on him to consult anyone. There is no suggestion anywhere in the clause that he should have any consultation at all about the ballot which he is proposing to fund if those concerned wish funds to be made available—not the 6 Trades Union Congress, not any of the trade unions, not the CBI, no one.

We believe that it would be wrong for the Secretary of State to act unilaterally without consultation, without seeking guidance from anyone. Perhaps the most sensible thing, and the least that the Government should be required to do, is to talk to these people whom I described as being involved in, and representative of, those who are concerned with the day-to-day management and regulation of industrial relations.

The Minister should recognise that ballots—heaven knows, we as Members of Parliament should realise this better than anyone else perhaps, are not just a simple matter of distributing ballot papers, allowing someone to put a cross and then gathering them together and counting the votes. We all know that it is a much more elaborate, complex and lengthy process than that. There is all the business of informing those who would be required to put their cross about where they should put it. We engage in lengthy persuasion of the electorate at considerable cost—the printing of leaflets, the election addresses, the canvassing and so on—all an essential and integral part of the democratic process if the crosses are to go in the right places eventually.

I think that it would help the Committee in reaching a decision on these matters if the Under-Secretary of State were to give some guidance about the extent to which the Government envisage these funds being available. Is it merely to be for the mechanical process of putting the cross on the paper and having the paper printed? Does it even cover the cost of printing? I do not know. These are enormously expensive matters, as we all know. I think of what is now the current limit for general elections. I know that I must be careful now if I hazard a guess about the amount I spent at the last general election. I had better be cautious, but it was certainly a large sum, and that was for merely one election.

Mr. John Grant
: Very well spent.

Mr. Walker
: It was indeed well spent. It certainly had the return that the electors of Doncaster hoped for. But, that apart, I suggest that we ought to look at this question in terms of all the activities associated with ballots in respect of advis- JPEG 7 ing, informing, persuading, and cajoling so that we reach the right democratic result. It might be helpful if the Under-Secretary were to tell us something about that.

I return to the theme of the amendment, namely, that the Secretary of State should be required to take outside guidance and advice, guidance which can be impartial. The Under-Secretary has kindly and thoughtfully provided the Committee with background notes on clauses, a development which I welcome and one which I hope will be copied elsewhere. In the background note on clause I, the Under-Secretary says that the Government are acting in accordance with the Conservative Party's manifesto and that what they told the electors at the time of the election was right.

I make no criticism of that, except to say that, with the best will in the world, the electors are not the best advisers on these matters. To respond for the sake of getting votes to what may be a popular demand at a particular time is not necessarily the best or the right action in these matters.

There are occasions when expert advice ought to be sought not—for the want of a better expression—from the popular mass. To follow what is the demand of the mass in this situation is perhaps to follow and not to lead, perhaps even to lead us down the road towards government by referenda rather than government based on policy arrived at by a consideration of what is right, appropriate and best in the circumstances.
