Member for Islington, Central (Mr. Grant) for the way in which he has presented the amendments, and I gladly respond to the general question with which he concluded his speech. Why do we need these notifications, and what is the purpose of the changes for which the clause provides? I can answer that shortly by repeating what I said at our last sitting.

At present, there is too wide a gap between the two dates in relation to a woman's confinement at which she is required to give an intimation of her intention to claim the right to be reinstated. The first date falls at II weeks before her confinement and the second falls one week before the date at which she intends to return, and that, as we know, may be 29 weeks after the confinement. We believe that to be much too wide a gap to be consistent with the ability of employers to plan ahead, either to know on what basis to recruit a replacement in the absence of the woman or to be able to fill on a permanent basis a gap which may have been kept open.

Therefore, the argument centres on the intermediate notification that can be requested by the employer at a date not earlier than seven weeks after the confine- JPEG 1159 ment, when he may ask in writing for confirmation. He will say "You said just before you started your maternity leave that you wanted to come back later. Now you have had the baby and a reasonable time has gone by"—not less than seven weeks, and he may be asking for this confirmation of intention a good deal later. "Now we want you, please, to confirm what you earlier said—or not, as the case may be because it will help us to know how to proceed."

That seems to us to be a perfectly reasonable change to enable planning to be carried out more reliably.

What is being said is that the employer can ask for written confirmation and, if it is not given, subsequently the woman will not be entitled to reinstatement under the provisions of the statute. I follow what lies behind the amendment. I think that it could be said that, by doing this, the employer might appear subsequently to have created, although not intentionally, a kind of trap. The difficulty can be put as follows. It may or may not happen, but I believe that it would be an extremely rare case where any employer would wish to hold a woman employee in these circumstances to a representation that she had made in response to his request for confirmation of her intention to come back. It would be so rare as to be virtually non-existent. But in law it is at least far from plain that a representation as to a future course of conduct cannot be relied upon. Be that as it may, it is one thing, and perfectly proper, for an employer to say "If you do not reply to this and give us the confirmation we are asking for, then you will lose your rights", but quite another to require him to go on at the same time and say "But you need not really mind much what you do say, because you are not going to be held to it". That is going too far, and I shall try to explain why.

The object here is to try to get a reasonably reliable statement of intention. The purpose is to enable planning to proceed, as much in the interests of somebody who has been taken on as a replacement temporarily for somebody who is away in order to have a child as for other reasons. We tend not to take into account the interests of those who have been temporarily recruited on the understanding that they will have to give up the job when the woman who has gone away to have her child comes back. They have an interest in knowing what the future will be, and so does the employer.

I understand the reason for this amendment, which would require the employer to say "You are on risk if you do not reply to this", but to make him go on and say "You need not pay much attention to what you say" goes much too far. Although I do not say that there will be many who fall into this category, I think that there will be some who say to themselves "There is nothing to lose. Let's say we will come JPEG 1161 back. Here, the notice actually says so." It will have the effect of diminishing the reliability, and hence the value, of the information that is given. That is the whole purpose. So, although I sympathise and would like to do so here, too, I am not able to show the same co-operative response to this amendment that I did to the previous one.

The effect of amendment No. 142 falls into two parts. The first part merely reflects what we have already discussed on amendment No. 138, that the written request from the employer should contain the information warning a woman of her loss of right to return if she fails to comply with the request. To that extent, the amendment is acceptable, but that only flows from what I have already said on amendment No. 138.

The second part requires that a pre- 1162 scribed form shall be used and that the Government shall consult with ACAS and the EOC before the form is produced. Here, I am afraid, I cannot be co-operative because, whereas we agree that this information ought properly to be given, we are anxious so far as possible to avoid requiring businesses—and especially small businesses—to use particular forms and to be held to a particular form of words.

We feel that that would add to the bureaucracy—we have used the word rather loosely and inaccurately—inherent in this legislation, and we think it important to stick to the main point, that the employee should be told clearly and accurately. It does not matter whether a particular form of words is used so long as that objective is achieved.

We do not, therefore, wish to make employers use a particular form. It can be put as simply as that. We think that it will be for them to make sure that they convey the information accurately. If they do not, and if it is found subsequently on any issue before the industrial tribunal that they have not adequately complied with this part of the Bill, that will be their funeral, not the employee's. So the burden will be upon them to convey the information accurately, but to achieve that purpose we do not think that a particular form of words is necessary, let alone one produced by the Government after consultation with ACAS, the EOC or anyone else.

Amendment No. 143 would allow employees to retain their rights to return to work after pregnancy or confinement, notwithstanding the request from their employer for confirmation of intention to return and the fact that they had not complied with that request, if it could not be proved that the employees received the written request. The wording is:

"(3C) The provisions of subsection (3) above shall not apply unless the employer can produce evidence that the employee had received the written request."

Here again, I entirely understand what lies behind the amendment, and, of course, I sympathise with it. But my advice to the Committee is that it is not necessary. The provision as it stands reads:

"Where not earlier than forty-nine days after the beginning of the expected week of confinement … an employee is requested in writing by her employer or a successor of his to give him written confirmation…".

JPEG 1163 The phrase "requested in writing" is the key. What does that mean? Clearly, it means more than writing out a request and leaving it on one's desk. It has to be communicated. It has to be sent. One is not requested in writing unless the request is made out and sent. The burden lies on the employer to show that the request has been made.

This is something which courts and tribunals have to deal with as a question of fact every day of the week. If a person is giving notice that he wants possession of his house, or of half a dozen other everyday things of which notice has to be given under the law, the burden lies upon him to show on the balance of the probabilities, in the court or in the tribunal, that he has given notice and that that notice has been received.

Under the clause, a woman might say "I claim my rights to be reinstated and they have been contested by the employer. He is not taking me back. He says that the reason is that he sent the seven-week notice and I did not comply with it. I did not give him confirmation. But I have to tell the tribunal that I never received the request." It then becomes a matter of fact.

The burden of proving on the balance of the probabilities that the request was made and communicated rests upon the employer. There are a number of ways in which he can meet it. At one extreme, he could say that he had used the recorded delivery procedure and produce the receipt and the signature. It would then be very hard for a tribunal to avoid finding that the request had been communicated. The employer could use the registered post, or he could simply say that he had given her in his office a written request, of which he had kept a copy, and then call a witness to corroborate that. There are many ways in which this evidence can be given. But it is not necessary to say:

"The provisions of subsection (3) above shall not apply unless the employer can produce evidence that the employee had received the written request."

I am not criticising in a tendentious or factious way the actual drafting, but the matter does not depend upon just producing evidence. It depends on the evidence being accepted, and that, as I have tried to show, is part of the law already.

There is this difficulty, It is not only unnecessary but if we legislate in this way 1164 by doubly emphasising the point, so to speak, we run into the old problem of the implications that may arise and the inferences that may be drawn in respect of other areas of our legislation that require notice to be given where the same emphasis is not applied.

What inferences would be drawn as to Parliament's intention? I believe that it would open up questions which have long been settled and create uncertainty where hitherto there has been a reasonable degree of certainty. My advice to the Committee is that the amendment is not necessary, that the burden rightly rests upon the employer in this context, and that it is adequately so expressed in the clause. I hope that it will therefore be seen that it is not for any lack of sympathy with the motive lying behind the amendment that I cannot advise my hon. Friends to accept it.
