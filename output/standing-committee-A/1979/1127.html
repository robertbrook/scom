It has been clear for some time that this state of affairs is unsatisfactory, and, as I hinted earlier, various attempts have been made to bring the law into line with the consideration that many directors do in fact give to the interests of the company's employees.

I say at the outset that I have never accepted what I may call the conflict theory which seemed to dominate a lot of our discussions in the last Committee stage, JPEG 359 that in well run companies boards of directors seek constantly to further the interests of the shareholders at the expense of the employees. From my own experience of business, I knew that that simply was not the case. In fact, in well run companies boards of directors actually further the interests of their shareholders by making sure that the interests of their employees are properly taken into account.

So we are not talking about introducing into the commercial world a practice which is totally unknown. We are talking about reminding what I believe to be a minority of companies and boards of directors of what is now the practice in most well run companies. I stress that in my own business we saw the interests of employees and shareholders as running together, we worked to promote both, and as a result both groups were better off. I wish to make clear at the outset that we are not introducing some great innovation into business practice and that most well run businesses conform to the principles that we try to enunciate in the clause.

As long ago as 1973, the Conservative Government introduced their Companies Bill, at which time I was Parliamentary Private Secretary to my right hon. Friend the present Secretary of State for the Environment, so I had the privilege of sitting silent on that Committee six years ago. I sat in Committee during the 24 sittings on the Bill in 1976. Therefore, Sir Stephen, you can well imagine that I have a personal interest in wanting to see the clause enacted to do what was identified six years ago as being necessary.

The Labour Government in their 1976 Bill started by providing that directors should, in the performance of their functions, have regard to the interests of employees, and then went on to suggest that the duty should be owed to and in some sense be enforceable by the employees. Since then, the Opposition appear to have modified their line somewhat, and I shall show in a few minutes why we consider that their intention in new clause 13 would in fact be better expressed by our new clause 20. I do not believe that there is a great deal of difference between the two sides of the Committee in our ambitions, and I hope that by the time I finish my remarks the Opposition will feel that our new clause perhaps does their job for them rather better than their new clause 13.

Our proposal, however, has its origins 360 in the statement that my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State made before we took office, when he wrote a letter, jointly with the Secretary of State for Employment, to Conservative Trade Unionists in May 1978, saying:

"Company law should be amended to reflect existing practice in industry which is that, in furthering the interests of the company, the board of directors should have particular regard to employees as well as to shareholders".

We on the Government side therefore wish to see company law state quite unambiguously that directors have a duty—that is, a mandatory and not a permissive requirement—to consider the interests of employees.

We start from the proposition that the directors' duty to the company is to carry out their functions in what they consider to be the interests of the company as a whole. Subsection (1) of new clause 20 does not seek to change this and therefore means that the directors, as a matter of obligation, shall continue to have regard to the interests of shareholders, present and future, in the course of carrying out their functions. It then goes on to specify that consideration of the interests of the company's employees in general, that is, taken as a whole, shall fall to be included as a factor in determining the performance of those functions.

It is almost a truism that the status of the body of shareholders and of the body of employees is different. The new clause recognises the interests of both. It does not give the directors a reason for preferring the interests of the employees at the expense of members, which is the risk that certain forms of permissive entitlement might carry. The reference to the interests of members is therefore intended to clarify that the duty to have regard to the interests of employees will fall to be resolved within the existing duty of directors to the company.

In other words, we are saying that the directors' duty to the company is defined. It is to the company. In the performance of that duty they should, as a matter of requirement, have regard to the interests of employees. But it is not a double duty. It is a clearer definition of their existing duty.

Shareholders in this situation have to choose between not dismissing a director or paying him substantial compensation in respect of the unexpired part of his contract.

The possibility of abuse of this sort has been widely recognised for some time. Provisions to protect companies against it are contained in the Stock Exchange listing agreement. 392 The listing agreement requires that, in listed companies

"any service contract granted by the company, or any subsidiary of the company, to any director or proposed director of the company not expiring or determinable within 10 years by the employing company without payment of compensation (other than statutory compensation) must be made subject to the approval of the company in general meeting."

Our clause applies the general policy of this requirement to all Companies Act companies, and restricts the length of unapproved service agreements to five rather than 10 years.

Members of the Committee may have noticed that the word "agreement" has in this context replaced the more familiar term "contract of employment". This is not just a simple drafting change. It in fact represents the main departure between our clause and the similar provision in the previous Government's Bill. The purpose of the more general term "agreement" is to prevent rather obvious evasive devices—in particular, the device by which a director arranges for a service or consultancy contract to be entered into with his personal company. In this case there is no "contract of employment" with the director personally, though the practical effect of such an arrangement is exactly the same as if there were. The use of the word "agreement" is a convenient way of covering all such evasive arrangements.

The general prohibition set out in subsection (1) therefore refers to the inclusion in any agreement of

"a term to which this section applies"

and the relevant term is defined in subsection (2)as any term by which a director's employment with his company may be continued for more than five years without reference to the company's approval.

The prohibition is limited to those terms which have not been approved by the company in general meeting, as the alternative approach of prohibiting companies altogether from granting long-term service agreements is too restrictive. There is no inherent vice in such agreements provided that they are approved by the company and not simply drawn up by the directors to protect their own positions.

The general prohibition also applies to agreements between a company's director and any subsidiary of that company. This is necessary to prevent avoidance of the new clause by a director negotiating JPEG 393 agreements with a company controlled by the company of which he is a director.

The Committee should note that the prohibition applies only to the term, or terms, in the agreement relating to duration of the agreement rather than to the whole agreement, so that any contract implied by the agreement which is distinct from the terms relating to duration is to remain valid, and enforceable.

The offending term, however, is made void by virtue of subsection (5) and is deemed to be replaced by a term allowing the company to terminate the agreement at any time, provided that reasonable notice is given. If the duration term contains an option which can be taken up by the director without further reference to the company to extend the service contract, and this extension would take the agreement above the five-year limit, the whole duration term, not just the option to extend, is to be made void. Thus, there is a not inconsiderable incentive to ensure that contracts to which the clause applies are properly approved.

Subsection (3) prevents an obvious abuse by providing that where, during the course of an existing agreement, a new agreement is entered into the effect of which is to extend the director's period of employment for more than five years from the date at which the second agreement is entered into, that agreement must be approved by the company even though the period specified in the second agreement does not exceed five years.

An example of the sort of abuse that is thus prevented is where a director during the first year of a four-year contract, without the approval of the company, enters into a further four-year contract so as to allow a total continuous period of nearly eight years. We are, however, providing an exemption to allow for an agreement entered into within six months before the expiration of any existing agreement. This is clearly necessary to allow for the perfectly normal and proper renewal of an agreement.

The provisions of subsection (4) ensure that all shareholders, irrespective of their right to attend and vote, have adequate time to acquaint themselves with the terms of any long-term service agreement before it is voted on. It also provides that the details must be available at the general meeting concerned.

All wholly-owned subsidiaries of any 394 company, however incorporated, are excluded from the provisions of this clause, as are all companies other than companies registered under the Companies Acts.
