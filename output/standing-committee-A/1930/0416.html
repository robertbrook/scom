STANDING COMMITTEE A.
Thursday, 16th April, 1931.

[Mr. CECIL WILSON in the Chair.]

[OFFICIAL REPORT.]

WORKMEN'S COMPENSATION BILL.

Clause 1.
—(Amendment of 15 amp 16 Geo. 5. c. 84, s. 9 (4).)

Sub-section (4) of Section nine of the Workmen's Compensation Act, 1925, is hereby repealed, and the following sub-section shall be substituted therefor:

(4) If a workman who has so far recovered from the injury as to be fit for employment of a certain kind has failed to obtain such employment, he shall be deemed not to be able to earn wages and the judge shall order that his incapacity shall, for the purposes of this Act, be treated as total incapacity resulting from the injury for such period, and subject to such conditions, as may be provided by the order, without prejudice, however, to the right of review conferred by this Act:


Provided that every such order shall be made subject to the condition that it shall cease to be in force if the workman receives unemployment benefit:


Provided also that no such order shall be made if it is proved to the satisfaction of the county counrt that the workman has not taken all reasonable steps to obtain employment as aforesaid.

The UNDER-SECRETARY of STATE for the HOME DEPARTMENT (Mr. Short)
: I beg to move, to leave out the word "such" ["obtain such employment"].

In moving this Amendment, it may be for the convenience of the Committee, and facilitate the passage of the Bill, if I make a general statement as to the Amendments which stand in my name upon the Amendment Paper. The Committee will remember that when this Bill was before the House on Second Reading, which it duly received, there was a general agreement as to the existence of a grievance arising out of the decision in the case of Bevan v. Nixon's Navigation Company, and there was an equal con- 326 sensus of opinion as to the desirability of finding some basis of agreement and such words as would restore the law as it was understood to exist in the Acts of 1923 and 1925. It was clear that there was considerable opposition to the exact terms of the Bill as it was drafted, and, in view of that opposition, I ventured to approach the promoters to see if they would be willing for some negotiations to take place. They expressed a willingness for my hon. and learned Friend the Solicitor-General and myself to make such overtures, and various conferences have been held with my right hon. Friend the Member for Chorley (Mr. Hacking), who is leading for the Oppotion. We discussed fully and clearly the Bill as drafted, and as a result of the warm co-operation of the hon. and learned Member for Norwood (Sir W. Greaves-Lord) and the hon. and learned Member for Altrincham (Mr. Atkinson), we were able to find a basis of agreement.

The various suggested Amendments were submitted to our Parliamentary adviser and draftsman, and he has embodied them in a new draft of the Clause. They completely change the drafting of the Measure, but we are all satisfied that they restore the law as it was up to the decision in the case of Bevan v. Nixon's Navigation Company. Having secured a draft of the amended Bill, I consulted the various parties again, including the promoters. I also saw the hon. Member for West Middlesbrough (Mr. Griffith), of whose interest in this matter we are well aware, for he was the first to call the attention of the House to the grievance arising from that decision. I am glad to tell the Committee that if these Amendments which stand in my name are accepted, there is complete agreement among all parties in the House. That applies to the promoters, who were consulted. In these circumstances, I trust that this statement will obviate a lengthy discussion, and that hon. Members will be satisfied with the assurance that I have given them, supported as it is by my hon. and learned Friend the Solicitor-General, by our learned advisers, by my right hon. Friend the Member for Chorley, and by the hon. Member for West Middlesbrough, that we have attained our purpose and object, and especially the object of the promoters. In order that the Committee may see at a glance JPEG 327 what the Clause will be like with these Amendments, I have secured a copy of it as it will read:

"(1) Sub-section (4) of Section nine of the Workmen's Compensation Act, 1925, is hereby repealed, and the following sub-section shall be stibstituted therefor:


'(4) If a workman who has so far recovered from the injury as to be fit for employment of a certain kind has failed to obtain employment and it appears to the county court judge either—

  1. that, having regard to all the circumstances, it is probable that the workman would, but for the continuing effects of the injury, be able to obtain work in the same grade in the same class of employment as before the accident; or
  2. that his failure to obtain employment is a consequence, wholly or mainly, of the injury,
the judge shall order that the workman's incapacity shall be treated as total incapacity resulting from the injury for such period, and subject to such conditions, as may be provided by the order, without prejudice, however, to the right of review conferred by this Act:


Provided that—

  1. no order shall be made under this sub-section if it appears to the judge that the workman has not taken all reasonable steps to obtain employment; and
  2. every such order shall be made subject to the condition that it shall cease to be in force if the workman receives unemployment benefit.'"

There are two further Amendments in my name on the Paper: At the end of Clause 1 to add the words:

"(2) As from the passing of this Act the provisions of the sub-section so substituted as aforesaid shall apply in any case where the accident happened on or after the first day of January, nineteen hundred and twenty-four, and an application for weekly payments to be reviewed in accordance with those provisions may be made, notwithstanding that there has not been, any change in the condition of the workman or any change of circumstances other than the passing of this Act,"

and in Clause 2, to leave out "1930," and to insert instead thereof the words:

"1931, and the Workmen's Compensation Acts, 192,5 to 1930, and this Act may be cited together as the Workmen's Compensation Acts, 1925 to 1931."

It is clear that these two Amendments stand on their own, and have no reference to the Clause as amended by the other Amendments to which I have referred.

Mr. DOUGLAS HACKING
. The Committee will be obliged to the Under-Secretary of State for the statement that he has made. He is quite right when he says that negotiations have taken place, and that he and the Solicitor-General very fairly met certain Members on this side of the House who, on the Second Reading of the original Bill, disagreed with the provisions then contained in it. The Under-Secretary of State should also be thanked for having taken the trouble to give us the text of Clause 1 as it will be amended if all the Amendments which he has on the Paper are acceptable to the Committee. I hope that they will be found acceptable, for if they are, the Bill, as far as we are concerned, will have an easy passage into law. If, on the other hand, they are not acceptable, all I can say is that there may be a great deal more difficulty—I say this in a friendly spirit—in getting this matter settled, and reversing the decision in the case of Bevan v. Nixon's Navigation Company.

The Under-Secretary has also said that it was the original intention of the Act that this decision should never have occurred; in other words, this decision upset the intention of those people who placed the original Workmen's Compensation Acts upon the Statute Book, and it is our desire and, I think, the desire of the promoters of the Bill, that there should be re-inserted into the original Acts what was always the intention of the House that they should contain. This Bill, as amended by the Under-Secretary, will do that; it will carry out the original intentions of the Workmen's Compensation Act. It will do that and nothing more, and for that reason we accept the statement that has been made by the hon. Gentleman. We will accept the Amendments when he moves them. We are grateful to him for the happy solution which we believe he has found, and we congratulate him and his legal advisers on the way that they have dealt with this rather intricate Measure.

I have had a letter from my hon. and learned Friend the Member for Norwood (Sir W. Greaves-Lord), who tells me that he cannot be here this morning as he is detained at the Preston Sessions. He entirely agrees with the Bill as it will, I hope, leave the Committee when the Amendments which the Under-Secre- JPEG 329 tary proposes to move are inserted. He agrees that the effect of the new proposals will be to put the law into the position in which everyone thought it was before the Bevan case. He believes that that will be fair to everyone, and will prevent any recurrence of an injustice —which we all agree that it was—such as occurred in that case. He wishes to give the Bill his blessing if it is passed in the amended form, and to associate himself with my congratulations and thanks to the Under-Secretary of State and to his legal advisers in what, I hope, will be a happy solution of this controversy.
