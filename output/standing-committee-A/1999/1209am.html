Mr. Loughton: We now come to clause 338 and part XXIV, which deals with the specifications of the warning notices. It sets out the mechanisms for dealing with third parties, time limits for making representations and so on.

Under the clause, the authority must allow the person to whom a warning notice is given to have access to the evidence. However, the way in which the clause is written allows the Financial Services Authority to say that it will disclose only the selected papers on which it relied. That is not good enough. The recipient should have access to all the evidence that is relevant to the case. That is the intention behind amendment No. 156.

Mr. Loughton: We are looking to replace subsection (5) with a reference to

"the proposed action on the person to whom the warning notice is given."

This is an important part of the Bill. The warning notices are an important part of the enforcement procedure, and the amendments will make the provision slightly fairer.

Mr. Howard Flight (Arundel and South Downs): Part XXIV is important. Security lawyers have made substantial representations to the Treasury and the Col. 1216 Conservative party about it. It deals with the proper separation of the judge, prosecutor and jury. There are some subtle but important points that the Government would be wise to consider if they are to get this primary legislation right. My hon. Friend the Member for East Worthing and Shoreham (Mr. Loughton) has already spoken to our amendments, and I shall return to other issues in the stand part debate.

I shall summarise the territories covered by the clause. First, people who are being prosecuted should have provided to them all the evidence that is held against them so that they can defend themselves. Secondly, there should be clear separation between the parties within the FSA, not just between the prosecutor and judge. The person who carried out the research into the allegations should be separate from the person who decides what to disclose to the tribunal. Finally, when the FSA issues a decision notice, there should not be any phase of enforcement until the proper period of review has passed.

Our two amendments would ensure that the person prosecuted has the full evidence disclosed to him and, secondly, that the proposed penalty is made clear to the person being prosecuted. Although we are nearing the end of our debates on the Bill in Committee, we feel strongly that the Government should take on board certain matters, even if they do not accept our amendments.

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Miss Melanie Johnson): I am grateful to the hon. Gentlemans for their explanation of the amendments. There is little between us, but I should like to respond to the points that have been raised. It is not right to replace the requirement under subsection (1)(c) to set out the arrangements for access to evidence with a requirement to set out the proposed action, because various provisions in other parts of the Bill already do that. For example, clause 178(2) and (3) deal with warning notices when a public censure or financial penalty is proposed. Similarly, clause 100(2) covers financial penalties for market abuse and clause 335(2) refers to the power to require an authorised person to pay restitution under clause 334.

However, it is right to require the FSA to give a formal indication of a person's right of access under subsection (5) to evidence that might inform his decision whether to make representations and, if so, what those representations may cover. I understand the Opposition's arguments that some wider right of access to evidence should be granted, although the test that they propose of—

"evidence relating to matters covered by the warning notice"

seems rather too broad.

We must bear it in mind that the FSA is a supervisor as well as an investigating authority, and may hold a large amount of evidence in relation to an authorised person that could be said to relate to a matter that is covered by a notice without being especially germane to it. It might be rather bureaucratic and unhelpful if the FSA were required to arrange access to all the information that it might hold that might be said in a loose sense to relate to a matter covered by a warning notice. However, as I said Image Col. 1217 when we discussed clause 140, we considered the case that the Law Society made for broadening the right of access, and in broad terms we are persuaded that a case can be made for aligning the requirement more closely to the rules on disclosure that apply in criminal cases.

Further work needs to be done in refining those ideas, but we have in mind a provision that would require the FSA to provide access to evidence that it has considered and on which it does not propose to rely, but which it believes could materially undermine its case. That would prevent the FSA from relying on particular expert witness statements while withholding other expert witness statements that were less favourable to its case. Such a provision should meet Opposition Members' substantive anxieties. However, several further issues may arise, including the best way to deal with public interest immunity and legal privilege. We must carefully consider the drafting to avoid unwanted knock-on effects in other areas.

We are also inclined to include a comparable right of access following the issuing of a decision notice. Just as people require access to evidence in order to consider whether to make representations, they may also need access to evidence in order to consider whether to refer the matter to a tribunal, especially if they have not opted to exercise their right at the first stage. In addition, because we believe that third parties implicated in a warning notice should have some rights to see the relevant evidence, we shall introduce amendments to broaden the right of access under the clause and to reproduce it under the next clause.

I have tried to give a reasonably full response to the amendments. This debate, like the Law Society's input, has been very useful and will be reflected in our proposals. I hope that the hon. Member for East Worthing and Shoreham will feel able to withdraw the amendment.
