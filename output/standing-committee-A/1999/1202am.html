Miss Melanie Johnson: I am grateful for the opportunity to make a few points about part XVI in our stand part debate.

Pooled investment schemes may appeal to many types of investors, offering people with limited time or investment skills or with modest means access to an attractive variety of investment returns, including those from equities. Such attractive returns would otherwise be available only to the better-off and better advised or more sophisticated investors who can buy professional advice or make investment decisions for themselves.

I sometimes think of collective investment schemes as the investment industry's best kept secret. Despite relatively powerful growth of about 370 per cent, in real terms over the past seven years, direct retail holdings in collective investment schemes amount to only 5 per cent, of all personal holdings of savings and investments. That is modest in comparison with the 16 per cent, held in bank and building society accounts and the 55 per cent, saved through life assurance and pensions funds.

However, that very growth tells a tale in itself, reflecting the growing popularity of, first, personal equity plans and now individual savings accounts. The Government hope to broaden and deepen the popularity of saving through ISAs as the schemes become better known and confidence in them develops. Much of that saving will use collective investment schemes.

Turning to the structure of a collective investment scheme, I shall first deal with a straightforward point about terminology. I have been talking about authorised collective investment schemes as if there is only one kind. In fact, there are two kinds: authorised unit trusts and open-ended investment companies. The first is a trust and the second a company. They are first cousins or even blood brothers, as they have a lot in common. There is a third kind of scheme, the so-called recognised scheme, which essentially involves certain kinds of overseas scheme that can be marketed to the public in the UK. I shall address that scheme in due course, as it relates to clauses 235 and 254. However, the essence of a collective investment scheme is that a number of investors choose to make common cause in investing their resources.

To make that structure work in practice, the FSA has power to make rules on a variety of ancillary matters. The most important of those is, arguably, pricing: the objective is to ensure that investors are treated fairly so that they have confidence in the schemes to which they have entrusted their funds. That is why some of the clauses that we shall discuss in future specify matters that the FSA will have power to regulate. The Government are considering reinforcing those powers and, in some modest way, extending them. When it is possible to do so, it often makes sense for the FSA to have the scope to adapt the rules from time to time to reflect the requirements of the changing market.

The provisions of part XVI ensure that the FSA will now have the power to specify the range of investments in which authorised schemes can invest, Col. 1082 although the FSA must, of course, have regard to our international obligations, for example the provisions for the undertakings for collective investment in transferable securities directive, which impose constraints on the types of investments in which UCITS schemes may invest.

In addition, the Bill contains some important new provisions, allowing for the first time, express provision for the FSA to grant rule waivers and modifications regarding authorised unit trusts and, we intend, OEICs. We have also introduced rights for the operators and trustees of authorised schemes to refer matters to the tribunal in certain circumstances. That change should benefit the industry and demonstrates our commitment to establishing a regime that delivers a properly equipped, yet fully accountable, FSA.

As well as those specific changes, the Bill delivers one fundamental improvement to the existing position—the introduction of a single regulator. At the moment, operators of collective investment schemes might be authorised by the FSA, regulated for investment management purposes by the Investment Management Regulatory Organisation and, in many cases, have their marketing activities regulated by the Personal Investment Authority. All those functions will be regulated by the FSA, which will be a distinct improvement.

Miss Johnson: We move on to chapter II of part XVI, which deals with the restrictions on the promotion of collective investment schemes. That plank of investment protection is critical and clause 208 broadly continues the marketing prohibition contained in section 76 of the Financial Services Act 1986, although it has been updated in line with the proposals under clause 19 concerning financial promotion by unauthorised persons. The clause aims to be more technology-neutral than existing legislation.

The Government amendments will bring clause 208 further into line with the basic restriction on unauthorised and unapproved financial promotion contained in clause 19, as amended by the Committee in July. The Government amendments propose a new formulation concerning invitations or inducements to engage in investment activity to ensure that the communications to be caught are more clearly promotional. I hope that they will not be controversial.

The basic position under clause 208 is that authorised persons cannot issue an invitation to participate in a collective investment scheme unless that scheme is approved—unless, that is, it is an authorised unit trust, an authorised OEIC or a recognised overseas scheme. The clause also allows exemptions for other schemes to be marketed other than to the general public in circumstances to be set out in the FSA's rules. That carries forward the broad picture painted by the 1986 Act, Col. 1084 The one significant difference between the existing position under section 76 of the 1986 Act and the position that clause 208 will create relates to territorial scope. I thank the hon. Members for Arundel and South Downs (Mr. Flight) and for Wells (Mr. Heathcoat-Amory) for raising that important issue by tabling amendments Nos. 154 and 155, which would limit the prohibition under clause 208 to communications issued

"to a person in the UK".

I should re-cap briefly on the position under the 1986 Act and on the Government's thinking. It should be remembered that the substance of the discussion applies only to unapproved collective investment schemes; authorised unit trusts, authorised OEICs and recognised schemes are exempt from the prohibitions under section 76 and will be exempt under clause 208. We are only considering vehicles such as venture capital funds, ostrich farms and other potentially risky schemes.

Section 76 applies to advertisements issued by authorised persons that are "issued in the UK". That phrase has been widely interpreted by the market as meaning that that section applies only to promotions received in the United Kingdom. The logic behind that interpretation is that an advertisement is not issued until it is communicated or received. The view is that the current territorial scope under the section applies, first, to advertisements that originate in the United Kingdom and are sent within it and, secondly, to advertisements that originate outside the United Kingdom and are sent into it, which I shall refer to as inward promotions.
