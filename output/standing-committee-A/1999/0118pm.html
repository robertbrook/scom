Government Resources and Accounts Bill

4.30 pm

clause Clause 9
title COMPTROLLER AND AUDITOR GENERAL: ACCESS TO INFORMATION

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take new clause 1—Comptroller and Auditor General-right of access to information—

  1. 'This section applies for the purposes of the examination by the Comptroller and Auditor General of the accounts of government departments and other bodies under or by virtue of—
    1. this Act, or
    2. the Exchequer and Audit Departments Act 1921, or
    3. any other enactment, or
    4. any arrangement between such department or body and some other body which provides for the accounts of that body to be subject to examination by the Comptroller and Auditor General.
  2. The Comptroller and Auditor General—
    1. shall have a right of access at all reasonable times to all accounting records and other documents relating to—
      1. such accounts, or
      2. separately, any money originating in the Consolidated Fund or Exchequer grant, and
      3. any payments funded by money that would otherwise be payable into the Consolidated Fund.
    2. shall be entitled to require from any person holding or accountable for any such document any assistance, information or explanation which he reasonably requires.
  3. Without prejudice to any other rights vested in the Comptroller and Auditor General, the rights conferred by subsections (2) and (3) are also exercisable against, a receiving body to the same extent as similar rights are exercisable against the receiving body by a paying body.
  4. Notwithstanding subsection (3), the rights conferred by subsection (2) are not exercisable against any paying body or receiving body the accounts of which are subject to audit under section 2 of the Audit Commission Act 1998 other than a body specified in section 98(1) of the National Health Service Act 1977.
  5. The following provisions shall cease to have effect—
    1. section 28 of the Exchequer and Audit Departments Act 1866 (access to departments' documents);
    2. section 9(2) of the Exchequer and Audit Departments Act 1921 (application of section 28 of 1866 Act).
  6. "Paying body" means any body which pays to a receiving body money originating in the Consolidated Fund or Exchequer grant.
  7. "Receiving body" means any body which receives from a paying body money originating in the Consolidated Fund or Exchequer grant.'.

Mr. David Davis (Haltemprice and Howden): My primary emphasis shall be on new clause 1.I apologise to the Minister because I shall speak at length; I hope that she will accept that the Public Accounts Col. 172 Committee regards the issue as substantive and requiring proper treatment.

Before I start on the technical matters—they are technical and, by the same measure, extremely important—I should say that those of us privileged to be MPs come here for various purposes, all of which fit within a couple of guidelines. We are here to exercise power, often on behalf of the powerless, and to exercise our authority and strength on behalf of the weakest and most vulnerable in our society. In doing that as parliamentarians, the Comptroller and Auditor General and the people working for him producing National Audit Office reports act as our eyes and ears. The new clause would ensure that the NAO, acting as Parliament's eyes and ears in terms of access, could achieve the aims first envisaged for it in the original Exchequer and Audit Department Acts 1866, 1921 and 1957, and in the National Audit Act 1983. I should deal with the new clause on that premise.

Clause 9 deals with the rights of the Comptroller and Auditor General. The clear intent of the legislation to be amended by the Bill was to give absolute access to all Government expenditure, which was spent exclusively by Departments. Subsequently, there have been considerable changes in the machinery of Government, especially in recent decades. Increasingly, departmental activities, functions and the delivery of departmental programmes are handled by bodies at arm's length from Ministers or are contracted out to private sector suppliers. Many of the new forms of delivery have the goal of more efficient and effective delivery of departmental programmes. The Public Accounts Committee shares that goal.

The changes have, however, resulted in serious gaps in the accountability to Parliament of Government spending. Far from meeting the original intention of the Exchequer and Audit Department Acts to ensure parliamentary accountability for all departmental expenditure, the Bill will set in concrete the weaknesses that have developed.

Clause 9 is unsatisfactory. The Exchequer and Auditor Acts vested the powers of access directly in the Comptroller and Auditor General and could be, and were, interpreted as providing him with rights to papers outside the Department. The 1866 Act says that, for his audit of Departments:

"The Comptroller and Auditor General shall have free access, at all convenient times to the books of accounts and other documents relating to the accounts."

Mr. Oliver Letwin (West Dorset): I hope that we can clear up one point before my right hon. Friend continues. I suspect that it will be material to his argument and to his response. Has the Comptroller and Auditor General always been able to employ private sector auditors when that seems to be an appropriate means of enforcing his jurisdiction?

Mr. Davis: That is correct. Often, the National Audit Office employs private sector auditors because it is unnecessary to have the NAO's own skills, because extra skills are required or because a distant light touch must be exercised. It is important that the Comptroller and Auditor General can direct audits, a Image Col. 173 point to which I shall return in detail when I consider regularity.

Clause 9 is restrictive—it specifically makes Departments the gateway through which the Comptroller and Auditor General must gain access to the information required. The difference between the old and the new legislation may be subtle, but substituting powers directly invested in the Comptroller and Auditor General with gateways through which he must pass diminishes his independence.

The clause is unsatisfactory because it repeals, without supplying an equivalent provision, section 9(2) of the Exchequer and Audit Department Act 1921. Section 9 provides the only statutory basis for the Comptroller and Auditor General's powers of information and documents within many executive, non-departmental public bodies today. The statutes that established those bodies provided for the Comptroller and Auditor General to be appointed auditor, but did not make provision for his access to documents and information. The Comptroller and Auditor General relied on section 9(2), which provided for the access powers enjoyed in relation to departmental appropriation accounts, for powers applying to the other accounts that he audited.

The Treasury and other Departments must have accepted the Comptroller and Auditor General having such access to documents and information as they made no special provision in audit clauses of the Acts establishing the bodies. Through the repeal of section 9(2) of the 1921 Act, the powers and role of the Comptroller and Auditor General in several important executive and non-departmental public bodies will be thrown into doubt. They include many of the 19 bodies that the Government have established, including the new Legal Services Commission, the regional development agencies and the General Teaching Council. I have previously complimented them on having made him the auditor of the bodies.

The most important weakness is that clause 9 does not take into account the need to update the legislation to provide the Comptroller and Auditor General with the access needed to provide Parliament with similar levels of independent assurance over departmental expenditure as could be provided in 1866.

Despite the Government's claim that the measure is modernising, the Bill confines the Comptroller and Auditor General's powers of access to information within the walls of Departments as if Government machinery had stayed the same since 1921. The need to bring Government accounting into the 21st century is recognised, but nothing is done to update the rights of Parliament from those of the 19th century. It locks in place a reduction in the assurance that Parliament receives over departmental expenditure because of recent changes in the ways in which Government deliver public services.

I should like, first, to explain the changes and their impact on Parliament's scrutiny of the Executive; secondly; to turn to the arguments used against the Col. 174 Committee's proposed improvements; thirdly, to consider the arguments against addressing them in the Bill; and, finally, to explain the new clause in detail.

The main new bodies that conduct functions on behalf of Government fall into two main categories: first, there are private sector companies delivering contracted-out activities, such as the computer centre that processes £4 billion of civil service pay a year. Departments are committed to pay more than £70 billion over 25 years to private finance initiative contractors. Secondly, there are bodies in the independent and voluntary sectors that provide public services, often delivered at a local level, which are largely or wholly funded by Departments or funding bodies. They include housing associations that help to deliver social housing programmes that are worth more than £1 billion a year on behalf of the Housing Corporation.

Parliament has the right to expect the same standards of probity and regularity in the use of public funds wherever they are spent. Payment of public funds to service deliverers may be irregular or represent poor value for money if the services have not been delivered satisfactorily. Through existing powers of access to Departments, the Comptroller and Auditor General can examine the checks that Departments have made on the financial management or performance of service deliverers to which they have delegated responsibility. Such assessments may not provide adequate evidence for the Comptroller and Auditor General to reach an independent view.

It is worth giving examples of the circumstances in which the Comptroller and Auditor General needs independent access. The Department of Health pays about £140 million a year to provide milk for young children. Most of that sum reimburses retailers for milk that is supplied in exchange for tokens that are issued to parents in receipt of family credit or income support. The Department contracts out the processing of claims by retailers to the private sector, which is reasonable enough. The National Audit Office needs to examine systems and records that are held by the contractor to confirm that payments are being made only to those eligible under the scheme and at legal rates. In other countries, such schemes are characteristically afflicted by fraud. That is not a small consideration.

Another example is the Prison Service contractors who run three prisons that are designed, constructed, managed and financed under the private finance initiative. Payments made by the Prison Service to the contractors depend in part on the number of prison cells that are made available. The Comptroller and Auditor General needs to be able to verify independently that the prison cells exist and that procedures operated by the Prison Service to monitor cell availability are sufficiently robust to support contractors' claims. The access of the National Audit Office to public sector prisons is automatic, but to private sector prisons it is by negotiation, which I shall cover in detail in a moment. Relying on departmental assessments, checks and controls means accepting a Department's assurances that its performance is Image Col. 175 satisfactory. That does not meet the requirement of independent audit.

I find it difficult to understand how the Government can accept that the Comptroller and Auditor General should have to negotiate his way into such organisations. When he has no statutory right of access to such bodies, he is dependent on the body and its sponsoring Department for access, which he has to negotiate on an individual basis. That is administratively burdensome. It diminishes his independence and impedes scrutiny by the Public Accounts Committee on behalf of the House.

I want to share with the Committee the most notable examples of the frustration that the Comptroller and Auditor General and the Public Accounts Committee faced as a result of the problems caused by depending on administrative arrangements that were granted at the behest of Departments and the Treasury. It took five years of negotiation before they obtained access to the Housing Corporation and other executive non-departmental public bodies. The Comptroller and Auditor General was unable to provide the Public Accounts Committee with an assurance that Camelot had correctly handed over national proceeds of the lottery for four years because the company and its auditors refused to provide the evidence. The behaviour of the Office of the National Lottery has recently been criticised by the Comptroller and Auditor General. Perhaps the right hon. Member for Swansea, West (Mr. Williams) will remind the Committee of those exciting events, almost all of which happened without the co-operation of the bodies concerned.

Mr. Davis: Of course, my hon. Friend is right. I was about to say that the lack of statutory access rights impedes the Comptroller and Auditor General's ability to follow up serious problems raised by whistleblowers or to respond to Members of Parliament who have concerns about the use of public funds in their constituencies. That is another case of fragmented rather than joined-up government.

A relatively high proportion of whistleblowers and Members contacting the Comptroller and Auditor General do so in connection with contractors' abuse of public funds. Such cases clearly demonstrate that negotiating access as an alternative to the independently guaranteed statutory rights leads to the justification of Parliament and it being denied the required assurance on public funds.

Mr. Howard Flight (Arundel and South Downs): What access does the Comptroller and Auditor General have to the Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions and the Department of Health, both of which are involved in selling off huge amounts of property? I turn now to the arguments that I have heard against providing the Comptroller and Auditor General with statutory powers. The first is that it would lead to duplication of work between auditors, because local public spending bodies, private contractors and other bodies in the independent and voluntary sectors that help to deliver departmental programmes are audited by private firms.

The Comptroller and Auditor General would not repeat the audit work that such firms do to provide a true and fair view of the accounts. Private firms that audit such bodies do not audit to the standards of public audit. Unless contracted to do so, they do not examine whether funds provided to the body have been used properly or effectively. Their responsibility is to the bodies themselves, not to Parliament.

Moreover, such auditors do not always appreciate the special nature of the audit required of public money. For example, in several recent reports, the Comptroller and Auditor General has drawn to the Public Accounts Committee's attention failures in the handling of public money and the conduct of public business in the further education sectors. The most striking example was that of Halton college, where some £6.4 million claimed from the Further Education Funding Council could not be justified from college records. That position had developed over several years, despite the fact that the college's auditors had been required to certify their funding claims. That finding prompted the Further Education Funding Council to review the quality of work undertaken by private firms that audit colleges, and identified anxieties about the standard of audit in 11 out of 26 cases.

In the case of Gwent tertiary college, with which the right hon. Member for Swansea, West is very familiar, the college was taken to the brink of collapse by incurring a deficit of £6.8 million and by significant Image Col. 181 weakness in controls of claims for European funding. The college auditors had repeatedly certified that all grants and income had been used for the purposes for which they were received, but they had not conducted a detailed testing of expenditure using primary records to support their audit opinion. Private sector auditors are weak, especially in relation to regularity and the use of money for the purpose for which it was given.

A second argument that has been advanced against a statutory right is that it would mean that each organisation that entered a relationship with central Col. 182 Government, including, for example, the providers of paper clips and catering and stationery suppliers, would be subject to intervention by the Comptroller and Auditor General. That would be unnecessarily bureaucratic, invasive and inhibit the involvement of the private sector in the delivery of public services, but a statutory power of access should be based on the principle that the Comptroller and Auditor General should have at least as much access—no more than necessary, but as least as much access—as the Government reserve to themselves in paying out funds. That is the test.

The Government rightly secure access to bodies to which they have delegated service delivery, so that they can monitor the services for which they remain accountable. They do not reserve such powers for the paper clips, catering and stationery suppliers. As I said earlier, the Audit Commission in England and Wales and the Accounts Commission in Scotland have rights of access that extend to contractors. Those rights have not brought local Government to a standstill or prevented innovative partnerships between the public and private sectors at local level. Indeed, from my observations, central Government can sometimes learn from local government and some of those innovative partnerships.
