It throws a great deal of light on a ghost whose presence is not referred to in the Bill but whose immanence should pervade the whole discussion of the Bill's finances. I want to bring the ghost out into the light. He has some unappealing names. He is called "the emerging deficit" and even 144 "the uncovered liability". Perhaps it is the natural modesty of the Minister and the Joint Parliamentary Secretary that they do not want to look at this uncovered liability, this naked ghost, and do not want attention drawn to him.

When the insurance scheme was designed, it included large numbers of persons whose contributions, because of their age, even when their employers' contributions were taken into account and a flat-rate supplement was payable by the State, could not meet a pension at sixty-five for a man and sixty for a woman. We included in the scheme at that time a very large number of persons of whom that was true. In spite of the fact that there were large numbers whose contributions would have to be very high if they were to cover the cost of the pension that was promised, it was agreed that nobody should be charged contributions which were more than were sufficient to cover the pension liability of a young person entering the scheme at sixteen, and paying throughout his working life, except for intermissions which might occasionally arise from sickness and unemployment. It was agreed at that time by Parliament that this large number of middle-aged and elderly people who could not qualify for pension on a basis of contribution then fixed should nevertheless, by the age of sixty-five and sixty, qualify for the full rate of pension.

Parliament then assumed a large uncovered liability. It was certainly agreed in Parliament at the time when the Act was passed in that form. There was a solemn pledge by Parliament that, in spite of the fact that it had been decided not to levy on middle-aged and elderly people larger contributions than those paid by those entering the scheme at an early age, the uncovered liability which would eventually emerge would be met by the State. It was agreed with the T.U.C., which is very definite that the promise was given at that time.

Everybody knew that the deficit was bound to arise and everybody took for granted that when the time came the strength of the nation would be such that the taxpayer could meet the emerging deficit. Let us, therefore, look at Table 3 of the Government Actuary's Report. I will not go into all the details which might be obtained from calculations JPEG 145 based on Table 2. I can simplify the matter, though I think it is rather in favour of the Government's case, by taking Table 3.

On the Government's own calculations, the Exchequer liability of the taxpayer would be in 1961–1966, £172 million. It is going to be reduced by this scheme to £3 million. In 1966 the liability, which if no change had been made by these proposals, would have been £258 million, will be reduced to only £4 million. During the next period, 1971–1976, a liability which would have been £338 million will be reduced to only £3 million. Not only is the liability during this first fifteen years going to be drastically reduced in this way in the last period by no less than £335 million, but eventually under the Government's proposals the liability is to be wiped out and a surplus is to be provided. The liability of £398 million disappears and is replaced by a credit of £10 million. In other words, the total Exchequer liability is wiped out and a surplus is provided towards the national insurance fund.

What is the magic of this? How is it possible to remove in this way an emerging cost which everybody agreed such a short time ago as 1946 would eventually fall upon the taxpayer? The magic is that the person who in future is to pay graduated contributions under these proposals is to pay the Exchequer liability. It does not come from an increase in the flat-rate contributions. Indeed, for a time the flat rate contributions are to be reduced and eventually when they are increased they are not to be increased above the present level. There is to be no increase in the flat-rate contributions and yet a reduction in the emerging cost to the Exchequer liability running after fifteen years to over £300 million. If there is no increase in the flat rate contributions, and if there is to be a wiping out of the Exchequer liability, there is only one conclusion to be drawn, and that is that the graduated contribution is to pay the Exchequer liability. Suddenly after all these years—I admit that there was a partial shift in the right bon. Gentleman's last Act, but I will not talk about that now because I do not want to introduce unnecessary complication—the Exchequer liability is to be placed on the shoulders of a selected group of tax- 146 payers; that is to say, people who earn between £9 and £15 a week.

The way this is done is vividly illustrated in Appendix 1 of the Government Actuary's Report. Let us take, for example, the extreme case and look at the situation as forecast by the Government actuary for the year 2001–2. In that year the graduated contribution coming from employed persons earning between £9 and £15 a week is estimated to be £265 million, and the graduated contribution from the employers of those persons is estimated to be £272 million, giving a total of £537 million. That will be paid in 2001 as contributions. How much will be paid in benefits? A little further down the table hon. Members will see that graduated benefits will be £199 million. The people will pay £537 million in contributions and receive £199 million in benefits. Similar figures can be seen for other years. For example, in 1981–1982 graduated contributions from employed persons will be £193 million, and from employers £198 million, giving a total of £391 million; and the graduated benefits will be £63 million. The same thing applies to the year 1971–2, which is not very far away now: contributions from employers and employed at graduated rates total £295 million, and benefits for the graduated contributors £16 million; they get £16 million for a payment of £295 million. That is how it is done.

We cannot see any flaw whatever in this argument. The burden of liability fully undertaken by the taxpayer, by the Government of the day and by the Opposition of the day on behalf of the country in 1946 to meet the emerging cost of paying pensions for the elderly or middle-aged who entered the scheme in 1946 is to be met from now on under these proposals, to a gradually increasing extent, by a special tax on incomes of between £9 and £15 a week. That is the essence of the proposals in this Bill.

When my hon. Friend the Member for Coventry, East (Mr. Crossman) asked the right hon. Gentleman why he stops at £15 a week, and why he should not extend the graduated contributions further up the scale of income to, say, £50 a week, the right hon. Gentleman said that he thought that it would be unfair, or it would not be a proper discharge of the duty of the State, to compel people to make provision for their old age above JPEG 147 the level of £15 a week and that above that level of income they should make their own provision for their own old age. We do not agree with that concept.

But what do hon. Members opposite Who agree with it think about this? Suppose they think it is not right to compel people who earn above £15 a week to make superannuation provision for their old age, and they ought to be left to make arrangements with the insurance companies, do they think it right that people earning between £9 and £15 a week should be paying for other people's pensions? If it is wrong to compel a man earning over £15 to make provision for his own pension, why is it right to make a man earning between £9 and £15 a week bear the cost of other people's pensions? There is the nub of the whole question and the essence of our criticism of these proposals.

We cannot accept a proposal which in this way lays the burden solely upon this band of income. No wonder that the result of these proposals, which require contributions of £295 million from a selected group of contributors with a payment of only £16 million in benefit, or, as is required later, contributions of £537 million and only £200 million in benefit, is that individual contributors get less pension out of these proposals than they would get from private insurance. That is bound to be so under a financial system of that kind. My hon. Friend the Member for Loughborough (Mr. Cronin) during the Second Reading illustrated this effect upon the individual contributor of this method of financing the emerging deficit.

When he comes to discuss these matters, the right hon. Gentleman will no doubt say: " Yes; but face the facts. There is an emerging deficit. Tell us how you would propose to meet it. " No doubt he will point to the proposals which we put forward in our superannuation plan and will say that our plan provides for shifting some of the burden of the emerging deficit from the taxpayer to the contributor. But in 148 doing that we include a much wider range of contributions.
