Clause 1.

10.30 a.m.

Mr. A. J. Champion
: I beg to move, in page 1, line 11, to leave out from " programme " to the end of the line.

The debate on this Amendment to some extent will extend but not overlap the debate which we had on Tuesday. The Minister has rightly described the Bill as an enabling Measure, but by the inclusion of the words in line 11

"of a duration of not less than three years "

he is placing himself under an unnecessary restriction in this connection, because he is not giving himself enough scope. It appears to be equally the case at the other end of the five-year period, with which we shall deal later. It is true, however, that the Minister is giving effect to some of the limitations which he set himself in his White Paper.

As I understand it, the schemes under the Bill are intended to make provision both for a farmer who has 20 acres and who just creeps in under the standard labour requirement calculation of 250 standard man-days, and for a farmer who farms 100 acres and who just gets in under the 460 standard man-days, as well as all those who come in between the two. Clearly, the permutations between those wide limits are as great as the permutations on the eight-match treble chance football pool, although I imagine that not many members of the Committee understand what I am talking about there. The White Paper itself makes this point, for we read:

"There will be many possibilities varying according to the circumstances of each farm business."

Within those wide limits, it is not difficult to visualise circumstances in which capital and no other single thing is the limiting factor; there are circumstances in which there could be no other 52 single factor limiting the farmer in getting the full value from his land. In such a case, the payment of the farm business grant in one or two years instead of being spread over three years, or even the farm business grant given in total in the first year, might well do the trick, but it seems to me that the Minister is preventing himself from doing that by the limitation which he is imposing in the Clause.

My small farming experience is on a hill farm in South Wales. I am bound to say that the harrowing period was when we were seriously under-stocked for the capacity of the holding; getting the four additional cows and the few extra sheep which made all the difference was the greatest struggle of all. That is the period which my right hon. Friend the Member for Don Valley (Mr. T. Williams) described last Tuesday as the period of Gethsemane—the period of tears and struggle. Once we on our small farm had managed to get those additional cows, the possibilities of even that small farm expanded considerably. The use of fertilisers became a possibility, a mowing machine was bought and we no longer had to wait for our hay-making equipment until a good neighbour had finished his hay harvest. All this in turn led to a higher stock-carrying capacity. It enabled us to go through this extremely difficult period into a period in which we were going reasonably well, having regard to the circumstances of the time—for, of course, those times were very bad indeed for the farmer.

On Second Reading, my right hon. Friend the Member for Don Valley made some fun of the schemes by pointing to the fact that the 20-acre farmer would receive a farm business grant of £120, paid in four instalments. My right hon. Friend wondered which half of the heifer the farmer would buy with the sum of £30. In his reply the Joint Parliamentary Secretary said:

"As I have already said, the first two payments come in the first twelve months. If a man proposes to buy extra stock, we want him to increase the capacity of his holding first.. We want him in the first twelve months to devote himself to increasing it. We hope that after that, with the improvement that he will get through this grant and the field husbandry grant, he will be in a position to buy at least one heifer or one bullock and not one half, as the right hon. Gentleman said. It will be found that the figures work out fairly well."—[OFFICIAL REPORT, 10th November, 1958; Vol. 594, c. 148.]

JPEG 53 I must admit that that sounds fair enough. But if the farm's present capacity is such that the holding could immediately carry the two heifers or bullocks which the Joint Parliamentary Secretary hopes the farmer will eventually get, why impose an artificial time restriction upon him? Putting two heifers down to calf straight away, if the holding could carry them, together with the field husbandry grant, might well enable that farmer to work up to more than two additional heifers or bullocks.

These are matters which the N.A.A.S. man could very well decide by a glance at the farm. I am positive that if a district advisory officer had visited our farm in 1923 he would have seen at once that all we required to put us on our feet and to make the farm a satisfactory holding was four instalments of the business grant straight away, not spread over a long period. Knowing this industry, we recognise that the N.A.A.S. will be given a tremendous job. I am sure that the officers will manfully undertake that job, but, while it is right that the N.A.A.S. man should be given the limits of acreage and the size-of-farm-business tests and standard man-hour tests, I do not think there is the slightest necessity to impose upon the judgment of the N.A.A.S. man the restriction about the length of time it will take to bring the farm up to a decent standard and to enable the farmer to stand on his own feet and gain a satisfactory living, having regard to inevitable limitations of size, etc.

In this connection, it will not be necessary for the N.A.A.S. man to decide the credit worthiness of the farmer, although I am sure that it would not be beyond the capacity of most of these people who are to be entrusted with deciding the adequacy of the programme, to make such a decision. But I think that the length of time which a programme should take and the point at which the capital injection should be made is a matter which he could well decide, and if we were to leave it to him, I am fairly sure that he could decide at which point it would be best to make the capital injection. I repeat that in some cases it would be better to inject at the start rather than in driblets over the three years.

54 I should have preferred to have the last Amendment carried by the Committee rather than this Amendment, which is a second best but which is nevertheless a helpful Amendment to the Minister. I hope he will accept it. As I have said, any debate on the Amendment to a large extent can only underline what was so well said on the previous Amendment. The problem of the small farmer is so often merely that of obtaining that little additional capital at the start. Speaker after speaker in the last debate emphasised this aspect of the problem, and if speakers on the Government benches had backed their opinions by a vote, we should not merely have frightened the life out of the Joint Parliamentary Secretary, as clearly we did at the end of Tuesday morning, but should have defeated the Government and caused the Minister to think again on what is the crucial problem of the small farmer—additional capital at that right point.

This is a second best, as I freely admit, but it nevertheless offers some possibility of the Minister doing the right thing in this connection. I hope that he will accept it.

Mr. Sidney Dye

My right hon. Friend the Member for Don Valley (Mr. T. Williams) has contended from the start that the Bill was formulated inside the Ministry from an administrative point of view. It looks neat and tidy to administer from Whitehall, but all practical people—the National Farmers' Union and everybody else connected with agriculture—have urged upon the Minister the need to amend this Clause so that it is not merely a tidy piece of administration for officials to carry out but brings to small farmers who need it the help which will enable them to make their farms profitable. 10.45 a.m.

We are arguing from a practical point of view that when the advisory officer goes on to the farm to talk over with the farmer his scheme of improvement, if that farmer is a young and energetic farmer he will want to get on with it straight away. He will want to improve the fertility of his land, improve his grants, and then have the stock and equipment to deal with the greater production. Our contention is that the system of grants spread over four instalments in three years does not allow the man to go forward. The Minister is brindling him, holding him back, and preventing him from really getting ahead with the job on the farm.

From a practical point of view, therefore, I would say, as my hon. Friend has said, that this is a second best. Instead of a rigidly applied scheme all over the country for every kind and condition of small farm, we are asking that there should be greater elasticity. If all the people connected with a farm—the farmer himself, the visiting official and the agricultural committee—feel that, instead of giving four driblets, the farmer should get one sum that will enable him in the first year to increase production and profitability, then that is a sound proposition.

I am just wondering what the Minister can say in objection to this, because, as I said, if the same kind of scheme is operating right throughout the country then it is administratively tidy. But the Minister, of all people, knows that farming varies in different parts of the country and that the needs of the small farmer vary very much indeed. Therefore, he is asking for trouble in many cases if he just gives small grants and then the farmer gets into difficulty because, for some reason or other, he cannot continue the scheme and then a revocation of it is wanted. I think that this indicates the limit of the confidence which the Minister has in the ability of 56 our small farmers to get on to the job, improve the farms and make those farms profitable.

I could give many illustrations of the rapidity with which any land in the country which has been starved of fertilisers and is understocked will increase its output of grass and crops tremendously within a year or two years. That is the great thing. I could give instances of where it is not now a question of making two blades of grass grow where one used to grow, but of making twenty blades of grass grow where previously one grew. The period of the year over which grass will grow can be extended; it can be made to start growing in March and to continue growing until the end of November by proper fertilisation and proper management, whereas without those things the grass may not be suitable for turning cattle on to it until the end of April or early May.

In fact, 1st May, that was the general date for turning cattle out to grass in a big part of the country. But now we can do it in March, and, as I say, continue right through the year until the end of November. If, then, the best advice enables the small farmer to put up his production to such an extent, he should be given help to enable him to do it, and should not be restricted either in the amount, as indicated in the scheme, or by those four instalments.

If the sole method by which the Minister is proposing to help the small farmer is by grants and advice, then I say to him that he should break with this rigid system and should allow those who are giving the farmer the advice to say, " You can do that this year; the amount of money we can make available to you can be used in whole this year and it will be much more profitable this year than next." That is what we want to see. I want to speed up the thing. I do not want to see farmers who are energetic young men and who want to go ahead held back. This bridling in the form of a grant offered under this scheme seems to me to be the greatest hindrance.

Therefore, from a practical point of view, and knowing at any rate the eastern part of England very well, I can say that if the Government want to make this scheme the greatest possible success JPEG 57 amongst small farmers then we must ease up the scheme, allow it to be more elastic, give both the advisers to the small farmers and the committees more discretion and encourage the small farmers to go ahead more rapidly. I am sure it will pay the country in the long run.
