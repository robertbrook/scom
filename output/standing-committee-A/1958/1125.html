The Chairman: Before I call the first Amendment I would remind the Committee that there is a Financial Resolution. I do not propose to direct that it shall be read, but if Members of the Committee desire to see it they will find copies of it on the Table.

clause Clause 1.
title —(SCHEMES FOR GRANTS FOR INCREASING EFFICIENCY OF SMALL FARM BUSINESSES.)

Mr. Frederick Willey: I beg to move, in page 1, line 7, after " grants ", to insert " or loans ".

It may help to expedite our proceedings if I preface my speech with a few general remarks. We did not oppose the Second Reading of the Bill. We recognise that it is a necessary enabling Measure. We recognise, at the same time, that the Bill is specifically drawn to implement the White Paper proposals. We are not very enthusiastic about those proposals; we think that they are open to serious criticisms and are inadequate; but our purpose in discussing the Bill in Committee is to seek to widen its scope.

We do not propose to discuss the White Paper proposals, but we hope that the Minister will regard our Amendments sympathetically because their sole purpose is to provide him with wider powers. The White Paper proposals have been boosted for electoral purposes, but I accept them as being no more than a Civil Service scheme to implement the policies of the Government, and to the extent that we refer to them we are merely demonstrating the ways in which we think the Bill as at present drawn is inadequate.

Col. 4 The purpose of the Amendment is to ensure that the Minister should have power to make not only grants but loans. There is now a clear division between the two sides of the Committee. Hon. Members on this side have made it clear that they think that the credit facilities afforded to the agricultural industry are inadequate, and they will seek ways and means of extending them. I hope that the Minister will recognise that after the next General Election there will be a change of Government and, therefore, a change of Governmental attitude, and that it is quite legitimate that we should seek not to be inhibited in any way.

The Joint Under-Secretary of State for Scotland (Lord John Hope): I suppose the hon. Member got that from Aberdeen.

Mr. Willey: I am not trespassing in Scottish affairs.

I hope that the Minister will concede that this is an enabling Bill, and that it ought not to prevent recourse to the provision of credit facilities. We are not seeking to discuss the White Paper, but I hope that the Minister will leave the matter open, so that we can return to it if necessary and consider the provision of loans.

In support of my argument I would repeat that we are not enthusiastic about the present proposals. We do not think that they go far enough, and if the Minister accepts that as a possible criticism of his scheme I should think that he will want to leave open the possibility of making greater provision for loans. I would also remind him that there is a difference of opinion between him and the producers' organisation, the National Farmers' Union, on this point. The Union did not oppose the husbandry grants, but it thought that they should be supplemented by interest-free loans, the interest and the amortisation being a charge on the Price Review.

I recognise that the Union was in difficulties in discussing this matter, and naturally wished to lighten the impact of any provision made by the Minister upon the Price Review award. I appreciate that especially in the light of the difficulties which the Union experienced in relation to this year's award, but there are much more substantial arguments in support of the Union's proposals. I am not resting my case upon the ambiguity Image Col. 5 of the Price Review White Paper, but there is something in the Union's argument, viewed against the background in which farming has been aided over the past few years, that additional finance should be provided. If we consider the farm improvement grants, which we discussed fairly recently, and also have regard to the fact that 80 per cent. of the successful applications so far have been made by owners, and regard this scheme as being largely a tenant matter, we see that there is something in the broad case put forward by the Union that some finance should have been provided outside the Review moneys.

There is also something in the argument that the scheme should be underwritten by the State; that the State should obviously have some interest in the continuance and success of the scheme, but I rest my case in support of the Union's proposals upon broader practical grounds. The Union accepts part of the Government's proposals. It accepts the provision of husbandry grants. If those grants are to be successful in achieving the Government's objective, however, much more money may have to be provided, and I should have thought that it could possibly be provided by way of loans. If we regard the scheme in two compartments, the farm business grants will probably be inadequate and ineffective. My right hon. Friend the Member for Don Valley (Mr. T. Williams) referred to this question during the Second Reading debate. Most of us saw John Cherrington's article the other day, in which he made out a very persuasive case against part of the Government's proposals, and said that if they were implemented the small farmer would probably lack the capacity to manage the flush of grass and to provide the stock to eat it. Farm business grants are not sufficient for this purpose.

I know that this is a matter which could be argued both ways, but I would remind the Committee that the Report of the Committee on Grass Utilisation seems to support this view. It certainly calls attention to the gaps in credit for the farmers, especially medium-term credit to purchase stock, and long-term credit for tenant farmers. This being so, I would have thought that the Minister would at any rate go so far as to say that he cannot be absolutely Col. 6 sure about the proposals that he is making; that it would be wise to provide for the supplementation of any such provision by providing for the possibility of loans, and that loans might well provide the much greater amount of capital which appears to be needed and does not appear to be provided by the present scheme.

I also press the Amendment because we cannot be very satisfied about the intentions of the Government. I say that for two reasons. The Minister would concede that this is a short-term scheme. In the light of present Government agricultural policy we naturally suspect that whereas the under-20-acre man is now written off the small farmers who qualify for this scheme will also be written off after it has run out. We should like a more continuing interest in the problem of the small farmer, and all members of the Committee would probably concede that the advantage of loan aid is that it provides that continuing interest.

Again, if we turn to the White Paper and see what the Minister has said we cannot feel very satisfied. In anticipation of an Amendment such as this the Minister saw to it that an argument was deployed in paragraph 13, but it is certainly not a very satisfactory presentation of any case there may be against our present proposals. It speaks of loans at normal rates of interest. That is a typical Civil Service line of argument. It is not in issue at the moment. We are talking of loans bearing no interest, or a preferential rate of interest. We are talking of loans which will give some special aid to the small farmer. The White Paper refers to the inability of the Government to add to Exchequer commitments, and says that the Government cannot face a loan fund. This, again, is begging the question. We are here concerned with the question whether the present aid will be adequate, and if this approach in itself is not sufficient we should consider whether we ought to have an alternative approach. If we have to have an alternative to provide a greater measure of aid, the proposals for making loans will be a less heavy burden than the providing of further grants.

The White Paper rather gives the case away by saying that the Government hope that as a result of these proposals the small farmers will find it easier to obtain extra credit. We, too, hope that they Image Col. 7 will. It is for that reason that we are making proposals about credit, but if the small farmers are to be more creditworthy the Government ought to provide for the possibility of giving credit in respect of such schemes as they have in mind under the White Paper.

The Minister himself did not seem to be so optimistic about this matter. I would remind him that when questioned upon the subject of the provision of loams he said:

"The sort of chap whom we are trying to help is not the sort of chap who likes to have loans, and I shall be very sorry for any successor of mine who tried to get the money back."

That is a revelation of the purposes of the scheme. It is therefore no exaggeration to say that the scheme may well be the second phase in writing off small farmers. We are anxious that this attitude should not obtain.

Hon. Members on this side of the Committee believe credit to be one of the essential needs of the agriculture industry. We cannot accept the argument, as apparently the Minister is putting it, that the small farmer is not the sort of person who will seek credit. It is one of our major purposes to see that by one means or another we encourage a reliance upon credit.

The observation of the Minister that he would be sorry for a successor of his who tried to get the money back gives the lie to the whole scheme. We earnestly hope that the small farmer will become creditworthy. For that reason we also hope that the Minister will not reject this Amendment, or be carried away by the enthusiastic support he has received from the Economist, which stated that his scheme displayed some degree of toughness. That is high praise from the Economist, but it is disturbing to anyone interested in the agriculture industry.

I am not asking the Minister to accept all the arguments which I have advanced in favour of this Amendment. I am asking him to accept that there is a need and that it is desirable for small farmers to resort to aid which can be given by means of credit. This is an enabling Bill. I hope, therefore, that whatever may be the mind of the Minister at present he will not rule out the possibility of providing Col. 8 credit but, on the contrary, will indicate that he is not unfavourably disposed towards the provision of credit to small farmers or others in the industry.

Mr. Sidney Dye: There is undoubtedly a difference of view between hon. Members on each side of the Committee of the terms of this Amendment. It would appear to me that the Minister has put himself in grave difficulty because, on the question of loans to small farmers, he made the statement, already quoted by my hon. Friend the Member for Sunderland, North (Mr. Willey), that he would be sorry for those who had to try to get the money back. If that is the extent of his confidence in the small farmer, it is very small indeed. If that represents the extant of the right hon. Gentleman's belief in the future of the small farmer, then that future is limited. The question arises, if the Government have no faith in the future of the small farmers of this country, why give them grants?

Our point is that if grants are necessary, so also are the loans which are not at present available to small farmers on a sufficiently large scale. If anyone is to consider helping the small farmers by discussing cropping and their business arrangements, and so on, with the idea of the Government providing grants to assist them, why not at the same time discuss still larger improvements to small holdings which might be carried out by means of loans? The Government are saying that if this matter is dealt with by way of grants and loans, the two sources of money should be provided by separate departments—grants from Whitehall and loans from Lombard Street or somewhere else, wherever farmers can get them in the easiest way—and probably at the highest interest rates. In my opinion, that is a great flaw in the scheme.

The other day I questioned the Minister on the matter of loans to statutory smallholders. I was told that by 31st September last 1,225 advances had been approved, totalling £1,119,837. In 44 cases special steps had been taken to recover the payments which were overdue. I do not know how long a period is covered, but it would appear to me that those 44 cases represent only about 3 per cent. or 3½ per cent. of the advances. Apparently, therefore, the Minister is basing his opposition to the idea of loans on the fact that there were 3½ per cent. Image Col. 9 of failures. That is indeed a very small proportion. The question to which the Minister should apply himself is, what advantage accrued to the other 96½ per cent. who also received loans?

Those county council smallholders have done their work effectively and efficiently, but could they have done it as well without these advances being made available to them? Who were the men to whom this money was made available? They were men with known experience in agriculture and with ability. They had been approved by a committee of the small holdings committee. They represent the very type of man who ought to have assistance. They possess the knowledge and ability necessary to manage a small farm, but they lack capital. This scheme has proved a success when applied to statutory smallholdings. I agree that it is a limited success but we are asking that the assistance be made available to other smallholders who do not come under the county council, but are tenants.

The Minister has put himself in the wrong, I think, in asserting that his reasons for not granting a loan is that the people who receive such loans will not, or may not, be able to repay them. I should have thought a scheme could be worked out which would enable a small farmer to make use of such a loan, and repay both capital and interest, in such a way as to enable him to extend his business and make it more profitable. Then he would be in a better position not only to repay the loan, but to conduct his farm more efficiently.

My right hon. Friend the Member for Don Valley (Mr. T. Williams) said during the Second Reading debate that a small farmer with 20 acres could expect a maximum of £120 spread over four payments by way of grant. What improvements can a small farmer make towards improving the profitability of his holding on receipt of four separate grants, unless at the same time he is able to increase his stock? It would be no advantage to him to improve his grassland unless he could increase the stock to consume the extra grass, and produce more beef and milk, or some other commodity, which he could sell at a profit. So that, in addition to a grant to improve his grassland, a small farmer must receive aid to enable him to purchase more stock.

Col. 10 If the Minister argues that there are credit facilities available which would enable a farmer to increase his stock by means of hire-purchase, my reply is that the interest rates are so high that I would not recommend a small farmer to use such a method to increase his stock; and I do not think that the Minister should do so.

There is a further aspect which I consider of importance. If a small farmer who wishes to increase his stock is left to his own devices, he will increase the production of eggs, milk, pork and bacon, because they are the easiest commodities to produce and require less capital. They involve the expenditure of a certain amount of time and the provision of some initial loan or extra money. In that way a farmer can increase the number of his poultry or pigs or add another cow to his herd and increase his milk yield.

I asked earlier what was the purpose of this Bill from the point of view of increased production. Was it intended just to enable a small farmer to produce more of such commodities as eggs, milk, bacon and pork of which there might be a surplus at present? Or was the idea—and this I think would be a better conception—to enable the small farmer to produce other commodities which might be in short supply because of importing difficulties and of which this country might find itself short in the next few years? Beef is a good example.

What would a small farmer need to enable him to rear more cattle for the production of beef as against milk production or pigs and poultry? He would require more capital to enable him to increase his production of those commodities which the nation needs. I speak from practical experience. I am a small farmer, although I do not come within this scheme. I have only 80 acres; therefore, on an average basis. I should come in; but on man-days per year I am a long way out because I am not producing those commodities about which there is difficulty. I am producing beef on a comparatively large scale. To do that a small farmer must have far more capital than is necessary to rear poultry and pigs.

At present I have on my holding 52 beef cattle which would probably be valued at between £60 and £70 each. A small farmer with a limited amount of capital cannot do what may be done by a small farmer who has the necessary Image Col. 11 capital. Each year I have to pay £3,000 to £4,000 for store cattle to fatten on my farm. If a small farmer desired to go in for rearing calves, he would have to have the cows in order to produce the calves, or he must be able to buy calves and keep them for a period before he can resell them. The proposed grants will not enable any small farmer to increase his beef production. He cannot do that by means of £120 or £220 spread over four instalments. It will enable him to improve his grassland, and probably to increase his production of other crops, but it will not enable him to improve the animal side of his husbandry. And the cost in interest rates from other sources of credit are so high as to make that avenue unprofitable.

11.0 a.m.

I speak as a practical small farmer, and I say without hesitation that this scheme, without adequate credit facilities in addition to grants, will be of very little value to our small farmers. The Minister's view seems to be, " Well, the small farmers seem to be such a poor financial proposition that if they had loans they could not repay them." That view shows very little confidence in the future of the small farmers. Or, of course, it may be a long-term forecast of the Conservative Party's attitude towards agriculture, that there will not be a future for the man with ability but without the capital necessary to extend his production so as to make his holding comparable in efficiency with that achieved by the Danes, the Dutch and others with experience of this sort of thing.

From our knowledge of what has taken place in Denmark, we know that that country could not have become a leading bacon-producing country had not the basis of its system been grounded in the past on credit facilities that were good and easy. Those facilities must be available all the way along, and I do not think that we can look at the progress made by small farmers in other countries without returning to the conclusion that progress has been made, not only by the farmers' knowledge of their job but by the added amount of loans made available to them at the vital time of the development of their holdings. That Col. 12 was the basis of the progress made years ago in Ireland.

It is for that reason that I say that if the efficiency of our small farms is to be brought up to the level of that of any other country in the world, and comparable with that of our large farms, the proper way to go about it is to give the farmers the capital. That is particularly so of the younger men, those below 50, who have a number of years before them. They want so to set out their farms as to promote better and more efficient management. Instead of these comparatively small driblets of grants, small as compared with the grants given to the bigger farmers, we should put into operation, in conjunction with the banks, with other financial institutions, the county agricultural executive committees and the district committees, some system such as that I have suggested. On that basis, this scheme could be made a real success.

Quite recently, I have come across a number of cases where bank managers have asked certain very efficient farmer customers to look at some of their neighbours with big overdrafts, and to recommend how those less efficient farmers should change their methods of production so as to make profits. That is the kind of thing that we should like to see put into operation on the smaller farms, with men of known business ability working through the county and district agricultural committees, with the added power to provide this essential element of credit.

If that were done, this scheme could be made a success. Without it, I do not think that it can. Without it, the scheme will be just a short-term business in which, to use the Minister's phrase yet again—and he must expect to have it cast back at him—we are having just a little flutter now, but the credit future seems a little dim for these people.

We have avoided stipulating the rate of interest. The National Farmers' Union recommended no interest charges in certain cases. Personally, I have never asked for anything for nothing, and would not do so now. I believe that people should be prepared to pay for what they receive, whether it is credit, or other facilities. In this case, however, the interest rates need to be small, the period limited, and the guidance of the Image Col. 13 agricultural executive committees should be available.

I am convinced that that is the first vital improvement that the Minister ought to make in his Bill. He ought not to take the attitude, " The Conservative Party has considered this, and this is as far as we can go. We have not much time to consider anything else, because we want it put into operation quickly." Instead of that, it is far better to spend a little more time in so improving the Bill as to make it a credit to the House, a credit to the Minister, and a credit to the country.

Sir James Duncan: I will not vote for the Amendment, for reasons that I shall give. Reference has been made to the small farmer's problem in getting capital, and the Amendment raises the question of whether or not the Government should give the credit directly. I shall not vote for the Amendment, because I do not think that the Government are the right people to give the loans. Mr. Thomas Fraser: They have given the hon. Gentleman up.

The difficulty is that although we are improving the creditworthiness of the farmer, that creditworthiness may not continue after the receipt of the grant.

There is no specially increased creditworthiness for the landlord except, possibly, in the increased value of the farm. I can see a difficulty arising there first, of the tenant farmer's inducing the landlord to spend, and, secondly, of the landlord's obtaining the capital on such terms as to make it a business proposition for him to improve the holding.

I should like to see the Scottish Agricultural Securities Corporation much less remote, and much more speedy in dealing with loan applications. I have heard complaints that it is too slow, and too remote. In the case of tenant holdings, little or nothing can be done, because, in Scotland, we have the peculiar system under which money can be lent only on property and not on movable equipment. There is there a real problem, and the Government must look at it.

Admittedly, this Measure will give a great deal of help, but it will not do everything. I disagree with the Amendment because I think that here it is wrong to make the Government lend the money, but there is a credit problem here which would be better solved by a speedier and more humane approach to loan applications than that at present shown either by Image Col. 15 the Agricultural Mortgage Corporation in England, or by the Scottish Agricultural Securities Corporation in Scotland.

The bankers are willing to help on the short term, but there still remains the problem of long-term loans for buildings. I hope that the Government will look at that problem very carefully, and do what they can to improve the situation. I do not believe that they should do it directly; but, indirectly, if this scheme is to work, they have an obligation to deal with this question of capital.
