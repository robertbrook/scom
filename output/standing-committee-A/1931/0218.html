The evidence of the need for such powers as I propose will be found, first, in the fact that it is only county councils that could possibly carry out such schemes, which would really be the backbone of all subsequent development, to which everything else would be subsidiary, and rightly and properly would fit in to these main features. Evidence is to be found, thirdly, in the experience we have had of the construction of arterial roads. The general idea underlying the Amendment is the present method whereby road maintenance is governed and controlled. County councils have the obligation to maintain county roads, while the subsidiary roads, being almost exclusively of local interest, are dealt with by the local authorities, and thus there is room for the county activities in dealing with the main roads, and local activities in dealing with the local roads. It is on that principle that the Amendment is conceived. I have conceived the idea of regarding the county as the district from the experience of Scottish local administration. In Scotland, the county is divided into districts governed by district committees for certain health JPEG 127 and other purposes, and it is possible for county councils to vest in themselves as a whole the powers and duties of the district committees, and then they can operate as district committees. Similarly, I am proposing that a county should operate as a district for this purpose. It would be quite simple to achieve this object without very considerable dislocation of the text of the Bill.

The basic principles on which I propose to proceed are to leave the county councils to prescribe the main, central and dominating features of the scheme, and to leave to the local authorities the filling in. Unless we do that, we shall never get any broad conception of regional planning. We shall get town planning which will be a continuation of a sort of peddling principle whereby we provide really for so many houses to the acre, and for an unfortunate and unnecessary interference with regard to elevations and that kind of thing. There is no power that I can see in the Bill to enable any broad, constructive scheme to be laid down. Under Clause 2 (2), county councils appear to have some power, but that is somewhat illusory in that a county council can become a town planning authority only if the county district authority agrees voluntarily to relinquish its powers. There is no initiatory power conferred on the county councils under that provision.

Mr. McENTEE
: I would cut out the co-opted members altogether, but, if the right hon. Gentleman is inclined to allow them on joint committees, I would urge him to reduce the number. I know of cases when co-opted members have actually been in the majority although only a third were members of the committee. I can see a joint committee of that character spending large sums of public money which would not be done if there were a fuller attendance of elected members. I am not sure whether there is any ruling body which could undo a thing like that, but in any case it appears to me to be a danger which we should not allow.

There is another danger that strikes me. When you get a joint committee of different authorities coming together for a common purpose, each would have its own district particularly in mind, and the welfare of its own district would always be uppermost. It might be, at the first meeting at which co-opted members were appointed, if there were three authorities, two of them might be one or two short of their full representation and the other might take advantage of it to give its own particular district a permanent majority and get considerable advantages for it. That is a danger that we ought to avoid. Generally speaking, I think the experience of the Ministry of Health and the complaints they have had of similarly constituted committees should induce them to hesitate before they allow so high a number as a third of the actual constitution of the committee. If the right hon. Gentleman is not prepared to strike out the principle of co-option, he should, at any rate, agree to a considerable reduction in the proportion.
