Sir H. YOUNG
: If the hon. and gallant Member considers the circumstances which this Sub-section meets he will find that there is no hardship of the kind he describes. The circumstances are these. A man owns a plot of land on a highway. A town planning scheme may fix a building line, and owing to the fixing of that building line enough land may not be left to the owner of the plot of land to make it serviceable for building purposes. In those circumstances he is entitled to full compensation, and cannot be deprived of it. What the Sub-section says is this. It may be that the owner of the plot does not want to carry on, and he might pull the building down and go to compensation. What the Sub-section provides is that in those circumstances he shall give notice to the authority of his intention to pull down the building and, if he does, it is entirely at his own option, then the local authority shall have the right to acquire JPEG 759 the site. From the point of view of a local authority that is fair, because otherwise they might have to pay compensation for a site which might be left vacant and unprofitable for a long time.

If, on the other hand, the authority has the right to acquire the building which, ex hypothesi, the owner no longer desires to use himself, then, until the coming into operation of a scheme, the authority will be able to let the building and recover a little of the loss they would suffer. It is a reasonable provision to enable an authority to avoid loss by a process which inflicts no injury on the owner. As to compensation, of course the greater includes the less. If the owner is paid the full purchase value of a plot of land which he no longer desires to use himself that includes and covers any right to compensation; he has sold at its full value something which he no longer desires to use himself. In those circumstances this is really a reasonable provision to enable a local authority to avoid loss, which is avoidable, without inflicting any hardship on the owner concerned.
