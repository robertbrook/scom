Sir HENRY CAUTLEY: I beg to move, "That the consideration of the Clause be postponed."

I make this technical Motion with a view to eliciting the Minister's views as to the conduct of these proceedings, and also with a view to getting a reply to some questions that I wish to put to him.

The CHAIRMAN: Order! We do not allow smoking in these Committees.

Sir H. CAUTLEY: In the first place, I desire to enter a protest against the way in which this Bill is being rushed. The Second Reading of the Bill, which has some 58 pages, took place only on Tuesday last, and the Committee was set up on Thursday. Many Members did not receive notice of it till Saturday morning. The House of Commons, I believe I am right in saying, is composed as to no less than 75 per cent. of new Members, and it is quite impossible for such Members to read and understand this Bill and to take counsel with their friends or with those who are interested in the Measure so as to put down Amendments; and further, as to such Amendments as have been put down, nobody in this room has had the opportunity of reading them until this morning. I should like to ask what is the reason of this haste.

My interest in this far-reaching Measure is that a number of my constituents have asked me to interest myself in it because they are very much alarmed as to the scope of the Bill and as to where it is going to lead. I am not going to say much about the Bill now, but as far as I understand it, it deals with Col. 6 the whole of the land and buildings of England, Scotland, and Wales, and it removes the control from those who have hitherto had control of the development and puts it in the hands of local authorities as to whose fitness to be entrusted with such large powers very many people have the gravest apprehensions. Further, a number of persons with whom I have been in communication regard the Bill as placing a dead hand over the land, buildings, and development, which is extremely undesirable. Therefore, I enter this protest for my own part, and I would ask the Minister what is the reason for this haste and why he is so pressing it forward.

In the next place, I would like to ask him to do this: Most of us do not know the terms of the Bill that was under consideration in the last Parliament under the Socialist Government, and it would be a great convenience and would very much expedite the consideration of the points that will come before this Committee if the right hon. Gentleman could see his way to having a copy of the Bill prepared, showing which are the parts of the old Bill and which are the new parts. I do not anticipate that that would cause any very great expense or trouble to the officers of the Department promoting the Bill. My last point is to ask whether, during the course of the hearing before this Committee, the right hon. Gentleman will be in a position, or will put himself in a position, to give us some estimate of the cost that will be imposed on all the local authorities in the country by the passage of this Bill. I am informed—and I believe it to be the fact—that it will require the appointment of a separate town planning officer on every local authority, and in the majority of cases of a considerable staff to carry out the details of the Measure; and it is not to be forgotten that if the local authorities themselves think it undesirable to proceed with it, or to do so with energy and zeal, the Minister himself has power to take it upon himself to do so.

Mr. M. W. BEAUMONT: Before the Minister replies, I wish to add a few words to the observations of my hon. and learned Friend the Member for East Grinstead (Sir H. Cautley). I had intended to raise a discussion of this nature on the first Amendment on the Paper, Image Col. 7 but I think it will probably be for the convenience of the Committee to take that discussion now, and I should like to ask the Minister to state as clearly as he can what are his intentions with regard to the conduct of this Bill. This is not a non-controversial Bill, but a Bill to which there is considerable opposition and one which some of us would gladly see dead and certainly must be unwilling to see on the Statute Book unless it is very radically amended. On the other hand, we are all busy people and do not like sitting in this Committee room morning after morning for weeks on end, and it is not our intention to offer that rather factious opposition which it might be possible to put forward if the Government conducting the Measure were one in which we had not the utmost confidence; but I am bound to confess that we are not encouraged in that attitude by what my hon. and learned Friend has referred to as the hurrying on of the Bill.

If I may say so without offence, I think it was fortunate for the Minister that the Bill got its Second Reading on the first day after the adjournment of the Session. My hon. and learned Friend has mentioned the difficulty which many of us, and particularly the new Members, have in understanding, until we get practice, Bills and Amendments thereto, and I doubt if this Bill would have had the pleasant passage on Second Reading Which it did in fact get, had there been more time for Members to discuss it and realise its provisions fully. On the top of that, the Bill is brought into Committee a week after its passage on Second Reading. I speak subject to correction as a Parliamentary two-year-old, but I do not think it is customary to be quite so quick, and certainly the Socialist Government never, among their many crimes, hurried us quite so much as this.

The PARLIAMENTARY SECRETARY to the MINISTRY of HEALTH (Mr. Ernest Brown): Action!

Mr. BEAUMONT: I see the force of the hon. Member's comment. On the other hand, if the cry of "Action" is to be such that the opposition to this or any other Bill are not to be given adequate time to consider their Amendments and their position, I think the action may well end in delaying rather than expediting Col. 8 Measures. I wish to join in my hon. and learned Friend's protest. I know, for instance, that one of the keenest Members of this Committee, one who has taken a leading part on all the previous stages of this Bill, my Noble Friend the Member for West Derbyshire (Marquess of Hartington) went to Ireland with no idea that this Bill would be coming on so soon. I only discovered it on Friday morning, and managed, by hard work over the week-end, to obtain consideration of the first Clause, but the Minister and the Parliamentary Secretary will see, by the small number of Amendments on the Paper on Clause 1, how inadequately our work has been done, and I hope the Minister will not try to rush this Bill through without giving us a chance to voice our objections or to consider the matter carefully.

It is true that this Bill, or one very much like it, has been through a Committee of this House already, but very few of the Members of this Committee were also on that Committee—and the House has changed, most of us, I think, will agree, very much for the better since that time—and they must have time to consider this Measure anew. On the Committee stage of the last Bill the present Attorney-General said:

"In the friendliest possible spirit towards this Bill, I would express the hope that the Minister will not, from the beginning, adopt the attitude that the drafting of this Bill, which is a difficult one, came down from the Mount, and cannot be improved."—[OFFICIAL REPORT (Standing Committee D), 28th April, 1931; col. 621.]

That is our attitude. There are certain salient points on which we consider that this Bill is intolerable. If we know that we shall be met in a friendly spirit and that the Minister will try to get as nearly as possible an agreed Bill, it will be very much easier for all of us to cut these discussions and get, at the end of a short Committee stage, I will not say a good Bill, because I do not think anything embodying the principle in this Bill could be good, but 'a very much better Bill than the one now before us, a very much improved Bill, by general consent; and the Minister will get on to the Statute Book what I know is very dear to his heart with very mach less trouble and much more quickly than if we have to fight and discuss at length every point of substance which we feel it right to bring forward. I make these observations in the hope Image Col. 9 that the Minister will make a statement on these points, so that we shall know exactly where we are and what his intentions are; and we can then progress with reasonable celerity.

Mr. HURD: While I am very much in harmony with much that my hon. Friends feel, I do not think it ought to go out from this Committee that this Bill is being rushed through Parliament in a very indecorous fashion. I sat for many weeks on the Standing Committee that considered this Bill in great detail and went into every aspect of it, and we have had two elaborate discussions on the Floor of the House. We have also had the Measure discussed in the public Press and in various public bodies all through the country. There is a universal feeling that we are getting into a horrible mess, and it is urgently necessary that something should be done to establish some degree of orderliness. Anybody who would suggest that my right hon. Friend the present Minister of Health is rushing pell-mell upon this question without the fullest details is showing an ignorance of my right hon. Friend and his methods. I do not see anything hurried and precipitate in the way this Bill has been brought forward. The call for it is urgent, and the sooner we get on with it the better it will be for the public generally.

The MINISTER of HEALTH (Sir Hilton Young): I cordially welcome the observations of the hon. and learned Member for East Grinstead (Sir H. Cautley) and the hon. Member for Aylesbury (Mr. M. W. Beaumont), because I think they will assist us in clearing up matters at the outset of the discussions on this Bill. The hon, and learned Member for East Grinstead mentioned one point on which we can assist him to an understanding of this Bill, and that was by showing him the particulars in which the present Measure differs from the previous Bill. That is a most reasonable request, with which I will comply in whatever is the most convenient way. I am not sure which will be the most convenient way, because the differences are very slight. I explained on Second Reading that the only differences between this Bill and the Bill which was threshed out in the last Parliament are that on this occasion we have inserted all those accommodations and arrangements which Col. 10 were arrived at in Committee on the previous Bill. On one matter, dealing with betterment, we have been able very much to simplify the Bill in a manner which should commend itself to hon. Members. However, I will arrange to have the information provided in whatever is the clearest and most convenient form.

On another point he raised, I will give him the best information I can when we come to the relevant point in our proceedings—will give him whatever guess we can make at the cost which may be thrown upon local authorities. He will understand that the information can only be by way of estimate. I think his fears that it will cast any considerable cost upon local authorities in relation to the actual work done will not be realised. If I understood him aright, the hon. Member for Aylesbury asked me in what spirit the opposition to this Bill which he has demonstrated is to be met in this Committee. Let me hasten to say I hope it will be met in a spirit of accommodation and reasonableness. I welcome, in particular, as likely to save our time here, what he said as to the difference between what may be called general opposition and criticism of points of acknowledged difficulty directed towards amending the Bill in specific particulars. I freely recognise that this is a difficult and anxious province of administration, and that we are feeling our way towards the best solution of certain very difficult problems, and on certain matters which are clearly in the minds of my hon. Friends opposite, I shall be eager to welcome any constructive suggestions which will improve the working of the Measure or introduce safeguards for the protection of this or that public interest.

Our proceedings will be conducted in a spirit of reasoned accommodation, And by no means in a spirit of antagonism, but I believe we can save very much time by concentrating discussion upon what are known to be the difficult parts of the Bill, and not allowing it to range too widely over principles which were established on Second Reading. Some criticism has been advanced on the grounds of haste. I do not think that is quite justified, and in that I agree with my hon. Friend the Member for Devizes (Mr. Hurd). It is quite normal for the first Bill which obtains a Second Reading Image Col. 11 in the House to come upstairs to Committee straight away. That is necessary in order to avoid wasting the time of hon. Members, and, what is more important, wasting valuable Parliamentary time. If the first Bill is put off it may prejudice the chances of some useful Measure towards the end of the Session. It is only the circumstance that this happens to be the first Bill which produces the impression of haste. When the hon. Member was speaking I considered whether there was any substantial inconvenience to hon. Members, in the fact that our proceedings began this morning, and I do not really think there is, because the Clause we shall be discussing this morning, the first Clause, is really nothing more than the general principle of the Bill, and speeches on it must be rather of a general and Second Reading character. The Clause does not raise any difficult technical matters such as would require consideration and the consultation of other Statutes.

Therefore, I do not think we are doing anything unusual or placing any inconvenience on hon. Members in asking them to save their own time and Parliamentary time by continuing the discussion of Clause 1 this morning. I hope that with this explanation of the assistance which we shall be able to give, and the spirit in which all constructive criticism will be met, and of the circumstances under which we meet, the hon. and learned Member for East Grinstead will see his way to withdraw his Motion.

Brigadier-General CLIFTON BROWN: Though not wishing to obstruct, I support the protest that this Bill has been very much rushed. It is all very well for the hon. Member for Devizes (Mr. Hurd) to say that a similar Measure has been considered in Committee several times, but I and several others were not on that Committee. Captain WATERHOUSE: I am sure the Committee are most grateful for the words of my right hon. Friend the Minister of Health. We hope that the Attorney-General, who took a rational and constitutional view in the discussions on the previous Measure, will honour our proceedings by his presence here from time to time.

Sir J. WALKER SMITH: I hope that the Minister will agree with the proposal for some postponement of the consideration of this Measure. There are many new Members who think that rather indecent haste has been shown in again submitting to this House a derelict Measure of the late Socialist Government, which the present Government happened to find when they came into office. It may well be that there are Col. 14 very few differences between this Measure and the relic which they found, but that does not make it any more acceptable. It is a new Bill to Members of this Parliament, and the fact that a similar Measure received a good deal of consideration under totally different circumstances does not in the least commend this Bill to our favourable consideration. Circumstances have changed considerably. The Minister in a very conciliatory explanation indicated that he would proceed on the lines of endeavouring to obtain reasoned accommodations to which all would subscribe. But he seemed to be unduly impressed with the question of cost to local authorities. I know that that is a matter which impresses his Department very much. But the cost to the Government or to the local authorities will not concern the whole of us so much as the deadly, ghastly, cost to the whole country which the prohibitive and restrictive nature of this Measure will involve. That is what concerns many of us and we would like a little more time for the consideration of the matter from that point of view. No possible harm could come from a postponement for the purpose of enabling this Measure, in all its implications, to be better understood.

It is not as though there were no powers already available for dealing with this question. Ample powers already exist. No one is gasping at the present time for additional powers. There are plenty of provisions already which local authorities can get along with and there is certainly no clamant demand from local authorities or indeed from anyone for this Measure. Local authorities have as much as they can manage in the existing powers. Powers of this kind have indeed existed for the last 22 years, and I suggest to the Minister that if he left this matter alone and did not interfere, and did not keep on whipping up the local authorities to clamour for further powers, the local authorities would go along peacefully, quietly and successfully dealing with the enormous amount of town planning which the present provisions enable them to deal with.

It is to be remembered that after the local authorities had had these powers for about 16 years, so little demand was there for additional powers, that additional powers had to be placed upon Image Col. 15 them, not by way of option, but as compulsory powers. It was found necessary to compel local authorities to prepare town planning schemes in respect of boroughs with a population of over 20,000. They did not want the powers. If the Ministry would take its whips off the backs of the local authorities, and leave those authorities alone and allow them to digest the enormous meal of legislation, already given to them, the net result to the public would be very much better. I think that this derelict Measure of the late Socialist Government might be left over for the purpose of having its provisions better explained to the public, and for the purpose of investigating whether there is any demand from any quarter whatever for such a Measure. I hope that the Minister will agree to postponing the further consideration of the Bill.

Sir H. CAUTLEY: I much appreciate what the Minister said, especially about my second and third points. Motion, by leave, withdrawn.

Sir J. WALKER SMITH: I beg to move, in page 1, line 7, after the word "scheme," to insert the words:

"enabling a local authority or a county council or a joint committee constituted for the purposes of this Act to prohibit or restrict the use of land for building purposes."

This Amendment is intended to indicate the real object of the Bill. As, I understand it, the Bill seeks to make an appeal upon the basis that there are no financial difficulties or encumbrances involved in connection with it. According to the Preamble, it is a Bill to "facilitate" certain things. It is described as a Bill

"to provide for the protection of rural amenities and the preservation of buildings and other objects of interest or beauty."

These are most desirable objects, and if they were not placed in the forefront of the Bill, it would get practically no support. These matters are placed in the forefront in order to convey the impression that they represent the main object of the Bill. But this Preamble is merely a smoke-semen behind which all sorts of Col. 16 operations are to take place. If the objects which I have quoted were all that the Bill was to provide for, I do not think that there would be any opposition to it from any quarter. So truly do the people of this country love the amenities of the country, that they would accept almost any Measure which would effectively and efficiently provide for the maintenance of those amenities. But when we proceed further we find that the Bill is also "for charging on owners of property expenditure on improvements." It certainly makes ample provision for those charges. Later, it is indicated that another of its objects is "to facilitate the acquisition of land for garden cities."

Reading that one would not think that there was anything particularly objectionable in the Bill. One might imagine that it was for the purpose of enabling garden cities to obtain land in circumstances in which at present they were unable to do so. That is an entirely wrong impression. Powers already exist for that purpose. The charging on owners of property of expenditure on improvements would be all right, provided that the alterations made were improvements, and provided that the benefits of such improvements accrued before the charges were made. There is no word in the Preamble to suggest that this is really a Bill to give powers of a restrictive and prohibitive nature in respect of the whole of the built-up areas of the country. Yet that is the real and primary intention of the Bill. That is the greatest change which the Bill proposes. In moving this Amendment I submit that that intention should appear early in Clause 1, so that those who read the Bill shall not receive the verbiage of this Clause as a sort of soporific and so that they shall not be led to believe that this is just a nice, friendly, genial Measure for the purpose of facilitating progress. That it is nothing of the kind is revealed by Clause 12 where hon. Members will find the provisions which town planning schemes will contain with respect to buildings and building operations.

These provisions are all of a limiting, restrictive and prohibitive nature. There is no provision anywhere for facilitating or assisting. Everything is controlling and prohibiting. There are prohibitions, restrictions and limitations imposed upon prohibiting and restricting local autho- Image Col. 17 rities by a prohibiting and restricting Ministry of Health. That is the atmosphere which pervades the whole Bill. It seems to be designed for the purpose of preventing anyone getting on with anything unless he goes through a whole lot of worry and trouble and incurs irritating expense and inconvenience. The real intention of the Bill being as I have described it, I think it is just as well that that intention should be placed in the forefront of the Measure so that we may know the sort of proposal with which we are dealing.

Captain WATERHOUSE: This Amendment was put down mainly for the purpose of getting a declaration from the Minister as to whether or not be agrees, first, that local authorities have at present adequate powers to develop on what lines they like, and, secondly, that this whole Bill is therefore merely one to restrict and prohibit. On an Amendment to which I should like to confine myself strictly, according to the Rules of Order, they invite me to make a fresh Second Reading speech. I have given careful consideration to this Amendment to see whether we could find in it any constructive improvement of the Bill. I am afraid we cannot.
