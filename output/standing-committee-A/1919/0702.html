ALLIENS RESTRICTION BILL

SECOND DAY'S PROCEEDINGS.
NEW CLAUSE.
—(Deportation of Former Enemy Aliens.)

(1) Every former enemy alien who is now in the United-Kingdom shall be deported forthwith unless he shall within one month after the passing of this Act make an application to the Secretary of State in the prescribed form to be allowed to remain in the United Kingdom, stating the special grounds on which such application is based, and unless the Secretary of State after due inquiry shall grant him a licence to remain.

(2) The applicant shall advertise notice of his application in some paper circulating in the district in which he resides or in which, if he is interned, he was residing previously to his internment.

(3) The Secretary of State may, not less than one calendar month after the date of such advertisement, if he is satisfied that there is no adverse report on the applicant by the military or by the police authorities, grant such licence, subject to such terms and conditions (if any) as he shall think fit, on any one or more of the following grounds, namely:—

  1. That the applicant, although a former enemy alien, is in fact a member of a nation or a race hostile to the States recently at war with His Majesty and is well disposed to His Majesty and his Government;
  2. That the applicant is seventy years of age or upwards and has been at least fifteen years resident in the United Kingdom;
  3. That the applicant is suffering from serious illness or infirmity of a permanent nature;
  4. That the applicant has one or more sons who voluntarily enlisted and served in the British Navy or Army or the Navy or Army of one of the Allied Powers;
  5. That the applicant has lived for at least thirty-five years in this country and married a British-born wife;
  6. That the applicant, although her husband is a former enemy alien living abroad or deported, was at the time of her mariage a British subject or citizen of an Allied State.

(4) If the application for a licence is made on any ground other than one or more of those above specified, the Secretary of State either shall refuse the application or may, if he is satisfied that owing to the exceptional circumstances of the case deportation would involve serious hardship of a personal nature on the applicant, grant him a licence under this Section.

(5) In granting a licence under this Section, the Secretary of State may include in the licence the wife of the applicant and any child or children of his under the age of eighteen.

(6) A list of the persons to whom such licence is granted together with a statement of the special grounds on which it is granted and of the exceptional circumstances (if any) under which it is granted shall, as soon as may be after the granting of the licence, be published in the "Gazette."

(7) Any licence so granted may be at any time revoked by the Secretary of State.

(8) If such licence is not granted or if, having been granted, it shall be revoked the Secretary of State shall make an Order (in this Act referred to as a Deportation Order) requiring the alien to leave the United Kingdom and thereafter to remain out of the United Kingdom for a period of seven years after the passing of this Act. Sir JOHN BUTCHER
: I beg to move

"That the Clause be read a second time."

Putting it in a few words, the object of the Clause is to make provision that all former enemy aliens should be deported from this country, subject to certain exemptions. I make provision for those exemptions in the Clause, and in doing so I was largely helped by the experience I had when I was a member for some months last year of the Advisory Committee which advised the Home Secretary of that day as to what enemy aliens should be exempted from internment. As I and other members on that Committee found, it was most desirable that we should have specific instructions from Parliament as to the classes of people we should exempt. For that reason it is desirable to embody these general directions for exemption in the Clause. As to the general policy of deporting these former enemy aliens, upon that I imagine that most Members of the House of Commons made statements in the course of the Election, statements which were of a fairly strong character. I should be glad to hear if anyone did not. I desire to insist most strongly upon the fact that in making those statements they were not following a line of policy inaugurated by themselves. They were giving effect in their Election pledges to the definite statements and pledges which had been given already by the Prime Minister and by other prominent members of the Government before the General Election. Probably those pledges are within the recollection of the Committee. Let me recall one or two of them. Speaking at the Queen's Hall before the Election, the Prime Minister said:

"The Germans have abused our hospitality. They spied and they plotted. They have assisted JPEG 31 Germany in the forging of plans for the destruction of the country which has offered them hospitality and, if opportunity had offered them, they would have assisted in the execution of those plans, to the ruin of the land which had given them shelter. They have, therefore, forfeited any claim to remain."

Hon. Members will notice that that applies not merely to the Germans who were interned at the moment of the Armistice, but to all the Germans in this country. In order to emphasise the matter, there was an interruption by someone who wanted to know what the Prime Minister was going to do to get rid of these enemy aliens. His answer was:

"I have repeatedly said that in my judgment these people, having abused our hospitality, must not get another opportunity of doing so."

That is not all. There was a pledge given by the present Lord Chancellor, then Attorney-General, and one of the prominent members of the then Government and of the present Government. He said in a speech before the Election:

"I tell you here as a Minister and a member of the Coalition Government that it is the declared policy of that Government to send back to Germany every Boche in this country."

Could anything be more clear? Could anything be more decisive in its language? If the Home Secretary is, as I trust he is not, going to tell us that he is not going to give effect to this positive declaration of the Prime Minister and of the then Attorney-General, now Lord Chancellor, I venture humbly to suggest that he will have to ask the Prime Minister and the Lord Chancellor to resign or possibly to adopt some other course.

If that be the general policy laid down by the Government, I put it to the Committee, is there any conceivable reason which an ingenious Home Secretary could suggest why these definite pledges should not be embodied in the Bill? This Bill is brought forward for the express purpose of restricting aliens. Is it not the most proper, natural, and, indeed, essential thing that these definite pledges should be embodied in this Bill, which the ordinary man in the street—indeed, the ordinary commonsense Member of Parliament—will see is the only possible way in which these pledges can be redeemed? My right hon. Friend may say, "I prefer to legislate by Order in Council." We are sick of Orders in Council, absolutely dead sick. The country is dead sick of them too. In every possible shape and form Orders in Council 32 have been passed in scores and masses during the War, sometimes without Parliament knowing much about them. I grant that it was necessary during a great war. We had to take no risk. We had to legislate quickly and suddenly in order to provide against the terrible dangers with which we were confronted. It would have been impossible to pass these restrictions in the ordinary way by means of an Act of Parliament. Therefore we were all agreed and the country submitted with great patience to the innumerable restrictions imposed upon it during the War by Orders in Council, for this reason only, that it was necessary for our salvation. The War is over. Peace has been happily declared. If there is one result of that peace which is present to the mind of every man and woman in this country, the first thought, of course, is one of deep thankfulness to the men who fought for us and who won the War, the next thought, perhaps, is that we want to return to normal conditions of freedom and Parliamentary government, under which we shall have no more of these Orders in Council and no more of this; legislation without the express consideration of Parliament.

There is no reason why, in this particular matter, when we are carrying out a pledge of the Prime Minister, we should leave it to the discretion of the Home Secretary, however competent and however much we trust and admire him, and there is no reason why we should not embody it in an Act of Parliament. I grant that the Home Secretary said on the Second Reading of this Bill that there are some things which are of a temporary character which will have to be dealt with during the next year or two, and which will have to be dealt with by Orders in Council. Be it so. I make that admission frankly, but is there any conceivable reason why it should be left to the discretion of the Home Secretary? My right hon. Friend is here to-day. No doubt he is doing his best. I do not quite know what he is doing, but I have no doubt that he is doing his best. If he ceases to hold his office is it to be assumed that the next Home Secretary would not issue new Orders in Council? If this Government fell—of which there does not seem to be much appearance—a new Home Secretary might come into Office who would say, "I am not bound by these pledges. I shall issue Orders in Council allowing all Boches to remain." I do not JPEG 33 say that is an imminent danger, but as a matter of principle that is the reason why those pledges should be embodied in legislation. The Home Secretary, in a passage in his speech on the Second Reading, admitted that his Bill would be required to be amended, and had he not given those pledges I believe the Bill would have been thrown out. Everyone who spoke pointed out the absurdity and the unconstitutional character in a time of peace of legislation by Order in Council. On the 15th April the Home Secretary said this:

"If any hon. Member can suggest to us any particular direction in which our experience is sufficient to justify a provision being made permanent, as we propose to make permanent the provisions dealing with enemy aliens, who come to stir up unrest, it will be considered with the greatest care, and. if found feasible, will he adopted."

You have this extraordinary position that there is to be a permanent provision preventing enemy aliens from stirring up unrest. So far the right hon. Gentleman has not made provision for sending back to their own country and keeping out in future any enemy aliens. The right hon. Gentleman went on to say:

"We have not absolutely excluded from this Bill all measures of a permanent character. We have only retained an existing power to proceed by Order in Council. I am fortunately able to tell the Committee why that was. In 1915, under pressure from Parliament, the Home Secretary of that day began interning enemy aliens in this country. I will not go into the history of that matter, but anyone who was in the last Parliament will remember what a struggle there was to get the Home Secretary of the day stimulated into any kind of action. However, he was stimulated into action and began interning. Then he set up a Committee for the purpose of giving exemption to those whom he thought ought not to be interned. I was not on that Committee, but no one will deny that that Committee was exceedingly lenient. They created exemptions wholesale and left an enormous number of Germans uninterned, in some cases on the most trivial excuses. Why do I say that?

In 1918, again under the pressure of Parliament, the Home Secretary said that he would have a further revision of these uninterned Germans and he set up again this Committee which formerly advised him in 1915 in order to revise the exemptions which they had granted in 1915. I sat on that Committee, which was presided over by Mr. Justice Sankey with extraordinary JPEG 35 tact and great judgment. In the course of our revision of these exemptions granted in 1915, we came across an immense number of cases of Germans who ought to have been interned. We advised their internment and they were interned. Unfortunately, we had not anything like time to complete that revision. The Armistice came and our labours ceased. An immense number of Germans who had been granted exemptions in 1915 remained uninterned. Why? Because we had not been able to reach their cases and revise the whole list. Consequently, in 1918 an enormous number were exempted who, if they had come before our Committee, would, I think, have been locked up. Let me say one word about these uninterned Germans. Many of those who were uninterned and deliberately given exemption were young Germans. The Committee thought it was better to allow a certain number of these men loose in order that they might do work on munitions and other national work rather than keep them in idleness doing nothing. For that reason they were exempted from internment. I want to know from the Home Secretary whether these young Germans have been sent home. I trust that they have, at any rate, I submit that they ought to be. If the Clause I propose is passed, most unquestionably they will go to their homes and we shall be glad to get rid of them.

In that state of things what this Clause proposes is to send back all enemy aliens in this country to their own countries, but we propose to do something more kind to the Germans than was mentioned either by the Prime Minister or by the Lord Chancellor. We propose to grant exemptions in certain proper cases, exemptions which I have formulated as well as I could with those who have helped me in regard to this Clause. I am revealing no secrets of what happened in the Advisory Committee when I say that from time to time cases came before us upon which we should have been only too grateful for the assistance of Parliament in laying down principles upon which we should intern or exempt them. Time after time there were certain differences of opinion in the Committee, as I suppose there are in every Committee, and had it not been for the tact of Mr. Justice Sankey I doubt if we should have held together. The moral of it to my mind was that whenever a Committee of that sort is set up for the purpose of exempting Ger- 36 mans from internment or repatriation, it is vital that Parliament should lay down in general terms the principles upon which exemptions should be granted, and then, as we have done in this Clause, leave a certain discretion in certain cases to the Home Secretary in respect of cases which could not be provided for or seen in advance. I will go through the Clause very shortly. The first Sub-section provides that every former enemy alien now in the United Kingdom shall be deported forthwith unless he makes an application to the Home Secretary stating the grounds upon which he claims exemption. Again, that is the result of my experience upon the Committee. People claimed exemption upon the most frivolous and ludicrous grounds. If Parliament lays down the grounds upon which they may be exempted they will know where they are.

The second Sub-section provides for advertising notice of the application in a local paper. That is exceedingly important, because it enables the persons in the locality, who know the man, to have knowledge that he is applying for an exemption, and if there is anything special to be communicated either to the police or Home Secretary about him, in all probability that will be done. Sub-section (3) lays down the principles upon which exemption may be granted. The first is that of an exemption in favour of friendly aliens, men like the Poles, Czecho-Slovaks, and so on, who are certified by the proper authority to be well-disposed to this country. The second case of exemption is that of a man of seventy years of age and who has been at least fifteen years resident in the United Kingdom. That seems to be reasonable. The third is where an applicant is suffering from serious illness or infirmity of a permanent character. The fourth is one with which we should all feel sympathy, that is where the applicant has one or more sons who voluntarily enlisted and served in His Majesty's Forces or with one of the Allied Powers. The fifth is where an applicant has lived for at least thirty-five years in this country and married a British-born wife. That is inserted partly for the benefit of the Brtishborn wife and partly for the applicant's own benefit. The last exemption is for a woman who has married a former enemy alien who is now abroad, but who was at the time of her marriage a British subject and who wants to remain in this country. She, although technically JPEG 37 an enemy alien, ought to be exempted. This Sub-section provides for the discretion of the Home Secretary in special cases which are not covered by the previous exemption. I grant cases might arise which have not been specially provided for by these provisions, and in these cases he had to consider and be satisfied

"that owing to the exceptional circumstances of the case deportation would involve serious hardship of a personal nature on the applicant."

There is also provision for a list of persons to whom the licence is granted. I recommend this Clause to the Committee as carrying out the pledges of the head of the Government and some of the most responsible members of the Government. I recommend it to the Committee because I think it is a good opportunity of taking the first step in avoiding pernicious legislation by Order in Council and returning to normal peace times. I recommend it because I think in this way, and this way only, will you enable the Committee which the Home Secretary appoints to advise him to do their duty and to carry out the wishes of the country, and instead of being influenced in their decisions, as it is possible they might be, by personal or passing reasons, to be guided in their decisions by principles which have received the sanction of Parliament.
