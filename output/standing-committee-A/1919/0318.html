So far from having any jealousy of the new Ministry of Health, the Board of Education is extremely anxious to co-operate with the work of that Ministry in every way possible, but perhaps it would be to the advantage of the Committee if I were to state very briefly my view as to what the relations ought to be between the school medical service on the one hand and the Ministry of Health on the other. I think it would be very difficult and very inconvenient from the administrative point of view, and also injurious from an educational point of view, if the school medical service were cut out of the educational machinery of the country and transferred en bloc to a new Ministry of Health. That is the first position which I wish to explain to the Committee. On the other hand, I think it would be equally injurious if the school medical service were not under the suzerainty and general directions of the Ministry of Health, and if it were not reinforced by the weight of the authority properly belonging to that Ministry. I think it would be difficult to cut the school medical service out of the educational machinery of the country. Our system of education is, as the Committee knows, administered by 317 local education authorities. They are interested in education. They are interested in that side of education which deals with the physical welfare of the children. They administer the schools. They appoint the teachers. They appoint the doctors. They control and administer the physical training of the schools. They are responsible for the drainage of the schools, for the hygienic condition of the schools, and they receive grants from the Board of Education to cover their whole activities.

I do not know if the Committee has ascertained the views of the 317 local education authorities as to the proposed transfer of their functions, in respect of the health of the school children, to the Ministry of Health, but I conceive that the attitude of those authorities would be hostile to the proposal, which, I understand, was made JPEG 73 by the hon. Baronet the Member for the University of London (Sir P. Magnus), and for this very obvious reason. The local education authorities deal now with one Government Department—the Board of Education. They receive grants from that Department. They frame schemes for the educational development of their respective areas, which are submitted to that Department, and their schools are inspected by the officers of that Department. If the health activities of these schools were to be transferred to the Ministry of Health at the present moment, every local education authority would be subject, in respect of its health administration, to two authorities. It would be exposed to two inspections, and I think the local education authorities would view that step with disfavour. Nor would it be very easy to disentangle those portions of the grant which are made in respect of the heating, lighting and drainage, we will say, of the school, those portions which are due in respect of physical training, and those portions which are due in respect of medical inspection. I think there you would meet with a very considerable administrative difficulty. Then there is this further consideration which I would like to put before the notice of the Committee. At the present moment, under present conditions, our local education authorities are not elected ad hoc; they are bodies of men who have been elected on general municipal issues. Many of them are interested in education in the strict sense of the term. They are interested in the diffusion of knowledge. Others are not particularly interested in the side of educational work, but are very greatly interested in the physical welfare of the children, and you will find in every local education committee a section which is mainly interested in the work of the schools from the physical point of view. I think it would be unfortunate if these two aspects of national education—the physical aspect on the one hand and the strictly educational on the other—were dissevered. I think both aspects of education would suffer in consequence. I think one of the great sources of strength in the present working of our educational system is that we get a conference of these two extremes of interest in every educational committee of this country.

While I think, therefore, it would be inadvisable to cut out the medical side of our educational work from the administrative supervision of the Board of Education— 74 and I think, perhaps, an additional argument is furnished by the tact that the school medical service, although only established in 1907, has been, by the confession of everybody, one of the most successful branches of medical education conducted in this country—while I think that would be a misfortune, at the same time I think it would be an equal misfortune if the administrative work were not carried on under the suzerainty of the Ministry of Health. The position of the Board in urging local education authorities to establish clinics, to improve their system of inspection, or to carry out any of the hundred and one different methods of improving the health of the children, would be enormously strengthened if the Board felt it had behind it the weight of authority of the Ministry of Health, and I feel that with Sir George Newman occupying the dual position of Chief Medical Officer of the Board of Education and also Chief Medical Officer of the Ministry of Health, we have a complete guarantee of the harmonious working of the two systems, and that, in all the circumstances of the case, it would be very much the most practical course to adopt to leave the administration of the school medical service where it is, as an integral part of school life, as an integral part of the functions of the local education committee, and, at the same time, to secure for that system the additional support and sanction of the supervision of the Ministry of Health.
