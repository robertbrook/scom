STANDING COMMITTEE A
Thursday, 27th March, 1919

[Sir ARCHIBALD WILLIAMSON in the Chair.]

MINISTRY OF HEALTH BILL.

FIFTH DAY'S PROCEEDINGS.
NEW CLAUSE.
—(Application to Ireland.)

(1) In Ireland it shall be the duty of the Chief Secretary to take all such steps as may be desirable to secure the effective carrying out and coordination of measures conducive to the health of the people, including measures for the prevention and cure of diseases, the treatment of physical and mental defects, the collection, preparation, and publication of information and statistics relating thereto, and the training of persons for health services.

(2) The provisions of this Act with respect to consultative councils shall apply to Ireland as they apply to England and Wales with the substitution therein of the Lord Lieutenant for His Majesty, the Chief Secretary for the Minister, and the Dublin Gazette for the London Gazette, and with the addition of the following provision:—

For the purpose of giving advice and assistance to the Chief Secretary in connection with his powers under this Act a council shall be established (which shall be called the Irish Public Health Council consisting of the following persons:—

  1. The Vice-President and the two other Commissioners of the Local Government Board for Ireland;
  2. Such one or more of the Irish Insurance Commissioners as may be nominated by the Chief Secretary;
  3. The Registrar-General of Births, Deaths, and Marriages in Ireland; and
  4. Such number of registered medical practitioners, not exceeding three, as may be from time to time nominated by the Chief Secretary, one of whom shall be a woman and one of whom shall be a medical practitioner who is registered on the medical register in respect of a diploma in sanitary science, public health, or State medicine.

(3) The expenses of the Chief Secretary and of the Irish Public Health Council under this Act, including such remuneration of the medical practitioners who are members of that Council as the Treasury may determine, shall be paid in like manner as the expenses of the Ministry.

(4) Save as aforesaid, or as otherwise expressly provided in this Act, the foregoing provisions of this Act shall not apply to Ireland.—[ Mr. Arthur Samuels.

The ATTORNEY - GENERAL for IRELAND (Mr. A. W. Samuels)
brought up, in substitution of the foregoing, the following new Clause:—

Application to Ireland.

(1) For the purpose of promoting the health of the people in Ireland and exercising the powers conferred on him by this Act the Chief Secretary shall be the Minister of Health for Ireland, and it shall be his duty as such Minister to take all such steps as may be desirable to secure the effective carrying out and co-ordination of measures conducive to health, including measures for the prevention and cure of diseases, the treatment of physical and mental defects, the collection, preparation, and publication of information and statistics relating thereto, and the training of persons for health services.

(2) The provisions of this Act with respect to consultative councils shall apply to Ireland with the substitution therein of the Lord Lieutenant for His Majesty and with the addition of the following provision:—

For the purpose of giving advice and assistance and making proposals to the Chief Secretary in connection with his powers and duties under this Act a council shall be established (which shall be called the Irish Public Health Council) consisting of the following persons:—

  1. The Vice-President and the two other Commissioners of the Local Government Board for Ireland;
  2. The chairman and such two others of the Irish Insurance Commissioners as may be nominated by the Chief Secretary;
  3. The Registrar-General of Births, Deaths, and Marriages in Ireland;
  4. A registered medical practitioner, who shall act as chairman of the Council under the direction of the Chief Secretary, and three other registered medical practitioners, one of whom shall be a woman and one of whom shall be a medical practitioner who is registered on the medical register in respect of a diploma in sanitary science, public health, or State medicine;
  5. Six other persons having practical experience of matters relating or incidental to or affecting the health of the people.

(3) The Chief Secretary shall from time to time nominate the persons who are to be members of the Irish Public Health Council under paragraphs ( d) and ( e) of the preceding Subsection.

(4) The expenses of the Chief Secretary and of the Irish Public Health Council under this Act, including such remuneration of the chairman and other members of that Council as the Treasury may determine, shall be paid in like manner as the expenses of the Ministry.

(5) Save as aforesaid, or as otherwise expressly provided in this Act, the foregoing provisions of this Act shall not apply to Ireland.

Mr. SAMUELS

In framing these provisions for setting up a Health Council in Ireland, the Government has been most anxious to meet, as far as possible, the very strong representations which have been made from hon. This is as far as we can go, having regard to existing Irish conditions, with a machine or portion of a machine, which we hope will be effective and real in its working during this transition period. "For the purpose of giving advice and assistance, and making proposals to the Chief Secretary."

"( a) The Vice-President and the two other Commissioners of the Local Government Board for Ireland."

The whole administration, as I pointed out on the first day, is on the public side under the Local Government Board for Ireland, practically in all parts—

"( b) The chairman and such two others of the Irish Insurance Commissioners as may be nominated by the Chief Secretary,"

That will enable a very full representation to be given to the Insurance Commissioners. "( c) The Registrar-General of Births, Deaths, and Marriages in Ireland;"

It is essential, of course, that the Registrar-General should be a member of such council. I think you had a debate here in relation to his position in England, and it was pointed out that he is really a portion of the Local Government Board here. He is not so in Ireland, and accordingly we include him for the purpose of giving statistics relating to health and other matters which are exceedingly well done in Ireland under his direction. The next proposal is

"( d) A registered medical practitioner, who shall act as chairman of the council under the direction of the Chief Secretary, and three other registered medical practitioners, one of whom shall be a woman and one of whom shall be a medical practitioner who is registered on the Medical Register in respect of a diploma in sanitary science, public health, or State medicine;"

This is where we commence the non-official branch of the Council. Under ( a), ( b) and ( c) all the persons taking part in this council will be of an official character. Of course, there will be the Chief Secretary himself, and then there are the Vice-President and the two other Commissioners of the Local Government Board for Ireland. The Vice-President is Sir Henry Robinson, and no other man, I think, has an equal grasp of all matters connected with local legislation in Ireland, and the two other Commissioners, namely, the Medical Commissioners, who, I think all medical men—and I am sure my hon. Friend the Member for Trinity College, who has an intimate knowledge of all JPEG 265 medical matters in Ireland—will agree is a most excellent official, and that is. Dr. Coey Bigger. The other Commissioner is Mr. Burke, who is thoroughly familiar with all local associations. Then there are the Chairman and two others of the Irish Insurance Commissioners and the Registrar-General, making seven members, and with the Chief Secretary giving a representation of eight on the official side. Paragraph ( d) brings us to the non-official side. It is provided that a registered medical practitioner shall act as chairman of the Council, under the direction of the Chief Secretary. It has been thought desirable that we should have some person who is non-official who would be able to devote more attention to the work than the over-burdened officials could, and whose assistance would be called in to help the work of the council. It is better to have someone to devote himself exclusively to it and to be the convener of the council.
