ALLIENS RESTRICTION BILL

THIRD DAY'S PROCEEDINGS.
NEW CLAUSE.
—(Prohibition of Former Enemy Aliens Entering United Kingdom.)

(1) No former enemy alien shall for a period of seven years after the passing of this Act be permitted to land in the United Kingdom either from the sea or from the air, or to remain in the United Kingdom without the licence of the Secretary of State, which shall not be granted except as hereinafter provided.

  1. The Secretary of State may grant a licence to land and to remain in the United Kingdom on such terms and conditions as he shall think fit to—
    1. any former enemy alien if the Secretary of State upon application made to him in the prescribed form is satisfied that the applicant is a member of a nation or race hostile to the States recently at war with His Majesty and is well disposed to His Majesty and his Government;
    2. any British-born wife of a former enemy alien and to her child or children being under the age of nineteen.
  2. If a former enemy alien other than as mentioned in the last Sub-section desires to land in this country for a temporary purpose he shall before so doing make application to the Secretary of State in the prescribed form, and the Secretary of State may, after due inquiry, grant him a licence to land and to remain in the United Kingdom for a period not exceeding one month on such terms and conditions as they may think fit.
  3. If a former enemy alien be accidentally forced to land from an aircraft in the United Kingdom without any such licence as aforesaid he shall within twenty-four hours report himself to the nearest police authority, and shall not be permitted to remain in the United Kingdom.—[ Sir Herbert Nield.]

The effect of the Clause is to prohibit a former enemy alien from entering the United Kingdom for a period of seven years after the passing of the Act. I suppose this is the very first time one has had to provide against persons landing from the air. The Clause proposes that during this period no subject, except those like the Czechs and other nationalities of 72 South-East Europe, shall be allowed to land except under conditions of a licence to be granted by the Secretary of State. That licence should be limited to a temporary purpose only, so that he is required at the expiration of the licence either to quit the Kingdom or to make further application for a renewal of the licence on special grounds. There is a special Subsection which requires anyone landing from aircraft to report himself to the police within twenty-four hours, and, of course, subject to his not obtaining that licence, to leave the Kingdom when directed. It may be said that this is unprecedented so far as this Kingdom is concerned. My answer to that is that the conditions under which the War has been waged, and indeed the conditions under which it was planned, justify us for the present in treating the Germans as a race apart. We are not alone in that, because other countries are actually proposing legislation of a similar character.

I made inquiry as far back as February by question in the House to the Foreign Office, asking for particulars of proposed legislation, if any, which was being put forward in Allied countries, and I have persistently pursued that matter both in correspondence and in questions in the House until the present time. It was not until the day before yesterday that I received an official answer, having a few days before had a letter from the Under-Secretary for Foreign Affairs inviting me to call at the Foreign Office and to inspect the reports which had ultimately been received from these various centres, which were so voluminous that he could not possibly put them into a Parliamentary Paper. As I have not yet had an opportunity, nor do I think it is proper that I should go to the Foreign Office and make inspection and take my own extracts, I prefer to ask the Foreign Office to inform the House what are the provisions that other Allied countries are proposing to adopt. I have never seen any contradiction or any whittling down of that statement. If that be true, then America is determined upon a policy of exclusion for a definite term of years. We were told by the Lord Chancellor, while he was Attorney-General, in one of his speeches, which has been repeated more than once in the House of Lords in his. presence, that there were at the date of his speech, within the compass of the General Election, already 20,000 Germans waiting at Rotterdam to enter England, all of whom had taken care to get a protective Dutch nationality or Swiss nationality, so as to be able to claim to be of neutral nationality. He warned his hearers of that condition of things, and said that the Government would take steps to prevent it. If the Government are pledged, as the Debate here yesterday showed, towards a very wide system—the representatives of the Government here did not take the pro- 74 posals that were then put before them, but they did say in effect they were going to carry out Government policy by Order in Council—and if it is desirable to get rid of some of these people who are in this country—we should like to get rid of the whole of them with a few exceptions—then, a fortiori, we ought to take steps to prevent the immigration of Germans who have not hitherto been here, or who have been here and returned to Germany to fight in the War, and who propose to come back to resume their avocations in this country. That surely is a matter almost beyond argument.

Perhaps the Government do not think that the provisions of the new Clause are altogether what they themselves would wish. I should be glad to hear about that. I think it is sufficient to call attention to the fact that a general policy of restricting alien immigration will not do in this case. We know how the country has been treated by the complete failure of the Aliens Act of 1905, how when it applied to an emigrant ship bringing more than a certain number of people it was subject to the order of the Secretary of State, and it was an order issued by Lord Gladstone, then Home Secretary, which destroyed very largely the beneficial effect of that measure as regards entry into the ports. As my hon. and learned Friend pointed out yesterday, we not only want to keep out the German, the ordinary working-class German, but we want particularly to keep out those people who come over in the saloons of steamships with first-class tickets, who have money at their command. We know that they are able and astute men, and that before the War they came over deliberately for the purposes of State, quite apart from purposes of gain, and wound a coil round British industry and British life which we hope to sever after our experiences during the War.

You cannot shut your eyes to the fact that Germany to-day is an absolutely different place from the Germany of 1870. Up to 1870 Germany contained much that was good, and no doubt contributed very largely to the sciences, to the knowledge and, indeed, to the home life of the world. A curious instance of this, which shows the tremendous change which has taken place in Germany, and it is that change which justifies this Clause, relates to music. Those of us who are familiar with Church music, and those of us who are JPEG 75 members of the Temple in particular, know that if there was one person who loved music it was the famous old organist, Dr. Hopkins. Our most popular hymn tunes were written by Germans. [An HON. MEMBER: "Shame!"] I do not think that. But I think it is important as showing what Germany was before the evil spirit of the Hohenzollerns gripped it as it has done during the last forty years. Germany was a God-fearing nation in those days, and could teach the people of the world a good deal in regard to home life and home rule and the way to fear God. From 1870 onwards there has been a great change. Since 1870 there has been a shocking and deplorable change, and it is a matter for very serious consideration that we should have legislation of this character. In the elementary schools, in the Universities, and in every branch of learning, and in the Lutheran Church of Germany every effort has been made, most systematically and most successfully, to turn the current of people's thoughts entirely, and some people assert that they have actually set up for themselves a new standard of living and an absolutely new-Deity to worship. The result is that you find Germany to-day is an entirely different thing from the old Germany. The united German Empire bringing into line all those that were formally independent States that has caused the mischief to permeate the whole of what is known as the German Empire.

Can you believe that people who have behaved as they have can be trusted? It is not the military caste only. It is impossible to shut one's eyes to the fact that the German population as a whole have applauded the shocking doings of the Army during the War, the deplorable outrages that have taken place in the matters of conducting the War, the way in which the civilian population of occupied territories have been treated, and the policy of bombing industrial centres like our own on the pretence that they were fortified places. If they were fortified places they could have been of no possible avail to Germany for the uses of the War unless they had been arsenals for the manufacture of war materials. They dropped their incendiary and explosive bombs on places 76 which they knew perfectly well had no concern at all with the manufacture of munitions or with any operations of war. They have again and again shown themselves absolutely oblivious of all the rules of civilised warfare, and their civil population have applauded their deeds. Such a thing is unthinkable of the German nation as it formally existed. That kind of spirit was shown by the scuttling of the ships on Sunday week and by the destruction of the colours which they were under treaty to give back to France, which shows that they are as demented as a whole as they were during the early days of the War when they thought they were going to make world conquests. Under these circumstances we are justified in saying that they shall not enter this country for a period of years. I do not know that we are wedded to seven years; but it must be a definite period to mark the sense of those countries who have had to submit to the barbaric methods of the Germans, and let the world see and young Germany see that the methods on which Germany has been brought up are wrong and condemned by the whole civilised world. From the commercial point of view it was positively derogatory to us, and it must have been with feelings of shame that we English had to grant letters of naturalisation in the early days of the War to the full-blooded German — Baron Schroeder, because it was said that unless we did so the whole of our money market would have been paralysed. Why should we ever have allowed such a condition of things? We have allowed the Metal Market and the Chemical Trade, even in our country, to be captured by them. I hope that by pasing this Clause, modified as the Committee may think fit, we may mark the disapprobation of the British people of the methods employed against them.

—(Prohibition of Former Enemy Aliens Holding Land, etc.).
