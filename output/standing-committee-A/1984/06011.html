 When we debated the sittings motion at our first sitting I said that I wished to raise the question of the effect of the Bill on the status and future of BNOC staff. You drew my attention, Mr. Knox, to the fact that I could not take that issue very far while we were discussing the sittings motion and so I was encouraged to find a way to raise the matter and to move an amendment to give some protection to the staff, which I feel is more than justified. Amendment No. 30 aims to give that protection.

It is worth recalling the background and the events that have led up to the present position. At our first sitting I pointed out that Opposition Members felt that if there had not been a reneging, there had been at least a dissipation of what appeared to be a clear and unequivocal assurance that I had received on Second Reading.

I raised the matter on Second Reading for the express purpose of trying to safeguard the position of some members of BNOC staff who would not wish to transfer to the agency, and the manner in which I did that is recorded in the Official Report at the foot of column 201 and the top of column 202. Had I thought that there would be an attempt to wriggle out of commitments made by the Parliamentary Under-Secretary when he replied, I should have worded my request differently.

I anticipated the problem that might arise when some members of staff, particularly those employed in the land pipelines division of BNOC, were forced to transfer to the agency and would not be offered redundancy terms or conditions of service that were satisfactory to them.

One cannot easily intervene in a winding up speech to obtain clarification, but in any case I felt that the Under-Secretary gave a reassuring response. He said that the Government had

"neither means nor intention of forcing BNOC staff to transfer."—[ Official Report, 14 May 1985; Vol. 79, c. 253.]

 102 He said that staff would be offered jobs, but there would be no intention of forcing them to transfer.

Given the context in Which the matter had been raised and because the Minister understood the worries of staff, I took comfort from that assurance, as did the staff of BNOC. So it was a surprise to hear that within a day or so of Second Reading some BNOC staff were told that the Bill provided for their transfer to the new agency and that their contracts of employment would be transferred. Certain members of staff were told that they would have to go or resign, without the right to the severance or redundancy terms that were being offered to other members of staff.

Provision for the transfer of contracts of employment is made in paragraph 2 (a) of schedule 2, which states:

"the effect of section 3 of this Act in relation to any contract of employment with the Corporation in force immediately before the transfer date is merely to modify the contract (as from that date) by substituting the Agency as the employer (and not to terminate the contract or vary it in any other way)".

 When I first read that, I assumed it was meant to ensure that people would not have worse terms and conditions of employment, and would not be disadvantaged. However, they are being disadvantaged by the insistence that certain members of staff must either go across or resign, as the Minister put the choice.

On Second Reading, the Minister said:

"Schedule 2(2) makes provision for the contracts of employment of BNOC staff to be transferred in their entirety to the agency, so that the employees' terms and conditions are not adversely affected by the transfer."— [ Official Report, 14 May 1985; Vol. 79, c.195.]

 The transfer of staff from the corporation to the agency, unwillingly or reluctantly, with reduced career opportunities:and worries and concerns about the long-term future of, the agency will have an adverse effect on staff if they are compelled to transfer without being offered an alternative of severence or redundancy or enhanced arrangements for transferring to the agency. That is what amendment No. 30 seeks, to enshrine in the Bill.

In our debate on, the sittings motion I referred to the context, environment, and background of the problems that the staff have faced, and I should like to deal with that in more detail now. Uncertanty and worries, about, the future of those who have been working in the land pipelines division for the past 12 or 18 months did not start when the Minister announced the abolition of the corporation. The staff had lived and 'worked under an increasingly dark cloud of uncertainty for at least 18 months before the announcement of abolition.

During our discussion on the sittings motion, the Minister revealed—after prompting from Opposition Members—that in the two years or so before the announcement of the. abolition of BNOC, the Government had been reviewing the activities, management structure and the whole land pipelines and storage installation system that is to be transferred to the agency.
