Mr. Cowans
: The amendment arises from the debate on Second Reading. We are worried that, without agreement or consultation, new towns will be able to take decisions which affect their local authorities. The hon. Member for Mid-Worcestershire (Mr. Forth) will recall that on Second Reading I talked about company towns. My point was valid because new towns have sometimes sold off large portions of land which subsequently have been developed without due regard to the surrounding area.

For instance, in my area we have bitter memories of a town with, in effect, one employer. I refer, of course, to Jarrow. Anyone who reads " The Rape of Jarrow " will understand what I mean. One large shipbuilding concern provided almost all the employment for the town. I admit freely that there are good and bad employers, but that employer had a virtual veto over all that happened in the town. He threatened that if he did not get planning permission for his projects he would close the shipyard so that the town faded. Inevitably, he got his way, irrespective of whether it was good or bad for the town, because the consequences of opposing him were too frightening. The amendment seeks to avoid such situations.

We are asking only that local authorities are consulted and taken into account. We must not forget that the Bill deals with the winding up of new towns. Many of us are not happy about that, but we must live in the real world. One has only to count the number of Conservative Members in this room to realise that it is obvious that the Bill will be enacted.

The amendment is concerned primarily with what will happen afterwards. It is no good winding up new towns and allowing the development of situations such as that which faced Jarrow. The problem could be solved by amending the clause in the way that we suggest so that local authorities were consulted at the time when they could do some good.

I do not think that the Minister can say anything against our intentions. We are trying to be construestructive in this matter so that local authorities are fully consulted. We want all the issues to be taken into account when, ultimately, much against our will, property or land is sold. At least the local authority will have had an input, will have made its case, for or against, and will have drawn attention to any possible consequences.

That goes to the heart of the matter. In the clause, the Government themselves say that the people, the area and their well-being should be considered. The amendment clarifies that. We are writing specifically into the Bill an aim which the Government leave rather vague, to put it kindly. We believe firmly that " local " means local. Local authorities are elected by local people, and it is right and proper that 6 they should be brought in at the early stages on matters that will affect their area.

I hope that what I say about the present practice of the Commission for the New Towns and of the new town development corporation will reassure the hon. Gentlemen. Of course, on major issues the Commission will have to find out, and give full weight to, the views of the local authority. The district council will be the authority to which the commission will have to look for planning permission for the development of any of its land. On many other aspects, there will be—and certainly should be—a close inter-relation between the activities of the commission and those of the local authority.

The commission already works closely with local authorities in towns where it has taken over the assets of the development corporation. At Corby, for example, the joint industrial development committee has played a significant role in attracting jobs to the town, as the hon. Member for Tyne Bridge said earlier. A programme has been agreed at Corby between the Commission and the district council, and funded jointly. This has ensured the two bodies working closely together for the good of the town.

The commission intends also to establish local liaison committees in further towns where it takes over assets. The chairmen of these liaison committees will be members of the commission. The district councils will choose their own representatives. Representatives of the county council and the local business community can be invited to attend as appropriate.

The commission has shown the importance that it attaches to these local liaison committees by initiating them while it is still planning its own arrangements. The first meeting of the local liaison committee at Skelmersdale will take place tomorrow week.

The Bill lays on the commission the duty to have regard to the welfare and convenience of those living, working and carrying on business in the towns where it has responsibilities. The basic step in 8 carrying out that duty is to decide what will conduce to the welfare of those who live and work there. The views of their elected representatives should play a full part in that process of consultation.

With that explanation I hope that the Opposition will feel that they need not press the amendment to a Division.
