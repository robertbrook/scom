A much more important part of our amendments relates to the costs, losses and debts that were incurred by BNOC in 1984—the result not of BNOC's decisions or judgments, but of the judgments or misjudgments of Ministers. The burden of our case, and the essence of amendment No. 60, is that the financial problems and losses incurred in 1984 by the BNOC should not be reflected, transferred to or visited upon the new agency.

The sins of the Government and Ministers which led to the consequential financial losses for the BNOC in 1984-85 should not be visited on the agency. The purpose of amendment No. 60 and the JPEG 299 thrust of our argument about the transfer of liabilities from the corporation to the new agency is that neither the corporation nor the agency can be expected to pick up the tab for any losses resulting from ministerial judgments and decisions.

Let me outline our reasons for making that case. The downfall of BNOC began last year and is well described in BNOC's 1984 annual report. BNOC attempted to prop up the price in the summer and autumn of last year and again at the beginning of this year. The losses created by that action were a result not of BNOC's judgments but of ministerial misjudgments. That is why we believe that our amendment is justified.

The accounts on page 10 of the annual report reflect not BNOC's performance, but the ministerial performance. Therefore, the liabilities and assets that can be identified as the Minister's responsibilities, should not be transferred from BNOC to the new agency.

I think that we would all agree that Lord Cobham has always been a man of great integrity, both in public service and as chairman of BNOC. On page 3 of the annual report, he states:

"the profits which had been earned by the hard work of the staff"

 were lost as a result of the decision to pursue a different line of financing. He goes on to describe how the losses were incurred as a result of the corporation taking into account the United Kingdom national interest.

The same point is made forcefully by the chief executive, Mr. Goskirk, in his statement. He explains how, as a result of the Government's decisions in consultation with BNOC, the corporation did not move the price—although its commercial judgment would have been otherwise—and therefore the losses were incurred. Let me draw the Committee's attention to Mr. Goskirk's comment on page 8 of the annual report:

"The Corporation considered that its own commercial interests could best be protected by a reduction in term prices; however the Corporation is also obliged to take into account the wider national interest of which it cannot be the arbiter, and accordingly after consultation with Her Majesty's Government and in full recognition of the losses which would inevitably result, it was decided to continue the then-existing contract prices. Many of the Corporation's customers thereatfer chose to terminate or reduce their contract purchases and by the end of the third quarter roughly half of the Corporation's freely tradeable crude oil was being sold on the spot market. Substantial losses resulted."

 That paragraph clearly states that it was not BNOC's commercial judgment but the Government's judgment, or misjudgment, that created the losses. They had a tremendous effect on the balance sheet and profit and loss account of BNOC, but we feel that none of the Government's sins should be visited on the new agency.

We do not yet have Hansard for the last part of Tuesday's sitting. But during our debate on the amendment of my hon. Friend the Member for Rother Valley;(Mr. Barron) Which dealt with the 300 issue of national interest, the Minister accepted full responsibility. He accepted that the Secretary of State had the duty to decide what the national interest was. Apparently, last year and at the beginning of this year, BNOC was asked to lose money in the national interest and thus incurred the losses described in the report.

The amount lost as a result of the direct decisions of Government not to allow BNOC to shift its price was about £36 million. Secondly, as the annual report explains, provision has also been included in the accountsthe profit and loss accounts and the balance sheets at the end of the year—to JPEG 301 take account of the losses incurred in crude oil trading in the first quarter of 1985.

Again as a direct result of Government intervention, there was a loss of $120 on every barrel that BNOC traded. That is not because BNOC wanted it. Mr. Goskirk explains that BNOC's commercial judgment was to move the price and therefore not incur the losses. Had it done so, it would not have incurred the loss of $1.20 a barrel. That loss is entirely attributable to the Government and to the Ministers. They must pick up the tab, the gross cost of which is listed on page 10 as being £49.8 million in 'the first quarter. Although the Government presented Supplementary Estimates to the House—they were the subject of the Select Committee's report—they have not accepted that they should pick up the whole tab.

Secondly, despite the £40.9 million lost in the first quarter of 1985—again at the behest of the Government—BNOC received only £38.1 million grant, about £2 million short. Why did the Government not accept the total loss, the result of their misjudgment and interference in oil pricing policy in the summer and autumn of 1984 and in the first quarter of 1985? The gross cost of Government interference over that period was £85.2 million; the Minister must accept that as his responsibility. Only £66.5 million of that total was covered by grant.

 because the price of oil was kept up.
