The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State (Mr. John Butcher)
: It may be for the convenience of the Committee if I intervene at this stage to clear up one or two of the matters raised by the hon. Member for Coventry, North-West (Mr. Robinson).

As agreed, we have made available the details of the Shipbuilding Redundancy Payments Scheme. They are tabled and are available to hon. Members if they wish to rehearse them and to reassure themselves of the workings of the scheme, which has changed not one iota from the way that it has operated in the past.

First, let me reassure the hon. Member for Coventry, North-West that the scheme is not being ended as a result of the Government's proposal. It is being extended by 18 months. Labour Members have misunderstood the purpose of clause 1(3) if they believe that it precludes further extensions of the redundancy payments scheme. It is part of the workings and the constitutional rights of the House that no legislation can bind subsequent legislators. Such a provision, if included in the Bill, would be entirely ineffective. However, the Government have said clearly that they do not wish to extend the scheme after December 1986.

Let me take up the hon. Gentleman's assertions. The two previous extensions of the scheme, in 1981 and 1983, both required primary legislation to amend the 1978 Act. We used the Industry Act in 1981 and the Shipbuilding Act in 1982. But the primary legislation in 1981 and 1982 also required that the extensions be made by a statutory instrument, a cumbersome procedure requiring a second debate on an affirmative resolution in the House. The Government therefore decided, for the convenience of the House, that the latest extension should be made directly by the primary legislation in the Bill. That is achieved by clause 1(2).

There is, accordingly, no need for any reference to a statutory instrument, and the references to orders in section 2(4) of the 1978 Act are now irrelevant. They are therefore repealed by clause 1(3) of the Bill, which is no more than a tidying-up measure. Its deletion would have no effect on the operation of the Bill, and would certainly not cause the Government to introduce legislation in 1986 to extend the schemes. The purpose of our methodology is to extend the scheme through a one-step rather than a two-step approach.

If Labour Members still suspect that there is more in that approach than meets the eye, they will no doubt recall that it was used in the past primarily because we were not only discussing an extension. In the orders themselves, the structure of the schemes was being changed, enhanced or improved. Today we are proposing that the schemes should ride forward with exactly the same structure and benefits, and according to exactly the same calculations, as before.

Let me now reassure the hon. Member for Jarrow (Mr. Dixon), who made an important point in our last sitting about the cranking-up of the schemes in line with inflation or by whatever statutory method might 40 be required. The scheme is linked with the statutory scheme which works on a maximum-wage basis. If that maximum increases by a factor of one, this scheme will increase its maximum calculation by a factor of one and a half, as has always been the case. That, of course, adds to the generosity of the scheme. It is also linked with the level of wages in the shipbuilding industry, on which calculation of lump sums and weekly payments will of course depend.

I hope that Labour Members are now assured that the method we have chosen is simpler, that it fulfils our purpose and that it makes life far more convenient not just for hon. Members but, most important of all, for the management and work force of British Shipbuilders.
