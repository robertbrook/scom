"a democratically elected local authority."

Mr. Harry Cowans
(Tyne Bridge): When we were so rudely interrupted by the clock on Tuesday, the Minister of State, Welsh Office, had just said that many local authorities might not welcome the idea of responsibilties being vested in them, as suggested in the amendment. That is a valid point, but as I pointed out, it is significant that they were not consulted. Moreover, if local authorities do not wish to be consulted, as our amendment proposes, there is no need to do so as the proposed new paragraph 7(1)(b) states that the Minister may


"vest in himself … or any accountable public authority".

 The amendment seeks to define clearly that " accountable public authority ".

Even if some local authorities do not want responsibilties vested in them, as the Minister of State fears, under our amendment the Minister could discuss it with them and then vest them in himself. So. while the Minister's point is valid, there is no substance in it because our amendment says that the accountable public authority should be democratically and locally elected. To the Opposition, that is more important that passing on that vested interest to a body that the Minister controls.

If the Secretary of State wishes to vest in himself—we do not agree with that—surely it is not necessary to have another provision to enable him to pass on the vested interest that he controls. What is the difference? He is virtually vesting in himself in any case. The Minister did not address himself to that point. New paragraph 7(1)(b) states that the Secretary of State may


"vest in himself, any other Minister of the Crown or any accountable public authority ".

 Yet new paragraph 7(7) defines " accountable public authority " as 38

"any statutory corporation a majority of the members of which are appointed by a Minister of the Crown."

 If the Secretary of State has the power to vest in himself, why does he need an extra power to vest in a corporation that he controls? That is fundamentally wrong and it should not be included in the Bill. If anything is to be vested in any body, that body should be the local authority. If, when the Minister discusses the matter with it, it does not want those responsibilities, he then has the power to vest them in himself.

New paragraph 7(7) seems to be a nice get-out for the Minister because if it looks a little dodgy—I put it no stronger than that—to vest in himself, he has a nice little loophole that enables him to pass on the matter to a corporation, which appears to be outside his control but is really controlled by him.

If that is not a loophole, I do not not know what is. If something looks as though it will catch the public eye or someone may become upset about it and the Minister does not want to be in the front line because he may be shot at, he has a nice little loophole so that he can pass on the criticism and say, " It's not me, it's that public authority over there." Only on reading the small print does one realise that the Minister has passed the power to himself, but without getting any of the flack. That is what we believe is the implication of this part of the Bill, and it is one reason why we shall push the amendment to a vote.

I shall repeat again and again and again that the more power we give to Ministers without any redress or opportunity to question them and to put them in the front line, the more powers will pass away from Members of Parliament. This measure is another example of that danger. The Bill and many others—the Minister is not alone—that are passing through the House give powers to Ministers. One wonders if the day will arrive when we shall not need Committees. We shall have a one-line Bill that says, " The Minister shall do anything he wants." We shall pass that clause and go home, full stop. There will be no democratic decisions.

By carrying the amendments, we shall put reins on the Minister—I put it no stronger—so that every time he acts he must justify what he does. That is good for the Bill, for democracy and for Members of Parliament.

The alteration now proposed means that the Secretary of State will no longer have to be provided with those basic staffing facts and, therefore, no longer will he have to satisfy himself of the adequacy of staffing arrangements for the future management of the housing assets. The buck will be passed to the local joint consultative committees and the staff commissions. Effectively, the Secretary of State will not be accountable to Parliament for staffing arrangements.
