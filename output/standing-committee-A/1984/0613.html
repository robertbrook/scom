It is interesting to know why the pipeline system was built and why it is where it is. The hon. Member for Enfield, Southgate mused on that subject, but said little. Its origin lies in strategy—strategic reserves—and national security. Mr Adams said that the system began in the nick of time before the second world war, when Whitehall planners and policy makers became increasingly worried and frightened about the prospect of a war with no strategic reserve of petroleum and oil products, particularly for fighter aircraft such as the Spitfire. It was a very close shave. In 1936, they began to build up the reserve, in a Munich-style, appeasement attitude, but it was not until quite late in the day that people recognised the need for a strategic reserve system.

In 1936, before the system was built, the military planners found that they had only 8,000 tons of fuel and fuel spirit—equivalent to what they thought was 10 days' war consumption. This is very relevant to what the hon. Member for Southgate asked about on Tuesday. In fact, it turned out to be only one day's supply of fuel and petrol products to oil the military machine.

Quite late in the day, the Whitehall planners decided that they needed a strategic storage system. In 1936, they began to build up a reserve. They calculated that they needed a reserve facility of 90,000 tons. The Air Ministry decided in August Image Col. 163 1936 that it needed at least 290,000 tons of strategic reserves and it started building storage tanks at aviation distribution depots—AFDDs. There were no sittings motions in those days—everyone got on with the job, as the Opposition are trying to do now.

The Whitehall planners then realised that storage tanks would not be much use in war time because they could be knocked out by aircraft. Therefore, they built the most remarkable pipeline system, which I have briefly described to the Committee. It was a buried or semi-buried system, which connected the storage tanks and provided a means of distribution for the petrol.

As a matter of need and policy, the fuel system had to be outside major urban areas in order to protect it. It was not to serve obvious points of commercial development. It was built to a deliberate strategic plan in the late 1930s and early 1940s. The first line went from Avonmouth, Bristol through Aldermaston to Walton-on-Thames, as far away from the risk of immediate attack as possible. It needed to serve the major concentration of airfields in the south east, but it was nevertheless away from the south east.

As we were importing oil, the planners then realised that they could not have a system from only one port and they started the next major system in Stanlow, Cheshire. I have lived next door to the system in Eastham, which has had an oil dock for some time. I played for a good cricket team there. That link went to the Humber. It provided an alternative source of supply and was again away from the risk of immediate air attack.

The system was built up in a remarkable way. It went south from Stanlow through Misterton and Sawtry. Two major east-west links were then built.
