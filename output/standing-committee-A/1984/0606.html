: I approach the amendment without optimism, and with no spirit of ebullience after the sad and crushing response to the last amendment. The Minister appeared to be in no way co-operative or helpful, so I cannot hope that this amendment will attract his support.

However, we are grateful to the Minister for providing the information about the length of tenure of office. Will he confirm in his reply that the Department of Energy has had to consult the Treasury to be given permission to offer a term of office of three years?

The amendment is desirable because the Treasury seems to have excessive and increasingly pervasive influence. If hon. Members on both sides of the Committee will seriously consider that point, they may arrive at the conclusion that I wish to put to the Committee: the influence of the Treasury is not invariably helpful, that its hand is sometimes clammy and perverse and other Departments ought to stand up for themselves more robustly.

Two Departments seem to have excessive influence under this Government. The Treasury is one. It is dominating and almost corrosive in its effect. The other is the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food, which apparently now has the right to appoint minders to serve on Committees whenever the House is considering legislation that has the slightest connection with agriculture. Those minders ensure that nothing is done or approved which could have a deleterious effect on the interests of that Department.

The Treasury's approach is more subtle. It makes sure that every possible reference is made to it in legislation. It ensures that all decisions that Committees try to make are qualified by the inclusion of words that allow the Treasury to intervene.

That is not good, either for the Treasury or for the Government. If Ministers feel that every little step that they may wish to take must be subject to the Treasury's approval, the point of absurdity will have been reached.

The Government have frequently said that they wish to reduce the size of the Civil Service. Their policy has brought despair, blighted careers, created fury and caused an increase in incompetence and difficulty. However, if the Government are to be consistent in their policy of reducing the size of the Civil Service, I suggest that the other Departments of state should enjoy a little more trust JPEG 71 and independence. If there were less toing and froing between the Treasury and Departments of state seeking permission for every tiny step, fewer civil servants could be appointed without harming the quality of the service. It is unacceptable that in Rotherham, where there has been an enormous increase in the number of unemployed, there has been no increase in the number of people employed by the Department of Employment to care for them. Indeed, the number of such employees has, in effect, been savagely cut. Yet there has been no reduction —as far as I can see—in the number of civil servants apponted to serve in the various Departments of state, whose job is to ensure that they are acting in a spirit of absolute docility in the face of the Treasury's imperialism —a strong word, perhaps, but possibly justified.

For example, the job that we are discussing is not a huge or vital one. If it were, three-year, part-time appointments would not be acceptable. Even Conservative Members might question the part-time nature of the appointments. However, if the appointments are to be part-time and for only three years, surely it is not necessary for every appointment and every change—perhaps even every change in emolument — to be subjected to the searching scrutiny of the Treasury.

Obviously, the Secretary of State and the Minister of State are experienced Ministers. They are not raw; they are not fools; they have some judgment; their colleagues, I imagine, trust their integrity and propriety.
