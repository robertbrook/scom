I was tempted to table amendments to the first line of the clause, especially after hearing the remarks of the hon. Member for Bedfordshire, North (Mr. Skeet). Clause 4(1) says:

"It shall be the duty of the Agency to dispose of or discharge, on the best terms reasonably obtainable, all of the property, rights and liabilities".

If clause 4 is passed, I want to ensure that no pressure Col. 330 —legislative, parliamentary or otherwise—will be placed on companies to give up their contractual rights in the purchase and trading of their oil with BNOC. The contracts were negotiated openly and freely and BNOC was given no statutory rights enabling it to purchase all the oil of those companies on a commercial basis or the whole of their participation oil for the life of the field without serving six or 12-month election notices.

The contracts were genuinely negotiated arrangements between BNOC and many smaller companies, and they suited both BNOC and the companies. There should be no compulsion—parliamentary, legislative or otherwise—or pressure on those contractual arrangements if the companies concerned do not want to unravel or terminate them. The Minister said that they would all have to be negotiated, and that clause 4 does not override people's rights. I am sure that he will confirm today that clause 4(1) does not override the rights of companies that have contractual relationships with BNOC.

Obviously, the agency will be able to see how far it can unravel the existing contracts that it inherits, but that is as far as it can go in negotiating the conclusion of those contracts. There should be no message from the Committee that clause 4(1) provides any legislative pressure for the contracts to be unscrambled.

It is worth repeating the case that we made on Thursday, and I hope that the Minister will assure us that there will be no attempt to browbeat or twist the arms of any smaller companies. If companies want to maintain those relationships, they should sit tight. That should be our recommendation. There is no reason why they should be forced to race off into the clutches of bigger oil companies or try to sell their oil in any other way. I hope that the Minister will confirm that they can sit tight and hold on to what they have, if it suits them, and that there is no reason why they should unscramble arrangements that were freely and genuinely entered into by BNOC, for mutual interest, because the Government have changed their mind and have decided to abolish BNOC and create this new agency.

I hope the Minister will confirm that clause 4(1) does nothing more than tell the agency to go ahead if it is possible, but that unravelling those contracts will not be a test of the agency's virility.

Secondly, I draw the Committee's attention to the structure of subsection (1) which states that the agency will

"dispose of or discharge … the property, rights and liabilities vested in it by virtue of section 3"—

—that is the transfer from BNOC—

"except such as may be determined by the Secretary of State to be property, rights and liabilities which, having regard to the matters mentioned in subsection (2) below, should continue to be vested in the Agency".

A common theme of the Minister's speeches throughout our debates has been that the agency should be allowed to get on with the practical issues. Why should Ministers and the Secretary of State be involved in staffing, preparing and organising the agency's accounts, and so on? The basic thrust of the Minister's argument has been that, once the agency is set up, it should get on with the job of establishing itself and arranging its affairs and business in the most efficient manner possible. Yet under subsection (2), the agency cannot tell the Minister what it needs to fulfil its functions; the Image Col. 331 Secretary of State is to determine what assets the agency shall have to operate the powers and provisions in clause 2.

Why should not the agency itself say which assets, properties, rights and contracts it wants as the basis of an operation to fulfil the obligations placed upon it under clause 2? Subsection (1) provides that the Secretary of State will determine which property, rights and so on the agency will retain and which it will dispose of to fulfil its functions under clause 2. Surely the best body to decide that will be the agency. Time and again, the Minister's theme has been that the agency and not Minister will know how best to deal with those practical issues. Why should not the agency decide what it must retain and dispose of to fulfil its functions under clause 2.

How will Parliament know what the Secretary of State has decided to leave with the agency and what it must dispose of? There is no such provision in the Bill. How will those of us who have been involved in the detailed discussion of the Bill and the House as a whole learn what the Secretary of State has decided to leave the agency to hold and what is unnecessary for the discharge of its duties under clause 2? The assets may be considerable. In what parliamentary form will we know how they will be divested or retained by the agency?

Our consistent view has been that the agency should not be as minimalist as the Minister argues. On the contrary, there is scope for flexibility to allow it to trade successfully in managing the royalty oil under clause 2(1)(d) and to exercise other roles. Clause 2(1)(d) says that the agency shall have power

"to buy, sell or otherwise deal in petroleum on its own account".

Subsection (2) says that the agency shall retain certain assets on the Secretary of State's direction so that it can fulfil its functions under the motor clause of the Bill—clause 2.

We debated clause 2(1)(d) at some length. We believe that there is scope for the agency to buy, sell or otherwise deal in petrol on its own account. What functions in addition to the management of royalty oil will the agency have? What properties, rights and so on will it retain to fulfil its functions under clause 2(1)(d), which clause 4(2) says will determine which assets and rights the agency inherits from BNOC?

There is no case for excessive divesting of assets inherited by the agency from BNOC. That could lead to the stifling and minimising of the agency's functions under clause 2. The Minister steered that clause through unscathed despite attempts by Conservative Members to remove some aspects of it. We supported him and argued for the more expansionary role laid down in clause 2.

The hon. Member for Bedfordshire, North was right to say that clause 2 provided a fair degree of scope for the agency to fulfil its functions. It is important that the properties and rights retained by the agency under clause 4(1) and (2) are adequate to enable it to fulfil its functions under clause 2(1)(a) and (d).
