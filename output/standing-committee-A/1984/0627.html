Oil and Pipelines Bill

New Clause 2
DUTY OF AGENCY TO PREPARE DEVELOPMENT PLAN (No. 2)

"(1) In respect of such activities as the Agency shall carry out on behalf of the Crown relating to pipelines and storage installations the Agency shall prepare within six months from the commencement date a five year plan for the development of investment in and expansion of the system.

  1. Such a plan shall include proposals to establish joint ventures with a company or companies, to establish financial and performance targets, and to move from a cost plus reimbursement basis to a profit sharing basis providing better incentives to the Agency.
  2. The five year plan shall be submitted for approval to the Secretary of State for Defence and the Secretary of State for Energy and shall be laid before Parliament.
  3. The Agency shall enter a contractual arrangement with the Secretary of State for Energy, in respect of the Agency's activities for at least the period of the plan". [Mr. Hardy.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

(Wentworth)

I take considerable pleasure in moving the new clause because it has enormous met it, which even hon. Members who have not displayed much interest in the Bill will recognise. I am confident that even the Minister will see its merit.

All hon. Members are aware of the problems and deficiencies of the British public transport system. We have all been irritated by road blocks and delayed because of congestion on our busy roads. All hon. Members—save those who, on principle, will not travel on railways because they are publicly owned—have been delayed at some time on the railways. Conservative Members who have travelled on continental railways such as those in France, will have experienced the smooth, gliding speed of trains there.

Let us compare the French trains with our railway system, in which there has not been enough investment, either in engines and carriages or in the permanent way. Ten or 15 years ago it was possible for me to write a letter by hand in a train between Doncaster and London; today I find it difficult even to do a crossword. That illustrates the problem.

364 There are also inadequacies in our investment in inland water transport. There is one new canal in Britain. The hon. Member for Reading, West (Mr. Durant)—I shall not, of course, seek to persuade him to intervene—with his interest in inland waterways, is well aware of the relevance of what I am saying.

Because of inadequate investment in British transport, we may well be in the same position at a time of emergency as we were when the pipelines system was established. The overriding reason for establishing the pipelines was that the Government of the day and their experienced advisers realised that at times of strategic emergency it was essential to have a pipelines system so that the public transport system should not be under extreme pressure.

The public transport system is often blocked. There has not been enough investment. The principle of public squalor and private affluence has been applied to transport. In fact, private affluence leads to the congestion which makes an expansion of the pipelines system desirable.

My hon. Friend the Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney, who has demonstrated in Committee his considerable wisdom and his enormous knowledge of these matters, has made clear in the new clause our belief that if the British economy is to prosper it must have a mixed character. There is scope for both private and public enterprise. That belief is contained in subsection (2) of the new clause. If Conservative Members have any intelligence, they will be convinced by the merit of subsection (1), which deals with the development of the pipelines, and they will see that subsection (2) refers to:

"proposals to establish joint ventures with a company or companies, to establish financial and performance targets, and to move from a cost plus reimbursement basis to a profit sharing basis providing better incentives to the Agency".

The Government want to increase the revenue accruing to the Treasury to assist tax cutting. That is a vain exercise in the poor areas of Britain. If they seriously wish to reduce taxation, they must seek to expand the Government's income, and here is a useful way of doing that. It would lead to investment, an improvement in our transport strategy and infrastructure, and it would provide jobs. Indeed, as a representative of an area that employes many steel workers, I have to say that an expansion of pipelines would not only make sense in transport terms, but it would be extremely valuable in providing substantial orders for the steel industry, which remains at below capacity operation because of the appalling condition of the British economy and British industry.

The sheer, clear, overriding merit of the new clause should more than counterbalance any discipline that the Government Whips might seek to impose in preventing the national interest being served.

So that there may be a steady and sustained development, my hon. Friend the Member for Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney has proposed in subsection (3) that a period of five years should be laid down for the expansion, and subsection (4) provides that the agency should enter into a contractual arrangement with the Secretary of State. We do not want it hived off to private ventures which may or may not—probably may—be under the control of foreign companies. Those foreign companies may be more perceptive of value and merit than certain JPEG 365 private sector operations in the United Kingdom, which, like the Government, are entirely obsessed with rooking the public interest to promote the private affluence to which I have referred.

The suggestion for a mixed economy and joint ventures emphasises the different approach that needs to be adopted in the management of our flagging and failing economy. Few Conservative Members would deny the need for the creation of jobs. I hope that all Conservative Members agree that if the new clause were accepted—I hope that the Minister will not have the audacity to confound our expectations—the investment that would accrue from the five-year development that we commend would be on the basis of British ordering. Some companies in the private sector show patriotism and try to purchase their material, plant and equipment from British sources—certainly, most public organisations do. Until Mr. MacGregor became chairman—he may not have the same attachments—the National Coal Board devoted 95 per cent of its investments to British equipment, machinery and raw materials. That is a useful basis and market for private enterprise. New clause 2 provides a real opportunity for private enterprise.

The last paragraph of the BNOC annual report for 1983 contains an interesting and relevant reference, It clearly demonstrates that at the time of the report, the Secretary of State had adopted an entirely approving approach to the matter. It seems that no Conservative Members, except perhaps the Minister, have had the foresight to bring that valuable document into Committee. As they have shown remarkably little interest in the Bill, they may not even have read it in preparation for their service in Committee. I feel, therefore, that it would be appropriate for me to read the relevant paragraph of the report:

"Commercial use has increased during the year by 3 per cent over 1982".—

few industrial activities in Britain showed a 3 per cent increase in 1982 or 1983—

"and total pipeline movements are now well in excess of 3 million tonnes per annum; the use of storage has also increased with plans for a further increase in 1984. During the year, a contract was concluded which will lead to the connection of Mobil's Coryton refinery to the Government's pipeline system, which will then transport products to a new commercial terminal to be built at Wymondham in Norfolk. This will result in a significant additional use of the system from 1985. The Corporation has entered into an agreement with the United States Air Force to act as its agent in connection with the hire of commercial storage facilities and associated services in the UK."

We are not complaining about that. If we examine the American system—for example, the NASA position—we see ample evidence of partnership between public and private sector interests, which is precisely what we are seeking to achieve. As the Government are frequently disposed to commend American achievements and approaches in both political and economic activity, I should have thought that they would be interested in at least a degree of emulation of such practices. Such emulation could easily be achieved by accepting the new clause.

Perhaps the Minister will tell us about events since the link with the Mobil refinery at Coryton. I should have thought that the growth and buoyancy that we have seen in this activity would have been maintained 366 or even accelerated, so that work could already be under way to extend the link. In view of the condition of the M1 motorway when I last used it—on that occasion I travelled only 20 miles in one and three quarter hours—there are ample grounds for an extension of the pipelines system. Given the Government's lust to take out the oil from our offshore fields at the fastest possible speed—we now take out far more than we use—surely much of that oil could be transported by a pipelines system cheaply, smoothly and without causing offence and congestion.

The Government may not be prepared to accept the new clause; they may argue that they are taking out the oil so quickly that there is no point in having a pipelines system of any quality and that, for the short life of our oilfields, we may as well rely on congesting and disrupting the public transport system. By accepting the new clause they would confirm that they were not operating only on short-term considerations.

The Government have a choice to make. By accepting the new clause, they can demonstrate wisdom and a degree of political balance, which is certainly needed and with which they have not so far proved familiar. The new clause would provide substantial orders and considerable industrial advantage. It would make a helpful contribution to our public transport problems and would be helpful environmentally. If the reporting and public relations aspects are properly handled, it will, to some extent, demonstrate to Britain and the world that the Government are less dogmatic and less obsessed by greed than many Opposition Members have come to suspect over recent years.

Gentlemen that I shall look to the agency for proposals on the pipelines' longer-term development.

In 1984, non-OPEC sources provided 24 million barrels a day, 376 compared with OPEC's present supply of 16 million or 17 million barrels a day. There has been a dramatic shift. The other major alteration is the growth of a futures market. Inside two years, a major futures commodity market has been established in the United States—the New York metropolitan exchange—which celebrates its second anniversary this year. I was fascinated to see how many million barrels of crude oil are traded forward as part of the futures market. It is a new development in the way oil is traded.
