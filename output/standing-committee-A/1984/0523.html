Oil and Pipelines Bill

10.30 am

Motion made, and Question proposed,

That during the proceedings on the Oil and Pipelines Bill the Committee do meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays at half-past Ten o'clock.—[ Mr. Buchanan-Smith.]

Mr. Ted Rowlands
(Merthyr Tydfil and Rhymney)
: May I first welcome you to the Chair, Mr. Knox. I am sure that you will keep us on the straight and narrow path of order.

I want to use the opportunity that the sittings motion provides to raise two important but unrelated issues that cannot easily be dealt with by amendments, although with some ingenuity it might be possible to do so. The first is urgent and cannot wait until we reach the relevant clause, and the second concerns the whole structure of the Bill.

The first issue arises out of an assurance given by the Parliamentary Under-Secretary when he wound up on Second Reading. Hon. Members will recall that in that debate I made a specific and proper request to the Government that staff at the British National Oil Corporation should not be compelled to transfer to the new agency but should be given a legitimate and reasonable choice of redundancy and severance terms or a transfer.

I made that request because the truncated nature of the new Oil and Pipelines Agency does not offer the range of opportunities, career-wise and otherwise, for younger members of staff to fulfil themselves. After all, although some functions are to be transferred, the nature of OPA will be very different from that of the BNOC. Therefore, it is only fair and reasonable that BNOC staff have the option of joining the new agency or taking severance and redundancy pay.

I was heartened and relieved by the Under-Secretary's reply. The key sentence is:

"As, fortunately, the Socialist state has not yet dawned in this country the Government have neither means nor intention of forcing BNOC staff to transfer. — [Official Report, 14 May 1985; Vol. 79, c. 253.]

I took some comfort from that and the Under-Secretary got a modest cheer for his comment. I was the butt of a good jibe, but I did not care. I thought that I had what I wanted —an assurance made in good faith that no member of the staff of BNOC would have to cross over to the new agency but would have the option of taking severance and redundancy pay instead. The BNOC staff were relieved to have that statement.

It is therefore with much regret that I say that that assurance has been dishonoured. Some BNOC staff will be offered employment in the new Oil and Pipelines Agency, and, unless the Minister gives the commitment that we thought had been given, they will not have the choice of taking severance and redundancy.

The staff concerned are those who now work in the oil pipeline division of BNOC. No one wanted to be unfair to the staff. Indeed, hon. Members of all parties felt that the service that the staff had given and their proficiency 4 and efficiency entitled them to be treated with the utmost care and sensitivity, following the disruption of their careers as the result of a Government decision.

That applies as much to the staff working on the pipeline side of BNOC as to those working on the participation agreements and the oil trading side. In fact, there are even more reasons why staff working on the pipeline side should be treated rather better than is proposed. They have had to face more than a period of sudden uncertainty, with the serious disruption of their career prospects as a result of the Minister's out-of-the-blue statement of March 14, basically to abolish BNOC. They have lived under a cloud of uncertainty, at least since mid-1983.

I understand—although I cannot find any parliamentary statements, either written or oral, or any other information—that there has been a major review of the functions, structure organisation and management of the Government's pipeline storage system—to which I shall come later. I asked the Library to use its computer to go through all the parliamentary questions and oral and written statements about the Government's pipeline storage system, but no reference to the review was found.

I said that I had two issues to raise before approving or rejecting the sittings motion. The second relates to the structure of the Bill. Many moons ago—almost 20 years —before victorious elections interrupted my promising academic career, I was a tutor. I established a course at the LSE on the processes of legislation. The Lord Chancellor's Office partly paid for my salary; I do not think that he would pay for me now.

I am sure that I have got very rusty. I spent four and a half years in the Foreign Office. It does not produce much legislation—only one-clause independence Bills. I recommend that the Minister of State to move to the Foreign Office, as one does not end up sitting in Committees day and night.

I talked about the question of legislation when we debated the parent of the Bill—a dubious parent—the Oil and Gas (Enterprise) Act 1982.

I am very concerned about the whole structure of the Bill. One aspect that I hammered at least into myself—I hope that I taught it to my students properly—was the difference between a section and a schedule. A section is a substantive clause, but a schedule fulfils a different purpose.

When we seek procedural clarification we turn immediately to our bible, "Erskine May". I am glad that copies are available in the Committee. It gives the definition of a clause and of a schedule.

It states: JPEG 7

"At the end of many bills there is found a set of schedules which contain matters of detail dependent on the provisions of the bill. A schedule is a part of the bill and is dependent on one or more of the preceding clauses, by means of which the provisions of the schedule are carried into effect."

Therefore, there is a clear distinction between a section and a schedule. A section or clause is a substantive piece of legislation, but a schedule, as "Erskine May" says, is supposed to contain

"matters of detail dependent on the provisions of the bill."

Let us consider whether the structure of the Bill conforms with the definition laid down in "Erskine May". Schedule 3 contains not matters of detail, but all the grant-making and subsidising powers of the Secretary of State, and all the borrowing and financial provisions for the agency. All those provisions are contained in a schedule at the back of the Bill. By any definiton those are not just


"matters of detail dependent on the provisions of the Bill."

The main provision, or the substantive provision relating to finance, is the pathetic little subsection (3) in clause 5. That is the sole provision which triggers the whole borrowing, grant-making, subsidising and loan-making powers provided in the Bill.

I challenge the idea that, by any stretch of the imagination, the definition in "Erskine May" is being met in this structurally defective Bill. Draftsmanship seems to have declined since my day. Certainly, we would not expect such a Bill to contain all the borrowing or rule-making powers in one small subsection and have the rest of the them tucked away in a schedule.

We have a right to challenge the extraordinary structure of the Bill and to seek an explanation for it. I am not making a party point, but a perfectly proper, legislative and structural point and I hope that Conservative Members will support me in that.

I believed that one of the fundamental purposes of the House was the scrutiny of finance. If we accept the structure of this Bill, that power, which has been used to the tune of £65 million during the past 12 months, will be tucked away at the back of schedule 3. We object to that. It is not only bad draftsmanship; the Bill is structurally defective. The Government should not push the provision away and pretend that it is nothing but a detail.
