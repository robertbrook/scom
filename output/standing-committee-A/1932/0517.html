—(Licensing of goods vehicles.)

Sir JOHN SANDEMAN ALLEN
: I beg to move, in page 1, line 13, to leave out from the word "used", to the end of line 4, page 2.

This Amendment proposes to leave out paragraph ( b) of Sub-section (1) The Amendment as hon. Members must realise raises a main issue and therefore I hope that in discussing it, I may not be trammelled by too close limitations. This is one of the big issues in connection with this Measure which is disturbing traders in the country generally. I have read carefully the debate on the Second Reading of the Bill and I may remark, incidentally, that while hon. Members were busy in the House of Commons dealing with the national issue which arises here in this connection, I was in the chair at an International Conference in Paris dealing with international issues arising on exactly the same question. It is rather interesting to realise that, in many respects, exactly the same points arise in connection with this matter in the international and in the national sense and that on a large number of these points, the international mind has come into line with our national mind. 90 I do not say that we should feel either supported or hindered by international views in dealing with our own national issue, but it is important to realise that other peoples have the same troubles as we have, and are trying to deal with them in much the same way.

The issue before us presents itself in four different ways. First, there is the question of a complete monopoly of the whole transport of the country. I think we may pass from that consideration. Second, there is the question of the monopoly of road transport and the rail transport. That is bound, ultimately, to end in an agreed monopoly between all concerned. Only two other alternatives are visible. There is the alternative of full and free opportunities of competition, which, as we know by experience, has already destroyed a great deal, and is likely to destroy more of what is really part of our permanent system of transport. It is most important, therefore, that the country should realise that what we must have is controlled competition on reasonable terms, so as to make available to the public the very best service in transport of all kinds, without sacrificing the interests of the country, in any direction. One realises that, on the whole, every effort has been made by those responsible for this Bill, to meet the issues involved. Many of us who have Amendments to move in this Committee will do so first with the desire to elucidate the difficulties, and arrive at an understanding of the questions which are before us, and, secondly, with the desire to deal with what seem to us, real hindrances in an otherwise admirable Measure.

The issue raised by this Amendment is one which, I feel sure, the Minister and the Committee would not wish to pass over quickly. We are dealing here with a very large proportion of the goods vehicles of the country. I understand that the goods vehicles number between 360,000 and 400,000, and, of that number, some 250,000 to 275,000 are private goods vehicles—engaged in what is called on the Continent werkverkehr—dealing with special interests and carrying goods in connection with their own industries. The whole issue is, as to how far it is necessary to involve these private traders in the system of inquiries, and so forth, proposed by the Bill. These traders JPEG 91 have a large, interest in the industry and trade of the country, and are only using these vehicles, as I say, in connection with their industries. What we have to consider is, are they to be brought into the malestrom of inquiries and difficulties of various kinds, which necessarily arise out of an endeavour to control competition in a proper manner?

This particular set of vehicles which I have indicated is obviously in a special position, and I recognise that the Minister, in drawing up the Bill, has realised the fact that these vehicles are in a different position from other vehicles, because he has put them into a special class known as the "C" class. In spite of the very clear manner in which he gave his reasons on the Second Reading, many of us do not feel satisfied yet upon this matter, and it is important to have a full discussion in this Committee on the necessity or otherwise of bringing these people into the system of licensing. Surely some arrangement could be devised which would provide more effective power of dealing with these matters under the existing law, which theoretically provides for all that it is necessary to do in these matters. I realise that one of the great difficulties is the question of return loads, which have nothing to do with the business to which the vehicles belong. That matter I understand is to be dealt with, and I think if we consider the question closely we will see that there is an enormous amount in the detailed provisions of the Bill which is quite unnecessary in connection with vehicles of that kind.

I daresay that the Minister will tell us that he considers these inquiries, and so forth, necessary in connection with these vehicles. I do not suppose that he wishes, deliberately, to expose these traders to unnecessary interference. But it does not seem desirable to those of us who represent business interests, that there should be, on a question of this kind, investigations into arrangements and contracts and so forth made in the conduct of business affairs. The less a Government interferes with our private affairs in trade, the better for us and the better for the Government.

Sir G. RENTOUL
: I submit to the hon. Gentleman that it must be so, when one considers the enormous number of vehicles to which these "C" licences will apply. If, as I suggest, it means a substantial additional burden to an important and growing industry, the onus rests on the Government to show an overwhelming case in support of their proposal, a case that will outweigh the disadvantages which are obvious, and which, indeed, leap to the eye when one considers this matter. I suggest that it does mean a substantial burden, if we only consider the number of vehicles involved. According to the figures supplied to me, the "C" licence will apply to 275,000 vehicles out of 370,000 vehicles used for carrying goods on the road. It will apply to three out of four vehicles used for the transport of goods and I fail to appreciate how anyone can suggest that that is not a very important issue.

Every manufacturer and trader who uses a vehicle for his own business, will have to obtain this additional licence. He will have to make formal application in the first place, to the Traffic Commissioner. He will have to supply full par- 94 ticulars, not only of the vehicle itself, but of the business in which it is to be used. The Traffic Commissioner, on the other hand, is not allowed to refuse the licence, but is entitled to attach conditions, and the Minister gives great importance to the imposition of those conditions. If the conditions are not observed, the licence can be revoked and a heavy fine imposed.

Surely all that must mean a heavy burden and therefore a hindrance to the development of trade and industry in this country. It must mean a substantial addition to the Government staff, because they will have to deal with more than 270,000 applications for those new licences, and, so far as one can see, there is really no practical purpose to be served at all. I am not quite clear as to exactly what particulars will be required from anyone applying for a "C" licence, but I rather fear this may be the beginning of a new form of inquisition of an altogether unreasonable kind. I submit, further, that this "C" licence is unnecessary because, as my hon. Friend has pointed out, all the obligations that one could imagine being attached to such a licence are already provided for under existing legislation. What are those conditions? The first, no doubt, would be to maintain the vehicle in a fit and serviceable state. That is very necessary, but there are existing regulations under the Road Traffic Act, passed only three years ago, for that very purpose. If it is said they are not observed that seems to me an argument for a more strict enforcement of existing legislation, and not an argument for scrapping it and saying there must be a new Act.

The second condition one imagines being imposed would be to observe the speed limit. The First Schedule of the Road Traffic Act deals with that, laying down different speeds for different types of vehicles, and fixing heavy penalties for their infringement. There is difficulty in enforcing those speed limits, and therefore this new method is desired. Then there are the hours of duty of the driver. That point is already provided for under the Road Traffic Act. When that Act was passing through the House of Commons we had hours and hours of discussion on all these matters, and are we really to be told, after only two or three years, that those discussions were to all JPEG 95 intents and purposes a waste of time because the Act is unenforceable? Finally, there is a provision for the keeping of records of the hours of work and the loads. That is a new provision, and it imposes a heavy burden, and a very strong case ought to be made out in support of it. Already the Minister has felt it necessary—and I am sure we all support the decision—to exempt agricultural vehicles. Surely the same reasons which apply to those vehicles apply to many other vehicles. My hon. Friend has already stressed the fact that the Royal Commission definitely reported against any licensing system of this kind for people using their own vehicles on their own business, and, so far as I am aware, we have not yet been told what has induced the Government to depart from the recommendation of a Royal Commission which devoted so much time and thought to this matter.

I am led to understand that the main reason why the Minister of Transport attaches so much importance to the "C" licence is that the provisions of the Road Traffic Act with regard to speed limits and the like have become a dead letter, or that the Home Office has found that they are very difficult to enforce, and therefore it is desired to achieve the same object by this new system of licensing. I feel very apprehensive about that. I do not see how it is going to work. The suggestion is, apparently, made that evidence of a breach of the speed limit is so difficult to obtain that it is difficult to bring cases before the courts in such circumstances as will lead to a conviction, or, alternatively, that magistrates are not sufficiently impressed by the evidence to do more than inflict a small and, may be, inadequate fine.

That being the state of affairs, it is suggested that the difficulty can be got over by imposing a new "C" licence on so many of these vehicles. How is that licence going to be granted or revoked? A magistrate finds himself unable on the evidence to convict, or does not think the case worthy of anything more than what is regarded as an insufficient penalty, yet a licensing committee, months afterwards, it may be, is to re-try the case, as it were, on hearsay evidence, and impose a penalty which in some cases may involve the loss of a man's livelihood. 96 It seems to me that is a risk to which people should not be subject, that no clear case has been made out for this new licence, and that this legislation has in it inherent dangers and difficulties. Therefore, I have no hesitation in supporting this Amendment, and I hope the Minister will give us a much fuller and clearer explanation of why this licence is necessary.
