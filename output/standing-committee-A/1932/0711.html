NEW CLAUSE.
—(Erection of weighbridges.)

The Minister may erect and maintain weighbridges for the purpose of testing the weights, laden and unladen, of motor vehicles.—[ Sir B. Peto.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Sir BASIL PETO
: I beg to move, "That the Clause be read a Second time."

I am moving this Clause in the absence of the hon. Member for Gloucester (Mr. Boyce). When the Minister was speaking on the Second Reading of the Bill he said:

"The next condition is with regard to speed limits and loading. Here, again, conditions do not differ in any way from the conditions which are now laid down by regulation. The only difference is that I hope in future we are going to be able to enforce them, at the present we have been unable to do so."—[OFFICIAL REPORT, 3rd May, 1933, col. 868; Vol. 277.]

In this Bill we have made what I can only term elaborate preparations for doing what the Minister indicated, that is, making the provisions as to weighing vehicles, loaded and unloaded, really effective. The only trouble is that the supply of weighbridges in the country is utterly inadequate and under the existing process of supplying weighbridges there does not seem to be any likelihood that a sufficient number will be forthcoming. The Committee will remember that in Clause 8 we have enacted that it shall be a condition of every licence that any provisions, whether contained in any statute or in any statutory rules or orders, with regard to the limits of weight laden and unladen and the loading of vehicles, are complied with. In Clause 15 we agreed to the appointment of examiners for the purpose of securing, in the case 750 of goods vehicles, their maintenance in a fit and serviceable condition and the observance of the provisions of the Road Traffic Act, 1930. In Clause 16 it is provided that an examiner may at any time, on production of his authority, exercise with respect to the weighing of goods vehicles all such powers as are, under Section 27 of the Road Traffic Act, 1930, exerciseable by a police constable.

There we have all the machinery for weighing loads and vehicles, and Section 27 of the Road Traffic Act, 1930, confers upon any person authorised by a highway authority or any police constable authorised on behalf of a highway authority the power to require the person in charge of a motor vehicle to allow the vehicle and any trailer drawn by it to be weighed, laden or unladen. The Committee has agreed to all these provisions because it has realised that the practice of overloading must be stopped; it is one of the main purposes of the Bill. Unfortunately, the weighbridges available at the present time are inadequate in number, and that fact, more than any other, is likely to result in the requirements of the law, both as it exists and as it is proposed to make it under the Bill, being ignored in the future as in the past. For, obviously, unless weighbridges are available for the examiners there can be no hope of enforcing the conditions as to the weight of vehicles and loads.

What is the position with regard to the provision of weighbridges? Under Section 27 (4) of the Road Traffic Act, 1930, it is laid down that it shall be lawful for a highway authority to erect and maintain or join with any other highway authority in erecting and maintaining weighbridges or other machines for weighing vehicles, or to contribute towards the cost of the erection and maintenance of any such weighbridges or other machines by any other authority or person. In Sub-section (5) provision is made for any expense so incurred by the highway authority being defrayed either as expenses for general county purposes or, in the case of a borough or district council, as general expenses incurred by the council in the execution of their duties under the Public Health Acts, 1875 and 1926.

In the past, highway authorities have not taken advantage of those provisions, and if, as the Minister stated, an attempt JPEG 751 is to be made to enforce the law, the provision of weighbridges should not be left to their discretion but should be undertaken by a central authority on some definite and well thought out lines. I am moving this Clause because I am convinced that unless the Minister takes power to erect weighbridges—there is nothing compulsory in this Clause—where the existing provisions for getting weighbridges erected do not operate we shall not have the necessary supply. The provisions of Sub-section (5) of Section 27 of the Road Traffic Act, 1930, enable a local authority to obtain a grant in respect of the expenditure on weighbridges. But a grant of 50 per cent., or even of 100 per cent. would not, in my opinion, ensure that the highway authorities would take action. There is more involved than the question of providing the money; there is also the question of whether there is to be any adequate distribution of weighbridges about the country, which is clearly a matter which is not best left in the hands of individual local authorities. It would be very unsatisfactory if there were an adequate number of weighbridges in one authority's area and practically none in the next and the next but one, so that a vehicle might have to travel 100 miles before it came to another weighbridge. For all these reasons I think the Bill will not be complete unless the Minister takes power to erect weighbridges upon a definite scheme under some central authority so as to ensure the provision of a suitable number of weighbridges in all parts of the country.

NEW CLAUSE.
—(Calculation of weight of container vehicles.)

For the purposes of Part I. of this Act and of the Road Traffic Act, 1930, section twenty-six (Method of calculating weight) of the last-mentioned Act shall have effect so that the weight of a vehicle constructed to carry goods packed in detachable containers shall include the chassis of the vehicle on which the container rests but shall not include the container.—[ Sir P. Dawson.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Sir PHILIP DAWSON
: I beg to move, "That the Clause be read a Second time."

The object of the Clause is to define more clearly the weight of an unladen vehicle, and for this purpose we must go back to Section 26 of the Road Traffic Act, 1930, which says:

"The weight unladen of any vehicle to be taken to be the weight of the vehicle inclusive of the body and all parts … . which are necessary to or ordinarily used with the vehicle when working on a road."

The object of this Clause is to clear up misconceptions which exist to-day, as is seen from decisions in the Courts, as to whether a container should be part of the vehicle or part of the load. Containers are becoming more and more popular, and their use is going to be of great benefit to those mostly concerned in transport, namely, the public at large, for whom I am speaking. Containers will vary in shape, size, and weight, according to the goods that have to be transported in them, and if the vehicle at one time has to carry one form of container, and at another time another form, and that container is part of the load, or, in other words, really takes the place of a packing case, it is not right that it should form part of the weight. We have, JPEG 759 in the furniture trade of to-day, a container going to the domicile, and various articles of china, furniture, and so on, are practically packed loose in it. It is then put on the vehicle, taken to the railroad, and thence to a port, where it is put into the hold of a ship, and eventually it is delivered to the consignee.

In connection with all deliveries the use of containers is becoming more and more extensive. In the Report of the Royal Commission on Transport, there is a definite recommendation that containers should be used much more, and, therefore, anything that will hamper their use and will make it more difficult for them to be employed in regular practice, should be avoided. It seems to me that the container, which will vary in size and weight with each kind of consignment, ought not to form part of the vehicle which will carry it, a vehicle which could carry on either with or without the container.

Mr. STANLEY
: My hon. Friend has made out an excellent case for the treatment of containers as not being part of the unladen weight of the vehicle. I do not know if the case was equally well made out in the 1930 Act, and whether it was a consequence of that that the Statute was so worded that in fact containers are not to-day treated as part of the unladen weight of the vehicle. The sort of container which my hon. Friend pictured to the Committee, namely, the substitution, for a large number of packing cases, of one all-embracing packing case, is to-day not counted in the unladen weight. The type of container that is counted is the type which, in fact, is an alternative form of body, not a container at all, and when hon. Members think that the object of this Clause is not for the purpose of taxation, but to arrive at the maximum weight which is safe for vehicles on the road, it is clear that if you say that a furniture van, where the body is fixed, may carry such and such a load, and then you say that if only they are able to detach that furniture body, they can carry so much more, either you will have to penalise the man who has the fixed body, by setting his maximum weight too low, or you must allow the man who has a detachable body to go on the road with a load which, in fact, is too heavy for safety.

760 That is the only case which has ever come up in the Courts. The only case where a judicial decision has been arrived at was that of a detachable furniture van, where the body was put on to the chassis, and could go off and pick up furniture, just as the ordinary, fixed-body furniture van could have done. It seems to me clear that that type of case should be treated, as it was, as an alternative body, and therefore included in the unladen weight, but I can assure hon. Members who believe, and rightly believe, in the increased use of containers in the future, instead of a large number of separate packing cases, that that type of case is met by the wording of the 1930 Act, and that that type of container would not be counted in the unladen weight of the vehicle.

NEW CLAUSE.
—(Carriage of goods of associated companies.)

A company (in this section referred to as "the carrying company") shall be deemed to be carrying goods for or in connection with its own trade or business if it shall be carrying goods for another company or group of companies associated with the carrying company by reason that the whole or the major part of its issued capital is held or owned by such other company or by one or more of the said group of companies, or it holds or owns the whole or the major part of the issued ordinary shares of any such other company, and every company shall be deemed to be included in the said group of companies if the major part of its issued ordinary shares are held or owned by or it holds or owns the major part of the issued ordinary shares of any other company in the same group.—[ Sir G. : As one who, I am afraid, has trespassed somewhat unduly on your patience and on the forbearance 798 of the Committee, I should like to associate myself with the expressions of gratitude to you, Sir William, for the courtesy with which you have conducted our proceedings and which you have extended to all of us. If, as a result of our labours, we may hope to have improved the Bill, I trust that the Minister will consider that we have made his task a little more interesting and stimulating than otherwise it might have been, because it must be rather enervating to be in charge of a Bill of this kind unless it meets with adequate criticism. I would like to add this to my congratulations to the Minister. I do not know whether he will regard it exactly as a compliment or not, but I have had the privilege of serving on a good many Standing Committees, and have never had occasion to be filled with so much admiration for the skill with which the Minister has succeeded in giving so little away, in resisting a series of admirable Amendments moved by myself and others, and in bringing the Bill through Committee almost unchanged. Whether it will be better or worse for that, time alone will show, but we should like to congratulate you, Sir William, on the conclusion of your labours with the Committee, and the Minister on the ability, and, I may add, the determination, with which he has conducted the proceedings to the conclusion of the Committee stage.

Mr. STANLEY

I quite realise that the task of the Committee, in dealing with a Bill of this kind, might have been made quite impossible if there had been a real desire on the part of hon. Members to prevent its passage into law. Several hon. Members showed that they were not at all deficient in power, when they felt it right to use their eloquence, both as regards quality and quantity of speech, and, if that power had been used for the purpose of wrecking the Bill instead of amending it, its passage would have been made difficult, if not impossible. I would only say to my hon. and learned JPEG 799 Friend the Member for Lowestoft (Sir G. The CHAIRMAN
: May I, in saying, "Thank you," add that I have never presided over a Committee which has been more considerate; and I am very much obliged to you all for the kind way in which you have conducted yourselves, 800 making it very easy for me to conduct the business from the Chair. I should like also to pay a tribute to the Clerks, who have done their work very excellently indeed. I appreciate their services very much, and they are well worthy of any thanks that we may give to them.
