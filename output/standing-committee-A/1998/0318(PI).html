Mr. Ottaway: The Minister kindly reminded me that we debated amendment No. 221 some two months ago, so I hope that my temporary lapse of memory in respect of that amendment—whatever its implications may have been—is understandable.

We have come to another very serious group of clauses. What we are trying to cover today is almost a Bill in itself. We will do our best to cover it at a decent pace. Perhaps I can start by noting that we shall be taking almost a Second Reading approach in our amendments to this part of the Bill.

While there is a role for strategic planning on a Londonwide basis, there is concern that the planning provisions will add an unnecessary extra tier to the current planning system, which could increase delays and costs and harm London's economic development without necessarily improving the decision-making process.

Col. 1322 London's current planning structure is well known. There is a national policy under the Secretary of State; there is a regional policy, which is more commonly known to everyone as SERPLAN, or the south-east regional planning conference, and which works under regional planning guidance 9; and there is a strategic policy, which works under RPG3 and is operated by the Secretary of State proposals from the London boroughs and the common council, more commonly known as the London Planning Advisory Committee, or LP AC. Then, of course, there will be unitary development plans, which are prepared by the London boroughs.

It would be helpful if we switched quickly to acronyms rather than talking in lengthy phrases. I shall hereafter refer to unitary development plans as UDPs and to the spatial development strategy as SDS. I hope that that will be of assistance to Hansard staff.

Planning control under the tiers that I mentioned is two-tiered—planning applications are determined by the London boroughs unless an application is called in by the Secretary of State to decide for himself. The Bill proposes a third tier. Strategic policy is to be set by the mayor with the London boroughs, but the boroughs will have no greater role in policy making than members of the public. That is one of our first concerns. The boroughs' UDPs will be in general conformity with the mayor's strategic policy. Three tiers will therefore be in place. Planning applications are to be determined by the boroughs, but the mayor will be able to direct refusal of specific applications, which will usually be major. The Minister set that out in answer to a parliamentary question a couple of days ago. The Secretary of State still has a power to call in applications.

The Opposition have a different approach. We agree that the mayor should take over strategic policy, but there should be formal borough involvement. That strategic policy should be given the same status as RGP3, which is what LP AC has at the moment. We also want to leave out of the Bill or to limit severely the mayor's power to direct refusal. If the mayor is concerned about an application, he should be able to ask the Secretary of State to call it in. That is the approach proposed by the amendments.

Amendment No. 1084 would make subsection (1) state:

"The Mayor shall … prepare and publish a document to be based on the boroughs' views and to be known as the 'spatial development strategy'."

It would therefore require the mayor to base the strategy on the boroughs' views.

I am optimistic that I may be able to persuade the Minister to accept the amendment. He will be aware that the Deputy Prime Minister pledged on Second Reading that the strategic plan would be based on boroughs' views. The Opposition have not sought to exaggerate that pledge, and the amendment is based exactly on the Deputy Prime Minister's words on Second Reading.

The Bill stipulates that the boroughs will be first able to express an opinion on the SDS when it is published in draft for public consultation. It involves them in the process to some extent, but does not require the mayor to Image Col. 1323 follow their views or to pay special attention to them. The amendment would strengthen the role of the boroughs, which know what is going on in their areas. The boroughs will carry out the vast majority of planning, so we hope and expect that the Government will fulfil the Deputy Prime Minister's pledge. Amendment No. 1095 follows through that theme. I shall be interested to hear the Minister's response.

Mr. Paul Burstow (Sutton and Cheam): Like the hon. Member for Croydon, South (Mr. Ottaway) I should like to make one or two general comments about this part of the Bill. I shall try to keep those comments brief and then move on to the detail of the amendments.

I agree with the hon. Member for Croydon, South that the record of our current discussions should contain a list of abbreviations at the beginning so that we can all speak in the shorthand relevant to this part of the Bill. The spatial development strategy—or SDS, as it should perhaps now be referred to—provides to London and to the mayor a useful tool in integrating the various policies and strategies that the mayor is responsible for putting together. It is where different strategies come together and where issues of conflict should be resolved. It should harmonise the strategies and ensure that they fit together.

The clauses give us hope that that is the Government intention, and are reassuring to that extent. The provisions allow the London boroughs to have reasonable involvement. Perhaps more importantly, they allow to the public and to other interested communities in Greater London involvement in developing strategy.

The issue that concerns us most relates to a point made by the hon. Member for Croydon, South. How will the boroughs relate to the SDSs, and how will UDPs be informed by them? The SDS is a new creature in the planning world. It will not have the same standing as the structure plan; it will have a slightly different standing. However, as I understand it, it will form a material consideration in planning applications. All those matters mean that we need to be clear exactly how those things will relate to each other—I shall return to a number of questions as we proceed through the clauses.

The amendments raise an important question about the need to build the boroughs much more closely into the process of drawing up the SDS. I entirely agree with the sentiment behind the points made by the hon. Member for Croydon, South, but I am not sure that the amendment will have the effect that he intends. The amendment states that the SDS shall be based on the views of the London boroughs. That can only mean that it must draw on the UDPS.

The problem with that is the UDPs are running at different rates and are at different stages in their development. Some are old and are now under review; some are just about to be adopted; some are undergoing public inquiry. To try to pull together a single spatial development strategy for London based on those plans would result in a patchwork quilt, rather than something that would enable the mayor to take a view of the current situation and how we should move in future.

Col. 1324 As the UDPs are on statutory timetables, it is not easy to knit them all together. However, the hon. Gentleman may have something else in mind.

Mr. Burstow: That is entirely right. As I read the Bill, the boroughs will be involved at the second stage; by then much work will already have been done to formulate the plan. There is a doubt about whether even the functioning bodies are directly involved in the first stage of drawing up the plan. London First has some interesting analysis. It has identified a seven-stage process that the Bill will put in place, which does not involve the boroughs until about stages five or six. They will not have a chance to have their say until a long way through the process.

As I understand it, the first stage will be that the mayor will prepare a draft SDS. At the second stage, there will be consultation with the assembly and functioning bodies. At the third stage, there will be redrafting to take account of views put to the mayor. At the fourth stage, the London boroughs will be consulted, along with all other organisations that the mayor deems to be appropriate. The mayor will then publish the SDS—presumably, taking into account the views of the boroughs and others. Then there will be stage six, which will be public examination of the SDS. Finally, although it is not explicit in the Bill, the definitive SDS will be published.

That is a lengthy process, and it will be interesting to hear how long Ministers envisage it taking especially in the first instance. Producing the first SDS, with the need to involve the boroughs and draw in other parties to ensure that everyone feels that the strategy meets the needs of London, is bound to be a lengthy, time-consuming process. We have only to consider how long it takes a London borough to produce a unitary development plan to realise that it takes an awful long time to move from early draft to final adoption. We will be interested to return to that later.

We agree with the sentiments of the amendments, but other later amendments deal with the question of involvement of the boroughs. The boroughs need to be involved earlier in the process than is currently provided for in the Bill. Our view is that they should be involved at the first stage, alongside the assembly and the functional bodies. It would be better to amend a later clause to achieve that.
