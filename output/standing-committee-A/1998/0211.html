Clause 87
MAYOR'S DUTY TO DISTRIBUTE CERTAIN GRANTS ETC BETWEEN AUTHORITY AND FUNCTIONAL BODIES

Mr. Peter Brooke (Cities of London and Westminster)
: I beg to move amendment No. 381, in page 47, line 35, at end insert—

'including a percentage, to be determined, of enhancement obtained during the previous year to the level of non-domestic rates in Greater London.'.

You, Mr. O'Hara, welcomed us back, and I welcome you back. I am entirely resigned to the Minister for London and Construction telling me that the wording of my amendment is technically defective. He can be as callous in his language as he wishes.

One of the mayor's key functions in conjunction with the London development agency will be to attract new investment to London and to secure regeneration of brownfield sites. The Bill as drafted exhorts the mayor to do that, but does not provide him with a direct financial incentive to do so. My amendment, however inexpertly it is expressed, is intended to remedy that situation by adding to the mayor's budget a share of any enhancement in the level of non-domestic rates attributable to Greater London.

For example, the London First centre—London First's inward investment subsidiary—has identified more than 30 major sites comprising more than 1,000 acres which are market-ready, and a much larger number of sites in the Lea valley, the Thames gateway, the Wandle valley and in west London, which require more investment to unlock their potential. Development of those brownfield sites will create employment and generate tax revenues for the Treasury, including a substantial addition to non-domestic rates. However, it will be a major task requiring substantial resources to help prepare sites for development and to market them to attract inward investment.

Under the present arrangements, the extra tax revenue generated and the costs involved are completely unrelated. If the GLA wants to step up its regeneration and marketing efforts, it will be dependent on bidding for a larger share of national resources or increasing the burden on council taxpayers. It would be much better for the cost of such efforts to be funded by part of the increased tax revenue that is generated. That arrangement would be simpler, fairer and more transparent.

Mr. Raynsford
: The right hon. Gentleman said that I could be as callous as I wished about the wording of the amendment. I would not wish to be in any way callous to the right hon. Gentleman, who has played a constructive role throughout the Committee. I must admit that from the wording of his amendment I was left far from clear about its intentions, until he gave us his admirable exposition.

The problem is that the clause is about the distribution of resources by the GLA, while the intention behind the amendment is to increase the resources available for distribution. I think that the right hon. Gentleman would accept that the 468 amendment does not logically fit the clause. I will not get into textual analysis about the quality of the drafting.

The income from the national rate is currently distributed among authorities in proportion to their population, and is shared between the district and upper-tier authorities according to the services they provide. That helps in equalising resources between rate-rich and rate-poor authorities, and, together with the revenue support grant system, allows councils to provide a similar level of service throughout the country while setting broadly the same rate of council tax.

The amendment appears to suggest that the rate income should be top-sliced before distribution, and that the top slice should go to the GLA. I can see no justification for that. It would run counter to the principle of resource equalisation that I have outlined and would undoubtedly generate considerable unhappiness elsewhere in the country.

The right hon. Gentleman has raised a serious issue, however, by asking what incentive there is for the mayor to seek additional investment in London. There is an obvious incentive: the economic health and well-being of the capital city, irrespective of the revenue available to the GLA. The mayor will want to liaise closely with inward investment agencies, such as the London First centre and others, to ensure that London continues to attract inward investment even if it does not generate additional revenue for the GLA. Ultimately, a prosperous city will be well placed to provide for and to meet the needs of its citizens, as the mayor will rightly want it to.

I urge the right hon. Gentleman to ask leave to withdraw his amendment, not because of its technical deficiencies, but because it would introduce a mechanism that would be inappropriate in the wider framework of local government finance and because the mayor will not need incentives to do his or her best for London. I am sure that the mayor will do whatever he or she can to boost the economy of this city without any such incentive.

Mr. Brooke
: It would be wrong of me to press the amendment, as the Minister has politely drawn my attention to the fact that it is aimed at an inappropriate place in the Bill. I shall, however, remark on an irony. Hon. Members on the Opposition Benches, or at least Conservative Members, are always more interested in the increase of resources than in the nature of their distribution. Labour Members are always more interested in their distribution than in their increase. That is a happy statement of the dichotomy between us.

The Minister was kind enough to refer to the thrust of the amendment. He will be aware of a misgiving that has existed in some local authorities since the change in the distribution of non-domestic rates. Such authorities are concerned that there is no longer the incentive that there used to be for specific local authorities to work with local business for increases in resources. The necessity of such an incentive is a continuing issue.

Mr. Raynsford
: I beg to move amendment No. 307, in page 47, line 41, at end insert—

'(3A) The instalments to be paid under subsection (3) above to a functional body shall be payments of such amounts, and shall be payable at such times, as will enable the body to meet its budgeted expenditure for the year as it falls due.


(3B) It shall be the duty of the Authority to pay instalments under subsection (3) above punctually.


(3C) In the application of subsection (3A) above in relation to a functional body, "budgeted expenditure" means expenditure which, in accordance with the calculations (or last calculations) made under section 70(4) to (7) above, the body is to meet out of payments by way of instalments under this section.'.

Clause 87, as drafted, places the GLA under a duty to pay functional bodies their share of the sums that it receives in general grants in accordance with their budgets. The GLA will make the payments by instalments during the financial year. The amendment will require the GLA to pay a sufficient amount in each instalment at an appropriate time to enable the functional bodies to meet their budgeted expenditure. That will ensure that each functional body has the funds to pay its way. The GLA will need to pay each instalment punctually, to ensure that the mayor cannot retain funds derived from general grants that are due to the functional bodies.

The amendment is small, but I hope that the Committee will recognise that it is necessary.

Amendment agreed to.
