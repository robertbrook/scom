Clause 210
OPENNESS

Question proposed [this day], That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Question again proposed.

Mr. Raynsford
: Before responding to the clause stand part debate on clause 210, I should like to take this opportunity to clarify a comment that I made before lunch about the effect of clause 208. As I said, that is a simple clause, which applies to the Metropolitan police authority the requirement to secure the maintenance of an efficient and effective police force in its area. I inadvertently said that that duty is currently placed on the Home Secretary, as police authority for the Metropolitan police district. That is not the case. The duty is currently applied under section 6 of the Police Act 1996 to all police authorities outside London.

The effect of clause 208 is therefore to apply to the MPA a provision that applies to all existing police authorities outside the capital. It ensures that the general functions of the MPA will be the same as those exercised by other police authorities. I wanted to put that 1242 clarification on the record, to ensure that no one felt that I had misled the Committee.

Clause 210 relates to the openness provisions of the MPA. In speaking to the clause, the hon. Member for Kingston and Surbiton (Mr. Davey) rightly welcomed its commitment to openness. I appreciate that welcome from the Liberal Democrats.

The hon. Gentleman highlighted a number of issues—in particular, freemasonry. I should make it clear to the Committee that the Home Secretary has given the Select Committee on Home Affairs a commitment that internal police force registers will be in place by this April. Work is in progress to achieve that objective. The hon. Gentleman also asked about publication of the registers. After the internal registers have been established, it will be necessary to agree arrangements with the police service that allow for public access to information that has been voluntarily disclosed by individuals.

The main provisions of the clause provide that meetings of the MPA shall normally be open to the public, that copies of agenda and reports for meetings shall be available for public inspection and that their minutes shall also be available for such inspection. Those provisions bring the MPA into line with provisions that apply generally to local government bodies under part VA of the Local Government Act 1972.

I believe that the clause is welcome, and having answered the questions of the hon. Member for Kingston and Surbiton, I hope that the Committee will agree that the clause should stand part of the Bill.

I did not know, until preparing for the debate, that although there has been a Deputy Commissioner for a couple of years, it is a title of honour; he is not a creature of statute. The Bill provides that the Met police will have a deputy as an official post holder provided by law, which will rightfully put it on the same footing as other forces in England. I am not sure about the City, because that is anomalous. However, I welcome the idea. It seems entirely sensible to have a senior officer and also to have provision for someone appointed to a recognised post of deputy commissioner.

The question is whether it is right that, alone among police forces, the chief police officer is appointed by the monarch. The right hon. Member for Cities of London and Westminster probably knows much more about this than any of us from his indepth historical knowledge. My understanding is that the monarch's appointment derives from the fact that the Commissioner was perceived as having a governmental or civil servant-like relationship to the monarch—he would, in particular, protect the monarch's person—unlike other chief officers of police. That is the reason for that format of appointment. My understanding is that in the other police forces in England, the chief officer of police is appointed by the Home Secretary on the recommendation of the police authority. We are arguing that the same procedures should apply in London as apply elsewhere.

I am aware of the reasons for objections to that proposal. However, I should like to pray in aid three things. First, the position of the Association of London Government is one that we advocate. Secondly, the belief is widely held on a cross-party basis in London. By coincidence, I heard on my radio this morning about Lord Harris of Haringey speaking at a meeting today and arguing that the appointment should be made by the MPA, or at least that it should make a recommendation to the Home Secretary. The third is the Macpherson report, which specifically recommended that London should not differ in this respect from other police authorities. So there are three supporting personae arguing collectively, with authority, and after reflection, for such a proposal.

I should like to make one point of substance about why this is a good idea and then deal with the reasons against 1246 it. Instead of being a force that is run by government, the Met would become a force that is truly accountable to the representatives of London. We were debating earlier the fact that the MPA will have a similar structure to other police services—a majority of elected representatives, a minority of magistrates and a third group appointed from elsewhere. If the MPA is to have control of the Met's strategy—not the operational, day-to-day service—withholding from the authority its power to appoint the commissioner would be to deprive it of the ultimate power. To use a timely parallel, it is a bit like giving the power to appoint and get rid of commissioners in Brussels to the Council of Ministers, not the European Parliament. Had the Commissioners in Brussels not resigned last night, they could have been collectively sacked by the European Parliament. As it happens, that was not necessary. So if we are really to democratise and rationalise London policing, the commissioner ought to be appointed by the MPA.

There is a second way of dealing with the defence of this argument. I know that the Met police have certain duties that are regarded as different from those elsewhere in the country. First, they are regarded as having royal policing duties, but so do the police in Norfolk, Tayside, Lothian, and all other forces when the monarch or the royal family travel elsewhere round the country. Secondly, it may be said that the Metropolitan police have special diplomatic functions. They do, because embassies and consulates are by and large based in London, but ambassadors and consuls do not do their work here alone; certainly consuls have offices in other parts of the country than London. It is no longer the case that the Met has exclusive jurisdiction over the work of heads of foreign missions and the British head of state.

Given that the Home Secretary currently has the power to appoint elsewhere, it seems perfectly proper and reasonable to compromise and to allow the Home Secretary to appoint the chief officer of the Met. That should be done in the same way as the Prime Minister appoints bishops in the Church of England—I do not pretend to advocate that, but it is current practice. After various processes he is given a list—it happens to be a list of two—from which he selects a name. He is also entitled to reject the names.

I am happy for Ministers to say, as a way of accepting the general proposition, that they are willing to concede that it should not be a monarch's appointment, or it should be a monarch's appointment in name only—although I would rather it was not a monarch's appointment at all; we would prefer that it came via nomination from the assembly, it being the body charged with policing in London. The Home Secretary would then choose from the names that the assembly had produced.

There needs to be a power to remove a chief of police, just as there needs to be a power to remove other chief officers of public services. They can be suspended and, if necessary, they need to be able to be removed. At present, that power is vested outside the new authority. It seems proper that the London police system ought to have the power to remove a chief of police if that was thought to be appropriate, but only in particular circumstances and with checks and balances.

Thus, if ever it was to happen, two bodies would have to agree, one by majority and the other by a two thirds majority. I hope that Ministers accept that we ought to modernise London police structures as well as change them, and that they will therefore be sympathetic to the amendments. I am entirely happy to accept that they might be taken away for redrafting and presented with a ministerial formulation of words, if that was an acceptable compromise.

Amendment No. 985 would keep the statutory obligation to consult local people on their concerns and priorities for policing in the Metropolitan police district with the Commissioner, rather than transferring it to the new police authority. It is also important to understand why, for areas outside London, the Police Act 1996 places the requirement to consult on police authorities. Taking the views of local people into account is part of the process by which police authorities draw up their policing plans for the area, and the link between public consultation and local policing objectives must be explicit. It is for a police authority to hold the police to account for the objectives that have been set, and the police authority must be aware of the priorities and concerns of local people in doing so.

Section 96(2) of the Police Act 1996, as amended by the Bill, will require the MPA to consult the Commissioner on the most appropriate arrangements for consultation. Although the MPA will be the body that is required to consult, it will naturally consult with the Commissioner on the most appropriate arrangements for consultation.

Paragraph 98 of schedule 17 amends the 1996 Act to bring arrangements for consultation within the MPD into line with other police areas. That is consistent with the policy of giving the MPA similar functions and duties to those of other police authorities. While the Home JPEG 1281 Secretary is the police authority for the Met, separate arrangements are justified, but once an elected authority is in place that justification will no longer exist. The Bill will not stop the Metropolitan police from seeking the views of the public, but it will ensure that the MPA can fulfil the proper role of a police authority.

Amendment No. 1050 refers to the issuing of guidance to PCCGs by the MPA. That is a requirement that is not imposed on any other police authority. Furthermore, as PCCGs are not statutory bodies, for the amendment to work, another amendment would be required to constitute them as such.
